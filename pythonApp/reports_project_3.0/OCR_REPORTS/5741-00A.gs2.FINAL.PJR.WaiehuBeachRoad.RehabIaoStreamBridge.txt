SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
New pavements will be constructed as part of the Waiehu Beach Road 
(Route 3400), Rehabilitation of Iao Stream Bridge project. Based on our field exploration 
and the proposed grading for the project, we anticipate the majority of the pavement 
subgrade soils for the new roadway generally will consist of granular fill material and basalt 
formation. Due to the composition of the subgrade materials, we believe that a permeable 
drainage layer should be incorporated into the pavement design for the new roadways. 
Therefore, we recommend considering only pavement structural sections with a 
permeable drainage layer in the pavement design. 
Based on the traffic data provided, the R-value of the subgrade materials, and the 
economic analyses performed, the rigid pavement structural section was the most 
economical. For rigid pavement construction, a significant amount of roadway closures will 
be required to allow time for the concrete pavement to cure and harden.  Although a rigid 
pavement section was most economical, we recommend using a flexible pavement 
structural section for the new pavements for the project to significantly reduce the amount 
of roadway closures. The recommended pavement structural section, consisting of 
4.0 inches of AC over 8.0 inches ACB over 6.0 inches of UTPB over 6.0 inches of ASB, 
should be used for the design of pavements for this project. 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to 
saturation of the base, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, special attention should 
be given to the surface and subsurface drainage of the pavements. As a minimum, the 
pavement surface should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry 
surface water off the pavement quickly to appropriate drainage structures. To collect 
infiltrated water into the pavement structural section, we recommend providing a pavement 
edge drain system (hydraulically connected to the untreated permeable base) along the 
edges of the new pavements. 
The text of this pavement justification report should be referred to for detailed 
discussion and specific recommendations for design of pavements.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This pavement justification report presents the results of our analyses performed 
for the design of new pavements for the Waiehu Beach Road (Route 3400), 
Rehabilitation of Iao Steam Bridge project located in the Wailuku area on the Island of 
Maui, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our 
geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the design of pavements 
only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations 
noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The existing Waiehu Beach Road is a two-lane undivided roadway classified as 
“Primary Rural Arterial.”  The existing roadway consists of two 11-foot lanes with 3-foot 
to 5-foot wide shoulders.  The project will entail widening the existing roadway and 
bridge structure to accommodate two 12-foot lanes with 8-foot wide shoulders in each 
travel direction.   
The existing bridge structure is a three-span bridge with a span length of about 
176 feet measured from abutment to abutment.  The bridge was built in circa 1954.  It 
appears that the abutments and the two intermediate piers for the bridge structure are 
supported by spread footings bearing on the dense alluvial soils (cobbles and boulders 
with “hardpacked” sand) present at relatively shallow depths below the stream level.  
Based on the available “as-built” drawings, the bottom of the spread footings extend to 
depths of about 12 to 25 feet below the existing ground surface.   
It should be noted that the grades behind the abutments of the bridge structure 
were raised from the original ground surface by the placement of 10 to 20 feet of 
embankment fills.  It is anticipated that the embankment fills placed behind the existing 
abutment structures may consist of cobbles and boulders similar to the other 
embankment fills in the area.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes, the older West Maui 
(Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala 
(Pleistocene Epoch). The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain located 
between these two volcanoes. 
The older West Maui Volcano generally is comprised of three lava flows. The 
lower unit was formed during the Pliocene to early Pleistocene Epoch and is identified 
as the Wailuku Volcanic Series. The lavas of the Wailuku Volcanic Series generally 
consist of thinly-bedded a’a and pahoehoe lavas. Overlying this unit are lavas of the 
Honolua Volcanic Series (late Pleistocene Epoch) that consist of alkalic lavas and stiff 
trachyte.  
Volcanic inactivity occurred during the Middle Pleistocene Epoch where 
weathering of the lavas, carving of canyons, and deposition of materials occurred. 
During the late Pleistocene Epoch, renewal of volcanic activity occurred near the 
southern coast with lava flows of the Lahaina Volcanic Series. The lavas of the Lahaina 
Volcanic Series generally consist of olivine-rich basalt and nepheline basanite. 
Stream erosion of the West Maui Volcano produced broad alluvial fans on the 
eastern and southwestern sides of the mountain.  The large accumulation of alluvial 
material is partly due to the loss of water from the streams to the permeable lavas of 
Haleakala that built the Maui Isthmus.  These alluvial fans may be more than 900 feet in 
thickness. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, the sea stood about 350 feet lower than the 
present sea level.  During this period, sand was blown inland and extensive lithified 
calcareous dunes were formed on the Isthmus over the alluvial fans.  Near the northern 
coast, some of the dunes are as much as 180 feet high.  The project site is within the 
northern portion of the Isthmus of Maui and east of the West Maui Volcano.

2.2 
Site Description 
The bridge widening project site is along the portion of Waiehu Beach Roadway 
at the Iao Stream crossing in the District of Wailuku on the Island of Maui, Hawaii.  The 
existing bridge is a three-span, concrete bridge structure and is approximately 183 feet 
in length and about 32 feet in width (supporting two lanes of traffic). 
The streambed channel is lined with concrete from the sidewall aprons to the 
channel floor. We did not observe flowing water within Iao Stream at the time of our field 
exploration. 
The existing ground along the bridge approaches and bridge structure generally 
slopes down away from the bridge structure at about level to thirty-five horizontal to 
one vertical (35H:1V) inclination. Steeper slopes are present at Iao Stream where the 
existing ground surface slopes down toward the stream at about 2H:1V inclination. 
Based on the topographic map provided, the elevations of the existing roadway grade 
range from about +31 to +47 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). 
2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
11 borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1, 2, 2A, 3 through 10, extending to depths 
ranging from about 15 to 62 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate 
boring locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 and 2.2. 
The borings drilled along the upper roadway level (Boring Nos. 1, 4, 5, 8, 9, and 
10) generally encountered a surface fill layer about 1 to 10.5 feet thick.  The fill layer 
was underlain by dune sand and alluvial deposits extending to about 62 feet, the 
maximum explored depth. In three of the borings drilled along the upper roadway level, 
dune sand was not encountered below the surface fill layer. 
Within the existing channel, our borings generally encountered a concrete slab 
about 10 to 11 inches thick underlain by a fill layer to about 3 to 6.5 feet deep.  Below 
the fill layer, an alluvial deposit was encountered extending to 62 feet below the existing 
ground surface.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the current design concept and guidelines, we envision that new 
pavements will be constructed for the roadway realignment project. In general, the 
following sections of this report provide discussion on the areas necessary for pavement 
analyses and design. 
3.1 
Methodology of Pavement Design 
Two types of pavement structural sections (flexible and rigid pavements) were 
considered in the pavement analyses. The flexible pavement sections presented herein 
generally were determined based on the methodology described in Chapter 3 of the 
revised Pavement Design Manual dated March 2002. The Pavement Design Manual 
was prepared by the State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation, Highways Division, 
Material Testing and Research Branch. The pavement design methodology is based on 
the Hveem Stabilometer method developed and used by the California Department of 
Transportation (Caltrans). 
The design procedures for the rigid pavement sections are generally based on 
the design procedures described in the Portland Cement Association “Thickness Design 
for Concrete Highway and Street Pavements.”

Rural” roadway. Therefore, pavements will need to be designed for a pavement life of 
30 years. Design traffic parameters were provided by the State of Hawaii - Department 
of Transportation, Highways Division. The following table summarizes the design traffic 
parameters used in our pavement analyses.

3.3 
Design Subgrade Conditions 
We envision the majority of the roadway along Waiehu Beach Road generally will 
be constructed in a cut condition. Based on the field exploration, we anticipate the 
majority of the pavement subgrade soils will generally consist of loose to medium dense 
sands, and medium dense silty sand and sandy gravel. 
We performed our pavement designs with the assumption that the pavement 
subgrade soils will generally consist of the sand, silty sand, and sandy gravel with a 
minimum laboratory Resistance (R) value of about 55. Therefore, an R-value of 55 has 
been adopted for the subgrade materials in our pavement design analyses. If site 
grading exposes soils other than those assumed in the pavement design, additional 
tests should be performed to confirm and/or revise the recommended pavement section 
for actual field conditions.

3.4 
Design Pavement Section 
Due to the nature of the pavement subgrade materials anticipated, we 
recommend incorporating a permeable drainage layer into the pavement design. 
Therefore, we have only considered pavement structural sections with a permeable 
drainage layer in our analyses. Details of the drainage analysis used for this project are 
presented in the attached Appendix C. Based on the relatively high traffic volumes 
anticipated for Waiehu Beach Road, we believe that asphalt concrete base (ACB) 
should be used as the base material for the project. Therefore, we did not consider 
pavement sections utilizing untreated aggregate base courses in our pavement design 
analyses.

An economic analysis was performed on the four pavement structural sections 
presented above to evaluate the initial construction cost and life cycle cost of the 
pavement sections. Based on our cost comparisons of the pavement design options, it 
appears that the Option C pavement structural section would result in the most 
economical pavement design over the design life of the pavement. The economic 
analysis results are presented in Appendix C. Although Option C pavement section was 
the most economical, we recommend using Option A pavement structural section for 
the project for the following reasons.  The time required before traffic can be placed on 
the new pavement is significantly longer for the concrete pavement as compared to the 
asphaltic concrete pavement.  Since the project will be constructed in phases and 
construction of a detour roadway may not be considered, the length of time of the traffic 
disruption will be extended significantly with a concrete pavement. 
3.5 
Subgrade Preparation Below Pavement Section 
We anticipate the majority of the new roadway pavements will be in a cut 
condition. In general, the area within the contract grading limits should be cleared and 
grubbed thoroughly at the on-set of earthwork. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, 
and other unsuitable materials should be removed and properly disposed off-site or 
disposed in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the 
excavated materials.  
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over-excavated to expose 
stiff and/or dense materials. The resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
aggregate subbase materials. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be 
properly disposed off-site. 
After clearing and grubbing, the future pavement areas should be excavated, 
where necessary, to the pavement subgrade level (i.e., bottom of the aggregate

3.6 
Fill Materials 
In general, the excavated on-site materials with particles no larger than 3 inches 
may be re-used as a source of general fill material. Imported fill materials, if required, 
should consist of non-expansive select granular material, such as crushed coralline or 
basaltic materials. The materials should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles 
no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 
30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The materials should have a laboratory 
CBR value of 20 or higher and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less. 
Imported fill materials should be tested for conformance with these recommendations 
prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use. 
3.7

optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Aggregate

3.8 
Pavement Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the base, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. It should be noted that the 
silty subgrade soils are susceptible to softening and loss of strength upon saturation. 
Therefore, special attention should be given to the surface and subsurface drainage of 
the pavements. As a minimum, the pavement surface should be sloped and drainage 
gradients should be maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate 
drainage structures. Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or

recommend providing a pavement edge drain system hydraulically connected to the

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based, in part, 
upon information obtained from literature research and our field exploration. Variations 
of subsurface conditions between and beyond the field exploration points may occur, 
and the nature and extent of these variations may be not become evident until 
construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to 
re-evaluate the recommendations provided herein. 
The boring locations indicated herein are approximate, having been taped from 
features shown on the topographic survey map transmitted by Wilson Okamoto 
Corporation on January 31, 2008. Elevations of the borings were estimated based on 
interpolation between the spot elevations and contour lines shown on the same plan. 
The physical locations and elevations of the borings should be considered accurate only 
to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict 
the approximate boundaries between the soil and/or rock types and, as such, may 
denote a gradual transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the 
times shown on the graphic representations and/or presented in the text of this report. 
These data have been reviewed and interpretations made to formulate this report. 
However, it must be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in tides, rainfall, 
temperature, and other factors.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
eleven borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1, 2, 2A, 3 through 10, advanced to 
maximum depths of about 15 to 62 feet below the existing ground surface.  The borings 
were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with auger and coring equipment.  
The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plates 2.1 and 2.2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field and by monitoring the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis.  Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified 
Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A.  Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-11. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings in general 
accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches.  In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop.  The blow counts 
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Core samples of boulder and cobble materials encountered at the site were 
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2113, Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation.  Core drilling is a rotary 
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the boulders and cobbles.  The material 
left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and 
description. 
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses.  Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered 
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt.  For example, if 3.7 feet of 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) 
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification 
and evaluation of soil properties.  The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings 
at the appropriate sample depths. 
One Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D 4318) was performed on a selected soil 
sample to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth. The 
test results are provided on Plate B-1. 
Six Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 136) were performed on selected soil samples to 
evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are provided on Plates B-2 and B-3. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D 3080) was performed on a selected soil sample 
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics. The test results are presented on 
Plate B-4. 
Four Unconfined Compression tests (ASTM D 2938) were performed on rock 
core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the underlying 
boulders.  The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depth. 
One laboratory Resistance (R) Value test (AASHTO T 190) was performed on a 
selected bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the soils.  The test results are presented on Plate B-5.
