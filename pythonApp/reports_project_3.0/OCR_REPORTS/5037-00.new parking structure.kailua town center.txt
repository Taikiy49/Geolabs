2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield volcano Koolau Volcano forms
the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae Volcano
became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank is partially
buried below the Koolau lavas in Central Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch seal levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to
fill the oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea
When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulated reef
deposits at correspondingly higher elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine
sediments in the quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused
streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef deposits The project site is situated
adjacent to Kawainui Marsh where the subsurface soil deposits are dominated by
lagoonal deposits and coralline soil deposits Land development and reclamation
projects within the last century have brought the project area to its present form

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by shifts
in the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before
or during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the

erupt The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but
most of the earthquakes are so small that they may only be detected by

a few cause minor to moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of

Soil Profile
Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered surface fills extending
to depths of about 2 to 3 feet below the existing ground surface The surface fills
were generally underlain by loose to medium dense beach deposits and very loose
and very soft lagoonal deposits extending to depths of about 25 to 26 feet below
the existing ground surface Below the beach and lagoonal deposits our field
exploration generally encountered medium dense coralline detritus with occasional
zones of medium hard coral extending to depths of about 60 to 70 feet below the
existing ground surface Alluvium consisting of soft to stiff silty clays was
encountered below the coralline detritus and extended to the maximum depths
explored
Because of the generally poor subsurface soil conditions at the site it was desired
to perform shear wave velocity profiling using seismic piezocone SCPTV testing
equipment at the site in an effort to more closely analyze the subsurface conditions
for seismic design considerations at the site The CPT method involves inserting a
sensitive probe into the soil at a constant speed Various sensors embedded in the
probe continuously measure the tip resistance side friction and induced pore
pressures as the probe is systematically pushed through the soil In general
subsurface explorations consisting of CPT yields more accurate results for highly
sensitive materials such as the soft and loose soils because the data is obtained
directly from the in situ soils Therefore CPT with seismic shear wave velocity
profiling is considered to be the industry standard for higher order analysis such as
detailed seismic analyses especially in these subsurface conditions
Shear wave velocity tests and piezocone penetration tests were performed to
depths of about 23 107 and 111 feet below the ground surface using seismic
piezocone SCPTU testing equipment at the approximate locations shown on
Site Plan Plate 2 Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field
exploration the average shear wave velocity for the top 100 feet of the soil profile
ranged from about 950 to 1 060 feet per second

Liquefaction Potential
Based on the 1997 UBC the project site may be subjected to seismic activity and
should be evaluated for the potential for soil liquefaction Soil liquefaction is a
condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the ground surface
undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess pore water
pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes In this
process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration such as
during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an
increase in pore water pressure If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to
dissipate the build up of pore water pressure the effective stress internal strength
of the soil is reduced
Under sustained vibrations the pore water pressure build up could equal the
overburden pressure essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and
causing it to behave as a viscous fluid During liquefaction the soil acquires a
mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal and vertical movements and if not
confined will result in significant deformations
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion It is generally acknowledged that
liquefaction will not occur if the deposit is greater than 40 to 50 feet below the
ground surface In deeper deposits the greater overburden pressure is generally
sufficient to prevent liquefaction from occurring The major factors affecting the
liquefaction characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows

1 Grain Size Distribution Fine and uniform sands and silts are more
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2 Initial Relative Density Loose sands and silts are most susceptible to

3 Magnitude_and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is directly

Predrilling
Based on our field exploration medium dense coralline detritus with zones of
medium hard coral were encountered at depths of about 25 feet below the existing
ground surface and extended to depths of about 60 to 70 feet below the existing
ground surface The zones of medium hard coral may be an obstruction to the
advancement of the pile during the pile driving operations Therefore we
recommend that each pile location be predrilled to facilitate the pile driving
operations through the coralline detritus layer encountered at the project site As a
minimum the depth of the predrilling at each pile location should be extended to
depths on the order of about 40 feet below the ground surface The recommended
predrilling depths should be confirmed and or modified by Geolabs during the test
pile program depending on the subsurface conditions encountered and the pile
driving equipment proposed by the contractor
Because a substantial portion of the compressive load capacity is derived from the
coralline detritus layer limiting the size of the predrilled hole for each pile location
will be an important design consideration Therefore we wish to emphasize that
the diameter of the predrilled holes should be limited to approximately 18 inches to
provide the driven piles with sufficient soil contact for vertical and lateral load
resistance If an over sized predrilled hole is encountered during construction the
annular space between piles and predrilled holes should be grouted with tremie
concrete immediately prior to pile driving to provide the recommended vertical and
lateral load resistance

Lateral loads imposed on the parking structure may be resisted by a combination of
the lateral load capacity of the driven piles and the passive pressure acting against

Pile Hammer Selection
As with most sites underlain by coral ledges and coralline detritus selection of the
pile hammer for use in driving the pile foundations will be of critical consideration
In general we recommend that the piles be driven with a hammer capable of
delivering energies in the range of about 40 000 to 50 000 foot pounds in the field
The hammer should be equipped with an energy level control such that driving
within the soft and or loose soils may be controlled at a lower energy to reduce the
potential for tensile stress development which could damage or cause cracking of
the piles
Selection of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the matching of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be
driven This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy
to the top of the pile and also reducing the potential for damage to the piles
Geolabs should review the pile driving equipment proposed to evaluate whether the
equipment is capable of installing the piles to the recommended capacity The
contractor should forward pile and driving equipment data forms to Geolabs prior to
construction for evaluation Geolabs should review the submittal and assess the
driveability of piles with the use of the selected hammer

The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling three borings extending to depths ranging from about 26 5 to 121 5 feet below
the existing ground surface In addition the test borings were supplemented with the
performance of three cone penetrometer test CPT probes which are discussed in

are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted
drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers rotary wash drilling and rotary coring
equipment
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and
Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration
Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
In addition relatively undisturbed samples of the soft and or loose lagoonal
deposits at the site were retrieved by hydraulically pushing a 3 inch OD thin wall Shelby
tube into the soil formation The hydraulic pressures needed to advance the sampler
are recorded as the Penetration Resistance in pounds per square inch psi per foot of
penetration on the Logs of Borings
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the

APPENDIX B
Seismic Piezocone SCPTU Testing
Due to the nature of the subsurface soil conditions at the site seismic shear
wave velocity profiling using Cone Penetrometer Test CPT equipment was performed
at the site in an effort to more closely analyze the seismic design considerations for the
project site Shear wave velocity tests and cone penetration tests were performed at
three probe locations designated as CPT 1 through CPT 3 as shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The probes extended to depths of about 107 111 and 23 feet below the
ground surface using seismic piezocone testing equipment SCPTU
The seismic piezocone penetration test consists of hydraulically pushing a 10 ton
steel electronic subtraction cone manufactured by with an apex
angle of 60 degrees and an area of 1 55 square inches 10 square centimeters
connected to a friction sleeve with an area of 23 25 square inches 150 square
centimeters into the ground The tip and side friction resistance and pore pressure
induced immediately behind the cone tip designated as the Us parameter during
penetration are measured by strain gages within the instrument and are recorded versus
the depth of penetration in about 2 inch 5 centimeter intervals When the material
encountered was too hard for further penetration by the CPT equipment which might
cause potential equipment damage the cone was withdrawn Subsequently the hole
was advanced by using NQ coring techniques to a depth of about 10 feet below the
bottom of the previous cone sounding The cone was then returned to the new hole
bottom for continued sounding
The cone carries a uniaxial horizontal geophone to detect the arrival of a shear
wave propagated from the ground surface The seismic measurements are made when
the SCPTU has stopped and a shear wave is sent into the subsurface A shear wave is
generated at the surface by striking a loaded plank with a switched hammer The
propagation time of the wave from the hammer blow to the cone was measured at each
depth interval The vector difference of these depths divided by the time difference
provided the average shear wave velocity over the depth interval
Logs of the seismic cone penetration tests are presented on Plates B 1 through
B 3 in Appendix B In general the plots include cone tip resistance friction ratio sleeve
friction over tip resistance pore pressure differential pore pressure ratio shear wave
velocity soil behavior based on the R zone and interpreted soil description The values
of the tip resistance friction ratio and pore pressure may be correlated to soil strength
and classification The center of the friction sleeve is approximately 4 inches 10 cm
behind the cone tip To calculate the friction ratio the average friction resistance and tip
resistance are compared at the same depth This involves an offset of the friction
resistance by the physical distance of 4 inches

Differential pore pressure ratio is calculated as a ratio of excess pore pressure to cone tip
