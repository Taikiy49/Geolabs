Preliminary Findings and Recommendations
Hillside Stabilization
Pu ohala Elementary School
Kaneohe Oahu Hawaii
In accordance with our fee proposal dated we have completed
the field exploration work pertaining to the hillside stabilization project located within the
existing Pu ohala Elementary School This letter summarizes the work performed thus far
on the project and presents our preliminary findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations derived from our analyses for the subject project
Project Considerations
The project site is located within the existing Pu ohala Elementary School at
Street in the Kaneohe area on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general
location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
Based on the information provided we understand that a portion of the hillside
fronting Pu ohala Elementary School on the southwest side of the main parking lot slipped
after heavy rains which occurred on The landslide has resulted in a
hillside that is bare and stands at a near vertical slope inclination The top and toe areas
of the hillside have an elevation difference of up to about 35 feet high

Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths of about
49 and 60 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the
borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on our field exploration at Boring No 1 the existing hillside is generally
underlain by highly weathered basalt materials saprolite consisting of stiff to very stiff
silty clays with friable fragments extending to a depth of about 59 feet below the existing
ground surface Below this depth our boring encountered refusal conditions which we
believe may be the underlying basalt rock formation
The subsurface conditions on the lower side of the hillside was explored by drilling
Boring No 2 located at the edge of the existing driveway within the school campus Our
boring generally encountered fill materials consisting of excavated saprolite about 30 feet
thick overlying boulder fills about 13 feet thick extending to a depth of about 43 feet
below the existing ground surface The fills were underlain by soft lagoonal deposits
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 59 feet below the existing ground
surface Based on our review of the geology in this area we believe that the school site
was previously a swamp or pond area which was filled up to the present elevations This
appears to be consistent with the subsurface conditions encountered in our boring We
envision that the boulder fill was used to stabilize the underlying soft lagoonal deposits
original in situ soils prior to capping with the excavated saprolite materials from a nearby
source as fill materials
Groundwater was encountered at depths of about 6 and 45 feet below the existing
ground surface at the time of our field exploration These depths generally correspond to
elevations of approximately 2 and 6 feet Mean Sea Level MSL Due to the proximity
of the project site to Kaneohe Bay it is likely that the water levels measured in the borings
during our field exploration were affected by tidal influences In addition the water levels
at the project site may be influenced by seasonal precipitation and storm surge
conditions

Soil Nails and Tied Back Wall Systems
Soil nails and tied back wall systems were also considered as remedial measures
to stabilize the existing hillside However due to the need for permanent easement
acquisitions and potential construction difficulties these options were not further pursued
at this time
Keystone Wall
A preliminary evaluation has been performed by Trans Pacific Land Corporation
TPLC on the Keystone Wall option as a remedial measure to stabilize the existing
hillside Based on the preliminary design presented in their engineering report it appears
that substantial excavation into the existing hillside slope will be required during
construction Therefore shoring and stability of the temporary excavation would be of
critical concern during construction and may increase the construction cost substantially
In addition further excavation into the existing hillside may cause additional disturbance
which results in increased risk of potential slope failures
We understand that the preliminary design performed by TPLC was based on

Concrete Retaining Wall
It is our opinion that conventional concrete retaining wall may be used as a
remedial measure to stabilize the existing hillside The following general guidelines may
be used for preliminary design of the retaining wall structure at the subject site
Wall Foundations
In general we believe that retaining wall foundations may be designed with an
allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 500 pounds per square foot psf bearing on
2 feet of select borrow subbase material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction Wall foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded
a minimum depth of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by
passive earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation
system A coefficient of friction of 0 45 may be used for footings bearing on the
compacted select borrow subbase material Resistance due to passive earth
pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per
square foot per foot of depth p c f This assumes that the soil around footings is
well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in
the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
Lateral Earth Pressures
We understand that a 2H 1V slope will be used behind the retaining wall structure
All retaining structures should be designed to resist the lateral earth pressures due
to adjacent soils and surcharge effects The recommended lateral earth pressures
for design of retaining walls expressed in equivalent fluid pressures are presented
in the following table

Backfill behind the retaining wall should consist of imported granular fill materials
compacted to between 90 and 95 percent relative compaction Over compaction
of the retaining wall backfill should be avoided The granular backfill should extend
from the back face of the wall to a imaginary plane extending upward
from the heel of the wall footing In addition a layer of filter fabric such as
Mirafi 180N or equivalent should be provided between the granular backfill and
the adjacent soils to reduce the potential for contamination of the backfill material
The lateral earth pressures provided do not include hydrostatic pressures that
might be caused by groundwater trapped behind the walls
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges line loads and point loads within a
horizontal distance equal to the depth of the wall should be considered in the
design For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the wall a
rectangular distribution with uniform pressure equal to 27 percent of the vertical
surcharge pressure acting on the entire height of the wall may be used in the
design for walls that are free to deflect Additional analyses during design may be
needed to evaluate the surcharge effects of point loads and line loads
Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the wall
discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used
instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also
be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall Unless
covered by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of
non expansive impervious material to reduce water infiltration behind the walls
