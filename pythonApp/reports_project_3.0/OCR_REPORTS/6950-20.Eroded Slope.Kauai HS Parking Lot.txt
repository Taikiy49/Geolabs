As requested, a site visit was made on May 21, 2015 to observe the conditions of 
the eroded slope and its proximity to the future Kauai High School parking lot planned 
along Lala Place. We understand significant erosion has occurred along a portion of the 
existing slope resulting in near-vertical cut face near the top of slope. Prior to our arrival on 
site, the northeast and northwest corners of the future parking lot curbs were staked-out 
for reference. 
Existing Site Conditions 
Based on the topographic map provided in the project plans, the existing slope 
ranges from about 40 to 50 feet in height with an average slope inclination of about one 
horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V). Existing ground surface elevations range from about 
+117 to +118 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) at the top of the slope to about +71 to +68 feet 
MSL at the base of the slope along Lala Road. 
At the time of our site visit, the existing slope had light to medium vegetative cover 
generally consisting of small trees and brush. Relatively large trees were observed along 
the top of the existing slope and the roots of these trees have been exposed from the 
apparent erosion.  
Based on our field observations, the most significant portion of the eroded slope is 
about 55 feet along the top of slope wide and occurs just below the northeastern edge of 
the future parking lot. The general location of the eroded section of the slope is shown on 
the attached Site Plan, Plate 1. 
Portions of the slope appeared to have collapsed due to the slope erosion resulting 
in near-vertical cut faces and under-cuts in some sections with maximum depths of about 
13 feet measured from the top of slope. Based on the curb layout in the field, it appears 
that the top of these cuts are about 8 feet away from the future parking lot curb line. The 
subsurface materials exposed from these cuts appear to be residual/saprolitic soils 
consisting of reddish brown clayey silt, which was observed to be dry and cracking in 
numerous locations. Photographs from our site visit are presented on the following page.

Discussions and Recommendations 
Based on the information provided and our field observations, we believe the 
existing slope may not be stable from a long-term standpoint. If the planned parking lot is 
constructed in its current location and configuration, additional erosion could occur as time 
passes resulting in possible undermining and subsequent distress to the new pavements. 
As a minimum, we recommend removing the trees at the top of slope to reduce the 
levering effect of the large trees on the top of the slope. We understand it is desired to 
expedite remedial measures for this issue because the Notice-to-Proceed for construction 
of this project is set for June 1, 2015. Therefore, we are providing the following options to 
resolve this issue for consideration.  
Option No. 1: Reconfigure Parking Lot 
In general, we believe the northern curb line should have a minimum setback of at 
least 13 feet from the top of the eroded portion of the slope. This would involve shifting the 
curb line about 5 feet south of its current position. Based on the information provided, the 
length of each parking stall is about 19 feet and the width of the drive aisle is about 26 feet. 
This could possibly be accomplished by reducing the length of parking stalls and width of 
the drive aisle, if appropriate (to be determined by the Civil Engineer).  
Option No. 2: Reconfigure Retention Basin and Shift Entire Parking Lot South 
We understand the slope inclinations for the retention basin range from about 
3H:1V to 4H:1V. If all the slope inclinations are steepened to 3H:1V and the size of the 
basin is reduced to match the original volume, the resulting net gain in lateral distance 
would be about 7 feet. Therefore, the parking lot could subsequently be shifted south by 
about 7 feet.  
Option No. 3: Reduce the Number of Parking Stalls 
Based on our field observations, we believe at least two stalls on the northeast 
portion of the parking lot would need to be eliminated for constructability of the parking lot. 
However, to reduce the potential for future pavement distress from additional erosion of 
the slope, we would recommend eliminating at least six stalls along the northeast portion 
of the parking lot. 
Option No. 4: Repairing the Eroded Slope 
We do not consider repairing the eroded slope a viable option due to the associated 
cost implications and the time it would take for designing and constructing the repair. In 
general, repairing the eroded portion of the slope would consist of removing any loose 
soils on the slope and placing Grouted Rubble Paving (GRP) from the bottom of the 
eroded area up to the top of slope to match the existing slope inclination. It should be
