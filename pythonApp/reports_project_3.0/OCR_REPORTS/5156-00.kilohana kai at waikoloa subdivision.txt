2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an
area of approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five
shield volcanoes which include the following Kohala which is long extinct Mauna
Kea which has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in
1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of Mauna Kea which has
formed the west central portion of the island The project site appears to be underlain
by basaltic lava flows of the Hamakua Volcanic Series of Mauna Kea which were
deposited during the Pleistocene Epoch Stearns and Macdonald 1946
The Hamakua Volcanic Series is typically composed of basaltic lava flows which
are mantled by a relatively thin layer of palagonitized ash locally referred to as Pahala
Ash The ash soil at the project site differs from Pahala Ash found in other portions of
the Island of Hawaii in that the in situ moisture content is generally lower and the soil
exists in a dry friable state The soils at the site have been subjected to wind erosion
which has resulted in the formation of a desert pavement on the surface of the site
This desert pavement is a surficial layer of cobbles and gravel which were left behind
as the finer ash was blown away
The lava formation at the site appears to be of a a and pahoehoe basalt type
flows which spread and ponded as they approached the ocean A a lava is typically
characterized by a porous rough and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged
accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders Typically a more
dense and layered lava rock material is contained within the lava flow core Pahoehoe
lava is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure
of vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lavas
Cavities are formed when the lavas are still in a molten state and represent both lava
tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes are

2 4 Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by
shifts in the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily
to volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or
during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The Island of
Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most of the earthquakes
are so small that only instruments can detect the minor earthquakes However some of
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to moderate
damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on
the Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the
Island of Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii over 90 percent of
earthquakes are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic activity and degree of
ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the southern portion of the
Island of Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater
than Magnitude 6 0 Based on information obtained from the United States Geological

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris deleterious materials and
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to
reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials to be used as
embankment fill materials We wish to point out that caution should be exercised
by the contractor while clearing and grading near collapsed lava tube features
and or cavities which may represent potential lava tubes or cavities that may
further collapse under the load of heavy equipment
After clearing and grubbing we recommend that the fill areas of the project site be
ripped to a depth of about 3 feet below the existing ground surface at the
commencement of grading The purpose of ripping the fill subgrades is to assist in
detecting and collapsing cavities and or voids contained in the near surface
materials As a minimum ripping of the ground surface should extend at least
3 feet laterally beyond the fill areas

Fills and Backfills
In general we recommend that the fill areas be capped with a minimum 2 foot thick
layer of structural fill material to serve as a choking layer for the larger sized
general fill materials below Structural fill materials should consist of well graded
granular materials generally less than 6 inches in maximum dimension with
sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass In
general the excavated materials may be re used as a source of structural fill
materials provided that the materials are processed to meet the maximum
particle size and gradation requirements presented above Where appropriate
such as within utility corridors the thickness of the structural fill layer may be
increased to a minimum of 1 foot below the invert elevation of the utilities to
facilitate future construction of the utilities
Fill and backfill materials below the minimum 2 foot thick structural fill layer may
consist of general fill materials General fill and backfill materials should consist of
well graded granular materials the majority of which are less than 12 inches in size
with an absolute maximum dimension of 18 inches Materials between 12 and
18 inches in particle size should be limited to less than 15 percent of the total
general fill materials The excavated on site materials if less than 18 inches in
maximum dimension may be used as a source of general fill or backfill materials
except for trench backfill provided that they are processed to meet the above
gradation requirements for general fill If the excavated materials do not contain
sufficient fines to produce a well graded material off site borrow or on site rock
crushing of larger rock fragments or boulders should be considered to provide the
required gradation and particle sizes to develop a well graded material
Where over cutting of the finished cut subgrades occur during the mass grading
operations backfilling of the over cut area should be treated as fill areas
Therefore the above recommendations pertaining to fill materials should apply to
the over cut areas and the fill materials required for backfilling will depend on the
depth of over cut below the finished subgrade

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in
loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction General fills and backfills
should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 18inches in loose thickness
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers
vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment
Rock fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of the
majority of the rocks or 5 feet whichever is less Placement should be done by
haul trucks traversing previously placed lifts and dumping at the edge of the
currently placed lift Spreading of the rock fill should be done by a large bulldozer
to facilitate seating of the rocks The rock fill should be watered heavily during
placement by water trucks traversing in front of the current rock lift face and
spraying water continuously during placement
Compaction equipment such as a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar heavy
compaction equipment providing suitable energy to achieve the required
compaction should be utilized The number of passes usually six to eight passes
to be made to achieve proper compaction should be determined at the on set of
site grading by the performance of a trial compaction test or by plate bearing tests
Subsequent layers of rock fill above the previously placed rock fill should provide a
choking layer of smaller sized boulder materials to fill in the voids between the
boulders of the preceding rock fill layer

The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling nine borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 9 extending to depths of
about 18 to 31 5 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using

The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural

materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 9
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the site were obtained by
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material
left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and
description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD also is a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
One Laboratory California Bearing Ratio test ASTM D 1883 was performed on
a bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The sample was remolded to near the optimum moisture content Results
of the test are presented on Plate B 1
One Modified Proctor Compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The results of the test are presented on Plate B 2

APPENDIX C
Percolation Testing
As part of our field exploration program we conducted four percolation tests in
the drilled borings using the constant head method In general the drilled borings had a
diameter of about 3 5 inches and extended to depths of about 15 8 to 23 9 feet below
the ground level at the time of the testing A water truck was used to pump clear water
into the boreholes for the constant head test method The flow rate was recorded by
timing and calculating the volume as a relatively constant flow rate was achieved
The results of the percolation tests conducted are presented on Plates C 1 through C 4
