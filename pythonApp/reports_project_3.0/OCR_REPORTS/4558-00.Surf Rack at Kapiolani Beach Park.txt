REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the western
third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern two thirds
of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active and its
eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in Central Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of the Koolau Shield The
geomorphology and subsurface conditions at the site are directly related to glacio eustatic
fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu
Coastal Plain The subsurface conditions in the Waikiki area are dominated by fossil
Pleistocene Age coral algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of the
sea which are intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic section
in this area is Koolau Basalt
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes as
a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world These glacio eustatic
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower relative to the
present sea level of Oahu About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level
occurred During that rise the valleys in the project area were drowned In the last 10 000
years or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine
sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments
resulting in thick deposits of soft loose sediments along the coast in the areas that were
formerly valleys and drainageways
In the early part of this century the Waikiki area consisted of low marshy wetlands As
the city of Honolulu grew and the Waikiki area was urbanized man made fills were placed to

Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have brought the Waikiki

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The project site is generally underlain by topsoil consisting of stiff clayey silts The
topsoil is underlain by fills consisting of stiff to very stiff sandy silts and medium dense silty
sands The fills are underlain by beach sand deposits consisting of medium dense to dense
silty coralline sand and gravel The beach sand deposits are underlain by lagoonal deposits

Surf Rack Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe that a
concrete slab on grade with thickened edge footings may be used to support the proposed
surf rack Our field exploration encountered clayey silts and fine beach sand at shallow
depths Therefore to provide for uniform support and to reduce the potential for undermining
the slab on grade foundation materials we recommend that a 6 inch thick layer of aggregate
subbase be provided below the concrete slab on grade The aggregate subbase should also
extend a minimum 12 inches laterally beyond the limits of the slab The aggregate subbase
material should be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test
procedures
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot psf may be used
for the design of the thickened edge footings bearing on the aggregate subbase material This
bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient
loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces The thickened edge footings should
be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade In addition
footings should have a minimum width of 12 inches Areas adjacent to the slab should be
backfilled tightly against the slab with the on site soils and graded to divert water away from
the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the slabs
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements the foundation should
be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of
the utility trench or should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines
This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our recommendations
we estimate total settlements of the foundations bearing on the aggregate subbase materials
to be less than 1 inch with differential settlements on the order of about 0 5 inches

If the actual loading of the light pole is higher than the assumed structural loads the drilled

The cast in place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally from
skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding soils An allowable compressive load
capacity of up to 6 kips per shaft may be used for dead plus live loads and may be increased
by 50 percent for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces The anticipated settlements
of the light pole foundations due to compression loads are estimated to be on the order of less
than 0 25 inches We believe that the settlements are elastic and should occur as the loads
are applied
Lateral loads imposed on the light pole foundations may be resisted by resistance of
the shaft soil system to deflect Lateral load resistance of the drilled shaft is a function of the
stiffness of the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable deflection at the top of
shaft and induced moment in the shaft As indicated above each drilled shaft may be
subjected to a lateral load of 1 000 pounds with an associated overturning moment of up to
about 10 000 foot pounds at the ground surface Based on our foundation analyses the
maximum moment induced in the drilled shaft increases slightly to about 10 800 foot pounds
acting at a depth of about 1 5 feet below the ground surface Our lateral load analyses
indicated that the estimated deflection at the top of the drilled shaft should be less than
0 5 inches

Site Preparation
We anticipate that the site grading work for the proposed surf rack project will consist
of minor cuts and fills on the order of about 1 foot to achieve the design finished grades In
general the area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared at the on set
of earthwork Unsuitable materials within the grading limits should be removed and disposed
of properly off site Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing should be
over excavated to expose firm or dense material and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with well compacted fill The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly
disposed of off site Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for
over excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variation in the over excavation
quantities
Existing underground utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the resulting
excavation should be properly backfilled with on site fill materials moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content placed in 6 to 8 inch loose lifts and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Utilities to be abandoned in place under the
proposed surf rack should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete under low pressure
After clearing the subgrade soils beneath the slab on grade foundation should be
scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The underlying
subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist until the placement of the 6 inch thick
aggregate subbase layer Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the
subgrade we recommend that the soils be thoroughly moistened to close all cracks or
prepared again Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to
inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and
replacement with well compacted fill
The 6 inch thick layer of aggregate subbase below the slab on grade with
thickened edge footings should be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with

ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density

Drainage
The finished grades around the surf rack should be sloped to shed water away from
the foundations and slabs to reduce the potential for ponding The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration into the subsurface Excessive
landscape watering near the foundation areas should also be avoided Planters next to
foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential
for significant water infiltration into the subsurface
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be properly
backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce
the potential for significant water infiltration In addition drainage swales should be provided
as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the
foundations and slab areas

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths of about 20 5 feet below
the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight hollow stem augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as
the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
