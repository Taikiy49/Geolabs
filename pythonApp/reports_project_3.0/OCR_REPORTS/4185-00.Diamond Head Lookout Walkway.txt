INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering evaluation performed
for the proposed walkway at the Diamond Head Lookout located in the Honolulu District on
the Island of Oahu Hawaii The site is located at the top of Diamond Head at the southwest
end of the crater and at the end of the Diamond Head Trail The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses These
recommendations are intended to assist in the design of the walkway foundations slabs and
guardrails only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to construct a walkway between the fire control building and the scenic
lookout structure The walkway is proposed to be about 5 to 6 feet in width and about
120 feet in length At this time it is proposed to construct an on grade concrete walkway
with steel railing on the interior side and simulated rock at the exterior side of the crater
located at the crater rim
It is our understanding that a low tech utilizing simple construction techniques
solution is desired so that incremental construction is possible Also with a low tech
solution the construction work will involve little disturbance to the operation of the scenic
lookout
In addition guardrails are planned for some of the lookout areas It is our
understanding that the guardrails will be 3 5 feet above grade and supported on posts
spaced about 8 feet apart

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while
Kooiau was stili active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4185 00 Page 4
against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad plateau referred
to as the Schofield Plateau
Subsequent to the formation of the Waianae and Koolau Ranges more than
30 separate late stage eruptions occurred at the southeastern end of the Koolau Range
These late stage eruptions created cinder spatter and ash cones referred to as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series Some of the well known landmarks such as Diamond Head Koko
Head Punchbowl and Tantalus were created in these late stage eruptions In some areas
with appropriate minerals the ash material cemented into a rock like material commonly
referred to as volcanic tuff
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The proposed project is located at the Diamond Head lookout located in the Honolulu
District on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The site is located at the top of Diamond Head at
the southwestern end of the crater and at the end of the Diamond Head Trail More
specifically the project site is located between the fire control building and the scenic lookout
structure on or near the crater rim
The project site contains three main structures These include the fire control building
a scenic lookout structure and an old underground military bunker The military bunker is
located southeast of the project area The fire control building also contains underground
military bunker structures and pipe railings All of three structures consist of reinforced
concrete Currently pipe railings and stairs with railings are located along the trail up to the
fire control building
The interior slope of the crater slopes at an average slope inclination of about
to 2H 1V The exterior slope of the crater slopes at an average slope inclination of about
1H 1V to 17 H 1V The ground elevations at the crater rim range from about 757 feet mean
sea level MSL at the fire control building to 740 feet MSL at the lowest point between the
fire control building and the scenic lookout structure and finally to 745 feet MSL at the

The project site contains brown dry brush and weeds throughout the majority of the

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the results of our exploration we believe that the proposed walkway
between the fire control building and the scenic lookout structure may be supported on
shallow foundations
Guardrail posts should be embedded a minimum of 5 feet into holes with a minimum
diameter of 6 inches The holes should be in filled with concrete
Based on the results of our exploration we recommend a slab on grade walkway
with a 1 foot wide 2 foot deep continuous footing This footing should be located near the
exterior side of the crater with a minimum set back distance of 3 feet measured from the
bottom edge of the footing to the slope face Since much of the flat area at the crater rim
is only about 3 to 4 feet cutting of the existing grade will likely be necessary to achieve a
flat area equal in width to the proposed walkway We anticipate cutting on the order of
about 1 to 3 feet
The above design is based on the assumption that adequate monitoring of the slope
erosion will be implemented The design is also based on the assumption that adequate
maintenance or remedial construction measures will be implemented if slope erosion of
more than 12 inches in either the vertical or horizontal direction at either side of the
walkway occurs beneath the walkway structure
We have considered the use of intermittent footings in lieu of a continuous footing
however either the spacing would have to be close or the footings would have to be deep
We believe that this would be more difficult to construct

Construction Considerations
The performance of piers will depend significantly upon the contractor s method of
construction construction procedures and workmanship As a result we recommend
that a representative from Geolabs Hawaii be present to observe the installation of
piers during construction In our opinion the following may have a significant impact
on the effectiveness and cost of the pier foundations
Difficult drilling conditions may be encountered and should be expected during
installation of the piers due to the presence of very dense volcanic tuff material and
possibly zones of loose material within the volcanic tuff
The load carrying capacity of piers depends to a large extent on the contact between
the pier and the surrounding soils Therefore proper construction techniques are
important The contractor should exercise care in drilling the pier holes and in placing
concrete in the holes The bottom of all piers should be cleaned of disturbed material
prior to constructing the foundation The bottom should also be fairly level Backfill
against the pier foundations should not be allowed

Groundwater conditions are not anticipated therefore placement of concrete by dry
construction methods is adequate If a column of water of 12 inches or greater is
observed the water should either be removed or placement of concrete by wet
construction methods using a tremie pipe will be required A low shrink concrete mix
with high slump 6 to 9 inch slump range should be used to provide close contact
between the piers and the surrounding soils The concrete should be placed in a
suitable manner to reduce the potential for segregation of the aggregates from the
concrete mix and to displace disturbed material from the bottom of the drilled shaft

information obtained from the field probes Variations of conditions between and beyond the
field probes may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not become
evident until construction is underway If variations then appear evident it will be necessary

The elevations of the probes were estimated from this map The locations and elevations

used

between soil rock types and as such may denote a gradual transition Water level data from
the probes were measured at the times shown on the graphic representations and or
presented in the text of this report These data have been reviewed and interpretations
made in the formulation of this report However it must be noted that fluctuation may occur
due to variation in seasonal rainfall and other factors
