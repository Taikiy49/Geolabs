2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Koolau
Volcano reached the end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago
The Waianae volcano became extinct while Koolau remained active therefore the eastern
flank of Waianae was partially buried beneath Koolau lavas as they coalesced and banked
against the Waianae Range
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels rose and fell in fluctuating
response to the cycles of worldwide continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and
advanced less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels
responded by falling below the present stands of the sea When the glaciers melted and
receded an excess of water became available such that the sea levels responded by
rising to stands that were above the present sea level
During the evolution and erosion of Oahu river deltas and alluvial fans composed
of accumulations of terrigenous sediments were formed During elevated stands of the
sea these sedimentary accumulations were deposited at the elevated interfaces of historic
shorelines and bays In addition the high sea level stands created accumulations of coral
reef deposits at correspondingly high elevations and also provided for the deposition of
shallow lagoonal and marine sediments in newly formed quiet water environments
protected by outlying fringing reefs In contrast the lower sea level stands caused
streams to carve channels and valleys in the aforementioned sediments and reef deposits
Furthermore subaerial exposure of the marine sediments and calcareous materials
caused consolidation of the soft deltaic and lagoonal materials and induration of the
calcareous reef materials to occur

The processes discussed above have resulted in the occurrence of coral reef and

Likewise terrigenous sediments are also found today buried in off shore marine

environments where they would not otherwise be found Furthermore recent and

3 7 Surface Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due
to saturation of the subbase and base course and or subgrade soils Therefore the
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures
Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction Development
of good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage
structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration
or premature failure of the pavements

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling eight borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 8 extending to depths of about 4 8 to 5 5 feet
below the existing pavement surface The approximate test boring locations are shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous solid stem augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on the Log Legend Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs
of Borings Plates A 1 through A 8
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings in general
accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample
depths
