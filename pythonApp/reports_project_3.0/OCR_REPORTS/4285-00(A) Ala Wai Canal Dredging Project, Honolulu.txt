EVALUATION OF SEAWALLS
ALA WAI CANAL DREDGING PROJECT
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration and
structural evaluation of the existing seawalls adjacent to the Ala Wai Canal in the Waikiki
area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The evaluation of the stability of the
seawalls was conducted in support of the proposed Ala Wai Canal Dredging project currently
in design by Belt Collins Hawaii Ltd The general location and vicinity of the project site are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
Based on the information provided it is desired to develop a set of construction plans
for dredging of the Ala Wai Canal in the Waikiki area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu
Hawaii In general we understand that the principal objectives of the Ala Wai Canal
Dredging project include the following
To beautify the canal
To enhance its recreational use
To restore its capacity for flood flows
To plan for management of silt to reduce impacts on the adjacent coastal
waters in Waikiki
The Ala Wai Canal is a 2 mile long waterway located on the inland border of the
densely urbanized Waikiki area as shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 Most of
the canal length is lined with retaining walls on both sides of the canal The potential dredge
areas include the entire Ala Wai Canal and the Manoa Palolo Drainage Canal from its
confluence with the Ala Wai Canal to the Date Street Bridge As part of the project
development it is desired to evaluate the conditions of the existing seawalls as they might
affect the design and stability of the proposed dredged slopes

SITE DESCRIPTION
The Ala Wai Canal project site is located in the Waikiki area of the Island of Oahu
Hawaii The Ala Wai Canal waterway is approximately 2 miles in length and averages about
250 feet in width The project site encompasses all of the Ala Wai Canal extending from the
bridge overpass at Ala Moana Boulevard to the Public Library located along Kapahulu
Avenue at the eastern end of the canal The lower portion of the Manoa Palolo Drainage
Canal extending southerly from the intersection with Date Street is also included in the
Ala Wai Canal Dredging project The Ala Wai Canal is located at the inland boundary of the
densely urbanized Waikiki area and thus receives influent from storm drains and drainage
ditches in the vicinity The location of the Ala Wai Canal Dredging project site is shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1
Several visits for reconnaissance of the site were made to observe the existing
conditions along the perimeter of the canal At the time of our reconnaissance in 1999 a
topographic survey plan was not available for use as the topographic survey was on going
The Ala Wai Boulevard and concrete sidewalks bound the southern margin of the Ala Wai
Canal The northern margin of the Ala Wai Canal is bounded by residential and commercial
properties at the western end of the canal Public parks owned by the City and County of
Honolulu including the Ala Wai Golf Course border the northern margin at the eastern end
of the project site The perimeter of the Ala Wai Canal is bounded by a low wall consisting

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions along the Ala Wai Canal were explored by drilling and
sampling 16 land borings designated as LB 1 through LB 16 ranging in depths from about
22 8 to 54 5 feet below the existing ground surface Some of the deeper borings were
advanced to the final depth by means of probing with a blunt point of 2 inches in diameter
The borings were drilled approximately 6 to 12 feet from the canal walls on the land side
and were situated on or adjacent to the sidewalk areas bordering the canal The
approximate locations of the 16 test borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In addition the subsurface conditions within the Ala Wai Canal and the Manoa Palolo
Drainage Canal waterways were explored by drilling and sampling eight water borings
designated as WB 1 through WB 8 The depths of the water borings extended to about
23 4 to 28 6 feet below the water surface The borings were drilled with a versa drill rig
placed on a fabricated floating platform raft with rotary wash drilling tools The eight borings
were drilled at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on the land boring data obtained for the project the perimeter of the Ala Wai
Canal appears to be underlain by brown silty sand and clayey silt soils representing fill

STRUCTURAL ASSESSMENT
The structural scope of work is primarily to assess the stability of the wall structures
lining the Ala Wai Canal to determine the design limits of dredging for the project Site
investigations were made to determine the physical conditions and properties of the walls
along the project length The physical survey was conducted by walking along the project
length and visually observing the conditions of the walls Physical measurements were done
by hand and photographs of the wall conditions are provided in Appendix C
As previously mentioned the canal is approximately 2 miles in length Most of the
canal length is lined with retaining walls on both sides of the canal For the purpose of this
report the walls adjacent to Ala Wai Boulevard will be referred to as the Makai walls and
the walls on the opposite side of the canal will be termed the Mauka walls For the
Manoa Palolo Drainage Canal the stream bank adjacent to the existing roadway and bike
path will be referred to as the Ewa bank Similarly the bank adjacent to the Ala Wai Golf
Course will be referred to as the Diamond Head bank

GENERAL FINDINGS
The Type Walls appear to possess an adequate factor of safety for overturning

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
It is apparent that a large portion of the existing retaining walls lining the Ala Wai
Canal has undergone and continues to undergo lateral and vertical movements The only
exception is the Type III Wall located along the mauka side of the canal between Ala Moana
Boulevard and Kalakaua Avenue where the condition of the wall appears to be good In
general the conditions of the existing walls range from fair to collapsed failed The walls
do not appear to exhibit excessive rotation but appear to exhibit distress related to sliding and
settlement It is also apparent that the existing wall distress presented in this report has been
on going for numerous years It is not apparent if the existing distress of the seawalls extend
back to prior to the 1978 dredging project
We understand that it is desired to perform the proposed dredging to the limits similar
to but not extend any further toward the seawalls than the previous dredging limits It
should be noted that the existing seawalls are likely founded on the cemented coralline
rubble coral ledge which appears to have been weakened over time and exposure to the
elements In addition the cemented coralline rubble appears to be relatively thin on the
order of about 2 to 4 feet in thickness Given the above considerations we believe that

Based on the generally poor conditions of these walls we recommend that these
walls be reconstructed with a wider base designed with adequate factors of safety for
overturning and sliding to meet the current standard of practice In addition we also
recommend that the walls be supported on a deep foundation system extending below the
coral ledge and through the soft and or loose materials underlying the existing coral ledge
to bear on more competent soils Based on these conditions and some site constraints we
GEOLABS INC

recommend that jet grouted column foundations be used to support the reconstructed wall
sections at the site
Consideration was given to the use of driven piles and or drilled shafts for support of
the walls However due to the vibrations generated during the pile driving operations we
do not believe that driven pile foundations would be an appropriate foundation system for
support of the walls In addition we believe that drilled shaft foundations would be too costly
to implement for support of the walls Therefore we recommend that jet grouted column
foundations be used to support the reconstructed walls
Jet grouted columns consist of stabilized soil cement columns utilizing a grout mix
delivered at pressure through a special drill bit that has high speed horizontal jets The
high pressure water or water and air jets are used to excavate columns in the materials to
be stabilized The soil cement columns are constructed by withdrawing the rotating drill bit
at a slow constant speed The rotating horizontal water and air jets would cut the soil and
mix the cement grout slurry introduced by pumping the tremie grout through nozzles in the
drill bit simultaneously with the soil cuttings The thoroughly mixed soil cuttings and grout
would then produce continuous columns of consistent geometry and quality The jet grouted
columns are then used as foundations to support the reconstructed walls
For this project we recommend that the jet grouted column foundations consist of
3 foot diameter minimum size jet grouted columns The jet grouted column foundations
should be spaced at about 6 feet center to center In addition we recommend that the
jet grouted columns have a minimum 28 day unconfined compressive strength of
600 pounds per square inch psi Based on these parameters we believe that each
jet grouted column may be designed with an allowable compressive load of 80 kips
In general the jet grouted column foundations should bear on competent foundation
soils below the soft loose soils underlying the coral ledge at the site Therefore we envision

APPENDIXA
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions along the banks of the Ala Wai Canal and the Manoa
Palolo Drainage Canal were explored by drilling and sampling 16 land borings designated
as LB 1 through LB 16 The borings were extended to depths ranging from about 22 8 to
54 5 feet below the existing ground surface Some of the deeper borings were advanced to
the final depth by means of probing with a blunt point of 2 inches in diameter The borings
were drilled approximately 6 to 12 feet from the canal walls on the land side and were
situated on or adjacent to the sidewalk areas bordering the canal The 16 land borings were
drilled at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In addition the subsurface conditions within the Ala Wai Canal and the Manoa Palolo
Drainage Canal waterways were explored by drilling and sampling eight water borings
designated as WB 1 through WB 8 These borings were advanced to depths ranging from
about 23 4 to 28 6 feet below the water surface The borings were drilled with a versa drill
rig placed on a fabricated floating platform raft equipped with rotary wash drilling tools The
eight borings were drilled at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer or geologist who monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered in the land borings are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through
A 16 Graphic representations of the materials encountered in the water borings are
provided on Plates A 17 through A 24
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The
blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Six specific gravity tests ASTM D 854 were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the engineering properties of the dredge materials Results of the tests are
summarized on Plate B 1
Seven organic content tests ASTM D 2974 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the soil characteristics of the dredge materials Results of the tests are also
summarized on Plate B 1
Three Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentations of the test results are
provided on Plate B 2
Thirty five sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 ASTM D 1140 including 18 hydrometer
tests ASTM D 422 were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation
characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification Graphic presentations of the test
results are provided on Plates B 3 through B 9
Five direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples of the soils
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics The results of the direct shear tests are
presented on Plates B 10 through B 14
