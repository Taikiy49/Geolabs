SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the results of our field exploration program it appears that the
proposed Saddle Road East Side MP19 to MP28 alignment predominantly is underlain
by basalt rock materials consisting of relatively young a a and pahoehoe lava flows In
general we believe that the excavated materials may be used as a source of fill
materials for embankment construction provided that the materials are properly
processed and placed in accordance with the recommendations presented in the Site
Grading section of this report
Based on our experience we believe that the basalt clinker materials associated
with the a a lava flows especially those exposed at the existing ground surface may
experience a volume decrease of up to about 20 to 30 percent when excavated
processed and used as fill materials On the other hand dense basalt formations may
experience an increase in volume of about 5 to 20 percent when excavated processed
and used as fill materials
In general we recommend that the subgrades of fill areas be ripped to a depth of
about 3 feet below the existing ground surface to detect and collapse near surface
cavities and or voids As a minimum ripping of the subgrades should extend at least
3 feet laterally beyond the limits of the fill areas After the fill surfaces have been ripped
to a depth of about 3 feet below the native ground surface the ground should be
proof rolled with a Caterpillar D 10 bulldozer or similar size bulldozer a minimum of four
passes to provide a relatively level surface
After leveling of the rugged surfaces with a bulldozer we recommend that the fill
subgrades be proof rolled with a large vibratory drum roller minimum 20 tons static
weight for a minimum of eight passes to help detect and collapse near surface cavities
and or voids The vibratory drum roller should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet
per minute Yielding areas loose areas or cavities disclosed during clearing and
proof rolling operations should be over excavated and backfilled with compacted fill
materials
It should be noted that ripping and proof rolling of fill subgrades in the porous a a
clinker materials likely will cause substantial compression of the porous near surface
materials Based on our observations we estimate that prepared subgrades may end

performed for the Saddle Road East Side Alignment of the proposed new Saddle Road
State Route 200 project located in the Districts of North Hilo and South Hilo on the

Background Information
The existing Saddle Road was constructed initially in 1942 by the United States
Department of the Army to provide access to the Pohakuloa Army Base on the Island of
Hawaii The road was released by the United States Department of the Army and
turned over to the Territory of Hawaii in 1945 The Territory of Hawaii maintained the
road until 1957 and then transferred the maintenance responsibility to the County of
Hawaii In 1965 Saddle Road was included in the State Highway System established
by the State of Hawaii However we understand that the County of Hawaii has
continued to provide maintenance work for Saddle Road up to this date

Regional Geology
The Hawaiian Archipelago consists of an elongated island chain comprising the
eight major islands of the State of Hawaii and hundreds of small islets and atolls
extending for more than 1 000 miles to the northwest of the Hawaiian Island of Niihau
The age of the islands in the Hawaiian Archipelago increases progressing from the
southeast toward the northwest direction
The Hawaiian Archipelago is believed to have formed as a result of plate tectonic
movement working in combination with a relatively deep stationary focal point of volcanic
heat and magma lava generation The Pacific Plate containing the upper earth s crust
underlying the Central Pacific Ocean continues to slowly move in a northwesterly
direction while the nearly stationary hot spot of magma generation continues to produce
eruptive emissions beneath the plate As a result a continuous chain of volcanic islands
formed and has slowly moved off in the northwesterly direction carried away from the
active center of volcanism by plate movement The Island of Hawaii is presently located
adjacent to the deep seated hot spot region The most distant volcanic island remnants
located far to the northwest of Hawaii have been reduced to submerged seamounts as a
result of a long history of land erosion and subsidence
The Island of Hawaii the largest and youngest island in the Hawaiian
Archipelago covers an area of approximately 4 030 square miles The Island of Hawaii
was formed by the coalescence of five primary shield volcano mountains The five
primary shield volcanoes in decreasing geologic age include the following
Kohala located at the northern tip of the island which is long extinct
Mauna Kea located at the northern central portion of the island which
has had some activity during recent geologic time
Hualalai located at the western portion of the island which last erupted
in 1801
Mauna Loa and Kilauea Volcanoes located at the southern and eastern
portions of the island both of which are still active

Submarine volcanic activity on the Island of Hawaii is believed to have
commenced in the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epoch about 1 to 2 million years
ago and continues today with eruptive activity at Kilauea Volcano It is believed that
the Island of Hawaii breached the ocean surface approximately 500 000 years ago
Due to the coalescence of the flanks of the volcanoes composing the Island of Hawaii
the older mountain flanks have been overlapped and buried by recent volcanic
eruptions therefore the evolution of the broad dome shape of the Island of Hawaii is
visible today
The summit elevation of Mauna Kea Volcano resides at about 13 796 feet Mean
Sea Level MSL and the summit elevation of Mauna Loa Volcano resides at about
13 679 feet MSL as shown on the Saddle Road Alignment Plan Plate 2 The broad
high elevation region of the Humuula Saddle is located between the summits of the
Mauna Kea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes The elevation of the Humuula Saddle region is
on the order of approximately 6 500 feet MSL
As an illustration of the recent volcanic activity on the Island of Hawaii over the
past 150 years or so Mauna Loa Volcano has averaged a caldera eruption
approximately every few decades Kilauea Volcano continues an on going active
eruption cycle which has spanned continuously since about 1983 Therefore the
southern and eastern portions of the Island of Hawaii remain volcanically active
whereas toward the north volcanism appears to be in its waning stages
A sixth volcano Loihi which is presently an active submarine volcano lies
offshore of the southeast coastline of the Island of Hawaii and may emerge some
hundreds of thousands of years in the future Due to the presence of on going volcanic
activity at and Loihi the Island of Hawaii is located in a zone of
active seismic influence The Island of Hawaii experiences frequent light to moderate
earthquakes associated with the movement of underground magma and shifting of the
earth s crust under the weight of the growing island formation Occasional large
earthquakes are usually less frequent based on recorded historic occurrence and may
involve geologic fault rupture and lateral offset

Local Site Geology
The Saddle Road East Side Alignment located between Milepost 19 and
Milepost 28 is located on the broad easterly facing mountain slopes formed by the
coalescence of lava flows which erupted from both the Mauna Kea and Mauna Loa
Volcanoes shown on the Saddle Road Alignment Plan Plate 2 In the vicinity of the
proposed Saddle Road East Side Alignment the older weathered lava flows associated
with Mauna Kea Volcano are buried by younger lava flows that were erupted from
Mauna Loa Volcano in historic time and in the late Holocene Epoch These younger
lava flows generally extend from the summit region of Mauna Loa Volcano down toward
the north and east directions The northerly flowing lavas banked against the southerly
facing flank of Mauna Kea Volcano and were diverted further downslope toward the
easterly direction where some of the historic flows approached the outskirts of the town
of Hilo
In sporadic locations where the surface lava flows from Mauna Loa Volcano are
thin in coverage the older lava rock formations of Mauna Loa and Mauna Kea
Volcanoes may be locally exposed at the ground surface The existing Saddle Road
alignment located between Milepost 19 and Milepost 28 was constructed to follow the
surface of these recent lava flows which extend in an east northeasterly direction
downslope toward the Town of Hilo
Based on our geological literature review and geotechnical field exploration of the
proposed road alignment the Saddle Road East Side Alignment between Milepost 19
and Milepost 28 traverses ground underlain by surface lava flows representing various
basalt rock formations including a a and pahoehoe type lava flows Our site
reconnaissance and subsurface drilling exploration did not encounter either surface or
buried soil deposits within the proposed project alignment between Milepost 19 and
Milepost 28 Some minor trace amounts of silty organic soils were encountered locally
in the shallow subsurface during bulldozer clearing of alignment access trails In
addition some coarse sandy and gravelly soils are anticipated to be produced by the
excavation and handling with heavy equipment physical breakdown of the basalt rock
clinker materials encountered at the project site

Climate
Over the ocean near Hawaii rainfall averages between 25 and 30 inches a year
The islands receive as much as 15 times that amount in some places and less than
one third of it in others This phenomenon is caused mainly by orographic or mountain
rains which form within the moist trade wind air as it moves from the sea over the steep
and high terrain of the islands Over the lower islands the average rainfall distribution
closely resembles the topographic contours Amounts are greatest over upper slopes
and crests and least in the leeward lowlands On the higher mountains the belt of
maximum rainfall lies between Elevation 2 000 and 3 000 feet MSL and amounts
decrease rapidly with higher elevations As a result the highest slopes are relatively
dry

Geophysical Surveys
Surface geophysical surveys consisting of seismic refraction SR and refraction
micro tremor ReMi methods were performed as part of the field exploration for the
project The following provides a brief description of the two methods of seismic
geophysical surveys performed at the project site
The seismic refraction exploration method is a proven non intrusive geophysical
means of evaluating the relative density thickness and depth of subsurface earth
materials such as soil and rock layers Seismic refraction is used to record the
compression wave primary or P wave response as the wave passes through or is
refracted at the interfaces of materials with different densities velocities in various
types of soil and rock media
The seismic refraction method is a useful subsurface exploration tool where
geologic layers possess increasing material density with increasing depth a condition
that normally holds true for about 80 percent of naturally occurring geologic deposits
However the seismic refraction survey method cannot be used to profile the subsurface
conditions where the P wave velocity of the earth material decreases with depth
P wave velocity inversion
In addition the recorded P wave velocities in mixed thin bedded earth layers that
may have variable P wave velocities such as interbedded loose basalt clinker and
massive hard rock lava flow sequences generally are averaged to obtain approximate
layer velocities Therefore the seismic refraction exploration method should be used to
supplement other sources of subsurface information such as drilled test borings
The seismic refraction method requires experienced interpretive judgment by the
analyst to identify various trends and inconsistencies that may be present in the
Hawaii California

recorded field data As a result the profile and wave velocity information obtained from
the surveys should be treated as an idealized data set where some variation should be
anticipated in the field conditions
A Smartseis S 12 12 channel exploration seismograph manufactured by
Geometrics Inc coupled with 12 geophones was employed for the performance of
each seismic refraction line surveyed A 12 pound sledge hammer impacting a metal
plate positioned at the ground surface was used to generate the seismic P waves
which were analyzed for the field exploration A Seismic Refraction Interpretation
Program produced by Rimrock Geophysics SIP was used to process the field data and
generate the subsurface profiles for each seismic survey location
The refraction micro tremor method is a new proven method for measuring
in situ shear wave S wave velocity profiles Testing is performed at the ground
surface similar to a seismic refraction survey using an array of 12 P wave geophones
linked to a conventional seismograph The seismic source consists of ambient site
noise or micro tremors which are continually generated by natural and cultural sources
Therefore unlike traditional seismic refraction surveys where a quiet survey
environment is needed to record the induced shot energy busy ambient noise from a
wide range of frequencies is most desirable The seismograph record is triggered and
the ambient noise is recorded by the geophone array for a set period of time Multiple
recordings are performed in the field for each survey location
Experienced personnel capable of interpreting the raw data using the SeisOpt
ReMi Version 2 0 software processed the data collected for this project A colorized
profile of the shear wave velocities was generated depicting the variation in material
shear wave velocity across the profile area Unlike the seismic refraction method the
refraction micro tremor method has the capability of imaging seismic wave velocity
inversions making it useful for detecting potential buried low velocity layers such as
weak rock layers or subsurface voids

Fills and Backfills
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered along the proposed alignment we
believe that the excavated materials consisting of basalt clinkers and basalt rocks
may be re used as a source of fill materials for embankment construction In
general we recommend that the fill areas of the project be capped with a minimum
2 foot thick layer of structural fill materials to serve as a choking layer over the
larger sized general fill materials Structural fill materials should consist of
well graded granular materials less than 6 inches in maximum dimension with
sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass
Consideration also should be given to limiting the maximum particle size maximum
particle size of 6 inches of the fill materials placed within utility line corridors if
known to facilitate future excavations for utility line trenches
Fill and backfill materials below the minimum 2 foot thick structural fill layer may
consist of general fill materials General fill and backfill materials should consist of
well graded granular materials the majority of which are less than 12 inches in size
with an absolute maximum dimension of 24 inches The excavated on site
materials if less than 24 inches in maximum dimension may be used as a source
of general fill or backfill materials other than for trench backfill provided that they
are processed to meet the above gradation requirements If the excavated
materials do not contain sufficient fines to produce a well graded material off site
borrow or on site rock crushing of larger rock fragments or boulders should be
considered to provide the required gradation and particle sizes to develop a
well graded material

Structural fill materials consisting of granular fill materials with a maximum particle
size of 6 inches should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to
at least 95 percent relative compaction General fills and backfills should be placed
in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose thickness or the largest dimension of
the majority of the materials moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of

Compaction of fill materials should be accomplished using sheepsfoot rollers
vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment
Due to the somewhat dry and sometimes windy site conditions at the western end
of the Saddle Road East Side Alignment project moisture conditioning of the fill
materials will be a critical aspect during fill placement In some areas the in situ

of the on site materials
Rock fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of the

placement by water trucks traversing the front of the current rock lift face and
spraying water continuously during placement

Potential Geologic Hazards
The most significant geologic hazard for the Saddle Road East Side Alignment is
the potential for inundation by future lava flows emitted from Mauna Loa Volcano
Based on the distribution of lava flows occurring within recorded historical time that are
in close proximity to the project site we believe that the potential exists for future lava
flows to affect the Saddle Road East Side Alignment
According to scientists at the Hawaii Volcano Observatory
Volcano remains active and is capable of erupting in the near future on the order of
years to decades Although its potential for future activity is certain it is unknown where
and when Mauna Loa Volcano may erupt next The Saddle Road project site resides in
the mapped potential lava inundation Zone 2 where Zone 1 carries the highest risk for

northeast rift zone of Mauna Loa Volcano Zone 2 and is therefore susceptible to lava
flows emitted from future eruptions of the northeast rift zone The following is a brief
discussion of the recent lava flow events that have affected the project site and vicinity
The recent lava flow of 1935 extends from the summit of Mauna Loa Volcano
northerly toward Mauna Kea Volcano and then easterly down toward the upper
Kaumana area Portions of Saddle Road were later constructed on this lava flow

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions along the proposed Saddle Road East Side Alignment
Milepost 19 to Milepost 28 were explored by drilling and sampling 29 borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 23 and Boring Nos 101 through 106 The
borings extended to depths ranging from about 5 to 35 feet below the existing ground
surface Nine bulk soil samples designated as Bulk 1 through Bulk 7 and Bulk 101 and
Bulk 102 were obtained of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics and the quality of the materials for use as embankment fills
The approximate locations of the test borings drilled and bulk samples collected
Plate 3 The approximate locations of the test borings and near surface bulk samples
also are shown on the Alignment Plans Plates 4 1 through 4 8 The borings were
drilled using both truck mounted and skid mounted drilling equipment equipped with
continuous flight augers and HQ triple tube wireline coring tools as appropriate
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 29
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and
Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration
Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock formations encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling

Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals

Geophysical Survey
The subsurface conditions along the Saddle Road East Side Alignment also were
explored by the performance of 22 seismic refraction survey lines The approximate
locations of the seismic refraction surveys conducted for the project are shown on the
Alignment Plans Plates 4 1 through 4 8
Refraction seismography is based on the transmission of elastic energy
compression waves through geologic materials The seismic refraction surveys were
conducted using a 12 channel signal enhancement seismograph with a 12 pound
sledge hammer as a seismic energy source Generally the denser or more
consolidated a material is the faster the transmission of the compression waves
These waves are refracted at interfaces between materials with contrasting densities
The refracted waves are received by geophones on the surface and recorded by the
operator
The recorded data is input into a desktop computer for processing which
involves the interpretive selection of the arrival times of the refracted waves After the
interpretive selection of arrival times the technique permits mathematical calculations
that yield approximate depths or thickness of materials below the survey lines and
provide their approximate compression wave velocities The velocities are averaged
along the travel path of the compression waves The calculated velocities may be
empirically related to the excavation characteristics of the materials

Profiles presented on Plates B 1 through B 22 Due to the lower P wave velocity of the
underlying clinker and a a lava flow materials velocity inversion layer profile
computations could not be performed using seismic refraction at the locations of SR 1
and SR 2

Seismic shear wave velocity estimates of the subsurface materials at the project
site also were obtained by using Refraction Microtremor Array SeisOpt ReMi methods
Obtaining shear wave velocity estimates of the subsurface materials using Refraction
Microtremor Arrays is based on the combination of commonly available seismic
refraction equipment simple recording with no source a wavefield transformation data
processing technique spectral analysis of surface wave SASW techniques and
multi channel analysis of surface wave MASW techniques
Surface geophysical seismic refraction surveys were performed at twelve
selected locations designated as ReMi 101 through ReMi 112 within the project limits
The approximate locations and orientations of the field surveys ReMi 101 through
ReMi 112 are shown on the Alignment Plans Plates 4 1 through 4 8
The seismic refraction exploration method is a non intrusive geophysical means
of evaluating the relative density thickness and depth of subsurface earth materials
such as soil and rock layers A Smartseis S 12 12 channel exploration seismograph
manufactured by Geometrics Inc coupled with 12 geophones was employed for the
performance of each seismic refraction line surveyed The horizontal distance between
the two adjacent geophones was set at 20 feet
The results of the seismic refraction surveys were evaluated by using the
SeisOpt ReMi software to estimate the subsurface shear wave velocities The software
transforms the refraction microtremor data recorded in the field using the seismic
refractometer and generates a velocity spectrum in the slowness frequency domain
The process yields the Rayleigh wave phase velocity dispersion curve Interpretation of
this dispersion curve is made to model the shear wave velocity profiles The seismic
refraction methods require experienced interpretive judgment by the analyst to identify
various trends and inconsistencies that may be present in the recorded field data As a
result the profile and wave velocity information obtained from the surveys should be
treated as an idealized data set where some variation should be anticipated in the field
conditions

Survey Results
The seismic refraction survey analysis is unable to compute a subsurface profile
due to a detected layer velocity inversion lower compression P wave velocity of
deeper geologic layers in the recorded field data In other words the deeper rock
layers have lower seismic P wave velocities than the surface rock layer therefore
layer thicknesses and boundaries cannot be computed from the data
Our interpretation of the results is that a hard pahoehoe lava flow Layer No 1
overlies a layer of soft to medium hard basalt clinker Layer No 2 associated with the
underlying a a type lava flow The soft to medium hard basalt clinker layer overlies a
medium hard to hard basalt rock layer Layer No 3 The thickness of the
aforementioned profile is estimated to extend about 20 to 30 feet below the existing
ground surface
Field Observations
A harder pahoehoe type surface lava flow appears to overlie deeper rock layers
composed of more porous basalt clinker that may be representative of older less dense
a a type lava flow Based on surface observations the top pahoehoe lava flow may be
about 5 to 10 feet in thickness Some nearby surface rock exposures provide an
opportunity to view the subject geologic profile In addition the adjacent Boring Nos 1
and 2 may be used as a supplemental reference to the estimated profile described

APPENDIX C
Laboratory Testing
A P P E N D I X C
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Fourteen Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 were performed on
selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in
soil classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on
Plates C 1 through C 3
Thirteen Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on
selected rock cores to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the various rock
formations encountered Results of the unconfined compression tests are summarized
and presented on Plate C 4
Five sets of Corrosion Tests including minimum resistivity
Chloride Content and Sulfate Content were
performed on selected bulk soil samples obtained during our field exploration The
results of these tests are summarized on Plate C 5
Five laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed
on bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils and the quality of the materials for use as embankment fills
The test results are presented on Plates C 6 through C 10
Two laboratory Resistance R Value and Expansion Pressure Tests ASTM D 2844
were performed on selected bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the
pavement support characteristics of the soils The test results are presented on
Plates C 11 and C 12
