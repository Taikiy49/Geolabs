2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two large shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield volcano Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active thus Waianae s eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas
in the central Oahu region
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the worldwide glaciers grew and advanced less water
was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of
the sea When the glaciers melted and receded a larger volume of water became
available such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level Thus the island
shorelines have transgressed and regressed in response to the fluctuations The
processes of terrestrial erosion and the associated depositional environments were
affected by these glacioeustatic sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the
erosional base level was correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths
below the present sea level As a result terrestrial sediments were carried further
seaward and deposited in what had been marine environments When the sea level
was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments accumulated at
higher elevations In addition it allowed for the deposition of marine deposits such as
coral and limestone in environments that had been covered with terrestrial soils and
rock
Interpretation of the historic geology of the coastal plain regions located on the
Island of Oahu such as the Hickam AFB and Bellows AFS project sites is complex due to
a long history of changes in sea level that occurred in relation to the island land surface
The overall changes in sea level in relation to the land surface resulted from a combination

of tectonic forces such as uplift and settlement of the island landmass and the previously
discussed glacio eustatic ocean level changes In other words the rise and fall of sea
level in relation to the island produced shorelines of emergence and submergence In
general the overall changes of sea level in relation to the landmass also caused the
erosional base level to fluctuate producing features such as drowned stream valleys filled
with marine deposits and conversely incised marine deposits filled with terrestrial
sediments or rock
Hickam Air Force Base Site
The geologic conditions underlying the site are the result of a complex series of
deposition and erosional events brought on by glacio eustatic sea level changes
and post erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Following the
cessation of activity of the Koolau Volcano alluvial materials were deposited in a
deltaic or estuarine environment in the area of Pear Harbor
The ancient sea level fluctuated several times both above and below the present
sea level Concurrent with a rise in sea level during the late Pleistocene Epoch
eruption of the Salt Lake Makalapa member of the Honolulu Volcanic Series
mantled the older alluvium with thick deposits of volcanic ash which consolidated
to form volcanic tuff locally called mudrock layers Subsequent erosion
dissected and thinned those mantles of tuff
During the recent epoch as the sea level rose to its current stand the area was
covered with layers of soft and or loose lagoonal marine deposits These deposits
were subsequently covered by man made fill placed during the middle portion of
this century for land reclamation projects during construction of the existing Hickam
Air Force Base Due to the proximity of the project site to the shoreline and
ocean relatively shallow and brackish groundwater conditions may be
anticipated at the Hickam AFB project site

Bellows Air Force Station Site

3 1 Slab On Grade Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the relatively light
structural loads anticipated for the project we believe that slab on grade foundations
with thickened edge footings may be used to support the proposed new structures
planned for the subject project The perimeter thickened edges of the slabs should be
embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent exterior grade In
addition the base of the thickened edge should have a minimum width of 12 inches
In general we believe that an allowable bearing pressure of up to about
2 500 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of shallow foundations
bearing on the recompacted on site soils or compacted fill material This bearing value
such as those caused by wind or seismic forces Lateral loads acting on the structures
may be resisted by frictional resistance developed between the bottom of the foundation
and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces
of the foundation system A coefficient of friction of about 0 3 may be used for footings
bearing on the recompacted on site soils or compacted fill materials Resistance due to
passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of about
300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soils around
the footings are well compacted or undisturbed Unless covered by pavements or
slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
In generai the bottom of footings should bear on the stiff or dense near surface
soils The bottom of the footing excavations should be recompacted to at least
90 percent relative compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface
prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete Soft and or loose materials

soils encountered during construction to confirm the adequacy of the above sections
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to
carry surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site
during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or
adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs be extended a minimum of
2 inches into the subgrade soil below the base and subbase course layers to reduce the
potential for migration of excessive landscape water into the pavement section
Alternatively a subdrain system could be constructed to collect excessive water from
landscaping irrigation For long term performance we recommend that a subdrain system
be constructed adjacent to the paved landscaped areas
3 4 Utility Trenches
We envision that new utility lines such as sewer water and drain lines will be
required for the project In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel
No 67 gradation should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches
above the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the
sides of the pipes Improper backfill material around the pipes and placement could
result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select granular fill
materials The on site clayey soils may be used as trench backfill in landscape areas
only The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not
less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future
ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the

3 5 Existing Underground Utilities
Existing underground utility lines may traverse the subject housing development
Therefore we recommend that all existing utilities within the project limits be accurately
located by the contractor prior to construction The contractor has the sole
responsibility to devise and implement an effective program to protect the utilities during
construction
Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the resulting
excavation should be properly backfilled with select granular fill materials The select
granular fill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
placed in 8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Utilities to be abandoned in place under the proposed structures should be
backfilled by pumping lean concrete under low pressure
3 6 Drainage
The finished grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away
from the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that
gutter systems be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted
away from the foundation and pavement areas Based on our field exploration and
laboratory test results it is our opinion that a gutter system should be installed around
the entire building for the housing units at the Bellows AFS site However we believe
that gutters may be required only along the walkways and patios for the housing units at
the Hickam AFB site Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs
should also be avoided In addition planters next to foundations should be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration
into the subsurface
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of our
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the sites were explored by drilling and sampling
14 borings extending to depths ranging from about 19 5 to 21 5 feet below the existing
ground surface Nine borings designated as H 1 through H 9 were drilled at the
Hickam AFB site and five borings designated as B 1 through B 5 were drilled at the
Bellows AFS site The approximate locations of the drilled borings are shown on the Site
Plans Plates 2 1 through 2 3 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered is presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 14
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and
drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2987 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Fifteen one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and
remolded samples of the soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface
soils The results of these tests are summarized on Plates B 1 through B 3
One sieve analysis test ASTM C was performed on a
selected soil sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in
soil classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on
Plate B 4
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed
on bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 5 and B 6
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates B 7 and B 8
