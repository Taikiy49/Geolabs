Soil Engineering Investigation
Proposed Interchange at Farrington Highway
for West Beach Resort Development
Hawaii
This report presents the results of our soil engineering
investigation performed for the above referenced project The
approximate location of the interchange and its vicinity are shown on
the Project Location Map Plate l
Purpose and Scope
The purpose of the investigation was to explore the subsurface soil
conditions at the proposed interchange to provide pertinent soil data
for use in the design and construction of the proposed project
The scope of work performed in this investigation was in general
accordance with our proposal dated and included the
Grilling and sampling of three 3 borings a series of seismic
refraction surveys laboratory testing engineering evaluation of the
field and laboratory findings and report preparation

The results of our field exploration and laboratory tests which we
used in the engineering analysis to formulate our conclusion and
recommendations are presented in the appendices of this report
Project Considerations
The proposed interchange consists of an overpass crossing and two
on off ramps connecting Farrington Highway to the proposed West Beach
Resort Development
The overpass across Farrington Highway has two 130 foot spans and
is supported by two abutments and a central pier Fills up to 12 and 20
feet are anticipated for the construction of the embankments at the
north and south abutment areas of the overpass respectively It is our
understanding that the southern abutment will be constructed on top of a
high fill slope
Loading on the abutments is anticipated to be 35 kips per foot
while that for the central pier is expected to be a total of about 5600
kips
The on off ramp will be constructed by cutting up to 50 feet into
the existing slopes on the north of the highway using a multiple slope
and bench system at an approximate gradient of H V
The general layout of the interchange is shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2
Site Description
The proposed site is located along Farrington Highway between Kahe
Point and Honokai Hale Subdivision
GEOLABS HAWAII
Community Planning Inc
I D 1796 Page 3
The topography is gently sloping down towards the ocean at a
gradient of about 4 percent on the makai or south side of Farrington
Highway The mauka or north side of the highway is fronted with an
upward slope at an initial maximum gradient of about 1 1 H V for about
10 to 15 feet which then changes to a flatter gradient of 14 percent
Another 1 1 upward slope exists at an approximate distance of 200 feet
to the north of the highway and the slope grades to a milder gradient of
18 percent as it goes further upward
Rock outcrops were noted in some of the cut sections along
Farrington Highway and along the steep cliff face about 150 to 200 feet
north of Farrington Highway
Subsurface Condition
The subsurface conditions were explored by utilizing geophysical
equipment Refraction Seismograph and drilling and sampling three 3
borings to depths of 19 5 to 34 5 feet below the existing ground surface
at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The borings disclosed hard weathered vesicular basalt rock
formation at depths of about 20 feet below the existing Farrington
Highway ground surface The basalt formation becomes much shallower in
Boring 2A mauka and exposed as outcrops along the mauka or north side
of Farrington Highway
Alluvium deposits were encountered above the weathered rock
formation Within the area explored by our borings the alluvium
generally consists of a 4 to 16 foot layer of stiff silty clay to clayey
silt overlying a thin layer 3 5 to 5 5 feet of silty sand In Borings
1A and 3A boulders and cobbles were encountered within the top 12 and 7
feet of the clayey layer respectively

drilled during the course of our field exploration
Discussion and Recommendations
General
The field exploration disclosed that weathered basalt rock
formation exists at medium depth of about 10 to 20 feet below the
existing ground surface across Farrington Highway At the northern high
ground area of the project site hard rock exposed as outcrops was
encountered
We believe that the near surface rock formation encountered can
provide sufficient foundation support for the northern on off ramp and
the north abutment of the overpass For the central pier because of
potentially large amount of settlement with spread footing foundation
we recommend that drilled caisson bearing on the underlying rock
formation be used For the southern abutment spread footing bearing on
the well compacted granular fill can be used
Overpass Foundation
It is our understanding that the overpass will be supported by two
abutment walls located on both sides of Farrington Highway and a central
pier Loading from the overpass onto the support varies from 35 kips
per foot along the abutment walls to a total load of 5600 kips on the
central pier
South Abutment
We understand that the south abutment will be constructed at the
top of a fill slope with a gradient of 2 1 H V The height of
the slope is anticipated to be about 20 feet
GEOLABS HAWAII
Community Planning Inc
I D 1796 Page 5
Based on the assumption that granular dredged coral will be used to
construct the fill slope we believe that spread footing bearing on
the well compacted backfill material can be used as foundation
support for the abutment An allowable bearing capacity of 4000
p s f is recommended The footings should be embedded at a
sufficient depth to achieve a 10 foot horizontal set back distance
from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope
Settlement due to the abutment load on the backfill material is
anticipated to be on the order of one fourth of an inch
Resistance to lateral loads from the proposed abutment can be
obtained by passive soil pressure and frictional resistance A
frictional coefficient of 0 4 may be used between the bottom of the
footing and the compacted granular backfill In addition an
equivalent fluid pressure of 200 p s f per foot of depth may be
used to determine the passive soil resistance
To minimize the effect of consolidation settlement of the
underlying natural soil due to the 20 foot fill slope the fill
should be placed as early as possible prior to construction of the
abutment Settlement gauges should be installed to monitor the
settlement and to determine when the construction of the bridge
abutment may commence For estimating purposes a minimum of
2 month waiting period should be allowed between end of filling and
the construction of the overpass
Central Pier
The basalt formation at the central pier area is overlain by a
2l foot thick layer of alluvium consisting of boulders cobbles
silty sand clayey silt and silty clay The silty clay layer with
thickness of about 9 feet was found to be moderately compressible
GEOLABS HAWAII
Community Planning Inc
W O 1568 00 C
I D 1796 Page 6
Due to the existence of the 9 foot thick layer of moderately
compressible material underlying the central pier shallow spread
footing type of foundation may experience settlement as high as 3
inches due to the heavy bridge load We believe that the presence
of large boulders and cobbles may hinder or preclude the use of
driven piles as foundation support
In light of the construction difficulty for pile and the large
settlement for spread footing foundation drilled caissons bearing
on the underlying basalt rock formation is recommended as
foundation support for the central pier
The caissons should extend into the competent basalt formation for
at least 2 feet For a 3 foot diameter caisson an allowable load
capacity of 90 tons per caisson may be used Settlements of the
caissons under the design loads are anticipated to be on the order
of less than one half of an inch
Boulders and cobbles might obstruct and slow down caisson
drilling Cobbles and smaller boulders particularly when in a
clayey silt matrix can generally be removed with caisson drilling
equipment but drilling progress will be somewhat slow Very large
boulders up to two thirds the shaft diameter may require removal
by special tools like boulder bits or buckets or in some cases
require some manual efforts using a jack hammer
Particular attention should be paid to ensure the cleanliness of
the drilled shaft of the caisson To prevent caving in casing may
be required
The caisson drilling and casting should be monitored by a
geotechnical engineer to see that they are placed on competent
bearing formation

To resist the lateral loads the passive earth pressure around the
caisson caps and tie beams may be used We recommend an equivalent
fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot of depth The
backfill against the caisson caps or tie beams should be properly
backfilled with select materials compacted to a dry density of at
least 95 of the maximum density as determined by ASTM D 1557 78
test method
North Abutment
Outcrops of the basalt formation were noted close to the proposed
north abutment Based on an interpolated rock profile using the
borings and seismic surveys depth to rock at the abutment location
is anticipated to be about 8 feet
We believe that spread footing bearing on the underlying bedrock
can be used as foundation support for the northern abutment An
allowable bearing value of 10 000 p s f is recommended on the
dense rock formation The bearing may be increased by one third
when temporary wind or seismic loads are considered
Resistance to lateral loads for the proposed abutment can be
obtained by passive pressure and frictional resistance A
frictional coefficient of 0 6 may be used between the bottom of the
footing and competent basalt formation In addition an equivalent
fluid pressure of 300 p s f per foot of depth may be used to
determine the passive resistance in soil and a lateral bearing
value of 5000 p s f may be used in dense rock formation

Lateral Soil Pressure
The recommended lateral earth pressures are as follows
Walls unrestrained at top 35 p c f equivalent fluid pressure
active condition
Wallis restrained at top 50 p c f equivalent fluid pressure
at rest condition
Additional load due to traffic should be included where applicable
The above lateral pressures assume that granular backfill material
will be used and weepholes provided through the abutment walls to
reduce the build up of hydrostatic pressure behind the walls
Site Grading
Fills up to 20 feet and cuts up to 50 feet are anticipated for the
construction of the proposed overpass and the on off ramp respectively

Prior to placing the compacted fill the site should first be
cleared of vegetation rubbish debris and other unsuitable
materials Any localized soft spots encountered should be
excavated and replaced with compacted select materials
The fill material should consist of granular dredged coral or
well graded excavated rock less than 6 inches in size
The fills should be placed in horizontal lifts not exceeding 8
inches in compacted thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 of
its maximum dry density per ASTM D 1557 78 The fill slope located
on the southern side of Farrington Highway should be overfilled and
then trimmed back to its final design slope gradient

Where the existing ground is steeper than 5 horizontal to 1

the new fill to the slope The filling operation should start at

Within the top 6 inches of the subgrade level of the ramp on top

Excavation
The field reconnaissance consisting of seismic refraction surveys
and observations of rock outcrops indicates that the site may be
underlain at fairly shallow depths by basalt formations A thin
layer less than 5 feet thick consisting of soil and boulders
rests on the rock formation
We believe that less than one third of the excavation into rock for
the proposed interchange on off ramps may be done by bulldozer and
ripping equipment
The more massive basalt will likely require special excavating
procedures such as hoe ram or blasting to make the proposed cuts
and benches

be cut for a vertical height of up to about 50 feet

Slope benches are planned at about 12 foot vertical height
intervals for landscape purposes
A cut slope ratio up to H V may be used in the hard rock
formation There may be a few feet of soil over the rock formation
at the top of the cut and therefore provisions should be made to
flatten the cut in soil to H V
In general surface runoff water should be diverted as much as
feasible away from the top of slope
The toe of the cut slope should be set back a minimum distance of
15 feet from the edge of pavement to provide a catchment zone for
potential rock falls
Design Review
Plans and specifications for site grading and foundation design
should be forwarded to the soils engineer for review and written
comments prior to construction to see that the intent of our
recommendations is included
Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the grubbing backfilling site grading
foundation and subgrade preparation be observed by the soils engineer
during construction to see that adequate compaction and sufficient
embedment of footing have been obtained and that the subsurface
conditions are consistent with the findings of our investigation
Any imported material required should be non expansive tested and
approved by the soils engineer prior to hauling to the site
If conditions encountered during construction are different from
those presented in this report then appropriate modifications to the
design should be made

Investigation Limitations
Our services consist of professional opinions and recommendations
made in accordance with generally accepted soil and foundation
engineering principles and practices This warranty is in lieu of all
other warranties either expressed or implied
The analysis and recommendations submitted in this report are based
on our site reconnaissance soil information from boring and laboratory
test data obtained from this soil investigation
Unanticipated soil conditions are commonly encountered and cannot
be fully determined by taking soil samples Unforeseen conditions such
as groundwater seepage cavities soft spots and expansive soil may
occur in localized areas and may require additional probing or
corrections in the field Therefore additional expenditures may be
needed during construction to attain a properly constructed project
Some contingency fund is thus recommended to accommodate these possible
costs

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling
three 3 borings to depths of 19 5 to 34 5 feet below the existing
ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 A truck mounted drill rig was used to drill the borings
The soils encountered in the borings were classified by visual and
textural examination in the field by our engineer who continuously
monitored the drilling operations The classifications were further
reviewed by visual inspection and testing in the laboratory All soils
were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System A summary of the materials encountered are
presented in the Log of Borings Plates A through A 3
Soil samples were obtained from the borings by driving a 2 inch
standard split spoon sampler with a 140 pound hammer free falling 30
inches The blow counts to drive the sampler the last 12 inches are
shown on the Log of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

Seismic Surveys

In conjunction with the sampling operations geophysical
exploration techniques were used to obtain further subsurface data
A series of two 2 shallow seismic refraction surveys were
performed in selected areas of the project site These surveys were
performed using a BISON Model 1570C Refraction Seismograph receiving
seismic signals generated by the impacts of a 10 pound sledge hammer
The signal enhancement feature of this seismograph provided a relatively
noise free signal as the result of repeated blows of the sledge hammer
The approximate locations of the tests are shown on the Site Plan Plate
2 and the results are presented on Plates C 1 and C 2
