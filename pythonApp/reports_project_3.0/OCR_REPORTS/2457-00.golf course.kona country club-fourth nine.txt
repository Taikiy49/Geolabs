INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our preliminary geotechnical
engineering reconnaissance for the design and construction of the mass
grading for the proposed Kona Country Club Fourth Nine Golf Course The
project site is located on the eastern slopes of Hualalai in the North
Kona District of the island of Hawaii The general location of the
project site is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our preliminary findings and recommendations

GENERAL SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is situated on the eastern slopes of Hualalai in the
North Kona District on the leeward side of the island of Hawaii The
general location of the site and its vicinity is shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
The site may be divided into two separate areas In the northern
half volcanic rock formation is exposed at the surface and vegetation
consists of isolated noni trees and haole koa with sparse stands of
dryland grasses In the southern half the site is densely vegetated with
grasses and there are thickets and stands of mature mango kiawe and
monkeypod trees
In general the topography of the site is rugged with steep slopes
indicative of a a lava flows exposed at the surface or mantled with a thin
soil cover
Elevations at the site vary from about 145 to 515 feet above Mean Sea
level

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Hawaii is composed of five 5 shield volcances
Kohala Mauna Kea and Kilauea built up by the
extrusion of basaltic lavas during the late Pleistocene and Holocene
epochs Hualalai is situated on the western side of the island and was
last active in 1801 1803
Hualalai is built of interbedded a a and pahoehoe lava flows Ala
flows are characterized by a rubbly surface of spiny clinker with a dense
inner core Pahoehoe flows are characterized by smoother ropy or billowy
surfaces with vesicular lava that grades denser with depth in the
individual flow unit In some areas of Hualalai the lava flows have been
mantled with a thin layer of volcanic ash Pahala Ash This ash soil
generally has high moisture contents and has poor strength
characteristics

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our exploration and analyses it is our opinion that the
development of the proposed golf course is feasible from a geotechnical
engineering point of view The basalt formation encountered at the site
should provide adequate bearing for the proposed golf course development
The excavated materials should provide a suitable source of fill material
if reduced to less than 12 inches in size
Grading
In general it is our opinion that customary grading practices should
be modified somewhat due to the anticipated rocky nature of the excavated
material
Prior to commencement of grading all areas within the proposed
grading limits should be cleared of all vegetation and deleterious
materials The resulting grub and spoil material should be disposed of
properly off site or stockpiled for use in landscaping
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2457 00 Page 6
The ash soil encountered in the southern half of the project site has
poor strength characteristics and should be stripped from the grading
areas and stockpiled for use as topsoil or be thoroughly blended into
rockfill material as discussed below
Due to the dry and sometimes windy site conditions moisture
conditioning of fill material will be necessary It is also advisable to
institute a dust control program during grading operations
Soft or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below
any areas designated to receive fill should be over excavated to fim
natural material and the resulting depression backfilled with well
compacted engineered fill
In general the excavated rock materials if reduced to less than 12
inches in size may be suitable as fill material The material if
reduced to less than six 6 inches in size should be adequate as a
source of trench and structural backfill if needed Imported material
if required should be non expansive with no particles greater than six
6 inches in size and no more than fifteen 15 percent passing the 200
sieve It is recommended that if additional off site borrow is required
it should be tested by the geotechnical engineer to determine if it is
suitable for its intended use prior to its delivery to the jobsite
Large boulders or rock fragments should be buried in the deepest
portions of the fills provided that they are not nested and an adequate
Choking layer consisting of 24 inches of well graded gravel such as base
course or six inch minus is used to cap the rockfill to reduce the
potential of loss of fines from the upper portion of the fill Care must
be exercised to avoid placement of any boulders in locations which would
conflict with future utility lines In general boulder or rock fragments
larger than 24 inches in size should be placed at depths greater that four
4 feet below finish subgrade level
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2457 00 Page 7
The excavated rock material may not contain sufficient fines to
produce a well graded material therefore the surface ash soil could be
mixed into the rocky excavated material to increase the percentage of
fines and to create a better graded fill material
Generally fills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding twelve
12 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 of the
maximum dry density as established by ASTM D 1557 78 Trench and
structural backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding eight
8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimm of 90 of the
maximm dry density per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills
composed of excavated volcanic material Instead a testing program to
determine the number of passes of a compactor needed to achieve the
desired level of compaction should be conducted at the start of the
grading phase of the project On the basis of this testing program the
number of passes may then be used as the field criterion for adequate
compaction
Any fills to be placed on slopes having ratios steeper than five
horizontal to one vertical 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into fim
residual soil or in situ volcanic formation to lessen potential
instability of the new fill see Plate 3 The filling operations should
start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted
layers in accordance with the above recommendations for fill placement
Excavation
The field exploration and visual observations made at the site and in
its vicinity indicate that the site is most likely underlain by a thin
surface layer of clinkery material or ash soil over ledges of dense basalt
at relatively shallow depths

The basalt formation will most likely require some hard excavation
such as heavy ripping hoerams or possibly blasting
Generally the excavation method should be left to the contractor
Blasting may be considered to expedite the excavation process However
care should be exercised to avoid overshooting which could disrupt the
formation resulting in a loss of strength
Blasting Considerations
It is our understanding that there are concerns regarding the use of
explosives at the site for excavation at the site due to the potentially
sensitive nature of the historic and cultural features at the National
Historic Site it is our opinion that most of the rock at the site could
be excavated without the use of explosives However the progress of work
would be slow and inefficient Further the vibrations caused by the
heavy ripping and or chipping with hoerams may be as detrimental if not
more so than those caused by carefully controlled blasting The following
discussions are presented to aid in the selection of an excavation method
Hazards or objectionable impacts from blasting operations generally
originate from one or more of the following
1 Flyrocks
2 Ground Vibration
3 Air Blast
Flyrock
Flyrock is the blaster s term for flying rock or other debris which
is launched into the air during open blasting by the force of the
detonating explosives These projectiles often have tremendous
kinetic energy and can crush or destroy things in their flight path
or at their point of impact
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O
2457 00 Page 9
Flyrock generated during the earthwork for the proposed road grading
could potentially strike nearby features If the flyrock has
sufficient kinetic energy it could create significant damage
The occurrence of flyrock may be reduced and possibly eliminated by
increasing the confinement or depth of the explosive charges and or
by using safety mats
Ground Vibration
With respect to the subject project ground vibration is probably the
most critical potential impact When an explosive detonates below
ground it generates intense stress waves in the surrounding
material These waves crushes the rock immediately adjacent to the
explosion and permanently distorts and cracks the rock for some
distance
When the intensity of the stress waves attenuates to the point that
there is no longer any permanent deformation of the rock the stress
is propagated through the rock as elastic waves These elastic waves
create ground vibration
The potential of the ground vibration to damage a structure is a
function of the intensity of the vibration and the natural frequency
of the structures According to the Du Pont Blaster s Handbook a
formula has been developed to relate the intensity of ground
vibration at a given point in terms of particle displacement
velocity and acceleration to the weight of the explosive charge and
the distance between the explosion and the point of interest
The equation to determine the peak particle velocity is in the form
of

Air Blast
The air blast from an explosion is a compressional wave in air
Noise is the portion of the air blast with frequencies between 20 and
and concussion is the portion with frequencies below 20
Hz The Du Pont Blaster s Handbooks also sets forth a scaled
distance relationship for air blast Air blast might be considered a
problem for a dryland stone structure such as the heiau
If blasting is to be used for the earthwork we recommend that the
scaled distances of the explosive charges be limited to those that
will generate peak particle velocities of 1 5 inches per second or
less at the structures Blasting should be performed in accordance

hard excavation will be required for utility line trenching

relatively competent and would require minimal shoring However areas of

The soils encountered were classified by visual and textural

drilling operations The classifications were further reviewed by visual
inspection and testing in the laboratory The soils encountered in the
exploration are presented on the Test Pit Logs Plates A 1 and A 2
Refraction seismography is based on the transmission velocities of
compression waves through various geologic materials Generally the
denser or more consolidated a material is the faster the transmission
velocity The compression waves are refracted at the interfaces between
materials and then received by surface geophones and recorded by the
seismograph The recorded data is down loaded to a portable computer for
processing either in the field or stored for later processing
The techniques will yield approximate depths or thickness of
materials below the survey area and give their approximate compression
wave velocities These velocities may be empirically related to the
excavation characteristics of the materials The following legend
explains the terms shown on the Seismic Refraction Survey Results Plates
