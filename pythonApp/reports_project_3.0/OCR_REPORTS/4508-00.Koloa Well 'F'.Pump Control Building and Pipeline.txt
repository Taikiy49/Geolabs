for the proposed Koloa Well F Pump Control Building and Pipeline project located in the
Koloa area on the Island of Kauai Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project
site are shown on the Project Location Map Piate 7
This report summarizes our findings based on the field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the
proposed project These recommendations are intended for the design of building
foundations retaining walls site grading pavements and water lines only and are subject
to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lava from the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene Epoch
more than 2 million years before present Following the cessation of this main shield
building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lava of the
post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the alluvial sediments
of the Palikea Formation
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series are generally characterized as thick flows of dense
basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various locales
Associated with the vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low cinder cones at
the vent Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of
Kauai
During the Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental glaciers
advanced and accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available
to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Kauai remained essentially stable
during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower relative to the

present sea level on Kauai Most of the soils in the project area are residual and saprolitic
soils derived from the in situ weathering of igneous rocks and alluvial soils deposited from
streams

Our field exploration generally encountered medium stiff to hard residual and alluvial
soils extending to depths of about 7 to 20 feet below the existing ground surface The
residual and alluvial soils were underlain by very stiff saprolitic soils extending to depths of
about 20 feet below the existing ground surface The saprolitic soils were underlain by soft
to medium hard basalt formation extending to the maximum depths explored of about

Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we recommend
that the pump control building be supported on a concrete slab on grade with thickened edge

footings Based on our laboratory tests the soils encountered at the project site exhibit a
high expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations To reduce the potential
for future distress to the slabs on grade foundation resulting from shrinking and swelling of
these clayey soils we recommend that the subgrade soils below the slab concrete and
footings be capped with at least 24 inches of aggregate subbase The aggregate subbase

Building Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we recommend
that concrete slabs on grade with thickened edge footings be used to support the new pump
control building Based on our laboratory tests the project site is generally underlain by
near surface clayey soils with a high expansion potential when subjected to moisture
fluctuations To reduce the potential for future distress to the slabs on grade resulting from
shrinking and swelling of these clayey soils due to changes in the moisture content we
recommend that the subgrade soils below the slabs be properly prepared and capped with
at least 24 inches of aggregate subbase The aggregate subbase course should extend a
minimum of 12 inches laterally beyond the perimeter of the slab on grade foundation In
addition the aggregate subbase should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction
An ailowabie bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot psf may be
used for the design of footings bearing on the compacted aggregate subbase or the on site
for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces Because the pump
control building slab will be subjected to equipment operation the 24 inch compacted
GEOLABS INC

to vibrations
Foundations should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent
finished grade Foundations located next to utility trenches and or easements should be
embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of
the utility trench or embedded to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement
Soft loose materials encountered at the slab or footing subgrade level should be
over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation should be
backfilled with fill materials with a maximum particle size of 3 inches moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction
lf the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate that total settlements of footings may be on the order of
about 1 inch We estimate differential settlements between adjacent footings to be on the
order of about 0 5 inches
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of

on site soils Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an

that surface water runoff should be diverted away from the foundations

to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs be extended a minimum of 2 inches
below the subgrade soils to reduce the potential for migration of excessive landscape water
into the pavement section Alternatively a subdrain system is recommended to collect the
excess water from landscaping irrigation For long term performance we believe that a
subdrain system should be considered adjacent to paved landscaped areas
Drainage
As indicated previously the on site soils exhibit a high expansion potential when
subjected to moisture fluctuations Therefore the importance of good surface drainage
should be emphasized The finished grades outside the new structure should be sloped to
shed surface water runoff away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential
for ponding It is also recommended that gutter systems be installed around the new
structure and that the discharge be diverted away from the foundation and slab areas
Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should be avoided Planters
next to foundations should aiso be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce
the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils
The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab
edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration

maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the foundations and slabs
