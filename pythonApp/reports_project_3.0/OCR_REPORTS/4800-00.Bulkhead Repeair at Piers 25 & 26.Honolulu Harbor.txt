SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered surface fills consisting of loose to
medium dense silty sands from the ground surface extending down to about 8 feet below
the ground surface The surface fills were generally underlain by medium dense to dense
silty sands and coral fragments extending to the top of the coral formation Soft to medium
hard coral formation was generally encountered from about 13 to 15 feet below the ground
surface extending to the maximum depth explored of about 26 5 feet below the ground
surface Groundwater was encountered at depths of approximately 6 3 to 7 8 feet below
the ground level at the time of our field exploration The groundwater levels encountered
in the borings generally correspond to between Elevation 0 2 and 1 7 feet MLLW
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we believe that a new sheet pile
system may be used to repair the deteriorated bulkhead at Piers 25 and 26 Based on the
relatively shallow retention depth of the bulkhead we recommend that a cantilever sheet
pile bulkhead be used for the replacement bulkhead In general the bulkhead should be
designed to resist the lateral earth pressures due to adjacent soils and surcharge effects
Therefore the sheet pile bulkhead should be designed for lateral earth pressures due to
the in situ soils which include the new fill the existing fills coralline deposits and coral
formation
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges line loads and point loads within a
horizontal distance equal to the depth of the sheet pile bulkhead should also be
considered in the design Due to the presence of medium hard coral formation starting at
depths of about 13 to 15 feet below the existing ground surface we believe that predrilling
along the sheet pile bulkhead alignment will likely be necessary to facilitate installation of
the sheet piles through the coral formation and other obstructions
Where required backfill below the groundwater level should consist of free draining
granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation wrapped in
a non woven filter fabric Mirafi 180N or equivalent In general the free draining granular
backfill should extend a minimum of 12 inches above the groundwater level
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific
recommendations for design of the project

SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Bulkhead Repairs at Piers 25 26 project located within Honolulu Harbor
on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the Bulkhead Repairs at
Piers 25 26 project These recommendations are intended for the design of the new sheet
pile bulkhead only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to
the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The project site is located at the existing Piers 25 and 26 of Honolulu Harbor on
the Island of Oahu Hawaii We understand that it is desired to repair the existing
concrete bulkhead system due to significant deterioration Based on the information
provided the backfill material behind portions of the existing bulkhead has eroded away
leaving void spaces in the subsurface We also understand that the concrete pavement
behind the concrete bulkhead has cracked and or collapsed at various locations
The limits of the bulkhead repair project will extend approximately 300 lineal feet
along Pier 25 and about 650 lineal feet along Pier 26 We understand that a new sheet
pile system will be installed behind the existing concrete bulkhead to retain the backfill
materials The existing concrete bulkhead deteriorated will be abandoned in place
Based on the topographic map provided the existing grades at the pier deck level
vary between about 7 and 8 feet Mean Lower Low Water MLLW We understand
that the existing concrete pavement extending about 10 feet laterally behind the bulkhead
along Piers 25 and 26 will be demolished prior to installation of the new sheet pile
bulkhead system The backfill behind the bulkhead will be recompacted and the surface
will be paved with asphaltic concrete We anticipate that earthwork consisting of minor

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion
incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series
The project site is located on the coastal plain of Southern Oahu The coastal
plain was built on the eroded flanks of the Koolau Volcano which forms the eastern
two thirds of the Island of Oahu The coastal plain was built by extensive accumulation of
alluvium derived from erosion of the volcano interbedded with coral reefs and associated
deposits
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation Most of the coastal plains were developed during the
Pleistocene Epoch when the sea levels fluctuated significantly As the glaciers grew and
advanced less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell
below the present stands of the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess
of water became available such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
The project site is generally underlain by deposits of calcareous sediments and
alluvium The deposition of soils at the site is likely associated with the nearby Nuuanu
Stream lagoonal deposits from Honolulu Harbor and coralline formation at greater

2 2 Existing Site Conditions
The project site is located at Piers 25 and 26 of Honolulu Harbor on the Island of
Oahu Hawaii The Bulkhead Repairs at Piers 25 26 project site is located to the south of
Nimitz Highway as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The existing Piers 25 and 26 are generally open and level Based on the

concrete bulkhead Numerous cracks and collapse of the concrete pavement were also
observed at the ground surface along the concrete bulkhead

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered surface fills consisting of loose to
medium dense silty sands from the ground surface extending down to about 8 feet below
the ground surface The surface fills were generally underlain by medium dense to dense
silty sands and coral fragments overlying coral formation Soft to medium hard coral
formation was generally encountered from about 13 to 15 feet below the ground surface
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 26 5 feet below the ground surface
Groundwater was encountered at depths of about 6 3 to 7 8 feet below the ground level at
the time of our field exploration
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we believe that a new sheet pile
system may be used to repair the deteriorated bulkhead at Piers 25 and 26 The new
sheet pile bulkhead should be designed to resist the lateral earth pressures due to
adjacent soils and surcharge effects In general we recommend that the sheet pile
bulkhead be designed for lateral earth pressures due to the in situ soils which include the
new fill the existing fills coralline deposits and coral formation Surcharge stresses due
to areal surcharges line loads and point loads within a horizontal distance equal to the
depth of the sheet pile bulkhead should also be considered in the design
Due to the presence of soft to medium hard coral formation from about 13 to
15 feet below the existing ground surface we believe that predrilling along the alignment
of the sheet pile bulkhead will likely be necessary to facilitate the installation of the new
sheet piles through the coral formation and other obstructions Detailed discussion of
these items and our geotechnical engineering recommendations for design are presented
in the following sections of this report
3 1 Sheet Pile Bulkhead
Based on the available information sheet piles will be used for the bulkhead
repair The new sheet pile bulkheads along the piers will serve two purposes 1 to
provide lateral support for the existing and new fills and 2 to maintain stability of the soil
mass Design of the sheet pile bulkhead will need to consider both overturning stability
W O INC Page 7
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
and overall slope stability Geotechnical design parameters to evaluate these stability
conditions are presented in the following sections The overturning stability will need to
be evaluated by the design engineer We have evaluated the overall slope stability for
the proposed sheet pile bulkhead and the results are presented in a subsequent
subsection of the report
Lateral Earth Pressures
Due to the relatively shallow retained depth of the new bulkhead we envision that
the new bulkhead will likely consist of a cantilevered sheet pile bulknead system In
general the bulkhead should be designed to resist the lateral earth pressures due
to the adjacent soils and surcharge effects Therefore we recommend that the new
sheet pile bulknead be designed for lateral earth pressures due to the in situ soils
which include the new fill the existing fills coralline deposits and coral formation
The recommended lateral earth pressures for design of the new sheet pile
bulkhead are provided on the apparent lateral earth pressure diagram presented on
Plate 3
We understand that a row of concrete piles currently provides lateral support to the
existing concrete bulkhead Due to the uncertainty regarding the lengths and
lateral capacities of the existing concrete piles we recommend that the lateral
resistance from the existing concrete piles be discounted in the design of the new
sheet pile bulkhead Therefore the new sheet pile bulkhead will need to be
extended to a depth to be self supporting
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges line loads and point loads within a
horizontal distance equal to the depth of the sheet pile bulkhead should be
considered in the design For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded
side of the bulkhead a rectangular distribution with uniform pressure equal to
31 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure acting on the entire height of the
bulkhead may be used in design Additional analyses during design may be
needed to evaluate the surcharge effects of point loads and line loads

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Unbalanced Hydraulic Pressures
For waterfront structures such as sheet pile supported piers the maximum lateral
pressures may be expected when the tide is at its lowest level A receding tide
may cause higher water levels behind the sheet pile than in front of it depending
on the type of soils behind the sheet piles The high water level behind the sheet
pile wall will introduce additional seepage pressure due to hydrostatic load For
soils encountered at the project site a hydrostatic pressure estimated based on
the maximum tide difference conservative may be used for the sheet pile design
Overall Slope Stability
Based on the anticipated sheet pile tip elevation of about 12 feet MLLW we have
evaluated the overall slope stability of the proposed sheet pile bulkhead Slope
stability analyses consisting of two dimensional slope cross sections using
Bishop s simplified method of limit equilibrium analysis were performed on the new
sheet pile bulkhead Based on our analyses the factor of safety for the sheet pile
bulkhead is at least 1 5 which is the minimum factor of safety normally
recommended
Corrosion Protection
Due to the exposure to salt water we envision that the new sheet pile bulkheads
would be subjected to a highly corrosive environment Therefore we recommend
that metallic substructures be designed for protection against potential corrosion
Consideration may be given to the use of other types of sheet piles such as vinyl
composite or other types of non corrosive materials in lieu of the conventional
steel sheet piles If a steel sheet pile system is elected for the project a corrosion
engineer should be consulted for detailed recommendations pertaining to
corrosion protection of the steel sheet pile bulkhead
Construction Considerations
As mentioned previously the borings generally indicated that soft to medium hard
coral formation is present at the site from about 13 to 15 feet below the existing
ground surface We envision that the tip elevation of the new sheet pile bulkhead

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared Vegetation old pavements debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to reduce
the potential for contamination of the excavated materials
After clearing finished subgrades in cut areas and areas designated to receive fills
should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative

compaction Yielding areas loose areas or voids disclosed during the clearing and
scarification operations should be backfilled with well compacted fill materials
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Fills and Backfills
In general we believe that the excavated on site soils may be re used as a source
of general fill or backfill material at the site provided that the materials are free of
vegetation deleterious materials and clay lumps and rock fragments greater than
3 inches in maximum dimension Due to the relatively shallow groundwater level
at the site the excavated on site soils may require some aeration to reduce the
moisture content of the soils prior to being re used as backfill materials
Where imported material is required as fill or backfill the material should consist
of non expansive select granular material such as crushed coral basaltic gravel or
cinder sand Imported materials should be well graded from coarse to fine with no
particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between
10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The imported fill material
should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum
swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883 Imported
materials should be tested for conformance with these recommendations prior to
delivery to the project site for the intended use
Where backfilling below the groundwater level is required we recommend that the
backfill consist of free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation wrapped in a non woven filter fabric Mirafi 180N or
equivalent The free draining granular backfill should extend to at least 12 inches
above the groundwater level Backfill above the free draining granular backfill may
consist of compacted on site soils or select granular materials

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
In general fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in
loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density Compaction of the fill and backfill
materials should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other
types of acceptable compaction equipment

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
four test borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 extending to depths of about
25 2 to 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface The test borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our personnel who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Core samples of the consolidated coralline materials encountered at the site were
obtained by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM
Standard Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a
rotary drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the consolidated coralline formation
The consolidated coralline material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically

Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate
sample depths
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core
run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The
results of the test are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depth Graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plate B 1
Two sieve analysis tests ASTM C 117 and C 136 were performed on selected
samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in
soil classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on
Plate B 2
