INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of a preliminary geotechnical
engineering investigation performed for the proposed Maintenance Hangar
at the Naval Air Station Cubi Point in the Republic of the Philippines
The general location of the project site is shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
PURPOSE AND SCOPE OF WORK
The purpose of our investigation was to evaluate the subsurface
conditions at the site to provide pertinent soil data such as shear
strength compressibility and soil classification in order to assess the
Suitability of the project site and the effect on existing buildings and
airfield pavements of the proposed 500 000 cubic yards of fill in the
Site

The design of the hangar foundation and parking apron pavement were
not included in our scope of work since the final location and design
details are not available at this time
The scope of the investigation was performed in general accordance
with our proposal dated and included a review of available
aerial photographs and old construction drawings field reconnaissance
drilling and sampling of nine 9 borings laboratory soils testing
engineering analysis of the field and laboratory data and report
preparation
The boring logs and the laboratory test results are presented in the
appendices of the report

SITE CONDITIONS
General
The project site is located on the western edge of the Cubi Point
Naval Air Station It is bordered by the South Taxiway on the north
San Bernardino Road and existing hangars on the east and the Triboa Bay
on the south and west
Most of the site is relatively flat and approximately 40 feet lower
than the adjacent hangar and taxiway area
At present the site is used as a recreational facility with picnic
tables a snack bar driveway and parking lot
There is an existing concrete sea wall along the western side of the
Site It is in a deteriorated condition and has fallen over in places
The existing ground elevation along the lower beach area is
approximately 7 feet
Geology
From available geologic literature the site is located within the
Western Volcanic Intrusive Province of Luzon and is composed of Pliocene
to Quaternary volcanic deposits At the project site the volcanic
deposits are primarily of pyroclastic origin and consist of lithic tuffs
believed to have been generated by explosive eruptions of Mount Natib to
the south

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
In general the field investigation and engineering analysis
indicate that from a geotechnical engineering standpoint the site is
suitable for the proposed fill If properly constructed the fill could
provide adequate support for hangar foundations and aircraft parking
apron pavements
Future investigations should be directed to the subsurface
conditions along the offshore and shoreline areas
Liquefaction Potential
When a loose saturated sand deposit is subject to vibration such as
during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume
causing an increase in pore water pressure If drainage is unable to
occur rapidly enough due to large extent of sand deposit to dissipate
the build up in pore pressure the effective stress internal strength
of the soil is reduced Under sustained vibration the pore water
pressure build up could equal the overburden pressure essentially
GEOLABS HAWAII
MILCON P 517
W O 1289 00 Page 8
reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to act as a
viscous fluid This phenomenon is called liquefaction The major
factors affecting the liquefaction characteristics of a soil are
1 Grain size distribution Fine and uniform sands are more prone
to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2 Initial relative density Loose sands are most susceptible to
liquefaction Liquefaction potential decreases with increased
relative density
3 Magnitude and duration of vibration Liquefaction potential
increases with increases in the magnitude and duration of the
earthquake
The present beach area is underlain by loose to medium density sand
and gravel The thickness of this layer varies from about 10 feet
inland to 26 feet near the shoreline area
A preliminary liquefaction analysis was performed using a simplified
empirical method developed by Seed and Idriss 1971 The analysis used
seismic data from a draft report on the Earthquake Hazard Reduction
Program Naval Facilities PWC Subic Bay P I Phase 1 by S K
Takahashi and C V Chelapati indicating a maximum earthquake magnitude
of 7 6 and a peak ground acceleration value of 0 25g following the
NAVFAC seismic criteria of 80 probability of not being exceeded in 50
years

MILCON P 517
The liquefaction analysis indicates that after site filling the
loose sand situated below the water table and deeper than 15 feet below
the existing ground surface may be susceptible to liquefaction during an
earthquake with a maximum ground acceleration of 0 25g This critical
soil condition could occur along the shoreline areas as indicated by
Boring 1 Away from the shore and 9 indicates that the
thickness of the sand and gravel layer may be less than 15 feet and
therefore may be less susceptible to liquef action
Liquefaction of the underlying sands could cause very serious damage
to structural improvements situated over the area therefore
densification of the loose sands along the shore area is recommended to
minimize the liquefaction potential In order to achieve densification
of the sands below 15 foot depth special deep compaction methods such
as those listed below may be considered
a Vibrating Probe A 30 inch 0 D open ended pipe pile is
vibrated by a vibratory pile driver into the loose sands causing
densification of the sands
b Vibroflotation A special crane suspended cylindrical
penetrator vibroflot is advanced into the loose sand by a
combination of vibration and water jetting When the vibroflot
reaches the desired densification depth the water jet is closed
and the vibroflot is withdrawn with vibration while sand or
gravel backfill is dumped into the hole from the ground surface
GEOLABS HAWAII
MILCON P 517
W O 1289 00 Page 10
c Dynamic Consolidation Heavy Tamping Heavy weights ranging
from 10 to 40 tons are dropped from a height of 50 to 130 feet
causing localized liquefaction and subsequent settlement of the
loose sands
d Compaction Piles Displacement piles are driven into the ground
at close spacing Granular backfill is then introduced into the
hole and compacted as the pile is withdrawn
The method used would depend primarily on the cost effectiveness of
each alternative and equipment availability
Based on the present subsurface information we estimate that the
area within about 150 feet from the present shoreline would require
densification Additional subsurface information is required along the
shoreline and offshore areas to determine whether densification is
required and if so the estimated depths of densification
Settlement
Ground settlements are anticipated due to the weight of the
substantial amount of fill planned Settlements would occur due to 1
compression of the fill itself 2 compression of the existing loose to
medium dense sand and gravel layer and 3 compression of the underlying
saprolite and tuff formation We anticipate that these settlements
would primarily involve elastic compression and therefore would occur
rapidly Large long term settlements are not anticipated

Assuming that the thicker deposits of loose sand and gravel under
the beach area will be densified to minimize liquefaction potential
estimated total settlements due to 43 feet of 111 would be about 12 to
18 inches in the central portion of the embankment area The settlement
would be reduced to about 6 inches at the edge of the embankment area
Ground settlements in the adjacent hangar and runway areas would
occur primarily due to compression of the saprolite and tuff formation
Estimated ground settlements 20 feet away from the edge of fill are
about 2 to 3 inches The ground settlements would reduce rapidly away
from the edge of the embankment area and therefore ground settlements
at the existing taxiway and hangar areas are anticipated to be minimal
Based on the laboratory test results we anticipate that 90 of the
settlements would occur within about 2 months after the embankment is
completed
We recommend that settlement gauges be installed both prior to
filling and after completion of filling to monitor the actual
settlement rates to determine when construction of improvements can
commence Settlement gauges could consist of conventional settlement
platforms which are monitored by the surveyors level readings A
significant disadvantage of this method is that the gauges are highly
susceptible to damage by the contractor s equipment during filling
operation This generally results in disruption of the settlement

Slope Stability and Wave Protection
The critical section for slope stability is the fill slope condition
at the seaward end of the proposed embankment The Slope stability will
be governed by the shear strengths of the compacted 111 and of the
underlying soils below the embankment Therefore additional
topographic and subsoil information is required in the offshore area to
evaluate the slope stability of the proposed embankment
For preliminary design and cost estimate purposes we anticipate
that 2 horizontal to 1 vertical 2 1 fill slope ratios may be used If
loose sands or soft soils are encountered in the offshore areas special
stabilization methods such as densification or removal and replacement
will be required In order to increase the slope stability it may be
advantageous to provide a large bench at about mid height of the slope
to accommodate the relocated San Bernardino Road
The toe of 111 along the shoreline will need to be protected from
wave action At present most of the shores in the Cubi Point area are
protected by dumped riprap with larger rocks ranging 2 to 4 feet in size
GEOLABS HAWAII
MILCON P 517

The fill material to be generated from the P 516 project site will
generally break down after compaction to fine sand or silt sizes which
are moderate to highly erodible Therefore an adequate filter
protection will be required between the fill and riprap Such a
protection may consist of filter fabric or graded filter rocks It
appears that a filter fabric may have an economic advantage over graded
filter rock layers
In general we believe that the quality of weathered tuff rock from
the Cubi Point area may not be suitable for armor riprap Therefore
imported rocks may be required The riprap protection blanket would
extend at least 8 feet below the lowest low tide DM 7 1 or into
competent rock

Site Preparation
At the onset of earthwork the areas to receive fill should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All existing vegetation
structures debris rubbish and any other unsuitable materials
should be removed and disposed of properly off site
The 111 site should be proofrolled with a heavy vibratory roller
with a minimum weight of in order to densify the near
surface sands and locate soft spots
GEOLABS HAWAII
MILCON P 517
W O 1289 00 Page 14
Existing foundations and structures could become obstacles for
future deep foundations and therefore should be removed and the
resulting depressions backfilled with compacted fill as specificed
in the following section Any soft or weak areas disclosed during
the clearing operations should be removed and properly repaired by
backfilling with compacted fill
Following the clearing and grubbing all areas designated to receive
ill should be scarified to a depth of 6 inches and compacted to a
minimum of 90 of the maximum dry density established by ASTM
D 1557 78
Fill Material
The saprolite and weathered tuff soils generated from the P 516
project excavation are generally suitable as fill material to be
placed above the water level
Where practical the better quality fill materials such as the
highly to moderately weathered tuff materials should be used within
the top 3 feet below the finished grade to provide a higher strength
subgrade condition which would allow thinner pavement sections for
the subsequent overlying aircraft pavements

material generally less than 12 inches in size and with less than
10 fines passing the No 200 sieve

Additional Investigation
Additional subsurface investigations should be conducted in the
offshore areas to determine the subsoil conditions and the necessary
procedures to provide a competent base for the new fill
Offshore soundings are necessary to obtain an accurate ground

Additional data or studies regarding wave heights currents and
other ocean characteristics at the site would be helpful in the design

evaluation of soil properties The results of the moisture content

through B 3 and plotted on Plates B 4 through B 9

evaluate its settlement characteristics The curves are presented on
Plates B 10 through B 14
Five triaxial shear tests unconsolidated undrained were
performed on saturated samples of the various soil types to evaluate
the shear strength parameters of the soils The test results are
presented on Plates B 15 through B 18
