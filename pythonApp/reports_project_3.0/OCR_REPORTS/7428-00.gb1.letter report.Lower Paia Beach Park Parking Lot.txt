PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project involves the reconstruction of the existing gravel-surfaced parking lot at 
the Lower Paia Beach Park.  We anticipate that the parking lot will be paved with asphaltic 
concrete. The parking lot will consist of 53 standard parking stalls, two standard accessible 
parking stalls, and one van accessible parking stall.  Based on the existing grades at the 
site, we anticipate that minor site grading work will be required to achieve the finished 
grades.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
Maui, the second largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago, was formed by the 
activity of two volcanoes, East Maui or Haleakala, and West Maui. The broad, gently 
sloping plain connecting the two volcanoes, known as the Maui Isthmus, was formed when 
lavas of the Haleakala Volcano banked against the already existing West Maui Volcano. 
The East Maui Volcano, better known as the Haleakala Mountain, makes up the 
eastern portion of the Island of Maui. Haleakala Mountain is considered to be dormant with 
the last eruption taking place on the southerly side of the mountain around 1790. 
Haleakala Mountain consists largely of the Honomanu Volcanic Series that built the

The volcanic rocks of the West Maui Volcano have been divided into three volcanic 
series. The oldest Wailuku Volcanic Series is comprised of basaltic lava flows and 
associated pyroclastic and intrusive rocks, which built the major shield volcano. The 
Wailuku Volcanic Series was covered by a thin, discontinuous “frosting” of andesitic and 
trachytic flows, domes, and pyroclastic deposits known as the Honolua Volcanic Series. 
After a long period of inactivity, where erosion incised deep valleys into the volcanic flows, 
volcanic activity returned with eruptions that produced the flows and cones of the Lahaina 
Volcanic Series. 
The isthmus was built by the deposition of sediments over the underlying basalts of 
the two volcanoes. During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fell to stands lower than the 
present sea level, exposing large areas of coralline sands on what was the ocean bottom. 
Wind action transported these coralline sands inland, forming extensive areas of sand 
dunes. The project site is located on the Maui Isthmus and is underlain by sand dune 
deposits. 
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
The site is at the Lower Paia Beach Park in the Paia area on the Island of Maui, 
Hawaii.  The site is on the north side of Hana Highway. The site is shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2.  
The majority of the site is an existing gravel-surface parking lot that is generally 
level.  The western end, about 100 feet wide, is generally undeveloped and covered with 
vegetation consisting of shrubs and tall grass.  The existing ground surface elevations at 
the gravel-surfaced parking area ranges from about +8 to +9 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). 
The existing ground surface elevations at the western end of the site within the 
undeveloped area ranges from about +8 to +10 feet MSL.   
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Our field exploration at the site generally encountered a thin layer of fill over alluvial 
and beach deposits extending to the maximum depths explored of about 11.5 to 12 feet 
below the existing ground surface.  The fill layer was generally about 1.5 feet thick or less 
and consisted of a 4 to 5-inch thick layer of gravel underlain by medium dense silty sand 
and medium stiff sandy silt. In one of the borings, a 1-inch thick asphaltic concrete layer 
was encountered at the surface. 
Below the fill layer, alluvial deposits consisting of medium stiff clayey silt were 
encountered to depths of about 1.5 to 2.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  The 
alluvial deposits were underlain by beach deposits consisting of loose to medium dense 
sand extending to the maximum depth explored of about 12 feet below the existing ground 
surface.

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the information provided, the geotechnical design considerations include 
site grading and pavements.  Detailed discussions and recommendations for design of site 
grading and pavements are presented in the following sections. 
Site Grading 
We believe that only minor site grading work will be required to achieve the finished 
grades at the site.  In general, areas within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly 
cleared at the on-set of earthwork.  Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site.  Soft, loose, and 
yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to receive fill or future 
improvements should be over-excavated to expose firm or dense material, and the 
resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fill.  The excavated soft, 
loose, and/or organic soils should be properly disposed of off-site.  In addition, contract 
documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over-excavation and 
compacted fill placement to account for variation in the over-excavation quantities. 
After clearing, the areas to receive fill and finished subgrades in cut areas should 
be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above 
the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction.  The compaction requirement for the finished subgrades in areas subjected to 
vehicular traffic should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of 
the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. 
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density.   
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement 
weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft and/or loose areas and 
replacement with well-compacted fill.  A Geolabs representative should evaluate the need 
for over-excavation in the field.

In general, the excavated on-site soils may be re-used as a source of general fill or 
backfill materials, provided that they are free of deleterious materials (vegetation) and are 
screened of particles greater than 3 inches in largest dimension.  Imported fill materials 
needed for the site filling should consist of non-expansive granular materials, such as 
crushed coral or basalt.  The materials should be well graded from coarse to fine with 
particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 
30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve.  The materials should have a laboratory 
CBR value of 20 or more and a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in 
accordance with ASTM D1883. Aggregate base course and aggregate subbase materials 
required for the flexible pavement section should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates 
and should conform to Sections 30 and 31 of the Standard Specifications for Public Works 
Construction (September 1986). Geolabs should test the imported fill materials for 
conformance with these recommendations.  
Fills and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, 
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  The top 12 inches of fill 
placed in areas subjected to vehicular traffic should be compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction.  In general, compaction should be accomplished by 
sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or other similar types of acceptable compaction 
equipment. 
Cut and Fill Slopes 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration, we believe 
that planned cut slopes will expose fill and alluvial deposits. In general, planned cut slopes 
may be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. 
Permanent fill slopes constructed using the on-site materials (or imported fill 
materials) may also be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. Fills placed on 
slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide 
stability of the new fill against sliding. The filling operations should start at the lowest point 
and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill 
placement recommendations. Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting 
back to the design slope ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. In addition, slope 
planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the 
finished slopes. 
Cut on fill slopes composed of the on-site beach sand deposit should be designed 
with a slope inclination of 3H:1V or flatter to remove the potential for long-term raveling of 
the fine sand slopes. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor the earthwork operations to observe 
whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation process, and to 
confirm whether the exposed soil conditions are similar to those encountered in our 
exploration.

Pavements 
Design traffic information and data was not available for the preparation of this 
report.  In general, we anticipate that the vehicle loading for the parking lot under 
consideration for this project would primarily consist of passenger vehicles with some light 
pick-up trucks and occasional heavy trucks only.  
We have assumed that the pavement subgrade will consist of the on-site silty soils. 
Based on the laboratory tests conducted, the CBR values of the on-site soils ranged 
between 9.2 and 37.0. A design CBR value of 9 was used in the analysis. We assumed a 
design life of 20 years and designed the pavement based on the 1993 AASHTO Guide for 
Design of Pavement Structures. Based on these assumptions, we recommend using the 
following flexible pavement design section for the new parking lot at Lower Paia Beach 
Park. 
Flexible Pavement  
  2.0-Inch Asphaltic Concrete  
  6.0-Inch Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction) 
  8.0-Inch Aggregate Subbase Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction) 
16.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness Over Compacted Subgrade  
The existing subgrade soils under the new pavement structural section should be 
scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to near the optimum 
moisture, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. 
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density. 
The aggregate base course and aggregate subbase materials should consist of 
crushed basalt aggregate compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction. CBR 
and field density tests should be performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered 
during construction to confirm the adequacy of the above sections. 
Paved areas should be sloped, and drainage gradients should be maintained to 
carry surface water off-site. Surface water ponding should not be allowed on-site during or 
after construction. Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or adjacent to 
the pavement areas, we recommend extending the curbs a minimum of 2 inches into the 
soils below the aggregate base course layer to reduce the potential for migration of 
landscape water into the pavement section. Alternatively, a subdrain system could be 
constructed to collect the excess water from landscaping irrigation. For long-term 
performance, we recommend constructing a subdrain system adjacent to the 
paved/landscaped areas.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling five 
borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5. The borings extended to depths of about 
11.5 to 12 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.   Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-5. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The tests results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
One one-inch Ring Swell test was performed on a relatively undisturbed sample of 
the near-surface soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the soils. The test results are 
summarized on Plate B-1. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits for soil classification. The test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.  Graphic 
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-2. 
Two Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 and C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification.  Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-3. 
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) tests (ASTM D1883) were 
performed on the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-4 through B-6.
