INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering reconnaissance
performed for the lao Stream Hydraulic Design project located in the Wailuku area on the
Island of Maui Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the
Project Location Map and Site Plan Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and preliminary geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field reconnaissance and engineering analyses These
recommendations are intended for the preliminary design of channel repair foundations of
the retaining walls and site preparation only The findings and recommendations presented
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The purpose of this project is to review channel stability and levee undermining
problems in lao Stream which is in the vicinity of the District of Wailuku on the Island of
Maui Hawaii shown on the Project Location Map and Site Plan Plate 1 This project
consists of a feasibility level hydraulic design for the lao Stream Flood Control Project
The existing flood contro channel in lao Stream was constructed in 1981 The
project will connect the existing concrete channel at about Station No 94 00 to a concrete
channel near Waiehu Beach Road at about Station No 22 00 a distance of approximately
7 200 feet The work includes the evaluation of a channel and bank protection design
developed by the U S Army Corps of Engineers Committee on Channel Stabilization The
evaluation includes determining the thickness stability and constructibility of the invert and
bank lining
The lao Stream has a drainage basin of about 10 square miles and is subjected to
relatively short duration high intensity rainfall and significantly large sediment loads The
maximum discharge of the stream experienced is about 4 100 cubic feet per second cfs
Theoretical engineering analysis indicated that average channel velocity through the
natural portion of the channel ranges between about 8 and 32 feet per second fps with
average velocities in excess of 20 fps at most places in the natural reach

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Maui is the second largest of the Hawaiian Islands and encompasses
approximately 728 square miles of land area The Island of Maui was formed by two
volcanic mountains Haleakala of East Maui and Puu Kukui of West Maui A broad low
elevation plain the Maui Isthmus was formed by the coalescence of the lava flows emitted
from Haleakala Volcanic activity began within the two mountains during the Pleistocene
Epoch and generally ceased by the Holocene Epoch During the Pleistocene Epoch the
island experienced episodes of partial submergence and emergence related to the last
significant ice Age sea level fluctuations During the Pleistocene Epoch when significantly
lower sea levels occurred the Island of Maui was connected as a single landmass to the
Islands of Molokai Lanai and Kahoolawe
Deeply eroded valleys spreading outward concentrically from the central higher
elevations incise the West Maui Mountains One of the larger valleys lao Valley drains
toward the east and northeast from the summit of Puu Kukui The West Maui Mountains
generally consist of inclined layers of basaltic lava flows from the Wailuku Honolua and
Lahaina Volcanic Series progressing from oldest to youngest in age A relatively long
period of erosion and sediment deposition occurred following the Honolua Volcanic Series
during which time thick sequences of sedimentary conglomerate were deposited along the
base of the mountains The lavas of the younger Lahaina Volcanic Series erupted on top
of and interstratified with the sedimentary deposits
Haleakala Voicano comprises the eastern half of the Island of Maui and generally
consists of layered basaltic lava flows from the Honomanu Kula and Hana Volcanic
Series progressing from oldest to youngest The southwestern portion of Haleakala is
believed to have erupted with lava flows as recent as about 1790

The lao Stream project site is located at the mouth of lao Valley in the vicinity of
Wailuku iao Valley is a large V shaped erosional valley that was deeply incised into the
rocks that filled the original caldera of the West Maui Mountains The lao Stream is the
primary perennial drainage feature of lao Valley The portion of lao Stream located within
the project limits dissects the recent and older alluvial and fluvial deposits that comprise

Isthmus The boulder deposits encountered in the lao Stream channel are of fluvial origin

Slope Lining and Levee Slope Repair
Alternative construction methods for slope and bottom protection including grouted
riprap and rolled compacted concrete RCC were considered for the project The following
sections present construction requirements and our engineering analysis
Grouted Riprap
Grouted riprap consists of stone bed and slope protection having voids filled with
grout or concrete to form a veneer of cementitious bonded aggregate armor
Components of the grouted riprap system include
e stable and properly prepared slope
e free draining sub base or bedding layer
e protection layer consisting of sound durable stone bonded by a
mixture of cementitious materials water aggregates and admixtures
Granular filter and sub base materials geotextiles sub drains weep holes cutoffs
and other special features are also included as needed
Grouted riprap is widely used in the following situations
an economical alternative to conventional riprap treatment where
required stone size cannot be economically produced
e repairing conventional riprap which has been damaged as a
consequence of being subjected to water velocities exceeding design
values
However extreme caution is advised to ensure that the stone displacement is
indeed velocity related and not the result of slope or foundation failure
Grouted riprap must be used only on properly designed slopes It must be
recognized that grouted riprap will crack and river side slopes of levees should not
GEOLABS INC
W O 4440 00 Page 12
be protected with grouted riprap Applying grout to salvage a failing conventional
riprap treatment without proper design to address the cause of the failure should not
be undertaken
Grouted riprap treatment requires special attention to the design of stable slopes
edge and toe protections sub base pressure relief and drainage stone size and
gradation stone quality and grout design
Stability of the materials to be protected by grouted riprap controls the design of
slope geometry in the same manner as it would for conventional riprap protection
Grouted riprap is generally considered to be a rigid structure but does not possess
significant strength to bridge sizeable voids or withstand uplift pressures Therefore
foundation support is critical
Sub base materials are required for grouted riprap to reduce the amount of grout
penetration and to protect geotextiles where necessary Typically a minimum of
6 inches of granular material is necessary under the grouted riprap to provide
adequate drainage interception and control The filter can consist of well graded
granular material or a uniformly graded granular material with an underlying
geotextile The filter snould be designed to provide a high degree of permeability
while preventing in situ soils or sediment deposition from penetrating the filter
causing clogging and failure Care must also be taken to insure that grout does not
penetrate through the overlying riprap stone to such an extent as to clog the filter
Pressure relief holes should be provided in grouted riprap and extended through the
grout into the underlying sub base
The top size of the grouted riprap stone may be smaller than that required for
conventional riprap One common design procedure is to evaluate the stone size
requirements for conventional riprap and determine availability and cost If the
required size of stone is not readily available or is cost prohibitive the maximum
GEOLABS INC
W O 4440 00 Page 13
size of economically available stone may be used if grout is applied The stone
should have less than 5 percent passing a 2 inch sieve in order to achieve
maximum grout penetration Grouted riprap may allow the use of non quarried
appropriately sized rock such as stream run gravel and cobbles in place of
crushed rock It is important that the stone is clean free of debris and satisfies the
gradation and stone characteristics requirements

Rolled Compacted Concrete RCC
Rolled Compacted Concrete RCC is defined as concrete compacted by roller
compaction a process for compacting concrete using a roller often a vibrating
roller
RCC may be considered for application where no slump concrete can be
transported placed and compacted using earth and rock fill construction
equipment Ideal RCC projects will involve large placement areas with little or no
reinforcement It may be considered in lieu of gabions or riprap for bank protection
especially in those areas where riprap is scarce
The properties of hardened RCC are similar to those of conventionally placed mass
concrete CMC The differences are lower void content due to lower water content
in RCC As with CMC tensile strength is generally the principal concern for design
Compressive strength for RCC is measured from cylinders fabricated as with the
GEOLABS INC
W O 4440 00 Page 15
size of the specimens determined by using conventional practice with respect to
aggregate size For well compacted RCC mixtures the influences of type of
cementitious materials and aggregate quality to the compressive strength are similar
to those for CMC RCC with high quality aggregates will produce compressive
strength equal to that of conventional concrete
The RCC production plant location is often located in the upstream reservoir A
location near the aggregate stockpiles is advantageous to minimize the
transportation of aggregate from stockpiles to the plant The plant must be
accessible and provide the required staging area for trucks hauling cementitous
materials in order to extend and continue operation during the production of RCC
at moderate to high production rates
For projects where the RCC volume is small and lower production is tolerable a
front end loader can be used as the aggregate feed system And a mass batch
system and a traditional mixer such as a rotating drum mixer have been
successfully used in the production and mixed RCC Uniformity of the mixing
operation is critical in producing good quality RCC
The selection of a transportation system for RCC is an integral part of the design
package The following systems can be used in the different situations
e Conveyor systems can be used for large scale project to transport RCC and
conventional concrete from the mixer to the placement area
e End dump trucks can also be used for large scale project
e Front end used where the haul distance is short
However care must be taken to prevent tracking dirt and contamination onto the
placing site and damage from vehicles turning on the lift surface
Lift thickness can vary depending on mixture proportions plant and transport
capacity placement rates spreading and compacting procedures whether or not
a bedding layer is used and size of placement area RCC should be spread to
provide a uniform surface capable of uniform compaction Each lift is compacted

Combinations of various sized dozers have been used to efficiently place RCC at

Retaining Structure Foundations
in general foundations may be designed based on an allowable bearing pressure
of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot psf for footings bearing on relatively
stiff dense alluvium consisting of silty and sandy soils and or compacted structural
fills This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
Foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded a minimum depth of
30 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade For sloping ground conditions
the footing should be embedded deeper to obtain a minimum 6 foot set back
distance measured horizontally from the outside edge of the footing to the face of
the slope Footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should be
constructed in stepped footings

Construction Considerations
The building close to the wall may need to be underpinned to avoid damage during
construction Conceptually the underpinning could consist of small diameter pipe
piles This would greatly reduce the potential for building damage and allow the
contractor more flexibility in the wall construction The wall should be constructed
in limited sections at a time to reduce the potential for a slope failure Some
provisions would also be required to temporarily protect this area from stream
damage during the construction period The underpinning would also reduce the
permanent loads on the retaining wall by transferring the building loads below the
wall foundation
Drainage
Retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the
retaining structure discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes Backfill
behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of general fill materials less than
3 inches in maximum dimension Unless covered by concrete slabs the upper
12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively impervious material on site soils
to reduce the potential for water infiltration behind the retaining structures
