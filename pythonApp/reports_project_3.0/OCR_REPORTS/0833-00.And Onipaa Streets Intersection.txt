INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of a soils engineering
investigation at the above referenced Manaiki Stream flood
control project
The stream improvements will consist of either reinforced
concrete rectangular channel lining roughly from 20 to 30
feet wide approximately 10 to 13 feet deep or concrete rubble
masonry retaining walls up to 13 feet high The improvements
extend about 2270 feet long down the existing Manaiki Stream
bed

The spacing of construction or expansion joints is not
yet available The unused width of flood control right
of way may later be developed as bikeway park playground
etc Approximately 610 feet of partially lined channel
mauka from Pineapple Place bridge will also be upgraded
PURPOSE SCOPE
The purpose of the investigation is to determine subsurface
conditions at selected adjacent locations accessible to
truck mounted drilling equipment develop soils informa
tion between borings by geologic reconnaissance along the
exposed channel bed and provide recommendations for design
of walls and slabs
The scope of subsurface exploration consisted of six
borings drilled at various accessible locations near the
length of the stream with sampling standard penetration
tests water level measurements and a geologic reconnaissance
of the existing Manaiki Stream bed and vicinity Laboratory
testing was performed on representative samples to help
provide the basis for our investigation and analyses This
report summarizes our findings and recommendations
The approximate boring locations are shown on Plate 1 and
the boring logs are presented in Appendix A Information
regarding the field explorations and laboratory testing
are also presented in the Appendices

SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is located in an existing residential
development A flood control right of way approximately
60 to 70 feet wide extends makai from Ala Mahamoe Street
bridge in between Mahiole and Onipaa Streets to the lined
channel east of Pineapple Place The existing invert
elevation of Manaiki Stream presently decreases from
71 to 25 feet along the right of way corresponding to
an average gradient of 2 percent The average gradient
of the lined channel mauka of Pineapple Place bridge is
approximately 1 5 percent The mauka stream channel near
Mahamoe Street is partially confined between existing
concrete rubble masonry walls Many gravel cobbles and
boulders are exposed in the present stream bed Some of
the boulders are large slabs of rock from six to eight
feet across
GEOLOGY
The ancestral Manaiki valley was eroded into the flank
of the Koolau Mountains which are composed of numerous
basalt flows The valley was then submerged after
emergence and local deposition erosion has continued
The upper portion of the Manaiki Stream is underlain by
alluvium or older valley fill which was deposited before
prolonged submergence
GEOLABS HAWAII
W P 4
Continuing erosion and deposition after emergence have
resulted in the lower portion of Manaiki Stream being
underlain by materials classified as recent alluvium
or flood channel deposits Since recent alluvium is
essentially reworked older alluvium any differences
between these two units are relatively slight through
out the project area The maximum size of boulders
generally decreases from two to four feet near Ala
Mahamoe Street bridge to one half to one foot near
the Mahiole Onipaa intersection
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Over 36 feet of brown yellowish brown and gray clayey
silt layers of silty sand cobbles and boulders were
encountered in the exploratory borings Intermediate
layers of coarse gravel cobbles and boulders were
encountered Boring 6 These alluvial deposits are
medium stiff and medium dense to dense The underlying
basalt was not encountered in the borings which extended
well below the proposed invert level
The groundwater was encountered in the borings at depths
ranging from 12 to 23 feet below the top of stream bank
These water levels correspond roughly to a couple of feet
above present stream grade mauka to one to two feet
below invert makai It is anticipated that water
levels will fluctuated depending upon climatic variations

CONCLUSIONS RECOMMENDATIONS
Based upon the results of field investigations laboratory
testing and engineering analyses the following conclusions
and recommendations are presented
The concrete rectangular channel may be supported at grade
or the concrete rubble masonry retaining walls may be supported
on continuous shallow foundations The foundation subgrade
should consist of undisturbed natural soils or compacted fill
Active pressures on walls below grade will depend upon water
levels type of backfill and relative compaction proximity
to existing concrete rubble masonry walls and existing concrete
linings that are not removed
The cobbles boulders and existing concrete structures will
make required excavations more difficult the silty soil is
readily excavatable using conventional earthmoving equipment
Dewatering may be accomplished by pumping from sumps The
on site silt soils less cobbles and boulders larger than
6 inches will generally be suitable materials for compacted
fill and backfill
Grading
Any soft wet soils and other soils contaminated by dele
terious materials should be excavated and removed to
designated spoil areas located outside and beyond a 1 1
gradient extending upward from the bottom of walls below
grade
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 833 00 P 6
Low design active pressures might be obtained by using
only select granular or sandy imported backfill It is
anticipated that backfill will consist of silty soils
Similar to on site soils For the design values recommended
in the following section Active Pressures the silty soils
should be used for the backfill material behind the channel
walls The backfill material should be fairly well graded
less than 12 inches in maximum size and have a plasticity
index less than 25
After excavating soft spots on site silty soils should
be placed in maximum nine inch thick lifts and compacted
to 90 percent relative compaction using ASTM Designation
D 1557 70 Modified Proctor as the standard
Although 90 percent compaction is recommended for the
above foundation subgrade only 85 percent compaction is
recommended for the wall backfill in the active zone behind
walls below future finish grade to reduce the build up of
lateral pressures Also heavy compaction equipment should
not be used in compacting backfill behind walls Back
filling and compacting behind the walls below grade is
recommended to minimize settlements of future construction
and to prevent dumping loose fill immediately behind walls
if no backfill were placed

Excavation
Due to the right of way being approximately twice as
wide as the proposed concrete rectangular channel it
is anticipated that excavations generally will be sloped
to prevent caving A slope of 1 1 is recommended for
temporary cuts of from 5 to 13 feet high In the event
the required space is not availabe sheeting and bracing
should be used
Since the construction passes through a residential
development structures adjacent to and near the top
of excavations should be continuously monitored for
cracks or any other indications of possible lateral
movements
Dewatering
Although the soil matrix is generally fine grained silt
it is anticipated that shallow excavations less than
5 feet deep may be conventionally dewatered by pumping
from sumps Dewatering should be performed without
pumping out appreciable soil fines pumping clear water
only Dewatering should also be coordinated with exca
vations sloping or supported so that stability is not
jeopardized

Weepholes should be provided in the channel walls and
Similar provisions such as pop outs should be provided
in the channel slabs to minimize uplift of build up of
hydrostatic pressures
Bearing Capacity Settlements
A net allowable bearing value of up to 2000 pounds per
square foot may be used for design of the channel slab A
six inch layer of coarse sand and gravel for uniform bedding
is recommended immediately underlying the concrete slab
Foundations of free standing concrete rubble masonry walls
may be designed for 2000 pounds per square foot with an
increase of 1 3 at the edge toe pressure to resist lateral
loads Continuous foundations should extend at least 1 5
feet below lowest adjacent grade
The settlement predictions in this type of deposit are very
difficult due to the large variability of the subsoil The
estimated settlements would be small if fill is compacted as
recommended in the section on Grading For the above recomn
mendations the estimated settlements are less than 3 4 to 14
inches depending upon the actually imposed soil pressures
Active Pressures
Walls below grade may be designed to resist an active earth
pressure equivalent to 40 pounds per cubic foot above water
level and 90 pounds per cubic foot below water

The effects of future nearby development should be
accounted for by adding one to two feet imaginary
backfill above walls Any structures such as concrete
rubble masonry walls above a 1 1 plane extending up
from the bottom of walls below grade would add extra
surcharge and increase the active earth pressures
recommended herein The amount of increase in hori
zontal earth pressure may be determined after the
affected locations have been established
We have recommended weepholes be provided through the
channel walls to reduce the buildup of hydrostatic
pressures
CONSTRUCTION MONITORING
Field inspection and engineering observation during the
project construction are important to permit correlation
between the soil data obtained during this investigation
and the actual soil conditions encountered during con
struction
Backfilling operations should be inspected by the soils
engineer and density tests should be taken to verify
that adequate compaction density has been obtained

APPENDIX A
FIELD EXPLORATION
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by
drilling six borings in accessible areas using truck mounted
rotary drilling equipment with continuous flight solid stem
4 inch auger Coring through boulders was performed using an
NX core barrel The borings were drilled to depths ranging
from 32 to 36 feet below the existing ground surface Undis
turbed and disturbed samples were obtained and standard pene
tration tests were performed
The soils encountered in the borings were classified by
visual and textural examination in the field by our engineering
geologist who continuously monitored the drilling operations
The classifications were reviewed by visual inspection and
testing in the laboratory The soils were classified in
general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System A graphic presentation of the soils is presented
on the Boring Logs Plates A 1 through A 6
In the borings soil samples were obtained by driving
split barrel ring sampler The sampler and standard pene
tration tests were driven using a standard 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches The blow counts used to drive the sampler
the last 12 inches are shown on the boring logs at the appro
priate depths
