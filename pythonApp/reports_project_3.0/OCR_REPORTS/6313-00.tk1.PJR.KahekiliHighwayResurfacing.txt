Introduction
This pavement justification report presents the results of our geotechnical
engineering evaluation and analyses performed for the Kahekili Highway Resurfacing
Piihana Road to Waiehu Beach Road project in Wailuku on the Island of Maui Hawaii
The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for
the rehabilitation and reconstruction of the existing pavements only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

Project Considerations
The roadway resurfacing project is along Kahekili Highway between about
Piihana Road and Waiehu Beach Road in the Wailuku area on the Island of Maui
Hawaii The approximate limits of the roadway resurfacing project are shown on the
General Site Plan Plate 2
The existing roadway within the project limits is a two lane undivided roadway
measuring approximately 1 7 miles Based on the information provided we understand

distress amounts to more than 15 percent of the total pavement area proposed for
rehabilitation the new pavement section will need to be designed based on a 20 year

Regional Geology
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes the older West Maui
Tertiary Epoch and the more recent East Maui also known as Haleakala Pleistocene
Epoch The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow gently sloping plain located between these
two volcanoes The project site is along the south and southwest flanks of West Maui
The volcanic rocks of the West Maui have been divided into three volcanic
series The Wailuku Volcanic Series is the oldest and is comprised of the basaltic lava
flows and associated pyroclastic and intrusive rocks that built the major shield volcano
The Wailuku Volcanic Series was covered by a thin discontinuous frosting of andesitic
and trachytic flows domes and pyroclastic deposits known as the Honolua Volcanic
Series After a long period of inactivity when erosion incised deep valleys into the
volcanic flows volcanic activity returned with the eruptions that produced the flows and
cones of the Lahaina Volcanic Series
In situ chemical weathering of the lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to
2 million years The weathering process has formed a mantle of residual and saprolitic
soils In general saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is typical of the
tropical weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation
with increased depth
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock mass from the slopes and movement of the material
downslope toward the axis of the valley by gravity to form colluvial deposits Once the
colluvium reaches streams in the valley alluvial processes become dominant and the
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium Large areas of alluvium can be
found in the lowland areas and bordering streams
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes
begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the
materials Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the
coarse grained portion of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or

Existing Site Pavement Conditions
The existing Kahekili Highway within the project limits is generally a two lane
undivided roadway except for the additional turn lanes at the intersection areas The
approximate limits for this roadway resurfacing project generally begin at the
intersection of North Market Street Piihana Road and Mokuhao Road in the south and
extend for approximately 1 7 miles towards the north and terminate at the intersection
with Waiehu Beach Road as shown on the General Site Plan Plate 2 Based on our
field observations and the information provided the traffic volume along this portion of
the roadway may be considered low with the average daily traffic count on the order of
about 4 400 vehicles both directions in the Year 2010
Along the east side of the roadway alignment a small 2 foot wide by 1 5 foot
deep trapezoidal ditch runs parallel with the roadway throughout the project limits The
4 foot wide shoulder areas beyond the edge of pavement generally consist of short cut
grass and utility posts The surface soils along the shoulder areas below the vegetation
generally consist of brown sandy silts to dark brown silty clays with gravel The existing
Kahekili Highway roadway alignment generally slopes down from south to north with
the exception of the southernmost portion of the roadway for approximately 500 feet
where the roadway alignment slopes up from south to north

consideration Detailed descriptions of our field surveys are presented in the following

Methodology of Pavement Design
In general the type and severity of the distresses exhibited in the pavement
areas would determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction of the existing
pavements should be performed Existing pavements with minor to moderate distresses
and severity are generally rehabilitated with an AC overlay with cold planing In the
method outlined for AC overlays the strength of the existing AC is evaluated based on
the type and severity of the distresses observed The existing AC may be reduced to
that of asphalt concrete base ACB Option 1 Untreated Base UTB Option 2 or
remain unchanged Option 3
The procedures used in determining the new pavement section follow those
described in Chapter 3 of the Pavement Design Manual dated March 2002 The
pavement design manual was prepared by the State of Hawaii Department of
Transportation Highways Division Material Testing and Research Branch The design
procedures for flexible pavements are based on the Hveem Stabilometer method
developed by the California Department of Transportation Caltrans

Surface Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due
to saturation of the subbase and base course and or subgrade soils Therefore the
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be
maintained to carry all surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage
structures Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction
Development of good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in
the drainage structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement
deterioration or premature failure of the pavements

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway alignment by drilling and
sampling seven borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 7 The borings extended to
depths of about 6 to 6 5 feet below the existing pavement surface The approximate boring
locations are shown on the Site Plans Plates 3 1 through 3 8 The borings were drilled
using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous full time basis These classifications were further reviewed visually
and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with
ASTM D 2487 Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 7
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some samples
were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D 1586
Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths The
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows
required for the specific sampler type used The blow counts may need to be factored to
obtain the Standard Penetration Test SPT blow counts
