In accordance with your request we made a visit to the project site on August 9
1991 to make observations of Burial Sites T 124 and D 21 18 These observations were
made for the purpose of developing geotechnical engineering recommendations
pertaining to the establishment of construction buffer zones around the subject burial
sites
This report summarizes our findings and recommendations
General Site Description
The Regent Kona Coast project site is situated in the District of North Kona on the
island of Hawaii Most of the site is raw undeveloped land composed of late Pleistocene
to Holocene lava flows originating from the northwest rift zone of Hualalai In the area of
the subject burial sites the lava flows are relatively fresh and have not developed any soil

Site T 124
This site is an elongated lava cavity with dimensions of about 160 feet in length and
a width of about 30 feet The maximum interior height of the cavity is about three 3 feet
Access to the burial cave is through a hole in the roof of the cavity
Geomorphologically the cavity is a large blister underlying a pressure ridge in a
pahoehoe lava flow Pressure ridges form when the front of the advancing flow slows
with relation to the body of the flow At the point the skin of lava flow is generally in a
plastic state rather than being a molten fluid The differential pressure causes this plastic
crust to buckle and arch upward often forming a cavity or blister under the crust
The roof of the T 124 cave consists of low ridge of very slightly weathered basalt
The thickness of the rock in the roof appears to vary between about 20 and 40 inches
The surface shows a irregular jointing pattern with joints spaced at about three 3 to five
5 feet apart The interior of the roof shows a joint spacing with a separation of about
five 5 feet The joints generally tend along the axis of the cavity
Some minor natural rock falis were noted inside of the cave Based on the
appearance of these rock falls it is believed that these falls are the result of spalling of
the roof along natural joints and that they are possibly the result of thermal expansion and
contraction of the rock due to heating and cooling during the day

Site D 21 18
This site is a dome shaped lava cavity that is roughly circular in plan view with a
diameter of about 20 to 25 feet The maximum interior height of the cavity is about five
5 feet Access to the burial cave is through a hole in the side of the dome
Geomorphologically the cavity is a blister underlying a dome shaped pressure
ridge or tumulus in a pahoehoe lava flow
The roof of the D 20 18 cave consists of low but steeply sided dome of very slightly

Discussions and Recommendations
General
It should be noted and understood that the collapse of lava cavities is part of the
natural geological weathering process Therefore it is not possible to prevent the caves
from collapsing on a geologic time scale without developing an elaborate internal shoring
and bracing system However it is our opinion that it is feasible on a real time basis to
reduce the potential of damage to the sites resulting from construction activities The
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2753 00 Page 4
primary construction related mechanisms which could cause damage would be collapse
due to excessive vibration and crushing of the roofs by equipment or earth loads
it is our understanding that the project archeologist has established a temporary
buffer zone of 100 feet from a portion of the caves It is our opinion that this large of a
buffer is conservative
In general it is our opinion that the roofs of both caves are stable under natural
conditions Although the rock in the roofs of both caves is jointed it appears the rock
is held together in a fashion similar to a keystone arch It should be noted that the caves
have withstood several seismic events or earthquakes within the past 30 years in which
the magnitude of the event exceeded 5 0 on the Richter Scale
Vibrations
Vibrations cause damage to geologic and cultural features through acceleration of
the structural elements of the feature or the development of resonant frequencies in the
structural elements of the feature Construction related damages are generally the result
of accelerations
Construction activities which can generate large scale vibrations are pile driving
impact excavation such as impact rippers hoerams or jack hammers blasting and
vibratory compaction of soils Generally pile driving and blasting create the largest
accelerations and have the greatest potential to cause damages
Since the Regent Kona Coast project site is underlain by competent rock formation
which can support large structural loads without the use of pile foundations there is no
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2753 00 Page 5
foreseeable reason to expect the use of pile driving equipment in the vicinity of the burial
sites
It is foreseeable that some blasting may be required within the Regent Kona Coast
project limits to facilitate excavation in the rock formation Site T 124 is situated in an area
which is lower that the proposed finished grade therefore no excavation activities will be
required in the vicinity of this site Site D 20 18 is situated in an area where no grading
is planned therefore no excavation activities will be required in the vicinity of this site
With respect to blasting activities in other portions of the development area it is
possible to scale the blasting agent charges versus the distance to a cultural feature to
limit the peak accelerations which will reach the feature
Blasting Considerations
When an explosive detonates below ground it generates intense stress waves in
the surrounding material These waves crushes the rock immediately adjacent to
the explosion and permanently distorts and cracks the rock for some distance
When the intensity of the stress waves attenuate to the point that there is no longer
any permanent deformation of the rock the stress is propagated through the rock
as elastic waves These elastic waves create ground vibration
The potential of the ground vibration to damage a structure is a function of the
intensity of the vibration and the natural frequency of the structure According to
the Du Pont Blaster s Handbook a formula has been developed to relate the
intensity of ground vibration at a given point in terms of particle displacement
GEOLABS HAWAII

between the explosion and the point of interest
The equation to determine the peak particle velocity is in the form of

K is the ground transmission constant which is empirically determined based on
the rock surrounding the explosives and at the point of interest
W is the weight of explosives used
m and n are geologically controlled empirical constants and
R is the distance between the explosion and the point of interest
Work by the U S Bureau of Mines and others indicate that the equation may be
reduced to
V 160 R W where

or more
Blasters use this equation to estimate the peak particle velocity for a given scaled

The air blast from an explosion is a compressional wave in air Noise is the
portion of the air blast with frequencies between 20 and and
concussion is the portion with frequencies below 20 Hz The Du Pont Blaster s
Handbook also sets forth a scaled distance relationship for air blast
The effects of air blast can be a consideration for much greater distances
depending upon atmospheric and or other conditions
If blasting is to be used for the earthwork we recommend that the scaled distances
of the explosive charges be limited to those that will generate peak particle velocities of
about one 1 inch per second or less at either of the burial sites This is less than the
normally accepted criteria of two 2 inches per second Blasting should be performed
in accordance with the applicable statutes rules and regulations and only by a qualified
operator with a proven record
With the exception of impact excavation and vibratory compactors the vibrations
generated by conventional construction equipment are generally not severe enough to
have a potential to damage structures Therefore in order to rationally evaluate a suitable
set back distance for construction equipment traffic in the vicinity of the caves we made
a conservative assumption that the vibrations from construction equipment were
equivalent to vibrations caused by pile driving We then referenced empirically developed
charts relating particle velocity during pile driving versus the distance from the pile driving
Typical charts are attached with this letter and indicate that a distance of about 15 to 25
feet would maintain the particle velocity in the ground less than two 2 inches per
second which is generally considered a safe level Therefore we recommend that no
construction equipment be operated within 25 feet of the burial caves Impact operations
and vibratory compactors should be restricted from operating within a minimum of 50 feet

Crushing Damages
For Site D 20 18 the above recommended 25 foot buffer will prevent crushing
damages since no earthwork is planned in the vicinity of this site However is our
understanding that the proposed grading for the Regent Kona Coast development will
require the placement of some fill over portions of Site T 124
In order to evaluate the strength of the existing roof of the cave the roof was
modeled as a beam with fixed ends thirty 30 feet apart In order to support the
proposed new fills our analysis indicates that the compressive strength of the rock should
be on the order of 4 000 p s i Based on past experience the lava rock often can
develop these strengths
For the case of Site T 124 the minimum 25 foot equipment operation buffer should
be maintained Any work including fill placement in this area should be performed
manually To provide the maximum amount of protection for the burial cave we
recommend that extremely lightweight material be utilized
It is our understanding that the proposed fill is landscaping and will not be used
for structural purposes Therefore we recommend that no compactive effort be applied
to any fill material within the buffer zone
Fill Material
There is a polystyrene foam material manufactured in Hawaii which has been used
on other projects as an extremely lightweight nonstructural fill material This
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2753 00 Page 9
material has a bulk density ranging from 1 0 to 2 0 pounds per cubic foot and is
supplied in rigid blocks that are approximately 12 feet long by 4 feet wide and 2
feet in height By comparison volcanic cinder generally has a dry rodded weight
or bulk density of about 75 pounds per cubic foot The material is easily placed
by hand and may be shaped with chainsaws The rigidity of the material would
help in bridging the cave roof Product literature concerning this material is
presented in Appendix A of this report
Based on an average fill thickness of 5 feet the total additional load imposed on
the site using four 4 feet of polystyrene capped with one 1 foot of cinder would
be on the order of about 80 pounds per square foot as opposed to a load of 375
pounds per square foot for fill composed entirely of cinder Although it is our
opinion that the cave roof may be capable of supporting the cinder fill the use of
the polystyrene will provide a greater factor of safety
In our opinion either the polystyrene or light volcanic cinder would be suitable for
use as fill over the site The final selection will depend upon the economics of
purchase and placement and availability of the material
Fill Placement
If it is elected to utilize the polystyrene material the material should be placed to
the limits of the 25 foot buffer zone The blocks should be placed in alternating
courses i e layers placed at 90 degrees to the previous course The blocks
should be brought to about one 1 foot below the proposed finished grade A
layer of Mirafi 140 filter fabric should be placed over the blocks to minimize the
infiltration of fines through the fill into the underlying cave The polystyrene and
Mirafi should then be capped with one 1 foot of cinder for landscaping purposes

over the site The cinder capping should only be lightly screened with no attempt

the work should be stopped immediately if caving of the roof is noted to allow
reassessment of these recommendations
LIMITATIONS
The recommendations submitted in this report are based in part upon information
obtained from visual observations Variations of conditions may occur and the nature
and extent of these variations may not become evident until construction If variations
then appear evident it will be necessary to re evaluate the recommendations given in this
report
