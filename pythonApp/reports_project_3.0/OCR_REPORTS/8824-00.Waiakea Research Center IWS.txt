Dear Mr. Meyers: 
Geolabs, Inc. is pleased to submit the results of our percolation testing performed 
at the Waiakea Research Station in the District of South Hilo on the Island of Hawaii. This 
letter report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations derived from 
our field percolation testing only. These geotechnical recommendations are intended to 
assist the client in the design of individual wastewater systems (IWSs) at the project site. 
The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted 
at the end of this letter report. 
Project Considerations 
The project site is at the University of Hawaii at Manoa’s Waiakea Research 
Station facility (Tax Map Key [TMK] No.:  2-4-071: 023) in the District of South Hilo on the 
Island of Hawaii.  The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. 
New IWSs with septic tanks and leach fields will be constructed at three locations 
within the facility. Three percolation tests were performed near the proposed leach field 
locations to aid in the design of the IWSs in accordance with the State of Hawaii, 
Department of Health's Administrative Rules, Chapter 11-62-31.2, "Wastewater 
Systems," and Chapter 10 of the Ten States’ Standards to evaluate the infiltration 
characteristics of the subsurface soils for disposal of wastewater effluent.  
Percolation Testing 
Three percolation tests, designated as P-1 through P-3, were performed near the 
proposed leach field locations in general accordance with the procedures outlined by the 
Department of Health for the design of IWSs.  The locations of the percolation tests are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.

The percolation tests were performed in borings drilled, extending to about 5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. Approximately 1 inch of gravel was placed at the 
bottom of the borings to protect the bottom from scouring and sediments. 
During testing, the test holes were filled with water and refilled as necessary. The 
time intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which the 
calculations of the percolation rates were based. 
Based on the percolation tests conducted for P-1 through P-3, the percolation rates 
are about 0.2 to 0.7 minutes per inch at the test elevations. In accordance with the 
"Manual of Septic-Tank Practice" prepared by the United States Department of Health, 
Education, and Welfare, the leach field location with the percolation rate obtained at the 
site would be suitable for siting the absorption system at these locations. The percolation 
test results are presented on Plates 3.1 through 3.3. 
Limitations 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from our percolation testing. Variations of subsoil conditions beyond 
the testing locations may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not 
become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be 
necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations provided herein. 
The percolation test locations indicated herein are approximate, having been taped 
from visible features at the project site shown on the topographic survey map transmitted by 
Okahara & Associates, Inc. on February 22, 2024.  Elevations noted on the percolation test 
forms were obtained from the same plan.  The physical locations and ground elevations of 
the percolation tests should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the 
methods used. 
This letter report has been prepared for the exclusive use of Okahara & Associates, 
Inc. and their client, the University of Hawaii at Manoa, for the specific application for the 
design of the new IWS at the project site as described herein in accordance with generally 
accepted geotechnical engineering principles and practices. No warranty is expressed or 
implied. 
The owner/client should be aware that unanticipated soil conditions are commonly 
encountered. Unforeseen soil conditions, such as perched groundwater, soft deposits, 
hard layers or cavities, may occur in localized areas and may require additional probing 
or corrections in the field (which may result in construction delays) to attain a properly 
constructed project. Therefore, a sufficient contingency fund is recommended to 
accommodate these possible extra costs.
