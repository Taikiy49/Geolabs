As requested, we conducted a field exploration and laboratory testing program in
support of the design of the future building structure and parking lot planned at the
Building D Pad within the Kapolei Commons development in the Kapolei area on the
Island of Oahu, Hawaii.
Project Considerations
The Building D Pad is within the Kapolei Commons development in the Kapolei
area in the District of Ewa on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The Building D Pad is situated
adjacent to Kalaeloa Boulevard at the east side of the retail development.
Based on the information provided, we understand the new building will be
constructed at the existing building pad prepared during the mass grading operations for
the Kapolei Commons development. Due to some slab movements observed at a few of
the Phase 1 buildings, we understand there are concerns that similar type issues could
affect the new building planned at the Building D Pad in the future.
Therefore, it is desired to conduct a drilling and soil sampling program at the
Building D Pad location. The information obtained would be used to analyze the specific
soil conditions that currently underlie this area for expansive characteristics. In addition,
the information will be used to evaluate the structural integrity of the existing building
pad and to formulate geotechnical recommendations for the parking lot design.

Field Exploration and Laboratory Testing Program
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling nine borings,
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 9, extending to depths of about 4.3 to 5 feet below
the existing building pad surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the
Site Plan, Plate 1.
The materials encountered in the drilled borings were classified by visual and
textural examination in the field by our geologist, who observed the drilling operations
on a near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed by visual
observation and laboratory testing. Soils were classified in general conformance with
the Unified Soil Classification System, as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A.
Graphic representations of the materials encountered in the drilled borings are
presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-9.
In addition to the field exploration work, samples of the coralline capping fill and
underlying clayey soils encountered in the drilled borings were collected and transported to
our laboratory for testing. Laboratory moisture content, unit weight, Atterberg Limits,
gradation, and ring swell tests were performed on selected soil samples collected, and the
test results are presented in the Logs of Borings and on Plates B-1 through B-3 of this
letter report.
Adequacy of Current Building Slab Design
As previously mentioned, some slab movements have been observed at a few of
the Phase 1 buildings within the Kapolei Commons development. Therefore, it is desired
to evaluate the adequacy of the current building slab design at the Building D pad based
on the information obtained from our field exploration and laboratory testing program.
In our geotechnical engineering report entitled “Geotechnical Engineering
Exploration, Kapolei Commons, Kapolei, Oahu, Hawaii” dated December 18, 2006
prepared for the Kapolei Commons development, we indicated that the surface clay soils
across the development site exhibit moderate to high shrink/swell characteristics when
subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents. Therefore, we recommended
providing at least 18 inches of non-expansive select granular fill materials below the
building slabs.
In order to evaluate the adequacy of this recommended building slab design based
on the site-specific subsurface soil conditions at the Building D Pad, we focused our field
exploration and laboratory testing program on the following aspects:

Expansive Characteristics of the Underlying Clay Soils
The expansive characteristics of the on-site clay soils underlying the Building D pad
play a major role in determining the adequacy of the current 18-inch non-expansive
capping fill requirement for the Building D slab. In order to evaluate the expansive
characteristics of the underlying clay soils, Atterberg Limits and one-inch ring swell tests
were performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration program. The
ring swell test results are presented on Plate B-1, and the Atterberg Limits test results are
presented on Plate B-2.
The clay soils underlying the Building D pad generally are classified as Sandy Clay
(CL) and Silty Clay (CH). Laboratory tests performed on the clayey soils yielded a ring
swell of mostly less than 3 percent, with the exception in Boring No. 7, which exhibited
swell values up to about 9 percent, indicating that the underlying clay soils exhibit a low to
moderate potential for shrinking and swelling when subjected to soil moisture fluctuations.
Based on the test results, it appears that highly expansive clays were not encountered at
the Building D Pad location. Therefore, we believe the current 18-inch thick non-expansive
capping fill layer recommended for the retail development should be adequate for the
Building D slab design.
Thicknesses of In-Place Non-Expansive Capping Fill

materials has been placed above the on-site clay soils at the Building D pad location,

on our field observations and measurements, it appears that the existing Building D pad is
underlain by non-expansive select granular fill materials, consisting of tannish brown silty
coralline sand with gravel (coralline capping fill) in a dense to very dense state within the
upper 2 feet. The thickness of the non-expansive capping fill measured at each boring

Structural Integrity of Existing Building Pad
As previously mentioned, it is also desired to evaluate the structural integrity of the
existing building pad. In order to evaluate this potential, we focused our geotechnical
engineering evaluation on the following aspects:
° Compaction of the Coralline Capping Fill
° Compaction of the Underlying Clay Soils
In accordance with the afore-mentioned geotechnical engineering report and the
project plans and specifications, the non-expansive capping fill and the underlying clayey
subgrade soils should be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of
the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557.
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the
maximum dry density.
The relative compaction of the coralline capping fill and underlying clayey soils
were evaluated by comparing the in-situ dry density of the material to its maximum dry
density (proctor value). Results of the relative compaction of the soil samples obtained at
each depth increment are summarized in the following table.

Based on the test results shown in the above table, it appears that the
compaction of the capping fill material (below the top 2 feet) and underlying clayey soils
has been compromised (with the majority ranging between about 60 and 88 percent
relative compaction). However, the test results indicated that the upper 2 feet of the
capping fill material generally exists in a dense to very dense state, possibly resulting
from the application of “Soil Cement” treatment at the building pad surface. These
results also are consistent with the relatively high blow counts needed to drive the
sampler through the in-situ soils during our field exploration work.
In addition, we understand the site grading contractor (JUS Construction, Inc.)
has expressed some difficulty in scraping the near-surface coralline capping material
within the parking lot areas. Based on these findings and our evaluation, we believe the
densely compacted capping fill material encountered in the top 2 feet within the building
pad should be adequate to support the anticipated floor loads and foot traffic. Therefore,
we recommend implementing the following procedures during the re-grading work of the
Building D pad.
The existing capping fill material should be over-excavated, where necessary, to
the bottom of the building slab cushion elevation. The over-excavated coralline capping
material should be stockpiled separately for reuse as a source of select granular fill for
the building pad construction. The over-excavated subgrade and areas to be raised to
the finished subgrade should be proof-rolled and recompacted to at least 90 percent
relative compaction. Select granular fill material to be placed on top of the proof-rolled
subgrade to the new finished pad elevation for the Building D Pad should be
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts of about
8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction.
We understand the design finished grades of the parking lot area will need to be
lowered by approximately 6 inches. Based on the borings drilled at the parking lot area, it
appears that the capping fill thickness ranges from about 24 to 60 inches. Therefore, we
envision the pavement structure will be underlain by at least 18 inches of capping fill

material, even with the lower new design finished grades. For the re-grading work at the
parking lot area, we recommend the existing capping fill material be over-excavated to the
bottom of the base course layer. The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a depth
of about 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.
As previously mentioned, it appears that the compaction of some portion of the
capping fill material (below the top 2 feet) and underlying clayey soils has been
compromised. Based on the anticipated footing bottom elevations, we envision that the
footings for the building structure may bear on these under-compacted materials.
Therefore, we recommend the bottom of the footing excavations be recompacted to at
least 90 percent relative compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing
surface prior to the placement of reinforcing steel and/or concrete.
If soft and/or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing
excavations, they should be over-excavated to expose the underlying firm materials. The
over-excavation may be backfilled with the on-site soils compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction, or the bottom of footing may be extended down to bear
directly on the underlying competent materials. A Geolabs representative should observe
and test the bottom of footing excavations to confirm that the recommended compaction of
the footing subgrades are achieved during footing construction.
Limitations
The findings and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon
information obtained from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and past project
experience in the project vicinity. Variations of subsurface conditions between and beyond
the boring locations may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not
become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, Geolabs
should be contacted to re-evaluate the findings and recommendations presented herein.
The field boring locations are approximate, having been estimated by pacing and
measuring from visible features noted on the Site Plan transmitted by P3 Management
LLC on July 13, 2010. The boring locations should be considered accurate only to the
degree implied by the methods used.
The stratification breaks shown on the Logs of Borings depict the approximate
boundaries between soil and/or rock types and, as such, may denote a gradual
transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown on the
graphic representations and/or presented in the text herein. These data have been
reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this letter report. However, it
must be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in seasonal rainfall and other
factors.
