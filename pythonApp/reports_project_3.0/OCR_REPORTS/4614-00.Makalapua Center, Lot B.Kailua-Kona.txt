REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is situated on the western flank of Hualalai Mountain which makes
up the west central portion of the island The lava formation encountered at the site
GEOLABS INC
W O 4614 00 Page 5
appears to be of both a a and pahoehoe flows which spread and ponded as they
approached the ocean A a lava is typically characterized by a porous rough and irregular
flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and
boulders Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an
internal structure of vesicular porous rock A large portion of the project site appears to
expose pahoehoe lava at the ground surface
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas especially pahoehoe lava
These cavities were formed when the lava was still in a molten state and they represent both
lava tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes are
formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure
which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Blisters and pockets are
generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted through
the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and along
clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they serve as
the major water transmission features

Fills and Backfills
In general we recommend that the fill areas be capped with a minimum 2 foot thick
layer of structural fill material to serve as a choking layer over the larger sized general
fill materials Provision of the 2 foot thick layer of structural fill would also facilitate
construction of the future improvements above the fills
Structural fill materials should consist of well graded granular materials less than
6 inches in maximum dimension with sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of
voids in the compacted mass In general the excavated materials may be re used
as a source of structural fill materials provided that the materials are processed to
meet the maximum size and gradation requirements presented Where appropriate
such as within utility corridors the thickness of the structural fill layer may be
increased to a minimum of 1 foot below the invert elevation of the utilities to facilitate
future construction of the utilities
Fill and backfill materials below the minimum 2 foot thick structural fill layer may
consist of general fill materials General fill and backfill materials should consist of
well graded granular materials the majority of which are less than 12 inches in size

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to
at least 95 percent relative compaction General fills and backfills should be placed
in level lifts not exceeding 18 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction of fill materials should be accomplished using sheepsfoot rollers
vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment
Rock fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of the
majority of the rocks or 5 feet whichever is less Placement should be by haul trucks
traversing previously placed lifts and placing at the edge of the currently placed lift
Spreading of the rock fill should be performed using a large bulldozer to facilitate
seating of the rocks The rock fill should be watered heavily during placement by
water trucks traversing the front of the current rock lift face and spraying water
continuously during placement Compaction equipment such as a Caterpillar D 9
bulldozer or similar heavy compaction equipment providing suitable energy to
achieve the required compaction should be utilized to place rock fills The number of
passes usually six to eight passes to be made to achieve proper compaction should
be determined at the on set of site grading by the performance of a trial compaction
test and or by plate bearing tests Subsequent layers of rock fill above the previously
placed rock fill should provide a choking layer of smaller sized boulder materials to
fill in the voids between the bouiders of the preceding rock fill layer
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of
rocks boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number
of passes by a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should
GEOLABS INC
W O 4614 00 Page 14
be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation
of a representative from Geolabs Based on this testing program the number of
passes may then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction For
preliminary estimating purposes compaction may be accomplished by using a large
vibratory drum roller having a static weight of at least 15 tons traveling at a speed
of about 300 feet per minute The roller should pass over the fill surface at least

Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar size equipment may be used in lieu of the

Foundation Probing
Based on our experience in the area voids and or cavities may be present and are
commonly encountered in the basalt rock formation at the project site To reduce the
potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of cavities below a footing
we recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting be implemented during
construction We recommend that the probing and grouting program be conducted for all
footings located in cut areas and for footings located in fill areas where the fill thickness is
less than 5 feet below the bottom of footing In general it appears that all foundations for
structures located at the northeast portion of the site mauka portion will require probing
and grouting
For estimating purposes we recommend that a probe hole be drilled at each spread
footing location and at 10 feet on center along wall foundations The probe holes should
be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to a depth at least 10 feet below the
planned bottom of footing elevation If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected
during the probing operation additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacing to
aid in delineating the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes
and cavities discovered may be backfilled with controlled low strength material CLSM
also known as flowable fill injected at low to moderate pressures As an alternative
sand cement grout with a slump range of 6 to 9 inches injected at low to moderate
pressures may also be used for the grouting operations
Because of the potential for encountering large cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that the probe drill be available on site until the probing and grouting operations
are complete Therefore it should be noted that a longer lag time between probing grouting
operations and foundation construction might be required in the construction schedule

The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings extending to depths of approximately 15 feet below the existing ground surface
The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings
were drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers

The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a

Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4 of Appendix A
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses
a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit
is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced fractures or
breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would
be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the
following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the Practical
Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
