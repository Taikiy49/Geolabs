SECTION 1.0 - GENERAL
1.1. Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Hapuna Irrigation Well No. 3 project for the Mauna Kea
Resort located in the District of South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii. The general
location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the proposed irrigation
well and pipeline installation project only. These recommendations are intended for the
design of site grading, well pads, pavements, and thrust blocks only. The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report.
1.2 Project Considerations
The project site is located on the mauka side of the existing Mauna Kea Resort
within the District of South Kohala, on the Island of Hawaii. The project consists of
approximately 1,500 lineal feet of access roads and pipeline, and a well site. We
anticipate that the access roads will be paved or covered with gravel. The access road
section may consist of asphaltic concrete pavement with small culverts at the ford
crossings. Details of the proposed structures were not available at the time this report was
prepared. In addition, we anticipate that only concrete pads, such as pads for the
electrical and mechanical equipment, would be constructed at the project site.
Based on the Topographic Survey Map provided, the existing grades at the
project site vary from about +710 to +735 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). In general, we
envision that only minor site grading will be required to provide a relatively level ground
surface at the well project site. However, cuts and fills of up to about 5 feet in height
may be required for the construction of the access road.

SECTION 2.0 - SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2.1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii, the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago, covers an area of
approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes, which include the following: Kohala, which is long extinct; Mauna Kea,
which has had activity during recent geologic time; Hualalai, which last erupted in 1801;
and Mauna Loa and Kilauea, both of which are still active.
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield
Volcano, which has formed the west-central portion of the island. The project site
appears to be underlain by basaltic lava flows of the Hamakua Volcanic Series of
Mauna Kea Shield Volcano, which were deposited during the Pleistocene Epoch
(Stearns and Macdonald, 1946).
The Hamakua Volcanic Series typically is composed of basaltic lava flows, which
are mantled by a relatively thin layer of palagonitized ash, locally referred to as
Pahala Ash. The volcanic ash soil at the project site differs from Pahala Ash found in
other portions of the Island of Hawaii in that the in-situ moisture content is generally
lower, and the soil exists in a dry, friable state. The volcanic ash soils at the site have
been subjected to wind erosion, which has resulted in the formation of a desert
pavement on the surface of the site. This desert pavement is a surface layer of cobbles
and gravel, which were left behind as the finer volcanic ash soils were blown away.
The lava formation at the project site appears to be of a’a and pahoehoe basalt
type flows, which spread and ponded as they approached the ocean. A’a lava are
typically characterized by a porous, rough, and irregular flow surface resembling a
jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders. Typically, a
more dense and layered lava rock material is contained within the lava flow core.
Pahoehoe lava is characterized by a smooth, rope-like or billowy surface and an internal
structure of vesicular (porous) rock. Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe
lavas. These cavities were formed when the lavas were still in a molten state and

2.2 Site Description
The project site is located to the east (mauka) of Queen Ka’ahumanu Highway
within the Mauna Kea Resort in the District of South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii.
The Hapuna Irrigation Well No. 3 site is located approximately 2,500 feet north of the
existing irrigation reservoir. A site plan showing the project location is provided as
Plate 2 of this report.
The project site generally slopes downward from east to west with localized
undulating topography. Based on the Topographic Survey Map, the existing ground
surface elevations vary from about +710 to +735 feet MSL. The existing slopes range
in inclination from near level to as steep as about three horizontal to one vertical
(3H:1V) in localized areas.
Based on our observations, the project site is located on vacant, undeveloped
land situated in a relatively arid climate. At the time of our field exploration, the site was
lightly vegetated with dried wild grasses and bushes, and a scattering of kiawe trees.
Several ravines also were observed at the project site, and pipe culverts were observed
at some of the ravine crossings. At the time of our field exploration, vehicular access to
the Irrigation Well No. 3 project site was made through an existing gravel road.

3.1.1 Site Preparation
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site to
reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials to be used as
embankment fill materials.
Following clearing and grubbing (or excavation to the access road subgrades),
the ground surface should be proof-rolled with a minimum 15-ton vibratory drum
roller for a minimum of eight passes to assist in detecting and possibly collapsing
near-surface cavities. We wish to point out that caution should be exercised by
the contractor while clearing and grading near the existing collapsed features,
which may represent potential lava tubes or cavities that may further collapse
under the load of heavy equipment. The vibratory drum roller used for the
proof-rolling operations should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet per
minute.

road corridor. Therefore, the proof-rolling operations should be conducted prior
to the pipeline installation in order to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes
during the proof-rolling operations.

3.1.2 Fills and Backfills
In general, we recommend that structural fill materials be used to fill the areas from
the existing ground surface to achieve the design subgrades due to the relatively
minor fills (up to 5 feet high) required for the project. Structural fill materials should
consist of well-graded granular materials of 6 inches in largest dimension with
sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass. The
excavated on-site materials may be re-used as a source of structural fill materials,
provided that the materials are processed to meet the maximum particle size and
gradation requirements.
If the excavated on-site materials do not contain sufficient fines to produce a
well-graded material, off-site borrow or on-site rock crushing of large-sized rock
fragments or boulders should be considered to provide the required gradation and
particle sizes to develop a well-graded material. Imported material, if necessary,
should be observed and/or tested by Geolabs for its suitability prior to being
transported to the site for the intended use.
3.1.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in
loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers
to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D 1557. Optimum moisture content is the water content (percentage by weight)

excavators and ripping with large bulldozers. However, some of the deeper
excavations into the dense a’a and/or pahoehoe formations may require the use of
hoe-rams, chipping, or blasting.

3.1.5 Cutand Fill Slopes
Based on our observations and field exploration, it appears that the cut slopes
along the new access road alignment generally would expose clinker and cinder
materials mixed with volcanic ash soils. In general, we believe that the cut slopes
may be designed with a slope inclination of 2.H:1V or flatter. In areas where dense
basalt formation is encountered at the cut slope face, the cut slopes may be
steepened to a slope inclination of 1H:1V or flatter. Large cavities that may be
exposed on the cut slope face should be backfilled and/or grouted, if necessary. A
representative from Geolabs should observe the cut slopes during construction to
evaluate whether the exposed cut slope materials are consistent with our
assumptions.
Permanent fill slopes constructed of the structural fill materials recommended
herein may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. Fills that are to
be placed on existing ground steeper than 5H:1V should be benched. The fill slope
face should be free of boulders (rock fragments greater than 12 inches in largest
dimension) and should be free of voids, which would allow erosion and migration of
fines to occur. In addition, slope planting or other means of slope protection should
be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for significant erosion of the
finished slopes.
Concrete Pads
We envision that concrete pads, such as pads for the electrical and mechanical
equipment, would be constructed at the project site. In general, we recommend that a
minimum 4-inch thick cushion layer of open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67
gradation) be provided below the concrete pad for uniform support. As an alternative,
the concrete pad may be constructed directly on the compacted structural fill subgrade
(in fill areas), provided that the subgrade is properly prepared and compacted. To
reduce the potential for substantial shrinkage cracks in the concrete slabs, crack control
joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the concrete pads with
expansion joints at right-angle intersections.

3.3 Pipelines
We understand that a water pipeline will be installed along the new access road
corridor. It is anticipated that most of the trenches for the pipelines will be excavated in
the clinker materials and the medium hard to hard basalt rock encountered in the
borings drilled for the project. In general, we recommend that a granular bedding layer
consisting of Ginches of open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) be
provided below the pipes for uniform support of the pipes.
Free-draining granular materials, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33,
No. 67 gradation), should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches
above the pipes. It is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the
sides of the pipes. Improper backfill material around the pipes and improper placement
of the backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes.
As an alternative, the granular bedding and the initial trench backfill (up to about
12 inches above the top of the pipes) may consist of 1-inch minus, well-graded granular
materials (aggregate base course). Where used, the 1-inch minus, well-graded
granular bedding and the initial trench backfill should be moisture-conditioned and
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction.
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of well-graded granular
materials less than 6inches in maximum. size. The backfill should be
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in about 12-inch loose lifts,
and mechanically compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction.

Because the new access road may be constructed with or without conventional
asphaltic concrete pavement, we believe that it is imperative that the access road
subgrade be properly prepared.
In addition, the access road surfaces should be sloped, and drainage gradients
should be maintained to carry surface water off the pavements. Surface water ponding
should not be allowed on the site during or after construction.

APPENDIX A

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling six borings designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 6. The borings were
advanced to a maximum depth of about 16 feet below the existing ground surface using
a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with coring tools. The approximate locations of the
borings drilled for the project are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-6.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer
falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586, Penetration Test and
Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
Core samples of the rock formations encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113, Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description.
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.
