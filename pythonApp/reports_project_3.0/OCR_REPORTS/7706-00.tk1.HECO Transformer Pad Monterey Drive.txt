PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located near the intersection of Monterey Drive and Monterey 
Place in the Wilhelmina Rise area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The project 
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
Based on the information provided, we understand that it is proposed to construct a 
new concrete pad for a HECO electrical transformer within the property of 3902 Monterey 
Place.  In addition, a one to two feet high Concrete Masonry Unit (CMU) retaining wall will 
be constructed around the transformer pad to provide the necessary grade separation.  A 
two to three feet high retaining wall is also planned along the lower side of the pad area. 
Due to the limited accessibility of the transformer pad area, we proposed a field 
exploration program consisting of Dynamic Cone Penetration (DCP) testing to evaluate the 
subsurface soil conditions at the project site.  Our experience in the area suggested that 
the project area is generally underlain by surface fills overlying residual and saprolite soils 
at shallow depths.  Special attention was given to the swelling potential and lateral earth 
pressures of the subsoils at the project site for the design of the new transformer pad and 
retaining structures.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions by performing DCP testing at two selected 
locations, designated as DCP-1 and DCP-2, to provide data and information for our 
evaluation and analyses.  The approximate DCP test locations are shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. 
The DCP test provides a measure of a material’s in-situ resistance to penetration. 
The test is performed by driving a metal cone into the ground by repeatedly striking it with 
a 17.6-lb (8-kg) weight dropped from a distance of 22.6 inches (575 mm). The penetration 
of the cone is measured after each blow and is recorded to provide a continuous measure

of shear resistance up to 10.2 feet below the existing ground surface. Test results can be

The DCP tests were generally conducted at 1-inch (25.4-mm) discrete depth

A sample of the near-surface soils was collected for laboratory testing to evaluate 
its swelling potential for the design of the transformer pad. The test results indicate that the 
near-surface clayey soils exhibit moderate shrink/swell characteristics when subjected to 
fluctuations in the soil moisture contents. Results of the laboratory ring swell test are 
presented on Plate B of Appendix B. 
Based on our literature review, field observations, DCP testing, and laboratory test 
results, it appears that the transformer pad site is likely underlain by stiff to very stiff clayey 
soils with moderate swelling potential when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture 
content.     
FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration and laboratory test results, the near-surface clayey 
soils at the new transformer pad area generally exhibit moderate shrink/swell 
characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents. Therefore, we 
recommend providing at least 12 inches of non-expansive select granular fill materials 
below the transformer pad due to the expansive nature of the on-site clayey soils.  
Detailed discussions and recommendations for the design of the transformer pad and 
retaining structures are presented in the following sections. 
Transformer Pad 
Our field exploration and experience in the area indicate that the near-surface 
clayey soils exhibit moderate shrink/swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in 
the soil moisture content. Unless slabs-on-grade constructed above these expansive soils 
are properly designed, there is a potential for future distress to the lightly loaded 
slabs-on-grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of the clayey soils due to changes in 
the soil moisture content. To reduce the potential for appreciable structural distress 
resulting from swelling of the subgrade soils, we recommend properly preparing the 
subgrade soils prior to fill placement. In addition, we recommend providing a minimum 
12 inches of non-expansive select granular fill materials to support the transformer pad. 
After clearing, the cut subgrades for the transformer pad should be scarified to a 
depth of at least 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum 
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557.

Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density. 
The slab subgrade should be kept moist until covered by the select granular fill.  
Where shrinkage cracks develop in the subgrade soil after compaction, we recommend 
preparing the subgrade soil again as recommended above. Saturation and subsequent 
yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require 
over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well-compacted fill. The need for 
over-excavation due to soft subgrade soil conditions should be evaluated in the field by a 
Geolabs representative. 
The 12-inch thick non-expansive, select granular fill required below the transformer 
pad should consist of non-expansive select granular materials, such as crushed coral or 
basalt. The material should be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger 
than 3 inches in largest dimension. The material also should contain between 10 and 
30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. In addition, the material should have a 
laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less 
when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883.  The non-expansive, select granular fill 
materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in level 
lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative 
compaction.  
Retaining Structures 
Based on the information provided, retaining structures less than about 3 feet high 
are planned around the transformer pad for grade separation. Based on the subsurface 
conditions encountered, the following guidelines may be used to design the retaining 
structures. 
Wall Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we believe that 
the retaining walls may be supported on a shallow foundation system bearing on 
the stiff to very stiff clayey soils. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 
3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the design of foundations 
bearing on stiff to very stiff clayey soils.  This bearing value is for dead-plus-live 
loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as those 
caused by wind or seismic forces. 
Bottom of wall footings should be embedded a minimum depth of 24 inches below 
the lowest adjacent finished grade.  Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction of 
the slope should be constructed in stepped footings.  Foundations located next to 
utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary 
plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench, or the bottom of 
footing should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines.  
This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade

Lateral loads acting on the retaining walls may be resisted by frictional resistance 
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by 
passive earth pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation 
system.  A coefficient of friction of 0.3 may be used for footings bearing on the stiff 
to very stiff clayey soils.  Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be 
estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot 
of depth (pcf).  The passive earth pressure value assumes that the soils around 
footings are well-compacted.  Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive 
pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches of the soils should be neglected.

Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges, line loads, and point loads within a

considered in the design. For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded

source of backfill behind the retaining structures. 
Drainage 
Retaining structures should be well-drained to reduce the potential for build-up of 
hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system for site retaining walls would 
consist of 1 cubic foot of permeable material, such as open graded gravel (ASTM 
C33, No. 67 gradation), wrapped with non-woven filter fabric placed at each of 
the weep hole locations. The weep holes should be spaced no more than 6 feet 
apart. 
The backfill from the bottom of the wall to the bottom of the weep hole should 
consist of relatively impervious material to reduce the potential for significant 
water infiltration into the subsurface. In addition, the upper 12 inches of the 
retaining wall backfill should consist of relatively impervious material to reduce 
the potential for significant water infiltration behind the retaining structure unless 
covered by concrete slabs at the surface.

Dynamic Cone Penetration Tests 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the transformer pad area by performing 
two Dynamic Cone Penetration (DCP) tests, designated as DCP-1 and DCP-2, extending 
to depths of about 9.8 and 10.2 feet below the existing ground surface.  The approximate 
locations where DCP tests were performed are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The test 
results are shown on the Dynamic Cone Penetrometer Data Sheet, Plates A-1.1 through 
A-2.7. 
The procedures used in our DCP tests are in general accordance with 
ASTM D6951, Standard Test Method for Use of the Dynamic Cone Penetrometer in 
Shallow Pavement Applications.  The DCP apparatus consists of a 5/8-inch diameter steel 
rod with a 60-degree conical tip. The rod is topped with an anvil that is connected to a 
second steel rod. This rod is used as a guide to allow a 17.6-lb hammer to be repeatedly 
raised and dropped from a height of 22.6 inches. The connection between the two rods 
consists of an anvil to allow for quick connections between the rods and for efficient 
energy transfer from the falling weight to the penetrating rod. 
Once the test apparatus is assembled, the DCP is placed at the test location and 
the initial penetration of the rod is recorded to provide a zeroing scale. While holding the 
rod vertically, the weight is raised to the top of the rod (22.6 inches above the anvil) and 
dropped. The penetration of the rod is measured after each drop. 
In the DCP tests conducted by Geolabs, the same equipment described above was 
used to record the number of blows required to advance the cone in 1-inch (25.4-mm) 
depth increments, as shown in the field logs provided in Appendix A.
