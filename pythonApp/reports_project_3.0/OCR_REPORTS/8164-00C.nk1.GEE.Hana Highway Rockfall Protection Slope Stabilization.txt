SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered residual soil and saprolite generally 
consisting of medium stiff to hard silty clay, sandy silt and clayey silt, and medium dense 
silty sand and boulders extending to depths of about 45 to 80 feet below the existing 
ground surface. Underlying residual and saprolitic soils, the basalt rock formation with 
interbedded layers of clinker were encountered to the maximum depth explored of about 
129.5 feet. The basalt formation was generally closely to slightly fractured, slightly to 
highly weathered, and soft to hard. The clinker layers generally consisted of sandy gravel 
or stiff to hard clayey silt and sandy silt. We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled 
borings at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on our field observations, soil borings, and engineering analyses, we 
recommend vegetation clearing, slope scaling, and installing an anchored wire mesh 
system on the Paia side slopes and the Hana side slopes from approximately Sta. 185+00 
to Sta. 196+00. Rockfall mitigation measures may not be necessary from Sta. 196+00 to 
the eastern project limit at about Sta. 204+00 based on our analyses and the 
considerations presented in the body of this report. However, drainage improvements at 
the top of the slope may be considered to mitigate future erosion. 
Prior to the installation of the anchored wire mesh, the existing slopes should be 
cleared and grubbed, and rock slope scaling should be performed to remove loose rocks 
and precariously positioned rock fragments from the slope faces. Rock slope scaling work 
should be performed by an experienced crew and typically proceeds from the top of the 
slope and works downward in a controlled and orderly manner. The rock slope scaling 
will typically require temporary closure of the roadway during the time that rock is being 
scaled or removed from the slope. We understand that it is desired to have the roadway 
remain open during the slope scaling work. Therefore, the utilization of a movable 
temporary rockfall barrier within the active scaling area may be considered. 
The installation of an anchored wire mesh system on the slope face serves to 
mitigate the movement or detachment of soil and rock material from the slope face in the 
years following the scaling work. In addition, the anchored wire mesh system will help to 
stabilize sections of the existing slope prone to shallow slope failures. Based on our 
evaluation of the slope, we recommend that the grouted soil/rock anchor be a minimum 
of 12 feet long, spaced at 8-foot on center in a staggered pattern. The drilled hole for the 
grouted soil/rock anchor should be a minimum of 6 inches in diameter.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the proposed Hana Highway Rockfall Protection, Milepost 10.18 to 
Milepost 10.49 project in Hana on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project location and 
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our field 
exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the proposed project. These 
recommendations are intended for the design of slope stabilization and rockfall hazard 
mitigation only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the 
limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The proposed rockfall protection/slope stabilization project is along Hana Highway 
between Milepost 10.18 and 10.49 on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. We understand that the 
project site is listed as the second worst rockfall site on the Island of Maui. The project 
involves developing rockfall protection/slope stabilization measures for the project site. 
There are two slope areas at the project site with a valley in between that extends 
down to Maliko Bay. The slope on the Paia side is up to about 130 feet in height, and the 
slope on the Hana side is up to about 80 feet in height. During our site visit, the existing 
slopes were observed to be heavily vegetated with trees and brush. Residual soils, 
saprolite, basalt rock formation, and volcanic clinker were observed on the exposed slope 
face.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
Of interest to our geotechnical engineering analysis are the surface and 
subsurface materials encountered at the project site, the engineering properties of the 
materials encountered, and the variability of the subsurface conditions across the project 
site. Therefore, the following subsections provide a description of the geologic setting of

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes: the older West Maui (Tertiary 
Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala (Pleistocene Epoch). 
The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain between these two volcanoes. The 
project site is at the northern portion of this gently sloping plain. 
The Isthmus of Maui was created by lava flows from Haleakala ponding on 
West Maui. It is comprised of alluvium washed from the slopes of West Maui and 
East Maui (Haleakala). The erosional processes are dominated by the detachment of soil 
and rock masses from the mountain walls, and the soil materials are transported 
downslope toward the isthmus primarily by gravity as colluvium. Once these materials 
reach the stream in the central portion of a valley, alluvial processes become dominant, 
and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium. 
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of Haleakala Mountain, which 
makes up the eastern portion of the island.  The bulk of the Haleakala Shield Volcano 
was built during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic 
lava flows of the Honomanu Volcanic Series. During the Pleistocene Epoch, the 
characteristics of the lava changed to very hard, thickly bedded flows of andesitic 
composition. These lavas have been grouped as the Kula Volcanic Series. Typically, the 
basalt rock formation consists of thinly to thickly bedded a’a and pahoehoe-type lava 
flows. Based on available geologic information and geomorphological interpretation, the 
project site appears to be underlain by basaltic lava flows of the Kula Volcanic Series of 
Haleakala Volcano. The lava flows have weathered and are characterized by the red 
colors of the overlying residual soils.

2.2 
General Site Conditions 
The project site is located along Hana Highway between Milepost 10.18 and 10.49 
in the vicinity of Maliko Bay. Within the project limits, Hana Highway consists of an 
asphaltic concrete paved two-lane roadway with one lane of traffic in either direction. A 
bridge is located at the bottom of the valley near Maliko Bay. This S-shaped section of 
highway generally abuts relatively steep cut slopes on either side of the valley. We 
understand that the land above these tops-of-slopes are privately owned. The project 
limits and existing highway alignment are presented on the Overall Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, the slopes on the Paia side (west side) of the existing bridge generally 
consist of a previously cut slope with an intermediary bench at about the midpoint of the 
overall slope height. The bench width is about 18 feet near the northern project limit and 
tapers down to about 9 feet on the southern end of the bench. Both the upper and lower 
slope faces have relatively steep inclinations of about three-tenths horizontal to 
one vertical (0.3H:1V) to 0.5H:1V.  
The existing slope immediately east of the bridge (Hana side) also contains a 
bench at about mid-height of the overall slope, ranging in width from about 20 to 40 feet. 
The upper and lower slope inclinations range from about 0.5H:1V to about 0.7H:1V. The 
slope inclinations decrease to about 2.5H:1V towards a small valley at about Sta. 188+50, 
then steepens again to about 0.7H:1V to 0.4H:1V from about Sta. 190+00 onward. The 
existing slope from approximately Sta. 190+00 to Sta. 196+00 was not observed to have 
an intermediary bench. The height of this slope face tapers down from about Sta. 195+00 
to Sta. 196+00, decreasing from about 50 feet in height to about 5 to 10 feet in height 
directly adjacent to the highway. The slope height continues to decrease in height towards 
the eastern end of the project limits at about Sta. 204+00.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The project site is underlain by medium stiff to hard and medium dense residual 
and saprolitic soils underlain by soft to hard basalt formation interbedded with clinker 
layers. Based on our field observations, the existing slope faces at the project site 
generally expose similar materials to those encountered in our borings at the 
corresponding depths.  
Based on our field observations, soil borings, and engineering analyses, we 
recommend vegetation clearing, slope scaling, and installing an anchored wire mesh 
system on the Paia side slopes and the Hana side slopes from approximately Sta. 185+00 
to Sta. 196+00. Rockfall mitigation measures may not be necessary from Sta. 196+00 to 
the eastern project limit at about Sta. 204+00 based on our analyses and the 
considerations presented in the body of this report. However, drainage improvements at 
the top of the slope may be considered to mitigate future erosion.  
Detailed discussions of these items and our geotechnical recommendations for the 
design of the project are presented in the following sections. 
3.1 
Slope Failure and Rockfall Mechanisms 
Based on our understanding of past events at the project site and our site 
reconnaissance, it appears that there are three main mechanisms generating slope failure 
and rockfall events at the site. The three mechanisms related to slope failure and rockfall 
are a large amount of surface water on the slope face, raveling failure, and differential 
erosion of alternating volcanic rock layers. 
Based on the information provided and our field observations, we do not anticipate 
deep-seated slope failures at the project site. Instead, we anticipate shallow slope failures 
may develop on the cut slope faces composed of residual soil and saprolite due to 
saturation of the near-surface soils creating weakened planes susceptible to shallow 
slope failure.  
Raveling of one or several rocks from the slope may occur due to a multitude of 
causes. The hazard develops when the raveling rock falls and has sufficient velocity and

volume to reach the roadway surface. Raveling may be caused by water pressure behind 
the rock, differential weathering or erosion along faults or shear zones, weakening of rock 
due to temperature and moisture cyclic fluctuations, and loosening by animals or trees 
and wind. Raveling may also result from ground vibration. Due to the weathered condition 
of the basalt rock slope surfaces at the project area, raveling is an important process that 
continually works to shed weathered and broken rock materials from the slopes. 
One of the most frequent causes of raveling is by invasive tree roots prying on the 
existing cracks located on the rock slope surface. Tree roots (especially of the larger 
trees) frequently develop and grow larger in the discontinuities of rock materials. The 
added force of wind action on tree limbs and branches may create leveraged forces that 
are transferred to the roots lodged within the discontinuity. Over time, the repetitive 
leveraging of the discontinuity can loosen rock and cause it to fall. Because of the 
numerous large trees at the project site, we believe that tree root pry is a significant 
mechanism for creating rock slope instability because of the hardy growth of trees and 
vegetation on the slope. 
In addition to raveling processes, inter-layered rock with variable strength and 
rates of weathering commonly exhibits accelerated erosion and rockfall. The weaker 
layers tend to erode and undercut the more weathering resistant rock located above. This 
may lead to the raveling of the harder layers or failure of undercut blocks of rock as 
support from the underlying weaker layers is removed. Layers of weathered clinker were 
observed beneath ledges of basalt formation in multiple locations during our site 
reconnaissance. In addition, layers of weathered clinker were encountered between 
layers of basalt rock formation in the three borings drilled.  
3.2 
Rockfall Simulation Analysis 
Our site reconnaissance program was performed to visually identify and locate 
potential rockfall hazards, such as loose surface boulders and unstable rock 
outcroppings, within the project study area. The site reconnaissance also permitted us to 
evaluate probable rolling trajectories in relation to the existing topographic features 
encountered in the field. Following the site reconnaissance, selected information recorded 
in the field was input to the CRSP computer program to validate the field evaluation.

The CRSP computer program is a widely accepted engineering tool used by 
experienced personnel to estimate potential rockfall behavior by simulating rockfall 
activity based on input parameters that are assigned on a site-specific basis. The input 
parameters for this project were based on the observations and measurements collected 
in the field. The program provides a statistical evaluation of potential rockfall behavior 
based on the typical hillslope topographic profile and other input information such as rock 
size, shape, and parameters used to describe ground surface irregularities. Information 
obtained from the CRSP analysis includes the predicted falling rock velocity, bouncing 
height, kinetic energy, and roll-out distance. The output information is useful to assist in 
the site-specific design of various rockfall mitigation schemes, such as rockfall impact 
barriers or other rockfall containment systems.  
The slope profiles used in our CRSP analyses were generated from the 
topographic survey map provided. Sections were cut in areas where potential rockfall 
sources were observed and where evidence of past rockfall activity was noted in the field.  
Based on CRSP analyses, we estimate that approximately 90 percent or more of 
the simulated spherical boulders about 1 to 3 feet in diameter may reach Hana Highway 
for the Paia side slopes and the Hana side slopes from approximately Sta. 185+00 to 
about Sta. 196+00. Therefore, we believe that a rockfall mitigation scheme is necessary 
for these sections of the project alignment. For the rest of the project alignment, our CRSP 
analyses estimated that less than 10 percent of the simulated spherical boulders about 
1 foot in diameter might reach Hana Highway. Therefore, we do not believe that a rockfall 
mitigation scheme is necessary from approximately Sta. 196+00 to the eastern project 
limit at about Sta. 204+00. 
3.3 
Rockfall Mitigation Measures 
Based on field observations and discussions with the State of Hawaii Department 
of Transportation personnel, there appear to be numerous instances of rocks falling on 
the sides and on the roadway and creating potentially hazardous conditions.   
Based on our field observations and our rockfall simulation analysis, we believe 
that a moderate to high rockfall potential exists at the project site. Therefore, priority 
should be assigned to the implementation of a rockfall protection system at the project

site. In addition, drainage improvements should be made at the top of the slopes to

Based on our site reconnaissance and evaluation, we believe that there are 
two potentially viable long-term rockfall protection systems for the project site and are

vegetation removal and slope scaling alone is generally estimated to be less than about 
5 to 6 years because the weathering of rock is an ongoing process of erosion. Beyond 
this period, the slope will likely need to be scaled again to remove the build-up of loose 
rocks. 
For the second option with the implementation of drainage improvements, 
vegetation removal, scaling, and installation of an anchored mesh system, the useful life 
of the improvements may be increased to about 10 to 15 years (or the life of the anchored 
wire mesh based on the corrosive environment at the project site).  
The installation of an anchored wire mesh system on the slope face serves to mitigate 
the movement or detachment of soil and rock material from the slope face in the years 
following the scaling work. In addition, the anchored wire mesh system will help to stabilize 
sections of the existing slope prone to shallow slope failures.  
Based on the above, we recommend that the second option consisting of 
implementation of drainage improvements, vegetation removal, scaling, and installation 
of anchored wire mesh be selected for the Paia side slopes, and the Hana side slopes

3.4 
Slope Vegetation 
Based on our observations, the existing slopes and tops-of-slopes are generally 
densely vegetated, with mixed vegetation consisting of thick ground cover grasses, small 
to large-size trees and thick brush.  
Based on our evaluation, the existing vegetation should be removed. Existing 
ground cover vegetation should be removed to completely expose the slope surface in 
preparation for slope scaling and the installation of slope improvements. All existing 
woody stem shrubbery and trees on the slope face should be cut flush with the finished 
ground surface and poisoned to reduce the potential for undesired re-growth on the slope 
face, with the exception of banyan trees. The lower limbs extending from the existing 
banyan trees should be trimmed to facilitate contractor access to the slope and 
construction activity. We do not recommend the removal of the existing banyan trees due 
to the destabilizing effect the tree removal work would have on the rock slope.  
We observed several large, mature trees growing along the top edge of the slope. 
The existing large trees located within about 5 feet of the slope crest should be cut and

Large trees with cantilevered branches and trunks growing from the slope face and 
along the slope crest are a hazard because of the levering action imparted on the slope 
materials by wind loading on the tree canopy. In addition, invasive root penetration into 
the fractured rock materials comprising the slope face can result in the mechanical 
widening of rock fractures, ultimately resulting in a higher risk for slope instability and 
falling rock. Large trees with broad canopies have the potential to destabilize the crest of 
the slope because the tops of the slopes are commonly composed of erodible soils and 
weathered rock materials. Large trees growing at the exposed crest of a slope have the 
potential to sway and lean or topple and may result in significant disturbance to the slope 
crest by loosening and erosion of the weaker soil and rock materials comprising the top 
edge of the slope. 
3.5 
Rock Slope Scaling 
Rock slope scaling consists of experienced crews employing manual labor and 
hand tools to remove loose rocks and precariously positioned rock fragments from the 
slope face. The scaling crews performing the rock slope scaling should be experienced 
in the performance of rock slope scaling by rappelling and/or working from crane baskets. 
Rock slope scaling work typically proceeds by starting from the top of the slope and 
working downward in a controlled and orderly manner in conjunction with the combined 
progress of all the scaling crews working on the slope face. Rock slope scaling should be 
typically performed on slopes with a slope inclination steeper than about 2H:1V. 
In general, each rock slope scaling crew would consist of a scaling supervisor, who 
should be a specialist in rock slope scaling, and three experienced rock slope scalers. 
The scaling supervisor should have a minimum of 5 years of high slope scaling 
experience with a minimum of 2,000 hours of demonstrated experience supervising slope 
scaling. The scaling supervisor should have completed the American Red Cross “basic 
first aid course” (or equivalent) and should have experience or training in the use of 
emergency remote rescue techniques.

A slope scaler is an individual who is engaged in accessing the slope face and

The scaling crews would be supported by an ancillary ground crew (such as a

deposited at the base of the slopes (on the roadway). 
Prior to the start of the rock slope scaling, a series of eyebolt anchors will need to 
be installed along the top of the slope. The eyebolt anchors will be used as rappelling 
anchors. The eyebolt anchors may also be used to anchor other slope scaling apparatus 
such as a grouser or other “spider” type platform. These platforms may be used to 
supplement the manual slope scaling to loosen/remove topographic overhangs and 
ledges. The scaling platforms are essentially dragged up and down the slope face to 
abrade and remove weakened ledges and overhangs that may be too difficult to be 
removed by hand and/or manual methods. 
It should be noted that rock slope scaling work may be considered “hazardous” 
work and would be classified as “high risk” work. Visibility is the single most essential 
attribute to the successful performance of rock slope scaling by the rock slope scaling 
crews. Therefore, night work for rock slope scaling at the site should be discouraged for 
safety reasons (of the scaling crews). In addition, the difficulty in maintaining proper and 
complete illumination of the work site makes night work for rock slope scaling undesirable 
and virtually impossible. Furthermore, it should be noted that night work for rock slope 
scaling should be avoided because of potentially poor visibility and shadow conditions, 
which may reduce the ability of the rock slope scalers to properly assess the rock slope 
conditions and the presence of loose materials and precariously located rocks. 
The rock slope scaling will typically require temporary closure of the roadway 
during the time that rock is being scaled or removed from the slope. We recommend 
conducting all the rock slope scaling work during the day with some temporary roadway 
closure. In addition, we envision that access to emergency vehicles would be provided at

3.6 
Anchored Wire Mesh System 
The anchored wire mesh system is a slope treatment that involves the installation 
of high-strength steel wire mesh panels anchored onto the slope face with grouted 
soil/rock anchors in an effort to prevent the soils and rocks from moving or falling, thus 
providing stability. The primary intent of this stabilization system is to closely pin the wire 
mesh to the slope face as a retention system. A typical detail of the anchored wire mesh 
system is provided on Plate 5. 
The slope to be anchored with steel wire mesh must be trimmed of vegetation and 
scaled of loose rocks prior to the installation of the anchored wire mesh system. Detailed 
descriptions of the individual components of the anchored wire mesh system are 
presented below: 
3.6.1 High-Strength Wire Mesh 
The wire mesh should be alloyed high-strength carbon steel wire with a minimum 
tensile strength of 256,000 pounds per square inch (psi). The wire mesh should be 
woven construction, diamond-shaped, and made with 4-mm (0.157-inch) diameter 
wire. The ends of each wire should be formed into a loop and should be twisted. 
The loops of the wire mesh should be fastened together to prevent the unraveling 
of the mesh. The wire should be galvanized with a zinc/aluminum coating with a

minimum weight of 0.40 ounces per square foot (120 grams per square meter) for 
Level 3 drawn Zn5-Al wire in accordance with ASTM A1007.  
3.6.2 Press Claws (Compression Claws) 
The press claws (formerly known as compression claws) should be 6-mm 
(0.236-inch) diameter carbon steel bars and hot-dipped galvanized with a minimum 
layer thickness of 85 microns. The smaller (standard) press claws should be used 
to fasten the mesh panels together, and the bigger (special) press claws should 
be used to fasten the mesh panels to the boundary ropes. 
3.6.3 Spike Plates 
The spike plates should be made from 7-mm (0.28-inch) thick steel and should be 
hot dipped galvanized with a minimum layer thickness of 85 microns. The spike 
plate should be diamond shaped with a width of 205 mm (8.07 inches) and a length 
of 330 mm (13 inches). 
3.6.4 Boundary Ropes 
Boundary ropes should have a diameter of 12 mm (0.5 inches) and should be PVC 
coated. The rope should consist of 6 by 19 construction, IWRC, and galvanized 
with a minimum breaking strength of 23,940 pounds. The rope should meet 
Federal Specification RR-W-410D or equivalent, including galvanizing. 
3.6.5 Grouted Soil/Rock Anchors 
The grouted soil/rock anchors should consist of 1.375-inch diameter solid threaded 
bar (Grade 75) or 1.57-inch outer-diameter and 0.63-inch inner-diameter hollow 
threaded bar (118-kips minimum yield load capacity). 
The anchored wire mesh system and all associated hardware, such as the 
high-strength steel wire mesh, press claws, and spike plates, should be powder coated 
with a black pigment or another color as directed by the owner. Boundary wire ropes 
should be PVC coated in a black matte finish unless directed otherwise by the owner. All 
other exposed steel elements should be painted with black colored rubberized paint. The 
following provides a brief outline of the anchored wire mesh installation procedures.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, extending to depths of about 86.5 
to 129.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, two bulk soil samples, 
designated as Bulk-1 and Bulk-2, were obtained to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the near-surface soils. The approximate boring locations are shown on 
the Overall Site Plan, Plate 2, and Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.3, respectively. The 
borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers 
and coring tools.  
Our field engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-3.3. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on 
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were

Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered 
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any 
drilling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 
5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs 
of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality 
of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and 
Minerals."

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Ten Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The graphic presentation of the test 
results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Three Unconfined Compression tests (ASTM D2166) were performed on selected 
in-situ samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the on-site clayey 
soils. The test results are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Individual stress-strain curves of the unconfined compression tests are presented on 
Plates B-2 through B-4. 
One Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression test (ASTM D2850) was 
performed on a selected in-situ soil sample to evaluate the undrained shear strength of 
the on-site clayey soil. The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressure was used 
as the applied confining pressure for the relatively “undisturbed” soil sample. The test 
results and the stress-strain curve are presented on Plate B-5. 
Five Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. Results of the direct 
shear tests are presented on Plates B-6 through B-10. 
Seven Uniaxial Compression tests (ASTM D7012 Method C) were performed on 
selected rock cores to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the rock formation 
encountered. Results of the unconfined compression tests are presented on Plate B-11. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-12 and B-13.
