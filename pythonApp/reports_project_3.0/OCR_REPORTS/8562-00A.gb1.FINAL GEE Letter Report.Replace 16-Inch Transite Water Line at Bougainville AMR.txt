PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located at Aliamanu Military Reservation (AMR) in Honolulu on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. This project will consist of replacing approximately 5,750 linear feet 
of 16-inch transite pipe with a 16-inch ductile iron pipe.  
The replacement water line will begin at S1/S2 Tanks on the west side of AMR.  The 
water line will continue north along Bougainville Drive and then turn south on Aliamanu 
Drive.  The water line will turn on Bougainvillea Loop and head towards AMR Child 
Development Center near Kauhini Road. The new water line will terminate near Valve 2540.  
The existing transite water line will need to remain in operation during the installation of the 
new water line.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae and 
Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late 
Pliocene in age (2.7 to 3.4 million years ago) and forms the bulk of the western one-third of 
the island. The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene 
(Ice Age) in age (2.2 to 2.5 million years ago) and forms majority of the eastern two-thirds 
of the island. The end of volcanic activity associated with the Koolau Volcano was followed 
by a long period of landmass erosion whereby deep stream valleys were carved into the 
dome-shaped Koolau Shield Volcano. 
Approximately 850,000 to 1,000,000 years ago, some limited volcanic activity 
resumed with the eruption of the Honolulu Volcanic Series from secondary vents scattered 
across the eastern portion of the Island of Oahu. The eruptions occurred along the 
established secondary rift zones of the Koolau Volcano. Some of the most notable 
landmarks located on the Island of Oahu, such as Punchbowl and Diamond Head Craters, 
and including Aliamanu Crater, were created by the eruption of the Honolulu Volcanic 
Series. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available 
to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When 
the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to elevations above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic sea 
level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was correspondingly 
lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. When the sea level 
was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments filled pre-existing 
depressions and accumulated at higher elevations. 
The rock formations are mainly inclined down towards the central volcanic vent. The 
volcanic tuff rock comprising Aliamanu Crater was created by the fall-out deposition of 
super-heated volcanic ash and cinders containing some entrained blocks of basaltic lava 
rock, which were emitted from the Aliamanu volcanic vent.

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Detailed discussions and recommendations for the design of the water line and 
appurtenances are presented in the following sections. 
Trench Excavation 
We anticipate that the pipe inverts of the new 16-inch ductile iron water line would be 
on the order of about 5 feet below the existing ground/pavement surface. Installation of the 
proposed water line will involve excavation into the underlying fills, volcanic tuff formation, 
and clayey and silty alluvial soils. In general, we anticipate that the alluvial soils along the 
proposed water line alignment may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment. 
Where medium hard volcanic tuff is anticipated, trench excavations into the volcanic 
tuff formation may require chipping and/or the use of hoerams to remove the materials and 
to achieve the design invert elevations. The contractor must exercise care to avoid 
over-ripping, which would disrupt the structure of the rock formation and result in a potential 
loss of bearing strength for foundations in its vicinity and also could result in undermining of 
the excavation sidewalls. 
Trench Excavation Support 
In general, we anticipate excavation depth of up to about 5 feet below the existing 
ground/pavement surface would be required for the installation of the new water line. Deeper 
excavations may be required where the water line traverses existing underground utilities. 
Where deep excavations (greater than 5 feet in depth) are planned, temporary shoring 
should be implemented for the trench excavations. Based on our site observations, we 
believe that temporary cut slopes and benching for open-cut excavations are not feasible 
since the water line alignment will be adjacent to pavements and existing structures. 
Therefore, trench walls would have to be cut near-vertical, necessitating the use of 
temporary shoring during construction. 
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable safety 
requirements. The contractor should be solely responsible for the adequacy and safety of 
the shoring installation. The contractor’s representative should be on-site at all times during 
excavation and construction work for the opportunity to promptly observe changing or 
unforeseen conditions, such as high groundwater, inappropriate construction sequence or 
techniques, etc., which may affect the shoring stability. 
Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal to 
the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential for 
excessive ground movement. 
It is important to install adequate shoring and to maintain it tight against the 
excavation walls with proper bracing during construction. The properly braced shoring is 
essential to reduce the potential for appreciable lateral movements of the adjacent ground

water line is finally backfilled. 
Pipe Cushion 
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the 
load-supporting capacity of the pipe. Therefore, the pipe cushion is an important design 
consideration. For pipes bearing on the underlying fills, alluvial deposits, and volcanic tuff 
formation, a minimum of 6 inches of sand or crusher screenings meeting the requirements 
stipulated in Subsection 209.02 of the Water System Standards, State of Hawaii (2002) 
should be provided below the pipe. Where soft and/or loose soils are encountered at or near 
the invert of the pipes, a stabilization layer consisting of an additional 24 inches of 
open-graded gravel, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), wrapped 
in a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) should be provided below the cushion 
layer for uniform support. A typical trench detail is provided on Plate 4. 
The cushion layer should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width of 
one-fourth (¼) of the outside pipe diameter or trench width, whichever is greater, and should 
extend 12 inches above the pipe. Before the placement of cushion material, a Geolabs 
representative should observe the excavated trench bottom to confirm that firm materials 
are exposed at the bottom of the trench or whether a stabilization layer should be provided 
below the cushion layer. 
Trench Backfill 
In general, the first zone of backfill extending from the cushion material to at least 
12 inches above the top of the pipes should consist of sand or crusher screenings meeting 
the requirements stipulated in Subsection 209.02 of the Water System Standards, State of 
Hawaii (2002) to reduce the compaction effort required and resulting stresses on the pipe. 
In addition, it is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for the 
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the 
sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe 
damage.

Trench Backfill Compaction and Placement Requirements 
Backfill materials consisting of aggregate subbase materials should be 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 
8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
The upper 3 feet below the pavement grade should be compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction. 
Aggregate base course should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to 
the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of 
the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or other 
types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or ponding should not 
be allowed. 
Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during backfilling to 
assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper moisture 
content. Where compaction is less than required, additional compactive effort should be 
applied with adjustment of moisture content as necessary to obtain the specified 
compaction.

Valve Vault Foundations 
We envision that concrete valve structures may be required for the proposed water 
line. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend an 
allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) for the foundation 
design of the concrete valve structures. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and 
may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by seismic 
forces. A bedding layer consisting of a minimum of 6 inches of granular material, such as 
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), should be provided below the bottom 
slab of the valve structure.  The bedding layer should also be extended a minimum of 
12 inches beyond the perimeter of the bottom slab. 
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate the total settlement of the valve vault structure to be less 
than ½ inch. 
Lateral loads acting on the valve vault structure may be resisted by friction between 
the base of the foundation and granular bedding material and by passive earth pressure 
developed against the near-vertical faces of the embedded valve vault.  A coefficient of 
friction of 0.35 may be used for foundations bearing on the granular bedding material.  
Resistance to lateral loads due to passive pressure may be estimated using an equivalent 
fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf).  Unless covered by

Thrust Blocks 
We envision that thrust blocks will be required at some locations to provide resistance 
to the thrust forces due to the pressurized pipes. The thrust force acting on the pipes may 
be resisted by passive earth pressure acting against the face of the thrust block. We 
anticipate that majority of the planned inverts would be about 5 feet below the existing 
pavement/ground surface. However, some of the thrust blocks may need to be located at 
deeper depths in areas where the new water line traverses existing utilities. 
Based on anticipated subsurface conditions, we recommend using a lateral bearing 
pressure of 1,800 psf for the design of thrust blocks bearing against the stiff clayey soils, 
and a lateral bearing pressure of 4,500 psf for the design of thrust blocks bearing against 
medium hard volcanic tuff.  In general, the volcanic tuff would be encountered mainly along 
Bougainville Drive.

Settlements 
Primary settlement of the water line is normally caused by the difference in the unit 
weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted backfill material 
placed over the pipes. The net increase in loading will cause settlement of the underlying 
subsoils below the trench invert. Based on our engineering analyses, settlement on the 
order of less than 1/2 inch is anticipated for the project. 
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good 
workmanship will be engaged during construction. Additional settlement could occur if 
improper trench support is used. 
Corrosion Potential 
The design of the water line should consider the effects of the corrosive environment 
on the substructure.  Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil 
characteristics regarding the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the 
lower the resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to the corrosion 
of metallic objects. 
A total of nine field resistivity surveys, designated as R-101 through R-109, using the 
in-situ Wenner 4-Pin Method and an AEMC Model 4630 resistance meter manufactured by 
AEMC Instruments. Locations of the field resistivity surveys are presented on the General 
Site Plan, Plate 2, and the Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.9. The method utilizes four surface 
probes or pins (electrodes) to evaluate the average resistivity (an indication of corrosivity) of 
the underlying soil mass.  The field resistivity survey was used to evaluate the average 
resistivity values of the soil mass at the approximate survey locations. Results of the field 
resistivity surveys are presented on Plates D-1 through D-4 in Appendix D. It should be 
noted that the field resistivity survey may be influenced by temperature, weather conditions, 
soil moisture, existing underground utilities and other factors. 
The soil resistivity surveys were performed at the existing ground surface along the 
areas adjacent to the existing roads.  The depth of penetration of a resistivity sounding is a 
function of the electrode spacing.  Since the depth of the water line invert was not available 
at the time of our testing, the average soil resistance along the proposed alignment was 
evaluated at each survey location with the electrodes aligned at an equal spacing of 
generally 2, 3, 4, 5, and 10 feet at the ground surface.   
Based on the Board of Water Supply, City and County of Honolulu, Water System 
External Corrosion Control Standards, Volume 3 (2021), soil corrosivity can be categorized 
as Corrosion Category A (Moderately to Severely Corrosive) or Corrosion Category B 
(Negligibly to Mildly Corrosive).

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling ten borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 110, extending to depths of about 11.5 feet below 
the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the General 
Site Plan, Plate 2 and Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.9. The borings were drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and textural 
examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice for 
Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.  Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-10. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the boring drilled in general 
accordance with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the boring drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required 
for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained by 
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, Diamond 
Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow 
bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit is 
mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were described in 
general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods 
for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International 
Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual length 
of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For

The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative quality 
of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is sound 
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any drilling, 
mechanical, and handling-induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is 
recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be 
shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe 
the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of 
Rocks and Minerals” by Robert S. Carmichael (1989).

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Three Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits to aid in soil classifications. The test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic 
presentations of the test results are provided on Plate B-1. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM D6913) was performed on a selected soil sample to 
evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. A graphic 
presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate B-2. 
Three Uniaxial Compression tests (ASTM D7012, Method C) were performed on 
selected core runs to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the volcanic tuff 
rock formation encountered. Test results are presented on Plate B-3. 
Corrosivity tests, including eleven sets of pH (ASTM G51), Chloride Content (EPA 
300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), were performed by Geolabs, Inc. and Eurofins 
TestAmerica on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration. The test results 
are summarized on Plate B-4.
