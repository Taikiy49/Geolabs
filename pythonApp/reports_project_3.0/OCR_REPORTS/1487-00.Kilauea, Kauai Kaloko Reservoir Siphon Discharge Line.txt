PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed project consists of the following
1 A 20 inch diameter ductile iron pipe approximately 600 feet
long and extending down to as much as 25 feet below the existing
ground surface
2 A flexible floating inlet structure located inside the
reservoir and hinged to the rigid 20 inch siphon pipe
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located near the northeast rim of the existing
Kaloko Reservoir and about 3 miles southeast of Kilauea town in the
Kilauea District on the island of Kauai
The topography of the site is fairly steep and slopes down towards
the reservoir in a southerly direction at a slope ratio of about 2 1 to

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The project site is situated on a broad gently sloping plateau along
the north shore of the island of Kauai This plateau was built during
the late Pliocene and Pleistocene by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from
the Pooku Kapaka and other now cbscure vents of the Koloa Volcanic
Series
The site is located along the northwest rim of the vent for a cinder
cone of the Koloa Volcanic Series
The project area appears to be situated near the edge of the
reservoir embankment fill In the lower portion of the borings the
soils encountered appear to be residual soils derived from the in situ
weathering of the ash and cinders that were built up during eruptive
phases of the cone activity The upper scils encountered in the borings
appear to be residual soils however the soft consistency suggests that
they may be part of the embankment fill

Soil Conditions
In general the soil conditions encountered along the alignment
appear relatively uniform Most of our drilled borings encountered a
surface layer about 10 to 20 feet thick of medium brown clayey silt
that is underlain by about 10 feet of soft brown clayey silt down to
about elevation 720
Underlying this soft layer our borings encountered medium to stiff
sandy silts and silts decomposed ash and cinders to depths of 29 5 to
36 5 feet the depths of the borings drilled
A profile depicting the generalized soil conditions is shown on
Plate 3
Groundwater Conditions
At the time of our exploration groundwater was encountered in 3 of
the 4 borings made in Boring 1 at 4 5 feet in Boring 2 at 24 feet and
Boring 4 at 15 5 feet However some soil samples from Boring 3 showed
wet seams which appear to indicate zones of seepage The profile of the
water level encountered is shown on Plate 3 It appears that the water
encountered is due to seepage from the reservoir
Additionally discussion with personnel of the Kilauea Agronomics
Inc indicated that some leakages occur along the northern and western
sides of the reservoir when sufficient water is stored in the reservoir

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The borings disclosed that the siphon alignment is underlain by wet
and soft soils Therefore deep excavations into these soils would
require shoring and bracing to minimize failure of the trench walls
Additionally seepage water from the reservoir was encountered which
indicates that dewatering may be necessary during the pipe installation
Trench Excavation
The estimated depth of excavation varies from 15 to 25 feet below the
existing ground surface along most of the alignment Easy digging is
anticipated through the soils
The borings encountered soft and wet soils which indicate that the
alignment may be situated over some of the old embankment fill Due to
the relatively poor soil conditions and depth of excavation it is our
opinion that proper shoring and bracing would be necessary to prevent
failure of the trench walls The excavation should be provided with
proper bracing in accordance with the anticipated loading conditions to
meet the applicable safety requirements The design and safety of the
trench supports should be the responsibility of the contractor The
apparent pressure diagram presented on Plate 4 may be used as a guide for
construction cost estimate purpose and for preliminary design of the
trench support system
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1487 00 Page 6
The ground adjacent to and near the top of the excavation should be
continuously monitored for cracks or dips and other indications of
movements and possible sloughing of the trench walls
In order to reduce the depth of trench excavation it may be
desirable to excavate the upper portion utilizing a temporary cut slope
ratio of down to a suitable depth and then utilize a large backhoe
at this lower level to complete the trench
If practicable the excavation should be made in short sections and
backfilled as soon as possible
Heavy equipment and stockpiles should be kept away from the tops of
the open trenches
Dewatering
Groundwater was encountered in the borings at various depths It
appears that the water was a result of seepage from the reservoir
Therefore depending on the level of water in the reservoir dewatering
may be required during the pipe construction
The method of dewatering should be left open to the contractor pro
vided adequate stability of the excavation is maintained The project
dewatering operations should be done without pumping out the soil fines
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1487 00 Page 7
pumping clear water only and should be coordinated with the excavation
shoring support so that the stability of the excavation is not jeopar
dized The operations should be carried out without creating a quick
condition or softening the bottom of the excavation
In order to minimize the possibility of dewatering and to improve the
stability of the excavation we recommend that the reservoir be emptied
or water level lowered prior to construction of the siphon
Trench Backfill
In order to minimize seepage and piping along the pipe special
provisions are recommended for the backfill around the pipe
Concrete cutoffs are recommended along the portion of the pipe
extending from the reservoir to about 500 feet downstream in order to
reduce the possibility of seepage along the pipe The concrete cutoffs
may consist of 12 inch thick concrete sections poured around the pipe and
extending to the full width of the trench and about 2 feet above the
pipe The cutoffs may be spaced at about 50 feet intervals
In order to reduce the possibility of piping along the downstream end
of the pipe due to build up of seepage pressure porous granular fill
such as ASTM C 33 No 67 gravel is recommended as backfill material
around the pipe for the lower 500 feet of the alignment to allow relief
of excess water pressure Provisions should be made to allow drainage of
this gravel layer at the downstream end of the siphon

The on site excavated soil may be re used as trench backfill
material However due to the high natural water content of the soils
air drying of the soils would be necessary prior to compaction The
backfill should be mechanically compacted to a minimum of 85 of its
maximum dry density established by ASTM D 1557 78 The trenches should
be kept free of water during backfilling
Earth Pressure Loads on Pipe
Loads on buried conduits are influenced by the width of the trench
the size of the pipe the unit weight of backfill material and the
frictional resistance between the backfill material and the trench side
walls To compute the vertical loads on the pipes a soil unit weight of
110 p c f and a coefficient of friction of 0 25 are recommended Earth
forces upon the pipe increase rapidly with the width of the trench and
therefore the width of the trench should be kept to a minimum
The new utility line construction in general will not materially
increase the vertical loading upon these underlying soils if no
additional fills are placed upon the existing ground surface The net
difference in loading between the weights of the excavated material and
backfill will be essentially small Therefore significant settlements
are not anticipated if proper construction techniques particularly for
shoring and dewatering are utilized

Design Review
Plans and specifications for the siphon should be forwarded to the
soils engineer for review and written comments prior to construction to
see that the intent of our recommendations is included
Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the site grading excavation and backfill
operations be observed by the soils engineer during construction to see
that adequate compaction has been obtained and that the subsurface
conditions are consistent with the findings of our investigation
If conditions encountered during construction are different from
those presented in this report then appropriate modifications to the
design should be made

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling
4 borings to depths of 28 3 to 36 5 feet below the existing ground
surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 A
truck mounted drill rig using 4 inch diameter continuous flight auger was
used to drill the borings
The soils encountered were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by one of our engineers who continuously
monitored the drilling operations The classifications were further
reviewed by visual inspection and testing in the laboratory The soils
encountered in the borings are presented in the Log of Borings Plates
A 1 through A 4 All soils were classified in accordance with the
Unified Soil Classification System
Soil samples were obtained by driving either a 2 4 inch I D
ring sampler or a standard penetration sampler with a 140 pound hammer
free falling a distance of 30 inches The blow counts to drive the
sampler the last 12 inches are shown on the Log of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
