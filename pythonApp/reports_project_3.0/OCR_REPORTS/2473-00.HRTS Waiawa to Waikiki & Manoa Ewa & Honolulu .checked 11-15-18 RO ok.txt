INTRODUCTION
In accordance with your request we have conducted a preliminary geotechnical
and geological reconnaissance of the proposed Honolulu Rapid Transit System Project
This report summarizes our work on the project and presents our findings
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2473 00 Page 2
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to construct a new fixed light rail transit system from the Waiawa
Interchange area to Waikiki and to the University of Hawaii Manoa
The locally preferred alternative among the originally proposed alignments run from
Waiawa at the U S Navy Ewa Drum Storage site along Kamehameha Highway past Aloha
Stadium and Honolulu International Airport to Keehi Interchange and Dillingham
Boulevard The selected route then follows Dillingham Boulevard to North King Street
becoming an underground system at Aala Park and proceeding under Hotel Street to
Kapiolani Boulevard At Kapiolani Boulevard the system will head along Kapiolani
Boulevard Waimanu and Kona Streets past Ala Moana Shopping Center to Kalakaua
Avenue At Kalakaua the line will branch into the Waikiki Line along Kalakaua and Kuhio
Avenues to the Honolulu Zoo at Kapiolani Park and the University Line following
Kapiolani Boulevard to University Avenue and then along University Avenue to the
University of Hawaii Manoa
The portion of the system along Kapiolani Boulevard to Kalakaua Avenue and
throughout the Waikiki Line is being considered as an option for an underground system
The total length of the system is approximately 17 miles including the one and one
half mile University Line one mile of the system is currently contemplated as underground
construction
Under the current development concept the majority of the proposed transit
system will be an elevated above ground fixed rail system with rails supported by and
suspended between piers Relatively large vertical loads and high overturning moments
are typically generated by these long span structures with large live loads
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2473 00 Page 3
Portions of the system will be underground These underground portions may be
constructed either by cut and cover or by tunneling depending upon the final design
This preliminary report was prepared solely for project planning purposes and
therefore does not contain sufficient data nor appropriate recommendations for detailed
design of the transit system foundations

Soil conditions along the proposed transit system alignments to develop an up dated

report cannot be construed as including sufficient detail upon which to base even
preliminary design or construction cost estimates

tasks

soils reports published and unpublished pertinent to the proposed transit
system corridors and their vicinities

proposed alignments to provide additional subsurface data at areas where
it was lacking

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active and
its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu
The proposed transit system corridors are located along the southern flank of
Koolau and its geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to
glacio eustatic fluctuations of sea level during the Pleistocene and the genesis of Pearl
Harbor and the Honolulu Coastal Plain The subsurface conditions along the majority of
the routes are dominated by fossil Pleistocene age coral algal reef deposits laid during
high stands of the sea which intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the
stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt
The Waianae and Koolau volcanic shields were built during the late Pliocene and
early Pleistocene by thin bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities
ceased approximately 5 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicates
that the island of Oahu has subsided as much as 1200 feet since the cessation of this
early volcanic activity Macdonald and Abbott 1970
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2473 00 Page 5
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across their mouths The growth of
the reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed
behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the
lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the Pleistocene Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a result
of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to
fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower
relative to the present sea level on Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
corresponding higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs
The concurrent growth of reefs and deposition lagoonal sediments may have also
resulted in deposits of coral algal limestone and marls within the predominantly lagoonal
sedimentary unit
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef
deposits Exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused consolidation of
the soft deltaic and lagoonal deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials In
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2473 00 Page 6
addition renewed erosion of upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous
alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward by deposition of alluvium over the
oreviously deposited lagoonal sediments
The geologic history of the project area has been further complicated by the
deposition of recent pyroclastic materials and lava flows from eruptions of the vents of
members of the Honolulu Volcanic Series including Aliamanu Salt Lake Makalapa
Kamanaiki Punchbowl and other minor vents These
post erosional i e following the cessation of shield building events were
contemporaneous with the Pleistocene sea level fluctuations
Many of the eruptions were rather explosive in nature owing to the interaction of
groundwater with the rising magma resulting in the expulsion of steam under extremely
high pressure together with large quantities of pyroclastic material predominantly ash
and cinder These deposits of pyroclastics have consolidated to form volcanic tuff locally
referred to as mudrock One of these eruptions Aliamanu caused a diversion of
Halawa Stream to the north of its original location into the area of what are now the
Southeast and Magazine Lochs of Pearl Harbor during the Kaena 95 feet high stand
of the sea
The eruptions of the vents blanketed much of what
is now the metropolitan Honolulu area with a fine black cinder locally known as black
sand These eruptions also produced a thick lava flow which filled the bottom of Manoa
Valley
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2473 00 Page 7
About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred During that
rise the deep valleys in the project area were drowned In the last 10 000 years or so
sea level has adjusted to its present level Terrigenous and marine sediments have
continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in
thick deposits of soft harbor sediments along the coast in the areas which were formerly
valleys and drainageways
Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have brought
the Ewa Honolulu area to its present form A great many of those projects were
originally accomplished for agricultural residential and or military development Many of
the resulting fills are of poor quality in terms of their capacity to support large structural
loads

DISCUSSIONS
General
Our reconnaissance indicates that the proposed transit alignments will cross a wide
variety of subsurface conditions Therefore several different foundation systems will
probably be required to support the fixed rail system It is critical therefore that detailed
geotechnical engineering exploration and analyses be conducted as part of the design
efforts for the proposed transit system
Subsoil Characteristics
Based on our review and on previous experience in the areas of the proposed
transit corridors the following discussions summarize the general characteristics of the
materials which we anticipate will be encountered along the transit route
1 Koolau Basalt Tkb
The Koolau basalt is the oldest geologic unit within the project area It
generally consists of interbedded pahoehoe and a a flows of basaltic lavas
and may have a mantle of cobbles and boulders or residual soil overlying
the top of the rock
It is anticipated that various types of volcanic rock sub components such
as clinker layers and lava tubes could also be encountered
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2473 00 Page 9
The Koolau basalt is the aquifer material of the Pearl Harbor and Honolulu
Artesian Basins which are the major developed groundwater bodies
supplying fresh water for the Island of Oahu
The aquifer is the permeable lava rock while the upper confining member
caprock comprises the relatively impermeable alluvial and lagoonal
deposits There is no lower confining member as is the usual case in most
aquifers At the bottom the fresh water level is confined by salt water
according to the Ghyben Herzberg Principle which is based on the principle
that fresh groundwater floats on saline groundwater in coastal regions The
ratio of densities between fresh and salt water causes the salt water to be
displaced downward about 40 feet below sea level for every foot that the
fresh basal water table or potentiometric head rises above sea level
Structurally the Koolau basalt and its residual soils are among the strongest
of all the units encountered within the study area Therefore the basalt and
related residual soils saprolites make excellent bearing formations due to
their high strength characteristics in an undisturbed state However its use
for foundation support in many parts of the project area is severely limited
by the considerable depths to the top of the basalt and the artesian
conditions expected where the basalt is encountered at relatively low
elevations These conditions could increase the difficulty in construction
and consequently project costs i e long pile lengths where the basalt layer
is deep and difficult dewatering where artesian conditions are present
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2473 00 Page 10
Older Alluvium Qa
The older alluvium is assigned the position of being the second oldest unit
in the stratigraphic column of the project area even though some of the
coral algal reef deposits may be intercalated with the alluvial deposits
This unit generally overlies the Koolau basalt and consists of terrigenous
sediments which have béen transported by stream action from the upper
reaches of the streams The materials consist mainly of very stiff to hard
brown clayey silts and silty clays Gravel lenses representing buried stream
channels are sometimes encountered Portions of the older alluvium unit
contain some weathered basalt boulders and cobbles These coarse
grained parts of the unit may also contain artesian water of the basal fresh _
water system or as a sub aquifer
The old lagoonal deposits of the older alluvium unit consist of marine or
lagoonal sediments which have been desiccated and consolidated into very
stiff to hard gray clayey silts
The old lagoonal deposits consist almost exclusively of clayey silts of rather
uniform texture and grain size with occasional shell fragments
The older alluvium materials generally exhibit high strength values which
leads toa relatively high bearing capacity Frequently these materials show
a moderate to high shrink swell potential however this is not considered
a constraint for the type of structures proposed on this project Structurally
the older alluvial materials are considered to be sufficiently competent to
support relatively large structural loads with proper design
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2473 00 Page 11
3 Coral Algal Deposits Qis
This unit consists of fossil remnants of coral algal reefs and their related
detrital debris deposits In Hawaii the detrital deposits tend to be the
dominant portion of any coral rock assemblage
The detrital deposits are weakly to strongly cemented coral debris
consisting of gravel sand and silt sized clastic fragments Some of the
coral fragments were dissolved and the solution cavities filled with cemented
and or uncemented fine sands and silts
Due to the granular nature of the majority of the coral algal deposits and the
degree of cementation frequently present this formation generally has high
in situ strength characteristics Structurally the coral algal reef deposits are
capable of supporting heavy foundation loads
4 Honolulu Basalt Qhb
The Honolulu Basalts generally consist of interbedded pahoehoe and a a
flows of basalt lavas and may have plastic alluvial clays with cobbles and
boulders overlying the top of the rock
It is anticipated that various types of volcanic rock sub components such
as clinker layers and lava tubes could also be encountered
The basalt frequently acts either as a perching or confining member for
groundwater in the Honolulu Aquifer system Therefore groundwater may
be encountered in excavations located within this unit
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2473 00 Page 12
Structurally this basalt is also one of the strongest of the units encountered
_within the study area Therefore the basalt makes an excellent bearing
formation due to its high strength characteristics in an undisturbed state
5 Honolulu Tuffs Qht
Tuff from vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series consist of volcanic ash
which consolidated or hardened to form a medium hard rock that resembles
siltstone or sandstone locally called mudrock The tuff is frequently found
overlying coralline deposits or alluvium units
Since the formation is a medium hard rock it has very high strength
characteristics Structurally the tuff is very competent
This formation has relatively low permeability and groundwater is generally
not encountered in this formation When it does occur it is usually in the
form of seepage along joints
6 Black Sand Rtsp
These soils were developed from volcanic cinder sands laid down by
eruptions of the vents of the Honolulu
Volcanic Series They generally form a relatively thin less than 10 feet
mantle over coral tuff or basalt and consist of a surface layer a few feet
thick of brown sandy silt silty sand underlain by relatively unweathered
cinder sand The sand is usually of medium dense to dense consistency
and is frequently weakly to moderately cemented
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2473 00 Page 13
Groundwater is usually encountered as a basal water table in low elevations
The cinder sands generally have rapid permeability and therefore
dewatering may be difficult
7 Recent Alluvium Ra
The recent alluvium consists mainly of very soft to medium stiff brown to
dark gray clayey silt and organic silt with varying amounts of sand and
infrequent pockets of gravel and cobbles
These soils are generally the result of sedimentation in a very low energy
environment such as an estuary or bay Frequently the soils assigned to
this unit accumulated very rapidly under water and have never been
exposed Therefore there is a tendency to encounter underconsolidated
sediments which settle under the weight of minimal loading
Included in this unit are subunits of localized thin layers about 10 feet of
lagoonal marine sediments consisting mostly of very soft to medium stiff
gray clayey silts These gray silts are identified as lagoonal facies subunits
of the young alluvial unit
The soils assigned to this unit are generally considered to be poorly to very
poorly suited for use as support for large foundation loads due to their low
in situ strength characteristics and the tendency for settlements to occur in
structures overlying soils of this unit
8 Recent Sand Rs
This mapping unit represents recent deposits of beach and near shore
marine sands These sands are generally composed of medium grained
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2473 00 Page 14
fragments derived from the coral aigal reefs which have been reduced in
size and rounded by water action The consistency of the sands is
generally on the order of medium dense to dense
These recent sands are generally capable of supporting moderately heavy
loads in their natural state Densification of the sands usually improves their
bearing capacity
9 Man Made Fill Rf
_ This mapping unit represents low areas which were filled during
urbanization The majority of these low areas are underlain at relatively
shallow depths by Recent Alluvium and were originally marshy areas A
small part of the filled lands are underlain by coral
Groundwater in the fill areas is generally expected to occur as a basal water
table near sea level However higher perched water tables may be
encountered
Along most of Nimitz Highway in the Downtown area and in parts of
Waikiki it appears that the man made fills are underlain at relatively shallow
depths by coral algal limestone The fill materials range from clays to
gravels and intermediate mixtures of soils The consistencies range from
soft or loose to hard or very dense
The quality and composition of the fill is variable These fills were frequently
placed without control Therefore it is difficult to make any general
statement regarding capacity to adequately support imposed loads
GEOLABS HAWAIl
W O 2473 00 Page 15
It may be stated however that the capability of these man made fills to
support structural loads is partly dependent upon the design loads and the
quality of the fill and its placement and partly dependent upon the materials
underlying the fills The actual fill materials may be of suspect quality
For areas with shallow fills overlying coral algal formations it is our opinion
that foundation loads may be supported by relatively deep spread footings
and or short pile foundations bearing on the coral algal formation
Quite frequently in areas underlain by Recent Alluvium fills were only
intended to support very lightly loaded structures on shallow spread
footings and therefore only form a thin crust over the soft alluvial
materials
Along Pearl Harbor and at the mouths of major streams man made fills
were generally placed over soft sediments for development along the
shoreline areas The fill materials range from clays to gravels and
intermediate mixtures of soils The consistencies range from soft or loose
to hard or very dense
Foundation Support
Drilled Pier and Footing Foundations
In general we anticipate that drilled pier or spread footing foundations may be
used to support the proposed transit system structures in the areas mapped as
Koolau Basalt Older Alluvium and Coral Algal Deposits
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2473 00 Page 16
Pile Foundation
In the areas mapped as Recent Alluvium and in most areas mapped as Man made
Fill we anticipate that deep pile foundation systems will be required to support the
rail system piers Based upon our study we anticipate that in many of these
areas bearing strata may be encountered at depths ranging from about 50 to 150
feet below the existing ground surface
Ideally the piles would develop their vertical load capacity primarily from end
bearing on the competent bearing strata generally at great depths However it is
our opinion that where the bearing strata is very deep friction piles may be driven
to a sufficient depth that they could develop sufficient vertical load capacity from
adhesion between the piles and any medium stiff medium dense or stiffer denser
subsoils encountered before end bearing is reached
Anticipated Surface Conditions
The majority of the proposed transit alignments are situated in developed or
urbanized areas Therefore surface conditions may be congested but are generally
underlain by relatively competent materials or at least a relatively stable crust overlying
poor subsoil conditions In most areas the surface soils would be sufficiently competent
to support construction activities
Critical surface conditions are identified as those areas where the surface soils may
have insufficient strength to support the loads of heavy construction equipment such as
pile driving cranes or caisson drilling rigs Critical surface areas are situated along
Kamehameha Highway between Pearl City and Makalapa particularly along Aiea Bay in
_ the Keehi Lagoon Mapunapuna Moanalua Gardens area and along Nimitz Highway at
lwilei In these areas it may be necessary to construct a stable working platform for such
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2473 00 Page 17
equipment This platform may be temporary for construction only or may be a permanent
improvement for maintenance purposes
The construction of the working platform will have a significant effect on the design
and cost of the project The construction of the working platform may include a filter
fabric and gravel stabilization layer or the placement of temporary planking over the soft
ground It must be recognized that if a permanent stabilization layer is selected the
weight of the new fill over soft compressible soils will cause ground settlements which
may induce downdrag forces on the piles thereby reducing their allowable load carrying
capacity
Stabilization of the ground surface may be needed in these areas to allow access
for heavy equipment If stabilization is required existing underground utilities should be
located It should be determined whether the stabilization may have potential impacts on
any of these buried utilities
Excavation
For the underground portions of the proposed transit system excavation will be
required to allow installation of the underground structures Based on our review of
available data it is anticipated that both soft ground and hard excavation will be
encountered along the proposed alignments The method and excavation techniques
should be left to the discretion of the contractor within safe construction practices and the
applicable regulations It is anticipated that either the cut and cover method or tunneling
could be utilized
Most of the proposed underground portions of the transit system are situated in
highly developed urban areas with a significant amount of existing facilities along and
adjacent to the alignment Consideration should be given to prequalifying the contractor
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2473 00 Page 18
to select a construction team that has sufficient experience and capabilities in the
installation of underground structures
It is anticipated that most of the excavation will have to be braced shored or
otherwise stabilized to reduce the potential of movement of the excavation walls Such
movement could destabilize the subsoils supporting adjacent facilities which could
potentially cause settlements and or other damages The possible stabilization
techniques include but are not limited to the installation of temporary sheeting and
shoring the sloping of the excavation sidewalls for temporary stability and or grouting
_of the subsoils to create a competent stable mass
The method and techniques of stabilization should be left to the discretion of the
contractor within safe construction practices and the applicable regulations It is
recommended that a preconstruction damage survey be conducted to establish
oreexisting conditions along the excavation alignment to reduce the potential for claims
against previous conditions and or damage Itis further recommended that a surveillance
program be implemented during construction to monitor for potential signs of instability
It is strongly recommended that additional geotechnical engineering exploration be
conducted with particular emphasis on the anticipated tunneling conditions geotechnical
design recommendations and construction considerations for the underground portions
of the transit system
Groundwater Conditions
The project area is located within the Pearl Harbor and Honolulu artesian aquifer
basins As previously discussed artesian groundwater occurs within the Koolau Basalt
The potentiometric head of this aquifer varies spatially temporally and in response to
pumpage Generally at any location where the foundations encounter Koolau Basalt at
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2473 00 Page 19
an elevation of less than 20 feet there is a significant chance of encountering artesian
groundwater
In addition groundwater is also encountered in the other geologic units described
within the project area Generally this groundwater occurs as a basal water table at or
near sea level This groundwater is usually brackish or saline In some isolated areas
perched groundwater and or sub artesian groundwater may be encountered Any
excavations which penetrate to or below sea level will most likely encounter groundwater
Dewatering
Based on our review of the available data it is our anticipation that most of the
proposed underground portions of the proposed transit system will encounter
groundwater levels within their excavation limits Therefore dewatering of the
excavations will be required to allow construction to be constructed in dry
conditions
It is anticipated that excavations in areas underlain by coral formation will
encounter highly permeable subsoil conditions which will require extensive
pumping to dewater the excavations unless precautions are taken to limit the flow
of groundwater into the open excavation
Dewatering in soft soil conditions may cause an areal drawdown of the
groundwater levels in the area surrounding the excavation This areal drawdown
could result in a loss of the buoyant forces which are partially supporting the soft
subsoils and any facilities situated in or on the surface of the soft soils This loss
of support could potentially cause settlement of the soft soils and potentially result
in damages to the existing improvements
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2473 00 Page 20
The techniques and methods of dewatering should be the express responsibility
of the contractor However we recommend that further geotechnical engineering
exploration be conducted to emphasize the groundwater conditions and potential
dewatering parameters along the proposed underground portions of the transit
system
Additional Exploration
The basis of this report was limited to a search of available literature and review
of available soils geologic data and information relevant to the proposed transit system
alignments The subsurface conditions along the proposed alignments are highly variable
and complex The discussions and recommendations presented in this report are general
in nature and as such cannot be considered adequate for final design or even preliminary
cost estimates
We feel that it is absolutely necessary that full scale geotechnical studies including
borings field and laboratory testing be developed and implemented prior to the
oreliminary and final design stages of the subject project The recommendations
presented herein are contingent upon the performance of such an exploration by the
geotechnical engineer

APPENDIX B
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at selected locations within the mapping area were
explored by drilling and sampling five 5 borings to depths ranging between 41 0 and
450 0 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the
Project Location and Soils Geologic Map Plate 1 The borings were drilled using truck
mounted equipment
The material encountered were Classified by visual and textural examination in the
field by our geologist who continuously monitored the drilling operations These
classifications were further reviewed by visual inspection in the laboratory All soils were
classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System A graphic
presentation of the materials encountered are presented on the Boring Logs Plates B 1 1
through B 5 3
Soil samples were obtained from the borings by driving a standard penetration
sampler or 3 O D Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer free falling 30
inches The blow counts to drive the sampler the last 12 inches are shown on the Boring
Logs at the appropriate sample depths
In soft cohesive soils samples were obtained using a thin walled tube sampler
Shelby Tube The sampler was hydraulically pushed The gauge pressure required to
advance the sampler is shown on the Boring Logs at the appropriate depths
Core samples were obtained by an HQ wireline core barrel The core recovery
REC and rock quality designation RQD values are shown on the Boring Logs at the
appropriate sample depths
