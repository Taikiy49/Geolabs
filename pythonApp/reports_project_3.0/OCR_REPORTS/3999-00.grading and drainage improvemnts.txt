REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island
The younger shield volcano Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3999 00 Page 4
in age Ice Age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae
became extinct while Koolau was still active After a long period of volcanic inactivity
during which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls which are then transported
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these
materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become
dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium In general
stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the stream flows transmit
large volumes of water for very short durations Because of this transport of sediments is
intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load consists of a poorly sorted mixture
of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When the erosional base levels change
these sediment loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes begin
to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the material
Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the coarse grained portion
of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate
Simultaneously erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors and upstream in
headwaters This continued erosion generates material which is transported downslope
covering the older alluvial deposits Depending on the local base level and rate of
transport these new sediments are generally transient in terms of geologic time
In addition their consistency and density are generally less than those of older partially
consolidated deposits
The project site is situated near the base of the Koolau Pali a high cliff
which extends along most of the windward side of the Koolau Range on the island of
Oahu The climate along the Pali is typified by a high to very high average annual rainfall
Following the main shield building stage of the Koolau volcano in the late Pliocene to
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3999 00 Page 5
early Pleistocene Epoch stream erosion landsliding and deep weathering of the basalt
rock became the dominant agents affecting the morphology of the area
The project site is located near the base of a spur ridge extending northerly from
the Koolau Range The site generally exists as a drainage way bounded on both sides
by hills The hills are believed to be composed of residual and saprolite soils derived from
the in situ weathering of volcanic rock The bottom of the drainage gully and lower slopes
of the adjacent hills are believed to be composed of alluvial and colluvial materials
deposited by erosional processes
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The proposed project site is located on the eastern perimeter of the existing Valley
of the Temples Mortuary and straddles a gully The site exists as a bowl shaped site with
a drainage gully bordered by hills to the east south and west and the existing Chapel Hill
Road to the north The site is densely forested with guava plum lauhala and hao tree
growth
Ground surface elevations range between about 190 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
at the bottom of the basin to about 270 MSL at the top of the hill on the western
boundary of the site A narrow channel exists at the gully bottom which widens into a
relatively broad basin area at the north central portion of the site The stream was
observed to be dry at the time of our field exploration The existing western hill slope
appears to be on the order of about one and one half horizontal to one vertical 172 H 1V
with some localized steeper segments The existing eastern slope appears to be on the
order of about 3H 1V
An existing 18 inch drainline with headwall exists at the lowest elevation of the site
within the broad basin area The drain line appears to trend northerly beneath Chapel Hill
Road to an outlet off site Another headwall and drain outlet was observed at the
northern portion of site obscured beneath vegetation This drain appears to be connected
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3999 00 Page 6
to catch basins located on Chapel Hill Road The drain appears to feed the main 18 inch
drainline located at the basin bottom
Based on our site reconnaissance the hills bordering the eastern southern and
western perimeter of the site appear to consist of residual and saprolite soils Surface
outcroppings of rock formation were not observed on the upper portions of the slopes
The lower portions of the eastern and western slopes were observed to consist of
generally moderately consolidated alluvial deposits consisting of brown clayey silt and
gravel with cobbles and scattered boulders Some localized in situ rock formation was
observed near the southern limit of the site at the gully bottom The rock outcroppings
were observed as slightly weathered very dense fractured basalt which may represent
intrusive dike rock material The location of the rock outcropping appears to be very near
the proposed Headwall B 1 location shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on our observations the majority of the gully and the broad basin area is
believed to be underlain by alluvial and colluvial deposits consisting of clayey silt with
clayey gravel cobbles and scattered boulders The deposits within the gully were
observed as generally medium stiff to stiff and wet The banks bordering the gully were
observed to consist of generally consolidated stiff medium dense and moist alluvial and
colluvial soils of clayey gravel with cobbles and some boulders
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the lower elevations of the project site were
explored by the excavation and backfilling of six shallow hand dug test pit excavations
and hand auger borings to depths of about 1 to 6 feet below the existing ground
elevation The approximate location of the test pits are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our field exploration generally encountered stiff to medium dense alluvial and
colluvial deposits extending to the maximum depth explored of about 6 feet below
ground level in the vicinity of the existing gully and basin area The alluvial deposits
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3999 00 Page 7
encountered on the bank and lower eastern slope were observed to be generally stiff to
very stiff
Multiple ground surface probings with a steel rod indicate that significant soft soil
deposits may not exist at the lower elevations of the site Probe refusal was generally
encountered at depths of about 1 to 2 feet below ground level Isolated areas within the
stream invert attained probe penetration of 2 to 3 feet with moderate pressure
Based on ground surface probings and shallow excavations made for the
collection of bulk soil samples from hillslope cut areas the slopes comprising the hills
bordering the stream were observed to consist of generally stiff to very stiff residual and
saprolite soils
Groundwater levels were not encountered in the test pits at the time of our field
exploration However the soils were observed to be generally wetter in the gully bottom
than the adjacent bank areas It should be noted that the Kaneohe and Ahuimanu areas
tend to exhibit variable groundwater levels related to seepage and perched water table
conditions Furthermore the water level at the project site could be influenced by
variations in seasonal precipitation or other factors occurring outside the project site

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
It is our opinion that the proposed project is feasible from a geotechnical
engineering stand point provided that our recommendations are followed The wet soil
conditions and high to very high average annual rainfall at the project site will require
some special earthwork construction methodology
Based on the results of our exploration the proposed 10 to 20 feet of fill may be
supported on the in situ clayey alluvial and saprolite soil materials provided a ground
settlement program is adhered to We believe that the new fills planned would induce
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3999 00 Page 8
some ground settlements as a result of the consolidation of the underlying soils
Settlements on the order of about 6 to 12 inches may be anticipated for fills overlying the
medium stiff to stiff alluvial stream deposits Therefore special attention should be given
to the recommendations provided in the Settlement section of this report
The proposed mausoleum structures may be supported on the fill material derived
from on site sources provided the fill materials are properly compacted and meet the
specifications presented in this report Based on our site reconnaissance we anticipate
the near surface clays and silts may be excavated with normal heavy excavation
equipment The on site clayey silt materials generated from the excavations may be
used as a source of fill or backfill materials provided that they are screened of the
over sized materials greater than 6 inches in largest dimension depending on the
usage and properly moisture conditioned Special attention should be given to the

Site Grading
Based on the existing terrain at the subject site and the preliminary grading plans
provided we anticipate that site grading work for the proposed project will generally
consist of cuts less than about 15 feet and fills less than about 25 feet thick Items of
grading that are addressed in the following subsections include 1 Site Preparation
2 Fills and Backfills 3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 4 Excavation
5 Cut and Fill Slopes and 6 Subdrainage
Site grading operations should be observed by a_ representative of
Geolabs Hawaii It is important that a representative from our office observe the site
grading operations to evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3999 00 Page 9
site preparation process and to confirm that the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to
those encountered in our field exploration
Generally the on site soils should be suitable for use as engineered fill provided
the high in situ moisture contents can be substantially lowered to achieve 85 percent
compaction Since the project site is located in a high rainfall area the on site materials
have very high in situ moisture contents of about 15 to 30 percent above optimum
Therefore significant drying of excavated materials will be required prior to their use as fill
and or backfill
Due to the high in situ moisture content of the on site soils and the high amount of
rainfall occurring in the area it is our opinion that the compaction requirement for this
project may be reduced from the standard of 90 percent to a minimum of 85 percent
maximum dry density as established by ASTM D 1557 78 Even with this reduced
85 percent minimum compaction requirement we believe substantial moisture
reduction would still be required along with the use of light compaction construction
equipment The required aerating to lower the soil moisture and more intense
compaction effort would generally reduce the rate of fill placement at the site
Based on previous experience in the area a shrinkage factor of about 12 to
15 percent due to the differences between the natural and compacted soil densities may
be considered for on site cut and fill quantities
Fill Settlement
Due to the presence of potentially soft compressible soils at the existing stream
channel location we estimate that the placement of up to 20 feet of new fills in this area
would induce some ground settlements on the order of about 6 to 12 inches due to the
consolidation of the underlying deposits beneath the new fills Based on our engineering
analyses we believe that a settlement period of about 3 months would be adequate to
achieve approximately 90 percent of consolidation The new fills should be placed as
GEOLABS HAWAII

early as possible Subsequent sections of this report will discuss in greater detail the
ground settlement monitoring program recommended
Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site or in a designated
area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials
Existing drainage utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the resulting
excavation should be properly backfilled with select granular fill material placed in
6 to 8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas
designated to receive fill should be over excavated to expose firm natural material
and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted engineered
fill
After clearing and grubbing areas designated to receive fill should be scarified to
a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture and compacted to a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close cracks or the
subgrade should be re scarified moisture conditioned and recompacted
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Saturation and subsequent
yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage
may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted engineered fill
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3999 00 Page 11
If the subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade preparation the subgrade
soils should be proof rolled using a minimum 10 ton smooth drum roller and a
woven geotextile such as Mirafi 500X or equivalent should be placed on the
subgrade prior to placement of fill material Alternately the pumping areas may
be over excavated a minimum of 6 inches below the finish subgrade to expose
firm natural material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with

Fills and Backfills
The on site silty clay and clayey silt materials generated from excavations may be
used as a source of fill or backfill materials provided that they are screened of the
over sized materials greater than 6 inches in largest dimension depending on the
usage and properly moisture conditioned
If imported material is required the material should be non expansive granular
material such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material
should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 6 inches
in largest dimension The material should have a laboratory California Bearing
Ratio CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell value of
1 percent or less Imported fill materials should be tested for conformance
with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its intended
use Aggregate base course and select borrow subbase materials should meet
the material requirements for Base Course and Subbase Course as specified in
Sections 30 and 31 respectively of the Standard Specifications for Public Work
Construction Department of Public Works City and County of Honolulu
September 1986 Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for
its intended use

General fill material should be free of organic material and highly expansive clay

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
General fill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 10 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction Imported fill should
be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to at least 90 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
Oversized material greater than 8 inches in maximum dimension may be placed
within deep fills below 10 feet from finish grade that are set back away from the
fill slope Oversized material should also be set apart from other large material to
avoid nesting and allow finer material into the interstices to create a dense mass
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of soils are critical elements of
earthwork observations and soil density tests should be performed by
Geolabs Hawaii during site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the
required degree of compaction and the proper moisture content on each fill lift
Where compaction is less than required additional compactive effort should be
applied with adjustment of moisture content as necessary to obtain the specified
compaction

Fill Slopes
Based on the materials encountered in our field exploration cut slopes should be
designed with a maximum inclination of 172H 1V or flatter for the proposed
project Fill slopes may be designed with a maximum inclination of 2H 1V or
flatter Fills placed on existing slopes having slope ratios steeper than 5H 1V
should be keyed and benched into the firm and stable materials to enhance the
stability of the new embankment fill against sliding see Plate 3 The filling
operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal
compacted layers in accordance with the above recommendations for fill
placement Fill slopes should be constructed by over filling past the design limits
of the slope and cutting back to the design slope to provide adequate
compaction at the edges of the slope In the event that over cutting of a slope
occurs keying and benching requirements should be implemented instead of
backfilling the slope to the design grade with sliver fills Water should be diverted
away from the tops of slopes or channelized and slope planting should be
provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished
slopes
For cut and fill slopes steeper than 3H 1V with vertical heights greater than
25 feet we recommend that a minimum 8 foot wide bench be provided at the
mid point of the slope to reduce the potential for erosion due to surface water
run off

Considering the wet climate of the area seepage conditions may be present at
localized areas at the site Therefore we recommend that provisions be

Ground and Settlement Monitoring
Based on our exploration data we believe that a 3 month waiting period will be
required for this project The actual settlement rate should be monitored during
construction and provisions should be made for potential delay in the project
construction schedule based on the estimated duration if a longer settlement
period is required
To monitor the actual settlement rate we recommend that settlement gauges be
installed at selected locations as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The settlement
gauges should be installed on the existing ground surface at the future fill areas
and on the finish grade level of fill areas
The settlement gauges should be installed by Geolabs Hawaii A typical detail for
settlement gauges is presented on Plate 4 The gauges should be read optically
by a professional land surveyor and the readings should be transmitted to our
office for review The readings should be transmitted on a weekly basis during the
duration of the settlement period and or until the settlement readings indicate that
the settlement rate has stabilized
It should be recognized that it is difficult to accurately predict the time required for
the filled ground to settle Settlement rates are greatly affected by minute qualities
of the subsoil structure and the history of the subsoil deposition
GEOLABS HAWAII

A proper contingency plan should be devised to accommodate the potential for
delay in the construction schedule and possible extra costs involved with a delay
Surcharge
To reduce the waiting period for the fill ground settlements to occur it is our
opinion that implementation of a surcharge program should be considered for the
site especially since surplus soils may be available from the planned cuts at the
site The lateral extent of the recommended surcharge area should generally
conform to the location of the axis of the buried stream channel The excavated
surplus soils generated from the on site cuts may be used as surcharge The
surcharge height is not critical and may extend from 5 to 10 feet above finish grade
depending on the availability of the surplus soils In general the surcharge
materials may be placed without compaction effort for this project

outlined in the Site Grading recommendations of this report

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based in part
upon information obtained from the field borings Variations of conditions between and
beyond the field borings may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not
become evident until construction is underway If variations then appear evident it will be
necessary to reevaluate the recommendations presented in this report
The test pit and bulk sample collection locations in the field were determined by
taping from structures and features indicated on the preliminary grading plan provided by
Lyon Associates Inc on Elevations of the test pits were estimated by
interpolating from the elevation contours on the same plan The locations and elevations
of the field test pits should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the
methods used
The stratification breaks shown on the Logs of Test Pits depict the approximate
boundaries between soil rock types and as such may denote a gradual transition
Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown on the graphic

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by the manual excavation of
shallow test pits with hand auger boring extensions and are designated as Test Pit Nos 1
through 6 The test excavations were extended to depths ranging from about 1 to about
6 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2 The test pits were typically excavated by pick and shovel and where
appropriate extended by hand auger drilling at the pit bottom to reach the final depth
explored
The materials encountered in the test pits were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Project Geologist who monitored the excavation operation
on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the
materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Test Pits Plates A 1 through A 3
Selected soil samples were obtained from the excavations by manual collection of
the materials with a hand trowel Where appropriate field penetrometer and torvane
shear tests were performed on the excavation sidewall These field measurements are
presented on the Logs of Test Pits
In addition to the test pit exploration manual probings of the ground surface with a
one half inch diameter steel rod were performed at selected locations throughout the
project site The hand probe information was used to assist in the evaluation of the
shallow subsurface for potential deposits of soft or compressible soils in and near the
stream channel
