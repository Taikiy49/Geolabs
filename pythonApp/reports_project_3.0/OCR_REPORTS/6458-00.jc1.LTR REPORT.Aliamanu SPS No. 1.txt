PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is desired to upgrade the existing sewage system to mitigate the odors to the
surrounding areas Based on the information provided we understand that the project
consists of design and construction of new underground pump station emergency
generator building control building and other miscellaneous maintenance structures
Based on the information provided we understand that the new underground pump
station is about 27 feet below the ground surface with about 14 feet of design sewage
fluid Therefore excavation and backfill of up to about 30 feet deep may be required for
the new underground pump station construction
Based on the topographic survey map provided the existing grades at the project
site vary from about 52 to 53 feet Mean Sea Level MSL We anticipate that site
grading consisting of minor cuts and fills of less than about 2 feet will be needed to
achieve the design grades In addition trench excavation and backfill of up to about
25 feet deep is also anticipated for the new sewer line installation

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of the
Waianae and Koolau Volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of the
western third of the island while the younger Koolau Volcano forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that the Waianae Volcano became extinct

while the Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below
Koolau lavas in Central Oahu After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The project site is near the northwestern flank of the Aliamanu vent complex of the
Honolulu Volcanic Series As the molten magma ascended through this vent it reacted
with the groundwater and generated steam The steam caused explosive eruptions
which deposited low cones of ash material that have consolidated to form tuff locally
referred to as mudrock
The Aliamanu tuff is a water laid ash deposit that was laid down when the sea
level was higher than the present level The Aliamanu eruptive event deposited its
material over alluvium originating from Halawa Stream Alluvial deposits have since filled
a portion of the Aliamanu crater Subsequently man made developments in the last
century have brought the site to the existing conditions
FIELD EXPLORATION
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 The borings extended to about 31 5 feet
below the existing ground surface using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight solid stem augers The boring locations are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D 2487
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A of Appendix A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered and other observations such as
groundwater levels are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2 of
Appendix A
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Standard Test Method for Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined

LABORATORY TESTS
Samples recovered from our field exploration were visually examined and
classified in our laboratory in Honolulu Hawaii Selected samples were tested to evaluate
the physical characteristics and engineering properties of the soils encountered The
laboratory testing program was formulated with the emphasis on evaluating the moisture
density plasticity and expansion of the soils encountered
Moisture Content and Unit Weight tests were performed on selected soil samples to
aid in defining the interface between the various soil layers encountered The moisture
content tests were performed in general accordance with ASTM D 2216 The unit weight
tests were performed in general accordance with ASTM D 2937 The test results are
presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths in Appendix A
Three Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate
the liquid limit LL plastic limit PL and plasticity index PI as an aid in classifying the
soils The Atterberg Limits tests were performed in general accordance with
ASTM D 4318 Graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plate B 1 of
Appendix B
Five one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on samples of the on site soils to
evaluate the swelling potential of the soils The test results are presented on Plate B 2 of
Appendix B
One Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression TXUU test ASTM D 2850
was performed on a selected soil sample to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the
clayey soils encountered The approximate in situ effective overburden pressure was used
as the applied confining pressure for the relatively undisturbed soil sample The test
results and the stress strain curves are presented on Plate B 3 of Appendix B

MAT FOUNDATIONS
We understand that the new underground structures planned at the site will be
constructed at a depth of approximately 27 feet below the existing ground surface Based
on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe that a mat
foundation system may be used to support the new underground structures A net
allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot psf may be used in
the design of the mat foundation bearing on the on site clayey materials This bearing
loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
In general we recommend placing the foundation mat on a minimum 12 inch thick
layer of cushion fill to provide uniform support The cushion fill should consist of
open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation which would also serve as a leveling
course We envision that waterproofing would be provided for the new underground
structures planned
Footings for the proposed structures should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches
below the lowest adjacent finished grade Foundations next to utility trenches or
easements should be embedded below an imaginary 45 degree plane extending upward
from the bottom edge of the utility trench or the foundations should extend to a depth as
deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging
adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the
potential for appreciable foundation settlement
The bottom of foundation excavations should be compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction prior to the placement of reinforcing steel Soft and or
loose materials encountered at the bottom of footing excavations should be removed to
expose the underlying firm or dense materials The over excavation should be backfilled
with approved on site fill materials compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate the total mat foundation settlement to be less than 1 inch

SHALLOW FOOTING FOUNDATIONS
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we
recommend using spread or continuous footing foundation system to support the new
building structures at or near the existing grade An allowable bearing pressure of up to
2 500 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of foundations bearing
on the compacted on site clayey soil The bottom of foundations should be compacted to
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior to placement of reinforcing steel and
concrete
This allowable bearing pressure is for dead plus live loads and may be increased
by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces

SLAB ON GRADE
Based on the laboratory test results the on site clayey soils exhibit a moderate to
high potential for shrinking and swelling when subjected to moisture fluctuations To
reduce the potential for future distress to the slabs on grade resulting from shrinking and
swelling of the on site clayey soils the subgrade should be over excavated 2 feet and
replaced by non expansive granular select fill with 90 relative compaction Prior to
placing the non expansive granular fill we recommend scarifying the clayey subgrade
soils to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioning the soils to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture content and compacting to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction
We recommend using reinforced slabs on grade for the new structures to reduce
the potential for appreciable slab cracking resulting from expansion of the near surface
clayey soils The reinforced slabs on grade should be a minimum of 5 inches thick and
reinforced with No 4 reinforcing bars spaced at 12 inches on center in both directions A
cushion fill about 4 inch thick consisting of open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation should be placed below the slabs on grade for uniform support
It should be emphasized that the areas adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled
tightly against the slab edges These areas should also be graded to divert water away
from the slabs and to reduce the potential for water ponding around the slabs and
foundations
A Geolabs representative should observe the footing excavations prior to placing
the reinforcing steel and or concrete to confirm the foundation bearing conditions and the
required embedment depths Observation of the foundation excavations should be
designated a Special Inspection item in accordance with Section 1704 of the
International Building Code 2003

SITE PREPARATION
Based on the existing topography we anticipate that the proposed site grading may
consist of minor cuts and fills of about 2 feet or less relative to the existing ground surface
in order to achieve the design finished grades In addition an excavation of up to about
30 feet deep will be required for construction of the new underground structures Based
on the generally limited grading anticipated for this project the following general guidelines
may be used for specifying the earthwork requirements
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly Vegetation debris deleterious material concrete slabs
asphaltic concrete pavements and other unsuitable materials should be removed and
disposed of properly off site or in a designated area to reduce the potential for
contamination of the excavated materials Existing utilities to be abandoned should be
removed The resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with general fill materials
The general fill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content placed in 6 to 8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction
Soft and or yielding areas encountered during clearing should be over excavated to
expose firm natural materials and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with
compacted fill In areas to receive fill and finished subgrades in cut areas the subgrade
should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of
the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
Where shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the clayey subgrades
we recommend re scarifying moisture conditioning and recompacting the subgrade soils
as recommended above Subgrade soils should be kept moist until covered by
subsequent layers of fill materials Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed
subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the
soft areas and replacement with compacted fill at no additional cost to the owner
It should be noted that the on site clayey materials exhibit moderate to high
expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations based on our laboratory test
results Therefore the on site clayey soils should not be used as backfill directly under
pavements and other structures The on site clayey soils may be used as a source of
trench backfill material for the sewer lines where pavements or other structures will not be
constructed above The excavated on site soils to be re used as a source of general fill
materials at the site should be free of vegetation deleterious materials and clay lumps
and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension

EXCAVATION
Construction of the new underground structures and sewer line installation will
require an excavation of up to about 30 feet below the existing ground surface Based on
our field exploration the proposed project site is generally underlain by medium stiff to stiff
clayey materials It is anticipated that the near surface materials may be excavated with
normal heavy excavation equipment such as excavating with a backhoe excavator
However it should be noted that the deeper excavations may encounter boulders and
volcanic tuff formations and may require the use of hoerams or chipping
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the subsurface materials are
based on our visual observation of the existing site conditions and field data from the
borings drilled at the site We recommend that contractors proposing to bid on this project
be encouraged to examine the site conditions and the boring data to make their own
interpretation

UNDERGROUND UTILITY LINES
In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular
materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be
used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide
adequate support around the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce
the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate
support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in backfill
settlement and pipe damage
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils with a
maximum particle size of 6 inches The backfill materials should be moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture content placed in about 6 to 8 inch loose lifts and
mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the
potential for appreciable future ground subsidence Where trenches are located below
pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should
be compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction
