REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late 
Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age. 
After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time erosion incised deep valleys into the 
Koolau Volcano, volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff 
cone formations. These series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
The project site is on the coastal plain of Southern Oahu. The coastal plain was built on the 
eroded flanks of the Koolau Volcano, which forms the eastern two-thirds of the Island of Oahu. 
The coastal plain was built by extensive accumulation of alluvium derived from erosion of the 
volcano, interbedded with coral reefs and associated deposits. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. Most of the coastal plains were developed during the Pleistocene Epoch 
when the sea levels fluctuated significantly. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. 
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above its present level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic sea level 
fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was correspondingly lower, and 
valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. When the sea level was high, the 
erosional base level was raised such that sediments accumulated at higher elevations. 
The project site generally is underlain by deposits consisting of calcareous sediments and 
lagoonal deposits. A surface layer of man-made fill was placed over these deposits for the 
development of the Kaka’ako area within the last century.

Liquefaction Potential 
The current code adopted by the City and County of Honolulu for the design of building 
structures is the International Building Code (IBC), 2018 Edition. Based on the IBC 2018, the 
project site may be subjected to seismic activity and should be evaluated for potential soil 
liquefaction. 
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the 
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build-up of excess pore water 
pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes. In this process, 
when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration (such as during an earthquake), 
the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an increase in pore water pressure. If 
drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up of pore water pressure, the 
effective stress (internal strength) of the soil is reduced. Under sustained vibrations, the pore 
water pressure build-up could equal the overburden pressure, essentially reducing the soil 
shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid. During liquefaction, the soil

Seismic Design Considerations 
Because the project site is likely underlain by liquefiable soils, the project site should be 
classified from a seismic analysis standpoint as being Site Class F based on Chapter 20 of the 
American Society of Civil Engineers (ASCE) Minimum Design Loads for Buildings and Other 
Structures, 2016 Edition (ASCE 7-16). Per Section 20.3.1 of ASCE 7-16, a site-specific ground 
motion study will need to be performed for the proposed new structure to determine the 
appropriate values for seismic design considerations. Based on previous projects in the area, a 
reduction in the site class is likely. To determine the appropriate Site Class for the project, we 
recommended performing shear wave velocity testing and a site-specific elastic response 
spectrum during the geotechnical investigation phase of the project.

Foundation Considerations 
We understand that the details of the proposed structures have not been determined at 
the time this letter report was prepared. However, we envision that the site will be developed 
into two 42-story high-rise residential buildings constructed of reinforced concrete and metal 
framing. Based on our experience in the area and similar structures, we anticipate that the new 
high-rise building structure may have maximum column loads (dead-plus-live loads) of up to 
about 3,500 to 4,000 kips. 
As mentioned above, the site is underlain by soft/loose compressible lagoonal deposits. 
Based on the thickness of the soft/loose compressible lagoonal deposits near the existing 
ground surface and the anticipated relatively heavy building loads, we believe the use of a 
shallow foundation system would not be feasible for high-rise buildings. Therefore, it is our 
opinion that a deep foundation system should be considered in support of the new buildings 
planned.  
Since the project site is located in the urban area of Kakaako, we envision there may be 
concerns regarding excess noise and vibrations generated during the typical pile driving 
operations. Drilled shaft foundations are highly effective in soil formations that can stay open 
after drilling until concrete placement. Unfortunately, the loose sands underwater at the 
project site likely will not stay open following drilling. Therefore, partial-depth temporary casing 
and/or the use of drilling fluids (polymer slurry) will likely be necessary to maintain the integrity 
of the drilled hole during drilled shaft installation, resulting in construction difficulties and 
higher costs. Augered cast-in-place (ACIP) piles have been successfully designed and 
constructed on similar projects in the area and have proven to be more cost-effective at smaller 
diameters. Therefore, we believe the use of ACIP piles may be desirable to support the 
proposed structures. 
A detailed field exploration program will be required to provide subsurface information 
for the final foundation design when details of the proposed structures are better defined. 
Precast Prestressed Concrete Piles 
Driven concrete piles were considered because their installation method generally does 
not generate spoils. However, we believe the use of driven pile foundations may pose 
some significant design and construction issues for the project development due to the 
relatively high structural load demand and the presence of relatively thin, hard coral 
ledges anticipated at various depths underlying the site. The presence of relatively thin, 
hard coral ledges anticipated at various depths across the project site would result in 
the potential for driven piles to “hang up” on the relatively thin, hard coral ledges and 
the potential need to increase the size of the pile caps substantially to accommodate 
the additional replacement piles. In addition, we envision there may be concerns

Cast-in-Place Drilled Shafts 
Drilled shaft foundations are highly effective in soil formations that can stay open after 
drilling until concrete placement. Unfortunately, the loose sands underwater at the 
project site likely will not stay open following drilling. Therefore, partial-depth 
temporary casing and/or the use of drilling fluids (polymer slurry) will likely be necessary 
to maintain the integrity of the drilled hole during drilled shaft installation. This would 
increase the construction difficulty and costs for the foundations. In addition, the 
anticipated shallow groundwater level at the project site may also pose some 
construction difficulties because proper observation of the sides and bottoms of the 
drilled shafts may not be possible. 
One of the primary advantages of the drilled shaft foundation systems is that 
construction would not generate appreciable noise and vibrations normally associated 
with other deep foundation systems, such as driven pile foundations. However, 
construction of the drilled shafts will involve generating substantial spoils that will need 
to be disposed of. It should be noted that the drilled shafts are typically slower to 
construct than the other foundation types presented herein.  
In general, drilled shaft foundations are not as cost-effective as ACIP piles at smaller 
diameters. Therefore, we believe that drilled shaft foundations may not be appropriate 
for this development. 
Augered Cast-In-Place (ACIP) Piles 
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions, we believe ACIP piles may be 
considered to support the new high-rise building structures. Augered cast-in-place 
(ACIP) piles are constructed by rotating a continuous flight hollow-stem auger (generally 
10 to 30 inches in diameter) into the ground until either the planned pile tip depth or 
termination (refusal) criterion is achieved. At the termination depth, grout of a specified 
consistency is pumped into the augered hole through an orifice located in the drilling bit 
at the tip of the hollow-stem auger. The auger is slowly withdrawn from the hole, and 
the pile shaft is formed as a continuous flow of grout that replaces the soil removed by 
the auger. After the auger stem is removed, the reinforcing steel cage is inserted into 
the grouted hole (through the fluid grout) within the set-time window allowed by the 
grout.

Ground Floor Slabs 
For new building construction, we anticipate the ground floor slabs likely will consist of 
reinforced concrete slabs-on-grade. We envision the slabs-on-grade for new building structures 
will be supported either on new compacted fills placed to raise the existing ground surface or 
on the existing ground conditions. 
Because the potential for significant damage and distress to the building slabs-on-grade 
(not structurally supported) resulting from seismically induced ground settlements from soil 
liquefaction is likely at this site, we believe the potential for damage could further be reduced 
by increasing the building slab thickness (5 inches minimum) and by incorporating steel 
reinforcement (No. 4 bars at 12 inches on-center in each direction) into the concrete slab. 
New fills needed to raise the project site to the finished subgrades will induce some 
ground settlements due to the consolidation of the underlying soft to very soft compressible 
clayey silts and organic silts/clays that are anticipated at the site. Therefore, we recommend 
placing the new fills as soon as practical to allow the anticipated ground settlements to occur 
prior to slab-on-grade construction.
