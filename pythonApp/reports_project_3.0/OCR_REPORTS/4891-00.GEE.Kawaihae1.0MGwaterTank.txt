2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is situated on the western flank of Kohala which has formed the
northern portion of the island The project site is underlain by basaltic lava flows of the
Hawi Volcanic Series of Kohala which were deposited during the Pleistocene Epoch
Stearns and Macdonald 1946 The Hawi Volcanic Series is typically composed of
basaltic lava flows that are overlain by a relatively thin layer of palagonitized ash locally
referred to as Pahala Ash The ash soil at the project site differs from Pahala Ash found
in other portions of the Island of Hawaii in that the in situ moisture content is generally
lower and the soil exists in a dry friable state The soils at the site have been subjected
to wind erosion that has resulted in the formation of a desert pavement on the surface of
the site This desert pavement is a surficial layer of cobbles and gravel which were left
behind as the finer ash was blown away
The lava formation encountered at the site appears to be of a a and pahoehoe
basalt type flows which spread and ponded as they approached the ocean Aja is
typically characterized by a porous rough and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged
accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders Typically a more dense
and layered lava rock material is contained within the lava flow core Pahoehoe is
characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of
vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lavas These
cavities were formed when the lava was still in a molten state and represent both lava
tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes are
formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure

2 2 Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by shifts
in the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or
during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The Island of
Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most of the earthquakes are
so small that they can only be detected by instruments However some of the
earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of magma
are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the Island of
Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii over 90 percent of
earthquakes are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic activity and degree of
ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the southern portion of the Island
of Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than

Based on the over excavation and replacement recommendations we believe that
the water tank may be supported on a shallow foundation system consisting of spread
and or continuous footings To provide more uniform support we recommend that the
footings bear on 12 inches of aggregate base course compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction An allowable bearing pressure of up to 6 000 pounds per
square foot psf may be used for the design of footings bearing on 12 inches of
compacted aggregate base course over structural fill material This bearing value is for
dead plus live loads and may be increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as
those caused by wind or seismic forces
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 45 may be used for foundations bearing on the compacted aggregate base
course Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent
fluid pressure of 400 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the
soil around footings is well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the
passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grades If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements
they should be embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the
bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate total settlements of the foundations bearing on the
compacted aggregate base course to be on the order of 1 inch Differential settlements
between footings are estimated to be on the order of 0 5 inch

3 2 Slab On Grade
As previously mentioned the water tank will straddle both basalt rock formation and
volcanic ash soils To reduce the potential for future distress to the slabs we recommend
that the slab subgrade be over excavated to a minimum of 36 inches The resulting
over excavation should be backfilled with structural fill compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction In addition we recommend that the subgrade soils below
the structural fill layer be proof rolled with a minimum 15 ton vibratory drum roller a
minimum of six passes to help detect and collapse near surface cavities and or voids
Yielding areas loose areas or cavities disclosed during proof rolling should be over
excavated and backfilled with structural fill materials
To provide more uniform support for the slab the slab subgrades should be
capped with at least 12 inches of aggregate base course The aggregate base course
should consist of crushed basalt compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction For the design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of about
250 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection pci may be used for the compacted
aggregate base course The area adjacent to the slab should be backfilled tightly against
the slab with compacted on site soils and graded to divert water away from the slab to
reduce the potential for water ponding around the slab and foundation

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris deleterious material and
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site

3 6 Water Lines
We envision that new water lines will be required for this project In general for
support of the water lines we recommend that a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under the pipes The initial
backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes should consist of free draining backfills
such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from
compaction of the backfill It is critical that a free draining granular material be used to
reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide
adequate support for the sides of the pipes The use of on site soils as backfill
immediately around water line pipes is not recommended

3 7 Percolation Testing
A total of four percolation tests were performed in Boring and 12 to
evaluate the subsurface permeability conditions at the site Both the constant head and
falling head methods were used for the percolation tests For the constant head method a
constant flow of water was injected into the boring and the flow rate and head build up was
recorded For the falling head test method water was injected into the boring to fill the
hole After injection of water the drop of the water level in the boring was measured along
with time

Results of the constant and falling head percolation tests are presented in
Appendix C The test indicated that the basalt formation have coefficient of permeability
k in the range of about 1 3 x x 10 centimeters per second to 0 5 feet per minute
0 25 centimeters per second along the access road and about 4 0 x 10 feet per minute
2 x 10 centimeters per second near the water tank site
Based on the coefficient of permeability we estimate that a typical dry well with a
minimum diameter of 5 feet and a depth of about 20 feet would have a clean water
disposal capacity of approximately 1 0 cubic feet per second cfs at the project site
It should be emphasized that the hydraulic conductivity of the basalt rock
formation at the site is highly variable due to the presence or absence of significant
voids in the lava or the presence of clinker layers which greatly affect the disposal
capacity of a dry well In addition the build up of volcanic ash soils within the fractures
over time reduces the permeability of the basalt rock formation In general the greater
the number of clinker layers and or voids encountered by the dry well excavation the
higher the overall permeability and disposal capacity
Considering the potential for variable disposal capacities of the dry wells due to the
variable subsurface conditions across the project site we strongly recommend testing of
all dry wells during construction The dry well installation and testing program should be
conducted under the observation of a representative from Geolabs This would allow our
firm to monitor the subsurface conditions to evaluate the storm water disposal capacity
and to allow for additional recommendations to be made The drywell design

tests The owner client should be aware that substantial variations in subsurface
conditions may occur Therefore a contingency fund is recommended to accommodate

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
12 borings to depths of about 12 8 to 31 2 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using
truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring
tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered is presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 12
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the logs of borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained by using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations ASTM D 2937
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Nine sieve analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plates B 1
and B 2
One California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM D 1883 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the strength characteristics for pavement
subgrade support Results of the CBR test are presented on Plate B 3
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 A was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plate B 4
