SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered residual soils extending to the 
maximum explored depth of about 10 feet below the existing ground surface.  A surface fill 
layer was encountered in one of the borings.  In addition, basalt rock formation was 
encountered below the residual soil in one of the borings.  The fill and residual soils 
consisted of stiff to hard silty clay.  The basalt rock formation was very hard.  We did not 
encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. 
The design traffic parameters used in the analysis consisted of a design life of 
40 years, approximately 1,550 cars and light trucks per month, and approximately 
40 buses.  A total flexible Equivalent Single Axle Load (ESAL) of about 58,000 was 
calculated.  A design CBR-value of 3 to 4 was used in the pavement analysis.  Based 
on the above, we recommend using the following preliminary pavement design section 
for the project. 
Flexible Pavement Section

12.0-Inch Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction) 
18.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade

similar methods of stormwater management would be feasible for this project site. Due 
to the variability of the subsurface conditions, the infiltration capacity of the disposal 
system should be confirmed by conducting additional infiltration tests during 
construction.

1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project involves the construction of a parking lot at Field 4 of the 
Patsy T. Mink Central Oahu Regional Park in the Waipio area on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii.  The new parking lot will provide approximately 323 parking spaces and will be 
paved with asphaltic concrete as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
It is anticipated that site grading will be minimal with cuts and fills of less than 
about 5 feet in depth or thickness.  We anticipate that the use of Best Management 
Practice/Low Impact Development (BMP/LID) systems for the management of 
stormwater runoff may be needed.

performed in general accordance with our revised fee proposal dated May 14, 2016.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from 
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to 
be middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early 
Pleistocene in age.  As volcanic activity in Waianae ceased, lava flows from Koolau 
banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau, known as 
Schofield Plateau.  The project site is located on the Schofield Plateau. 
The project area is composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lava of the 
Koolau Volcanic Series.  These rocks are generally characterized by flows of jointed 
dense vesicular basalt rock formation with inter-bedded thin clinker layers.  In-situ 
chemical weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million 
years.  The weathering process has formed a mantle of residual soils, which grade to 
saprolite with depth.  In general, saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is 
typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks.  The saprolite grades to basaltic rock 
formation with increasing depth. 
2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The new parking lot is located within the Patsy T. Mink Central Oahu Regional 
Park complex in the Waipio area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The project site is 
located near Kamehameha Highway on the east side of the park as shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Existing parking lots are located at the north and south ends of the project site.  
The new parking lot site is covered with grass.   
Based on the topographic survey plan, the topography of the site is fairly level 
with the existing ground surface elevations generally ranging from about +342 to 
+345 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).  At the southern end of the new parking lot, the 
existing ground slopes downward to about Elevation +335 feet MSL.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the new parking lot site is generally underlain by 
residual soils extending to the maximum depth explored of about 10 feet below the 
existing ground surface.  A surface fill layer was encountered in one of the borings.  In 
addition, basalt rock formation was encountered below the residual soil in one of the 
borings. We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. 
Based on the assumed traffic parameters and laboratory CBR test results, 
preliminary flexible pavement design section is provided.  Cuts and fills up to about 
5 feet are anticipated.  Site grading recommendations are provided below.  
The installation of retention and infiltration systems for the on-site management 
of the stormwater runoff is being considered.  Based on the results of the infiltration 
testing, it appears that infiltration systems for stormwater management are feasible for 
the project site. Detailed discussions of our findings and geotechnical recommendations 
are presented in the following sections. 
3.1 
Pavement Design 
We anticipate that asphaltic concrete pavements will be used for the planned 
parking lot.  The preliminary flexible pavement section was generally determined based 
on the methodology described in the 1993 AASHTO Guide for the Design of Pavement 
Structures.  The WinPAS12 program by the American Concrete Pavement Association 
was used in the analysis.  
Design traffic information was not available at the time this report was prepared. 
The design traffic parameters used in the analysis consisted of a design life of 40 years, 
approximately 1,550 cars and light trucks per month, and approximately 40 buses per 
month.  Based on the above, a total flexible Equivalent Single Axle Load (ESAL) of 
about 58,000 was calculated. 
The laboratory CBR values of the surface materials at the project site ranged 
from 3.5 to 19.6 with swell values from 0.0 to 0.4 percent.  A design CBR-value of 3 to

The subgrade soils under the pavement areas should be scarified to a minimum 
depth of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum 
moisture, and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction. CBR tests and/or 
field observations should be performed on the actual subgrade soils during construction 
to confirm that the above design section is adequate. The aggregate base course 
should consist of crushed basaltic aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction. 
In general, paved areas should be sloped, and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavements. Surface water ponding should not 
be allowed on-site during or after construction. Where concrete curbs are used to 
isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas, we recommend extending the 
curbs a minimum of 2 inches into the subgrade soil to reduce the potential for migration 
of excessive landscape water into the pavement section. 
3.2 
Site Grading 
Based on the relatively level topography, we anticipate the site grading work for 
the proposed new parking lot will consist of cuts and fills up to about 5 feet in thickness.  
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
thoroughly cleared and grubbed.  Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site. 
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below cut 
subgrades or areas designated to receive fill should be over-excavated to expose firm 
natural material, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted 
fill.  The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be properly disposed of off-site

and/or used in landscape areas, where appropriate.  Contract documents should 
include additive and deductive unit prices for over-excavation and compacted fill 
placement to account for variations in the over-excavation quantities. 
After clearing and grubbing, the existing ground surface or finished subgrade 
level in cut areas should be scarified to a minimum depth of about 8 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content, and 
re-compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. The compaction 
requirement at the pavement subgrade level should be increased to 95 percent relative 
compaction.  Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as 
a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557.  Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
In general, we believe the on-site silty clay may be reused as a source of general 
fill materials, provided that the maximum particle size is less than 3 inches in largest 
dimension.  General fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent 
above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in 
loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  
Imported fill material, if required, should consist of non-expansive, select granular 
material, such as crushed coral or basalt gravel.  The material should be well graded 
from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension.  It should 
also contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve.  The 
material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of 
1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883.  Free-draining 
materials and highly permeable materials are not recommended for use as the 
non-expansive, select granular fill.  Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs 
for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the 
intended use.

Non-expansive, select granular fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
In general, cut and fill slopes planned at the site should be designed with a slope

accordance with the above general fill placement recommendations.  Fill slopes should

slope planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for 
erosion of the finished slopes. 
3.3 
Utility Trench 
It is anticipated that underground utilities may be installed for the project.  In 
general, good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling 
of the trenches for the proposed utilities.  The contractor should determine the method 
and equipment to be used for excavation, subject to practical limits and safety 
considerations.  The excavation should comply with all applicable local, state, and 
federal safety requirements.  The contractor should be responsible for trench shoring 
design and installation. Trench shoring and bracing should conform to the appropriate 
health and safety requirements. 
In general, we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) for support of the utility lines.  The 
initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes should consist of 
free-draining backfills, such as No. 3B Fine gravel, to reduce the potential for pipe 
damage from compaction of the backfill. It is critical to use a free-draining granular 
material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and 
to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes.  The use of on-site clayey soils 
as backfill around the utility pipes is not recommended and should not be allowed.

3.4 
Drainage 
The finished grades outside the parking lot should be sloped to shed water away 
from the pavements and to reduce the potential for ponding.  Surface water ponding 
should not be allowed on the site during or after construction. 
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the 
above pavement recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil 
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength.  The pavement excavations 
should be properly backfilled against the edges of the pavement immediately after 
paving to reduce the potential for water infiltration. 
3.5 
Subsurface Infiltration 
We understand the installation of retention and infiltration systems for the on-site 
management of stormwater runoff are being considered for the project.  In order to 
obtain subsurface infiltration information, we conducted falling head infiltration tests in 
three borings (Boring Nos. 1, 3, and 5) as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  
These tests were performed in general accordance with the procedures in 
Appendix D of the State of Maryland Department of the Environment “Stormwater 
Design Manual, Volumes I and II” (rev. 2009). These procedures are consistent with 
other state’s procedures and generally may be considered an industry standard. 
The field infiltration tests were performed by augering borings to the selected test 
depths of about 9 and 10 feet below the existing ground surface. Upon reaching the test 
depth, a 4-inch inside diameter PVC solid casing was set to the bottom of the drilled 
hole to allow infiltration only through the soil exposed at the bottom of the boring. Falling

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based in part 
upon information obtained from the field borings and bulk samples. Variations of subsoil 
conditions between and beyond the field borings and bulk samples may occur, and the 
nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until construction is 
underway.  If variations then appear evident, Geolabs should be contacted to re-
evaluate the recommendations presented in this report. 
The field boring locations indicated herein are approximate, having been 
estimated by taping from the features shown on the Parking Lot Layout drawing 
received from ParEn, Inc. dba Park Engineering on April 4, 2016.  Elevations of the field 
borings were interpolated from the spot elevations and contour lines shown on the same 
plan.  The physical locations and elevations of the field borings should be considered 
accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict 
the approximate boundaries between the soil types, and as such, may denote a gradual 
transition.  Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown on the 
graphic representations and/or in the text of this report.  These data have been 
reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report.  However, it must be 
noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in rainfall, tides, temperatures, and 
other factors.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling five borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, extending to maximum 
depths ranging from about 6.5 to 10 feet below the existing ground surface.  The 
borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with augers. The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2 Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-5. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. The pocket penetrometer test results 
are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Five one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils. The test 
results are summarized on Plate B-1. 
Three Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic 
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-2. 
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed 
on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the soils.  The samples were remolded to near the optimum moisture 
content.  The test results are presented on Plates B-3 through B-5.

Infiltration Tests 
In order to provide information on the infiltration rates of the on-site soils, we 
conducted three falling head infiltration tests at the boring locations shown on the Site 
Plan, Plate 2.  These tests were performed in general accordance with the procedures 
in Appendix D of the State of Maryland, Department of the Environment, 
“Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I and II” (Rev. 2009). These procedures are 
consistent with other state’s procedures and may generally be considered an industry 
standard. 
The procedure used for the infiltration consisted of augering boreholes to depths 
of about 9 to 10 feet below the existing ground surface.  After reaching the test depth, a 
solid PVC pipe, or well casing, was installed in the borehole flush with the bottom so 
that water could only infiltrate into the soil through the exposed bottom soil. 
Water was placed into the open pipe and allowed to pre-saturate the soil prior to 
conducting the infiltration test.  Falling head infiltration tests were performed to 
determine the infiltration rates of the underlying subsurface materials.  Each test 
consisted of four trials of filling the casing with approximately 24 inches of water and 
taking periodic readings over a one hour trial period.
