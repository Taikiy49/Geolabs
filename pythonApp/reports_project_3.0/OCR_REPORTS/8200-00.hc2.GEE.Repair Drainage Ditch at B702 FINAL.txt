SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 25.6 and 26.4 
feet below the existing ground surface.  In addition, six Dynamic Cone Penetration (DCP) 
testing, designated as DCP Nos. 1 through 6, extending to depths of about 2.1 to 10.3 feet 
below the existing ground surface were performed at accessible locations along the existing 
drainage ditch alignment.   
Our borings generally encountered a surface fill layer about 8 to 13 feet thick 
consisting of medium stiff to very stiff silty clays and clayey silts and loose to dense silty 
gravel and cobbles underlain by residual soils, saprolite and weathered basalt to the 
maximum depth drilled of 26.4 feet below the existing ground surface.  In general, residual 
soils and saprolite consist of very stiff to hard silty clay, sandy silty and silt.  Medium hard, 
moderately weathered basalt was encountered in one of the borings at a depth of about 22 
feet below the existing ground surface.  We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled 
boring locations at the time of our field exploration.  
 Based on the preliminary topographic survey plan, the existing ditch slopes range 
from about one and one-half horizontal to one vertical (1.5H:1V) to 3.5H:1V inclination.  
In addition, an existing fence line is located near the top of the slope at the western bank 
of the ditch.  The hydraulic information for the ditch design was not available at the time 
of writing this report. 
Based on the ditch bed and embankment restoration work anticipated for the project 
and the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration, the following remedial 
measures were considered for the proposed drainage ditch repair: (1) Concrete Ditch with 
GRP Slope Protection; (2) Fabric Form Concrete Lining; and (3) Gabion Basket/Reno 
Mattress. Detailed discussions and recommendations for the three options are presented in 
this report.  
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and 
recommendations.

Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Repair Drainage Ditch at B702 project in Halawa on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, 
Plate 1. 
This report summarizes our findings and presents our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering 
analyses. These recommendations are intended to assist in the design of the drainage 
ditch project only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the 
limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located near Buildings B702 and B702A at the southeast corner 
of Camp Smith in the Halawa area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  Based on 
the information provided, the existing drainage ditch embankment has eroded significantly 
over time due to high velocity storm drainage flows that discharge from a 42-inch diameter 
storm drainpipe immediately upstream of the drainage ditch.  In addition, the security 
fence post footings located adjacent to the top of the western drainage ditch embankment 
have been undermined due to a combination of overland surface runoff, feral animal 
activity, and drainage ditch flow along the bottom of the embankment. 
Based on the information provided, the project will include but is not limited to 
constructing a lined ditch to stabilize the eroded earthen ditch bottom and embankment.  
In addition, the chain-link security fence along the top of the western ditch bank will be 
repaired.  We understand that considerations will also be given to the use of gabions and 
other semi-flexible or flexible lining alternatives to provide the most feasible design 
alternative for the project.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was formed by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from 
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to 
be middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island.  
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice 
Age) in age and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. As volcanic 
activity in Waianae Volcano ceased, lava flows from Koolau Volcano banked against its 
eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau, known as Schofield Plateau. 
The project area is generally composed of basaltic rock built by extrusion of the 
lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series.  These rocks are generally characterized by flows of 
jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers.  In-situ chemical 
weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years.  The 
weathering process has formed a mantle of residual and saprolitic soils.  In general, 
saprolite is composed mainly of silty material, while residual soils are more clayey.  Both 
residual and saprolitic soils are typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks.  The 
residual soils and saprolite grades to a basaltic rock formation with increased depth. 
2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is on the Marine Corps Base Hawaii at Camp Smith in the Halawa 
area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The drainage ditch in need of repair is an earth lined 
drainage ditch and is located on the northern edge of the site where Buildings 702 and 
702A are located.  The drainage ditch slopes downwards gradually from northwest to 
southeast and is mostly composed of fill material (clayey silt/silty clay) over residual soil 
(silty clay).  The ditch varies from about 6 to 11 feet deep as it slopes downwards.  Heavy 
vegetation with tall California grass, Hale Koa trees and various vines and brushes 
congest the area.  Surficial layers at the sides and bottom of the ditch are composed of 
brown clayey silt/silty clay with sand and gravel with varying degrees of soil consistency.  
The western ditch embankment had been eroded and steep embankment slope was 
observed.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the preliminary topographic survey plan, the existing ditch slopes range 
from about 1.5H:1V to 3.5H:1V inclination.  In addition, an existing fence line is located 
near the top of the slope at the western bank of the ditch.  The hydraulic information for 
the ditch design was not available at the time of writing this report. 
Based on the ditch bed and embankment restoration work anticipated for the 
project and the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration, the following 
remedial measures were considered for the drainage ditch repair: (1) Concrete Ditch with 
GRP Slope Protection; (2) Fabric Form Concrete Lining; and (3) Gabion Basket/Reno 
Mattress. Detailed discussions and recommendations for the three options are presented 
in the following sections. 
3.1 
Concrete Ditch with GRP Slope Protection   
Based on the information provided, we envision that the proposed drainage ditch 
repair may consist of constructing a new trapezoidal drainage ditch structure to replace 
the existing eroded earth lined drainage ditch.  Grouted Rubble Paving (GRP) may be 
placed on the ditch slopes above the concrete structure for erosion protection. 
The new trapezoidal ditch may be supported on a shallow foundation system.  An 
allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,500 psf may be used for the design of foundations 
bearing on the recompacted in-situ soils. The bearing value recommended is for 
dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as 
those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
For foundations bearing on the recompacted in-situ soils, the bottom of the footing 
excavations should be recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction to provide 
a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to the placement of reinforcing steel 
and/or concrete. If soft and/or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing 
excavations, they should be over-excavated to expose the underlying firm materials. The 
over-excavation may be backfilled with select granular materials compacted to a minimum

directly on the underlying competent materials. 
We recommend providing an 8-inch thick gravel cushion layer wrapped in a layer 
of non-woven geotextile fabric, such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent, between the bottom of 
the concrete drainage structure and the underlying subgrade soils. The gravel cushion 
layer is intended to provide for more uniform bearing support and should consist of free-

A 4-foot deep cutoff wall should be provided at the upstream and downstream end 
of the new concrete ditch to reduce the potential for scour below the concrete ditch. 
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by a combination of frictional 
resistance between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive 
earth pressure developed against the near-vertical faces of the embedded portion of the 
footings. A coefficient of friction of 0.35 may be used for footings bearing on the 
recompacted in-situ soils. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated 
using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf). 
The passive earth pressure value assumes the concrete for the footings is cast neat 
against the relatively stiff in-situ soils. Otherwise, the soils around the footings should be 
well-compacted (minimum of 90 percent relative compaction). Unless covered by 
pavements or slabs, the passive pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches below the 
finished grade should be neglected. 
The GRP slope protection should be a minimum of 18 inches thick and be 
well-drained to reduce the potential for build-up of hydrostatic pressures. A typical 
drainage system would consist of 2-foot wide strips of a prefabricated drainage composite 
product placed on the face of the slope. The prefabricated drainage composite product 
should extend from the top of the slope to the base of the slope and should be 
hydraulically connected to a weephole at the base of the wall. The weepholes should be 
spaced no more than 5 feet apart and should be hydraulically connected to prefabricated 
drains.

3.2 
Fabric Form Concrete Lining 
Another remedial measure consists of the installation of fabric form concrete lining 
along the ditch slopes and bottom.  In general, fabric form concrete lining is a concrete-
filled system that provides performance in erosion control, scour protection and repair, 
environmental and marine construction applications.  They are used to protect channels 
and stream banks from the forces of flowing water. The fabric forms are constructed of 
woven, double-layer, synthetic fabric.  The linings and mats are installed by positioning 
fabric forms over the areas to be protected and then pumping fine aggregate concrete 
(structural grout) into the forms.  The fabric forms can be placed and filled either 
underwater or in-the-dry.  The fine aggregate concrete is used in place of conventional 
concrete because of its pumpability, impermeability, and absorption resistance.  In 
addition to its significant performance advantages, fabric-formed concrete is economical 
to install.  It eliminates the need for heavy equipment and steel reinforcement.  It generally 
does not require specialized labor or dewatering of the project site prior to installation. 
We recommend providing an 8-inch thick gravel cushion layer wrapped in a layer 
of non-woven geotextile fabric, such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent, between the bottom of 
the fabric form concrete lining and the underlying subgrade soils. The gravel cushion layer 
is intended to provide for more uniform bearing support and should consist of 
free-draining materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) or 
similar material.  
The fabric form lining should be anchored at the top of the slopes using anchor 
trenches.  In addition, the upstream and downstream ends of the lining should be buried 
to provide cutoff walls.  Weep tubes can be installed at designated locations along the 
fabric form concrete lining to provide adequate drainage behind the lining on the slopes. 
3.3 
Gabion Baskets/Reno Mattresses 
This alternative consists of lining the ditch slopes and bottom with gabion baskets 
and mats (Reno Mattress).  In general, gabions consist of rectangular cages or mats 
made of hexagonal woven steel wire mesh laced together and filled with stone. Gabions 
are manufactured with double-twisted hexagonal mesh woven from annealed mild steel 
wire that are zinc coated.  The galvanized polymer coated mesh is made from wire with

the above characteristic and coated with a special formulated highly corrosion resistant 
polymer.  In general, there are two types of gabions that may be used:  a regular gabion 
basket at 1, 1.5 or 3-foot thick; or a gabion mat at 6 or 9-inch thick. 
A gabion structure generally offers the following advantages: (1) flexibility; 
(2) permeability; (3) low environmental impact; and (4) ease of construction.  For this 
specific project application in restoring the ditch banks and bottom, the gabions and 
mattresses are permeable and self-draining, thus allowing the stormwater to run through 
the gabions and mattresses when compared to the other types of stream bank structures. 
The disadvantages of this option generally lie in the availability of the stones and 
it is labor intensive in construction.  Our experience in the past for gabion construction 
projects is the difficulty in locating a source of the stones required that need to be of 
certain sizes, generally between 3 and 8 inches and have a minimum unit weight of 
140 pounds per cubic feet (pcf).  In addition, the stone should consist of hard basalt of 
such quality that the stone should not disintegrate on exposure to water or weathering 
during the life of the structure.  This type of stone may not be readily available and may 
need to be specially produced by the Contractor using screenings from the quarry. 
Another disadvantage is the difficulty in filling the gabion baskets or mattresses on the 
existing 1.5H:1V slopes.   
Should gabions and mattresses be selected for use on this project, we recommend 
providing a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) below the gabions and 
mattresses for separation from the subgrade soils.

3.5 
Site Grading 
We anticipate that the site grading work for the project will generally consist of 
minor cuts and fills along the drainage ditch alignment.  At the on-set of earthwork, the 
area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared. Vegetation, 
construction debris, deleterious materials, and other unsuitable materials should be 
removed and disposed of properly off-site or in a designated area to reduce the potential 
for contamination of the excavated materials with the spoils.  
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing should be over-excavated to 
expose firm natural ground, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
well-compacted fill. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be properly disposed 
of off-site. Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over-
excavation and engineered fill placement to account for variations in the over-excavation 
quantities. 
Imported fill and backfill materials, if required, should consist of non-expansive 
select granular material, such as crushed coral or basalt. The material should be 
well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension. 
In addition, the material should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the 
No. 200 sieve. The material should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) 
value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested 
in accordance with ASTM D1883. 
Fill and backfill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers 
to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density 
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the 
water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor site grading operations to observe 
whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation process and to

3.6 
Cut and Fill Slopes 
Cut slopes exposing fills and residual soils consisting of medium stiff to very stiff 
silty clays and clayey silts with varying amounts of sands, gravel, cobbles, and boulders 
may be designed with a slope inclination of 1.5H:1V or flatter provided the cut slopes are 
covered with GRP or concrete lining or gabions. 
Permanent fill slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter.  
Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the existing 
slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding.  The filling operations should start 
at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance 
with the above fill placement recommendations.  Fill slopes should be constructed by

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths ranging from about 
25.6 and 26.4 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, two bulk samples of the 
near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1 and Bulk-2, were obtained to evaluate the 
subgrade characteristics of the near-surface soils. The approximate boring and bulk 
sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plates 2.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1.

Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, Penetration 
Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler

blow counts.

compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on 
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Dynamic Cone Penetration Tests 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the existing ditch alignment by 
performing six Dynamic Cone Penetration (DCP) testing, designated as DCP Nos. 1 through 
6, extending to depths of about 2.1 to 10.3 feet below the existing ground surface.  The 
approximate DCP locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The procedures used in our DCP tests are in general accordance with ASTM D6951, 
Standard Test Method for Use of the Dynamic Cone Penetrometer in Shallow Pavement 
Applications. The DCP apparatus consists of a 5/8-inch diameter steel rod with a 60-degree 
conical tip. The rod is topped with an anvil that is connected to a second steel rod. This rod 
is used as a guide to allow a 17.6-lb hammer to be repeatedly raised and dropped from a 
height of 22.6 inches. The connection between the two rods consists of an anvil to allow for 
quick connections between the rods and for efficient energy transfer from the falling weight 
to the penetrating rod. 
Once the test apparatus is assembled, the DCP is placed at the test location and the 
initial penetration of the rod is recorded to provide a zeroing scale. While holding the rod 
vertically, the weight is raised to the top of the rod (22.6 inches above the anvil) and dropped. 
The penetration of the rod is measured after each drop. 
In the DCP tests conducted by Geolabs, the same equipment described above was 
used to record the depth of penetration per hammer drop, as shown in the field logs provided 
on the Logs of DCP, Plates B-1.1 through B-6.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Three Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits.  A graphic presentation of the test results 
is provided on Plate C-1. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM D6913) was performed on a selected soil sample to 
evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. Graphic 
presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate C-2. 
One Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression test (ASTM D2850) was 
performed on a selected soil sample to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the in-situ 
soils. The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressure was used as the applied 
confining pressure for the relatively “undisturbed” soil sample. The test results and the 
stress-strain curves are presented on Plate C-3. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected sample to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested.  Results of the direct 
shear test are presented on Plate C-4. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support and swelling 
characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates C-5 and C-6.
