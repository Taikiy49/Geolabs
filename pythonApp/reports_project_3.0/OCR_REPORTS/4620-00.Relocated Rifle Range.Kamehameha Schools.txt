2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was formed by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two main
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau beginning in the mid Pliocene Epoch The older
volcano Waianae was the first to develop closely followed by the development of Koolau
Volcano For a period of time Oahu was comprised of two islands formed by each of the
volcanic centers The volcanoes continued to erupt and banked against the slopes of
each other with Waianae terminating its eruptive activity prior to the cessation of Koolau
The main shield building activities ceased approximately 3 5 to 2 5 million years ago
The project area is generally composed of basaltic rock built by extrusion of the
lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows of
jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers In situ chemical
weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years The
weathering process has formed a mantle of residual and saprolitic soils In general
saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of
volcanic rocks The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with increased depth Fills
placed during the initial development of the campus may underlie the ground surface at
portions of the project site

3 5 Settlement Monitoring Program
Based on the existing topography and the design finish grades for the new rifle
range facility we envision that new fills up to about 20 feet thick may be required in
localized areas for the earthwork operations In general ground settlements are
anticipated when new fills are placed over the existing ground to raise the site to the
proposed finished elevations Based on the generally competent subsoil conditions
anticipated at the project site we believe that the anticipated ground settlements will be

primarily the result of compression of the fill material under its own weight Therefore we
recommend that the new fills be placed as soon as practical to allow the fill ground
settlements to occur prior to construction of the footings and other improvements on top of
the fill
In general new fills to be placed should meet the material and placement
requirements presented in the Site Preparation section of this report Adequate
precautions must be taken to reduce the potential for erosion of the fill slopes during

Based on the anticipated fill heights for the project we believe that a settlement
waiting period on the order of about 4 to 6 weeks should be incorporated into the
construction schedule for the subject development The settlement waiting period
should commence after completion of the fill placement We believe that the majority of

waiting period prior to construction of the foundations and other improvements on the

3 6 Pavement Design
In general we anticipate that the vehicle loading for the new pavements of this
project will range from passenger vehicles light pick up trucks handivans to heavy school
buses Based on our field exploration and laboratory test results we anticipate that the
pavement subgrade soils will likely consist of the moderately expansive clayey soils
anticipated at shallow depths Based on the above assumptions we recommend that the
following flexible pavement section be used for preliminary design purposes
Flexible Pavements Subjected to Passenger Vehicles and School Buses
2 5 Inch Asphalt Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
12 0 Inch Aggregate Subbase 95 Percent Relative Compaction
20 5 Inch Total Pavement Thickness over Compacted Moist Subgrade
The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction The aggregate base course and aggregate subbase
should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to not less than 95 percent relative
compaction Additional CBR and field density tests should be performed on the actual
subgrade soils encountered during construction to confirm the adequacy of the above
pavement section
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to
carry surface water runoff off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the
site during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in
or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs be extended a minimum
of 2 inches into the subgrade soil below the aggregate base and subbase course layers to
reduce the potential for migration of landscape water into the pavement section
Alternatively a subdrain system could be constructed to collect excessive water from
landscaping irrigation For long term performance we recommend that a subdrain system
be constructed adjacent to the paved landscaped areas

3 7 Utility Trench
New utility connections may be required to serve the new rifle range facility In
general good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling of
the trench for the planned utilities The method and equipment to be used for trench
excavation should be determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety
considerations In addition the excavations should comply with the applicable local state
and federal safety requirements Trench shoring design and installation should be the
responsibility of the contractor
Based on the results of our field exploration it appears that conventional
excavation methods may generally be utilized for utility line excavation at the site The
majority of the surface fill soils encountered at the site are generally medium dense and
stiff to very stiff However some of the utility trench excavations may encounter pockets of
loose fill soils and dense cobbles and boulders as described in the boring logs which may
pose some excavation difficulties during the trench excavation
In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under
the pipes The initial trench backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of
free draining backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation to
reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from compaction of the backfill It is critical
that a free draining granular material be used to reduce the potential for formation of voids
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Use of the on site clayey soils as initial trench backfill immediately around the utility pipes
is not recommended
In general we recommend that a minimum cover of 3 feet be provided between the
top of the utility pipes and the finished grade to facilitate construction of the pavement
structure and compaction of the trench backfill The upper portion of the trench backfill
from the level 1 foot above the pipes to the finished subgrade should consist of the
approved on site soils or select granular material The backfill material should be

3 8 Drainage
The finished grades outside the rifle range facility should be sloped to shed water
away from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive landscape
watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to
foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the
potential for water infiltration into the subsoils Drainage swales should be provided as
soon as possible and maintained to drain surface run off away from the slabs and
foundations
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent loss of strength and or heaving of the near surface clayey soils The
foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after
setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
five borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 5 extending to depths ranging from
about 17 to 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations
shown on the Site Plan Plate 3 The borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling
equipment equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered is presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 5
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barre Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and
drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket
penetrometer tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
