INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed 0 6 Million Gallon MG Reservoir of the Kulani Water System in the South
Hilo area on the Island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering
recommendations derived from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses for the proposed new reservoir project These recommendations are intended for
the design of foundations site preparation and pavements only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located within the existing Kulani Correctional Facility in the
District of South Hilo on the Island of Hawaii Based on the information provided we
understand that it is proposed to construct a new reservoir as part of the Kulani Water
System Improvements project The general layout of the new reservoir is shown on the Site
Plan Plate 2
Based on the information provided the new reservoir will be of glass clad construction
with a concrete floor slab The reservoir measuring approximately 70 feet in diameter and
24 feet in height will be located on relatively level ground to the northeast of the existing water
tank and pump station The finish floor elevation for the new reservoir will be at approximately
5 315 feet Mean Sea Level MSL with a minimum 6 inch thick reinforced concrete slab
As part of the project construction connecting pipelines will be required to serve the
new reservoir In addition an access driveway and a 15 foot wide gravel service road will
be provided around the reservoir structure

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is situated on the northeastern flank of Mauna Loa The lava
formation encountered at the site appears to consist of pahoehoe flow which spread and
ponded as it approached the ocean Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or
billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly
GEOLABS INC
W O 4300 00 Page 5
encountered in pahoehoe lavas Cavities are formed when the lava is still in a molten state
and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities voids and inter flow cavities such as
blisters and pockets Lava tubes are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling
flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance
longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted
through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and
along clinker layers The permeability of the fractures and cavities is high therefore they
serve as the major water transmission features
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located within the existing Kulani Correctional Facility in the District
of South Hilo on the Island of Hawaii Situated on the northeastern flank of Mauna Loa and
less than five miles northwest of the Olaa rain forest boundary the project site receives on
average over 200 inches of rainfall a year with the region upslope receiving even more
precipitation Access to the front gate of the Kulani Correctional Facility may be via the
Stainback Highway which intersects Highway 11 a few miles south of Hilo
The surface of the project site is a relatively flat graded area consisting of crushed
Ava clinker in a sandy silt soil matrix Located approximately 100 feet east of an existing
water tank the area was probably graded at the time the existing tank was constructed
There has been little time for a soil profile to develop in the area Vegetation is mainly a
species of pine tree with sparse grasses Topography around the project site is described
as rolling with steep slopes marking natural drainageways The general trend of the surface
drainage is in an easterly direction
Based on the topographic survey map provided the existing ground surface
elevations across the project site generally range from about 5 322 to 5 324 feet MSL

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling two borings designated as
Boring Nos 1 and 2 at the proposed reservoir site to depths of about 15 and 30 feet below
the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site
Plan Plate 2
Our field exploration at Boring No 1 generally encountered hard basalt rock formation
interbedded with some thin clinker layers extending to the maximum depth drilled of
approximately 15 feet below the existing ground surface In Boring No 2 clinker materials
consisting of slightly weathered basalt cobbles in a silty sand matrix was generally
encountered to a depth of about 12 5 feet below the ground surface Below the upper clinker
deposit hard basalt rock formation interbedded with some thin clinker layers was generally
encountered and extended to the maximum depth explored of about 30 feet below the
existing ground surface Large cavities or voids were not encountered in the borings drilled
however lava tubes and cavities are commonly encountered in basalt formations Therefore
the potential for presence of lava tubes and cavities still exists at this project site similar to
other sites in the vicinity
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field
exploration However it should be noted that groundwater levels may vary significantly
depending on seasonal rainfall time of year and other factors
Detailed descriptions of the field exploration methodology are presented in
Appendix A of this report Descriptions and graphic representation of the materials
encountered in the drilled borings are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the subsurface conditions at the proposed reservoir
site generally consist of weathered clinker materials and hard basalt rock formation extending
to the maximum depth explored of approximately 30 feet below the existing ground surface
Based on our evaluation of the subsurface conditions with respect to the proposed reservoir

Based on the subsurface conditions encountered during our field exploration we
believe that a shallow foundation system consisting of a perimeter ring footing may be used
to support the proposed reservoir structure Due to the variable hardness of the weathered
clinkers and basalt rock formation anticipated at the foundation subgrade level we
recommend that at least 12 inches of compacted base course be provided below the footing
foundations for more uniform bearing support In addition it should be noted that the clinkers
encountered at the footing subgrade elevations bottom of the base course layer might exist
in a loose state Therefore the loose clinkers if encountered at the footing subgrade
elevations should be over excavated to expose the underlying dense materials or hard
basalt rock formation The over excavation may be backfilled with the excavated in situ
clinkers or imported base course material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction
Based on our field exploration and the proposed finished floor elevation we anticipate
that the new reservoir slab will straddle across the in situ clinkers and hard basalt rock
formation To reduce the potential for differential settlement and cracking of the slab
resulting from differing subgrade material we recommend that the existing subsurface
materials below the bottom of the tank floor be over excavated and be replaced with at least
12 inches of compacted base course
As previously indicated cavities are commonly encountered in the basaltic lava flows
Therefore prior to placement of the base course layer the excavated subgrades should be
proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton vibratory drum roller a minimum of six passes to assist
in detecting and possibly collapsing near surface cavities In addition we recommend that

Reservoir Foundations
Based on the existing topography and the subsurface conditions encountered at the
site we believe that shallow foundations may be used for support of the proposed new
reservoir An allowable bearing pressure of up to 4 000 pounds per square foot psf may
be used for the design of a perimeter ring footing bearing on 12 inches of compacted base
course over the recompacted in situ clinker materials or hard basalt rock formation
anticipated at the project site This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces
Due to the potential for presence of cavities in the basaltic lava flows in the vicinity
we recommend that a program of probing and grouting of the foundation locations be
implemented during construction A representative of Geolabs should observe the probing
and grouting operations to evaluate the extent of cavities if encountered Probing and
grouting requirements are further discussed in the Foundation Probing section of this
report
For footings bearing on the compacted base course over the recompacted in situ
clinker materials or hard basalt rock formation the bottom of the footing excavations should
be recompacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction where possible to provide a
relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to placement of the base course material If
soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations they
should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation may
be backfilled with on site granular soils or imported base course compacted to a minimum
GEOLABS INC
W O 4300 00 Page 9
of 95 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom may be extended down to the
underlying competent material
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 45 may be used for foundations bearing on the compacted base course over the
in situ clinker materials or hard basalt rock formation Resistance due to passive earth
pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 450 pounds per square foot
per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soil around footings is well compacted Unless
covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should
be neglected
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grades If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they
should be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane
extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the
utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for footings
bearing on the compacted base course over in situ clinker materials or hard basalt rock
formation as recommended herein to be less than 1 inch total with differential settlements on
the order of 0 5 inches
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of Geolabs
prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the foundation bearing
conditions and the required embedment depths

Foundation Probing
Based on our field exploration and previous experience in the vicinity of the site
cavities and or voids may be present in the basaltic lava flows encountered at the project site
To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of the cavities
below foundations we recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting at the
foundation locations be conducted during construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that probes be drilled at each interior
foundation location and at 10 foot intervals along the perimeter wall foundation The probe
holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below the
planned bottom of foundation elevation If cavities and or voids are encountered or
suspected during the probing operation additional probes should be drilled at closer spacing
to help delineate the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes and
cavities discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected at low to moderate
pressures A typical sand cement grout should have a slump of about 6 to 9 inches
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during bidding
In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and grouting operation
is complete The contractor should also be made aware that a longer lag time between
probing grouting operations and foundation construction might be required in the construction
schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of cavities
and to allow additional recommendations to be made if excess grout take and or changed
conditions are observed

Slab On Grade
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by weathered
clinkers and hard basalt rock formation at shallow depths To reduce the potential for
cracking of the tank slab due to differential settlement resulting from differing subgrade
materials we recommend that the materials below the bottom of the tank floor be over
excavated and replaced with at least 12 inches of compacted base course for more uniform
bearing support The 12 inches of compacted base course should extend a minimum of
2 feet laterally beyond the limits of the tank structure The base course should consist of
crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction For
the design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of about 250 pounds per
square inch per inch of deflection pci may be used for the compacted base course The
area adjacent to the reservoir slab should be backfilled tightly against the slab with
compacted on site soils and graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential
for water ponding around the slab and foundation
Site Coefficient
Basalt rock formation was encountered at relatively shallow depths from about 2 to
13 feet at the site Based on this information the project site may be classified from a
seismic analysis standpoint as being a stiff soil or rock site corresponding to a Type Soil
Profile A seismic site coefficient of 1 0 may be used for the project site for seismic analysis
of the project structure Table No 23 J of the Uniform Building Code 1991
Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and the design finish floor elevation of the new
reservoir we anticipate that minor site grading consisting of cuts of up to about 5 feet thick
will be required for the project construction
At the on set of earthwork the areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials
should be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas encountered
during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill should be over excavated
GEOLABS INC
W O 4300 00 Page 12
to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with
well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly
disposed of off site or used in landscaping areas if appropriate
After clearing and grubbing areas beneath future slabs and foundations should be
over excavated where necessary to provide the minimum 12 inch thick compacted base
course layer as recommended earlier Prior to placement of the base course layer the
subgrade soils should be proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton vibratory drum roller a minimum
of six passes to assist in detecting and possibly collapsing near surface cavities Soft loose
weak or yielding areas or cavities disclosed during site preparation operations should be
over excavated to expose firm ground and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with
structural fill material well graded 6 inch minus granular materials compacted to a minimum
95 percent relative compaction The material resulting from the over excavation should be
removed and properly disposed of off site The proof rolling operations should be conducted
under the observation of a representative from Geolabs
Aggregate base course material should meet the material requirements for Aggregate
for Untreated Base Section 703 06 as specified in the Hawaii Standard Specifications for
Road Bridge and Public Works Construction 1994 Imported fill material if required
should consist of non expansive imported granular material such as crushed coral or basalt
The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches
in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No
200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have
a maximum swell of less than 1 percent Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs
for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its
intended use
Base course and imported granular fill materials should be moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness
and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of
GEOLABS INC
W O 4300 00 Page 13
the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
Site grading operations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs It is
important that a representative from our office observe the site grading operation to evaluate
whether undesirable materials are encountered during the scarification and excavation
process and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those encountered in our
field exploration
Service Road Pavements
New service road with asphalt concrete paving may be required to serve the new
water reservoir Considering the light vehicular traffic anticipated for the service roadway
the following pavement section may be used based on the results of the borings and visual
observations
2 0 Inch Asphalt Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
8 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness over Compacted Moist Subgrade
The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 10 inches moisture
conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction If basalt rock formation is exposed at the pavement subgrade elevation
the pavement subgrades should be proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton vibratory drum roller
a minimum of six passes to assist in detecting and possibly collapsing near surface cavities
The base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to not less
than 95 percent relative compaction CBR and field density tests should be performed on the
actual subgrade soils encountered during construction to confirm the adequacy of the above
section The recommended section considers only light traffic conditions and assumes that
good drainage will be provided adjacent to paved areas

Water Lines
We envision that new water lines will be required for this project In general for
support of the water lines we recommend that a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under the pipes The initial
backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes should consist of free draining backfills such
as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from compaction of the
backfill It is critical that a free draining granular material be used to reduce the potential for
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the
sides of the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finish grade may consist of the on site granular soils or select granular
material The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed
in about 8 inch loose lifts and mechanically compacted to at least 90 percent relative
compaction Where trenches are located in paved areas the upper 3 feet of the trench
backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative
compaction
For utility lines located below the footprint of the reservoir tank the utility trenches
should be backfilled with concrete or lean concrete up to the bottom of the base course
layer The base course layer recommended below the tank floor should be placed above the
concrete to provide more uniform support for the tank slab The concrete around the utility
lines below the reservoir tank structure should be placed neat against the sides of the
excavation In addition the concrete backfill around the water line should extend laterally
beyond the perimeter of the tank a minimum of 3 feet
Since basaltic rock formation is anticipated at the project site trench work for the
water line installation may involve excavation in the basaltic rock formation and will require
some use of the hoeram or chipping Care must be exercised by the contractor to avoid
over ripping especially within the reservoir tank footprint which would disrupt the structure
GEOLABS INC
W O 4300 00 Page 15
of the rock formation resulting in a potential loss of bearing strength for foundations in its
vicinity As previously indicated the trench excavations within the tank structure footprint
should be backfilled with concrete or lean concrete placed neat against the sides of the
trench excavation up to the bottom of the 12 inch thick base course layer below the tank
Drainage
The finished grades outside the reservoir structure should be sloped to shed water
away from foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive
landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided These drainage
requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above foundation
recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be properly
backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of the concrete to
reduce water infiltration In addition drainage swales should be provided as soon as
possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away from slabs and
foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings to depths of about 15 and 30 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using truck
mounted drilling equipment equipped with coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation and
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered is presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced fractures or
breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would
be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the
following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the Practical
Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
