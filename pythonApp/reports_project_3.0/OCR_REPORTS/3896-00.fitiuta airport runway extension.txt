INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Fitiuta Airport Runway Extension project within the existing Fitiuta Airport
on the island of Ta u American Samoa The general location and vicinity of the project site
are shown on the Project Location and Vicinity Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for
the proposed runway extension project These geotechnical findings and recommendations
are intended for the design of pavements reinforced earth slopes and site grading only The
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the
end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
American Samoa is comprised of a group of seven islands including Tutuila Aunu u
Ofu Olosega Ta u Rose and Swains Island The seven islands encompass an area of
about 76 square miles and are located approximately 2 300 miles south southwest of the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3896 00 Page 5
State of Hawaii The island of Ta u is one of the three small islands of the Manu a group
Ofu Olosega and Ta u The Manu a Islands are remnants of several volcanoes along
the eastern part of the crest of a submarine ridge known as the Samoan Ridge
The island of Ta u was the largest volcanic center and built during the Pliocene and
early Pleistocene Epochs by the extrusion basaltic lava from a single shield volcano rising
more than 12 000 feet above the ocean floor and more than 3 000 feet above sea level
The summit of the shield volcano collapsed forming a caldera and was partially filled with
ponded lava flows and pyroclastic deposits Secondary volcanism appeared as two small
lava shields on the extreme northwest and northeast ends at the volcanic vents on rift
zones radiating from the caldera During the late Quaternary a complex of tuff deposits
around the northwest corner of the island near Faleasao coupled with flows of basalt and
olivine basalt at the northeast corner built the shelf at Fitiuta In general dikes are not
frequently observed however numerous dikes and sills are exposed on the south side of
the island Sedimentary deposits consisting of sand and gravel lie mainly along the shore
and a fringing reef borders much of the island The thick deposits of clays and silts may
be found in two marshes near Ta u Village

Pavement Joints
In general joints should be provided in rigid pavements for the following reasons
1 To reduce potential curling and warping stresses in the pavement caused by
temperature and moisture gradients across the pavement slabs
2 To reduce and control cracking due to volume changes in the concrete
3 To reduce damage to immovable structures
4 To facilitate construction
In general the maximum spacing of joints for lightly loaded rigid pavements should
be 12 5 feet for longitudinal joints and 15 feet for transverse joints Therefore we
recommend that a joint layout pattern consisting of square grids with a maximum spacing of
12 5 feet be used for the proposed runway extension In addition pavement grooving should
be provided on the finished slab for skid resistance Pavement grooves should be transverse
to the centerline in the direction of the drainage slope Paragraph 505 Jointing of Light
Load Rigid Pavements of the Advisory Circular should be referred to for joint details and
construction requirements

Subbase Preparation
The new rigid pavement section recommended for this project should consist of a
Portland Cement Concrete surface overlying a crushed aggregate subbase course The
primary functions of the subbase course consist of the following
1 To provide uniform support for the pavement surface course throughout the
design life
2 To improve load transfer across the joints
3 To reduce the potential for pumping by providing drainage through the use of
a material with little or no fines
4 To provide a construction platform to expedite construction
The subbase materials required should conform to the project specification
requirements The on site excavated granular soils such as the in situ sandy gravel may
be reused as a source of subbase material provided that they meet the project specification
requirements The crushed aggregate subbase course should be moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content placed in thin lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to not less than 100 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test
procedures for the crushed aggregate subbase Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density

Reinforced Earth Slopes
As mentioned earlier in this report the new fill embankment at the safety areas will
be designed with a fill slope inclination as steep as 1H 1V In general we believe that the
new fill slopes may be designed with a slope inclination as steep as 1H 1V provided that
the earth materials are reinforced with adequate reinforced slope geogrids or geotextiles
to strengthen the fill soils
Reinforced slope geogrids are generally polymer grid structures with high tensile
strength It generally provides a cost effective solution to slope stability problems which
may include the following insufficient right of way high surcharge loads poor quality fills
high seismic forces steep slopes or difficult landslide repairs When reinforced slope
geogrids are placed in soil the grid geometry interlocks with adjacent soil creating a
soil geogrid composite with greatly enhanced engineering properties Different grid
configurations are available to provide optimum soil grid interaction for a range of soil types
and slope reinforcement applications Reinforced slope geotextiles work in a similar
manner to reinforced slope geogrids The construction methodology is briefly described
as follows
As the slope is constructed horizontal layers of reinforced slope geogrids or
geotextiles are placed in the compacted fill at pre determined levels The lengths of the
reinforced slope geogrid or geotextile layers are designed to anchor potential failure zones
into stable interior sections of the embankment or hillside As forces develop within a soil
mass the high modulus reinforced slope geogrids or geotextiles are immediately pulled into
tension The reinforced slope geogrids or geotextiles transfer this tensile force from the
unstable soil back into the less stressed portions of the slope and stability is thus
maintained
Based on the grading plans we envision that a reinforced earth fill slope with a
slope inclination of 1H 1V is planned around the runway extension at both ends of the
existing runway We envision that imported non expansive select granular fill soils will be
used for the reinforced earth fill embankment Therefore we believe that a friction angle

construction will likely need to be imported Where imported fill material will be required for
the embankment construction the imported fill material should meet the requirements of
non expansive select granular fill as described below As previously recommended fill
materials for the reinforced earth slopes should consist of imported non expansive select
granular fill material such as crushed coral basalt or cinder sand The material should be
well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension
The material should have a California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 25 or higher and a swell
potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D 1883 It should also contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200
sieve
In addition the reinforced fill material should have an angle of internal friction of at
least 34 degrees when tested by the standard direct shear test ASTM D 3080 The sample
to be tested should be compacted to 95 percent relative compaction at moisture contents
above the optimum moisture The backfill materials should also have a Plasticity Index of
less than 6 when tested in accordance with ASTM D 4318
Fill materials for the reinforced earth slopes should be placed in loose lift thickness not
exceeding 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to at least 95 percent of the maximum dry density established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 91 Test Method Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density Compaction should
be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable
compaction equipment Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed to compact
the fill materials
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3896 00 Page 18
Since the existing terrain is sloping the new fills should be keyed and benched into
the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations
should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in
accordance with the fill placement recommendations discussed above For fill slopes with
a vertical height greater than 20 feet we recommend that a key be constructed at the toe of
the fill slope to provide stability of the new fill embankment against sliding The key should
be at least 12 feet wide and embedded a minimum of 2 feet below the lowest adjacent
finished grade A typical sketch of keying and benching is provided on Plate 7
Excessive water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and slope planting
should be provided as soon as the fill slopes are completed to reduce potential erosion of the
finished slopes In general slope benches are not required for the reinforced earth slopes
except at site specific locations Where slope benches are necessary we recommend that
a minimum 8 foot wide bench be provided at the mid point of the slope to reduce the
potential for erosion due to surface water run off Construction of earth berms interceptor
drainage ditches and the use of geotextile fabrics and erosion control mats at the fill slope
face should be considered to reduce the potential for erosion thus enhancing the long term
stability of the fill slopes

Settlement Monitoring
Ground settlements are anticipated when new fills are placed over the existing ground
to raise the site to the proposed finished elevations These settlements will affect the
construction schedule and performance of planned improvements on the fills Based on the
current grading concept new fills up to about 50 feet high will be required for the construction
of the proposed runway extensions Due to the substantial fill heights planned for the project
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3896 00 Page 19
compression of the fill material under its own weight may be anticipated during construction
In order to reduce the effects of the anticipated settlements on the pavement structures and
other improvements constructed on fills a settlement waiting period should be implemented
after placement of the fills and prior to construction of improvements such as the pavement
structure on the fills Based on the existing ground conditions and the compaction
requirements we estimate that the settlement waiting period may be on the order of about
6 to 8 weeks
It should be recognized that it is difficult to accurately predict the exact time required
for the filled ground to settle Therefore a settlement monitoring program should be
established to evaluate the magnitude and rate of the settlements during construction prior
to construction of the pavement structure and other improvements on the fills Provisions
should be made for potential delays in the construction schedule if a longer settlement period
is required To monitor the filled ground settlement we recommend that a minimum of five
settlement markers at each runway extension be installed near the finished grades after
completion of embankment construction The markers should be read optically once a
week by a qualified professional surveyor and the readings should be transmitted for review
and comment in a timely manner

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions along the proposed runway extension alignment were
explored by drilling and sampling four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 to
depths ranging from about 22 to 47 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate
locations of the drilled borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 3 The borings were drilled
using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation and
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler
the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the logs of borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of the dense basalt rock formations encountered at the site were
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and would be shown on
the Logs of Borings as REC 80

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in

fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50

APPENDIX B

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed on
selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The
results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Three sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 were performed on selected samples of the
soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification
Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are presented on Plate B 1
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the
soils Dynamic compaction of the samples was performed by dropping a 10 pound hammer
from a height of 18 inches The samples were compacted in 5 equal layers with each layer
receiving 56 blows of the hammer The samples were soaked for 1 day prior to the
penetration test Results of the tests are presented on Plates B 2 and B 3
