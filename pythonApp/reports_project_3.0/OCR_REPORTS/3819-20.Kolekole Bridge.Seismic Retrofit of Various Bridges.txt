for the Seismic Retrofit of Kolekole Bridge in the District of South Hilo on the Island of

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an
area of approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the eruptive activity
of five major shield volcanoes Kohala which has long been extinct Mauna Kea which
has had some activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801
and Mauna Loa and Kilauea which are both considered to be active
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield
Volcano which had been built up by successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and
pyroclastic materials The most recent lava flows that comprise the ground surface and
shallow subsurface at the project site are believed to be Pliocene to Pleistocene in age
approximately 12 million years to 2 million years before present time and belong to the
upper member of the Hamakua Volcanic Series
The lava formation at the site generally appears to be of pahoehoe type flow which
is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of
vesicular porous rock The basalt formations of the project area are typically layered with
thin flows of dense basalts and scoria basalts Clinker layers are also commonly found
interbedded between the pahoehoe flows In situ weathering of the basalt formation has
yielded layers of soft highly weathered basalts interbedded with more competent hard
basalt formation
Cavities or voids are commonly encountered in pahoehoe type lava These
cavities are formed when the lava is still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes
intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes are formed
when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which
may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Blisters and pockets
inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii and in the heads of valleys the erosional
processes are dominated by detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and

2 2 Faulting
Based on our review of available geologic information the Kolekole Bridge project
site is not located in the immediate proximity of mapped geologic fault features It appears
that there are no known faults within about 5 miles of the project site Due to the close
proximity of the project site to active volcanoes and mapped fault zones the project site is
considered to be located in a seismic zone of potential recurring activity Earthquake
activity associated with fault movement and underground magma movement should be
anticipated However the potential for fault rupture at the ground surface of the project
site is unlikely

The abutment structure may be considered a large footing bearing on the
abutment fill soils for resistance of lateral loads during a seismic event in the

3 2 Rock Anchors
As mentioned previously a passive system to reduce the longitudinal lateral
deflection of the intermediate piers of the bridge structure is planned as part of the seismic
retrofit of Kolekole Bridge The passive restraining system will include rock anchors
installed between Pier Nos 1 and 2 and between Pier No 5 and Abutment No 2
A rock anchor is constructed by drilling a borehole installing a steel tendon or
reinforcing bar into the borehole and grouting the borehole The basic components of a
grouted rock anchor consist of the anchorage and the bonded and unbonded lengths of
the steel tendon or reinforcing bar In general the unbonded length of the rock anchor is
the portion of the steel tendon or reinforcing bar that does not have direct contact with
the grout allowing the tendon or reinforcing bar to elongate elastically and transferring
the resisting force from the structure to the bonded length The bonded length of the rock
anchor is the portion of the steel tendon or reinforcing bar that is directly bonded to the
grout allowing it to transfer the applied tensile load into the ground
In general the rock anchors would derive support principally from the bond
between the grout and the soft to medium hard severely to closely fractured basalt rock
with interbedded clinker layers encountered at the project site The bond stress which is
the pullout resistance per unit area of the grout soil interface contact is variable depending
on the type of soil and grout the overburden stress and the construction procedures used
in installing the rock anchors For a rough estimate an average bond stress of
2 500 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the soft to medium hard severely to
closely fractured basalt rock formation The bond stress used in the design will need to be
confirmed in the field during construction Based on our analyses detailed
recommendations pertaining to the rock anchor design are presented in the following

Construction Considerations
The performance of the rock anchors depends significantly upon the contractor s
method of construction and construction procedures As a result of the potential
variations in the subsurface materials encountered at the project site we recommend
that a representative from Geolabs be present to observe the installation of the rock
anchors during construction Construction of the rock anchors should be performed by
a specialty contractor experienced in the construction of rock anchors In our opinion
the following may have a significant impact on the effectiveness and cost of the rock
anchors
Based on our field exploration drilling of the rock anchor holes may encounter
dense basaltic boulders and cobbles and soft to medium hard severely to closely
fractured basalt rock Special drilling tools for drilling into the boulders and cobbles
and the basalt rock formation will be required Based on our observations during
our field exploration it was observed that the sidewalls of the uncased borings
3 inches in diameter caved in within the boulder and cobble layers clinker layers
and the severely fractured basalt rock Therefore temporary casing of the drilled
holes for the rock anchors will be required when encountering boulders cobbles
clinker layers and the severely fractured basalt rock at the site
Grouting of the rock anchors has a major impact on the load carrying capacities of
the rock anchors A neat cement grout or sand cement grout mix should be used
Placement of grout should be by tremie methods beginning from the bottom of the

Rock Anchor Testing
In general we recommend that a creep test be performed on the first rock anchor to
be installed at each concrete restraining block before construction of the remaining
rock anchors The creep tests should consist of subjecting the rock anchor to at least
133 percent of the design loads and the load should be held for at least 8 hours
Pullout tests proof tests should be performed on all the remaining rock anchors
during construction to confirm the bond stresses used in the design We
recommend that all of the rock anchors constructed be tested for pullout The
pullout tests should consist of subjecting the rock anchors to at least 133 percent
of the design loads and the load should be held for at least 10 minutes
Creep and pullout tests on the rock anchors are integral parts of the design
Therefore we recommend that the creep and pullout tests be conducted under the
observation of a representative from Geolabs

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 The locations of the borings drilled
are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The depths of the borings ranged from about 42 8 to
55 7 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using truck mounted
drilling equipment and portable drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers
and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches Some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock formations encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the hollow
core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
Very Poor 0 25
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100
The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its
relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense basalt formation with a high
RQD value would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous
methods of excavation

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Four unconfined compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on selected
rock core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the underlying
basalt rock or boulders The test results are shown on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths and are summarized on Plate B 1
