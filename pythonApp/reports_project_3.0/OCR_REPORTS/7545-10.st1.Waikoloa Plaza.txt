SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered volcanic ash and clinker materials underlain by 
basalt formation. Volcanic ash of about 2.5 to 5 feet thick, consisting of stiff sandy silts 
with various amounts of gravel, was generally encountered at the ground surface. Based 
on the presence of cobbles and boulders at the ground surface, some cobbles and 
boulders may be embedded in the volcanic ash layer. The volcanic ash was generally 
underlain by clinker materials consisting of medium dense sandy gravel with some 
cobbles. Basalt formation was encountered at depths ranging from about 7 to 12 feet 
below the ground surface and extended down to the maximum depth explored of about 
30 feet below the ground surface. Basalt formation encountered were generally hard to 
very hard and slightly weathered. We did not encounter groundwater in the borings drilled 
at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend using 
shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings to support the new buildings planned. As 
an alternative, foundations for support of the structural loads of the new structures 
planned also may consist of thickened-edge slab footings. 
In general, the new structures should be constructed on similar foundation 
subgrade materials in order to reduce the potential for differential settlement and to 
provide for uniform support across the structures. In order to provide similar foundation 
subgrade materials for the future structures, consideration should be given to 
over-excavating the cut subgrades within the new building pads by at least 2 feet. The 
over-excavation should be backfilled with compacted structural fill. Where the new 
structures will straddle varying subgrade conditions, special provisions should be made 
for these locations. 
Due to the presence of volcanic ash soils, soft or pumping ground conditions may 
be encountered during construction, especially if the foundation excavations are exposed 
to inclement weather. Where soft or pumping ground conditions are encountered at the 
footing subgrades, the footing subgrades should be over-excavated until firm materials 
are encountered and backfilled with compacted select borrow subbase materials (3-inch 
minus granular materials) wrapped on all sides with filter fabric. The select borrow 
subbase material layer should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture 
content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to 
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers 
an area of approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of the 
following five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea (activity during the 
recent geologic time), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 – 1803), and Mauna Loa and Kilauea 
(both still active). 
The project site is situated on the western flank of Mauna Kea Mountain, which 
makes up the north-central portion of the island. Based on available geologic information 
and geomorphological interpretation, the project site appears to be underlain by basaltic 
lava flows of the Hamakua Volcanic Series of Mauna Kea Volcano, which were deposited 
during the Pleistocene Epoch (Stearns and Macdonald, 1946). 
Following the cessation of volcanic activity in the Kohala and Mauna Kea 
volcanoes, activity from the other shields resulted in widespread aerial fall of ash over 
much of the island. This ash has weathered rapidly into a fine silty soil, which is typified 
by low in-situ densities and high field moisture contents. In some locales, when the field 
moisture content is sufficiently high, the ash soil exhibits thixotropic properties (i.e., the 
soil temporarily loses strength when remolded and regains its strength after internal pore 
pressures dissipate). In more arid areas, the ash soil exists in a dry friable state even 
though field moisture contents can approach 100 percent of the dry unit weight. Our 
exploration indicates that the site is underlain by a surface mantle of this drier volcanic 
ash soil, which should transition to basalt formation and clinker at a deeper depth. 
The lava formation underlying the site generally consists of a’a and pahoehoe 
basalt type flows, which spread and ponded as they flowed toward the ocean. A'a lava 
typically is characterized by a porous, rough, and irregular flow surface resembling a 
jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders. Typically, a 
denser and more layered lava rock material is contained within the lava flow core. 
Pahoehoe lava is characterized by a smooth, rope-like or billowy surface and an internal 
structure of vesicular (porous) rock. Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe 
lavas. Cavities form when lavas are still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes

Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is in the Waikoloa area of the District of South Kohala on the Island 
of Hawaii. The project site encompasses an area of about 19.5 acres. The project site is 
bounded by Waikoloa Road to the north, Pua Melia Street to the south and east, and 
vacant undeveloped parcel to the west. 
The project site is generally an open, undeveloped land. Vegetation is sparse, 
mostly consisting of wild grasses and a few shrubs. A portion of Lot 2 appears to have 
been graded to a level pad in a fill condition. 
The surface materials generally consist of silty volcanic ash materials with varying 
amounts of sands and gravel. Some cobbles and boulders are also present at scattered 
areas around the ground surface. The ground surface is generally sloping down from east 
to west with local undulations. Based on the topographic survey map provided, the 
existing ground surface elevations range from about +952 to +1,014 feet Mean Sea Level 
(MSL). The topographic survey map of the project site is provided on Plate 2 for reference.

2.4 
Earthquakes and Seismicity 
Generally, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the 
tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic 
activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground movement 
of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The Island of Hawaii 
experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so small that they can 
only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of the earthquakes are strong 
enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of magma

3.1.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and 
other unsuitable materials, should be removed and disposed of properly off-site. 
As mentioned above, previously placed fills were observed at Lot 2 of the site. Fill 
materials previously placed at the site should be removed prior to placement of 
additional fills or construction of new improvements. 
Loose and/or soft soils, where encountered, should be over-excavated to expose 
firm and/or dense materials, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
well-compacted fills. The excavated loose and/or soft soils should be disposed of 
properly off-site and/or used in landscape areas, where appropriate.

After clearing and grubbing, the cut subgrades and areas to be filled should be 
scarified to a depth of about 8 inches (where possible), moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction. The compaction requirement should be increased to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction under areas subjected to vehicular 
traffic. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as 
a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D1557. 
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) corresponding to 
the maximum dry density. 
Where scarification is not practicable, such as in areas exposing dense basalt 
formation, subgrade materials should be proof-rolled with a minimum 20-ton 
vibratory drum roller for a minimum of eight passes. The vibratory drum roller also 
should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute. 
In general, the new structures should be constructed on similar foundation subgrade 
materials in order to reduce the potential for differential settlement and to provide for 
uniform support across the structures. In order to provide similar foundation subgrade 
materials for the future structures, consideration should be given to over-excavating 
the cut subgrades within the new building pads by at least 2 feet. The over-excavation 
should be backfilled with compacted structural fill. 
3.1.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, we recommend capping the fill areas of the project site with a minimum 
2-foot thick layer of structural fill materials. As mentioned above, consideration 
should be given to over-excavating the cut subgrades within the new building pads 
to provide at least 2 feet of compacted structural fill. Structural fill materials should 
consist of well-graded granular materials generally less than 6 inches in maximum 
dimension with sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted 
mass. In addition, structural fill materials should contain less than 30 percent 
particles passing the No. 200 sieve by weight.  
In general, the excavated on-site materials, including volcanic ash materials, may 
be reused as a source of structural fill materials, provided that they are processed

or basalt. The select granular fill should be well-graded from coarse to fine with

Boulders (i.e., rock fragments larger than 12 inches in maximum size but less than

1.

the optimum moisture, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative

"Rock" fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of the

3.3 
Probing and Grouting 
As mentioned above, cavities and/or voids are commonly present in the basaltic 
lava flows. To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from the 
collapse of cavities below foundations, consideration may be given to implementing a 
program of cavity probing and grouting of the building foundations during construction. 
Based on the preliminary grading plan, the majority of the new buildings will be 
supported on compacted structural fills, or recompacted on-site volcanic ash or clinker 
materials. In general, we believe the risk of potential collapse of cavities below the 
foundations would be low, especially where the depth to basalt formation exceeds 5 feet 
below the bottom of foundations. Therefore, we believe probing and grouting may 
generally be omitted for the new commercial structures planned at the site. However, if a 
higher degree of assurance against potential collapse of cavities below foundations is 
desired, a probing and grouting program below the foundations may be implemented, 
especially where depth to basalt formation is less than 5 feet below the bottom of 
foundations. 
Where the probing and grouting program is implemented, we recommend drilling 
probe holes at 10-foot on centers for the continuous strip footings and/or thickened-edge 
wall foundations. In addition, probe holes should be drilled at each isolated spread footing

3.8 
Permeability Testing 
Six constant head permeability tests were conducted in selected drilled borings to 
provide data for the design of the storm water disposal system planned at the project site. 
The permeability tests were performed within the clinker and basaltic rock formation at 
depths of about 15 to 30 feet below the ground surface. The permeability test results are 
presented in Appendix C. 
Based on our field permeability tests, the subsurface materials generally have 
coefficient of permeability (k) of 2.2 x 10-2 to 8.0 x 10-2 feet per minute at the locations 
tested. Based on the field permeability test results, we believe an average hydraulic 
conductivity of about 0.05 feet per minute may be assumed for the subsurface materials 
encountered at depths of about 15 to 30 feet below the existing ground surface. The 
hydraulic conductivity value roughly corresponds to an infiltration rate of about 
540 gallons per day per square foot of surface area (0.00083 cubic feet per second per 
square foot) at those locations. 
It should be emphasized that the hydraulic conductivity of the on-site materials is 
highly variable due to the presence or absence of significant voids/fractures in the lava or 
the presence of embedded clinker seams, which greatly affect the disposal capacity of 
the seepage pits and infiltration trenches. In general, the greater the number of clinker 
seams and/or voids/fractures encountered at the seepage pits and infiltration trenches, 
the higher the overall permeability and disposal capacity.

foundations and slabs also should be avoided. Planters next to foundations should be 
avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for excessive water

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling 
nine borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 9, extending to depths of about 15 to 
30 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled with a truck-mounted 
drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Topographic Survey Map and Site Plan, Plates 2 and 3, 
respectively. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on 
the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-9. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained by 
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, Diamond 
Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow 
bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit is 
mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were described in 
general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3.  The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods 
for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International 
Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic 
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Four Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected 
samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distributions is provided on Plate B-2. 
Seven Uniaxial Compressive Strength tests (ASTM D7012) were performed on 
selected rock core samples to evaluate the uniaxial compressive strength of the basalt rock 
formation encountered. The test results are presented on Plate B-3. 
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-4 through B-6. 
Three Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557A) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships. The test results are presented on Plates B-7 through B-9.

Permeability Tests 
As part of our field exploration program, we conducted six permeability tests in 
selected borings using the constant head test method. In general, the borings had a 
diameter of about 4 inches and extended to depths of about 15 to 30 feet below the ground 
level at the time of the tests. 
The constant head tests were performed by pumping water into the boreholes until 
the water surface stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a sufficient period of time. The 
water height and the rate of pumping were then measured to provide data upon which to 
base calculation of the subsoil permeability. Results of the permeability tests conducted in 
selected borings are presented on Plates C-1 through C-6.
