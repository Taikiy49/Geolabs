INTRODUCTION
In accordance with the request of Princeville Development Corporation we have
drilled two 2 test borings at the subject site and conducted percolation testing in the
borings
The approximate location of the project site and its vicinity is shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1 Our work was performed in general accordance with our proposal
to Princeville Development Corporation dated
This report summarizes our findings from the drilling and percolation testing at the
project site
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located in the Kilauea area on the northern side of the island of
Kauai Hawaii The site is currently fallow land which was formerly used for agricultural
purposes It is proposed to subdivide the site to create residential lots The project will
be developed as affordable housing for Princeville employees
The site is in an unsewered area It has been proposed to utilize centralized on
site treatment to handle domestic sanitary wastes generated in the subdivision area It

GENERAL INFORMATION
The project site is located in the Kilauea area on the northern side of the island of
Kauai Hawaii The general topography of the site and the location of the wells on the site
is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
There are no known active drinking water supply wells within one quarter mile of
the site Drinking water in this portion of the island of Kauai is obtained from wells
tapping the basal aquifers in Waimea Basalts The boring were drilled and the well was
installed in a basalt formation that appears to be Koloa Basalts

PHYSICAL CHARACTERISTICS
The site is located in the Kilauea area in the Hanalei District in the northern portion
of the island of Kauai Hawaii The climate of the area is mild with an average annual
temperature of about 74 F and an average annual rainfall of about 78 inches per year
The site is situated on a plateau to the north of the central mountainous portion of
the island The topography of this plateau is a gentle slope down towards the north from
the mountains to the ocean
The site was formerly used for agricultural purposes and has iain fallow for several
years
Regional Geology
The island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of the lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene
more than 2 3 million years before present Following the cessation of this main shield
building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of the basaltic lavas
of the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the alluvial
sediments of the Palikea Formation
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the island of
Kauai These rocks are generally characterized as thick flows of dense basalt extruded
from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various locales Associated with the
vents are pyroclastic material e g usually forming low cinder cones at the vent

GEOHYDROLOGIC CONSIDERATIONS
The ground surface elevation at the site at the time of drilling and testing was
approximately 315 feet above Mean Sea Level

the site Some seasonal fluctuations may occur in response to periods of extended
precipitation

Sea Level appear to be anomalous and seem ito indicate that the groundwater is in a
perched condition Therefore it is unlikely that the shallow groundwater encountered
below the site is in direct communication with either the basal Waimea aquifer or the
coastal waters to the north

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Permeability Testing
During our previous geotechnical exploration drilling falling head percolation tests
were performed in the borings at depths of up to 30 feet below the existing ground
surface
These tests indicated that the upper residual soil and saprolite have coefficients of
permeability in the range of about 9x10 to 3x10 centimeters per second This value is
considered to be relatively low for disposal of sewage effluent through injection wells or
seepage pits
Upon completion of the first boring a four 4 inch diameter Schedule 40 well
screen and casing was installed in the boring The upper six 6 feet of the well was
cased and the remaining interval was screened to allow water to flow from the well bore
to the surrounding formation A gravel pack was installed in the annular space between
the well screen and the formation Bentonite and grout seals were installed above the
gravel pack to the ground surface
Atter installation the well was developed by airlift pumping and surging until a
minimal amount of fine soil material was noted in the discharge from the well Following
development the static groundwater level was measured at about 16 5 feet below the
existing ground surface Constant head injection tests were then conducted in the well
for periods of approximately three 3 to four 4 hours Calculations of the data obtained

during these tests indicated a hydraulic conductivity on the order of about 2 1x10
centimeters per second
These tests indicate that the test well is able to accept approximately 42 gallons
per minute with a head build up of about 15 feet i e an injection rate of 42 gallons per
minute fills the test well to within one 1 foot of the ground surface This is considered
a very low injection rate
The results of these tests are presented in Appendix B
Effluent Disposal
Based on data provided to us the capacity of the proposed treatment plant would
have been about 30 000 gallons per day with a peak flow rate of 30 gallons per minute
However a low injection rate of about 42 gallons per minute completely filled the
well during our injection testing Our testing was conducted with clean potable water
Since it is not possible to treat sewage to provide completely clean effluent some
particulate matter will be introduced into the injection well during operation This along
with biological activity in the well will tend to clog the injection well and cause it to fail
It is our opinion that it is not prudent to use injection wells as the disposal method
at the project site since failure of an injection well could greatly impact the operation of
the wastewater system
Discussions with Mr Larry Dill of the Keith Companies indicate that it may be
possible to increase the size of the individual lots which would allow the use of individual
wastewater systems It is our understanding that he is consulting with representatives of
the Department of Health to further explore this alternative

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based in part upon
information obtained from field data points such as drilled borings and percolation tests
Variations of conditions between the field data points may occur and the nature and
extent of these variations may not become evident until operation of the injection wells
if constructed If variations then appear evident it will be necessary to re evaluate the
findings presented in this report
The location and elevation of the field data points were determined by others We
can not assume responsibility for the accuracy of the location and or elevation of the
borings
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the field data points
depict the approximate boundaries between soil types and as such may denote a
gradual transition
Water level data from the field data points was collected at the times given on the
graphic representations and or in the text and appendices of this report This data has
been reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report However it
must be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variations in rainfall tides temperature
and other factors

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by the drilling and sampling
of two 2 borings to depths ranging between 137 0 and 191 0 feet below the existing
ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 Truck
mounted rotary drilling equipment was utilized to drill the borings using hollow stem
auger air percussion and wireline coring tools
Samples of the rock material encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which uses a hollow bit
to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the annulus of the bit is
mechanically recovered for examination classification and testing The recovered core
were logged by an Engineer or Geologist from our firm Descriptions of the recovered
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 and would be shown on
the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material that is in excess of four 4 inches in length without any discontinuities
discounting any drilling induced fractures or breaks if 2 5 feet of sound material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 and would be shown on the
Boring Logs as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative
quality based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
Very Poor 0 25
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100
