SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the new Fire Pump Building project for Building 2065 within Schofield
Barracks in the Wahiawa area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the new
Fire Pump Building project for Building 2065 These recommendations are intended for
the design of foundations slabs on grade and site preparation only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
1 2 Project Considerations
The project site is located to the north of Lyman Road adjacent to the existing
Building 2065 within Schofield Barracks in the Wahiawa area on the Island of Oahu
Hawaii We understand that a new fire pump building is planned for the fire alarm
sprinkler system at Building 2065
We understand that the new fire pump building will be approximately 20 feet wide
by 25 feet long The finished floor will be set at an elevation of approximately
878 9 feet Mean Sea Level MSL The fire pump building will be located to the south of
Building 2065 In addition we understand that a new driveway is planned on the east
side of the fire pump building The new driveway will consist of asphaltic concrete
pavements
Based on the topographic survey the existing ground surface elevation range
between about 876 to 878 feet MSL We anticipate that minor site grading work will be
required to achieve the design finished grades

1 3 Purpose and Scope
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the subsurface
conditions at the project site The subsurface information obtained was used to develop a
soil rock data set for the formulation of geotechnical recommendations pertaining to the
design of foundations slabs on grade and site preparation for the new Fire Pump Building
project In order to accomplish this we conducted an exploration program consisting of
the following tasks and efforts
1 Mobilization and demobilization of a truck mounted drill rig and two operators
to and from the project site
2 Drilling and sampling of two borings extending to depths of about 21 and
24 1 feet below the existing ground surface
3 Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the borings by a field
geologist from our firm
4 Laboratory testing of selected soil samples obtained during the field
exploration as an aid in classifying the materials encountered and evaluating
the pertinent engineering properties
5 Engineering analyses of the field and laboratory data for the purpose of
developing geotechnical recommendations pertaining to the design of
foundations slabs on grade and site preparation for the new Fire Pump
Building project
6 Preparation of this report summarizing our work on the project and
presenting our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
7 Coordination of our overall work on the project by an engineer from our firm
8 Quality assurance of our work and client design team consultation by a
principal engineer from our firm
9 Miscellaneous work efforts such as drafting word processing and clerical
support
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration and the Logs of Borings are presented
in Appendix A of this report Results of the laboratory tests performed on selected
samples are presented in Appendix B

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and the younger shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to
early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series
of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as
the Honolulu Volcanic Series
Schofield Plateau was formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded against
the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene Epoch The
dips of the lava beds generally range from 3 to 5 degrees The lava flows on the plateau
have weathered to depths of 50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the red colors of the
soil The deep narrow canyons such as Waikakalaua Kipapa and Kaukonahua are a
result of this weathering The brown and reddish brown soils generally consist of ancient
alluvial deposits overlying the weathered rock

2 3 Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we recommend
that the new structure be supported on a shallow foundation system consisting of spread
and or continuous footings bearing on the hard to very hard silty clays and or clayey silts
The footing should extend down a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent
finished grade The bottom of footing elevations should be recompacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the
bottom of footing elevations the soft and or loose materials should be over excavated to
the underlying firm or dense materials The resulting over excavation should be backfilled
with well compacted fills
Based on our laboratory tests the on site silty clays and clayey silts exhibit a slight
potential for shrinking and swelling when subjected to moisture fluctuations To reduce the
potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from shrinking
and swelling of the silty and clayey soils we recommend that the subgrade soils
underneath the slab areas be properly prepared and capped with at least 6 inches of
aggregate subbase course The aggregate subbase course should be compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Because the fire pump building slab will be
subjected to equipment operation the 6 inch compacted aggregate subbase course
would also serve to provide uniform support of the slab subjected to vibrations
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering
recommendations for design are presented in the following sections of this report

3 2 Slabs On Grade
Based on our laboratory test results the on site clayey and or silty soils exhibit a
slight potential for shrinking and swelling when subjected to moisture fluctuations To
reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from
shrinking and swelling of the clayey and or silty soils we recommend that the subgrade
soils underneath the slab areas be properly prepared and capped with at least 6 inches
of aggregate subbase course The aggregate subbase course should be compacted to
a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Because the fire pump building slab will
be subjected to equipment operation the 6 inch compacted aggregate subbase course
would also serve to provide uniform support of the slab subjected to vibrations
The aggregate subbase course should be placed as soon as possible after the
subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented in the
Site Preparation subsection of this report The aggregate subbase course and the
underlying subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist until the final placement of
concrete Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend that the soils be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or prepared again
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather
and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted fill
We envision that some exterior concrete walkways may be required for the
proposed project We recommend that concrete walkways be supported on at least
6 inches of aggregate subbase or non expansive select granular fill The aggregate

Based on the proposed finished floor elevation and the existing ground surface

additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and engineered fill placement to
account for variations in the over excavation quantities
Existing underground utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the

3 5 Underground Utility Lines
We envision that new utility lines and utility connections will be required for the
proposed project In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular
bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be
used under the pipes The initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes
should also consist of free draining backfill such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the
potential for damaging the pipes from compaction of the backfill It is critical to use this
free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of
pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill
could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils
clayey silts and silty clays or select granular fill materials The backfill should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in about 8 inch loose
lifts and mechanically compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to
reduce the potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement
areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
3 6 Drainage
The finished grades outside the new structures should be sloped to shed surface
water runoff away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding
It is also recommended that gutter systems be installed around the new structures and
that the discharge be diverted away from the foundation and slab areas Excessive
landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should be avoided Planters next to
foundations should also be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the
potential for water infiltration into the subsoils
The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab
edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths of about 21 and
24 1 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers The approximate
locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample
depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Two 1 inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples of the
surface soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The test results
are presented on Plate B 1
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of
the test are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM D 1883 was performed
on a selected sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils The results of the test are presented on Plate B 3
