SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration disclosed that the majority of the project site is generally
underlain by medium stiff to stiff silty clays of approximately 2.5 feet below the
existing ground surface. Below the surficial fill layer, our borings generally encountered
very loose to medium dense sandy soil extending to the maximum depth explored of
approximately 23.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Shallow groundwater table
was encountered in the drilled borings at depths of about 2 feet below the existing ground
surface at the time of our field exploration.
Based on the existing grades at the project site, we envision that approximately
4.5 feet of compacted structural fill material will be placed at the site in order to
accommodate the flood height based on the Flood Maps. Based on this grading concept,
we believe that shallow foundations consisting of spread and/or continuous footings may
be used for support of the proposed two-story structure planned at the site. An allowable
bearing pressure of up to 2,500 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the design
of the footings bearing on the compacted structural fill material placed at the site.
Our laboratory test results indicate that the near-surface clayey soils exhibit
moderately high potential for shrink/sweil when subjected to changes in the moisture
content. Therefore, we recommend that concrete slabs-on-grade be supported on a
minimum of 3 feet of non-expansive, select granular fill material. Exterior concrete
walkways and driveways may be supported on at least 18 inches of non-expansive select
granular fill or select borrow subbase to reduce the potential for structural distress to the
future slabs-on-grade resulting from the potential swelling of the surficial clayey soils and
to provide more uniform support. Special attention should be given to the subgrade
preparation requirements presented in the "Site Preparation" section of this report to
reduce the potential for shrink/swell effects on floor slabs and flatworks.

SECTION 1.0 - GENERAL
1.1. Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the new Duplex Faculty Housing project located at 55-482 Moana Street
within the existing Hawaii Campus of Brigham Young University in the Laie area on the
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown
on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and
engineering analyses. These recommendations are intended for the design of
foundations, slabs-on-grade, retaining structures, and site preparation only. The
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at
the end of this report.
1.2 Project Considerations
The project site is located at 55-482 Moana Street (TMK: 5-5-13: 025) within the
existing Hawaii Campus of Brigham Young University in the Laie area on the Island of
Oahu, Hawaii, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. We understand that the new building
will be two stories high and consist of Concrete Masonry Unit (CMU) walls and slab on
grade construction. Detailed information pertaining to the structural loads was not
available at the time this report was prepared. Based on our experience with similar
projects, wall loads on the order of about 5 kips per lineal foot are anticipated for the
proposed building.
We understand that the design finish floor elevation for the new duplex building
will be set at about +10.60 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). Based on the existing
topography and the design finish floor elevation, we envision that new fills on the order
of about 4.5 feet thick will be required to achieve the design finish grade for the
proposed construction. A low retaining wall may be required along the property line for
grade separation resulting from the new fills placed at the site.

SECTION 2.0 - SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2.1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu, encompassing approximately 604 square miles of land area,
may be divided into four basic geographic provinces. The provinces include: the
1) Wai’anae Mountains, 2) Ko’olau Mountains, 3) Schofield Plateau, and 4) Coastal
Plain. The Island of Oahu was formed by the merging of basalt lavas erupted from the
two prominent volcanoes, the Wai’anae and Ko’olau Volcanoes. Of the two, the.
Wai’anae Volcano is the older having breached the ocean surface nearly 4 million years
ago. The Ko’olau Volcano rose above sea level approximately 2.7 million years ago
making it the younger of the two volcanoes.
In general, the two volcanoes were built by the long-term eruption of thinly
bedded a’a and pahoehoe lava flows, which erupted from elongate and linear trending
volcanic vents associated with the prominent rift zones of each of the volcanoes. The
Ko’olau lavas banked against the lava flows of the Wai’anae Volcano to form the
Schofield Plateau of the central interior portion of Oahu. The Wai’anae Mountains,
which form the western third of the Island of Oahu, contain the oldest basalt rock
formations on the Island of Oahu. The Ko’olau Mountains form the eastern two-thirds of
the island and include the rock layers exposed along the Ko’olau Pali extending from
Waimanalo to Kahuku.
During the final stages of eruption and continuing well after the cessation of
Wai’anae and Ko’olau eruptions, the island mass began to subside thousands of feet
under the tremendous weight of the rock. In addition to the subsidence of the island,
the force of stream erosion worked in conjunction with the large-scale fracture and
slumping of portions of the land mass to form the topography of today. Between
850,000 to 70,000 years ago, volcanic activity returned to the Island of Oahu with the
eruption of ash, cinder, and basalt lavas from vents located throughout the eastern and
southeastern portions of the island. These most recent eruptions are collectively known
as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. Subsequently, erosion by streams and ocean waves

some of the shallow sea coralline deposits are visible on the flanks of mountains or
buried beneath terrestrial sediments, evidence of the higher and lower stands of the sea
in prehistoric time.
2.2 Geology of Laie Area
The coastal area of La’ie lies within the Coastal Plain province of Oahu and is
located to the northeast of the Ko’olau Mountain province. As a result, much of the
generaily flat land area is underlain by unconsolidated coastal sediments (coralline silts
and sands) with pockets of hard, cemented sand dunes (sandstone) and
coral/limestone rock formation. Progressing toward the hills of the Ko’olau Mouniains,
the subsurface conditions gradually change to reflect an increase in thickness of
terrestrial sediments, such as the alluvial soils derived from the hills and valleys located
to the southwest of the coastline. The alluvial soils may overlie the buried coral and
sand deposits in the subsurface.
The coral and limestone rock encountered buried in the subsurface often contain
locally occurring void and cavity structures originating during the deposition of the
calcareous deposits in shallow marine environments. In addition, some of the voids
develop following the coral and limestone deposition as a result of the slow percolation
of fresh water down through the geologic strata leaching the calcareous mineralogy of
the materials.

SECTION 3.0 - DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our review of the soil boring data indicates that the project site may be underlain
by moderately expansive clayey soils overlying very loose to medium dense sandy
subsoils at shallow depths. In order to accommodate the flood height requirements
shown on the Flood Maps, we envision that the project site would be raised by placing
approximately 4.5 feet of compacted structural fill material over the existing ground.
Based on the grading concept and the subsurface soil conditions encountered at
the new duplex building site, we believe that a shallow foundation system consisting of
spread and/or continuous footings may be used to support the proposed two-story
structure planned at the site. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,500 pounds per
square foot (psf) may be used for design of footings bearing on the compacted structural
fill material placed over the in-situ soils.
Our laboratory swell test results indicate that the surficial clayey soils exhibit a
moderately high shrink/swell potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations. Therefore,
we recommend that concrete slabs-on-grade be supported on a minimum of 3 feet of
non-expansive, select granular capping fill material. Exterior concrete walkways and
driveways may be supported on at least 18 inches of non-expansive select granular fill
or select borrow subbase to reduce the potential for structural distress to the future
slabs-on-grade resulting from the potential swelling of the surficial clayey soils and to
provide more uniform support. The non-expansive select granular fill should extend
laterally a minimum of 2 feet beyond the new slab-on-grade footprints. It is our opinion
that the 4.5-foot thick compacted structural fill layer placed at the site for flood control
purpose may also serve as the capping layer recommended herein.
Based on the current grading concept, we understand that new fills on the order of
about 4.5 feet thick will be required to raise the existing ground surface to achieve the
design finish grade of the new building. Due to the presence of loose sandy subsoils
underlying the site at shallow depths, we recommend that the new fills be placed as soon
as practical to induce the anticipated ground settlements to occur prior to the construction

3.1. Foundations
Based on the grading concept, we understand that approximately 4.5 feet of
compacted structural fill material will be placed over the project site in order to
accommodate the flood heights needed for flood control purpose. Based on this
grading concept and the anticipated subsurface soil conditions at the new duplex
building site, we believe that shallow foundations consisting of spread and/or continuous
footings may be used for support of the proposed two-story structure planned at the
project site. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,500 pounds per square foot (psf)
may be used for the foundation design of the proposed new duplex building bearing on
the compacted structural fill material. The bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and
may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces.
For footings bearing on the compacted structural fill material, the bottom of the
footing excavations should be recompacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to placement of
reinforcing steel or concrete. If soft and/or loose soils are encountered at the footing
excavations, the soils should be over-excavated to expose underlying firm materials.
The over-excavation may be backfilled with structural fill compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction.
The compacted structural fill should meet the requirements of the non-expansive
select granular fill material presented in the “Site Preparation” section of this report.
Compacted structural fill should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted

to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the

adjacent below-grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the
potential for foundation settlement.
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance

earth pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A
coefficient of friction of 0.35 may be used for footings bearing on the compacted
structural fills. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf). This
assumes that the soils around the footings are well compacted. Unless covered by
pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be
neglected.

order of about one (1) inch total with differential settlements on the order of

3.2. Slabs-On-Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs-on-grade will be used for the proposed duplex
building construction. Based on our laboratory test results, the near-surface clayey soils

non-expansive, select granular capping fill material. Based on the grading concept, we
understand that the slabs-on-grade for the proposed duplex building will be supported
by the near-surface fill soils overlain by 4.5 feet of new compacted structural fills placed
to raise the existing ground surface to the design finish subgrades at the building pad
area. We believe that the 4.5 feet of compacted structural fill should be sufficient to
reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs-on-grade resulting from
shrinking and swelling of the clayey soils due to changes in the moisture content.
In general, we recommend that a minimum 4-inch thick cushion layer consisting of
No. 3 Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) or basaltic termite barrier (BTB) be
placed below the slabs to provide more uniform support of the slabs and to serve as a
capillary moisture break. To reduce future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to
floor coverings, an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the gravel or
BTB cushion layer. Flexible floor coverings, such as carpet or sheet vinyl, should be
considered since they can better mask minor slab cracking.

content, and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Slab
subgrades should be kept moist until covered by concrete. Where shrinkage cracks are
observed after compaction of the subgrade, we recommend that the soils be thoroughly
moistened to close cracks and recompacted to provide a firm base prior to concrete

3.3 Retaining Wall Structures
Based on the information provided, we understand that a low retaining wall may
be required along the property line for grade separation resulting from the new fills
placed at the site. In general, the following guidelines may be used for design of these
retaining structures planned at the site.
3.3.1 Retaining Structure Foundations
In general, we believe that retaining structure foundations may be designed with an
allowable bearing value of 2,500 psf. The foundations should have a minimum
width of 18 inches and should be embedded a minimum depth of 24 inches below
the lowest adjacent finished grade. For sloping ground conditions, footings should
extend deeper to obtain a minimum 6-foot setback distance measured horizontally
from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope. Footings oriented
parallel to the direction of the slope should be constructed in stepped footings.

3.3.3 Drainage
Retaining wails should be well-drained to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic
pressures. A typical drainage system should consist of a 12-inch wide zone of

the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes. As an alternative, a
prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used

hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall. Unless
covered by concrete, the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of impervious
material, such as the silty clay soils, to reduce the potential for significant water
infiltration behind the walls.

3.5 Settlement Monitoring Program
Based on the current grading concept, new fills on the order of about 4.5 feet
thick will be required to raise the existing ground surface to achieve the design finish
grade for the proposed new duplex housing project. As previously indicated, very loose
to medium dense sandy soils may be anticipated at depths of about 2.5 feet to the
maximum depth explored of approximately 23.5 feet below the ground surface across
the building footprint. Therefore, some ground settlements may occur when the new
fills are placed over the potentially compressible soil deposits. Therefore, we

recommend that the new fills be placed as soon as practical to allow the anticipated
ground settlements to occur prior to construction of the footings on top of the fill.
In general, new fills to be placed underneath the building slab should meet the
material and placement requirements presented in the “Site Preparation” section of this
report. Adequate precautions must be taken to reduce the potential for erosion of the fill
slopes during construction and in the settlement waiting period needed to attain the
required consolidation.
Based on the granular nature of the compressible soil deposits encountered at
the project site, we believe that a settlement waiting period of about one month should
be incorporated into the construction schedule for the project development. The
settlement waiting period should commence after completion of the fill placement. It
should be recognized that it is difficult to accurately predict the exact time required for
the filled ground to settle, since the settlement rates will be affected by variations of the
subsoil structure and the history of the subsoil deposition. For the soft subsoils
anticipated at the project site, we believe that the estimated settlement period could
vary by as much as 100 percent from the actual settlement period. Therefore, the
actual settlement rates should be monitored, and a settlement monitoring program
should be established to evaluate the magnitude and rate of the estimated settlements
during the settlement waiting period prior to construction of the foundations on the fills.
In addition, provisions should be made for potential delays in the construction schedule
if a longer settlement waiting period is required.
To monitor the actual settlement rate, we recommend that a minimum of

filling to establish a baseline. Subsequent readings of the settlement gauges should be
taken on a weekly basis for the entire settlement waiting period.

3.6 Utility Trench
We envision that new utility connections may be required for the proposed
project. In general, for support of the utility lines, we recommend that a granular
bedding consisting of 6 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) be
used under the pipes. Free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel
(ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), should also be used for the trench backfill up to about
12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce
the compaction effort on the backfill, thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes.
It is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of
voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the
pipes. Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to
the pipes.
We envision that loose sandy soils will be encountered at or near the invert
elevations along some of the utility lines planned for the project. Therefore, we
recommend that a subgrade stabilization layer consisting of 24 inches of No. 2 Rock
(ASTM C 33, No. 4 gradation) wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or
equivalent) be provided below the bedding layer for uniform support for the new utility
line. The stabilization layer should also extend beyond the sides of the pipe a minimum
width of one-fourth the outside diameter of the pipe or 12 inches, whichever is greater.
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of select fill material. The
backfill should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in
maximum 8-inch level loose lifts, and mechanically compacted to not less than
90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence.
Where trenches are below pavement areas, the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below
the pavement subgrade should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative
compaction.

3.7 Drainage
The finished grades outside the duplex building should be sloped to shed water
away from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding. It is also advised that
gutter systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away
from the foundations and pavement areas. Excessive landscape watering near the
foundations and slabs should also be avoided. Planters next to foundations should be
avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration
into the subsoils. Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and
maintained to drain surface run-off away from the slabs and foundations.
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent loss of strength and/or heaving of the near-surface clayey
soils. The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings
immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration.
