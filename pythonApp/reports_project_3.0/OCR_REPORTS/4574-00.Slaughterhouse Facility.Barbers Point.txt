performed for the New Slaughterhouse Facility project located at the Barbers Point
Agricultural Park in the District of Ewa on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location
and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations derived from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for the design of
foundations slabs on grade site preparation pavements and leaching field only The
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the
end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is comprised largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea levels
rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of accumulated
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the
sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials Land development and reclamation projects
within the last century have brought the project site to its present form

Foundations
Based on the current design concept and the generally competent subsoil
conditions encountered at the site we believe that spread and or continuous footings may
be used for support of the proposed new slaughterhouse facility An allowable bearing
pressure of up to may be used for design of footing foundations bearing on the
recompacted in situ soils or new compacted fills This bearing value is for dead plus live
loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by
wind or seismic forces
For footings bearing on the in situ stiff dense soils the bottom of the footing
excavations should be recompacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction to provide a
relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to placement of reinforcing steel or
concrete If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing

of the footing excavations should also be recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of

Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be required for the construction of
the new facility Based on our field and laboratory test results we believe that new building
slabs may be constructed on grade provided that the slab subgrade is properly prepared in
accordance with the recommendations presented below and in the Site Preparation
section of this report
In general we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer consisting of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be placed below the slabs to provide
more uniform support of the slabs and to serve as a capillary moisture break To reduce
the potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an
impervious moisture vapor barrier is recommended on top of the gravel cushion layer
Two inches of moist fine sand may be considered above the moisture barrier to provide
protection of the moisture barrier and to aid in curing of the slab concrete Flexible floor
coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered since they can better mask
minor slab cracking
The sub slab in the freezer room should be placed over 12 inches of open graded
granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation to reduce
the potential for formation of ice lenses underneath the floor slab Where the concrete
slabs on grade will be subjected to future vehicular traffic or machinery vibrations such as
the loading Zone or equipment pads a 6 inch layer of aggregate subbase is recommended
in lieu of the 4 inch thick gravel cushion layer In addition the 2 inch sand and vapor
barrier may also be omitted for these slabs The aggregate subbase should consist of
GEOLABS INC
W O 4574 00 Page 12
crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction We
recommend that the concrete slabs be designed by a structural engineer for the intended
loads and should be reinforced as a structural slab to accommodate some amount of
differential movement
For design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of about 250 pounds
per square inch per inch of deflection pci may be used for the compacted aggregate
subbase Where slabs are intended to function as rigid pavements a minimum slab
thickness of 6 inches may be used for preliminary design purposes Provisions should be
made for proper load transfer across the slab joints which are subjected to vehicular traffic
The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at least
12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
Slab subgrades should be prepared by scarifying the subgrade soils to a depth of
about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton vibratory drum roller to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction The compaction requirement should be increased to 95 percent
relative compaction for slab subgrades that will be subjected to future vehicular traffic
Slab subgrades should be kept moist until covered by concrete Where shrinkage cracks
are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soils be thoroughly
moistened to close the cracks and recompacted to provide a firm base prior to concrete
placement Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement
weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement
with well compacted fill

pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort on
the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is critical to use this free
draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes
and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper backfill material and
placement could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of on site soils or select granular
fill material The backfill should be placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and
mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the
potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the
upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Based on our field exploration the proposed site is generally underlain by dense
to very dense coralline deposits near the existing ground surface Therefore
excavations for underground utility installation will involve cuts into the underlying dense
to very dense coralline deposits It is anticipated that excavation near the existing
ground surface may be accomplished with normal heavy excavation equipment such as
ripping with large bulldozers However deeper excavations into the hard coral
formation may require the use of hoerams or by chipping
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the materials are based on our
visual observation of the existing site and field data from the borings performed at the

Drainage
The finished grades outside the new facility should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from the
foundation areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should
also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms
and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent loss of strength The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled
against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the
potential for water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible
and should be maintained to drain surface runoff away from the slab and footing
foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the new slaughterhouse facility inside the existing
western warehouse were explored by drilling and sampling three borings designated
as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths ranging from about 16 5 to 21 5 feet
below the existing ground surface In addition two shallow borings designated as
Boring Nos 4 and 5 were drilled to depths of about 5 feet below the existing ground
surface at the proposed leaching field site for field percolation testing The approximate
locations of these borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled
using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1
through A 5
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and
drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX C
Field Percolation Testing
The field percolation rates of the on site soils at the proposed leaching field site
were estimated by performing two falling head percolation tests in Boring Nos 4 and 5
Field percolation testing were performed in general accordance with the State of Hawaii
and Chapter 10 of the Ten States Standards to evaluate the permeability of the subsurface
soils for wastewater disposal The test results are presented on Plates C 1 and C 2
The percolation tests were performed by drilling 4 inch diameter boreholes to a
depth of approximately 5 feet below the existing ground surface Six inches of gravel were
then placed at the bottom of the boreholes to protect the bottom of the holes from scouring
and sediments
During testing the holes were carefully filled with water to a depth of about
12 inches over the gravel pack The time intervals and water drops were then recorded to
provide data upon which to base calculation of the percolation rates
