SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered medium stiff/dense surficial fills overlying very 
soft/loose to very stiff lagoonal deposits and medium dense to very dense coralline detritus 
extending to depths of about 16 to 16.5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Groundwater was encountered at about 5.3 to 10.4 feet below the existing pavement in the 
drilled borings at the time of our field exploration, equivalent to elevations of about +0.0 to 
+1.0 feet MSL. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change 
due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
We anticipate that the majority of the new sewer force main pipe inverts will be on 
the order of about 2 to 7 feet below the existing ground surface. However, the pipe invert 
will be extended to depths of about 10 to 12 feet below the existing ground surface, where 
the new force main traverses under an existing utility line. Installation of the proposed force 
main will involve excavation into the underlying fills, lagoonal deposits, and coralline detritus. 
In general, we anticipate that the in-situ soils along the proposed force main alignment may 
be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment. 
Where deep excavations (greater than 5 feet in depth) are planned, temporary 
shoring or sloping and benching would likely be implemented for the trench excavations. 
Based on our site observations, we believe that temporary cut slopes for open-cut 
excavations are not practical. In this case, trench walls would have to be cut vertically with 
the use of shoring during construction. One possible method of shoring would be to use 
interlocking sheet pile shoring. The use of a sheet pile shoring system may also serve as a 
cut-off wall to aid in the dewatering operations. 
Dewatering of excavations may be necessary where the existing groundwater level 
is above the bottom of the proposed excavation. Dewatering of the excavation may involve 
moderate to large volumes of water. Based on the results of our field permeability tests, we 
estimate that the subsurface permeability of the on-site soils may be on the order of about 
8.6 x 10-3 centimeters per second. 
In general, the near-surface sandy and gravelly soils encountered during our field 
exploration may be used in the upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches 
above the pipes to the pavement subgrade, provided that the materials are free of 
vegetation, deleterious materials, organic material, and clay lumps and rock fragments 
greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
geotechnical recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Repair Hickam Force Main and Flow Meter project at Fort 
Kamehameha Wastewater Treatment Plant (WWTP) located at Joint Base Pearl 
Harbor-Hickam (JBPHH) on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general 
vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project. 
These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of trench 
excavation, dewatering, thrust blocks, and pavements only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report.

SECTION 2. SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. The Koolau shield reached the 
end of its main shield-building phase about 2 million years ago. 
The geologic conditions underlying the project site are the result of a complex 
series of deposition and erosional events brought on by glacio-eustatic sea level changes 
and post-erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch. Following the 
cessation of volcanic activity of the Koolau Volcano, alluvial soil materials were deposited 
in a deltaic or estuarine environment in the Pearl Harbor area. 
The sea level fluctuated several times, receding perhaps 100 meters below and 
rising about 30 meters above the present sea level. Concurrent with a rise in sea level 
during the late Pleistocene era, the eruption of the Salt Lake-Makalapa member of the 
Honolulu Volcanic Series mantled the older alluvium with thick deposits of volcanic ash, 
which consolidated to form volcanic tuff (locally known as “mudrock”) layers. Subsequent 
erosion dissected and thinned those mantles of volcanic tuff. 
During the recent epoch, as sea level rose to its current stand, the area was 
covered with soft and loose lagoonal marine deposits. These deposits were subsequently 
covered with man-made fills placed during the middle portion of this century for land 
reclamation projects during the construction of the existing Pearl Harbor Naval Shipyard. 
The project site is located at the edge of the former shoreline of Pearl Harbor. The 
subsurface conditions of the project site generally consist of fill materials overlying 
weathered volcanic tuff materials, coralline formation, and alluvium deposits at greater 
depths.

3.1 
Trench Excavation 
We anticipate that the majority of the new sewer force main pipe inverts will be on 
the order of about 2 to 7 feet below the existing ground surface. However, the pipe invert 
will be extended to depths of about 10 to 12 feet below the existing ground surface, where 
the new force main traverses under an existing utility line. Installation of the proposed 
force main will involve excavation into the underlying fills, lagoonal deposits, and coralline 
detritus. In general, we anticipate that the in-situ soils along the proposed force main 
alignment may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment. 
Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal 
to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential for 
excessive ground movement. In addition, the excavated soils should be stockpiled at least 
15 feet away from existing underground utilities to reduce the potential for appreciable 
ground movement or subsidence, which may damage the existing underground utility 
lines. 
The above discussion regarding the excavation of the subsurface materials is 
based on field data obtained from the borings drilled at the project site. We recommend 
encouraging contractors to examine the site conditions and soil data to make their own 
interpretation.

It is important to install adequate sheeting prior to the excavation and to maintain 
it tightly against the excavation walls with proper bracing during excavation. The properly 
braced sheeting is essential to reduce appreciable lateral movements of the adjacent 
ground into the excavation, which may result in potential settlements or distress to 
adjacent structures or other improvements, such as pavement or utilities.  
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable 
safety requirements. The contractor should be solely responsible for trench excavation 
and the adequacy and safety of the shoring installation. The contractor’s representative, 
who should be required to continuously present on-site during excavation and 
construction works, will have the best opportunity to promptly observe changing 
conditions during construction, such as unforeseen subsoils, unexpectedly high 
groundwater table, inappropriate construction sequence or techniques, etc., which may 
affect shoring stability.

3.3 
Pipe Cushion 
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the 
load supporting capacity of the pipe. Therefore, the pipe cushion is an important design 
consideration. For pipes bearing on the stiff/dense surface fill soils, a minimum of 6 inches 
of compacted granular cushion material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
Gradation), should be provided below the pipe. Where soft and/or loose soils are 
encountered at or near the invert of the pipes, including but not limited to the lagoonal 
deposits, a stabilization layer consisting of an additional 24 inches of open-graded gravel, 
such as No. 3B Fine gravel, wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or 
equivalent) should be provided below the cushion layer for uniform support. A typical 
trench detail is provided on Plate 3. 
As an alternative, No. 2 Rock (ASTM C33, No. 4 gradation) wrapped in a 
non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) below the bedding layer may be 
considered as the stabilization material when the above 3B Fine Gravel stabilization layer 
is not able to bridge over the soft/loose lagoonal soil conditions.

3.4 
Trench Backfill 
It should be noted that loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the 
trench, the unit weight of the trench backfill material, and the friction resistance between 
the backfill material and the trench walls. To calculate the vertical loads on the buried 
pipes, we recommend that an average unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic feet (pcf) for 
the trench backfill material and a coefficient of friction of 0.3 could be used. Earth forces 
acting upon the pipe generally increase rapidly with the width of the trench. Therefore, 
the width of the trench should be kept to a minimum. Traffic loads on the buried pipes 
should also be considered for the portion of the pipes located in roadway areas. 
The first zone of backfill extending from the cushion material to at least 12 inches 
above the top of the pipes should consist of select free-draining granular material (ASTM 
C33, No. 67 Gradation) to reduce the compaction effort required and resulting stresses 
on the pipe. In addition, it is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential 
for the formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support 
for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill could result in backfill settlement and 
pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the subgrade should consist of the excavated on-site surface fill soils or imported fill 
materials that are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, organic material, and clay 
lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension. It should be 
noted that the fill requirements presented herein are intended as guidelines only and may 
be modified based on Geolabs’ additional laboratory testing and field observations on the 
available fill materials.

Imported fill materials used for trench backfill should be well-graded from coarse 
to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in the largest dimension. The material should 
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material 
should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher and a swell 
potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. Geolabs 
should test the imported fill materials for conformance with these recommendations. 
3.5 
Trench Backfill Compaction and Placement Requirements 
Backfill materials consisting of the on-site surface fill soils should be 
moisture-conditioned to at least above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not 
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction. The upper 3 feet below the pavement grade should be compacted 
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
Imported fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture, placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Aggregate base course and subbase 
materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed 
in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of 
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established 
in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by 
dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or 
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or ponding 
should not be allowed to compact the excavated surface on-site fill soils or imported fill 
materials. 
Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during backfilling to 
assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper 
moisture content.

Vault Foundations 
We understand that a new concrete vault structure will be required for the 
proposed flow meter. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we 
recommend an allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,000 pounds per square foot (psf) 
for foundations bearing on the stiff/dense surficial fill. This bearing value is for 
dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as 
those caused by seismic forces. 
If soft and/or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the vault structure 
excavations, a stabilization layer consisting of an additional 24 inches of open-graded 
gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation) wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N 
or equivalent) should be provided below the bedding layer for uniform support. 
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate the total settlements of the valve vault structure to be less 
than 1 inch. 
Lateral loads acting on the vault structure may be resisted by friction between the 
base of the foundation and on-site soils and by passive earth pressure developed against 
the near-vertical faces of the embedded vault.  A coefficient of friction of 0.30 may be 
used for foundations bearing on the compacted on-site soils.  Resistance to lateral loads 
due to passive pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 
250 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf).  Unless covered by pavements or 
slabs, the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below the finished grade should be 
neglected. 
The vault structure should be designed to resist the lateral earth pressure due to 
the adjacent soils and surcharge effects.  The following lateral earth pressure, expressed

3.8 
Settlements 
The primary settlement of the force main is normally caused by the difference in 
the unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted backfill 
material placed over the pipes. The net increase in loading will cause the settlement of 
the underlying subsoils below the trench invert. Based on our engineering analyses, the 
settlement on the order of less than 1 inch is anticipated for the project. 
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good 
workmanship will be engaged during construction. Additional settlement could occur if 
improper trench support is used. 
3.9 
Dewatering 
Dewatering of excavations will be necessary where the existing groundwater level 
is above the bottom of the proposed excavation. Groundwater was encountered at depths 
ranging between about 5.3 to 10.4 feet below the existing ground surface in the borings 
drilled during our field exploration, equivalent to elevations of about +0.0 to 1.0 feet MSL. 
Based on the groundwater levels anticipated, dewatering may be required at the planned 
excavations for the project construction. Therefore, dewatering provisions will need to be 
included in the contract documents for the proposed construction. Since the excavation 
may involve the discharge of groundwater, a NPDES permit may be required for this 
discharge. The contractor should consult their independent consultant for the latest 
regulations and information pertaining to the permit application.

Because of the granular nature of some of the subsurface soils, we anticipate the 
granular soils encountered are relatively permeable. Therefore, dewatering of the 
excavation may involve moderate volumes of water. Dewatering by means of a well-point 
system along the outside of the excavations is generally not recommended. The resultant 
areal depression of the natural groundwater table could induce consolidation of the 
compressible subsurface soils resulting in potential ground settlement, which could affect 
the existing structures. The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on 
depressing the natural groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor 
prior to dewatering. 
It is our opinion that a cut-off wall system, such as interlocking steel sheet piles, 
should be considered to aid in dewatering the excavation. However, sumps will be needed 
to collect water that percolates up into the base of the excavation or infiltrates through the 
sheet piles. The sheet piles should be driven to a sufficient depth to reduce the potential 
for areal ground subsidence and to reduce the amount of dewatering within the 
excavations in areas underlain by granular subsoils with generally high permeability. The 
use of an interlocking steel sheet pile shoring support system with jet-grouted soils (or 
tremie concrete plug) is relatively watertight, which should allow the groundwater levels 
outside the excavations to be maintained close to the original pre-construction levels. 
Therefore, some type of groundwater control requirement should be specified in the 
contract documents. 
Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the contractor. The selection of 
equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor, and he/she 
should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during 
construction depending on the conditions encountered. The dewatering method selected 
should have minimal impact on the groundwater level surrounding the proposed 
excavation. The dewatering operations should be coordinated with the shoring support 
so that the stability of the excavations is not jeopardized. The operations should be carried 
out without softening the bottom of the excavations.

3.10 Slab-On-Grade 
We anticipate the near-surface soils at the project site exhibit relatively low 
expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations. To reduce the potential for 
future distress to the lightly loaded slabs-on-grade resulting from shrinking and swelling 
of the on-site soils due to changes in the moisture content, we recommend capping the 
slab subgrade with a minimum 6-inch thick layer of non-expansive select granular fill. 
Prior to placing the non-expansive select granular fill, we recommend moisture-
conditioning the subgrade soils to above the optimum moisture content and compacting 
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction, where possible. The underlying 
subgrade soils and select granular fill should be wetted and kept moist until the final 
placement of slab concrete. Where shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of 
the subgrade, we recommend preparing the soils again as recommended above. 
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather 
and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with 
engineered fill. 
For interior slabs (not subjected to vehicular traffic or machinery vibration), if 
applicable, we recommend placing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting 
of open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) below the slabs. The open-graded 
gravel cushion fill would provide uniform support for the slabs and would serve as a 
capillary moisture break. To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration through 
the slab and subsequent damage to floor coverings, an impervious moisture barrier is 
recommended on top of the gravel cushion fill layer. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor slab-on-grade construction to observe 
the subgrade preparation and/or the compaction quality of the fill material.

3.13 Corrosion Potential 
Laboratory corrosion tests, including pH value, minimum resistivity, chloride 
content, and sulfate content, were performed on a selected sample obtained during our 
field exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of the near-surface soils at the project site. 
The test results are summarized and presented on Plate B-5 of Appendix B. 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower 
the resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to the corrosion 
of metallic objects. 
On the basis of the laboratory resistivity and pH results, the subsurface soils at the 
project site have resistivity values of about 3,000 ohm-cm and pH values of between 8.0 
and 9.0 within the upper 5 feet of soils, corresponding to a corrosion rating of 2 (Very 
Corrosive) based on the guidelines provided by the City & County of Honolulu – Board of 
Water Supply. Therefore, we recommend properly designing near-surface metallic 
substructures (such as piping) for protection against the potential for corrosion. 
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent, in part, on the chloride content and sulfate content found in the 
soil. In general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm), 
sulfate content of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH greater than 5.0 may be considered 
“non-corrosive” to underground concrete pipelines and structures. 
Based on the relatively low values of chloride content and sulfate content tested 
on the in-situ materials, we believe that the near-surface soils at the project site may be 
considered “non-corrosive” and either Type I or Type II (Type I/II) cement may be used 
for the concrete in contact with the ground. It may be appropriate to consult with a 
professional corrosion engineer to review the test results and provide detailed 
recommendations for corrosion protection.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1.1 and 1.2, extending to the depths explored of 
about 16 to 16.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations 
are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2.  
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. A graphic presentation of the test 
results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Three Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. Graphic 
presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on Plate B-2. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-3 and B-4. 
One set of Corrosivity test, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity (ASTM 
G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), was performed 
by our office and Cerco Analytical, Inc. on selected soil samples obtained from our field 
exploration. The test results are summarized on Plate B-5.

Field Permeability Tests 
The permeability of the near-surface materials at the project site was evaluated by 
performing constant and falling head permeability tests in Boring No. 1.2 at the 
approximate location shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The tests were performed at a 
depth of about 15 feet below the existing ground surface. 
During the falling head test, the test hole was initially saturated. After saturation, 
the falling head percolation test was performed by filling up the test boring with clear 
water. Time intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which 
calculations of the permeability values were based. The results of the falling head 
permeability test are presented on Plate C-1.
