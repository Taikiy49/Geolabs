Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Kuamoo Road Retaining Wall in the Vicinity of in the District of
Kawaihau on the Island of Kauai Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project
site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the project
These recommendations are intended for the design of the permanent tieback anchor
retaining wall only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to
the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2
Project Considerations
The proposed retaining wall repair project is located near on Kuamoo
Road in the District of Kawaihau on the Island of Kauai Hawaii The general location
and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
There are two existing Cement Rubble Masonry CRM retaining walls in the
vicinity of on Kuamoo Road Based on the information provided we understand
that the existing CRM Wall No 1 has experienced deterioration and is about 130 feet long
and 6 to 8 feet high CRM Wall No 2 is about 60 feet long and 5 to 7 feet high Both
walls are too low for guardrail connections and do not meet the current State of Hawaii
Department of Transportation and Federal Highway Administration standards
Based on the current design schemes we understand that the existing CRM Wall
No 1 will be restrained by a permanent tieback anchor system with a new facing The
new facing may be constructed using either cast in place or shotcrete A new guardrail
system will be installed on top of CRM Wall No 1 restrained by the permanent tieback
anchors while a new guardrail system will be installed in front of CRM Wall No 2 to
accommodate the State and Federal standards

Regional Geology
The Island of Kauai is believed to be composed of a single large shield volcano
built by the extrusion of basalt lava belonging to the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series In
general the lavas erupted over a long period of time during the Tertiary Pliocene Epoch
more than 2¼ million years before present A long period of volcanic inactivity combined
with deep erosion of the existing land surface occurred that was then followed by renewed
volcanic activity with the extrusion of basalt lava belonging to the younger Koloa Volcanic
Series along with concurrent deposition of alluvial sediments of the Palikea Formation
The age of the Koloa Volcanic Series is believed to be early Quaternary
Pleistocene Epoch in time
Basalt formations belonging to the younger Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the
ground surface of the eastern half of the Island of Kauai including the Kawaihau area
The rock formations are characterized as generally thick lava flows of dense basalt
extruded from vents aligned as north south and northeast southwest rifts Associated with
the Koloa vents are pyroclastic lava materials such as cinder and volcanic ash which
typically form isolated cones central to the vent center
The ground surface of the project area is composed of basalt rock built by the
extrusion of lava of the Koloa Volcanic Series The rocks are generally characterized by
dense flows of jointed vesicular basalt interbedded with thin clinker layers The tropical
weathering process over thousands of years has created a deep mantle of completely
weathered rock defined as saprolite or residual soil The residual soils grade to saprolite
soil interbedded with highly weathered rock with increasing depth In general saprolite
soil is composed mainly of silty and sandy materials that show some relict rock texture and
is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite soils gradually grade to
less weathered basalt rock with increasing depth

Existing Site Conditions
The existing CRM retaining walls are located on the Kuamoo Road in the vicinity of
in the District of Kawaihau on the Island of Kauai Hawaii The Kuamoo Road is a
scenic drive and is accessed from Kuhio Highway The existing Kuamoo Road is an
asphaltic concrete paved roadway and consists of one travel lane and one shoulder lane
in each direction with a total width of about 30 feet
Based on the information provided we understand that Kuamoo Road was initially
constructed in the late 1960 s Several improvements were conducted in 1970 s We
envision that majority of the roadway in the vicinity of the two CRM walls was generally
constructed in cut conditions based on the interpretations from the topographic map
provided A steeper cut slope approximately five horizontal to six vertical 5H 6V was
observed on the up slope side south of the roadway and extended about 40 feet high
followed by a relatively flatter slope A natural steep slope about 1 H 1V was observed on
the down slope side north of the roadway
The ground slopes down to Opaikaa Stream located north of the Kuamoo Road
The Opaikaa Stream extends toward the east and merges with the Wailua River located to
the south of Kuamoo Road
Based on the topographic map provided we understand that the existing elevations
of the site range from 120 to 160 feet Mean Sea Level MSL

Permanent Tieback Anchor System
Based on our field exploration the subsurface conditions behind the existing CRM
wall No 1 generally consist of medium dense saprolite materials and highly to
moderately weathered basalt rock formation Fill material consisting of stiff clayey silt
was only encountered near eastern end of the CRM wall No 1 about 6 feet behind the
CRM wall at the depths of about 1 5 to 3 feet The idealized subsurface profiles at
Stations 10 50 and 11 00 are presented on Plates 3 1 and 3 2
Based on the generally competent subsurface conditions and the current design
concept we recommend that a permanent tieback anchor system consisting of two levels
of the tieback be used to provide the lateral support for a new concrete retaining wall in
front of the existing CRM wall The new concrete retaining wall will be constructed by
either cast in place or shotcrete In general the permanent tieback anchor system will be
designed to provide resistance to the lateral earth pressures and lateral loads imposed
from the traffic loading on the roadway Details of the earth pressure diagram for two
levels of tiebacks for at rest condition are presented on the attached Apparent Pressure
Diagram Plate 4
In general the permanent tieback anchor system is constructed by drilling a
borehole installing a steel tendon or reinforcing bar into the borehole and grouting the
borehole The basic components of a grouted permanent tieback anchor consist of the
anchorage and the bonded and unbonded lengths of the steel tendon or reinforcing bar
In general the unbonded length of the tieback anchor is the portion of the steel tendon or
reinforcing bar that does not have direct contact with the grout allowing the tendon or
reinforcing bar to elongate elastically and transfer the resisting force from the structure to
the bonded length The bonded length of the tieback anchor is the portion of the steel
tendon or reinforcing bar that is directly bonded to the grout allowing it to transfer the
applied load into the ground
Details of tieback anchor are shown on the attached
Plate 5
We envision that the permanent tieback anchors would derive support principally
from the bond between the grout and the extremely to highly weathered basalt rock

Construction Considerations
The performance of the permanent tieback anchors depends significantly upon the
contractor s method of construction and construction procedures As a result of
the potential variations in the subsurface materials encountered at the project site
we recommend that a representative from Geolabs be present to observe the
installation of the tieback anchors during construction Construction of the tieback
anchors should be performed by a specialty contractor experienced in the
construction of permanent tieback anchors In our opinion the following may have
a significant impact on the effectiveness and cost of the tieback anchors
Based on the subsurface conditions in the area drilling of the tieback anchor holes
may encounter hard basalt rock formation Our laboratory tests conducted on
selected rock core samples indicated that the unconfined compressive strength of
the basalt rock is up to about 12 500 psi Therefore special drilling tools for drilling
into and through the hard rock will be required Temporary casing of the drilled
holes for the permanent tieback anchors will be required if caving in conditions
occur during drilling of the tieback anchor holes especially in the fill and saprolite
layers
Grouting of the tieback anchors has a major impact on the load carrying capacities
of the tieback anchors A neat cement grout or sand cement grout mix should be
used Placement of grout should be by tremie methods beginning from the bottom
of the hole The grout should be placed through the tremie pipe by pressure

Our borings encountered some severely fractured basalt rock and localized voids
As the nature of the basalt rock formation it is not uncommon that grout loss from
the drilled hole exceeds a few times the volume of the annular space between the
drilled hole and tieback The contractor should anticipate the potential grout loss
and incorporate the necessary costs into the unit prices during the bidding process
Tieback Anchor Testing
In general we recommend that a creep test be performed on the first tieback
anchor to be installed at each concrete retaining wall before construction of the
remaining tieback anchors The creep tests should consist by subjecting the
tieback anchor to at least 150 percent of the design loads and the load should be
held for at least 8 hours
Pullout tests proof tests should be performed on all the remaining tieback
anchors during construction to confirm the bond stresses used in the design We
recommend that all of the tieback anchors constructed be tested for pullout The
pullout tests should consist of subjecting the tieback anchors to at least
150 percent of the design loads and the load should be held for at least
10 minutes
Creep and pullout tests on the tieback anchors are integral parts of the design
Therefore we recommend that the creep and pullout tests be conducted under the
observation of a representative from Geolabs

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
of five borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 5 The borings were advanced to a
maximum depth of about 23 0 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were
drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight auger and rotary
coring tools The approximate locations of the borings drilled for the project are shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer geologist who monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 5
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barre Sampling of Soils by
driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained by using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core
run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

Project Considerations
The proposed retaining wall project is located near on Kuamoo Road in
the District of Kawaihau on the Island of Kauai Hawaii There are two existing Cement
Rubble Masonry CRM walls at present in the vicinity of The existing
CRM wall No 1 has experienced deterioration and is about 130 feet long and 6 to 8 feet
high CRM wall No 2 is about 60 feet long and 5 to 7 feet high Both walls are too low
for guardrail connections and do not meet the current State of Hawaii Department of
Transportation and Federal Highway Administration standards
Under this project the CRM wall No 1 will be replaced with a new cast in place
cantilevered concrete retaining wall to be constructed with a tieback system A new
guardrail system will be installed on top of the CRM wall No 1 and in front of the existing
CRM wall No 2
Four boreholes will be drilled to depths of approximately 25 feet below the existing
ground surface for the new concrete wall One additional borehole will be drilled to a
depth of 20 feet below the existing ground surface for probing of the depth of batter of the
existing CRM wall No 2 Drilling will be performed using a truck mounted drill rig Soil
samples will be collected from each borehole using standard split spoon samplers
Details for the proposed structures were not available at the time this fee proposal was
prepared We anticipate that the project site is underlain by residual soils overlying
weathered rock formation at greater depths

Purpose and Scope
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering exploration is to obtain an overview
of the subsurface conditions at the site to develop a soil rock data set for the formulation
of geotechnical engineering recommendations for the tieback system design of the
retaining wall To accomplish this we propose an exploration program consisting of the
following tasks and work efforts
1 Mobilization and demobilization of a truck mounted drill rig and
two operators to and from the project site
2 Drilling and sampling of five boreholes to depths of 20 to 25 feet below the
existing ground surface
3 Coordination of the field exploration boring stakeout utility toning and
logging of the borings by a field engineer geologist from our firm
4 Laboratory testing of selected samples obtained during the field exploration
as an aid in classifying the materials encountered and evaluating their
engineering properties
5 Analyses of the field and laboratory data for the formulation of geotechnical
engineering recommendations pertaining to the design of the proposed
retaining wall
6 Preparation of a formal report summarizing our work on the project and
presenting our findings and recommendations
7 Coordination of our overall work on the project by a project engineer from
our firm
8 Quality assurance of our work and client design team consultation by a
principal engineer from our firm
9 Miscellaneous work efforts such as drafting word processing and clerical
support
