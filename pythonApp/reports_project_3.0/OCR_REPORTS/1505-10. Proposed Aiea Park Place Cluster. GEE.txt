in the design of the site grading residential structure foundations and

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is situated in Aiea Gulch a steep sided valley in
Aiea Oahu Hawaii The general location of the site and its vicinity is
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
Most of the site is currently vacant land and is densely vegetated by
grasses scrub dryland trees and bushes such as Haole Koa Keawe and
Iantana Near the center of the site there is a large stockpile of soil
with rubble and construction debris This soil is generally expansive
silty clay being imported from off site
The ground surface in the lower portions of the site generally slope
toward the central axis of the valley at a grade of about 4H 1V The
ground surface below the vegetative cover is very stony and is littered
with many cobbles and boulders Some of the boulders exceed 3 feet in
diameter In the upper portions of the site rock outcrops predominate
and the grade becomes steeper averaging about 2H 1V
The site is drained by the intermittent stream in the central axis of
the gulch During our field exploration water was observed in the stream
bed and an area of seepage was noted on the east bank of the stream near
the stockpile area

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is currently proposed to subdivide and grade the site to construct

Regional Geology
The island of Oahu is composed of two 2 shield volcanoes Waianae
and Koolau built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas during the late
Pliocene and Pleistocene epochs Preliminary reconnaissance of the site
and its vicinity indicates that the area is underlain by thin residual and
transported soils derived from the weathering of Koolau lavas
Koolau basalts are typically thin bedded primitive lavas with a large
percentage of pahoehoe flows Cavities or voids such as lava tubes
are common in pahoehoe lava flows and are frequently encountered in cuts
or excavations
Aiea Gulch is typical of the steeply sided valleys of the leeward
side of the eastern portion of the Koolau Range After the initial period
of erosion alluvium and colluvium accumulated in the floor of the valley
followed by the formation and or deposition of a surficial clay soil
derived from the chemical weathering of the basic igneous rocks forming
the Koolau Range
Soil Conditions
Based upon our field exploration the subsurface conditions may be
categorized into three 3 general types of material as described below
1 ADOBE a dark brown to gray highly expansive clay soil
containing some organics roots and a few cobbles and boulders
This soil is characterized by a very high shrink swell potential
and low shear strength It is not considered suitable for use as
structural fill material or for foundation support
GEO 00015
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1505 10 Page 6
2 KOOLAU BASALT slightly to highly weathered basaltic bedrock
deposited as lava flows originating from the southeast rift of
the Koolau volcano and subsequently exposed by erosion This
material has excellent strength and little or no shrink swell
potential when excavated and placed as fill It is considered
excellent for foundation support and is a good source of fill if
reduced to manageable size Due to the hardness of portions of
the rock it may present difficulties in excavation
3 STOCKPILED SOIL The stockpile was placed in several stages and
consists of soils from several different areas This material
was not specifically included in our scope of work Therefore
the stockpile was not explored in detail nor was the material
tested However field observations indicate that much of the
soil appears to be expansive silty clay MH CH and that the
stockpile contains some organics demolition debris and other
miscellaneous materials
In general the lower slopes and the valley floor are mantled by a
surface layer of adobe clay This layer varies in thickness from about
10 feet in the lowest portion of the site along the intermittent stream to
zero where rock outcrops become the dominant surface feature
The clay layer overlies Koolau Basalt and varies considerably in
thickness throughout the site but generally decreases in thickness with
increasing elevation In the upper portions of the project site the soil
tapers out and the basalt is exposed at the surface
Generally groundwater level was encountered in the test borings
during our exploration The depth to water data and time of measurement
are shown on the Boring Logs Also following the rains which occurred
during our final field exploration efforts in late August and early
September 1988 seepage was noted where one of the access trails crossed
the stream bed near the stockpile site

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The field exploration disclosed that the project site is underlain by
a generally thin layer of adobe clay over rock formation with exposed
rock ledges in the upper slope
Deep cuts will be required along the upslope lots to create
sufficient level areas for the development Most of the cuts will be in
hard basalt rock formation therefore relatively hard excavation is
anticipated
Of the material types encountered at the site adobe clay
stockpile and rock formation the excavated rock is the most suitable
fill material Therefore the adobe clay and stockpiled soil should be
used in the deeper portions of the fill and capped with the smaller sized
excavated rock
Also an abundance of boulders are anticipated from the clay stratum
and rock excavation therefore boulder fills may be required

Material and Compaction Requirements
The adobe clay soil generally exhibits moderate to high swelling
potential Therefore the excavated adobe clay should be hauled
off the site or used in the deeper portions of the fill by blending
as discussed above and placement at a minimum of 3 feet below
finished grade
Much of the fill material will be generated from excavation in the
rock formation In general the excavated rock material less than
12 inches in size may be used as general fill material Most of the
fills will be placed along sloping areas which will require the use
of these smaller size materials which can be well compacted to
provide the necessary stability of the fills
To facilitate later excavation of house foundations and utilities
the upper 2 feet of fill should be limited to material smaller than 6
inches in size if sufficient smaller size granular material can be
generated
Fills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding ten 10 inches
in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 of the maximum
ary density as established by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 Structural
backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding eight 8
inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 of the
maximum dry density as established by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78

Conventional compaction testing is generally not practical in fills
consisting of excavated volcanic material Therefore we recommend a

Many boulders will be generated from the rock excavation Boulders

Excavation
The field exploration indicates that much of the site is underlain by
vesicular basalt formation at shallow depths
The vesicular basalt is generally only slightly weathered and hard
Based on our seismic surveys it is our opinion that most of this
basalt formation can be ripped with a HD41 dozer and possibly even
with a D 9 caterpillar tractor equipment or similar However due
to the variability of volcanic rocks local harder rock layers should
be anticipated and may require special excavating techniques such as
hoe ram excavation
The above discussion regarding the rippability of the underlying
basalt materials at the site is based on our field observations of
existing rock slopes visual examination of NX size rock cores
obtained from drilled borings and interpretation of the seismic
refraction survey data We recommend that all prospective
contractors bidding on this site should examine the rock cores
recovered from our borings if available and the rock outcrops and
make their own interpretation
Two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V fill slopes are recommended
It appears that most of the cut slopes along the eastern hillside
slopes will be in the basalt rock formation Cut slopes as steep as
H V may be used in dense unweathered basalt However due
to the presence of weathered zones and clinker layers which are
typically encountered and would require flatter slopes a design cut
slope ratio of H V is recommended for cuts into competent
rock formation

Drainage
The finished lot grade outside the slab pavement and footings should
be shaped to shed water away from foundations and to avoid ponding
condition Also it is advised that gutter systems be installed around
the house and that gutter water be diverted far away from the foundations

should also be avoided

bottoms and drains to prevent water from entering the subsoils

of the above recommended foundations since ponded water could cause
excessive subsurface soil saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of
strength The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled tight
against the wall or slab footing immediately after setting of the concrete
to prevent ponding condition
Drainage swales should be provided around the structures as soon as
possible and maintained to drain all surface run off away from the slab
and footing foundations

Natural Hillside Stability
In Hawaii one of predominant forces in weathering and erosion is the
geologic process known as mass transfer This process is the movement
of material down the side slopes of valleys primarily under the influence
of gravity The process of mass transfer ranges from very slow movement
of less than one inch per year soil creep to very rapid movement such
as the landslides which occurred on New Year s Eve of 1987 88 in Kuliouou
Niu and Hahaione Valleys
Soil avalanches are part of the normal weathering and erosion
processes in Hawaii These avalanches are generally small surficial
slumps or skin tears of the thin soil cover which occurs on steep slopes
and are generally not indicative of mass instability of the slope itself
They are also much more prevalent in the wetter windward side of the
island than a dryer area like Aiea
The preponderance of slides and slumps which have occurred during
this winter of 1987 1988 are most likely the result of having had a few
years of abnormally dry weather followed by the prolonged and the
torrential rains of the current wet season During the dry periods the
soil cover on the steep slopes desiccated and began to crack forming
fissures for water to enter and accumulate This season s long periods of
rainfall allowed for deep saturation of the soils and the heavy rainfall
events added water faster than the soils allowed percolation and runoff
This caused portions of the saturated soils to become overloaded and to
fail or slide
GEO 00027
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1505 10 Page 18
It should be noted that the recent New Year s Eve storm was an
extremely rare occurrence and the probability of reoccurrence is generally
believed to be very small
It can be seen from the above discussion that it is not possible to
predict the occurrence and extent of these soil avalanches as they are
primarily dependent on weather conditions
However due to the dryer weather conditions it is our opinion that
Aiea Gulch may be considered less susceptible to soil avalanches than many
of the other valleys
