In accordance with our revised fee proposal dated November 24, 2014, we have 
prepared a set of engineering guidelines and an Activity Hazard Analysis (AHA) 
pertaining to the proposed test pit exploration activity at the project site. The guidelines 
and AHA presented herein are developed from a geotechnical engineering perspective 
to facilitate the excavation of test pits in a safe, stable, and controlled manner.  
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
Based on the information provided, there are three undocumented fill sites 
encompassed by the study. The three fill sites are designated as HDOH Area Nos. 1, 2, 
and 3. The three sites are located on the down slope (south) side of Reservoir Service Road 
in the upper portion of the Kamehameha Schools Kapalama Campus. The Reservoir Service 
Road is an asphalt paved road accessed by Waonahele Road, which provides vehicle 
access to two existing reservoir tank sites.  
HDOH Area Nos. 1 and 2 are adjacent to fill sites situated west of the existing upper 
reservoir tank site. HDOH Area No. 3 is situated near the end of Reservoir Service Road and 
appears to be much smaller than HDOH Area Nos. 1 and 2. 
We understand the three fill sites may contain undocumented soil and rock materials 
as well as construction debris and some landscape waste. The fills were not placed under 
controlled and engineered conditions. It is desired to evaluate the long term stability of the fills 
and possible mitigation measures that may be taken to reduce future erosion and slide 
movements.

We understand that test pits will be excavated by an environmental consultant to 
evaluate the contents and condition of the fills from an environmental perspective. In addition, 
we understand that Geolabs Inc. will assist in the geotechnical engineering evaluation of the 
fill sites with respect to the potential for ground subsidence or movement during the test pit 
excavation efforts. A Geolabs representative will observe the condition of the existing fills as 
exposed by the test pits. In support of the combined environmental and geotechnical 
engineering exploration, Geolabs Inc. has prepared the following Guidelines for Test Pit 
Excavation and Activity Hazard Analysis (AHA) from a geotechnical engineering perspective. 
It should be noted that Geolabs, Inc. will not collect samples of the fill materials for 
testing and our engineering evaluation will be based on visual observation of the test pit 
excavation activity and the materials exposed by the temporary pit excavations. 
PURPOSE OF TEST PIT EXPLORATION  
We understand the purpose for the fill site exploration is to characterize the existing fill 
materials utilizing environmental protocol for material sampling and testing. The test pit 
excavation and environmental investigation will be performed by others under separate 
contract. In addition, the fill material stability will be evaluated during test pit exposure by 
Geolabs, Inc.  Geotechnical engineering recommendations pertaining to the stability of the 
existing fills will be provided by Geolabs, Inc. in a formal report. We understand the test pits 
will be dug using a track backhoe excavator. The maximum depth of exploration will be about 
20 feet below the existing ground surface utilizing vertical cut trenches and encompassing a 
minimized excavation footprint. 
The following are guidelines for test pit excavation and backfilling to facilitate the safe 
and controlled subsurface exploration activity at the project site.  In addition, an Activity 
Hazard Analysis (AHA) worksheet is provided on Plates 1 through 6 to assess the potential 
hazards associated with the planned activity. 
GUIDELINE PLAN FOR TEST PIT EXPLORATION 
1. 
Ensure a competent person is observing the excavation activity on a 
full-time basis (monitor person). The competent person shall have the 
following experience. 
a. 
Geotechnical engineering soil analysis and characterization. 
b. 
Ability to visually detect potentially hazardous conditions that could 
result in trench instability and soil movement. 
c. 
Authority to stop work if unstable trench conditions are 
encountered.

2. 
Test pit exploration should be conducted under generally fair and dry 
weather conditions. Continuous rainfall and/or ponding or flowing surface 
water should be terms for excavation cessation until site conditions and 
weather improve.  
3. 
Trench shoring will not be utilized for temporary test pit excavation; 
therefore, no entry of personnel into any pit deeper than 3 feet below the 
adjacent existing grade is permitted. 
4. 
Due to limited available space, excavation terracing will not be allowed to 
enable excavation reach to the maximum proposed depth of 20 feet below 
the existing ground surface. 
5. 
Excavator machine should work (park) from the fill area interior and reach 
outwards toward the fill site perimeter for digging. 
6. 
Heavy chipping with the backhoe bucket and use of a hoe ram is not 
permitted. During light to moderate chipping on hard but yielding 
materials, the excavation and overall fill area vicinity should be visually 
observed by the monitor person for signs of potential ground instability 
which may include development of cracks and fissures, slope and trench 
sidewall raveling, and strong ground vibration. 
7. 
Test pit exploration will be a cut-and-cover operation. Open trenches are 
not permitted prior to moving to the next dig site. Trenches should be 
completely backfilled prior to the day end of shift. 
8. 
Trench edge should not encroach within 20 feet from the existing top of fill 
slope edge, which is visually expressed by the break (change) in existing 
slope inclination. 
9. 
Excavation spoils should be deposited no closer than 20 feet or within a 
1:1 line of projection spanning from the bottom of the trench to the toe of 
the spoils stockpile. 
10. 
Excavated spoils should be segregated by size during the excavation 
progress. Boulders and blocks of material larger than about 4 feet in 
greater dimension should be separated from finer soils and debris during 
the progression of excavation to facilitate the trench backfill. 
11. 
While logging/observing the trench excavation, personnel should be aware 
of the potential for trench sidewall instability. A monitor person should be 
used to observe the excavation and surrounding area for the development 
of ground cracks and excessive sloughing of material from the trench 
sidewalls.

12. 
Personnel should maintain a minimum safe distance of about 10 feet 
during excavation and backfilling of the trench. Personnel approaching the 
excavation rim should employ a monitor person when approaching the 
excavation edge for necessary measurement and observation. Excessive 
raveling or caving of excavation may require excavation termination or 
require the deployment of a safety tie back line for persons approaching 
the excavation edge for necessary data recordation. The geotechnical 
engineer or representative (monitor person) shall determine when trench 
excavation should terminate based on visual signs of potential trench 
instability. 
13. 
A source of water for misting should be available on site for use when 
excessive visible dust is generated during excavation and backfilling. 
Airborne dust should be avoided and mist should be applied to reduce the 
potential for fugitive dust. The dust control misting should produce no 
ponding or ground surface runoff. 
14. 
Test pits should be terminated at a depth of less than 20 feet or once 
native ground conditions below the fill are encountered. 
15. 
Backfilling of the trench should be performed by placing lifts of 3 feet or 
less of material in the trench. Slight to moderate compaction effort should 
be applied to each lift using the backhoe bucket to seat the backfill 
material and reduce/remove potential entrained voids. Excess excavation 
spoils should be scattered and spread thinly to restore the pre-existing 
ground surface condition and grade. 
EMERGENCY RESPONSE PLAN 
Based on the Guideline Plan for Test Pit Exploration, safety controls for the test 
pit excavation rely on visual observation and detection of potential trench and ground 
surface instability by the geotechnical monitor during progressive excavation. Should 
trench instability develop, as evidenced by uncontrolled sidewall raveling or collapse 
involving blocks or masses of material, the geotechnical monitor will determine if the 
exploratory trenching should immediately terminate or proceed at a slower pace. If the 
geotechnical monitor determines that the trench should be terminated for safety 
reasons, necessary exploration information should be immediately collected followed by 
prompt backfilling of the trench. 
Should a sudden trench cave-in occur, all personnel and equipment shall move 
back a safe distance until the pit stabilizes (minimum of 20 feet). After evaluation of the 
condition by the geotechnical monitor, and if trench stability permits, necessary 
engineering/environmental information may be collected using a tieback lifeline for 
limited personnel approach to perform direct observation/measurement. Should the 
geotechnical monitor determine the caving trench is too unstable for personnel to 
approach the rim, the trench should be abandoned and backfilled immediately.
