INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our subsurface investigation
for the design of the new terminal building portion of the project at
Lihue Airport Kauai Hawaii State Project No K 1076 The general
site location is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This project is a part of the proposed New Lihue Airport Complex
which in addition to the new terminal building also includes
construction of the terminal roadways automobile parking area sewer
water and drain systems major drainlines and channels aircraft
parking aprons and realignment of cane haul roads and irrigation
systems Soils investigations had been performed for these related
projects and soils reports submitted under Separate covers The
general layout of the new Lihue Airport Terminal Building is shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1106 30 Page 2
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of the investigation is to explore the subsurface
conditions at the site to provide soil parameters for the design of
the proposed structure The following scope of services was performed
in general accordance with our proposal dated
1 Drilled 15 borings to depths ranging from 16 5 to 31 5 feet
below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled
concurrently with those for the design of the rough grading
realignment of cane haul roads irrigation systems and
drainage lines Separate reports were issued for these
improvements
2 Conducted various laboratory soil tests including CBR
laboratory compaction direct shear moisture density pH
resistivity and shrink swell tests to examine the charac
teristics of the site soils
3 Evaluated the ground conditions for the proposed terminal
building foundation support and provided recommendations for
the site grading and pavement thickness designs
4 Prepared a written report for the design use

PROJECT CONS IDERAT IONS
This portion of the project will include the main terminal
building a long passenger concourse holdrooms related enplaning and
deplaning loading bridges and tour bus loading and unloading areas at
both ends of the terminal A general layout of the terminal area is
presented on the Site Plan Plate 2 It is our understanding that
this layout has been modified Slightly from the original scheme upon
which our boring layout was based However site conditions appeared
relatively uniform and additional borings were deemed not necessary
According to the sets of drawings for the project dated May 21
and the new main terminal building will be about 240 by
1450 feet in plan dimension with two levels of facilities The upper
level identified as Terminal Level will include passenger handling
facilities concourse and bus parking areas The lower level iden
tified as Apron Level is 12 to 13 feet lower and will include
service yard for baggage handling and employee locker areas It is
also our understanding that there will also be free standing light
poles about 60 feet high adjacent to the terminal building
According to the project structural engineer for the terminal
building the anticipated foundation loads for the project will be on
the order of 100 kips per column Heavy overturning loadings are
anticipated at some of the column foundations as well as the loading
bridge and light pole foundations

The majority of columns will be spaced at about 25 feet spans in the
direction of the longer axes of the terminal building while column
spacing may vary from 20 to 25 feet in the direction of the shorter

It is our understanding that the Apron Level below the terminal
and the concourse will be used as service yard for baggage handling

SITE CONDITIONS
Surface Conditions
The project site is located on the outskirts of Lihue Town near
the eastern coastline of Kauai Hawaii The site for the new terminal
complex is situated to the southwest of the existing airport terminal
and is bounded by Kapule Highway on the west Ahukini Road to the
north the present Taxiway A to the east and southeast and sugar
cane fields to the southwest
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1106 30 Page 5
At the time of our field exploration in February 1982 the area
was cultivated with sugar cane by the Lihue Plantation Company Ltd
There were two cane haul roads extending into the area from Kapule
Highway Irrigation ditches crossed the site in many directions
leaving soft wet areas in the vicinity of the ditches Many weathered
basalt boulders were exposed along the existing irrigation ditches
The ground surface at the proposed terminal area generally varied
from about Elevation 160 on the southwestern Side to 150 feet at the
northeastern side of the concourse area It then slopes generally
down toward the northeast at gradients of about 1
Subsequent to the above field exploration in 1982 site clearing
and mass grading work had started in May 1983 under the K 1072 project
Geology
The island of Kauai was built up by a single large shield
volcano During the Tertiary period basaltic lavas of the Waimea
Canyon Volcanic Series built a roughly circular island to a height
over 17 000 feet above the surrounding sea floor Near the end of the
Tertiary Period this main shield building volcanic phase came to a
halt
GEOLABS HAWAII
Following a long period of erosion and subsidence volcanism
renewed during the Pleistocene epoch producing the Koloa Volcanic
Series These post erosional volcanics laid a veneer of fresh
basaltic lavas over the older Waimea Canyon Series across most of
the eastern half of the island
After the cessation of late volcanism the geologic forces of
weathering erosion and deposition during the late Pleistocene and the
Recent epochs have shaped the island to its present form
At the site the lava rocks of this late volcanism had been
deeply weathered to form a mantle of reddish brown residual clayey
silt soil

Subsurface Conditions
In general most of the project area was under cultivation at the
time of our exploration in 1982 The subsurface conditions consist of
a thin layer of topsoil over a relatively thick mantle of residual
soil consisting of stiff reddish brown clayey silt
The topsoil at the surface consists of dark brown slightly
organic clayey silt The topsoil has been tilled and plowed over and
intermixed with some decayed and burnt vegetation and other organic
matter from cultivation activities The topsoil layer is generally
about 1 foot thick
Below the topsoil layer was a thick layer of reddish brown clayey
silt of the residual soil derived from decomposition of the basalt
rock The soil was stiff to hard at shallow depth however it graded
to less stiff at depths of about 10 to 15 feet It was noted that the
moisture contents generally tended to increase and densities decrease
at 10 to 15 foot depth In general the in place clayey silt soil
exhibits low swelling potential However some of the laboratory test
results indicated that if the soil is recompacted at moisture contents
below the optimum moisture content the compacted soil may have
moderate swelling potential
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1106 30 Page 8
Boulders were encountered in some of the borings Based on
visual observations of the existing drainage ditch cuts it appears
that significant amount of boulders could be generated from the site
cutting and utility excavation
Groundwater Level
Groundwater was generally not encountered in the drilled borings
except at about elevations ranging from 135 to 137 feet in Borings 13
and 18 drilled near the existing main irrigation ditch
Although many of the remaining borings were drilled to depths
below elevation 137 feet they did not encounter groundwater It
appears that the observed groundwater levels may reflect localized
seepage conditions probably originating from irrigation ditches
reservoir and other sources The borings indicated that the seepage
may be carried by a weathered clinker or cinder layer underlying the
area

From a soils engineering point of view the site is suitable
for the proposed development Spread footings are recommended for
foundation support More specific discussions and recommendations are
presented in the following subsections
Site Grading
As a result of the rough grading work unsuitable material should
not remain at the site
However there will be an interim period of no activity between
the rough grading work and the actual terminal building construction
Therefore there may be some erosion and re deposition of soft silt
materials as well as germination of vegetation Any soft material or
other unsuitable materials that may have been left in place such as
those in the previous irrigation ditches should be removed and
replaced with suitable compacted fill
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1106 30 Page 10
It is probable that some small amount of new fills or backfill
may be required at various areas Prior to new filling the area
should be cleared of any loose materials vegetation and debris
Because the on site soil is Slightly expansive when compacted on
the dry side of optimum moisture content sufficient water should be
used to moisture condition the on site soil to the wet side of optimum
moisture content 30 before compaction in order to reduce its
swelling potential
Fills and backfills should be placed in maximum 8 inch lifts and
compacted to a minimum of 90 of its maximum dry density established
by ASTM D 1557 78
The finished slope ratios of not steeper than 2 1 H V for
compacted fill and H V for cuts into stiff residual soil are
recommended
Spread Footing
As discussed earlier the site is underlain by thick layer of
very stiff to hard reddish brown silty soils This type of material
generally can provide adequate foundation support for the proposed
structure
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1106 30 Page 11
An allowable soil bearing pressure of 3000 p s f may be used
with the bottom of the footing at a minimum of 2 feet below the lowest
adjacent finished grade Footing may bear on either undisturbed
natural soil or compacted fill A one third increase may be allowed
for the soil bearing pressure under wind or seismic loads
Any weak or soft soils encountered at the bottom of the footing
excavation should be over excavated and backfilled with suitable
on site material or Select Borrow type imported material These
backfills should be compacted to 95 of the ASIM D 1557 78 maximum
dry density Loose soil fallen into the footing excavations should be
removed prior to concrete placement
Occasional boulders may be encountered in footing excavations
If encountered small boulders should be removed and the cavity
backfilled with concrete Large boulders if not loosened should be
chipped to at least 8 inches below the bottom of the footing The
resulting over excavation should be backfilled with concrete
When footings are close to retaining walls or other buried
structures they should be deepened or situated in such a way that the
footing should be located below the 45 degrees plane drawn upward from
the rear edge of the wall or buried structural footings Stepped wall
footing may be used

Estimated Settlements for Spread Footings
For the column loads on the order of 100 kips we believe that
the total settlement will be up to one half of an inch with differ
ential settlements up to one fourth of an inch on a distance of 20
feet
Foundation for Light Poles Rotunda and Columns
With High Overturning Movements
It is our understanding that free standing light poles about
60 feet high will be required For design of the pole foundations
utilizing flag pole equation in the Uniform Building Code we
recommend allowable lateral bearing pressures of 250 p s f per
Foot of depth be used
For rotunda footings this allowable lateral bearing pressures
should be reduced to 150 p s f per foot of depth to limit accumulative
lateral deformation
Retaining Walls
A long retaining wall of about 13 feet high will be required for
the new terminal structure It is our understanding that some
portions of the retaining wall will act as free standing cantilevered
wall and others will be restrained
GEOLABS HAWAII
W Page 13
The general guidelines for retaining wall design are listed below
1 For lateral earth pressures the following can be used
Wall unrestrained at the top 35 p s f equivalent
active condition level fluid pressure per
backfill foot of depth
Wall restrained at the top 50 p s f equivalent
at rest condition fluid pressure per
level backfill foot of depth
Backfill material behind retaining walls should consist of
granular material such as Select Borrow or Aggregate Subbase
material Such granular backfill should extend at least 6
feet behind the back of the retaining wall Otherwise a
higher active earth pressure would result
If the wall backfill is to consist of the on site clayey silt
soils with only about 1 to 2 feet of granular gravel curtain
behind the wall the active earth pressure should be
increased to 45 p s f per foot of depth
2 Surcharge load may be converted to equivalent wall height of
1 foot high for every 100 p s f of surcharge We recommend
that suitable surcharge be selected according to the potential
usage of heavy construction equipment behind the new wall
To avoid damage to the wall we further recommend that such
an assumed surcharge load be stipulated in the construction
document
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1106 30 Page 14
Where two retaining walls abut each other at an angle that
portion of the walls should be considered as restrained
because the movement will be limited by the abutting wall
The walls then gradually become less restrained and change to
free standing condition away from the abutting joint
Although there is no set guideline for these transition
zones we suggest that transition zones be considered to
Start at a distance equal to the height of the wall
An allowable bearing value of up to 3000 p s f is
recommended for retaining wall foundations bearing on the
stiff soil
A passive earth resistance of 300 p s f per foot of depth
may be used for wall footings on level areas This passive
pressure does not apply for walls constructed on slopes
A base friction factor of 0 4 can also be used against
Sliding of the wall foundations
The wall foundations on relatively flat areas should be
embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the outside finished
grade
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1106 30 Page 15
For wall footings situated on slopes they should be embedded
an additional amount to maintain a minimum 10 foot horizontal
set back distance measured from the Slope face back to the
outside edge of the wall footing
8 Subdrain system behind retaining walls should be provided to
prevent build up of hydrostatic pressure Any subdrain
should be properly constructed and drain freely by gravity
into open space or drain manhole The subdrain pipe should
not be tied to the down spout of roof drains or other type of
small diameter drains This is to avoid drainage water from
flowing back into the subdrain System and into the back of
the retaining walls
9 The backfill material should be placed in 8 inch horizontal
lifts and compacted to 90 of the ASTM D 1557 78 maximum
density Only a light weight or hand operated compaction
equipment should be used to compact the backfill Other than
that heavy rollers should not be used within 2 feet from the
back of the retaining wall in order to avoid exerting
excesSive pressure on the wall

W O 1106 30 Page 16
For only a short duration we believe that the on site residual soils
could stand at a near vertical cut position during the retaining wall
construction However we recommend that the vertical cut portion be
limited to not over 6 feet in height with the portion higher than 6
feet be trimmed back at a 1 1 H V Slope ratio or it should be
properly braced and shored
It is further recommended that the retaining wall excavations be
limited to about 50 foot segments Several segments may be opened

Subgrade soil should be firm moist undisturbed residual
soils or compacted fill at 90 ASTM D 1557 78 density Any
soft or unsuitable materials at the subgrade should be
removed and the over excavation backfilled with suitable
compacted fill
It is important that the subgrade soils should be kept moist
and not be allowed to dry out The conditions of the

concrete placement

the floor covering

APPENDIX B
Moisture content unit weight and Atterberg Limit deter
minations were performed on selected samples to aid in the
classification and evaluation of soil properties The results
of these tests are presented on the log of borings at the
appropriate sample depths The Atterberg Limits are also
Summarized on Plate B 1 and plotted on Plate B 2
One laboratory CBR test was performed on a bulk
sample obtained near the proposed finished elevation to evaluate
the subgrade support Capacity of the compacted soil The test
result is summarized on Plate B l
Seven direct shear tests were performed on undisturbed
Samples to evaluate the strength characteristics of the
natural soil The test results are presented on Plates B 3
through B 9
Four consolidation tests were performed on undisturbed
samples to evaluate the settlement characteristics of the
natural soils The test results are presented on Plates B 10
through B 13
Thirteen one inch ring swell tests were performed on
remolded and undisturbed samples to evaluate the swelling
potential of the soils in the natural and compacted condition
The test results are summarized on Plates B 14 and B 15
