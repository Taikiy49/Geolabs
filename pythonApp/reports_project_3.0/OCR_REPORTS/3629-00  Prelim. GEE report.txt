INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our preliminary geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the proposed Gymnasium Addition project located within the
Marine Corps Base Hawaii MCBH at Kaneohe Bay on the island of Oahu Hawaii The
general location and vicinity of the site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and preliminary geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These preliminary recommendations are intended for planning and preliminary
design purposes only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located approximately at the intersection of Lawrence and
Mokapu Roads within the Marine Corps Base Hawaii MCBH at Kaneohe Bay on the
island of Oahu Hawaii Based on the information provided it is proposed to construct an
addition to the existing Base Gymnasium for use as a physical fitness center
Based on the current design concept the proposed project includes construction
of a one story structure with a footprint of approximately 11 000 square feet immediately
adjacent to the southeastern side of the existing Base Gymnasium We understand that
the new structure will be constructed of masonry walls and metal roofing with on grade
concrete floor slabs

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in
age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which erosion cut valleys deep into
the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder
and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The site of the MCBH at Kaneohe Bay is located on Mokapu Peninsula which was
built by three volcanic vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series These vents are believed to
be among the oldest of the series The pyroclastic deposits and lava flows from vents
formed salients permitting the growth of a coral reef that merged with the main body of
the island of Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age the sea level fell below the present sea
level in response to continental glaciation Subsequently sands from emerged coral
reefs were blown inland by the prevailing winds to form sand dunes along the northern
portion of the airfield area An extensive deposit of unconsolidated calcareous marine
sediments primarily consisting of beach sand made up of water worn coral shells and
organisms covers the western tip of Mokapu Peninsula Man made fills consisting of
marine sediments dredged from the ocean floor were used to build up the area around
the southern and western shorelines of Mokapu Peninsula

DISCUSSION AND PRELIMINARY RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our preliminary field exploration the proposed project site is underlain by
layers of medium dense to dense sands and medium stiff to stiff silts and clays extending
to depths of about 26 3 to 30 2 feet below the existing ground surface Based on the type
of building proposed and the subsoils encountered at the site we believe that a shallow
foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings bearing on the stiff
in situ soils or new engineered fills may be used for support of the proposed one story
structure
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3629 00 Page 8
Based on the current design concept new fills of up to about 4 to 5 feet relative to
the existing ground may be needed to attain the design finish grade The 4 to 5 feet of
new fills may induce potential ground settlements resulting from compression of the
underlying medium dense and medium stiff soils In addition a portion of the proposed
gymnasium addition is located adjacent to the existing Base Gymnasium building where
an existing fill slope is located Therefore the proposed gymnasium addition will straddle
differential fill thickness As a result the major geotechnical engineering consideration for
the proposed project is the potential for total and differential ground settlements across
the structure located above differential fill thickness
Since detailed information pertaining to structure and site grading have not been
finalized yet this report is prepared with the intent of providing general geotechnical
engineering information for planning and_ preliminary design purposes
Proposers contractors wishing to bid on this project should retain a qualified geotechnical
engineer to evaluate potential foundation support schemes and the effects of the potential
ground settlements on the structure based on the design scheme selected The
successful design build team should perform a design level geotechnical engineering
exploration to develop finalized foundation and grading recommendations for the
proposed project
Detailed discussions and preliminary recommendations pertaining to the planning
and preliminary design of the proposed project are presented in the following sections of

Ground Settlements
As previously indicated the new fills may induce ground settlements as a result of
compression of the underlying medium dense and medium stiff soils Because the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3629 00 Page 9
proposed building will straddle differential fill thickness there is potential for differential
settlements across the building structure We recommend that the magnitude and rate of
the potential ground settlements at the site be further evaluated during detailed design by
the design build team after the structure and site grading have been finalized However
in order to reduce the potential for distress to the building structure due to ground
settlements construction of the structural improvements including the building retaining
walls and utilities within the fill areas should be delayed until the settlement readings
indicate that the settlement rates have stabilized Therefore a settlement monitoring
program should be established during and after fill placement in order to monitor the
settlement rate and to determine the time for building construction We also recommend
that a proper contingency plan be devised to accommodate the potential delay in the
construction schedule
Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our preliminary exploration we
believe that a shallow foundation system may be used to support the proposed one story
structure Allowable bearing pressures on the order of 2 000 to 2 500 pounds per square
foot p s f may be used for preliminary design of isolated and continuous footings
bearing on the in situ soils and or on compacted engineered fill needed to raise the
grades The allowable bearing pressures are for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as wind and or seismic forces In
general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent
finish grades For the portion of the new gymnasium addition adjacent to the existing
building footings for the gymnasium addition should extend to the same elevation as the
bottom of footings for the existing building to reduce the potential settlements due to
surcharge effects The excavations for the new footings adjacent to the existing building
should not extend below the existing building foundations
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3629 00 Page 10
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement
In general lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional
resistance developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil A
coefficient of friction of 0 3 may be used for footings bearing on the stiff in situ soils or
new engineered fills Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using
an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f This
assumes that the soil around the footings is well compacted Unless covered by
pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be
neglected
Footing excavations should be observed by a qualified geotechnical engineer prior
to the placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the foundation bearing
conditions and the required embedment depths

Site Grading
We envision that site grading for the proposed project will involve minor cuts and
fills of up to about 4 to 5 feet relative to the existing ground surface in order to achieve the
design grades Site grading operations should be observed by a qualified geotechnical
engineer or his her representative It is important that a qualified geotechnical engineer
or his her representative observe whether any undesirable materials are encountered
during the excavation and scarification process and whether the exposed soil conditions
are similar to those encountered in this and subsequent explorations
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to reduce potential
contamination of the excavated materials The existing sheds and their foundations
should be completely removed and the resulting excavation should be replaced with
compacted engineered fill
Areas designated to receive fills and finished subgrades should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Any soft weak or yielding areas disclosed during clearing operations should be
over excavated to expose firm ground and the resulting excavation should be filled with
engineered fill moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3629 00 Page 14
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Prior to placement of the fill
material the over excavated subgrade should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction The excavated soft and or organic
soils should be properly disposed of off site
In general the excavated on site soils may be reused as a source of general fill
materials provided that they are placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
We envision that imported fill materials will be required to raise the site to the
design grades In general imported fill material should consist of non expansive material
such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be
well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in maximum
dimension The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 12 or more and should
have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less

Drainage
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations
and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
foundations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and subsequent
loss of strength or heaving of the near surface soils The foundation excavations should

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples
of the soils to evaluate its liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results
of the tests are summarized on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample depths and
presented on Plate B 1
Four sieve analysis tests ASTM D 422 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
