INTRODUCTION
In accordance with our service agreement with R M Towill Corporation we have
observed the installation of eighteen 18 seepage pits and injection testing of five 5
seepage pits at the subject site along the Kapolei Village Lower Channel in the District
of Ewa on the island of Oahu Hawaii The approximate location and vicinity of the project
site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1

This report summarizes our observation services during the installation of the
seepage pits and presents the results of the injection tests of the seepage pits at the
project site
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is situated in the Ewa district on the island of Oahu Hawaii and is
part of the Villages of Kapolei development project The proposed Villages of Kapolei
development consists of a planned residential community with related facilities The entire
project will be developed in several phases
As part of the overall infrastructure of the development a drain channel has been
constructed along the southerly and easterly boundaries of the development area This
channel will be used to intercept runoff originating within and above the development
area
The channel will serve as both a detention basin and an infiltration basin To
supplement the capacity of the channel a series of 18 seepage pits have been
constructed along the south portion of the drainage channel The purpose of seepage
pits is to increase the design capacity of the drainage channel by 50 percent during a
100 year storm with a duration of 24 to 48 hours
Under the State of Hawaii Department of Health Administrative Rules seepage pits
for the disposal of surface run off are considered injection wells and permits for the
construction and operation of the seepage pits must be obtained under the Underground
Injection Control UIC program

UO 1729 from the Department of Health before the seepage pit installation

GENERAL INFORMATION AND SITE DESCRIPTION
The Villages of Kapolei development is located in the Ewa District on the island of
Oahu Hawaii The general location of the project site and its vicinity are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1 The site is located at Latitude N and
Longitude W The development consists of eight villages a golf course
roadways and utilities including the Kapolei Village drainage channel The proposed
drainage channel has a L shaped alignment The shorter leg of the L or the upper
leg begins near the south side of Waimanalo Road and extends southward to the longer
lower leg of the channel which parallels the north side of Franklin D Roosevelt Road
The drainage channel terminates adjacent to the Barbers Point Naval Air Station Coral Pit
a former borrow site for coral materials In general the lower channel bottom is relatively
flat and slopes gently down in the northern direction with an existing ground surface
elevation of about 30 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
The seepage pits are located along the south side of the lower channel bottom
general locations of the seepage pits are shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 and 3

PHYSICAL CHARACTERISTICS
The climate of the Ewa area is mild with an average annual temperature of about
78 F and an average annual rainfall of about 40 inches per year In general the
topography of the project site can be characterized as gently sloping terrain with an
overall slope and drainage in a southerly direction
Regional Geology
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
basaltic shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the
bulk of the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority
of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still
active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2746 10 Page 6
The project site is located on the Ewa Coastal Plain which abuts the southern flank
of Waianae and its geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to
glacio eustatic fluctuations of sea level during the Pleistocene and the genesis of Pear
Harbor and the Ewa Coastal Plain The subsurface conditions in the majority of the
coastal plain are dominated by fossil Pleistocene age coral algal reef deposits laid during
high stands of the sea which intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the
stratigraphic section in this area is Waianae Basalt
As volcanic activity on the island of Oahu drew to a close the island began to
subside slowly While the island was sinking fringing coral algal reefs began to grow on
the seaward margins of the island
During the Pleistocene or Ice Age global sea levels rose and fell in response to
the great continental glaciers As the glaciers grew the global sea level fell below the
existing level and as the glaciers melted and retreated the sea level rose to heights above
the current stand of the sea The land mass of Oahu remained essentially stable during
these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
corresponding higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef
deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of upper areas of the
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2746 10 Page 7
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the reef
materials and lagoonal sediments
Agricultural land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years
have brought the Ewa Honolulu area to its present form
Subsurface Conditions
Our field exploration consisted of observation and logging of the installation of the
18 seepage pits which were drilled to depths of about 60 to 64 feet below the existing
ground surface of the channel bottom The approximate locations of the seepage pits
are shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 and 3
In general our field exploration encountered coralline soils which include coral reef
formation coral gravel and coral sand to the drilled depths of about 60 to 64 feet below
the existing channel bottom surface Detailed descriptions of the subsurface conditions
encountered are presented on Plates A 1 through A 9 Seepage Pit Logs
Groundwater was encountered in all of the seepage pits at a depth of about 28
feet below the existing ground surface of the channel bottom at the time the seepage pits
were drilled
Earthquake Conditions
The City and County of Honolulu formally adopted the 1988 Uniform Building Code
UBC with amendments which assigned Oahu to Seismic Zone 2A Since no new
structures will be associated directly with the seepage pits seismic conditions are not
considered to be significant

Land Use
The site is an area which is planned for residential community use and includes
related facilities The site is currently under development for these purposes and the
seepage pits together with the Lower Channel are parts of the proposed infrastructure
system in this development which will be used to intercept runoff originating within and
above the development area Therefore construction of seepage pits at the site is
consistent with this use
SYSTEM OPERATION
Kapolei Village Lower Channel will form a part of the drainage system for the
development of Villages of Kapolei Based on the current design concepts the storm
water run off from Kapolei Village 3 Makakilo Gulch and Makalapa Gulch will flow into
Kapolei Golf Course currently under construction The golf course will function as a
detention basin while allowing some of the run off to infiltrate through surface percolation
After passing through the golf course the run off will discharge into the drainage channel
The storm water run off from other development areas will travel overland and through
drainage pipes to the drainage channel which will collect the run off
The drainage channel is also designed as a detention structure Run off entering
the channel will be disposed of by infiltration through the sides and bottom of the channel
In addition 18 seepage pits have been constructed along the Lower Channel to increase
the run off disposal capacity of the drainage channel Based on the design criteria
provided the seepage pit system is intended to increase the design capacity of the
drainage channel by 50 percent during a 100 year storm with a duration of 24 to 48
hours which means that the seepage pit system should have a capacity of 24 445 gallons
per minute

The seepage pits were constructed in general conformance with the specifications
of the UIC Application UO 1729 The seepage pits were drilled to approximately 60 to
64 feet As discussed in our letter to the Department of Health dated in
lieu of the 24 inch diameter perforated casings proposed in the application 18 inch
diameter casings were used Our understanding is that this modification is acceptable
based on discussions with the Department of Health
GEOHYDROLOGIC CONSIDERATIONS
The ground surface elevation at the site at the time of seepage pit installation and
testing was approximately 30 feet MSL Groundwater in the area was encountered at
about 28 feet below the existing channel bottom surface at the time of the seepage pit
installation The groundwater appears to consist of brackish water in an unconfined
coralline formation aquifer Based on area topography and the subsurface conditions
encountered the down gradient direction for groundwater appears to be toward the
south
The logs of the drilled seepage pits are presented in Appendix A Generally the
seepage pits encountered coralline sands and gravel
PERMEABILITY TESTING
After the seepage pits had been drilled and cased to their intended depths
Seepage Pit 9 10 and 18 were tested by the injection of relatively clear water
to confirm the subsurface permeability conditions at the site The injected water was
pumped from another seepage pit at a minimum of 200 feet from the seepage pit being
injection tested
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2746 10 Page 10
A paddle wheel flow sensor was used to monitor the flow rate Flow rates for the
injection test were typically above 520 gallons per minute gom and the maximum flow
rate used was about 554 GPM Each test duration was about five hours During the
injection of water the changes in water level in the seepage pits were recorded with
respect to time and rate of injection by an electric water level measuring device
The tested coefficient of permeability of the subsurface soils at the project site was
in the range of 0 5 and 3 0 feet per minute although most of the injection tests results
were over 1 5 feet per minute The results of the injection testing program indicate that
the infiltration capacity of the seepage pit system should exceed the required design
capacity The detailed results of these tests are presented in Appendix B
The UIC Application UO 1729 indicated that an injection test flow rate of 1 000
gpm for three to four hours would be used As summarized in our letter to the
Department of Health dated difficulties in performing injection testing at
the rate necessitated use of a lower rat of about 500 gom Based on discussions with
the Department of Health it is our understanding that injection testing at a rate of 500
gpm for a longer duration of about five hours was acceptable
