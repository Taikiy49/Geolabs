We have conducted an evaluation of the existing loose boulder deposit with respect 
to potential rockfall hazard conditions at the project site.  This report summarizes the 
findings based on our field reconnaissance of the existing site conditions.  The findings

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is adjacent to the recently constructed Kaiwakiloumoku Cultural 
Center at the Kamehameha Schools-Kapalama Campus in the Honolulu area on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1.   
A cluster of three loose surface boulders was reported as a potential rockfall hazard 
in an undeveloped slope area southerly of the new cultural center building.  The boulders 
reside at the crest of a localized rock ledge escarpment.  We understand it was desired to 
evaluate the potential rockfall hazard condition for mitigation with respect to public access 
on the existing Waonahele Road located downslope from the boulder deposit. 
This report presents the results of our field evaluation and includes our 
recommendations for rockfall hazard mitigation at the project site.   
PURPOSE AND SCOPE

boulder deposit with respect to potential rockfall hazard conditions that could affect public 
safety on the lower roadway at the project site.  Recommendations for rockfall hazard 
mitigation are provided based on our visual observations.  In order to accomplish the

GENERAL SITE CONDITIONS 
The project site is at the Kamehameha Schools-Kapalama Campus in the Honolulu 
area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The boulder deposit is approximately 150 to 200 linear 
feet southerly (downslope) of the rear of the existing Kaiwakiloumoku Cultural Center 
building above Waonahele Road, as indicated in Photograph Nos. 1 and 2 on Plate 3.   
The surface boulder deposit resides within undeveloped and vegetated rocky 
terrain on the edge of the very gently-inclined upper slope terrace next to a near-vertical 
rock ledge, as illustrated on Plate 2. The existing Waonahele Road is less than 
approximately 60 linear feet downslope from the boulder deposit. The roadway is a main 
thoroughfare for the school campus. 
SITE RECONNAISSANCE 
A field reconnaissance was conducted by our geologist to evaluate the existing 
condition of the surface boulder deposit with respect to potential rockfall hazard conditions.  
The boulder deposit consists of a localized cluster of three (3) basaltic boulders residing 
on the edge of the very gently sloping (approximately 10H:1V inclination) and relatively 
smooth slope surface underlain by hard basaltic rock formation material.  Photograph Nos. 
3 and 4 on Plate 4 show the location and condition of the boulder cluster deposit. 
The gentle slope containing the boulders is bounded by the near-vertical rock ledge 
escarpment of approximately 14 feet in height.  The cluster of 3 boulders resides at and 
near the edge of the gentle slope above the rock ledge.  Immediately below the rock ledge 
there is an existing flattened terrace with an average width of about 25 feet. The lower 
terrace is nearly level, vegetated with tall grass, and is mantled by near-surface soils and 
forest litter overlying basaltic rock formation.  At the outer (southern) edge of the terrace is 
an existing steepened rock cut bank of about 8 to 12 feet high adjacent to the roadway 
elevation at Waonahele Road.  A sketch illustrating the general site condition is presented

The surface boulder deposit consists of three individual large diameter boulders, 
identified as Boulder Nos. 1 through 3, as indicated on Plates 2 and 3.  The boulders are 
slightly to moderately weathered, hard to very hard, and generally intact with minimal 
visible continuous open fractures. The boulders are not embedded in the ground surface 
and appear to have been mechanically placed or pushed into their current setting 
positions.   
Based on our evaluation, Boulder No. 1 appears to be a significant potential rockfall 
hazard and is of high risk for toppling movement.  We recommend the 
stabilization/removal of this boulder. In contrast, Boulder Nos. 2 and 3 appear to be in 
generally stable slope settings and do not appear to represent significant falling rock 
hazards. Photograph No. 5 (Plate 5) shows the precarious basal contact of Boulder No.1 
at the top edge of the rock ledge. 
Boulder No. 1 
Boulder No. 1 is the largest block of rock and is at the edge of a generally stable 
hard rock ledge precipice.  Boulder No. 1 is sub-angular in shape and is standing on-end 
with a precarious center of gravity in relation to the adjacent ledge precipice. The boulder 
appears minimally balanced on underlying rough rocky material. Boulder No. 1 has 
approximate dimensions of about 9 feet high by 7½ feet wide and 5½ feet deep.  It 
presents a high risk potential rockfall hazard due to its precarious balanced position at the 
edge of the near vertical rock ledge. 
Should Boulder No. 1 topple by erosion or application of an external force (such as 
earthquake, severe vibration, or mechanical strike), the rock would drop onto the terrace 
below with minimal outward rolling and bouncing. The character (inclination, width, and 
surface composition) of the slope terrace is very favorable for retention of the fallen rock. 
Based on our observations, it is our opinion that the boulder may not pose a falling rock 
hazard for the Waonahele Road.  However, the rock does pose a falling rock hazard to the 
slope terrace immediately below the deposit. 
Boulder Nos. 2 and 3 
Boulder Nos. 2 and 3 are smaller in size, roughly equidimensional to oblong in 
shape and are similar in dimension, shape, and ground surface setting. Boulder Nos. 2 
and 3 are not in direct contact with Boulder No. 1 and the two rocks appear to be in 
generally stable settings with substantial ground contact and basal support with a low 
center of gravity. The very gentle slope underlying the boulder deposit is not conducive to 
the natural initiation of rock movement without a mechanical force.  In the outside chance 
that the boulders happen to be pushed over the ledge, the lower terrace below would likely 
retain the boulders without adverse risk to the Waonahele Road below.

Rockfall Hazard Mitigation 
Based on our evaluation, we recommend the complete removal of Boulder No. 1 
from the ledge and deposition of the rock onto the terrace below. The rock may either be 
toppled as a single unit in a slow, controlled manner or mechanically broken into pieces 
and dropped to the lower terrace, depending on the contractors preference. Controlled 
toppling may be conducted slowly by air bag or mechanical levering so as to limit the 
falling momentum and velocity.  As previously mentioned, the potential hazard to the 
roadway is minimal; however, it would be prudent to provide temporary roadway traffic 
stoppage during the actual movement of the rock to the lower terrace. 
Based on our evaluation, Boulder Nos. 2 and 3 present a minimal potential rockfall 
hazard to the terrace below. This is due to the generally stable slope setting of the rocks. 
However, potential vibration and/or movement may be generated during the removal of 
Boulder No. 1.  Therefore, it would be prudent to have the boulders removed in 
conjunction with the work effort for the removal of Boulder No.1.  
Once the rock material is moved down to the terrace, the rocks and/or rock 
fragments should be kept a minimum of about 15 feet away from the outer edge of the 
lower terrace leading down toward the roadway. If rock fragments are generated by the 
removal, the rock fragments should be spread as a single layer of fragments close to the 
base of the rock ledge. 
LIMITATIONS 
The findings and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from visual site observations only.  Variation in the surface and 
subsurface conditions between our observation points may occur, and the nature and 
extent of these variations may not become evident until actual construction is underway.  If 
variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the findings and 
recommendations provided herein. 
It should be noted that slopes composed of rock materials (rock slopes) deteriorate 
with the passage of time due to natural weathering processes, wet-dry and hot-cold 
cycles, and erosion conditions.  Due to the inherent deterioration of rock slopes resulting 
from natural processes (weathering, wet-dry and hot-cold cycles, and erosion conditions), 
the potential for rockfall hazard at any site changes with the passage of time.  Therefore, 
the findings and recommendations contained in this letter report may be used only within a 
reasonable time from the date of issuance of this letter report.  Land use, site conditions, 
and/or other factors may change with the passage of time.  Therefore, additional work to 
evaluate the applicability of the findings and preliminary recommendations contained in 
this letter report due to changes with the passage of time will be required.  There are no 
guarantees in the professional engineering and architectural design fields with respect to 
potential rockfall hazards due in large part to the unpredictable nature of rockfall activity, 
which is affected by many external variables including natural and man-induced causes.
