PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of this geotechnical engineering investigation was to
explore the subsurface soil conditions at the site in order to provide
data for use in the design of site grading building foundation and
pavement design for the proposed multi family development The general
location of the site and its vicinity is shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 1
The results of our field exploration and laboratory tests which we
used in the engineering analysis to formulate our conclusions and
recommendations are presented in the appendices of this report The
approximate test pit and boring locations are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2
This report summarizes our findings and recommendations
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located about one quarter mile south of the Mauna
Kea Beach Hotel between Fairways 14 15 and 18 of the golf
course Queen Kaahumanu Highway is located about 2000 feet east of the
site and Hapuna Beach State Park Road is located about one half mile
GEOLABS HAWAII
W Page 3
south of the site A narrow cinder road and jogging path border most of
the westerly boundary of the site The approximate location of the site
is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
The topography is usually characterized by fairly smooth rolling
hills and in some cases poorly defined gullies and drainageways Local
relief is generally less than 30 feet
The site is an arid type land that is sparsely covered with dry
grasses and with keawe trees in the lower areas and drainageways Access
through the site is by 4 wheel drive vehicles over unimproved narrow
dozer trails
Drainage is down towards the west oceanside The ground elevations
at the site range from about 170 to 70 feet
In general the ground surface is covered with basaltic gravel and
cobbles The ash soils underlying the surficial rocks are highly
desiccated which is consistent with the arid climate
Rock outcrops are mostly limited to exposures in the gullies and cuts
made for access roads

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed plan is to grade the site for a single family and duplex
residential development The general site plan indicates that twenty
six 26 one story structures with access roads and parking areas are
anticipated
A preliminary grading plan indicates that most of the buildings will
be located on fills varying from about 5 toe 20 or more feet in height
The exceptions to this were noted at Buildings 20 21 and 23
where the structures are generally in cut section generally less than 15
feet and Buildings 14 and 26 which appear to be in a cut and fill
section
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an
area of approximately 4000 square miles The island was formed by the
activity of 5 shield volcanoes These are Kohala which is long
extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity during recent geologic times
Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both
still active
The site is located on the northwestern flank of Mauna Kea mountain
which makes up the northern part of the island and consists largely of
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1489 10 Page 5
the older Hamakua Volcanic Series The lava flows at the site are Aa
basalt types with dense zones of hard rock and loose zones of clinkers
The clinker phase is highly permeable
A thin dry desiccated and erodible layer of volcanic ash locally
referred to as Pahale Ash covers the surface of the flows
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were investigated by excavating
twenty three 23 test pits with a Caterpillar D9 dozer with ripper in
various portions of the site to help delineate the near surface geclogi
cal conditions and to obtain bulk samples for testing Also nine 9
borings were drilled at the site to depths of 10 to 20 feet below the
existing ground surface to delineate the subsurface geological
conditions
The approximate locations of the test pits and borings are shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2
The test pit legs and borings are presented in Appendix A
Soil Conditions
The field exploration disclosed that the site is generally covered
with a thin less than 3 foot thick surface soil layer of dry
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1489 10 Page 6
desiccated clayey to sandy silt volcanic ash mixed with cobbles and
boulders over lava rock formations The ash soil has little cohesion and
may be highly susceptible to wind and water erosion
The ash soil layer in the vicinity of Test Pit Nos 11 and 20 and
Boring No 2 appears to be thicker than the rest of the site and may vary
from 5 to 10 feet in depth Otherwise the subsurface conditions
throughout the site appears to be relatively uniform
Below the ash layer is the Aa lava flow which is composed of dense
fractured to massive basalt It is anticipated that individual flows may
be up to 10 to 15 feet thick and underlain with clinker layers
No water was encountered in the test pits or borings during the field
exploration
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATTONS
The field explorations disclosed that the site is underlain by a thin
generally less than 3 feet thick layer of volcanic ash mixed with
cobbles and boulders over lava rock formations From a geotechnical
point of view the site is feasible for the proposed condominium
development
The underlying volcanic rock should provide adequate bearing for
the proposed structures related roads and parking areas
GEOLABS HAWAIH
W Page 7
Thick volcanic ash soils extending down to about 8 to 10 foot depths
were encountered at two locations at the site High fills up to about 20
to 30 feet or more are planned for these locations Our laboratory
consolidation test data indicate that the soil structure may be subject
to collapse when completely saturated Therefore in order to minimize
the possibility of large settlement in this area we recommend that the
ash deposit be removed and stockpiled and the excavation backfilled and
compacted with on site select granular materials The estimated limits
of these thicker soft ash deposits are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
We anticipate that the underlying excavated hard Aa rock may have a
swelling factor of about 25 to 30 which may produce some excess material
that may have to be disposed of off site It is our opinion that an
overall swelling factor of 10 to 15 may be considered for the various
materials
Most of the excavated rock is expected to break up within a size
range of 1 to 12 inches
Only the smaller sized 12 inch minus excavated rocky material
should be used in the roadway embankments to minimize later difficulty in
utility trenching The larger boulders may be used in the deeper fills
in accordance with the recommendations presented below

Site Grading
Site Preparation
Prior to construction of roadway and site grading surface vege
tation debris stockpiles and organic material should be removed and
wasted off site In areas to receive fill or at grade construction
tree roots should be removed to a minimum depth of 3 feet and the
resulting excavation backfilled and compacted to 90 of its maximum
density as established by ASTM D 1557 78
In fairly level areas prior to receiving fill the surface ash soils
should be removed and stockpiled for use later as landscape topsceil
or blended with the excavated rock material generated from the cut
areas The ground surface should then be scarified to a depth of 12
inches moistened and compacted to 90 maximum density as per ASTM
D 1557 78
Due to the dry and windy site conditions the contractor should have
an adequate water supply for moisture conditioning the soils in the
grading area It is also advisable to institute a dust control
program during grading to minimize wind erosion and airborne dust
nuisance

Excavation
The field exploration indicates that the site is underlain at fairly
shallow depths by dense Aa basalt mantled with a thin layer of ash
soil mixed with cobbles and boulders
It is anticipated that the ash and cobbles may be excavated with
normal excavating methods Some hard digging may require the use of
a hoe ram and even some blasting if large accretionary lava balls
boulders are encountered Also much of the upper fractured rock
layers can be ripped with heavy equipment
The more massive basalt will likely require special excavating
procedures such as hoe ram or blasting to make the proposed cuts or
utility trenches

Foundations
We believe the on site rocky ground and compacted granular fill
resulting from the above site grading can provide suitable foundation
support for the proposed buildings
Structural fill should be placed in accordance with the recommend
ations under Site Grading and should be observed by a soil technician
or engineer
Spread and wall footing foundations may be used for the proposed
structures
All exterior footing bottoms should be embedded a mininum of 24
inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade The minimum embedment
for interior footings may be reduced to 18 inches below the finished
grade
For the footings bearing on the compacted rock fill an allowable net
soil bearing pressure of 4000 p s f may be used An allowable bearing
value up to 6000 p s f may be used for footings bearing on weathered
rock or cemented clinker Higher bearing values may be obtained on dense
basalt if required These values may be increased by one third when
temporary wind or seismic loads are considered
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1489 10 Page 15
Slabs and spread foundations located close to the tops cf the fill
slopes should have sufficient embedment to cbtain a minimum horizontal
set back distance of 8 feet measured from the outer edge of the footing
to the slope face
The ground surface should be shaped to divert surface runoff away
from building foundations and slabs
It is anticipated that the footings if constructed in accordance
with the above recommendations will have negligible settlements
The foundation plans for the units should be forwarded to the soils
engineer for review
On Grade Slab
Based on a non expansive slab subgrade condition anticipated at this
site on grade slab should be placed over a 4 inch layer of base course
compacted to 90 of its maximum density as established by ASTM D 1557
78 The thickened edge around the on grade slab should extend a minimum
of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished ground surface
Depending on the structure of the underlying rock formation a
choking layer consisting of well graded rock materials may be required
between the underlying rugged rock surface and the upper cushion fill or
concrete slab

Retaining Walls
Several retaining walls are planned for the proposed development
Test pits or borings were made near some of the proposed wall
locations Also a fairly deep gully at the central upper portion was
observed and based on these visual observations consolidated clinker
material or basalt rock is anticipated at the wall locations For the
foundations of the retaining walls the footings should be embedded a
minimum of 2 feet into the consolidated clinker material or 6 inches
into dense basalt whichever occurs first

the filled areas or clinker material and 6000 p s f may be utilized

considered

clinker materials should be used as backfill directly behind the walls

The work under this section includes clearing and grub
bing preparation of fill areas placement and compaction of fill
and removal and backfilling of underground structures These
specifications are of a general nature and may be superseded by
specific requirements or recommendations within the soils report
IT
Clearing
All areas within contract limit lines shall be cleared of
trash debris organic matter and all other deleterious
materials These materials shall be removed and disposed
off site
Preparation of Fill Areas
A Ioose surface soil shall be removed to expose
competent natural soils Localized soft pockets shall be
undercut and backfilled with compacted select materials

B All surfaces shall be inspected and approved by the soils
engineer prior to the placement of any fill
Cc After approval the surface shall be scarified to a depth of

compacted as specified herein
D Where the existing surface exceeds a slope of 5 horizontal
to 1 vertical 5 1 the ground surface shall be keyed and
benched into competent soil to bond the fill to the existing
ground surface
Fill Materials
Material for fill shall consist of on site soils or approved
inported material Import material shall be tested and approved
a minimum of seven 7 working days prior to transportation to
the site Fill material shall contain less than 5 percent
organic matter and other deleterious material and shall not
contain rocks or lumps in excess of twelve 12 inches in
diameter

Placement and Compaction of Fill
A The fill shall be placed in horizontal loose layers not to
exceed eighteen 18 inches in thickness Each layer shall be
spread uniformly and blade mixed to attain uniformity of
material and water content within each layer
B Rock fragments less than twelve 12 inches in diameter may
be utilized provided they are not concentrated in any one area
and that there is sufficient fine grained material to fill the
voids between the rocks
C Boulders encountered during excavation may be used in deep
fills provided that the following are followed
1 Boulders shall be spread out and must not be nested
together They should be placed in such a way that
the compaction equipment will be able to compact
around them

No part of the boulder shall encroach any closer to

dimension and in no case be closer than 3 feet to

No boulder larger than 3 feet in its largest
dimension shall be placed in the fill It may be
necessary to reduce the size of larger boulders or
dispose of them off site
Care must be exercised to avoid placement of boulders
in the proposed utility alignments to prevent
difficulty in the later excavation of the utility
trenches
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable

Instead a testing program consisting of visual
