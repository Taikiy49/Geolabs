PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Ramirez Residence site is within an old Waimanalo agriculture area adjacent
to Waimanalo Forest Reserve near the bottom of Koolau Mountain as shown on the
Geology Map Plate 2 The residence is bounded by Dragon Garden Hawaii Bonsai
Culture Center on the southwest and Mahailua Street on the southeast

concerning possible earthwork activities in the past It is desired to explore the existing
subsurface conditions to evaluate and or quantify whether new fill material was placed on

perform slope stability analysis for a slope area near the northwest end of the parcel
adjacent to the neighboring property
The study is divided into two phases The first phase consists of visual site
observations research and plan review to determine a detailed subsurface exploration
program and scope of work for the second phase of the investigation This report covers
the results of our research and plan review in the vicinity of the above property

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the
Waianae and Koolau volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which erosion incised
deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows
followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the Honolulu
Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was

available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by the glacioeustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly low and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level When
the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported downslope
toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these materials reach
the stream in the central portion of a valley the alluvial processes become dominant and
the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the stream
flows transmit large volumes of water for very short durations Because of this transport of
sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load consists of a poorly
sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When the erosional base
levels change these sediment loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes begin
to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the material
Chemical processes also cause induration or cementaion of the coarse grained portion of
the sediment resulting in a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate
Simultaneously erosion continues in areas above the valley floors and upstream in
headwaters This continued erosion generates material which is transported downslope
covering the older alluvial deposits
Depending on the local base level and rate of transport these newer sediments are
generally transient in terms of geologic time In addition their consistency and density are
generally less than those of the older partially consolidated deposits
The project site is near the bottom of the Koolau Mountain within an old agricultural
land development as shown on the Geology Map Plate 2 A series of stream features
were observed traversing the site Currently an intermittent stream was observed in the
northwestern end of the property We anticipate that the project site would be underlain
by a thin layer of agricultural fill alluvial soil deposits and residual saprolite material at
greater depth Agricultural land development and other projects within the last century
have brought the project site to its present form

A preliminary geotechnical engineering assessment was carried out by reviewing
the aerial photo image taken between 1980 and 2008 and conducting site

We acquired aerial photos from other private commercial companies taken in
and 2008 The approximate boundary of the
property is outlined on each aerial photo as shown on Plates 3 through 9 The property
line boundaries were obtained from the GIS website of the City and County of Honolulu
and overlaid onto the aerial photos
In addition probing was conducted near the northwestern end of the property
using the Dynamic Cone Penetrometer DCP to explore the near surface soil
consistency Two DCP probes were conducted extending to depths of about 3 to 5 feet
below the ground surface Details of the DCP probing are presented in the Logs of
Borings Plates 10 1 through 11 2
In general the DCP is advanced by driving an 8 mm diameter 60 steel cone tip
with an 8 kg hammer falling 575 mm in vertical height The blow counts were recorded
and converted to standard penetration resistance using correlation
between Penetration Index PI and SPT Sowers and Hedges and later Livneh and
Ishai Both PI and SPT blow counts are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
depths
Based on the review of the aerial photos there is no evidence suggesting major
grading work was conducted within the Ramirez Residence during the photograph
periods The ground topography in the property appears similar through the period
between 1980 and 2008 and remains at the present time Surface vegetation is also
similar but with some variations due to seasonal growth
Based on the results of the DCP probing the calculated SPT blow counts within
approximately 5 feet deep varied from about 11 to 30 blows per foot The variation may
be caused primarily by encountering cobble and or gravel No soft soil SPT blow count
less than 5 blows per foot was encountered within the explored depth
The preliminary evaluation indicates that no major grading took place at the
property between 1980 and 2008
