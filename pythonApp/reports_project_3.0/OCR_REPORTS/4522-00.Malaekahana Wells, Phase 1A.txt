REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was formed by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger shield
Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still
active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking against its
eastern flank
The processes of terrestrial erosion and the associated depositional environments
were affected by these glacioeustatic sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low
the erosional base level was correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths
below the present sea level As a result terrestrial sediments were carried further seaward
and deposited in what had been marine environments When the sea level was high the
erosional base level was raised such that sediments accumulated at higher elevations In
addition it allowed for the deposition of marine deposits such as coral and limestone in
environments that had been covered with terrestrial soils and rock
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock mass from the slopes and movement of the material
downslope toward the axis of the valley by gravity to form colluvial deposits Once the
colluvium reaches streams in the valley alluvial processes become dominant and the
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium Large areas of alluvium can be
found in the lowland areas and bordering streams
GEOLABS INC
W O 4522 00 Page 5
When alluvial and colluvial deposits are left in place or buried for long periods of
time chemical processes begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown
or weathering of the material The chemical processes also cause induration or
cementation of the coarse grained portion of the sediment resulting in a poorly
consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate Simultaneously erosion continues in
areas above the valley floors and upstream in headwaters This continued erosion
generates material which is transported downslope covering the older alluvial deposits
The well site area is generally composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lavas
of the Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows of jointed
dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers In situ chemical weathering of the
Koolau lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years The weathering process has
formed a mantle of residual and saprolitic soils In general saprolite is composed mainly of
silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite grades
to basaltic rock formation with increased depth The subsurface conditions along
Malaekahana Stream are composed of soils deposited by continuous erosion of the residual
and saprolitic soils and basaltic rock located in the upper areas of the valley The areas near
Kamehameha Highway is composed of lagoonal and beach sand deposits

recommend that the proposed pump control building be supported on a shallow foundation

Building Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we recommend
that the proposed pump control building be supported on a shallow foundation system
consisting of spread and or continuous footings An allowable bearing pressure of up to
3 500 pounds per square feet psf may be used for design of the control building
foundations bearing on the new fills This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may

Slabs On Grade
We understand that concrete slabs on grade will be provided for the project The
near surface soils encountered at the project site consist of silty clays and clayey silts which
exhibit a high potential for shrinking and swelling when subjected to moisture fluctuations
Therefore to reduce the potential for structural distress to the slabs on grade resulting from
shrinking and swelling of the silty clays and clayey silts we recommend that the soils beneath

the slab areas be over excavated and replaced with a minimum of 24 inches of aggregate
subbase course below the slabs The aggregate subbase course should also extend a

aggregate subbase course should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction
The aggregate subbase course should be placed as soon as possible after the
subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented in the

Exterior concrete slabs and walkways are not as sensitive to movements and may

Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the load
supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the pipe bedding is an important design
consideration For pipes bearing on the clayey silty and gravelly sandy soils a
minimum of 12 inches of compacted granular bedding material such as No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be provided below the pipe For pipes
with inverts in soft loose soils an additional 24 inch of No 3B Fine gravel wrapped in
a filter fabric Mirafi 140N or equivalent should be provided below the bedding layer
for more uniform support A typical section of the trench is presented on Plate 3
The granular bedding should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width of
one fourth 1 4 the outside pipe diameter and should extend half way up the sides of
the pipe Before the placement of bedding material the excavated trench bottom
should be observed by a representative of Geolabs to confirm that firm materials are
exposed at the bottom of the trench or if the bedding should be extended deeper as
recommended above
Backfill
In general the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the Water System
Standards State of Hawaii 1985 Volume 1 The first zone of backfill extending from
the bedding material to at least 12 inches above the top of the pipes should consist of
select free draining well graded granular material to reduce the compaction effort
required and resulting stresses on the pipe The granular material should be well
graded less than 1 inch in maximum size and should have less than 10 percent
passing the No 200 sieve Compaction by water ponding or jetting may be allowed
provided the granular material is self draining such as No 3B Fine gravel
The trench backfill from 12 inches above the top of the pipes to the finished subgrade
may consist of the excavated on site clayey gravelly and sandy soils The backfill
should be placed in thin lifts normally 8 inches in loose lift thickness with each lift
mechanically compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative

compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 A typical section of the trench is presented on Plate 3
It should be noted that boulders were encountered in Boring Nos 5 and 6 Boulders
encountered in the excavated soils should not be re used as trench backfill In
addition excavated on site soils in some areas may be wet Therefore it may be
necessary to aerate the wet excavated soils prior to placement to permit compaction
of the backfill material to the specified requirements
Trenches should be kept free of water during backfilling operations Should backfilling
below the groundwater level be necessary free draining granular material such as No
3B Fine gravel should be used to reduce the potential of future settlement of the trench
backfill Where the free draining granular materials are used a filter fabric such as
Mirafi 180N or equivalent should be used to wrap the free draining materials

We envision that thrust blocks will be required at some locations to provide resistance

Based on the available information the planned inverts are about 5 to 7 feet below the
existing ground surface
Based on our field exploration stiff to very hard clayey silty soils and medium dense
gravelly sandy soils were encountered at shallow depths We recommend a lateral

Primary settlement of the new water main construction is normally caused by the
difference in the unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier

Soil Corrosion Study
A soil corrosion study which included a field soil resistivity survey and a laboratory soil
analyses study was performed to obtain an overview of the corrosion characteristics of the
subsurface soils along the proposed16 inch diameter water main planned at the project site
Our work was performed in general conformance with ASTM Test Designation G 57 95 and
the requirements provided in the Water System External Corrosion Control Standards
Volume 3 1991 Detailed findings and discussions are presented in the following
subsections of this report
Field Resistivity Survey
Geophysical survey equipment i e resistance meter was mobilized to the site on
August 3 2000 for the field survey The geophysical survey was completed on August
19 2000 In general our field work was performed in sunny conditions with generally
dry surface conditions
Ninety three soil resistivity surveys were conducted at a maximum of 300 foot intervals
at thirty one locations designated as R 1 through R 31 along Malaekahana Valley
Road The soil resistivity surveys were performed by using the in Pin
Method and a resistance meter Nilsson Model 400 manufactured by Nilsson Electrical
Laboratory Inc The method utilizes four surface probes or pins electrodes to
GEOLABS INC
W O 4522 00 Page 23
evaluate the average resistivity an indication of corrosivity of the underlying soil mass
The average resistivity values of the soil mass extending from the existing ground
surface to the test depth was assessed by using this method at the approximate survey
locations as shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 1 through 2 6
The soil resistivity surveys were performed at the existing ground surface along the
proposed alignment of the 16 inch diameter water main The average soil resistance
along the proposed alignment was evaluated at each survey location with the
electrodes aligned at equal spacing of generally 3 to 6 feet at the existing ground
surface The depth of penetration of a resistivity sounding is a function of the electrode
spacing
The resistance meter Nilsson Model 400 impresses a voltage between the outer
electrodes causing an electrical current to flow between them The voltage drop
between the inner electrodes is then measured by a sensitive built in voltmeter As
a result the soil resistance in ohms can be found directly on the dial The soil
resistivity in ohm cm which is directly related to both resistance and spacing can
then be computed
The evaluated soil resistances the computed resistivity values and the corresponding
corrosion ratings are presented on Plates C 1 through C 5 of this report Graphical
presentation of the soil resistivity values and the corrosion ratings along the proposed
water main alignment is provided on Plate C 6 It should be noted that the soil
resistivity at the site might be influenced by existing underground structures
temperature weather conditions and other factors

Findings and Discussions
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics with
regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects In general the lower the
resistivity the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure
Conversely the higher the resistivity the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion
to metallic objects
In general our test results indicate that the subsurface materials along the 16 inch
diameter water main alignment from about Station 0 00 to Station 31 00 generally
have corrosion ratings of 1 The subsurface materials from about Station 31 00 to the
end of the 16 inch diameter water main have corrosion ratings between 2 and 5
Localized areas between Station 31 00 to the end of the 16 inch diameter water main
have corrosion ratings of 1 such as Stations 33 and 63 33
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and
structures is dependent on the pH and the chloride and sulfate contents found in the
soil For underground concrete pipelines or structures the laboratory test results
indicate that the subsurface materials encountered at Stations 00 and 91 00
have corrosion ratings of 1

water away from the slabs and foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding
Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should be avoided Planters

reduce water infiltration into the subsoils

water infiltration In addition drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and
should be maintained to drain all surface run off away from slabs and foundations

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
One one inch ring swell test was performed on a selected soil sample to evaluate the
swelling potential of the near surface soils The results of the tests are summarized on
Plate B 1
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of
this test are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Three sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification
Results of the test are presented on Plate B 3
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM D 1883 was performed
on a bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The results of the tests are presented on Plate B 4

APPENDIX C
Soil Corrosion Study
APPENDIX C
Soil Corrosion Study
Ninety three soil resistivity surveys were conducted at a maximum of 300 foot intervals
at thirty one locations designated as R 1 through R 31 along Malaekahana Valley Road The
soil resistivity surveys were performed by using the in Pin Method and a
resistance meter Nilsson Model 400 manufactured by Nilsson Electrical Laboratory Inc The
results of the soil resistivity surveys are summarized on Plates C 1 through C 5
In addition a laboratory analyses study was performed on 10 selected soil samples
The soil samples were tested by Environmental Laboratory of the Pacific The laboratory tests
included the pH value EPA SW9045B chloride content and sulfate content
The results of the soil analyses are summarized on Plate C 7
