EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
A geotechnical assessment was undertaken to assess the existing subsurface
conditions at the Ewa Villages Subdivision B Phase 4A area The
subdivision was previously mass graded by placement of about 5 to 8 feet of
fill materials Following mass grading the roadway and utility improvements
were completed about 4 to 5 years ago However the individual house lots
are currently undeveloped The mass grading and infrastructure installation
work were performed by Oahu Construction Company Ltd Based on visual
observations and limited surveying of the subdivision infrastructure it appears
that settlement of the ground surface cracking in the asphalt concrete
pavements sagging of portions of the concrete rolled curb and gutter and
various sewer and drain line defects have occurred at the site
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling 10 borings extending to
depths of about 16 5 to 31 5 feet below the ground surface at the approximate
locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 Our field exploration generally
encountered a pavement section consisting of approximately 2 inches of
asphalt concrete on 4 to 16 inches of medium dense coralline base course
overlying 12 to 36 inches of loose to medium dense subbase course and
granular fill Below the pavement structural section medium stiff clayey fill and
backfill materials overlying very stiff to hard in situ alluvial soil deposits were
encountered extending to the maximum depth of the borings Soft soils with
organic materials were encountered in Boring Nos 4 and 5 between depths
of about 8 and 12 feet below the existing ground surface These depths
correspond to about the original ground surface prior to the mass grading

findings with regard to the distresses observed at the site

INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering evaluation performed
for the Infrastructure Deficiency Correction Project at the Ewa Villages Subdivision B
Phase 4A area of the Ewa Villages Development in the District of Ewa on the Island of Oahu
Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our literature research field exploration and
laboratory testing In addition this report presents our evaluation and analyses pertaining
to the probable causes of the observed distresses and provides geotechnical
recommendations for the repair work The findings and recommendations presented herein
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT BACKGROUND
The project site is located at the Ewa Villages Subdivision B Phase 4A area of the
Ewa Villages Development in the District of Ewa on the Island of Oahu Hawaii Based on
the grading plans provided Subdivision B is divided into 126 lots covering a total area of
approximately 19 65 acres We understand that the area has been previously mass graded
with the placement of approximately 5 to 8 feet of fill materials The roadway and utility
improvements for the subdivision were completed about 4 to 5 years ago however the
individual properties are currently undeveloped The mass grading and infrastructure
installation work were performed by Oahu Construction Company Ltd with Pacific Rim
Consulting and Inspection Corporation performing the field compaction testing

have occurred in the project area after completion of the infrastructure installation Various

locations the pavements have sunk around the utility structures such as the manholes In
addition large shrinkage cracks were observed in the soil surfaces of the undeveloped lots

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age As volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from Koolau banked
against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau known as Schofield Plateau
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of this plateau generated
sediments which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and to the
south these sediments accumulated and interbedded with marine sediments and coral algal
reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge The thickness of the sedimentary wedge
ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1 Highway to over 1 000 feet at Ewa
Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation or caprock
over the artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu Deposition of sediments has continued
from earlier geologic time through the present Agricultural development within the last
100 years and recent mass grading work have brought the Ewa Villages area to its present
form

Remnants of Previously Existing Irrigation Ditches
Based on the construction drawings provided we understand that a network of
irrigation ditches previously existed on the southern portion of the project site These
irrigation ditches were generally on the order of about 3 to 5 feet deep and were likely
ponded with water and soft soils with organic materials during their functional days
The approximate location of this network of irrigation ditches overlaid on the current
site development plan indicates that some portions of Roads B and C traverse these
previously existing irrigation ditches as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 During our current
field exploration two borings Boring Nos 4 and 5 were drilled along Road B that are in
proximity to the locations of these previously existing irrigation ditches The boring logs at
these two locations indicate that remnants of these previously existing irrigation ditches
appear to underlie the upper fill soils Low penetration resistance with less than 10 blows
per foot was encountered at a depth of about 10 feet in Boring No 4 and soft soils with
organic materials were encountered at depths of about 8 to 12 feet in Boring No 5
We believe that the remnants of these previously existing irrigation ditches may have
contributed to the extensive amount of distresses observed along Roads B and C as
compared to Roads A D and E This is further supported by the distress observed in the
sagging of the drain line along Road B between DMH No 5 and CB No 19 Our review of
the construction drawings indicates that the depths of these remnant soft soils encountered
in the borings generally correspond to the bottom elevations of the previously existing
irrigation ditches In addition the remnant soft soils also correspond to about the invert
elevations of the sagged drain line
Under standard earthwork protocol we believe that the soft soils at these previously
existing irrigation ditches should have been removed and replaced with compacted fills prior
to the site filling operations However our current field exploration indicates that a few or
possibly more of these previously existing irrigation ditches may not have been properly
removed and replaced with compacted fills during the site preparation operations for the
mass grading as required in the plans Based on the location of the previously existing
GEOLABS INC

irrigation ditches we believe that the soft soils encountered in the borings in these irrigation
ditches most likely resulted in the sagging of the utility lines and other pipe distresses
As mentioned above some of the utility lines may be underlain by remnants soft soils
with organic materials of the previously existing irrigation ditches We believe that the
remnants of the previously existing irrigation ditches may have resulted in the sagging of the
utility lines as well as other pipe distresses due to consolidation of the soft soils Since the
utility lines were installed back in 1994 and 1995 we believe that the adverse effects of these
soft and or organic soils on the existing utilities should have taken place Therefore we do
not anticipate further significant damages to the existing utility lines resulting from the
presence of the soft and or organic soils
Inundation of Utility Trenches

Reconstruction of the Pavement Section
The inadequately compacted base and subbase courses of the existing pavement
section will likely have an adverse impact on the future performance of the roadways
especially when the pavements are subjected to the generally heavy construction traffic
during the subdivision development If the existing pavement section is not properly
reconstructed we anticipate that additional deterioration of the roadway pavements would
probably occur in the future
Based on the previous discussion on the quality of the base and subbase courses
we believe that it would be prudent to remove the existing pavement section including the
concrete rolled curb and gutter and replace it with a new pavement section of similar
thickness to the original construction plans In addition the subgrade materials below the
pavement section may need to be over excavated by about 12 inches with provisions to
increase the over excavation to 18 inches or more if necessary and replaced with
compacted fills We believe that rehabilitating the pavements by sealing the longitudinal
GEOLABS INC
W O 2819 50 D Page 22
cracks in the asphalt concrete pavements as originally proposed would not aid in the
long term performance of the pavements
Even with reconstruction of the pavement section there is the potential that settlement
of the fill and backfill may occur as a result of water infiltration into the inadequately
compacted fills and backfills To reduce the potential for pavement cracking due to
differential movement of the inadequately compacted fill and backfill materials we
recommend that a geogrid layer or equivalent be provided at the bottom
of the over excavated subgrade level Prior to the placement of the geogrid the
over excavated subgrades should be proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton vibratory drum roller
to not less than 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the
water content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
We believe that the excavated base and subbase course materials if not
contaminated with the underlying clayey soils may be reused as a source of subbase
material underneath the new base course layer We recommend that new base course
material consisting of crushed basalt aggregates be imported for the pavement
reconstruction work The base and subbase courses should be placed in level lifts not
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry
the surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site
during or after construction To reduce the potential for premature pavement distress
resulting from heavy construction traffic we recommend that the pavement reconstruction
work be delayed or performed in conjunction with the development of the subject subdivision

Replacement of Existing Utilities
We understand that it is currently desired to replace a small portion of the existing
utilities sewer and drain lines that has exhibited significant distress The remaining slightly
distressed sewer lines will be rehabilitated or repaired using the cured in place pipe CIPP
method Based on the above discussion we believe that the existing utilities supported by
the original ground should not experience further appreciable settlements Therefore
rehabilitation of the sewer lines using the CIPP method should be a reasonable rehabilitation
method
Based on our field exploration soft and or wet soils may be anticipated at the invert
elevations of the sewer and drain lines that are to be replaced Due to the presence of the
soft and or wet soils we recommend that the soft and or wet soils be over excavated by
a minimum depth of 24 inches below the bottom of the pipe bedding layer and be replaced
A stabilization layer consisting of 24 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation wrapped in a non woven filter fabric Mirafi 180N or equivalent should be
provided below the normal 6 inch thick bedding layer
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches
above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the compaction
effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is critical to use
this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches
of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill
could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The bedding layer and initial trench backfill should be wrapped in a non woven filter
fabric Mirafi 180N or equivalent and overlapped at the top by a minimum distance of
one half the trench width The granular bedding should extend beyond the sides of the
pipe a minimum width of one fourth the outside diameter of the pipe or extend to the edge
of the trench excavation whichever is wider
GEOLABS INC
W O 2819 50 D Page 24
If firm subgrade is encountered at the invert elevation of the replacement utility lines
the non woven filter fabric and crushed rock stabilization layer recommended for soft
subgrade condition may be omitted under this condition However the subgrade underneath
the utility pipes should be over excavated a minimum of 2 feet and be replaced with
compacted fills The need for the 2 foot over excavation and replacement with compacted
fills below the pipe should be determined in the field based on the exposed soil conditions
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
pavement subgrade may consist of the excavated on site soils excluding the soft and or wet
soils or imported select fill material The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future
ground subsidence The upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should
be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction
Utility Boxes
As mentioned previously development of shrinkage cracks around some of the
embedded structures such as electrical boxes etc may have removed the lateral
confinement to these structures resulting in the tilting or settlement of these structures In
addition subsequent surface water runoff over these shrinkage cracks has eroded the soils
resulting in loss of materials around these structures
One method to correct the affected structures will involve the removal of the loose
soils encountered below and around these structures to expose the adjacent firm materials
Backfills below and around the reconstructed or reset structures should consist of fill
materials moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The backfill should be placed tightly against the
structure and graded to divert water away from the utility boxes
It should be noted that backfilling and recompacting the soils around the utility boxes
would not be effective if the surrounding ground were allowed to dry and shrinkage cracks
GEOLABS INC
W O 2819 50 D Page 25
were to develop in the ground Therefore consideration may be given to the use of select
granuiar fill material as the backfill around the utility boxes to reduce the potential for
recurrence of the shrinkage cracks and erosion of the soil materials around the utility boxes
If select granular fill materials are to be used around the utility box structures the select
granular fill should consist of non expansive granular material such as crushed coral or
basalt The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than
3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles
passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more
and should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent
If it is elected not to remove the loose soils around these utility box structures we
believe that the shrinkage cracks around these structures may be backfilled with the on site
soils provided that the soils are placed at least 5 percent dry of the optimum moisture
content This serves to reduce the potential for recurrence of the shrinkage crack
development in the near future
Light Pole Foundations
We understand that new light pole foundations will be required as part of the repair
work for the infrastructure deficiency correction project Based on the information provided
the new light poles are of steel construction approximately 30 feet in height 400 pounds in
weight and 11 inches in diameter at the base
In order to develop the required bearing and lateral load resistances the light pole
structures may be supported by a foundation system consisting of cast in place concrete
drilled shafts Based on the anticipated structural loads and the subsoil conditions
encountered at the project site we recommend that drilled shaft foundations with a minimum
diameter of 24 inches and an embedment length of not less than 8 feet below the design
finished grades be used for the new light pole structures
The cast in place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally from
skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding soils An allowable compressive load
GEOLABS INC
W O 2819 50 D Page 26
capacity of up to 10 kips per shaft may be used for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces
Lateral loads imposed on the light pole foundations may be resisted by resistance of
the shaft soil system to deflect Lateral load resistance of the drilled shaft is a function of the
stiffness of the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable deflection at the top of
shaft and induced moment in the shaft Based on the anticipated foundation demand our
lateral load analyses indicated that the estimated deflection at the top of the drilled shaft
should be less than inch
The anticipated settlements of the light pole foundations are estimated to be on the
order of less than inch We believe that a significant portion of the settlements is elastic
and should occur as the loads are applied
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the contact
between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper construction
techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in drilling the shaft holes and
in placing concrete into the holes A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 6 to 9 inch
range should be used to provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the
surrounding soil The concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce segregation
of the aggregates from the concrete mix If pre cast concrete pole bases are used the gap
between the pole base and the surrounding soils should be backfilled with concrete or lean
concrete A representative of Geolabs should be present to observe the installation of drilled
shafts during construction to confirm the subsoil conditions and embedment depths
Shrinkage Cracks
Our field observations indicate that large shrinkage cracks are present at the existing
ground surface across the undeveloped lots It should be noted that water infiltration into the
subsurface through these shrinkage cracks could have a significant impact on the
performance of the underground utilities supported on the inadequately compacted fills and
backfills Due to the clayey nature of the upper fill and backfill materials these soils may
GEOLABS INC
W O 2819 50 D Page 27
become soft and lose their bearing strength when saturated resulting in potential distress
to the underground utilities and manhole structures supported on the filis and backfills In
addition water infiltration into the inadequately compacted fills and backfills would result in
compression of the inadequately compacted fill soils resulting in appreciable ground
settlements Therefore special attention should be given to the reduction of water infiltration
into the subsurface such as closing up the existing shrinkage cracks and controlling
landscape irrigation in the future when the subdivision is fully developed It should be noted
that Subdivision A also exhibited similar shrinkage cracks at the ground surface however
we did not observe the pavement cracks and utility line distress in Subdivision A as
compared to Subdivision B
We believe that the moisture content of the on site soils was kept moist and relatively
constant during the mass grading phase As a result the development of large shrinkage
cracks might not be evident during the site grading operations Due to the high plasticity of
the on site clayey soils shrinking of the near surface soils started to develop as soil moisture
was evaporated from the soil system under the prolonged dry climatic conditions
characteristic of the Ewa area These shrinkage cracks may close up to a certain degree
upon wetting as the soils expand during periods of wet weather or heavy rainstorms
However shrinkage of the on site soils and subsequent cracking of the ground surface will
recur when the dry season returns Therefore the development of shrinkage cracks will
recur when the on site clayey soils are subjected to prolonged dry climatic conditions which
causes large fluctuations in the moisture content unless some measures are taken to
maintain the soil moisture content relatively constant
In areas where water ponding occurs the wet ground will have a higher potential for
shrinkage cracks to develop since the wet soils will lose water more readily upon exposure
to dry conditions and will shrink more Therefore it should be recognized that this
phenomenon will recur and continue unless measures are taken to maintain the soil moisture
conient relatively constant in order to reduce the shrink swell behavior of the on site clayey
soils due to moisture fluctuations
GEOLABS INC
W O 2819 50 D Page 28
In addition shrinkage cracks observed at the site may cause future homeowners to
be concerned and may become a potential tripping hazard Shrinkage cracks located
adjacent to the house foundations are potentially detrimental since water can readily infiltrate
through the crack opening and soften the subsoils around the house foundations thus
affecting their performance Therefore a design level geotechnical assessment by the
developer of the subject subdivision would be desirable to provide specific recommendations
for the treatment of the shrinkage cracks and future site development

APPENDIX A

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
10 borings extending to depths of about 16 5 to 31 5 feet below the existing ground surface
at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using
a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer or geologist who observed the drilling operations on
a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 10
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained in general accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown
as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Seven Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test results are
presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentation
of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
Eight sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 D 1140 and four hydrometer ASTM
D 422 tests were performed to evaluate the gradation characteristics and to aid in soil
classification In addition four double hydrometer ASTM D 4221 tests were performed on
to evaluate the potentially dispersive characteristics of the on site soils Graphic
presentations of the test results are provided on Plates B 2 through B 6
The conventional consolidation tests were modified to evaluate the compressibility
characteristics of the soil samples under inundation of water The soil sample is allowed to
settle until it has stabilized under its overburden load Water is then added to the soil sample
to evaluate its collapse or swell potential in a saturated condition Sixteen sets of modified
consolidation tests were performed and the test results are presented on Plates B 7 through
B 9
Four laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the typical fill and backfill materials to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 10 through B 13
Four Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 91 were performed on bulk
samples of the typical fill and backfill materials to evaluate the dry density and moisture
content relationships The test results are presented on Plates B 14 through B 17
