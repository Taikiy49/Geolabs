Dear Mr. Eduardo: 
We have provided geotechnical engineering services in support of the F17 Replace 
Fuel Storage Tanks, Defense Fuel Supply Point project at the U.S. Army Garrison – 
Kwajalein Atoll (USAG-KA) in the Republic of the Marshall Islands. 
This letter report summarizes the vibration monitoring work performed on the project 
and presents the results of our monitoring program performed between March 13 and 
May 11, 2019. The project location and general vicinity are shown on Plates 1 through 3. 
Project Considerations 
Vibration monitoring was conducted in support of the test pile program at the Tank 8 
project site. Monitoring of the background vibrations prior to construction was conducted on 
March 13, 2019, which resulted in a maximum recorded resultant peak particle velocity 
(RPPV) of 0.016 inches per second. 
A pretest pile program was conducted from May 7 through May 9, 2019 to test the 
suitability of the hammer to drive the pile and the following hammer throttle settings were 
used during the pretest program: 100% (CRT100); 90% (CRT90); 75% (CRT75); 50% 
(CRT50). 
The hammer consisted of an ICE 416L vibratory that has a driving force of 100 tons 
and a centrifugal force of 90 tons and applies these forces at a frequency between 800 and 
1,700 revolutions per minute (rpm) with an impact amplitude of 0.9 inches. The pile 
consisted of 70-foot long 14x117 H-Pile that has been fitted with a 22-inch diameter conical 
ram point driven to a depth of about 65 feet. The test pile program began on May 10 and 
was suspended on May 11, 2019. The peak particle velocity threshold for critical structures 
was set at 1.0 inches per second by others.

Instrumentation 
Vibration monitoring equipment consisting of three Instantel Micromate Plus units 
were used to monitor the vibrations generated during pile driving operations. To measure 
vibrations, sensors (geophone) were placed at varying distances from the pile driving 
activities to assess magnitudes of the vibration in relation to horizontal distances from the 
vibration source.  
The vibration monitoring units consist of a three-component geophone and a signal 
conditioner/recorder unit. The geophone senses ground motion along three mutually 
perpendicular (orthogonal) axes; thus providing detection of ground motion in the vertical, 
transverse, and longitudinal directions. The signal conditioner/recorder unit receives and 
processes these signals to provide a record of vibrations. Resultant peak particle velocities 
(RPPVs) are automatically computed by vector summation of the motions along the 
orthogonal axes. 
For this project, the monitoring equipment was set at a scanning interval of every 
two seconds during the vibration monitoring process. During the two-second scanning 
interval, the monitoring equipment samples the vibration data at a rate of 2,048 data points 
per second and stores the resultant peak level of vibration during the two-second interval. 
The magnitude of the highest vibration level observed and its corresponding time was 
plotted and a histogram was generated at the completion of each vibration event period.  
Results and Recommendations 
Because the vibration amplitudes for a given energy source attenuate with increasing 
horizontal distance from the source in exponential form, the RPPVs versus the horizontal 
distance were plotted on the log-log scale as shown on Pile Driving Vibration Attenuation 
Plots, Plates 4 and 5. We selected multiple locations at varying distances to measure 
RPPVs induced by the pile driving operations in order to generate this graphic 
relationship.  
The results from the vibration monitoring during the pretest program are summarized 
on Pretest Vibration Monitoring Results, Plate 6. The 50 percent throttle setting (CRT50) 
failed to penetrate past a depth of 4 feet and was not included in our summary.  
For the test pile program, the 90 percent throttle setting (CRT90) was chosen to drive 
the piles. The recorded peak magnitude of the vibration measured during the test pile driving 
ranged from about 0.580 to 0.039 inches per second at the various locations. The resulting 
RPPVs associated with each pile and monitoring location are shown on Test Vibration 
Monitoring Results, Plate 7. 
We did not encounter peak particle velocity exceeding the 1.0-inch per second 
threshold during our monitoring.
