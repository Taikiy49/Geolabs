SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our exploratory borings drilled along the proposed roadway improvements site 
generally encountered an asphaltic concrete surface about 1 to 4 inches thick underlain by 
an aggregate base course layer to about 4 to 6 inches below the existing ground surface. 
Below the asphaltic concrete and base course, we encountered residual and saprolitic soils 
extending to the maximum explored depth of 16.5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
We did not encounter the asphaltic concrete layer in three of the borings drilled at the 
existing unpaved parking lots. We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at 
the time of our field exploration. 
Numerous pavement distresses were observed along Kokee Road, including 
alligator and block cracking, depressions, edge cracking, potholes, rutting, and weathering 
and raveling. The severity levels for the distress types ranged from low to high. New or 
newer pavement areas were observed along Area B at the project site. 
We understand that pavement reconstruction is not desired at this time, and alternate 
temporary pavement remedial measures are desired for the existing pavements. 
An AC overlay with 2 inches of PMA may be used over the cold planned surface for 
pavements in fair to good condition. For pavements with moderate distress, it is

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the 
extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene Epoch 
(more than 21/4 million years before present). Following the cessation of this main shield 
building phase, renewed volcanic activity occurred with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of 
the post-erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of alluvial 
sediments of the Palikea Formation. 
The majority of the Island of Kauai is covered by lavas of the Waimea Canyon 
Volcanic Series. These lavas consist of four distinct formations: Napali, Olokele, Haupu, 
and Makaweli. These formations are comprised of thin-bedded a`a and pahoehoe flows 
to massive basalt flows that ponded in calderas and graben. The project site is within the 
Napali Formation of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series. 
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of 
Kauai. These rocks are generally characterized as thick flows of dense basalt extruded 
from groups of vents aligned in north-south trends in various locales. Associated with the 
vents are pyroclastic materials, which usually form low cinder cones at the vent. 
During the Pliestocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred as a 
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great 
continental glaciers accumulated, the level of the ocean fell since less water was available 
to fill the oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded or melted, global sea levels 
rose because more water was available. The land mass of Kauai remained essentially 
stable during these changes, and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. These 
glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower 
relative to the present sea level of Kauai. 
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of 
terrigenious sediments in the heads of the old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations, and lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet waters 
protected by fringing reefs.

The basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lavas of the Koloa Volcanic Series is 
generally characterized by flows of jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin 
clinker layers. The weathering process has formed a mantle of residual soils which grade 
to saprolite with depth. In general, saprolite is composed of mainly silty material and is 
typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks. The saprolite grades to basaltic rock 
formation with depth.

Existing Pavement Conditions

Lookout and Puu Okila Lookout parking lots. Additionally, the Puu Ka Pele Picnic area 
and Kokee Picnic Pavilion parking lots were covered with crushed rock, and we 
understand that a new flexible pavement structure is desired for these areas.

 Methodology of Pavement Design 
Reconstruction of the existing pavements is usually performed for those areas 
where the observed distresses are likely caused by failures in the underlying base, 
subbase, and/or subgrade materials. These distresses include depression, rutting, 
edge cracking, and potholes. For the reconstruction option, the existing pavement 
sections requiring reconstruction should be completely removed and replaced with 
a new pavement section. The flexible pavement sections presented herein were 
generally determined based on the methodology described in the revised 
Pavement Design Manual dated March 2002 prepared by the State of Hawaii – 
Department of Transportation, Highways Division. The pavement design 
methodology is based on the Hveem Stabilometer method developed and used by 
the California Department of Transportation (Caltrans). We understand that the 
use of the above pavement design method is desired. 
Based on the design guidelines from the revised Pavement Design Manual dated 
March 2002, portions of Kokee State Roadway may be classified as “Low Volume 
Paved” roadway. Therefore, pavements will need to be designed for a pavement 
life of 20 years. Based on communications with SSFM International, Inc., we 
understand that traffic distribution from a 2020 HDOT project, Kokee Road to 
Entrance to Kokee State Park MP 6.72 – 14.10 may be used for pavement design 
of Areas A and B. Additionally, we understand that the 2007 design traffic 
parameters provided by SSFM International, Inc. may be used for Area C.

 Pavement Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the pavement 
surface should be sloped, and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry 
surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. Surface water 
ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development of good 
shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage 
structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement 
deterioration or premature failure of the pavements. 
To reduce the potential for subsurface water accumulating at the low elevation 
areas along the roadway, we recommend installing transverse subdrains at the low 
roadway elevation areas. We recommend using a 6-inch perforated PVC pipe for 
the subdrain system. The 6-inch PVC pipes should be installed with a minimum 
slope of 0.5 percent.

Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be 
over-excavated to expose firm natural material, and the resulting excavation should 
be backfilled with well-compacted engineered fill. 
After clearing and grubbing, the existing ground surface should be scarified to a depth 
of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted to 
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the 
in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density 
of the same soil established in accordance with AASHTO T-180 (or ASTM D1557). 
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to 
the maximum dry density.  
3.2.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, the on-site materials may be re-used as a source of fill material provided 
that the maximum particle size is less than 3 inches in the largest dimension and free 
of vegetation and deleterious materials. Because the project site is in a high rainfall 
area, moisture conditioning and drying of the on-site materials should be anticipated 
when the on-site materials are re-used as fill material. It should be noted that areas 
for aeration and drying of the on-site materials along the project site may be limited. 
Imported fill materials, if required, should consist of non-expansive select granular 
material, such as crushed coral, basalt, or cinder sand. The select granular fill should 
be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in the largest 
dimension. The material should also contain between 10 to 30 percent particles 
passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 
or more and should have a maximum swell value of 1 percent or less. Imported fill 
materials should be tested for conformance with these recommendations prior to 
delivery to the project site for the intended use. 
Aggregate base course and aggregate subbase materials should consist of crushed 
basaltic aggregates and should meet the requirements of Sections 703.06 and

3.2.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Fills should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in level 
lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to at least 90 percent 
relative compaction. The compaction requirement should be increased to 95 percent 
relative compaction for fills placed within 3 feet under the pavements. Because of the 
difficulty in achieving 95 percent relative compaction for the on-site materials, 
imported fill materials will likely be required within 3 feet under the pavements. 
Aggregate base course and aggregate subbase materials should be moisture-
conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 
inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction. 
Compaction should be accomplished by using sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, 
multiple-wheel pneumatic-tired rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction 
equipment. Where compaction is less than required, additional compactive effort 
should be applied with adjustment of moisture content as necessary to obtain the 
specified compaction. 
It should be noted that the project site is in an area of significant rainfall throughout 
the year. Therefore, earthwork operations in these high rainfall areas may be difficult 
and could result in slower than normal earthwork operations. 
3.2.4 Excavations

cuts into the medium stiff to hard clayey silt, silty clay, and sandy silt soils. It is 
anticipated that the medium stiff to hard clayey silt, silty clay, and sandy silt soils may 
be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment, such as ripping with a large 
bulldozer.

3.2.5 Cut and Fill Slopes 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings, it is anticipated 
that most of the cut slopes would expose soils consisting of medium stiff to hard 
clayey silt, silty clay, or sandy silt. In general, we believe that cut slopes into the 
soil material may be cut at an inclination of 2H:1V or flatter.  
Fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. Slope 
benches are recommended at maximum 20-foot vertical height intervals. Fills 
placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V (such as at the gullies) should be keyed and 
benched into the existing slope to provide stability for the new fill against sliding. 
The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level 
horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement 
recommendations. 
Fill slopes in excess of 5 feet in height should be constructed by overfilling and cutting 
back to the design slope ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. Track-rolling of 
slopes in excess of 5 feet in height should not be accepted. In the event that 
over-cutting of a slope occurs, keying and benching requirements should be 
implemented instead of backfilling the slope to the design grade with sliver fills. Water 
should be diverted away from the tops of slopes, and slope planting should be 
provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes.

A P P E N D I X   A 
Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
15 borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 15, extending to depths of about 5 to 
16.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, 15 bulk samples of the 
near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1 trhough Bulk-15, were obtained to evaluate the 
support characteristics of the near-surface soils. The approximate boring and bulk sample 
locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.4, and Plates 4.1 through 4.3. 
The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig with continuous flight augers.  
Our geologists classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-15. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Fourteen Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic 
presentation of the test results is provided on Plates B-1 and B-2. 
Two Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. 
Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on Plate B-3. 
Nine one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the soils under surcharge pressures. 
The swell tests results are presented on Plate B-4. 
Two Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression test (ASTM D2850) were 
performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the in-
situ soils. The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressure was used as the applied 
confining pressure for the relatively “undisturbed” soil sample. The test results and the 
stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-5 and B-6. 
To evaluate the Unconfined Compressive Strength of the on-site clayey soils, 
four unconfined compression tests were performed on selected in-situ samples in 
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D2166.  The test results are shown on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths and summarized on Table B-2. 
Individual stress-strain curves of the unconfined compression tests are presented on 
Plates B-7 through B-10. 
Two direct shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested.  Results of the direct 
shear tests are presented on Plates B-11 and B-12. 
Three Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils and potential borrow material to evaluate the dry density 
and moisture content relationships.  The test results are presented on Plates B-13 through 
B-15. 
Fifteen laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed 
on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the support characteristics of the 
soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-16 and B-30.
