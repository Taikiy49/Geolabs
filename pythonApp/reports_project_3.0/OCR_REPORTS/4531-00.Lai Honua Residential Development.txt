2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Maui is comprised of two major volcanoes the older West Maui Tertiary
Epoch and the more recent East Maui also known as Haleakala Pleistocene Epoch The
isthmus of Maui is a narrow gently sloping plain located between these two volcanoes The
project site is located near the southern end of the island on the lower southwest slope of the
East Maui volcano Haleakala
Haleakala was generally built in three distinguishable series of lava flows The lower
unit the Honomanu Volcanic Series late Pliocene early Pleistocene Epoch consists of
thinly bedded tholeiitic basaltic pahoehoe and a a lavas These lavas tend to be very
permeable and carry basal water Overlying this unit is the Kula Volcanic Series early
Pleistocene Epoch composed primarily of thicker alkalic a a flows which may contain many
inter stratified thin ash soil layers A long Quiescent period followed during which time
canyons were carved in the volcano although a few eruptions may have occurred during this
erosion interval in addition to erosion due to eustatic changes higher and lower stands of sea
level
The subsurface soils consist of fill material underlain by fine dune sand The fill material
and dune sand were underlain by basalt rock formation In general basalt rock is considered
to be relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite readily in both the horizontal and
vertical directions Water is transmitted through the porous rock matrix along joints through
fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and through clinker layers The permeability of the
clinker and cavities is high therefore they serve as the major water transmission features
The presence of soil seams on the other hand tends to reduce the overall permeability of the
rock formation

rock formation encountered at the project site Due to the anticipated high structural loads
imposed by the six story building there is a potential for loss of foundation support resulting
from collapse of the cavities To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting
from collapse of cavities below a footing we recommend that a program of cavity probing and
grouting be implemented for the six story building during construction
Due to the anticipated low structural loads of the two and three story buildings we
believe that the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of the cavities

below the foundations is relatively small If it is desired to further reduce the potential for loss

For estimating purposes we recommend that a probe hole be drilled at each spread
footing location and at 10 feet on center along wall foundations The probe holes should be
at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of
footing elevation If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing
operation additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacing to aid in delineating the

should be backfilled with controlled low strength material CLSM injected at low to moderate
pressures As an alternative the probe holes and cavities discovered may be backfilled with
sand cement grout with a slump range of 6 to 9 inches injected at low to moderate pressures
Because of the potential for encountering large cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that the probe drill be available on site until the probing and grouting operations

around the pipes and to reduce compaction of the backfill thus reducing the potential for

The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on site soils It should be noted that
boulders and rock fragments greater than 6 inches in maximum dimension should not be used

placed in about 8 inch loose lifts and mechanically compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction Where trenches are located in paved areas the upper 3 feet of the
trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction

utility installation will likely involve excavation in the basalt formation and may require hard
ripping or the use of hoe rams Care must be exercised by the contractor to avoid over ripping

potential loss of bearing strength for foundations in its vicinity

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
nine borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 9 extending to depths of about 14 to
27 5 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted
drill rig equipped with rotary coring tools The approximate locations of the borings drilled are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 9
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed
to drive the sampier the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples retrieved
in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer test are summarized
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the site were obtained by using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method
that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative quality
of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of materiai recovered from a coring
attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material is recovered
from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs
of Borings as REC 74

extending to depths of approximately 0 4 to 13 feet below the existing ground surface The
test pits were excavated on August 7 2000 The approximate locations of the test pits are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The test pits were excavated using a backhoe
The materials encountered in the test pits were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the excavation operations Soils were
classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on
Plate A Descriptions of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Test Pits

APPENDIX C
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX C
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
One 1 inch ring swell test was performed on a relatively undisturbed sample of the
surface fill soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The test results are
presented on Plate C 1
Two sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 and D 1140 were performed on selected
samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics and to aid in soil classification
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate C 2
