PROJECT DESCRIPTION 
The project site is located on the corner lot at the intersection of Dillingham 
Boulevard and Alakawa Street in the Honolulu area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The 
new storage development consisted of construction of a building addition abutting the 
renovated existing two-story building on the east as the Phase 2 development project.  
The general project vicinity is shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
Based on the geotechnical engineering report referenced above, it was 
recommended to support the new storage building addition on a deep foundation system 
consisting of 24-inch diameter ACIP piles with an allowable compressive load capacity of 
250 kips (125 tons) per pile. The ACIP piles would extend through the near-surface fills, 
lagoonal and alluvial deposits, and derive support primarily from end-bearing on the 
medium hard to hard basalt rock formation with design pile lengths ranging from about 55 
to 60 feet below the bottom of the pile caps. 
The general contractor for the project was Sound Building Systems, Inc. and the 
ACIP pile subcontractor was Kelikai, Inc. 
STATIC PILE LOAD TEST 
In order to evaluate the adequacy of the design ACIP pile lengths and load 
capacity, a static pile load test was conducted on one sacrificial ACIP load test pile. To 
better understand the load transfer mechanism at the pile-soil interface, the load test pile 
was instrumented with vibrating wire embedment strain gages installed at various depths 
along the length of the load test pile. The 24-inch diameter load test pile was installed to a 
depth of about 53.2 feet below the existing ground surface. 
A Bauer BG-22H drill rig equipped with automatic monitoring equipment and a 
continuous flight hollow-stem auger was used to install the load test pile. The load test pile 
was advanced and terminated at the basalt layer due to refusal conditions, defined as very 
hard drilling with no advancement. A truck-mounted hydraulic concrete pump with an 
average stroke volume of about 0.04 cubic yards per stroke was used to pump the grout into 
the pile system.  
The load test pile was loaded in general accordance with ASTM D1143, Quick Load 
Test Method. The 24-inch diameter load test pile was loaded to a maximum load of about 
550 kips. Based on our field observations and the results of the static pile load test, it is our 
opinion that the 24-inch diameter ACIP piles drilled to refusal conditions would be capable 
of providing sufficient compressive load resistance, meeting the design compressive load 
allowable capacity of 250 kips with a factor of safety of at least 2.0.

PRODUCTION PILE INSTALLATION 
The production ACIP pile installation program, consisting of installing 136, 24-inch 
diameter ACIP piles, including 16 ACIP piles that were installed as indicator piles at the 
beginning of the pile installation program, was conducted from April 28 through 
May 28, 2020 under the near-continuous (full-time) observations by our representatives.  
The same equipment (Bauer BG-22H drill rig equipped with hollow-stem augers) was used 
to install the production piles. Automatic monitoring equipment was mounted on both 
drilling/pumping equipment with the intent of providing auger depth, grout volume, grout 
pressure, etc., on a near-continuous basis. 
During the pile installation operation, logs were maintained for each ACIP pile, 
which included information pertaining to pile number, date installed, depth of pile, grout 
volume used, grout return depth, etc. The locations of the production ACIP piles installed 
are shown on the ACIP Pile Identification Plan, Plate 2. The production ACIP pile 
installation summary is presented on Plates 3.1 through 3.5 of this letter report. 
Based on our observation of the equipment performance and subsurface conditions 
encountered, it is our professional opinion that the production 24-inch diameter ACIP piles 
have been installed to sufficient depths and resistance to provide the design allowable 
load capacity of 250 kips per pile with a minimum factor of safety of 2.0.
