SECTION 4 0 REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes which include the following Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has
had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa
and Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of Hualalai Mountain which
makes up the west central portion of the island The lava formation encountered at the site
appears to be of both a a and pahoehoe type flows which spread and ponded as they
approached the ocean Ava lava is typically characterized by a porous rough and irregular
flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and
boulders Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal
structure of vesicular porous rock
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas especially pahoehoe lava These
cavities are formed when the lava is still in a molten state and they represent both lava tubes
intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes are formed when
molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may
extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Blisters and pockets inter flow
cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted through
the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and along
clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they serve as the
primary water transmission features

7 1 Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we recommend
that shallow spread and or continuous footings be used to support the proposed building
structures planned at the site An allowable bearing pressure of up to 4 000 pounds per
square foot psf may be used for the design of footings bearing on the basalt formation or
structural fill materials placed at the site This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and
may be increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces

In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade Foundations located next to utility ttenches or easements should be
embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the
utility tench or extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement
is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural
loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the footing subgrades the soft and or
loose materials should be over excavated and replaced with structural fill The
over excavation should be extended until dense materials are exposed The over excavation
should be backfilled with structural fill materials that are moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
As an alternative the bottom of footing may be extended down to bear on the underlying
competent material
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our recommendations
we estimate total settlements of the foundations bearing on the basalt formation and or
structural fill materials to be less than 1 inch Differential settlements between adjacent
footings supported on similar materials are estimated to be on the order of about 0 5 inches
Lateral loads acting on the building structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing material and by passive
earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient
of friction of 0 45 may be used for footings bearing on the basalt formation or structural fill
materials Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent
fluid pressure of 450 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the
soils around the footings are well compacted or are relatively undisturbed Unless covered by
pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected

7 2 Slabs On Grade
We envision that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed structures
planned at the site For interior slabs that will not be subjected to vehicular traffic we
recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill be provided below the slabs to
provide for uniform support The cushion fill material should consist of open graded gravel
such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation In addition the cushion fill layer
would also serve as a capillary moisture break
To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor
coverings an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the cushion fill layer
Placement of 2 inches of moist fine sand above the moisture barrier may be considered to
provide for protection of the moisture barrier and to aid in curing of the slab concrete
Provision of the 2 inches of moist fine sand above the moisture barrier is optional and will be
left up to the discretion of the project structural engineer Flexible floor coverings should be
considered on top of the slab since they can better mask minor slab cracking
Exterior concrete slabs such as walkways patios and pool decks may also be
required for the project In general we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion fill
layer consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be provided below
exterior concrete slabs that will not be subjected to vehicular traffic To reduce the potential
for significant slab cracking due to concrete shrinkage crack control joints should be provided
at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at right angle
intersections

We understand that retaining structures such as site retaining walls and basement

guidelines may be used for design of the retaining structures planned at the site
Retaining Structure Foundations
In general we believe that retaining structure foundations may be designed in
accordance with the recommendations presented in the Foundations section of this
report However the retaining structure footings should have a minimum width of
18 inches In addition foundations for retaining structures placed in areas with a

extended deeper to obtain a minimum 6 foot setback distance measured horizontally
from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope Footings oriented paralle

Dynamic Lateral Earth Forces
Since the project site is located on the seismically active Island of Hawaii Seismic
Zone 4 in accordance with the 1997 Uniform Building Code dynamic lateral earth
forces will need to be considered in the design of retaining structures The dynamic
lateral earth force due to seismic loading may be estimated using
16H pounds per foot of wall length for level backfill conditions where H is the height
of the wall in feet For a maximum 3H 1V sloping backfill dynamic lateral earth forces
due to seismic loading may be estimated using 28H pounds per lineal foot of wall It
should be noted that the dynamic lateral earth forces provided assume that the wall will
be allowed to move laterally by up to about 2 to 4 inches in the event of an earthquake
If this amount of lateral movement is not acceptable the retaining wall should be
designed with higher dynamic lateral forces for a semi restrained wall condition For
a semi restrained wall condition dynamic lateral forces due to seismic loading may be
estimated using 28H pounds per lineal foot of wall for level backfill conditions
The dynamic lateral earth force would generally act at the mid height of the wall The
dynamic lateral earth force presented above is in addition to the static lateral earth
pressures An appropriately reduced factor of safety may be used when dynamic
lateral earth forces due to earth pressures are accounted for in the design of the
retaining structure
Drainage
In general retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the retaining
wall The perforated pipe should discharge to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As
an alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may
be used instead of the permeable drainage material The prefabricated drainage
product should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the
retaining wall

Fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The compaction requirement for fills or backfills
below areas subjected to vehicular traffic should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent

as a percentage of the maximum dry density determined by ASTM Test Designation
D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight corresponding to
the maximum dry density
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practical in fills composed of rock
boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number of passes of a
compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be conducted at the start
of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a representative from Geolabs
On the basis of this testing program the number of passes may then be used as the field
criterion for adequate compaction
For preliminary estimating purposes compaction may be accomplished by using a
large vibratory drum roller having a static weight of at least 10 tons traveling at a speed of
about 300 feet or less per minute The roller should pass over the fill surface at least eight
times for 90 percent relative compaction and ten times for 95 percent relative compaction

7 6 Percolation Testing
Two falling head and ten constant head percolation tests were conducted in the
borings drilled to provide data for design of the proposed drywells and seepage pits
planned at the site Three percolation tests were performed at depths ranging from about
6 to 7 feet below the ground surface to evaluate the permeability characteristics of the
near surface fills materials Nine percolation tests were performed at depths ranging from
about 12 to 21 feet below the ground surface to evaluate the permeability characteristics
of the on site basalt formation The results of the percolation tests performed at the site
are presented in Appendix B of this report
The percolation tests indicated that the near surface fill materials generally have
coefficient of permeability k in the range of about 8 5 x 10 to 3 3 x 10 feet per minute
which corresponds to about 4 3 x 10 to 1 7 x 10 centimeters per second The percolation
tests indicated that the on site basalt formation generally have coefficient of permeability k

in the range of about 8 7 x 10 to 5 5 x 10 feet per minute which corresponds to about
4 4 x 10 to 2 8 x 10 centimeters per second One of our field percolation tests conducted
in the basalt formation was not able to establish a constant head in the drilled boring indicating
that the hydraulic conductivity is much higher than materials encountered in the other borings
Based on the results of our field percolation tests we believe that an average hydraulic
conductivity of about 0 05 centimeters per second may be assumed in the fill materials The
average hydraulic conductivity in the fill materials corresponds to a disposal capacity of
1 060 gallons per square foot per day in the seepage pits For the basalt formation at the site
an average hydraulic conductivity of about 0 10 centimeters per second corresponding to
2 120 gallons per square foot per day may be assumed for design of the seepage pits
It should be emphasized that the hydraulic conductivity of the basalt rock formation
at the site is highly variable due to the presence or absence of significant voids in the lava or
the presence of embedded clinker seams which greatly affect the disposal capacity of a
drywell or seepage pit In general the greater the number of clinker seams and or voids
encountered by the drywell excavation the higher the overall permeability and disposal
capacity
7 7 Underground Utility Lines
We envision that new underground utility lines will be required for development of this
project It is anticipated that most of the trenches for the utility lines will be excavated in the
surface fills and medium hard to hard basalt rock encountered at the site In general granular
bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is
recommended for support below the pipes
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes
It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes

Improper backfill material around the pipes and improper placement of the backfill could result
in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
As an alternative the granular bedding and the initial trench backfill up to about
12 inches above the top of the pipes may consist of 1 inch minus well graded granular
materials aggregate base course The granular bedding and the initial trench backfill should
be moisture conditioned and compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of well graded granular materials less
than 6 inches in maximum size The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture placed in about 8 inch loose lifts and mechanically compacted to at least
90 percent relative compaction Where trenches are located in paved areas the upper 3 feet
of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to not less than
95 percent relative compaction
Because basalt formation was encountered at the ground surface trench work for the
utility line installation will likely involve excavation in the basalt formation and will require hard
ripping or the use of hoerams Care must be exercised by the contractor to avoid over ripping
of the basalt formation which would disrupt the structure of the rock formation resulting in a
potential loss of bearing strength for foundations in its vicinity

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
eight borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 8 extending to depths of about 20 to
21 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill
rig equipped with rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 8
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained by using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method
that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative quality
of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from a coring
attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material is recovered
from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs
of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
