In accordance with your request we have made a study of the soil
conditions at the above referenced lot in order to provide foundation
recommendations for the proposed residence The study consisted of
sampling the subsurface soils by means of hand augering two probe holes
laboratory testing of the swelling potential of the soils and a review of
the preliminary house plans This report summarizes our findings and
recommendations
PROPOSED CONSTRUCTION
The proposed residence will be a two story structure approximately
32 by 42 feet in plan dimensions CMU retaining walls up to about 8 feet
high are planned
Concrete on grade slab is planned for the main floor and patio areas
A layout of the house is shown on the Site Plan Plate 1

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located on the eastern side of Pinaoula Street
near the intersection of Pinaoula Street and Pinaoula Place The site is
bounded by houses on the remaining sides
An existing house approximately 28 by 48 feet in plan is located on
the northern half of the site The proposed new residence would be
situated on the southern half of the same lot which is presently covered
with grass brush and trees
The elevation at the site generally varies from about 310 to 345
to 2 1 H V ratios
SUBSURFACE SOTT CONDITIONS
Two probings were hand augered to depths of 6 and 12 feet below the
existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site
Plan Plate 1
In general the probings encountered stiff reddish to mottled brown
clayey silt down to the bottom of the augered holes These soil
conditions appeared to be similar with those disclosed by the two 15 foot
borings drilled by our office within the same residential lot area for a
previous soil engineering study
No groundwater was encountered at the time of our field exploration

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The stiff reddish brown clayey silt encountered can generally provide
adequate foundation support for the proposed house provided that some care
is exercised during construction to minimize the swelling potential of the
soil
The field exploration and laboratory tests performed on the stiff
on site clayey silt soil indicate that this material generally has low
swelling potential at its present high moisture content Therefore to
reduce effects due to soil swelling under on grade slabs we recommend
that the subgrade soils underneath the slab be kept moist and protected
from drying from the start of construction until the slab concrete
placement
Select non expansive soils should also be used as backfill behind the
retaining walls to reduce the swelling potential of the natural soil
Lot Grading
Cuts and fills are planned to create a level area for the proposed
residence
Q
Prior to construction the area should be cleared of vegetation and
over excavated and backfilled with compacted non expansive soil
All imported fill materials should consist of non expansive soil
such as select borrow crusher waste or coral and should be tested and
approved by the soils engineer prior to being used in the site
The fills should be placed in horizontal lifts not exceeding 8 inches
in compacted thickness and compacted to a minimm of 90 of its maximum
dry density per ASTM D 1557 78

House Foundations
Spread or continuous wall footings may be used for the proposed
residence An allowable bearing value of 2500 p s f may be used on the
natural soil or compacted fill
Individual footings on level areas should be embedded a minimum of 12
inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade Continuous wall footings
should be embedded a minimm of 18 inches below the adjacent finished
grade
If located on a sloping area the footing should be embedded down an
additional amount to obtain a 6 foot minimum horizontal set back distance
measured from the outer edge of the footing out to the slope face This
additional embedment depth is required to help resist the downhill slope
creep movement and to develop the recommended bearing capacity of the
soil
If a footing is located next to a utility line it should extend to
the bottom of the utility trench to reduce footing settlement due to
settlement of the trench backfill

Slab On Grade
It should be noted that the on site clayey silt soils may be
moderately expansive if allowed to dry Therefore the on site soil below
the slab bottom should be kept moist prior to slab concrete placement
The thickened edge of the slab should be embedded a minimm of 18 inches
below the lowest adjacent finish grade
A 4 inch layer of 3B Fine gravel cushion fill should be provided
below the slab In order to protect against future moisture infiltration
an impervious moisture barrier may be incorporated below the slab
Retaining Wall
It is our understanding that CMU retaining walls as high as 8 feet
will be constructed for the proposed residence The guidelines for
retaining wall design are listed below
1 For lateral earth pressures the following can be used
Walls unrestrained at the top 35 p s f equivalent
active condition level fluid pressure
backfill per foot of depth
Wall restrained at the top 55 p s f equivalent
at rest condition level fluid pressure
backfill per foot of depth
An allowable bearing value of up to 2500 p s f is recommended
for foundations bearing on the stiff on site soil
2 A passive earth resistance of 300 p s f per foot of depth may be
used for the wall footing on level areas The passive pressure
must be reduced for walls on slopes
3 A base friction factor of 0 35 may also be used to determine the
resistance against sliding of the wall

4 The wall foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded
a minimm of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade
5 The wall foundations on existing sloping ground areas should be
embedded deeper to provide a minimm 8 foot horizontal set back
distance from the toe of the wall to the existing sloping ground
surface
6 Backfills directly behind the retaining wall should consist of
granular non expansive material such as select borrow crusher
waste or 3B Fine gravel The top 12 inches of backfill should
consist of non expansive impervious material to reduce water
infiltration behind the wall
7 A subsurface drainage system subdrains or weepholes should be
utilized behind the walls Subdrains should be properly
constructed to drain water away from the wall
Drainage
The finish lot grade outside the slab pavement and footings should
be shaped to shed water away from foundations and to avoid ponding
condition Also it is advised that gutter systems be installed around
the house and that gutter water should be diverted far away from the
foundations and walkways Excessive landscape watering near foundations
and slab should also be avoided
Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete
bottoms and drains to prevent water from entering the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance
of the above recommended foundations since ponded water could cause
excessive subsurface soil saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of
strength The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled tight
against the wall or slab footing immediately after setting of the concrete
to prevent ponding condition

Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and maintained
to drain all surface run off away from the slab and footing foundations
Design Review
Final plans and specifications for foundations site grading and
utility construction should be forwarded to the soils engineer for review
and comments prior to construction to see that the intent of our
recommendations is included
Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the lot grading footing excavations and
backfill operation be cbhserved by the soils engineer during construction
to see that adequate bearing has been obtained and that the subsurface
conditions are consistent with the findings of our study
Any imported material required should be non expansive tested and
approved by the soils engineer prior to hauling to the site
The recommendations given in this report are contingent upon the
above observations
