REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers an 
area of approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of the 
following five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea (activity during the recent 
geologic time), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 – 1803), and Mauna Loa and Kilauea (both 
still active). 
The project site is situated on the western flank of Mauna Kea Mountain, which 
makes up the north-central portion of the island.  Based on available geologic information 
and geomorphological interpretation, the project site appears to be underlain by basaltic 
lava flows of the Hamakua and Laupahoehoe Volcanic Series of Mauna Kea Volcano, which 
were deposited during the Holocene and Pleistocene Epoch (Stearns and Macdonald, 
1946). 
Following the cessation of volcanic activity in Kohala and Mauna Kea, activity from 
the other shields resulted in widespread aerial fall of ash over much of the island. This ash 
has weathered rapidly into a fine silty soil, which is typified by low in-situ densities and high 
field moisture contents. In some locales, when the field moisture content is sufficiently high, 
the ash soil exhibits thixotropic properties (i.e., the soil temporarily loses strength when 
remolded and regains its strength after internal pore pressures dissipate).  In more arid 
areas, the ash soil exists in a dry friable state even though field moisture contents can 
approach 100 percent of the dry unit weight.  Our exploration indicates that the site is 
underlain by a surface mantle of this drier volcanic ash soil, which should transition to basalt 
formation and clinker at a deeper depth. 
The lava formation underlying the site generally consists of a’a and pahoehoe basalt 
type flows, which spread and ponded as they flowed toward the ocean. A'a lava typically is 
characterized by a porous, rough, and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged 
accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders. Typically, a denser and 
more layered lava rock material is contained within the lava flow core. Pahoehoe lava is 
characterized by a smooth, rope-like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular 
(porous) rock. Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lavas. Cavities form when 
lavas are still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes (intra-flow cavities) and blisters 
and pockets (inter-flow cavities). Lava tubes form when molten lava drains from the cooling 
flow leaving a hollow tube-like structure, which may extend for a large longitudinal distance 
along the flow. Blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities) are generally smaller in horizontal 
extent.

total of 13 test pits, designated as Test Pit Nos. 1 through 12 and 7A, extending to depths 
of about 2 to 9.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate test pit locations 
are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.

Based on our test pits excavated, the subsurface conditions at the site generally 
consist of volcanic ash and clinker materials underlain by basalt formation.  The thickness 
of the volcanic ash materials ranged from less than 1 foot to about 9.5 feet at the test pit 
locations.  Volcanic ash materials generally consist of stiff sandy and gravelly silts.  It 
should be noted that many cobbles and boulders were scattered around the ground 
surface of the project site.  Therefore, some cobbles and boulders may be embedded in 
the volcanic ash layer. 
In some test pits, volcanic ash materials were underlain by clinker materials mixed 
with volcanic ash consisting of medium dense to dense silty gravel with some sand.  Some 
cobble-sized materials were also present in the clinker layer.   
The test pits were extended down until the excavator reached refusal conditions 
due to the presence of hard basalt formation. Based on the limited field exploration 
conducted at the site, it appears that the depth to basalt formation varies significantly 
across the project site.  In general, our test pits encountered dense basalt formation at a 
depth greater than about 6 feet below the existing ground surface.  Only five of the thirteen 
test pits extended less than 4.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  The maximum 
depth explored by the test pit excavation was 9.5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
It should be noted that the depth to basalt formation will likely vary significantly 
over relatively short distances, due to the undulating topography of the project site.  Dense 
basalt formation was encountered at Test Pit No. 7 at a depth of about 7.5 feet below the 
ground surface.  However, dense basalt formation was encountered at only about 2 feet 
below the ground surface at Test Pit No. 7A, excavated about 200 feet from Test Pit No. 7 
at the top of a small mound. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the test pits excavated at the time of our field 
exploration. However, groundwater levels likely will change in response to seasonal 
precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered during our field exploration are 
presented on the Logs of Test Pits in Appendix A, Plates A-1 through A-13. Photographs of

Based on our site reconnaissance and preliminary field exploration conducted at 
the site, we believe the proposed solar farm development project generally is feasible 
from a geotechnical engineering point-of-view. However, it should be noted that several 
geotechnical considerations may have the potential for some adverse impacts on the 
design and construction of the proposed project. Based on our experience and a review 
of the available information for this site, the geotechnical considerations include, but are 
not limited to, the following. These items are discussed further in the following

Mass Grading 
In general, the topography at the project site consists of rolling hills and undulating 
ground surface.  In order to utilize the majority of the project site for solar array installation, 
we envision that some mass grading may be required to provide a relatively level or a 
constant sloping subgrade.  Due to the large areal extent of the project site, substantial 
quantity of cuts and fills may be required for the project. 
As mentioned above, dense basalt formation may be present at shallow depths in 
many areas of the project site.  Therefore, significant cuts required for mass grading may 
require excavation into dense basalt formation.  Based on our experience in the vicinity 
of the project site, basalt formation may be slightly weathered to relatively unweathered 
and very hard.  Therefore, excavations in dense basalt formation will likely require the 
use of hoerams, chipping, or blasting.  In addition, crushing of the excavated rocks to 
reduce the particle sizes may be required prior to their use as a source of fill materials. 
Volcanic Ash Materials 
In general, the surface materials at the project site consist of volcanic ash 
materials.  We envision that site grading required at the site will involve placement of fills 
consisting of volcanic ash materials.  In addition, volcanic ash materials may be present 
at the subgrades of new structures and pavements required for the development of the 
project. 
Volcanic ash materials are generally difficult to compact since the strength of the 
materials is very sensitive to moisture content.  Volcanic ash materials at the site generally 
exist in a drier condition.  Therefore, some moisture-conditioning may be required to 
facilitate compaction of the materials.  However, it should be noted that excessive 
moisture in the volcanic ash materials may create pumping ground conditions.  Therefore, 
the contractor should use caution not to overwater when moisture-conditioning volcanic 
ash materials. Where soft or pumping ground conditions are encountered during 
construction, over-excavation and replacement with suitable fills may be required.  
Consideration may be given to mixing the volcanic ash materials with crushed rock 
to provide a well-graded structural fill.  Mixing volcanic ash materials with crushed rock 
would reduce the moisture sensitivity of the material and may facilitate easier compaction.

Based on the limited field exploration conducted at the site, it appears that the 
depth to basalt formation varies significantly across the project site.  Our test pits

encountered basalt formation from about 2 to 9.5 feet below the ground surface.  In 
addition, depth to basalt formation is anticipated to vary significantly over relatively short 
distances. 
The selection of the new foundation system for the solar arrays would need to 
consider the potential to encounter basalt formation at relatively shallow depths.  In 
addition, any mass grading required prior to the installation of solar array support system 
would need to be considered when evaluating the anticipated depth to dense basalt 
formation. 
We envision that the majority of solar array foundation systems, such as driven 
H- or C-piles, drilled micropiles, and screwed-in ground anchors, would be suitable in 
areas where basalt formation is not anticipated to be encountered during installation, such 
as in fill areas of the site.  However, the presence of shallow basalt formation layer may 
be problematic for some foundation systems. 
In general, we believe that a driven pile system will not be able to penetrate through 
the basalt formation.  In addition, drilled micropile system would need to have the capability 
to drill through the basalt formation by utilizing core barrels or percussion drilling techniques.  
In general, we believe that drilling with auger-type drill methods, such as for screwed-in 
ground anchors, would encounter difficulties penetrating through the dense basalt formation 
without percussion drilling techniques.  We recommend performing pile load testing on the 
potential solar array foundation system to evaluate its feasibility and applicability to the site 
conditions. 
Other Foundation Considerations 
We envision that some single-story buildings may be required for the project to house 
solar equipment and other appurtenances. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered 
at the project site, we believe shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings may be used 
to support the new buildings planned for the project. As an alternative, foundations for 
support of the new structures also may consist of thickened-edge slab footings. 
We envision that the building foundations will be supported on volcanic ash materials.  
Therefore, proper preparation and compaction of the foundation subgrades will be critical 
during construction.  Foundation subgrades should not be exposed to overwatering or 
inclement weather.   
In addition, due to the relatively shallow anticipated depth to basalt formation, the 
potential presence of cavities and voids should be considered in the foundation design of 
new structures.  Proof-rolling of foundation subgrades and implementation of a probing and 
grouting program of foundations should be evaluated during the design of the new buildings.

LIMITATIONS 
The preliminary analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, 
upon information obtained from the excavated test pits. Variations of the subsurface 
conditions between and beyond the field data points may occur, and the nature and extent 
of these variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then 
appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations provided herein. A 
design-level geotechnical engineering exploration should be conducted to confirm and/or 
modify the preliminary geotechnical recommendations provided herein. 
The field test pit locations indicated in this report are approximate, having been 
transferring coordinates obtained from a handheld Global Positioning System (GPS) unit on 
to a Google Earth image. The physical locations of the test pits should be considered 
accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the test pits depict 
the approximate boundaries between the soil and/or rock types and, as such, may denote 
a gradual transition. Water level data from the test pits were measured at the times shown 
on the graphic representations and/or presented in the text herein. These data have been 
reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. We did not encounter 
groundwater in our test pits at the time of our field exploration. However, it must be noted 
that fluctuation may occur due to variation rainfall, temperature, and other factors. 
This letter report has been prepared solely for the purpose of assisting our client 
(Engie North America, Inc.) in the planning and preliminary evaluation of the project only. 
Therefore, this letter report may not contain sufficient data, or the proper information, to 
serve as the basis for detailed design and preparation of construction drawings. A 
design-level geotechnical exploration will need to be conducted for the detailed project 
design. 
The owner/client should be aware that unanticipated soil and/or rock conditions are 
commonly encountered. Unforeseen subsurface conditions, such as soft deposits, hard 
layers, cavities, or perched groundwater, may occur in localized areas and may require 
additional probing or corrections in the field (which may result in construction delays) to 
attain a properly constructed project. Therefore, a sufficient contingency fund is 
recommended to accommodate these possible extra costs. 
This preliminary geotechnical engineering exploration conducted at the project site 
was not intended to investigate the potential presence of hazardous materials existing at 
the site. It should be noted that the equipment, techniques, and personnel used to conduct 
a geo-environmental exploration differ substantially from those applied in geotechnical 
engineering.
