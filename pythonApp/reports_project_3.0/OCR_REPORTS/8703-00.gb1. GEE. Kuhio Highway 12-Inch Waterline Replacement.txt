SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered about 3 to 7 inches of AC underlain by fills 
extending to depths of about 1 to 8 feet below the existing ground surface. Some fills closer 
to the stream crossing extended to depths of about 8 to possibly over 11.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface. The fills were underlain by beach deposits consisting of loose to 
medium dense sand extending to depths of about 8 to 11.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface. The beach deposits were underlain by coralline detritus consisting of very loose to 
loose silty gravel and sand extending to the maximum depth explored of about 11.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. 
We encountered groundwater in the drilled borings at depths ranging from about 
5  to 8.7 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration. It should 
be noted that the groundwater depth will vary based upon tidal fluctuations, storm surge 
conditions, rainfall, surface water runoff, seasonal precipitation, and other factors.  
We understand that the waterline will be installed using the conventional open trench 
(cut-and-cover) method. We envision that the invert depths of the new waterline will range 
from about 4 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface. Since the waterline alignment 
would likely be located within the existing roadways, we believe that temporary cut slopes 
for open-cut excavations are not practical. Therefore, it appears the trench walls would have 
to be cut near vertical, necessitating the use of shoring during construction. 
Some of the water main excavations may extend below the groundwater level; 
therefore, dewatering will be necessary in these areas. Because the project is in a developed 
area, dewatering should be conducted in such a manner that the dewatering will not cause 
areal ground subsidence, which may cause potential damage to the existing nearby 
structures and underground utilities. The contractor should consider a dewatering system 
that includes a cut-off wall and tremie concrete plug.  
Based on our field exploration, loose to medium dense sand is anticipated at the pipe 
invert depths. For the design of thrust blocks, we recommend using a lateral bearing 
pressure of up to 500 psf.  The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion 
and specific recommendations for the installation of the new waterline.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Kuhio Highway 12-Inch Waterline Replacement project in Kapaa on the 
Island of Kauai, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering 
analyses for the waterline replacement project. These recommendations are intended for 
the design of trench excavation, backfilling, thrust block, vault design, and pavement 
restoration only. In addition, general discussions on the excavation and dewatering 
requirements are included in this report for information purposes. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project is located along Kuhio Highway from Papaloa Road to just north of 
Kawaihau Road in Kapaa on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. Based on the information 
provided, the project involves the installation of a new 12-inch waterline about 
13,230 linear feet in length and appurtenances along Kuhio Highway. We anticipate that 
the waterline invert depths will range from about 4 to 5 feet below the existing ground 
surface. The new waterline will traverse three stream crossings identified as 
Waipoli Canal, Waikaea Canal, and Moikena Canal. We understand that the installation 
of the waterline across these stream crossings will be mounted on the existing stream 
crossing structures.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the 
extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene Epoch 
(more than 2-1/4 million years before present). Following the cessation of this main shield 
building phase, there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of 
the post-erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of alluvial 
sediments of the Palikea Formation. 
The majority of the Island of Kauai is covered by lavas of the Waimea Canyon 
Volcanic Series. These lavas consist of four distinct formations:  Napali, Olokele, Haupu, 
and Makaweli. These formations are comprised of thin-bedded a`a and pahoehoe flows 
to massive basalt flows that ponded in calderas and graben. 
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of 
Kauai. These rocks are generally characterized as thick flows of dense basalt extruded 
from groups of vents aligned in north-south trends in various locales. Associated with the 
vents are pyroclastic materials, which usually form low cinder cones at the vent. 
During the Pliestocene Epoch (Ice Age), there were many sea level changes 
because of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great 
continental glaciers accumulated, the level of the ocean fell since there was less water 
available to fill the oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded or melted, global 
sea levels rose because more water was available. The land mass of Kauai remained 
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. 
These glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and 
lower relative to the present sea level of Kauai. 
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of 
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations, and lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet waters 
protected by fringing reefs.

2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is located along Kuhio Highway from Papaloa Road to just north 
of Kawaihau Road in Kapaa on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. The approximate location of 
the project site is shown on the Overall Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Kuhio Highway is a two to three-lane highway consisting of asphaltic concrete (AC) 
pavement. In general, the lane configurations are generally two lanes in one direction and 
one lane in the opposite direction; one lane in each direction with the center lane as a left 
turn lane; and one lane in each direction with parking shoulders on each side. The existing 
pavement appears to be in fair condition.  
The three stream crossings are identified as Waipoli Canal, Waikaea Canal, and 
Moikena Canal. Kuhio Highway crosses over these stream crossings via bridge 
structures. At the time of our exploration, water was observed in each stream crossing. 
2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling 33 borings, designated as 
Boring Nos. 1 through 33, extending to a depth of about 11.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Overall Site Plan, 
Plate 2, and Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.7.  
Our borings generally encountered about 3 to 7 inches of AC underlain by fills 
extending to depths of about 1 to 8 feet below the existing ground surface. Some fills 
closer to the stream crossing extended to depths of about 8 to possibly over 11.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The fills consisted of dense to very dense silty gravel, 
medium stiff to very stiff silty clay and clayey silt, and very loose to medium dense sand 
with varying amounts of silt. 
Below the fills, beach deposits consisting of loose to medium dense sand were 
encountered in the borings extending to depths of about 8 to 11.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. The beach deposits were underlain by coralline detritus consisting of very 
loose to loose silty gravel and sand extending to the maximum depth explored of about 
11.5 feet below the existing ground surface.

4 to 5 feet below the existing pavement surface. However, the pipe invert will be extended 
to deeper depths where the new waterline traverses under an existing utility line or 
structure. Installation of the proposed waterline will involve excavation into the underlying 
fills and beach deposits. In general, we anticipate that the in-situ soils along the

3.2 
Trench Excavation Support 
We anticipate excavation depths of up to about 4 to 5 feet below the ground surface 
will be required for the installation of the new waterline. Deeper excavations may be 
required where the waterline traverses under an existing utility line or structure. Since the 
12-inch waterline alignment would likely be within the roadway limits, we believe that 
temporary cut slopes for open-cut excavations are not practical. Therefore, trench walls 
would have to be cut near-vertical, necessitating the use of shoring during construction. 
For shallow excavations with minor dewatering, we envision that trench shields 
may be used for the shoring of the excavated trenches. For deeper excavations that may 
involve substantial dewatering, one possible method of shoring would be to use 
interlocking sheet pile shoring. The use of a sheet pile shoring system may also serve as 
a cut-off wall to aid in the dewatering operations, which is further discussed in the 
“Dewatering” section herein. The sheet piles should be driven to a sufficient depth to 
reduce the potential for areal ground subsidence and to reduce the amount of dewatering 
within the excavations due to the relatively permeable nature of the subsoils. It should be 
noted that the excavations will likely encounter loose soils at the bottom of the excavation. 
Therefore, there is potential for bottom heave in these loose soil conditions. The

contractor should carefully evaluate the potential for the bottom heave and design the

It is important to install adequate sheeting prior to the excavation and to maintain 
it tightly against the excavation walls with proper bracing during excavation. The properly 
braced sheeting is essential to reduce appreciable lateral movements of the adjacent 
ground into the excavation, which may result in potential settlements or distress to 
adjacent structures or other improvements, such as roads or utilities. 
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable 
safety requirements. The contractor should be solely responsible for trench excavation 
and the adequacy and safety of the shoring installation. The contractor’s site 
superintendent, who should be required to continuously present on-site during excavation 
and construction works, will have the best opportunity to promptly observe changing 
conditions during construction, such as unforeseen subsoils, unexpectedly high 
groundwater table, inappropriate construction sequence or techniques, etc., which may 
affect shoring stability. 
However, it must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and 
the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation. 
Due to the complexity of the stress changes, it is difficult to accurately estimate the 
magnitude of movement. The magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship, such 
as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are installed, the subsoil 
conditions, the size of the excavation, and the rate of excavation. In addition, it should be 
noted that settlement of the existing ground may occur as a result of the vibrations 
generated during the installation of the sheet pile shoring. Therefore, the contractor 
should give special attention during the sheet pile installation process to reduce the 
potential for appreciable ground settlement. The use of an impact hammer or hydraulic 
press-in system instead of a vibratory hammer may be considered. 
It is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed properly 
and as early as practical, if necessary, and that the adjacent ground should be 
continuously monitored for cracks, dips and/or other indications of movements with 
instruments.

3.3 
Pipe Cushion 
The stress distribution against the bottom of the pipe has a significant effect on the 
load supporting capacity of the pipe. Therefore, the pipe cushion is an important design 
consideration. For pipes bearing on loose to medium dense sand, a minimum of 6 inches 
of compacted granular cushion material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation), should be provided below the pipe. Where soft and/or loose soils are 
encountered at or near the invert of the pipes, such as in the beach deposits and coralline 
detritus, a stabilization layer consisting of an additional 24 inches of open-graded gravel, 
such as No. 3B Fine gravel, wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or 
equivalent) should be provided below the cushion layer for uniform support. A typical 
trench detail is provided on Plate 4. 
The granular cushion layer should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width 
of one-fourth (¼) of the outside pipe diameter, or trench width, whichever is greater, and 
should extend 12 inches above the pipe. Before the placement of cushion material, a 
Geolabs representative should observe the excavated trench bottom to confirm that firm 
materials are exposed at the bottom of the trench or whether a stabilization layer should 
be provided below the cushion layer. 
3.4 
Trench Backfill 
It should be noted that loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the 
trench, the unit weight of the trench backfill material, and the friction resistance between 
the backfill material and the trench walls. To calculate the vertical loads on the buried 
pipes, we recommend using an average unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic foot (pcf) for 
the trench backfill material and a coefficient of friction of 0.3. Earth forces acting upon the 
pipe generally increase rapidly with the width of the trench. Therefore, the width of the 
trench should be kept to a minimum. Traffic loads on the buried pipes should also be 
considered for the pipes located in roadway areas. 
In general, the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the Water 
System Standards, State of Hawaii 2002. The first zone of backfill extending from the 
cushion material to at least 12 inches above the top of the pipes (or 12 inches above the 
groundwater level, whichever is higher) should consist of select free-draining granular

material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) to reduce the 
compaction effort required and resulting stresses on the pipe. In addition, it is critical to 
use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for the formation of voids below the 
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper

The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes (or

materials, organic material, and clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in 
maximum dimension or select granular material.  
Imported fill materials used for trench backfill, if required, should consist of select 
granular fill materials. Select granular fill materials should be well-graded from coarse to 
fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in the largest dimension and contain between 
10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have a 
laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of 
1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. Geolabs should test the 
imported fill materials for conformance with these recommendations. It should be noted 
that the fill requirements presented herein are intended as guidelines only and may be 
modified based on additional laboratory testing and field observations on the available fill 
materials conducted by Geolabs during the project construction. 
As an alternative, to facilitate the backfilling and reduce the time required for 
applying compaction effort, Controlled Low Strength Material (CLSM) may be used to 
backfill the trenches. The CLSM should be proportioned to produce a backfill material that 
is self-compacting and able to be excavated with conventional excavation equipment. The 
28-day compressive strength of CLSM should be between 50 and 150 pounds per square 
inch (psi). The CLSM should be tremied and designed to be placed underwater in the 
locations where underwater placement is required.

moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding

compaction. The upper 3 feet of backfill materials below the finished pavement grade 
should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
Aggregate base course used as part of the pavement restoration section should 
be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not

Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or 
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or ponding 
should not be allowed to compact the excavated surface on-site soils or imported fill 
materials. Precautions should be taken against the occurrence of pipeline flotation before 
and during trench backfilling due to the possible influx of large quantities of groundwater 
into the trenches. The buoyant uplift forces due to groundwater influx may lift empty or 
even partially water filled pipes. 
Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during backfilling to 
assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper 
moisture content. 
3.6 
Valve Vault Foundations 
We envision that concrete valve structures may be required for the proposed 
waterline. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend 
using an allowable bearing pressure of up to 1,500 pounds per square foot (psf) for the 
foundation design of the concrete valve structures. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live 
loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by 
seismic forces. A bedding layer consisting of a minimum of 6 inches of granular fills, such 
as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric 
(Mirafi 180N or equivalent) should be provided below the bottom slab of the valve structure.

3.7 
Thrust Blocks 
We envision that thrust blocks will be required at some locations to provide 
resistance to the thrust forces due to the pressurized pipes. The thrust force acting on the 
pipes may be resisted by passive earth pressure acting against the face of the thrust 
block. 
We anticipate that the majority of the planned inverts would be about 4 to 5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. However, some of the thrust blocks may need to be 
located at deeper depths in areas where the new waterline traverses the existing 
underground utilities. 
Based on our field exploration, loose to medium dense sand is anticipated at the 
pipe depths. Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions, we recommend using a 
lateral bearing pressure of 500 pounds per square foot (psf) for the design of thrust block 
structures. 
3.8 
Pipe Settlement and Construction Considerations 
The primary settlement of the new waterline installation is generally caused by the 
difference in unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted 
backfill material placed over the pipeline. The net increase in loading may cause settlement

Page 15

of the underlying subsurface soils below the trench invert. In addition, we anticipate that new 
waterline in some areas may be underlain by loose beach deposits and coralline detritus. 
For the new waterline, the total settlement on the order of about 1 inch may be anticipated. 
The settlement estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good 
workmanship will be consistently practiced by the contractor during waterline installation 
operations. Based on our knowledge and experience with similar loose soil conditions, 
significant settlement of the pipes may be attributed to the construction methodology and 
equipment used by the contractor. Therefore, special attention should be given to the 
installation procedures for the proposed waterline (especially in loose soil conditions) to 
reduce the potential for settlement distress in the future. 
As indicated above, the waterline underlain by loose soils should be supported by 
an approximately 24-inch-thick stabilization layer consisting of free-draining materials 
wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric. The non-woven filter fabric provides separation 
between the open-graded aggregates and the surrounding loose soils. It should be 
emphasized that the filter fabric should be pulled taut and relatively free of wrinkles prior 
to placement of the open-graded aggregates. In general, a minimum overlap of 2 feet 
should be provided between the ends of each roll of the filter fabric placed along the pipe 
alignment under relatively dry conditions. If the filter fabric is not properly overlapped, it 
is possible that the coarse aggregates placed would migrate through the openings in the 
filter fabric and mix with the underlying soft and/or loose soils. This may result in 
settlement of the pipes due to loss of cushion materials into the soft and/or loose soils. 
Considering the depth of the excavations and groundwater, some of the 
stabilization and bedding layer installation below the pipe invert may be performed in wet 
conditions. Therefore, we envision that it would be difficult to observe the fabric 
installation process below the groundwater level. In addition, we believe that it would be 
difficult for the contractor to assure that the proper minimum fabric overlap is maintained. 
Therefore, we believe that if the placement of the filter fabric in wet conditions is 
necessary, the seams of the fabric should be sewn (in lieu of overlapping) to provide a 
higher degree of assurance that gaps will not occur in the filter fabric. As a result, we 
recommend incorporating sewing the seams of the filter fabric into the construction

documents to reduce the potential for large-scale settlements of the pipes if the 
stabilization layer is installed in wet conditions. 
After the 24-inch-thick stabilization layer and pipe bedding are installed, we 
recommend removing the trench shoring to a level above the top of the stabilization layer 
and pipe bedding before the waterline is placed, if feasible. This will allow the stabilization 
layer and pipe bedding to expand out to contact the sidewalls of the trench. Additional 
pipe bedding material can then be placed to achieve the design pipe invert elevations. 
Based on our experience, pulling the trench shoring after installation of the stabilization 
layer, pipe bedding, and waterline may cause settlement of the waterline due to the 
flowing of the stabilization layer and pipe bedding material into the gap between the trench 
shoring and the trench sidewalls. 
3.9 
Dewatering 
Dewatering of excavations will be necessary where the existing groundwater level 
is above the bottom of the proposed excavation. Groundwater was encountered in the 
borings drilled at depths ranging from about 5 to 8.7 feet below the existing ground surface 
during our field exploration. Based on the groundwater levels anticipated and the 
permeable nature of the granular soils to be encountered during the excavations, 
dewatering will be required at the deeper excavations planned along the project 
construction. Therefore, dewatering provisions will need to be included in the contract 
documents for the proposed construction.  
It is anticipated that the granular soils encountered are relatively permeable. 
Therefore, dewatering of the excavation may involve moderate to large volumes of water. 
Dewatering by means of a well point system along the outside of the excavations is not 
recommended. The resultant areal depression of the natural groundwater table could 
induce consolidation of the loose subsoils resulting in potential ground settlements, which 
could affect the existing structures and utility lines. The potential impact of the dewatering 
system selected on depressing the natural groundwater table must be carefully evaluated 
by the contractor prior to dewatering. 
The contractor is responsible for dewatering for construction. The contractor 
should select equipment and methods of dewatering, and he/she should be aware that

 Dewatering Method 
Dewatering for construction is the contractor’s responsibility. The selection of 
equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor, and he/she 
should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during 
construction depending on the conditions encountered. The dewatering method 
selected should have minimal impact on the groundwater level surrounding the 
proposed excavation. The underlying soils at the project site may be moderately 
permeable and are capable of transmitting moderate to large quantities of water.  
It is our opinion that the definition of “Dewatering” in the contract documents should

conducted in such a manner that the dewatering will not cause areal ground 
subsidence, which may cause potential damage to the existing nearby structures and 
underground utilities. Therefore, consideration should be given to a dewatering 
system that includes a cut-off wall and tremie concrete plug.

c.

Where encountered at the bottom of excavations, permeable granular subsoils may 
be susceptible to piping and "quick" conditions. The dewatering operations should be 
carried out without creating a "quick" condition or softening at the excavation 
bottoms. Therefore, the project dewatering operations should be performed without 
pumping out soil fines (pumping clear water only) and should be coordinated with the 
shoring installation so that the excavation stability is not adversely affected. 
Excessive pumping, which removes soil fines, may result in "blowing" or heaving of 
the excavation bottom or sides. 
Groundwater drawdown outside the excavation will cause additional settlement 
resulting from the consolidation of the loose compressible soils. Therefore, the use 
of a deep well system outside the excavations to draw down the groundwater level 
should not be allowed. 
Special caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering excavations connected to 
existing utility trenches. If this occurs, the granular bedding and/or backfill in the 
existing utility trenches could act as subdrains and cause significant areal 
groundwater drawdown. Significant areal groundwater drawdown would result in 
ground settlements and potential damage to utility lines and/or other existing adjacent 
structures. 
For underground construction in a developed area, we believe that the importance of 
construction monitoring, including field observation and engineering supervision, 
should be emphasized. The extent of construction monitoring may vary with 
individual contractors. However, as a minimum, a pre-construction damage survey 
of existing buildings and structures within 100 feet of the proposed excavation and 
dewatering site should be conducted by the contractor, including photographs and 
crack surveys, prior to the commencement of construction.  Instrumentations such 
as settlement points and inclinometers should be considered as deemed necessary.

3.11 Corrosion Potential 
Seventeen sets of laboratory corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum 
Resistivity (ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), 
SECTION 3. DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS  
W.O. 8703-00

were performed by our laboratory and Eurofins TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. to evaluate 
the corrosivity of the on-site soils on selected soil samples obtained from our field 
exploration.  The test results are summarized on Plate B-29 of Appendix B.  
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower 
the resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure will 
have. Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to the 
corrosion of metallic objects.  Results of the resistivity testing indicate that the on-site soils 
have resistivity values of about 5,300 to 15,000 ohm-cm between Boring Nos. 1 and 23. 
The on-site soils encountered between Boring Nos. 23 and 33 have resistivity values of 
about 790 to 4100 ohm-cm.   
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete structures is 
dependent on the pH and the chloride and sulfate contents found in the soil. The pH 
values based on the test results range from about 8.21 to 8.93. Based on the chloride 
and sulfate content tests performed on the on-site soils, the tested chloride content values 
range from not detected (ND) within reporting limits to about 550 mg/kg and the tested 
sulfate content values range from ND within reporting limits to 180 mg/kg. 
Based on the results of the corrosivity tests, the on-site near-surface soils 
encountered between Boring Nos. 1 and 23 may be considered as negligibly to mildly 
corrosive (Corrosion Category B) based on the Board of Water Supply, City & County of 
Honolulu, Water System External Corrosion Control Standards dated 2021. The on-site 
near-surface soils encountered between Boring Nos. 23 and 33 may be considered as 
moderately to severely corrosive (Corrosion Category A). 
Based on the results of the laboratory corrosivity tests, it may be appropriate to 
consult with a professional corrosion engineer to review the test results and provide 
detailed recommendations for corrosion protection.

A P P E N D I X   A 
Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling 33 borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 33, to a depth of about 11.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Overall Site Plan, Plate 2, and 
Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.7. We used a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers to advance the borings. 
Our field engineer and/or geologists classified the materials encountered in the 
borings by visual and textural examination in the field in general accordance with 
ASTM D2488, Standard Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored 
the drilling operations on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were 
further reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general 
accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering 
Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-33. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used.  The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Four Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations of the test 
results are provided on Plate B-1. 
Twenty-Six Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on 
selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in 
soil classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plates B-2 through B-7. 
Eleven Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-8 through B-18. 
Eight laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-19 through B-26. 
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557 A) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships. The test results are presented on Plates B-27 and B-28. 
Seventeen sets of Corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum 
Resistivity (ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0) and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0) 
tests were performed by our laboratory and Eurofins TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on 
selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration. Results of the corrosivity tests 
are presented on Plate B-29.
