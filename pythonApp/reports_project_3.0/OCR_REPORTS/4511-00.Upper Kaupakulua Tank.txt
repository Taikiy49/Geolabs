performed for the Upper Kaupakulua Tank project in the Makawao area on the Island of

SECTION 4 0 REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Maui is comprised of two major volcanoes the older West Maui
Tertiary Epoch and the more recent East Maui also known as Haleakala Pleistocene
Epoch The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow gently sloping plain located between these two
volcanoes The project site is located near the northern end of the island and on the lower
northern slope of the East Maui Volcano Haleakala
Haleakala was generally built in three distinguishable series of lava flows The
lower unit the Honomanu Volcanic Series late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Epoch
consists of thinly bedded basaltic pahoehoe and a a lava These lava tend to be very
permeable and carry basal water Overlying this unit is the Kula Volcanic Series early
Pleistocene Epoch composed primarily of thicker alkalic a a flows which may contain
many inter stratified thin ash soil layers A long quiescent period followed during which
time canyons were carved in the volcano although a few eruptions may have occurred
during this erosion interval in addition to erosion due to eustatic changes higher and lower
stands of the sea level At the end of the rest period copious flows erupted along the
southwest and east rifts This is the third and upper rock unit known as the Hana Volcanic
Series Pleistocene Epoch The lava range in composition from ultra basic olivine
porphyries to non porphyritic alkalic basalts
The project site is located in an area associated with the Pauwela Haiku
association This association consists of well drained fine textured soils on low uplands
on the north facing slopes of East Maui These soils are gently sloping to moderately
steep and developed in material weathered from basic igneous rock

boulder layers and hard basalt rock ledges extending to the maximum depth explored of
approximately 75 4 feet below the existing ground surface Based on our evaluation of the
subsurface conditions with respect to the proposed water tank construction the primary
geotechnical considerations for the proposed water tank project include the following
Foundation support for the proposed water tank
Estimated foundation settlement of the proposed water tank
Site preparation and grading
Pavement design

during our field exploration we believe that a shallow foundation system consisting of
spread and or continuous footings may be used to support the proposed water tank

beyond the outside edges of the perimeter ring footing a minimum of 3 feet A layer of

7 1 Water Tank Foundations
Based on the structural loads provided and the subsurface conditions encountered
at the project site we believe that shallow foundations consisting of spread and or
continuous footings may be used for support of the proposed new water tank structure
Our field exploration indicated that soft soil pockets may be expected at or near the
foundation subgrade level of the proposed water tank To provide more uniform support
for the water tank structure we recommend that the subsurface materials below the
bottom of the tank floor and foundations be over excavated a minimum of 5 feet and be
replaced with compacted structural fill materials The over excavation for the
compacted structural fill should extend beyond the outside edges of the perimeter ring
footing a minimum of 3 feet The structural fill materials should consist of imported
non expansive select granular materials compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 A layer of structural geogrid such
as or equivalent should be provided at about the mid depth of the
structural fill to improve the foundation bearing support performance

For design of foundations bearing on the 5 foot thick structural fill placed over the
in situ saprolite soils we recommend that an allowable bearing pressure of up to
3 000 pounds per square foot psf be used This bearing value is for dead plus live
loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused
by wind or seismic forces For design of the tank floor slab a modulus of subgrade
reaction of 200 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection pci may be used for the
compacted structural fill materials
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 40 may be used for foundations bearing on the 5 foot thick structural fill
materials Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This
assumes that the soils around footings are well compacted The passive pressure should
be reduced for foundations located on slopes Unless covered by pavements or slabs the
passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grades Footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping ground
should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal set back distance of
8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the slope face
Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded
below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility
trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid
surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce
the potential for foundation settlement
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with the

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Based on our field exploration and laboratory tests results it appears that the in situ
soils exist in a relatively moist state with high moisture contents Therefore drying
or aeration of the excavated materials may be necessary prior to their use as
general fill materials Aeration to lower the soil moisture and more compaction
effort along with of thinner fill lifts to achieve the specified compaction may be
necessary and would generally reduce the rate of fill placement for this project
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or
other types of acceptable compaction equipment Water tamping jetting or
ponding should not be allowed to compact the on site clayey soils General fill
materials should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction

7 4 Water Lines
We understand that new 12 inch ductile iron influent and effluent water mains will
be installed for the water tank development For water lines located outside the water
tank limits a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 1 inch minus granular fill
materials or base course is recommended below the pipes The 1 inch minus granular
fill materials should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches
above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of well graded materials less than
3 inches in maximum dimension structural fill material The backfill should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in about 12 inch level loose
lifts and mechanically compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Where
trenches are located in paved areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the
pavement grade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
For water lines located below the footprint of the water tank the water line trenches
should be backfilled with concrete or lean concrete up to 12 inches below the tank slab
subgrade The remaining 12 inches of the water line trench backfill should consist of
structural fill material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction The
concrete around the water lines below the water tank structure should be placed neat
against the excavation In addition the concrete backfill around the water line should
extend laterally beyond the perimeter of the tank a minimum of 3 feet
Since isolated boulder layers and hard basalt rock ledges were encountered in
some of the borings drilled at the project site trench work for the water line installation
may involve excavation in these boulder layers and hard rock ledges and will require
some use of the hoeram or chipping Care must be exercised by the contractor to avoid
over ripping especially within the water tank footprint which would disrupt the structure
of the rock ledge formation resulting in a potential loss of bearing strength for foundations
in its vicinity As previously indicated the trench excavations within the tank structure
footprint should be backfilled with concrete or lean concrete placed neat against the

7 5 Corrosion Protection for Water Lines
Four sets of pH and minimum resistivity tests were performed to evaluate the
corrosivity of the on site clayey soils generally encountered at the subject site The
results of these tests are presented on Plate B 1 of Appendix B The minimum
resistivity values of the near surface soil samples tested ranged from 8 200 to
36 000 ohm cm which correspond to Corrosion Ratings of 4 to 5 moderately to mildly
corrosive based on the guidelines provided by the City County of Honolulu Board of
Water Supply Therefore we recommend that all metallic substructures such as ductile
iron water lines in contact with the soils be properly designed to be protected against
potential corrosion
7 6 Drainage
The finished grades outside the water tank structure should be sloped to shed
water away from foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive
landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be
properly backfilled against the wails or slab footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce water infiltration In addition drainage swales should be provided as
soon as possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away from slabs
and foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
eight borings extending to depths ranging from about 11 5 to 75 4 feet below the existing
ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 and 3 The
borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight
augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered is presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 8
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and
drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket
penetrometer tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
W O Page A 1
Appendix A continued
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
Very Poor 0 25
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100
The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its
relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense basalt with a high RQD
would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous methods of
excavation

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Four sets of pH ASTM G 51 and minimum resistivity ASTM G 57 tests were
performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration The results of
these tests are summarized on Plate B 1
Six one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results
of these tests are summarized on Plate B 2
One direct shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected soil sample
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested Results of the
direct shear test are presented on Plate B 3
Two laboratory California Bearing Ration CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were
performed on the near surface soil to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the
soil Results of the tests are presented on Plates B 4 and B 5
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on the
near surface soils Results of the test are presented on Plates B 6 and B 7
