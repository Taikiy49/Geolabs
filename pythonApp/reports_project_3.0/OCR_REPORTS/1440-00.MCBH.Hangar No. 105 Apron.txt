This report presents the results of our soil and foundation
investigation performed for the subject project The approximate
location of the project site and its vicinity is shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
Our work was performed in general accordance with our proposal of
July 17th
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of the investigation was to attempt to locate possible
voids or weakened zones and to evaluate the suitability of the near
surface soils for Taxiway B and Hangar No 105 Apron
W O 1440 00 Page 2
The subsurface soils may have been eroded or weakened from breaks
in a water line located near Hangar No 105 Our investigation consisted
of the following
1 Two and one half days field investigation using a
Ground Penetrating Radar GPR System to obtain profiles of the
subsurface interfaces
2 two days of test boring exploration drilling 17 shallow
borings using a standard split spoon SPT sampler to check
seventeen 17 of the thirty 30 suspected areas locations for
voids or weak areas that were indicated from the initial GPR
survey as having some anomalies and
5 pressure grouting at selected depths in three boring locations
to demonstrate the feasibility of strengthening the soils below
the pavements using a Zone Grouting method
The results of our field exploration using the GPR System borings
and grouting which we used in the engineering analysis to formulate our
conclusions and recommendations are presented in the appendices of this
report
The approximate locations of the GPR survey lines and borings are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
SITE DESCRIPTION
The site is a fairly level paved area located between Hangar No
105 and Taxiway B at Kaneohe Marine Corps Air Station It roughly
covers an area of about 380 by 250 feet in plan ft Most
of the site is A C pavement except for a narrow 25 foot wide by 200 foot
long strip of concrete pavement located at Taxiway B

Damaged Areas
Generally the only visible evidence of pavement damage at the
surface is at the pipe break and near the 12 inch water line alignment
alongside the roadway from Hangar No 105 to where it crosses Taxiway B
and some isolated small areas near Boring No 29 as shown on the Site
Plan
The damage noted to the pavement appears as small bulges or
ruptures where the water apparently flowed along the course of least
resistance until it reached the bottom of the A C pavement and ruptured
the surface where the paving is thin or weakened
Soil Conditions
Below 3 to 5 inches of A C pavement about 4 5 feet of medium to
dense tan silty sand and coral gravel underlain in some areas by soft or
loose sandy silt from about 5 to 7 foot depth or near the water level A
very loose zone or void was also noted in Boring No A from about 5 to
7 5 foot depth
Groundwater
Groundwater was encountered in the deeper borings The measured
water levels below the drilled borings are shown on the boring logs In
general the groundwater levels appear to be only about 5 to 6 feet below
the existing grades and may fluctuate due to tidal fluctuations

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The results of the first phase of our contract utilizing a SIR
Ground Penetrating Radar GPR System was submitted previously in the
form of a site map that depicted our consulting geophysicist s
interpretation of suspected anomalous zones pipes and suspected voids
Also we marked on that map approximately 30 locations where we proposed
to investigate the GPR anomalies by drilling shallow borings

The second phase involved the drilling of seventeen 17 borings
varying in depths from 3 to 9 feet and pressure grouting at three areas
along the water line The borings did not encounter any large voids at
16 of the locations drilled but at Boring A there does appear to be a
very soft zone or void at a depth of about 3 5 to 7 5 feet This boring
is near the 12 inch water line trench and approximately 75 feet from the
pipe failure
Following the drilling we performed some injection grouting
utilizing the Manchette Tube System at Boring Nos A C and E
These brief injection tests indicate that the underlying soils are
permeable and therefore a cement slurry or cement sand grout may have
good penetration We believe compaction grouting whereby the grout is
used to compress the soils into a dense mass thereby reducing its
compressibility could be used
Based on our evaluation of the Ground Penetrating Radar data soil
conditions and trial grouting attempts it is our opinion that compaction
grouting should be continued at the hole locations previously drilled and
also at those boring locations that have not been completed These
locations are indicated on the Site Plan Plate 2
