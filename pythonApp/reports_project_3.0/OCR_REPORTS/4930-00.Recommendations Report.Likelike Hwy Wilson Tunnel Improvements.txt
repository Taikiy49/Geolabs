1 INTRODUCTION
1 1 Purpose Scope
The Wilson Tunnel consists of two tunnels along Route No 63 Likelike
Highway on the Island of Oahu Hawaii This report summarizes recommendations
for improvements to leakage problems and structural repairs for both bores of the
Wilson Tunnel These recommendations are intended to serve as the basis for the
_ final design of an improvements construction contract The limits for the planned
improvements covered in this report begin at the Kalihi Portal and extends
approximately 1 200 feet east into both tunnels This report was prepared by
Geolabs Inc of Honolulu and Jacobs Associates of San Francisco under
subcontract to R M Towill Corporation prime engineer for the project The Wilson
Tunnel Improvements Project is being implemented by the State of Hawaii
Department of Transportation Highways Division
1 2 Relation to Previous Report
This report builds upon the data presented in the Likelike Highway Wilson
Tunnel Improvements Leak and Crack Remediation Inspection Report dated
This preceding report is referred to herein as the Inspection
Report
1 3 Remediation Criteria
Remediation efforts as defined by the scope of work for this report fall into
two categories 1 structural defects and 2 leakage The recommendations given in
this report adhere to the following criteria
1 Repair defects that impact the structural integrity of the tunnels
2 Repair defects to arrest progressive deterioration that might ultimately
affect structural integrity

2 REMEDIATION OPTIONS
2 1 Structural
In an overall sense the structural defects observed during the inspection of
the tunnels are only minor in nature consisting of cracks a few areas or joints with
spalls sometimes with exposed reinforcing bars For perspective in regards to the
structural defects the tunnel linings have substantial thickness compared to other
highway tunnels ranging from 27 3 to 4 feet thick
Dry cracks can be repaired with an epoxy or polyurethane resin It is
possible however that cracks experiencing continued movement will re open or
form new crack s adjacent to the repaired crack if sealed with an epoxy resin
Active cracks should be sealed with a flexible material such as polyurethane to
allow movement In cases of significant continued movement cracks should be cut
wider turned into an expansion joint and sealed with polyurethane see discussion
in Section 2 2
Small spalls can be repaired with an epoxy mortar while for larger spalls a
plasticized concrete mix or shotcrete can be used Preparation of the spall by
cleaning the area to remove all loose debris to expose fresh concrete and trimming
the edges of the spall to avoid feathering is critical to ensure a good repair All loose
rust must be removed and exposed reinforcing bars thoroughly cleaned before
filling If the gross section loss exceeds 15 corroded reinforcing bars should be
replaced note however that spalls of this magnitude were not observed during the
inspection Some epoxy mortars require priming of the cleaned reinforcement prior
to placement of the mortar

As noted in the Inspection Report leakage is primarily through circumferential
shrinkage cracks and construction joints The source of the water appears to be
rainfall from the surface that is infiltrating through the soil and rock in an unfavorable
situation with exacerbated recharge from the old sinkholes and poor surface
drainage It is also possible that voids behind the tunnel lining at the crown could
form reservoirs and conduits for water to collect and flow around and along the
lining Such voids are common in cast in place tunnel linings where they have not
been adequately backfilled by contact grouting Refer to the inspection report for a
discussion of what contact grouting may or may not have been conducted for the
tunnels
lt is not considered economically practical to reduce soil and rock
permeabilities in an attempt to reduce leakage into the tunnels This would require
consolidation and cut off grouting on a massive scale from the surface or from within
the tunnel However poor drainage conditions are present at the ground surface
above the tunnels where leakage is most severe Although improvements to the
surface drainage to route surface flows away from the tunnels might reduce leakage
in the tunnel these types of improvements are likely not practical to implement on a
large scale and near term basis given that the area is located within a conservation
district
To reduce leakage into the tunnels it will be necessary to either seal the
cracks and construction joints in the tunnel lining or collect water leaking through
cracks and route the water into appropriate discharge pipes

Leak Sealing
Many different types of materials are available for sealing leaking cracks
each having different applications costs and limitations see Table 1 Sealant
options can generally be divided into two categories One option is to use a
continuous membrane that forms a barrier to water flow The second option consists
of injecting grout or resin to seal water pathways such as cracks
Membranes used to seal tunnels post construction are typically applied as
spray The two most commonly used spray on membranes in underground
applications are Liquid Boot by LBI Technologies a bituminous product and
Masterseal DR1 and 340F by Master Builders Technologies a polymer Other
post construction membranes are applied by trowel or brush such as Krystol T1 and
T2 but are less common in tunnel applications Surface cleaning is required for any
of these membranes prior to application and most products have limited application
on damp or saturated surfaces
Grouts used for sealing cracks can be made up of very fine particles such as
cement or microfine cement held in suspension during injection or by chemicals in
solution The primary characteristics used in selecting a grout for waterproofing are
its viscosity setting time strength permeability durability toxicity flammability and
cost
The types of cement grouts for leak sealing are summarized as follows
1 Sand Cement Grouts Commonly used to fill large voids or for contact
grouting of a concrete lining This type of grouting is not considered
applicable for the Wilson Tunnel as the leakage mechanism is most
commonly through cracks with less than a 0 03 inch aperture
2 Neat Cement Grouts Commonly used to seal joints in rock or cracks
in concrete with a significant aperture Requires sufficient confinement
of the grout until the grout has set otherwise the grout will flow
uncontrollably with incomplete results This type of grouting is not
considered applicable for the Wilson Tunnel as the leakage

Microfine Cement Grouts Used to seal joints in rock or cracks in
concrete Requires sufficient confinement of the grout until the grout
has set This type of grouting is technically feasible for the Wilson
Tunnel but is probably not as effective as other methods
Cement Chemical Grouts These types of grouts use cement in
combination with an epoxy or other chemical They can be very
effective at sealing leaks but often require a great deal of joint
preparation before application In addition cement chemical grouts are
not particularly effective on cracks subject to continued expansion as
the material is relatively brittle Also most products are rated for only
limited hydrostatic pressures Some products require application of
two or more components to effectively seal the crack Occasionally in
the area surrounding a crack that is sealed new cracks that were dry
will start to produce water These cracks must then be grouted if
inflows are unacceptable It is difficult to predict quantities of
secondary leaks as a result of chasing water inflows An example of a
cement chemical grout is by Kryton
Chemical grouts generally have lower viscosity than cement grouts In the
fluid state chemical grouts can penetrate into very fine cracks where cement grouts
cannot enter They are generally more costly than cement based grouts Types of
chemical grouts for leak sealing are summarized as follows
Silicate Based Grouts These grouts consist of sodium silicate gel and
a reactant usually calcium chloride Of the chemical grouts silicates
are usually the least costly and least toxic although they are a severe
skin irritant Silicate grouts have good durability but do suffer from
leaching and syneresis an exudation of water from the gel producing
shrinkage in the gel
Plant Product Based_Grouts Usually petrochemical by products
these grouts include lignin based grouts Most plant product based
grouts have fallen out of favor as acrylamides and polyurethane grouts
have become more popular in underground applications

into tighter cracks compared to microfine cement They usually consist

be controlled by varying the amount of catalyst used Viscosity of

Leak Management
In combination with or in lieu of crack sealing a leak management system
can be implemented This will entail collecting water leaking through cracks and
directing it to a discharge line In underground terminology this method is referred to
as panning Panning materials in common use consist of metal or synthetic
sheets Panning can take the form of detailed sheet metal work installed with
hangers paying close attention to drainage and water tightness of joints In the
past the sheet metal work was copper and done by tradesman that normally custom
fabricated drains and gutters for building roofs In more modern application the
panning consists of synthetic sheets that are secured to the surface to be panned
usually rock shotcrete or concrete by masonry nails The masonry nail
Page 9
State of Hawaii Department of Transportation
Wilson Tunnel Improvements Recommendations Report
penetrations are sealed with caulk and the edges of the sheets are sealed with
batten strips and caulk
As applied to the leaks in the Wilson Tunnel plenums panning would be
installed as continuous sheets or as strips targeting cracks that are leaking or could
potentially leak The intent would be to provide a durable maintainable system to
channel leakage to appropriate discharge points The top and side edges of the
panning would be sealed with a batten strip and caulk as noted and the bottom
edge would terminate near the intersection of the plenum arch and the plenum slab
see Plate 1 This bottom edge would be left unsealed for discharge onto the
plenum slab Water would then either run along the plenum slab in a specially built
trough or into a conveyance pipe For the trough option gutters would have to be
built up to contain water flow and the plenum slab in the trough would have to be
thoroughly sealed the intersection of the plenum slab and the plenum arch is
currently an area with cracking and leakage problems This concept of a trough in
the plenum corner was attempted in the past Contract 5 76 but was not
systematic
Water traveling in a trough or pipe would ultimately flow into drains through
the plenum slab on through pipes mounted on or in the tunnel wails similar to the
drains installed in Contract 5 76 see description in the Inspection Report Water
would be carried by the pipes down to gutter level for discharge into the roadway
drain inlets
A significant limitation to installing a panning system on the plenum arch is
the numerous conduits mounted on the arch n some cases it is possible to install
panning around a leaking crack between conduit mounting rods and brackets In
other cases conduit mounting rods and brackets will interfere with panning
installation such that it may not be possible to cover all of the leaking crack

All of the significant spalled areas listed in Tables 2 and 3 should be

3 2 Drainage
It appears that the existing ground surface topographic conditions may
contribute to the convergence of surface runoff and potential ponding of water above
the tunnels between the Kalihi tunnel portals and the ventilation building At this
location the tunnels have generally thinner overburden composed of alluvial and
colluvial soils found in this region Thus it is believed that surface water infiltration
from the myriad of stream channels and drainage ditches in the area may be
passing through the alluvial and colluvial deposits to reach the tunnel structures
The position of the access road embankment may be contributing to the potential
ponding of surface runoff within low gradient topography overlying the tunnel
alignment A drainage study is recommended to evaluate the stream flow and the
runoff characteristics of the area This study would also make a more detailed
evaluation of the 1954 sinkholes in relation to infiltration of surface water into the
ground and eventually the tunnel Improvements may be needed to facilitate the

the transmittal of surface water through the area Although it would be impractical to

diversion channels or drains should be considered to reduce surface water
infiltration in the flatter topographic areas located above the tunnels

3 3 Roadway Drainage
As noted in the Inspection Report most of the current roadway elevation is up
to 1 inch below the gutter level As a result of this condition a majority of the water
leaking from the plenum is forced to pond and or run along the roadway and
prevented from entering the drain inlets located along the gutters The ponding
and or running of water along the roadway not only pose an aesthetic problem but
also may increase the potential for vehicular skidding on the inclined roadway
Therefore surface drainage improvements to the roadways are recommended Two
methods of improving the surface drainage of the roadways were evaluated for
feasibility and cost effectiveness The two methods that were evaluated were
construction of new PCC pavements and construction of a thin bonded PCC overlay
over the existing PCC pavements

Based on the evaluation the construction of new PCC pavements to match
the existing gutter level would be a more feasible and cost effective way to improve
the surface drainage of the roadways The existing pavements should be
demolished and removed to expose the existing base course and ballast materials
The existing base course and ballast materials should remain in place and the new
pavements should be constructed on top of the existing base course and ballast
materials Based on the available plans the existing pavements consist of 8 inches
of PCC pavements underlain by 4 inches of crushed rock base over 8 inches of
ballast materials

50 years Because the ADT for Years 2003 and 2053 were not provided the ADT

3 4 Kalihi Portal Cut Slope
During the initial site visit to the Wilson Tunnel surface water was observed
to be collecting near the toe of the cut slope adjacent to the Kalihi Portal of the Kalihi
Tunnel Therefore a site reconnaissance was performed to determine the possible
sources of the surface water that was collecting near the toe of the cut slope
Details of the site reconnaissance of the cut slope are presented in the Inspection
Report The observations during site reconnaissance indicate that the surface water

collecting near the toe of the cut slope is due to seepage water from the face and the
toe of the cut slope
To reduce the amount of seepage water originating from the toe of the cut
slope and to reduce the potential instability of the cut slope a subsurface drainage
system should be provided near the toe of the cut slope The subsurface drainage
system should consist of a 12 inch wide zone of permeable material such as No 3B
Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation immediately around a 4 inch diameter
perforated pipe perforations down discharging to an appropriate drainage inlet

slope A typical detail of the subsurface drainage system located along the toe of
the cut slope is shown on Plate 3
The seepage water originating from the face of the cut slope and collecting
along the existing ground surface should be diverted into the nearby drainage inlets
The sidewalk shoulder area adjacent to the cut slope had an overgrowth of
vegetation and an accumulation of debris In addition the inlet to the catch basin
located at approximately Station 241 39 was obstructed with debris and vegetation

of the drainage sidewalk shoulder area should be performed to reduce the amount of
debris and vegetation accumulating within the area

3 5 Maintenance
It is recommended that the current maintenance procedures be augmented
with a scheduled maintenance regimen that includes the following
1 Cleaning of tunnel walls once a year with truck mounted power
brushes
2 Cleaning of roadway drain inlets and laterals once a year
3 Maintenance of the plenum panning system to seal leaks around the
edges of the panning replace damaged panning sheets clean or
repair the trough in the plenum corners and clean or repair drain pipes
in the tunnel walls and safety walks
4 Inspection of tunnels similar in scope to that documented in the
Inspection Report every 2 to 5 years as recommended by Federal
Highway guidelines 2002 Such inspections should also include the
roadway drain system
