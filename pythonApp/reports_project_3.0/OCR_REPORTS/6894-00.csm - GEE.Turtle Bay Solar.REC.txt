SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration at the Wastewater Treatment Plant site generally encountered 
very stiff to hard silty clay soils with a significant content of boulders and cobbles.  The 
Agricultural Irrigation and Golf Maintenance sites generally encountered a relatively thin 
surface layer of stiff to hard silty clay over dense to very dense coralline deposits at shallow 
depths. Groundwater was encountered in the test pits on the dates and at the times noted 
in the Logs of Test Pits. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, cast-in-place concrete drilled 
piers may be used to support the solar panel structures.  The drilled piers are designed to 
be embedded into the stiff clayey soils or coral encountered in the test pits.  Some cobbles 
and boulders and hard coral should be expected given the geologic conditions at the site.  
Therefore, the drilling contractor will need to provide drilling equipment (and coring tools) 
that will be capable of drilling through the cobbles, boulders and coral.  
For rigid shafts or piles, the lateral resistance due to passive earth pressure may be 
estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pcf.  For flexible shafts or piles, a 
coefficient of lateral subgrade reaction of 200 pci of deflection may be used.  In general, the 
drilled piers should have a minimum center-to center spacing of 8.0 times the shaft/pile 
diameter to avoid reduction in the lateral load capacity of the shaft/pile. 
Shallow spread and/or continuous footings may be used to support the relatively 
light structures planned at the site.  We recommend using an allowable bearing pressure of 
up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) to design shallow foundations bearing on the in-
situ soils. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third 
(1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
The text of this report should be referred to for more detailed discussions and 
specific design recommendations.

1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project involves the design and construction of three photo-voltaic solar 
electric generating systems within the Turtle Bay Resort complex in the Kahuku area on 
the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  Based on information provided to us, the solar facilities will 
be comprised of three separate sites, as follows: 
Turtle Bay Wastewater Treatment Plant site 
Agricultural Irrigation Well And Pump site 
Fazio Golf Course maintenance site. 
Our geotechnical engineering exploration focused on the subsurface ground 
conditions for foundation support of the solar panels.  A total of 12 test pits were 
excavated and sampled at the three sites to depths ranging between 1.6 and 10.0 feet 
below the existing ground surface (BGS) using a Case 580 Extendahoe combination 
backhoe-loader.  Soil samples were collected as grab samples from selected locations 
at the sites.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu is comprised largely of the weathered remnants of the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of 
the western third of the island while the younger Koolau Volcano forms the majority of 
the eastern two-thirds of the island.  It is believed that Waianae became extinct while 
Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in 
central Oahu. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation.  As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to 
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea.  
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that 
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet waters 
protected by fringing reefs.  The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in 
the sediments and reef deposits.  Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous 
materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and 
induration of the calcareous reef materials.  
2.2 
Project Site Geology 
The project site lies on the Kahuku Coastal Plain and the pediment of the 
northern end of the Koolau Mountain Range. The project area is generally classified 
from a geological standpoint as ‘Qa’ for Quaternary Alluvium (Sinton, et.al.) which 
consists of alluvial soils deposited since the Pleistocene Epoch.  Experience at the 
Turtle Bay Resort complex indicates that this alluvial material forms a thin mantle over 
residuum from Koolau Basalt at the southern side of the resort and over coralline and 
dune sand deposits to the north of Kamehameha Highway.

2.3 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project sites are located at separate portions of the Turtle Bay Resort 
property.  The following provide brief descriptions of each location. 
2.3.1 Turtle Bay Wastewater Treatment Site 
The treatment plant site is situated to the south of Kamehameha Highway across 
from the entrance to Kuilima Drive.  The plant consists of four aeration ponds that 
were constructed at the base of the northern end of the Koolau Range.  Archival 
records indicate that the southern portion of the plant was graded by cutting into the 
lower slopes to generate fill material for the northern portion of the site.  The 
proposed solar system is situated on the north facing embankment slope of the 
treatment plant facility with ground surface elevations ranging from about +10 to 
about +20 feet above Mean Sea Level (MSL).  This slope area is open lawn space 
covered with grass. 
2.3.2 Fazio Golf Course Maintenance Site 
This site is situated in the west central portion of the resort complex to the north of 
the existing Fazio Golf Course maintenance building, between the building and a 
golf course water feature.  The site is a level open lawn area with a ground surface 
elevation on the order of about +10 feet MSL.  
2.3.3 Agricultural Irrigation Well and Pump Site 
The pump house site is situated to the north of Kamehameha Highway in the 
eastern portion of the resort complex.  This site was originally developed as a 
potable water well site by the now defunct Kahuku Plantation.  Until recently, the site 
was heavily overgrown with haole koa (leucaena sp.) and other subtropical 
vegetation.  It was cleared shortly before our exploration. 
At present, the site is occupied by an old chlorinator house, an abandoned concrete 
slab and well pump equipment enclosed by a chain link fence.  The site is generally 
level with a ground surface elevation on the order of about + 5 to 10 feet MSL.

2.4 
Subsurface Conditions  
Based on our field exploration, the subsurface conditions are unique for each of 
the three sites.  The following provide a brief synopsis of the materials encountered at 
each of the individual sites. 
2.4.1 Wastewater Treatment Plant Site 
Our exploration at this site consisted of the excavation of five test pits with two pits 
situated in the upper area along the paved treatment plant road and another three 
pits in the lower area along the plant boundary fence.  The test pits were identified 
as Test Pits WWTP-1 through WWTP-5, inclusive.   
The test pits in the upper area (WWTP-1 and WWTP-2) penetrated embankment fill 
that was placed when the treatment plant was constructed.  This embankment fill 
generally consisted of very stiff to hard silty clay with a significant portion of cobbles 
and boulders in the soil matrix.  It is estimated that the matrix consisted of about five 
percent each of cobbles and boulders, or an estimated total of 10 percent of coarse 
material.  The embankment fill extended, at a minimum, to 10 feet below the 
existing ground surface (BGS), the maximum depth explored.  It should be noted 
that one of the pits was terminated at the shallower depth of 8.5 feet BGS when a 
large boulder was encountered. 
The test pits excavated in the lower portion of the treatment plant site (WWTP-3 
through WWTP-5) generally encountered about a foot of the same fill material.  In 
two of the pits, coralline materials were encountered below the fill.  One of the 
coralline horizons encountered was cemented dune sand which presented refusal 
conditions at a depth of about 8.0 feet BGS.  Another of the test pits encountered 
what appeared to be weathered basalt rock formation at the depth of about 8 feet 
BGS.  This weathered rock formation was also noted to contain ground water. 
2.4.2 Fazio Golf Course Maintenance Site

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration at the Wastewater Treatment Plant site generally 
encountered very stiff to hard silty clay soils with a significant content of boulders and 
cobbles.  The Agricultural Irrigation and Golf Maintenance sites generally encountered a 
relatively thin surface layer of stiff to hard silty clay over dense to very dense coralline 
deposits at shallow depths. Groundwater was encountered in the test pits on the dates 
and at the times noted in the Logs of Test Pits. 
Structural loading information for the solar panel structures was not available at 
the time this report was prepared. Based on the limited available space and 
assumptions of the foundation load demand, we envision that the solar panel structures 
will be supported on cast-in-place concrete drilled piers. A small reinforced concrete 
slab with thickened-edge footings may also be required to support solar equipment and 
accessories. Detailed discussions and recommendations for foundation design are 
presented in the following sections. 
3.1 
Solar Panel Foundations 
It is our understanding that the vertical loads for solar panel systems are minimal 
and the primary concerns are uplift and overturning due to wind loads on the panels.  
Generally, there are several options available to support solar panel arrays.  One typical 
support system is the use of H-beam or pipe posts driven about 10 feet below the 
existing ground surface. However, the presence of boulders, cobbles and hard coralline 
materials at the three sites would generally constrain this support system since these 
materials would most likely present refusal conditions to driven posts. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, we recommend using an 
ultimate skin friction of 2.0 kip per square foot (ksf) for the stiff to hard silty clay at the 
WWTP and dense coralline materials at the other two sites.  A factor of safety of 2.0 
should be used to determine the allowable compressive load capacity of the 
cast-in-place drilled piers.  The allowable compressive load capacity is for supporting 
dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as 
those caused by wind or seismic forces.

Uplift loads imposed on the drilled piers may be resisted by a combination of the 
dead weight of the drilled shaft or pile and shear along the shaft or pile surface and the 
adjacent soils.  An ultimate uplift skin friction of 2.0 ksf may be used to determine the 
uplift load capacity for transient loading.  The uplift load capacities should not be further 
increased for transient load applications.  For sustained uplift loads, we recommend 
reducing the uplift load capacity by 50 percent to resist sustained uplift loads.  The 
project structural engineer should check the capacity of the drilled shaft or driven steel 
pile member in tension. 
Lateral loads imposed on the solar panel structures may be resisted by the 
lateral load capacity of the drilled piers.  The lateral load resistance of the drilled shaft or 
steel piles is a function of the stiffness of the surrounding soil, the stiffness of the shaft 
or pile, allowable deflection at the top of the shaft or pile, and the induced moment in the 
shaft or pile.  
For rigid shafts or piles, the lateral resistance due to passive earth pressure may 
be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot 
of depth (pcf).  For flexible shafts or piles, a coefficient of lateral subgrade reaction of 
200 pounds per square inch per inch (pci) of deflection may be used.  In general, the 
drilled shafts or driven steel piles should have a minimum center-to center spacing of 
8.0 times the shaft/pile diameter to avoid reduction in the lateral load capacity of the 
shaft/pile. 
A Geolabs representative should be present to observe the solar panel 
foundation installation during construction. Geolabs observation of the foundation 
installation operations is necessary to confirm the assumed subsurface conditions.

conditions would have a significant effect on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled 
shaft foundations.

The load bearing capacities of the drilled shafts depend, to a large extent, on the 
friction between the shaft and the surrounding soils.  Therefore, proper construction 
techniques are important.  The contractor should exercise care in drilling the shaft 
holes and in placing concrete into the drilled holes.  The drilled shafts are designed 
to be embedded into the stiff clayey soils or dense coral encountered in the test pits.  
Some boulders should be expected in the clayey soil horizon given the geologic 
conditions at the site.  The drilled shaft contractor will need to provide drilling 
equipment (and coring tools) that will be capable of drilling through boulders and 
cobbles and hard coral. 
If groundwater is encountered in the drilled holes, then concrete should be placed 
by tremie methods.  A low shrink, high slump (7 to 9-inch slump range) concrete mix 
should be used to provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the 
surrounding soils.  The concrete should be placed promptly after drilling (within 
24 hours) to reduce the potential for softening of the sides of the drilled holes. 
3.2 
Shallow Foundations 
For relatively light structures planned at the site, we recommend using an 
allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) to design 
shallow foundations bearing on the in-situ soils. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live 
loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused 
by wind or seismic forces. 
In general, footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the 
lowest adjacent finished grades. The bottom of the footing excavations should be 
recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction (ASTM D 1557) prior to 
placing reinforcing steel or concrete. 
Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded 
below a one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V) imaginary plane extending upward from 
the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This 
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with

If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our

3.3 
Concrete Slabs-On-Grade 
Based on our field exploration, moderately expansive clayey soils blanket the 
three project sites at the existing ground surface. Unless slabs-on-grade constructed 
above these expansive soils are properly designed, there is a potential for future 
distress to the lightly loaded slabs-on-grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of the 
clayey soils due to changes in the moisture content. To reduce the potential for 
structural distress resulting from swelling of the subgrade soils, we recommend properly 
preparing the subgrade soils prior to fill placement. In addition, we recommend 
providing a minimum 2 feet of non-expansive, select granular fill materials below the 
building slab areas. The non-expansive, select granular fill also should extend beyond

3.3.1 Foundation Probing 
Our experience suggests that cavities and/or voids are commonly encountered in 
coral formations. To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting 
from the collapse of cavities and/or sinkholes below foundations supported on the 
native coralline materials and/or coral formations, a program of cavity probing and 
grouting at the foundation locations during construction is generally recommended. 
However, since no obvious cavities were encountered at the time of our field 
exploration, it is likely that the risk of cavities under the site is minimal.  If the owner 
is willing to accept the risk, the probing operation may be omitted for the solar farm 
area. Similarly, the foundation probing and grouting program may also be omitted 
for buildings and pads supported on 5 feet or more of compacted fill or on clayey 
soils, if the level of risk is acceptable.

3.4.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site. Soft 
and/or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill should be over-excavated to expose firm material, and the 
resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fills. Excavated soft 
and/or organic soils should be properly disposed of off-site and/or used in 
landscape areas, where appropriate. We anticipate the clearing and grubbing effort 
will be a lot less for areas of the site exposing the native coralline materials at the 
ground surface. Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit 
prices for over-excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in 
the over-excavation quantities. 
After clearing and grubbing, finished subgrades and areas designated to receive fills 
should be scarified to a depth of about 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to about 
2 percent above the optimum moisture, and recompacted to a minimum of

3.4.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, we believe the excavated on-site clayey soils (without deleterious 
materials and/or over-sized materials) may be reused as a source of non-structural 
fill materials. If required, imported general fill materials should consist of materials 
with particle sizes of 6 inches or less in maximum dimension and should be free of 
deleterious materials. The material should have a laboratory California Bearing 
Ratio (CBR) value of 8 or higher and a swell potential of 2 percent or less when 
tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883.

Non-expansive, select granular fill materials (structural fill) may be required under 
building slabs, exterior flatwork, and pavement sections. The excavated on-site

3.4.3 Compaction Requirements 
General fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture 
content, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The compaction 
requirements for finished subgrades subjected to vehicular traffic, such as the 
pavement subgrade, should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction. The structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts of about 
8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, 
and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction.  
Aggregate base course materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. Compaction should be accomplished by 
using sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction 
equipment.

Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the clayey subgrade soils and

3.4.4 Cavities and Voids 
Native coralline materials and coral formations generally are highly variable in terms 
of hardness, weathering, and fracturing. In addition, cavities and/or voids are 
commonly encountered in coral formations. In many cases, cavities and voids in the 
coral formation may be filled with loose coralline deposits and/or clayey alluvial soils. 
In areas where coralline formation is anticipated near the ground surface, special 
attention should be given to the subgrade preparation to reduce the potential for 
distress to the future improvements. 
In areas where the excavated subgrade exposes coral formation, the excavated 
subgrade should be proof-rolled with a minimum 10-ton vibratory drum roller to 
detect the presence of potential near-surface cavities, sinkholes, or loose zones. 
The vibratory drum roller should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet per 
minute. The proof-rolling operations should be performed in the presence of a 
Geolabs representative. If the proof-rolling operations detect a near-surface cavity 
or loose zones, the area should be ripped open, cleaned of the debris, and then 
backfilled with compacted fill materials or lean concrete. Therefore, contract 
documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over-excavation 
and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the over-excavation 
quantities.

3.5 
Coral Excavation 
The coral formation in the project area appears to be moderately weathered and 
medium hard to hard in nature. It is anticipated that the majority of the coral formation 
may be excavated with heavy excavation equipment, such as the large backhoe 
excavator. However, coral formations typically contain localized hard and crystallized 
zones. Therefore, we anticipate some difficult excavation conditions may arise in 
localized areas during construction. It is possible that the excavation for a portion of the 
utility line trenches will require the use of hoerams or chipping. The contractor must 
exercise care to avoid over-ripping, which would disrupt the structure of the coral 
formation, resulting in a potential loss of bearing strength for improvements in the 
vicinity. 
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the materials are based on our 
visual observation of the existing coral formation and field and laboratory data from the 
borings drilled. We recommend encouraging contractors to examine the site conditions 
and the subsurface data to make their own reasonable and prudent interpretation. 
3.6 
Utility Trenches and Pits  
We envision that new utility connections will be required for the project.  In 
general, good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling 
of the trenches for the proposed utilities.  The contractor should determine the method 
and equipment to be used for excavation, subject to practical limits and safety 
considerations.  The excavation should comply with the applicable local, state, and 
federal safety requirements.  The contractor should be responsible for trench shoring 
design and installation.  Trench shoring and bracing should also conform to the 
appropriate health and safety requirements. 
Granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, 
No. 67 gradation) is recommended under the pipes.  Free-draining granular materials 
such as No. 3B Fine gravel should also be used for the trench backfill up to about 
12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce 
the compaction effort on the backfill, thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes. 
It is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of
