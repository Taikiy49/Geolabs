1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering evaluation
performed by the application of the Federal Highway Administration FHWA Rockfall
Hazard Rating System RHRS program to the City and County of Honolulu Rock Slide
Potential Inspections project Our current work carries forward with the RHRS Stage 1
Rock Slope Inventory and the RHRS Stage 2 Preliminary Slope Rating established in
Volume 1 of the project The current work addresses the detailed rating of the Class A
hillslopes previously identified in the Rock Slope Inventory that are located adjacent to
City streets on the Island of Oahu Hawaii
This report summarizes our work efforts and findings associated with the
performance of the RHRS Stage 3 Detailed Slope Rating of the Class A hill slopes
Our work was performed in general accordance with the Federal Highways
Administration RHRS program which was initially developed and implemented by the
Oregon Department of Transportation ODOT and the National Highway Institute NHI
The findings presented in this report are based on our research field evaluation and
analyses conducted for the City and County of Honolulu Rock Slide Potential
Inspections project The detailed slope ratings are an integral component that support
the prioritization of remedial construction to reduce the potential hazards associated
with rockfall The findings presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the
end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
Many of the public streets owned and maintained by the City and County of
Honolulu traverse steep hillside terrain located throughout the Island of Oahu Due to
the steep terrain many of the City streets are located below man made cut slopes and
natural hillsides that are composed of basaltic rock altered by varying degrees of
weathering and erosion Over time erosion of the slopes by exposure to weathering

The RHRS is a comprehensive process that allows responsible agencies to
actively manage the rock slopes located adjacent to their roadway system In general

ultimate objective of assisting the prioritization and planning of remedial construction
work The six stages of the program are outlined as follows
1 Rock Slope Inventory Specific information on rockfall sites are gathered
Information required includes an inventory of all City owned streets from

slopes from geologic and soil maps and records of past rockfall activity
from City maintenance records

three broad classes A B and C This step will eliminate sites with
minimal or no rockfall potential Class C and separate sites with higher
potential Class A from those with less potential Class B in order to
focus the effort on the sites with higher risk

remedial work The rating system incorporates factors such as slope
height ditch effectiveness traffic volume sight distance roadway width
geologic characteristics climate and rockfall history A numerical score is
assigned based on these factors in order to quantify the rating The
numerical scores can then be used to prioritize site remedial work

2 1 Objective
The objective for Stage 3 of the Rock Slide Potential Inspections study is to
complete a detailed evaluation of selected hillslopes contained in the project slope
inventory The selected slopes consist of the 56 Class A slopes contained in the
slope inventory The detailed evaluation consists of a field investigation to conduct
site specific observations and measurements that are used to assign numerical scores
for 12 standardized evaluation categories A cumulated score is obtained for each of
the hillslope sites which represents a numerical means to differentiate risk for future
rockfall at the site The scores may be sorted from highest to lowest to obtain a
prioritization of sites for possible rockfall remediation based on the magnitude of the
score
Stage 4 of the RHRS program involves the development of conceptual level
designs and cost estimates which will provide an additional basis for the prioritization of
remedial work along with the numerical scoring of the sites A separate report identified
as Volume 6 will be developed to present the conceptual designs and cost estimates for
the 56 Class A hillslope sites

to the potential occurrence of rockfall The 12 evaluation categories include

SECTION 2 BACKGROUND
As mentioned previously the set scores associated with the benchmark criteria
are and 81 points however the full range of possible points available for
scoring span between 1 and 100 points depending on the category Using the full
range of points provides greater flexibility in scoring which may assist in finely
differentiating the relative impact of field conditions that may be extremely variable
However field judgment which must be applied equally throughout the rating is
required of the rater especially in the case of the subjective categories where direct
measurement of criteria are not performed
The methodology used for the detailed rating and score assignments is based on
the detailed instruction and illustrated examples provided in the RHRS technical
manual A thorough review of the technical manual should be undertaken prior to
conducting the field evaluations It is beyond the scope of this report to provide detailed
instruction on the implementation of the detailed slope rating using the RHRS program
The RHRS technical manual should be the primary resource that provides detailed
instruction for the program
A brief description of the 12 evaluation categories that comprise the detailed
slope evaluation is provided to assist in the general understanding of the program and
the scoring results presented in this report Detailed narrative description in addition to
various example drawings and photographs are provided in Chapter 5 of the RHRS
technical manual The RHRS program was designed to minimize the amount of
interpretation required of the rater It should be recognized that different raters while
rating the same criteria may produce scores with some variability The program is
designed to allow for some variability in scoring especially in the more subjective
categories though the variability should not be significant enough to drastically affect
the total score for a site
Some of the rating categories are based on direct measurement of
characteristics whereas other categories require a more subjective evaluation which
requires some interpretation and generalization It is recommended that only
experienced technical personnel conduct the field evaluations to limit potential
inconsistencies in the rating

Slope Height
The slope height category evaluates the risk associated with the vertical height of
the slope that may be responsible for potential rockfall The vertical slope height
may be measured directly or calculated based on the mathematical relationship
between measured angles of sight and horizontal distances The measured slope
height should include the tallest segment of hillslope from which rockfall may be
produced The slope height may include the vertical height of the lower cut slope
segment combined with the additional vertical height of the upper natural slope
segment where the rockfall source is anticipated Other cases may consider only
the lower cut slope segment if the upper natural slope has very limited rock source
The slope height category is a direct measurement category although some
interpretation is needed to judge the highest point of potential rockfall source
observed at the slope site
The measured or calculated slope height value is then entered into the exponent
equation to obtain the actual score for the slope height category The mathematical
equations needed for calculation of the slope height and slope height score are
contained in Chapters 4 and 5 of the RHRS manual In general the higher a
potential rockfall source is located on the hillside the more potential energy the
source will have The higher potential energy is considered a greater hazard
therefore a higher score is assigned as the slope height increases The slope
height category scoring criteria were not amended from the established criteria
outlined in the RHRS manual therefore the benchmark scores for slope height
shown in the RHRS manual reflect those utilized in this study
Ditch Effectiveness
The ditch effectiveness category evaluates the risk associated with the existing
ditch or shoulder geometry and its ability to restrict falling rock from reaching the
roadway Factors such as the ditch shoulder width depth and shape the
anticipated volume per rockfall event and other irregularities such as potential
launch features on the slope face must be considered The effectiveness of the
existing ditch shoulder area must also consider the slope height and inclination

Average Vehicle Risk AVR
The average vehicle risk category evaluates the risk associated with the
percentage of time a passing vehicle may be present in the rockfall section The
percentage of time is obtained by using the formula shown in Chapter 5 Section 5 5
of the RHRS manual The AVR equation requires input for three variables which
include the slope length average daily traffic ADT and the posted speed limit at
the site The calculated AVR value is then input to the exponential formula to
produce the actual score related to the AVR
It should be noted that official ADT information was not available for most of the
slope sites due to the many site locations involving residential roadways where City
ADT information is not available Thus our field evaluation included the
performance of a short term 30 minute duration count of vehicle traffic passing the
slope site The short term count was extrapolated to produce an approximated
24 hour traffic count Based on our review of these extrapolated traffic counts we
believe that it is generally representative of the actual intensity of traffic volume
The approximated ADT was used in the calculation of the AVR
The AVR represents the potential for a vehicle to be involved in a rockfall event by
assessing the average percent of time that a vehicle may be present within the
rockfall section For example a rating of 100 percent means that on average a
vehicle will be present in the rockfall section at a point in time Computed AVR
values greater than 105 percent receive the maximum score of 100 It should be
noted that computed percent values above 100 percent indicate more than one
vehicle will be present within the rockfall section at a point in time Therefore the
AVR indicates the general significance of the roadway route and likelihood of a
vehicle to be involved in a rockfall incident

Percent of Decision Sight Distance
The decision sight distance category compares the amount of sight distance
available at a site to the low design amount in relation to the posted speed limits
prescribed by the American Association of State Highway and Transportation
Officials AASHTO The percent of decision sight distance used for score
calculation is the ratio of the actual sight distance and the Decision Sight Distance
DSD Actual Sight Distance ASD is the shortest measured distance that a
6 inch tall object is continuously visible to a driver along the roadway The DSD is
the length of roadway in feet required by a driver to perceive a problem and then
bring the vehicle to a stop The DSD varies in relation to the posted speed limit with
increasing distances required for higher speed limits The relationship between the
DSD and the various posted speed limits is presented in Table 5 1 of the RHRS
manual The formula used to calculate the percent of decision sight distance is
located in Section of the RHRS manual The percent decision sight distance
value is then input to the exponential formula to compute the actual score related to
the percent decision sight distance category
The actual sight distance encountered throughout a rockfall section may vary
considerably depending on the presence of horizontal and vertical curves as well as
roadside obstructions such as walls and vegetation These limitations to sight
distance may limit a driver s ability to notice and react to rocks in the roadway
Thus higher scores are computed and higher risks are encountered when the ASD
decreases in relation to the DSD standard
Roadway Width
The roadway width category evaluates the maneuvering width that is available for a
vehicle to avoid a roadway obstacle The width is measured perpendicular to the
roadway and includes only the paved road surface available for vehicle usage
excluding the unpaved road shoulder and on street parking areas Where roadway
widths vary throughout the rockfall section the minimum width is recorded The
measured width is input to the exponential formula to compute the actual score for

Geologic Character
The geologic character of a rockfall section is subjectively evaluated using either of
two cases that have different rating criteria Case 1 is used to rate slopes where
rock jointing bedding planes and other physical discontinuities are judged to be
the dominant features which control rockfall Case 1 involves an evaluation of the
frequency and orientation of rock jointing in addition to the frictional condition of the
joint planes Case 2 is used to rate slopes where differential erosion and or
oversteepening of the slope is judged to be the dominant feature that controls
rockfall Case 2 involves the evaluation of the area coverage of differential erosion
features on the slope in addition to the magnitude of the difference in erosion rate
between the various materials
In general the judgement is made in the field to evaluate which case is more
applicable and perform the evaluation accordingly Where it may be unclear which
case is dominant both cases are evaluated and the higher case score is used for
the category rating For these reasons it is recommended that experienced
personnel such as a geologist or geotechnical engineer perform the subjective
assessment of geological character to obtain optimal information used in the
scoring The table provided in Section 5 8 of the RHRS manual presents the
benchmark criteria associated with Cases 1 and 2 criteria that are used to assign
scores Additional detailed descriptions and photographic examples are provided in
Section 5 8 of the RHRS manual
Case 1 Geologic Character
The Case 1 evaluation criteria is reserved for rock slopes where _ rockfall
predominantly occurs by movement along discontinuities inherent to the rock
formation Case 1 typically encompasses slopes composed of more uniform
layered basaltic lava rock and bedded volcanic tuff materials Rock jointing refers
to a variety of discontinuities such as bedding planes foliations fractures and fault
traces The term continuous refers to jointing that is greater than 10 feet in length

Hawaii California

The term adverse primarily refers to the position of jointing in relation to the slope
orientation and other features such as joint width groundwater seepage from joints
and the friction angle Jointed rock is more prone to movement than massive rock
and adverse orientation of the joints increases the potential for movement The
adverse orientation of joints may combine to form circular block wedge or toppling
type failures kinematically possible Therefore the structural condition of the joints
is rated on the basis of the physical character of the joints
The Case 1 criterion also involves an evaluation of the surface friction along the
rock jointing because movement along discontinuities is controlled by the degree of
roughness and irregularity of the joints Rock friction affects the potential for a
block to move in relation to another block Roughness and rock friction may
incorporate large scale features such as the degree of undulation along bedding
plane and joint surfaces in addition to the fine texture of the joint surfaces
Case 2 Geologic Character
The Case 2 evaluation criteria is reserved for slopes where rockfall is
predominantly caused by differential erosion and oversteepening of a slope which
leads to a loss of support of the earth materials Case 2 typically encompasses
slopes composed of variable layered basaltic clinker and hard rock colluvial talus
and saprolitic soil mixed with remnant rock Some slope erosion features may
consist of steep scarps overhanging slope components eroded gullies and
exposure of resistant rock within a matrix of deeply weathered material The
occurrence of erosion features on a slope face typically signifies that previous slope
failures and rockfall have occurred and additional instability could develop or
worsen Therefore the structural condition of the observed erosion is rated on the
basis of the area coverage and extent of the erosion features
The Case 2 criterion also involves an evaluation of the difference in erosion rate
between the various materials composing the slope Where slopes are composed
of contrasting soil and rock materials the rate of erosion between the materials
may vary thus producing differential erosion features on the slope Continued
differential erosion typically produces unsupported layers or zones of earth material

Block Size Volume of Rockfall Per Event
This subjective evaluation category includes an assessment of the anticipated
volume of rockfall material that may be produced during a rockfall event Rockfall
typically occurs as the failure of either individual blocks or as multiple blocks or
zones of rock material A judgement is made during field investigation to evaluate
which type of failure is most predominant or typical of the site and estimate the size
of the block or volume of material that may be involved in a future slope failure In
order to evaluate which type of failure is more typical a careful examination of the
slope character should be performed to identify old failure surfaces and scars and
existing potential hazards on the slope Observation of the existing accumulation of
slide debris and a review of available cleanup documentation may assist in the
evaluation of historic failures
Larger block size and greater volumes of slide material should receive higher
scores The estimated values are input into the exponential equations outlined in
the RHRS manual to compute the actual scores for the block size and rockfall
quantity categories
Climate
This evaluation category provides an assessment of the amount of precipitation and
water that may affect the slope The amount of precipitation and potential for water
to flow on the slope affects the weathering and erosion of the slope materials
Areas that receive higher amounts of precipitation and or experience water
seepage or continually wet surface conditions are generally more susceptible to
slope failure and rockfall It should be noted that the standard RHRS rating criteria
include an evaluation of the duration of freeze thaw periods Because the Island of
Oahu is not affected by this climate variable we have amended the RHRS rating
criteria to exclude the consideration of freeze thaw periods and instead have
focused on the amount of precipitation experienced at the site combined with the

depending on location The general distribution of rainfall on the Island of Oahu
was evaluated based on a review of available precipitation maps and rain gauge
information for various portions of the Island of Oahu
Rockfall History
This evaluation category rates the historical frequency of rockfall at a site as a
general indicator of the potential for future rockfall events Generally information
pertaining to the frequency of past rockfall activity is best obtained from
maintenance personnel who are familiar with problem areas and cleanup
requirements However only limited City information is available pertaining to past
rockfall events such as the specific location and magnitude of the event Typically
only general commentary reference to problem areas is available Therefore the
frequency of past rockfall events was estimated based on general comments
obtained from City maintenance division supervisors combined with our observation
of the hillslope sites As a result the scores assigned for the rockfall history
category are restricted to the established benchmark criteria which provide a
relative basis of assessment between rockfall sections

In general the evaluation categories that rely on field measurement of rating criteria

roadway width block size and rockfall quantity categories utilize the exponential
equation relationship to calculate the category score Thus the category scores

3 1 Findings
The 56 Class A hillslopes contained in the City and County of Honolulu slope
inventory were advanced to Stage 3 detailed slope rating of the RHRS program The
slopes were evaluated based on the established rating criteria and scoring function that
defines the exponential scoring system used for all 12 rating categories The detailed
ratings were performed in general accordance with the procedures outlined in Chapters
4 and 5 of the FHWA RHRS technical manual
The total score for each of the Class A hillslope sites represents the sum of the
individual scores assigned for each of the 12 evaluation categories comprising the
detailed slope rating The total scores for the Class A sites range from a low of 153 to
a high of 647 Table 2 presents the total score tabulation for the 56 Class A sites
arranged in order from the highest score to the lowest score The scores reflect the
ranking of the 56 Class A sites with respect to the detailed slope rating information
only Therefore the ranking provides an assessment of the relative level of risk
associated with potential rockfall The ranking based on the detailed slope evaluation is
a means to categorize the anticipated level of risk between the sites The site ranking
does not necessarily indicate that rockfall events would occur in the general order of the
ranking
The detailed slope rating effort involved completing a standard checklist type
form to record the pertinent field information used in the calculation of scores In
addition some supplemental sketches were drawn in the field to graphically represent
some of the existing field conditions The evaluation procedures and rating criteria were
performed in general accordance with the RHRS technical manual Deviation from the
procedures and rating criteria were minimized to keep the program in general
conformance with the established procedures Therefore it is recommended that future
slope evaluations follow the procedures outlined in the National Highway Institutes
RHRS technical manual The field data sheets for the 56 Class A slope sites have

Detailed Slope Evaluation and Rating
The findings presented in this report are based on field observations made of the
existing conditions encountered at the hillslope sites at the time of our site visit
The observations were recorded by a qualified two member team who has
experience in the systematic evaluation of geologic conditions pertaining to
potential falling rock hazards and hillslope stability in general accordance with
RHRS program It should be noted that only qualified and experienced personnel
using a standardized format for recording observations should conduct the slope
evaluation and rating The person s conducting future slope evaluation and rating
work should be thoroughly familiar with Chapters 4 and 5 of the RHRS technical
manual which outline the procedures and methods to be used in the evaluation In
order to maintain consistency in the program the person s performing the slope
evaluation should also fully understand the limited modifications described in this
report which were applied to the detailed slope rating procedures
The detailed slope ratings are based in part on our geological interpretation of the
existing site conditions observed at the time of our site visit In order to conduct the
detailed slope ratings some professional judgments were made pertaining to
the geologic conditions and behavior of potential rockfall with respect to the public
right of way The same team members conducted the detailed slope evaluations in
an effort to maintain objectivity and a perceptive relativity of the observations
recorded at the various hillslope sites
