INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Kekaulike Revitalization project in the Chinatown area of
downtown Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location of the site and
its vicinity is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 The exploration was performed
in general accordance with our fee proposal dated
This report summarizes our findings from the field exploration and our geotechn
ical engineering recommendations which were derived from analyses based on the data
obtained during the exploration The recommendations are intended for design input and
are not intended to form the basis for preparation of construction cost estimates or for
bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed Kekaulike Revitalization project involves the construction of a 4 story
commercial and residential building complex on the site as a public housing development
The proposed development will also include 1 level on the Kekaulike Street side and
2 levels on the Maunakea Street side of below ground parking which will extend down
to elevations 1 0 to 2 0 feet MSL
Basement excavations up to about eighteen 18 feet deep below the existing
grade and immediately adjacent to existing streets and buildings are anticipated
Therefore excavation support and a dewatering system will be required during construc
tion of the basement

project

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located in the Chinatown area of downtown Honolulu on the
island of Oahu The site is bounded by Maunakea Street to the southeast and Kekaulike
Street to the northwest To the northeast and southwest of the site are existing low rise
buildings facing Hotel and King Streets respectively
The majority of the site is occupied by an existing on grade municipal parking
facility The site is relatively level with existing elevations ranging approximately from 11
to 16 feet MSL
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling five 5 borings to
depths ranging from 115 0 to 151 5 feet below the existing ground surface within the
proposed project boundary The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2

Groundwater Level
The groundwater levels in the borings were checked at different times during our
field exploration Based on the groundwater level measurements and previous experience
in the project site the observed in situ groundwater level varied between elevations of
about 0 4 to 6 5 feet MSL
Normally groundwater levels near shoreline areas are generally between elevations
0 0 and 2 0 feet MSL The relatively lower groundwater level observed in the project site
could have been caused by water pumping in the adjacent neighborhood For the project
planning and design of the basement structure we believe that the in situ groundwater
level should be assumed to occur approximately at elevation 2 0 feet MSL

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
It is Our opinion that the proposed project is feasible from a geotechnical
engineering point of view Most of the competent near surface coral formation will be
removed by the basement excavation It appears that the underlying loose to medium
dense coralline deposits cannot provide adequate support for shallow foundations without
inducing excessive settlements under the proposed structure Therefore deep pile
foundations embedded in the basalt formation or bouldery old alluvium are recommended
to provide foundation support of the project
It is our understanding that construction of the basement parking structure will
involve excavation down to elevations 1 0 to 2 0 feet MSL Moreover the excavation will
abut the surrounding streets and buildings Therefore adequately designed and properly
implemented shoring and dewatering systems will be required for the basement
construction
Site Grading
It is our understanding that the finish floor elevations of the basement parking
structure have been set at elevations ranging from 1 0 to 2 0 feet MSL Site grading
involving excavation up to about 18 feet below the existing ground surface is anticipated
for the basement construction

It is Our opinion that the on site excavated coral material may be used as fill
material provided that the materials are processed down to relatively well graded

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
No major fill placement is anticipated for the project The fill backfill should be
placed in horizontal loose level lifts up to a maximum of 10 inches thick and
compacted to a minimum of 90 of its maximum dry density as determined by
ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
All fill or backfill below the water level should consist of clean granular material
such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 recommended above This
material is generally self compacting Additional densification may be obtained by
use of a vibratory compactor on the final level of the material

Shoring
The excavation for the underground structure will abut the adjacent Maunakea
Street and the existing low rise buildings on King Kekaulike and Hotel Streets An
adequately designed and properly constructed excavation support system will be needed
to reduce the potential damage to the surrounding utility lines streets and buildings
caused by earth movements toward the open excavation
Based on the borings it appears that the upper hard coral may obstruct sheet pile
penetration It is our opinion that a support system consisting of soldier piles and lagging
with temporary tiebacks or rakers could be adopted to provide support of the excavation
The use of horizontal bracing or struts inside the excavation does not appear practical
because of the anticipated wide excavation
Generally the soldier piles should consist of wide flange steel beams and heavy
timbers should be used for lagging The actual design of the soldier pile and lagging
system or other appropriate type of excavation support systems should be left to the
contractor
For estimating purposes the soldier piles should generally be installed at no more
than eight 8 feet on centers in predrilled holes extending a minimum of eight 8 feet
below the bottom of the excavation The bottom eight 8 feet of the predrilled holes
should be backfilled with tremie concrete after placement of the soldier piles
Tiebacks
A tieback is a structural system which uses an anchor embedded in the ground
to secure a tendon which applies a lateral force on a retaining structure Tiebacks
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2748 00 Page 10
have been employed primarily for temporary excavations generally utilizing soldier
piles with lagging for soil retention Since tiebacks do encroach underground of
the adjacent properties permission for encroachment from the owners of the
adjacent properties should be obtained prior to the tieback design installation
Tiebacks are advantageous since they are external to the excavation and
permanent construction may proceed without hinderance from internal bracing
A schematic sketch of the tieback support system is presented on Plate 3
Excavations supported by tiebacks usually experience less movement than those
supported by raker braced walls This is because the tiebacks are installed from
the temporary ground surface and excavation does not proceed below a given
anchor level until the anchors for that level are installed
At this time there are a number of different tieback systems available for
consideration including drilled and grouted anchors deadman anchors and helical
anchors It is recommended that the contractor retain a specialty subcontractor
or a structural engineering consultant to aid in selecting an appropriate tieback
system and to design the overall excavation support for the project
Alternatively a raker supported soldier pile system may be considered to provide
temporary support for the excavation sidewalls It should be understood that
rakers are acceptable but are less preferable than tiebacks This is because the
excavation in front of the lagging wall will have to proceed prior to installing the
rakers As a result raker braced excavations usually experience greater lateral
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2748 00 Page 11
movements at the top than tieback support systems In addition the rakers
project into the excavation and cause interference with construction of the
permanent structures
If the raker system is selected for excavation support it is recommended that the
following raker installation procedures be followed and extreme care be exercised
to reduce the ground movements during excavation
In the raker support system excavations in the central portion of the site may
proceed first leaving temporary berms around the anticipated excavation
boundary see Plate 4 In order to reduce potential ground movements the
berms should then be trenched for the installation of rakers The first set of rakers
should be installed as early as practicable As the berms are removed gradually
the second set of rakers should then be placed by trenching in Additional sets
of rakers as necessary may be installed by following the same procedure
We strongly recommend that the contractor retain a competent structural
engineering consultant to design and to coordinate the implementation of the
excavation support system
Shoring Construction Precautions
The soldier piles and timber lagging with either tieback or raker systems must
comply with all applicable safety requirements The adequacy and safety of the
excavation support should be made the sole responsibility of the contractor His
representative who should be continuously present on site will have the best opportunity
to observe any changing conditions during excavation and construction such as
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2748 00 Page 12
unforeseen subsoils unsuitable construction sequences or techniques etc which might
affect the excavation stability
Since lateral supports are installed as excavation proceeds movements will
inevitably occur due to changes in stress on the excavation sidewalls during excavation
Even with the best construction procedures the excavation will proceed ahead of support
installation The further excavation proceeds before the next level of support is installed
the greater is the potential for lateral ground movements
Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation supports should be installed
as early as practicable particularly if rakers are to be adopted The vertical and
horizontal ground movements outside the excavation should be monitored periodically
It is also recommended that a damage survey of all the existing buildings and
structures adjacent to the project site be conducted The survey should include
photographs and records of the cracks and existing conditions prior to the commence
ment of construction

Pile Foundations
Based on the preliminary exploration data it appears that the underlying loose to
medium dense coralline deposits cannot provide adequate support for shallow
foundations such as mat or spread footings without inducing excessive settlements
under the proposed structure Therefore deep pile foundations embedded in the basalt
formation or bouldery old alluvium are recommended for foundation support of the
project
Since the finish floor elevations of the basement parking structure may vary from
elevations 1 0 to 2 0 feet MSL it is our opinion that the project site may be cut down to
approximately elevation 3 0 feet MSL during initial grading This is to allow the machinery
and pile driving equipment to operate above the groundwater level for pile driving and to
reduce the wastage of piles above cut off elevation
Pile Capacities
Sixteen and one half inch inch octagonal prestressed concrete piles with
an allowable vertical pile capacity of 100 tons per pile may be used The piles may
develop a majority of the bearing capacity by end bearing on the underlying basalt
formation or bouldery alluvium with some frictional contribution from the pile shafts
embedded in the surrounding coralline deposits and alluvium A minimum pile
spacing of three 3 feet on centers is recommended
An allowable lateral pile capacity of up to 12 kips per pile may be used in the
design provided that the predrilled holes are properly backfilled as recommended
under the Predrilling Backfilling section
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2748 00 Page 14
An allowable uplift capacity of 25 kips per pile may be used for continuous pile
sections a minimum of 35 feet long
Pile Lengths
Based on the exploration results estimated pile lengths may vary from about 100
to 120 feet below elevation 3 0 feet MSL For the initial construction cost estimate
an average pile length of about 110 feet may be used It is anticipated that two 2
sections of pile and a splice can will be needed to obtain the 110 foot pile length
The top pile section should be a minimum of 35 feet long to provide the uplift
capacity
The above pile lengths are for initial cost estimation purposes The actual
production pile lengths should be determined after completion of an indicator pile
program
Pile Driving Criteria
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a minimum rated
energy of about 30 000 foot pounds The hammer should be equipped with an
energy level control so that driving within the loose deposits may be performed at
a reduced energy level to minimize the development of tensile stresses within the
piles which could cause damage cracking to the piles
Additional pile driving criteria are as follows
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2748 00 Page 15
1 The driving process should be continuous without interruption for each pile
A pile may be rejected or its design capacity modified when the driving
process is interrupted for more than four 4 hours
2 During easy driving when the pile tip is still within the loose sandy soil the
hammer stroke should be carefully controlled reduced energy so that
excessive tensile stresses are not induced in the pile
3 The pile driving may be terminated when the blow counts steadily increase
with depth and reach 9 blows per inch for the last three inches or when
aggregated blow counts are 120 blows for the last foot
Predrilling Backfilling
The borings indicated that the upper relatively competent coral may extend down
to elevation 3 0 feet MSL in the southeastern portion Maunakea Street side of the site

pile location within the southeastern portion of the site The approximate boundary of the
area that may require predrill is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 This would facilitate pile
driving in the erratic coral environment and help to maintain plumbness of the piles The
predrilled holes should have a minimum diameter of 20 inches for inch octagonal
piles The required predrill depth may be verified and or modified during the indicator pile
program

Indicator Pile Program
An indicator pile program should be undertaken to fulfill the following objectives
1 To examine the driveability of piles with the use of the selected hammer
2 To more accurately determine production pile order lengths and to further
verify delineate areas that may require different production pile lengths
3 To help evaluate and modify if necessary the predrill depths and driving
Criteria
To achieve these objectives we recommend that the indicator pile program should
consist of a minimum of ten 10 percent of the total number of piles as test piles for the
project The test piles may be incorporated into the actual foundation system later
When the final foundation plan is available the project geotechnical engineer
should be consulted for selection of the test pile locations The test piles should be cast
ten 10 feet longer than the estimated pile lengths Also the indicator pile program
should provide flexibility by allowing for addition of or relocation of the test piles as
deemed necessary to suit the conditions encountered during the test pile driving
A number of splice cans should be made available at the site during the indicator
pile driving period Splice cans may be fabricated with 1 4 inch thick high grade steel
and should be at least five 5 feet long

As a part of the indicator pile program a minimum of one 1 pile load test should
be budgeted The selected pile should be load tested to twice the allowable load capacity
in accordance with ASTM Test Method D 1143
The indicator piles should be driven with the same hammer which will be used for
production pile driving The indicator pile program should be conducted under close and

perimeter walls will be non yielding members and should be designed for at rest

1 For conventional retaining wall an active earth pressure of 35 p s f equivalent

Construction Dewatering
Dewatering is anticipated for the basement excavation and construction It is our
understanding that the site may be excavated down to elevations 4 0 to 5 0 feet MSL
to provide finish floor elevations of 1 0 to 2 0 feet MSL for the proposed basement
parking structure
Assuming in situ groundwater level at about elevation 1 0 to 2 0 feet MSL the
required maximum depth of dewatering is on the order of 6 to 7 feet Based on the
preliminary borings the required dewatering appears to be achievable with proper
dewatering methods
It should be understood that dewatering does not merely suggest the installation
of a pumping unit and pumping water out of the excavation Dewatering means any
systems required to lower the groundwater level and to exclude it from the excavation to
permit construction under dry conditions
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2748 00 Page 20
These systems may include but are not limited to well pumping grouting cut off
walls trernie plugs or any combination of the above and or other possible methods The
method and equipment to be used for the dewatering operations should be the choice
of the contractor within practical limits and safety considerations Regardless of the
method used dewatering operations should be done continuously and without pumping
out soil fines pumping clear water only
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to operation The
impact and safety of the dewatering system operations should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His representative who is continuously present on site
will have the best opportunity to promptly observe the effects of dewatering during
construction and to implement immediately precautionary or remedial measures including
but not limited to stopping or slowing down the dewatering operations as the situation
deems necessary
Caution should also be taken during dewatering operations not to dewater utility
trench es that connect to the dewatered excavation The resultant areal groundwater
depression may extend along the granular bedding or backfill of the trench es and could
cause settlements and damages to the utility lines and or other existing structures

under the pipes

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based in part upon
information obtained from field borings Variations of conditions between the field borings
may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until
construction If variations then appear evident it will be necessary to reevaluate the
recommendations given in this report
The locations of the field borings were approximately determined by taping from
objects indicated on the preliminary topographic survey map prepared by R M Towill
Corporation dated August 13 1991 Elevations of the field borings were interpreted from
the same map The physical location and the elevation of the field borings should be
considered accurate only to the degree implied by the method used
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the field borings depict
the approximate boundaries between soil types and as such may denote a gradual
transition
Water level data from the field borings were collected at the times given on the
graphic representations and or in the text of this report These data have been reviewed
and interpretations made in the formulation of this report However it must be noted that
fluctuation may occur due to variation in rainfall tides temperature and other factors

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling five 5 borings to
depths of 115 0 to 151 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate
locations shown on the Site A truck mounted drili rig was used to drill the
borings
The material encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who continuously monitored the drilling
operations The classifications were further reviewed by visual observation and testing
in the laboratory The soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System A graphic presentation of the materials encountered is presented
Soil samples were obtained from the borings by driving a 2 inch O D standard
penetration sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts to
drive the sampler the last 12 inches are shown on the Boring Logs at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples were obtained by a HQ wireline core barrel The core recovery
REC and rock quality designation RQD values in percentage are shown on the Boring
Logs at the appropriate sample depths
