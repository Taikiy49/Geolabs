REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield volcano Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age Ice
Age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became
extinct while Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau
lavas banking against its eastern flank
The project area is located on the southern flank of Koolau and is composed of
basalt rock built by the extrusion of the lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks
are generally characterized by flows of jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin
clinker layers For the last 1 to 2 million years weathering of the Koolau lava flows has
occurred forming a thin mantle of alluvial and residual soils which grade to basalt rock
formation with depth In the vicinity of the project site rock formation is located very close
to the existing ground surface However the soils mantling the rock formation are
generally composed of a dark brown adobe clay or clayey material This clay is known to
be responsible for a variety of foundation problems resulting from its high shrink swell
characteristic during cyclic wetting and drying of the soil

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by stiff to very
stiff clayey soils extending to depths of about 8 5 to 26 5 feet below the existing ground
surface Based on the subsoil conditions encountered at the project site we believe that
shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings bearing on 12 inches
of compacted select granular fill material over the in situ stiff clayey soils may be used to
support the proposed new residence Due to the high rainfall environment at the project
site we believe that the 12 inch select granular fill material recommended underneath the
building foundations would serve as a protective cover for the clayey subgrade soils in
GEOLABS INC
W O 4539 00 Page 7
reducing the potential for footing subgrade saturation from inclement weather during the
project construction
Based on our laboratory tests the in situ clayey soils exhibit moderately high
shrink swell potential when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents To
reduce the potential for distress to the future lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from
the swelling of the near surface soils we recommend that the on site clayey soils be
capped with a minimum of 18 inches of non expansive select granular fill capping fill
material below slab areas In general the subgrade soils should be compacted to provide
a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to the placement of fill material Use of
the vibratory action of a compactor should be carefully controlled to avoid inducing a
pumping subgrade condition The slab subgrade should be kept moist and free of
shrinkage cracks prior to placement of capping fill cushion fill and or slab concrete Our
geotechnical engineering recommendations pertaining to the design of foundations
retaining walls site preparation and pavements are further discussed in the following
sections of this report
Building Foundations
Based on the generally stiff subsoil conditions encountered at the site we believe
that shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the proposed
new construction An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square
foot psf may be used for the design of shallow foundations bearing on 12 inches of
compacted select granular fill material over the in situ stiff clayey soils This bearing value
is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such
as those caused by wind or seismic forces
Due to the high rainfall environment at the project site we believe that th
12 inch select granular fill material recommended underneath the building foundations
would serve as a protective cover for the clayey subgrade soils in reducing the potential
for footing subgrade saturation from inclement weather thus expediting the foundation
preparation work during the project construction Therefore for footings bearing on the

in situ stiff clayey soils or compacted fill material the bottom of the footing excavations
should be over excavated to a minimum depth of 12 inches below the design footing
bottom elevation If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the
footing over excavations the material should be over excavated to expose the
underlying firm materials The resulting over excavations should be backfilled with
select granular materials compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Hard basalt rock formation may be encountered at the bottom of footing

that the hard basalt rock formation at the bottom of footing be over excavated a minimum

settlements and provide more uniform bearing support
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 35 may be used for footings bearing on the select granular fill material A
coefficient of friction of 0 3 may be used for the site retaining walls bearing on the in situ
stiff clayey soils Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This
assumes that the soil around footings is well compacted The passive pressure should be
reduced for foundations located on slopes Unless covered by pavements or slabs the
passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
in general footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest

Slabs On Grade
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by clayey soils
with moderately high expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations To
reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from
shrinking and swelling of the clayey soils due to changes in the moisture content we
recommend that the subgrade soils be properly prepared and capped with at least
18 inches of non expansive select granular fill capping fill material below the slab cushion
The capping fill should also extend beyond the new slab on grade footprint a minimum of
24 inches

The non expansive select granular capping fill should be placed as soon as possible
after the subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented
in the Site Preparation section of this report The prepared capping fill and the underlying
subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist prior to the final placement of concrete
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that
the soils be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks Saturation and subsequent yielding
of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted engineered fill
For interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that a
minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation be placed below the slabs to serve as a capillary break and to provide
more uniform support of the slabs To reduce moisture infiltration and subsequent damage
to floor coverings an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the
No 3B Fine gravel Two inches of moist sand may be considered above the moisture
barrier to provide protection of the moisture barrier and to aid in curing of the slab concrete
Provisions of the 2 inches of moist sand above the moisture barrier will be left up to the
discretion of the project structural engineer Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or
sheet vinyl should be considered since they can better mask minor slab cracking
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project We
recommend that concrete walkways be supported on 12 inches of non expansive select
granular fill or aggregate subbase The select granular fill or aggregate subbase should be
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Control joints should be provided at
intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at right angle
intersections

Garage Slab and Concrete Driveway

Wall Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures behind the walls A typical drainage system should consist of about
1 cubic foot of permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation wrapped with non woven filter fabric at each of the weephole locations
The weepholes should be spaced not more than 6 feet apart Unless covered by
concrete or asphaltic concrete the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of
relatively impervious materials such as the on site soils to reduce the potential for
significant water infiltration behind the walls
Swimming Pool

accordance with the recommendations presented in the Building Foundations and
Retaining Walls sections of this report Since the swimming pool will be a water

provided under the swimming pool slab deck to reduce the potential for future slab
distresses resulting from the swelling of the on site expansive soils

Drainage
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems
be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from the
foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and
slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have
concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain surface
run off away from the slabs and foundations
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent loss of strength and or heaving of the near surface clayey soils The
foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after
setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings to depths of about 25 and 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate locations of the drilled borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The
borings were drilled using portable drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight
augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and
drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock coral formations encountered at the site were
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM
Standard Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling
is a rotary drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation
The material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination
and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
