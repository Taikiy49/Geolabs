PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The proposed Makena U-1 Ranch project [TMK: (2) 2-01-008: 108, 090] is in the 
Makena area in the District of Makawao on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The current master 
plan generally consists of mass grading for infrastructure development for a new 37-lot 
agricultural subdivision.  Based on the preliminary plans provided, mass grading consisting 
of cuts of up to about 20 feet in depth and fills of up to about 24 feet in height may be 
required to achieve design grades. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Based on our experience in the vicinity of the project site, we anticipate that the 
subsurface conditions may generally consist of surface gravel and cobble layer underlain 
by interbedded layers of dense basalt formation and a’a clinker. We anticipate that the 
surface materials generally consist of medium dense silty gravel and cobbles of less than 
about 5 to 10 feet in thickness.  Clinker materials generally consist of medium dense 
cobbles and gravel with some silt.  Basalt formation is anticipated at varying depths from 
near the existing ground surface to about 10 feet below the existing ground surface. The

Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
thoroughly cleared. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and other unsuitable 
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site to reduce the potential for 
contamination of the excavated materials. 
Areas to receive fills and finished subgrades in cut areas should be proof-rolled 
with a minimum 20-ton (static weight) vibratory drum roller for a minimum of eight passes. 
The vibratory drum roller also should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute. 
The proof-rolling operations should be performed in the presence of a Geolabs 
representative. Yielding areas, loose areas, or cavities disclosed during clearing and 
proof-rolling operations should be over-excavated and backfilled with compacted fill 
materials. Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for     
over-excavation of the collapsed lava tube features (and other features) and backfilled 
with compacted fill to account for variations in the over-excavation and backfill quantities.

Fills and Backfills 
In general, the near-surface silty gravel and cobbles anticipated at the site should 
be suitable for use as general fill materials, provided that the particle size is 3 inches or 
less in largest dimension. The on-site materials generated from the excavations may be 
used as a source of general fill or backfill materials, provided that they are screened of 
the over-sized materials and/or processed to meet the above gradation requirements 
(less than 3 inches in largest dimension). 
Select granular fill should consist of non-expansive granular material, such as 
crushed coral or basalt. The select granular fill should be well-graded from coarse to fine 
with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension. The material should have a 
California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher, and a swell potential of 1 percent or 
less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. The material also should contain 
between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. 
Aggregate base course and aggregate subbase materials required for the project 
should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates and should conform to Sections 31 and 30 of 
the County of Maui Standard Specifications (September 1986). Geolabs should test the 
imported fill materials for conformance with these recommendations. 
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
In general, fills and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The compaction requirement for 
the last lift of fill (finished subgrade) below areas subjected to vehicular traffic should be 
increased to at least 95 percent relative compaction. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D1557. Optimum 
moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum 
dry density. Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, 
or other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or ponding 
should not be allowed to compact the fills. 
Excavation 
As mentioned above, we anticipate excavations of up to about 20 feet below the 
existing ground surface may be required for the project. It is anticipated that the surface 
gravel and cobbles, a’a clinker materials, and highly fractured basalt formation may be 
excavated readily with normal heavy excavation equipment, such as excavators, and 
ripped with large bulldozers. However, some of the excavations in dense basalt formation 
will likely require the use of hoerams, chipping, or blasting.

Cut and Fill Slopes 
In general, we believe the cut slopes planned at the site exposing the silty gravel, 
cobbles and clinker materials may be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal 
to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. 
Permanent fill slopes constructed of the general fill and select granular fill materials 
recommended herein may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter.  In 
addition, fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V, such as against the existing fill slopes, 
should be benched. The fill slope face should be finished to a relatively smooth and 
well-compacted surface.

For cut and fill slopes steeper than 3H:1V with vertical heights greater than 30 feet, 
we recommend providing a minimum 8-foot wide bench at the mid-point of the slope or 
at every 30-foot height interval to reduce the potential for significant erosion due to surface 
water runoff. Where slope benches are provided (especially for fill slopes), concrete-lined 
swales should be provided on the benches to collect and divert surface water from the 
slopes to appropriate discharge outlets. In addition, construction of interceptor ditches 
and the use of geotextile fabrics over the fill slope face should be considered to reduce 
the potential for significant erosion, thus enhancing the long-term stability of the fill slopes. 
LIMITATIONS 
The findings and recommendations submitted in this letter report are based, 
in part, from our experience in the vicinity of the project site. Variations of subsurface 
conditions from those assumed herein may occur, and the nature and extent of these 
variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then 
appear evident, Geolabs should be contacted to re-evaluate the recommendations 
presented in this letter report.
