PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Kalakaua Avenue sewer rehabilitation project is located along Kalakaua
Avenue approximately between Kanunu Street and Kapiolani Boulevard in Honolulu on
the Island of Oahu Hawaii as shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 We
understand that the existing 6 and 8 inch diameter sewer lines have hydraulic and
structural deficiencies and that it is planned to install a new 8 inch diameter sewer line
The sewer line alignment will begin near the vicinity of the Kanunu Street and
Kalakaua Avenue intersection The sewer line will traverse to the south along the east
side of Kalakaua Avenue The sewer line will connect to an existing sewer line near the
vicinity of the Kapiolani Boulevard and Kalakaua Avenue intersection The sewer line
alignment is located along the eastern sidewalk along Kalakaua Avenue Based on our
observations the sidewalk consists of portland cement concrete Based on the
information provided we understand that both conventional open trench and trenchless
methods are being considered for installation of the new sewer line
REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of
the eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while

Koolau was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in
Central Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of Koolau and _ its
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic
fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the
Honolulu Coastal Plain The subsurface conditions are dominated by fossil Pleistocene
Age coral algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of the sea
which are intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic section
in this area is Koolau Basalt
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level
changes as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world
These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher
and lower relative to present sea level on Oahu About 15 000 years ago a relatively
rapid rise in sea level occurred During that rise the valleys in the project area were
drowned In the last 10 000 years or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand
Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy
estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in thick deposits of loose and or soft
sediments along the coast in the areas that were formerly valleys and drainageways
In the early part of this century the subject area consisted of low marshy areas
As the City of Honolulu grew and the subject area was urbanized man made fills were
placed to reclaim the marshy areas and lagoons The fill was placed upon lagoonal
deposits consisting of silty sands with coral fragments The lower clayey material was
deposited during periodic influxes of non marine alluvial debris which apparently
smothered the coral reef development Land development and reclamation projects
within the last 100 years have brought the subject area to its present form

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsoil conditions at the subject site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings to depths of about 30 and 30 3 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plates 2 1 and 2 2
Boring No 1 encountered 8 inches of asphaltic concrete underlain by fill material
consisting of dense silty sand medium stiff sandy silt and stiff sandy clay extending to a
depth of about 9 feet below the existing ground surface The fill material was underlain by
lagoonal deposits consisting of very loose sands and gravel extending to a depth of about
19 feet below the existing ground surface The lagoonal deposits were underlain by
coralline detritus consisting of very dense gravel extending to a depth of about 25 feet
below the existing ground surface The coralline detritus was underlain by medium hard
coral formation extending to the maximum depth explored of about 30 feet below the
existing ground surface
Boring No 2 encountered 10 inches of asphaltic concrete underlain by fill material
consisting of medium dense silty sand extending to a depth of about 5 feet below the
existing ground surface The fill material was underlain by lagoonal deposits consisting of
medium stiff clays and very loose to loose sands and gravel extending to a depth of about
24 feet below the existing ground surface The lagoonal deposits were underlain by
medium hard coral formation extending to the maximum depth explored of about 30 3 feet
below the existing ground surface

It is our understanding that the existing 6 and 8 inch sewer lines will be replaced
with a new 8 inch sewer line Based on the available information pipe inverts range from
3 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface Based on our field exploration medium

recommend that proposed sewer line inverts should not extend deeper than 5 feet below

Installation of the new pipe using trenchless methods and conventional open trench
method were evaluated for the project Based on our evaluation we believe that both

recommendations are presented in the following sections of this report
Trenchless Methods
Two categories of trenchless methods are available rehabilitation and conventional
trenchless Trenchless rehabilitation consists of reusing the existing pipe or pipe
alignment and includes pipe bursting slip lining and Cured In Place Pipe CIPP methods
Conventional trenchless methods consists of installing a replacement pipe generally
parallel to the existing pipe that includes microtunneling and horizontal directional drilling
Pipe Bursting
Pipe bursting consists of winching a pneumatic powered bursting tool through an
old section of pipe breaking and cracking the old pipe as it progresses A new pipe
section is pulled into place behind the bursting tool replacing the old pipe section
High Density Polyethylene HDPE pipe is typically used with pipe bursting
Conventional open trench method was used for the installation of the existing 6 and
8 inch sewer line The open trench method consists of excavating a trench wide
enough for the sewer line installation placing the sewer pipe and backfilling the
open trench with backfill material During pipe bursting the soil surrounding the
bursting tool will be compressed Ina backfilled trench compaction of the backfill
material will generally occur
Damage to existing utilities in close proximity of the bursting tool may occur for
existing utilities generally within 3 feet of the bursting tool Based on the existing
utility plan some utility lines are within this zone In these areas the utility
crossings may be exposed and the crossing utility moved further away from the
bursting tool during the pipe installation or the utility crossing may be supported
in place to reduce the potential for damage
For conditions where the bursting tool is close to the existing ground surface
surface heave may occur We anticipate that a 10 inch HDPE pipe will be required
to obtain an inside diameter of at least 8 inches For this HDPE pipe size

Slip Lining
Slip lining consists of inserting a new pipe of smaller diameter into the deteriorated
host pipe The new pipe is inserted either by pushing or pulling the pipe into the
deteriorated pipe The annular space between the existing pipe and the new pipe
is filled with grout
Excavation pits may be required to connect laterals with the main sewer line
Based on our exploration the excavation pits may encounter medium stiff sandy
and silty clays and medium dense silty sands
In summary we believe that installation of the new sewer line by slip lining method
is a viable option for this project
Cured In Place Pipe CIPP
CIPP consist of inserting a liquid thermoset resin saturated material into the host
pipe The liquid thermoset resin saturated material is inserted into the host pipe by
hydrostatic or air inversion or by mechanically pulling with a winch and cable The
material is then heat cured in place
Excavation pits may be required to connect laterals with the main sewer line
Based on our exploration the excavation pits may encounter medium stiff sandy
and silty clays and medium dense silty sands

Microtunneling
Microtunneling is an installation method where a pipe is jacked behind a remotely
controlled steerable laser guided tunnel boring machine Entry and exit pits will be
required These shallow pits are generally rectangular in shape to accommodate
the length about three to four tunnel diameters and width about one tunnel
diameter of the tunnel boring machine the jacking frame reaction block and pipe
sections to be jacked into place During the microtunneling process the excavated
material is removed by using a mixture of water and slurry pumped through a pipe
for disposal balanced screw auger or a screw conveyor system
Microtunneling is performed with specialized equipment that is very costly and is
generally limited to the smallest pipe size of 18 inches In addition we believe that
the planned invert depths are too shallow to use microtunneling for installation of
the sewer line
In microtunneling selecting the right machine cutter head for the ground condition
is critical Different types of cutter heads are used for various types of soil rock
Based on our field exploration medium stiff sandy silts and clays and medium
dense silty sands were encountered within the anticipated sewer line inverts
Squeezing may occur in medium stiff sandy clays encountered This may increase
the jacking forces and in some cases can stop progress of the tunneling machine
The medium dense silty sands encountered may ravel onto the pipe or may ravel
into the face of the tunneling machine Again this may increase the jacking forces
We believe that it would be very costly to install an 8 inch diameter sewer line with
invert depths of about 3 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface by
microtunneling Therefore microtunneling is not a viable option for this project
Horizontal Directional Drilling HDD
This trenchless steerable installation method uses a multi axis drilling machine to
bore a small pilot hole The pilot hole is then reamed to a larger size to
accommodate the new pipe The new pipe is pulled back into the reamed hole by
the drilling machine
It is generally more difficult to maintain an accurate alignment with horizontal
directional drilling as compared to microtunneling Since the sewer line is a gravity
type sewer line accurate alignment and grade are critical If the existing sewer line
is planned to be used as a pilot hole deviation in grade of the new sewer line may
occur The diameter of the drilling rods are much smaller than the diameter of the

existing pipe and therefore it may be difficult to maintain the center of the drill rods
at the center of the existing sewer line
Based on our field exploration medium stiff sandy silts and clays and medium
dense silty sands were encountered within the anticipated sewer line inverts
Because the silty sand soils have little cohesion the silty sand may cave into the
borehole causing hole stability problems of the pilot and reamed holes The
caved in materials will obstruct or divert reaming tools or the pipe string during pipe
pullback This will substantially increase the pulling stresses on the pipe string and
may impede pipe pulling or damage the pipeline
Because of the above reasons we believe that installation of the new sewer line
using horizontal directional drilling may not be a viable option
Open Trench Method
Conventional open trench method is a viable option and may be used for the
installation of the proposed sewer line We envision that the invert depths of the new
sewer line will range from about 3 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface Based
on our field exploration groundwater was encountered at depths ranging between
5 5 and 8 4 feet below the existing ground surface
Excavation Method
Based on our exploration medium stiff sandy silts and clays and medium dense
silty sands were encountered within the anticipated sewer line invert depths It is
anticipated that conventional excavation techniques may be used for the
proposed excavations However it should be noted that existing abandoned
underground utilities and other below grade concrete structures may exist at the
site and would require heavy equipment excavation In general the method and
equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by the contractor
subject to practical limits and safety considerations The excavated soils from the
trench excavation should be stockpiled no closer than a horizontal distance equal to
the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential
for ground movement
Excavation Support
As previously mentioned we envision that the new sewer line inverts will range
between about 4 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface Because the new
sewer line will be along the roadway sloping of the sides of the trenches may not
be possible due to the limited space Because medium stiff silts and clays and
medium dense silty sands were encountered in our borings shoring of the sides
of the trenches will be necessary to facilitate the proposed trench excavations
and to protect personnel working in the trench

Excavation support such as interlocking sheet piles may be used to shore the
trench excavations The excavation support and shoring system used must comply
with all applicable safety requirements It is recommended that a qualified
geotechnical engineer be retained by the contractor to design and evaluate the
shoring system used The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be
made the sole responsibility of the contractor
Even if good construction procedures are followed some movement of the shoring
system and the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses
during excavation Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to
estimate accurately the magnitude of ground movement The magnitude of ground
movement also depends greatly upon workmanship such as how quickly and
tightly the shoring supports are installed the subsoil conditions the size of the
excavation and the rate of excavation Therefore it is important to realize that the
excavation shoring should be installed properly and as early as practical and if
necessary the adjacent ground should be continuously monitored for cracks dips
and or other indications of movements
Groundwater
Based on our field exploration groundwater was encountered at depths ranging
from about 5 5 to 8 4 feet below the existing ground surface Based on the
envisioned sewer line invert depths we anticipate that groundwater may be
encountered within the trench excavation depths Therefore dewatering of the
proposed trenches may be necessary in some areas to facilitate the proposed
installation of the sewer lines
In general the dewatering operation should be conducted in such a manner that
the dewatering will not cause areal ground subsidence which may cause
potential damage to the nearby existing structures Therefore consideration
should be given to a dewatering system that includes a deep cut off wall to
reduce the volume of water to be removed within the excavation and to reduce
the areal extent of groundwater drawdown outside of the trench excavation
Based on our borings the near surface soils consists of medium stiff sandy silts
and clays and medium dense silty sands underlain by very loose to loose sands
and gravel The actual subsoil permeability may range broadly and also vary
locally in terms of orders of magnitude The permeability of the silts and clays at
the site may be considered slightly permeable and the permeability of the sands
and gravel may be considered moderately permeable Therefore the contractor
should pay special attention to the site specific dewatering plan for the proposed
excavations Percolation test were performed in the clayey sands and sandy
clays encountered in our borings Based on our test results the hydraulic

Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a great effect upon the load
supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore pipe bedding is an important design
consideration In general a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation or similar free draining material is
recommended under the pipes and manhole structures located over medium
stiff medium dense soil conditions If soft and or loose soils are encountered at the
invert elevations of the utility lines and manhole structures we recommend that an
additional 24 inches of No 3B Fine gravel wrapped in a filter fabric Mirafi 180N or
equivalent be provided below the new pipe and manhole structures for more
uniform support under such conditions The granular bedding should extend
beyond the sides of the pipe a minimum width of one fourth 1 4 the outside pipe
diameter A typical section of the trench detail is presented on Plate 3 Before the
placement of bedding material the excavated trench bottom should be observed by
a representative from Geolabs to confirm whether firm materials are exposed at the
bottom of the trench or if the bedding should be extended deeper as recommended
above
Backfill
In general trench backfill should be performed in accordance with the Standard
Specifications for Public Works Construction Department of Public Works City and
County of Honolulu September 1986 Free draining granular materials such as
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be used for the trench
backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around
the pipes and to reduce compaction of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of
damaging the pipes Compaction by water ponding or jetting should not be allowed
for this project Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and
damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on site soils with particles
less than 6 inches in maximum dimension or select fill material

compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction
