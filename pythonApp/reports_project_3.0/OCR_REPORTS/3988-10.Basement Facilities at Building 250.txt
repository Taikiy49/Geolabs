INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Basement Facilities at Building 250 in the Makalapa Naval Housing area
of Pearl Harbor on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for
the proposed project These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations
retaining structures and associated site preparation only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3988 10 Page 5
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lava banking
against its eastern flank
After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which erosion incised deep valleys into
the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and
tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series Due to
the interaction of groundwater with the rising magma many of the eruptions resulted in
steam explosions that expelled large quantities of pyroclastic material consisting
predominantly of ash and cinder Some of these eruptions produced ash that cemented
together rapidly to form a firm rock known as tuff locally known as mudrock This type of
cemented material underlies the proposed project site starting at depths of about 2 5 to
10 feet below the ground surface Man made fills cover the mudrock to the existing ground
elevations

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling five borings designated as
Boring Nos 1 through 5 at the proposed project site extending to depths of about 25 to
40 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Boring Nos 1 through 3 were drilled in the general vicinity of the proposed basement
facilities In general our field exploration encountered a surface layer of fill generally
consisting of silty sand or silty gravel The fill varied in relative density from generally dense
near the surface in Boring No 3 to very loose in Boring No 1 The thickness of the surface
fill layer varied from about 3 to 10 feet Underlying the surface fill layer was brownish gray
slightly weathered volcanic tuff which is locally known as mudrock The tuff was generally
in a medium hard to hard condition and extended to the maximum depth drilled of about
40 5 feet
Boring Nos 4 and 5 which were drilled in the area of the property retaining wall
generally encountered a thin layer of fill consisting of a mixture of silty sands and gravel The
fill extended to depths of about 4 to 6 feet below the existing ground surface Medium dense
to dense silty sands sandy gravel highly weathered volcanic tuff mudrock were encountered
below the fill and extended to the maximum depth drilled of up to about 29 5 feet

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered a near surface fill layer
overlying medium hard to hard volcanic tuff mudrock formation in the vicinity of the
proposed new basement facility structures In general the near surface fill layer consists of
granular soils and varies from loose to medium dense in relative density The near surface
fill layer varies from about 3 to 10 feet in thickness A relatively thin layer of fill and highly
weathered volcanic tuff underlie the area of the new northeast property retaining wall
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings at the time of our field exploration
Based on our field exploration we believe that the proposed basement facility
structures elevator stairway structure mechanical electrical room and new concrete
retaining wall may be supported on a shallow foundation system bearing on the medium
hard to hard undisturbed volcanic tuff mudrock formation We anticipate that in order for
the foundations to bear directly on the volcanic tuff formation excavations on the order of
about 15 to 20 feet below the existing ground surface followed by subsequent backfilling will
be required for the project construction It should also be noted that the location of the
volcanic tuff formation might have been altered by the previous construction Therefore the
depth to the top of the volcanic tuff formation may be highly variable across the site due to
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3988 10 Page 8
previous excavations and construction at the site In addition due to previous construction
in the area some construction debris may be anticipated in the excavations made within the
project area
In general the excavated on site granular soils may be re used as a source of
controlled fill materials It should be emphasized that backfill behind the retaining walls
should be placed and compacted to the recommendations provided herein Special attention
should be given to avoid over compaction of the retaining wall backfill Our geotechnical
engineering recommendations pertaining to the design of foundations retaining structures
and site preparation are further discussed in the following sections of this report

Tie Rods and Deadman Anchors
Tie rods and deadman anchors will be required to provide the necessary lateral
restraint for the new reinforced concrete retaining wall and to reduce the lateral
deformation of the wall We understand that the tie rods and deadman anchors are
currently planned at about 8 feet below the finished grades We anticipate that the
deadman anchors will be located in the granular fill layer above the volcanic tuff
mudrock formation Based on these soil conditions medium dense to dense
granular soils we recommend that the following design parameters be used for
sizing the deadman anchors
The ultimate resistance of the deadman anchors may be estimated based on a
coefficient of friction of 0 35 between the base and top of the deadman anchors
and the surrounding soil In addition passive earth pressure equivalent to a fluid
weighing 350 pcf and an active pressure equivalent to a fluid pressure of 35 pef
may be used to evaluate and size the deadman anchor In order for the anchorage
system to develop the full capacity the anchor must be located a sufficient distance
behind the retaining wall such that the resisting anchor passive wedge does not
intersect the active soil wedge behind the retaining wall as illustrated on Plate 3
An effective friction angle of 34 degrees may be used to estimate the active and
passive zones for the design of deadman anchors
Drainage
In general retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations facing down at the base of the
wall discharging to an appropriate outlet As an alternative a prefabricated drainage
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3988 10 Page 13
product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used instead of the drainage
material The prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically connected
to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall
We recognize that it will be very difficult to drain the retaining walls for the basement
facilities Considering the fairly permeable volcanic tuff mudrock formation
anticipated at the site we believe that the likelinood of hydrostatic pressure build up
should be low Therefore we believe that the recommended subdrainage system
may be omitted for the basement retaining walls which will be difficult to drain
However it should be noted that some seepage water is expected in the volcanic tuff
mudrock formation Therefore special attention should be given to waterproofing
of the basement retaining walls
Site Preparation
Based on the plans for the proposed basement facilities project we anticipate that
minimal site grading work will be required for construction of the project In general we
envision that relatively deep excavations followed by a significant amount of backfill
placement will be required for the project construction
At the on set of earthwork the areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be
removed and disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas encountered during
clearing below areas designated to receive backfill should be over excavated to expose
firm natural material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted
fill or controlled fill The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed
of off site or used in landscaping areas if appropriate
Slabs and concrete foundations of the existing structures to be demolished should be
completely removed Over excavation resulting from the demolition should be backfilled with
controlled fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the
resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with controlled fill material

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
five borings extending to depths of about 25 to 40 5 feet below the existing ground surface
at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using
a truck mounted drill rig and portable drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight
augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler or a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler
with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler
the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
