This technical memorandum presents our preliminary geotechnical
recommendations based on our recently completed field exploration for the above project,
pending our ongoing laboratory testing program. A detailed summary of our
recommendations will be contained in our geotechnical engineering report. The report
should be consulted when it becomes available.
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling four borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, extending to depths of
about 16.5 to 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface.
Our borings generally encountered a pavement section consisting of about 4.5 to
7 inches of asphaltic concrete (AC) pavement overlying 5 to 11 inches of granular base
material. The pavement section on the western half of Paiko Drive was generally
underlain by fill material consisting of loose sands extending to depths of about 3 to 5 feet.
We encountered loose to medium dense beach deposits consisting of coralline sand
extending to depths of about 3 to 5 feet on the eastern half of Paiko Drive. Beneath the
fill and beach deposits, lagoonal deposits were encountered, extending to the maximum
depth explored of about 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The lagoonal
deposits generally consisted of very loose to medium dense silty/gravelly sand and sandy
gravel, and very soft sandy silt.
Groundwater was encountered in the drilled borings at about 1.6 to 3.6 feet below
the existing ground surface. Due to the proximity of the project site to the Pacific Ocean,
groundwater levels are expected to vary with tidal fluctuation. In addition, groundwater
levels may change due to storm surge conditions, seasonal precipitation, surface water
runoff, and other factors.
TRENCH EXCAVATION
Based on the information provided, we understand that the new water main pipe
invert levels will be about 4 to 5 feet below the existing pavement surface. Installation of
the proposed water main will involve excavation into the underlying fills, beach deposits,
and lagoonal deposits. In general, we anticipate that the in-situ soils along the proposed
water main alignments may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment.

TRENCH EXCAVATION SUPPORT 
We anticipate excavation depths of up to about 4 to 8 feet below the pavement 
surface will be required to install the new water main (anticipated water main and manhole 
invert depths plus over-excavation for stabilization layers described in subsequent 
sections). Where deeper excavations (greater than 5 feet in depth) are planned, 
temporary shoring or sloping and benching should be implemented for the trench 
excavations. Based on our site observations, we believe that temporary cut slopes for 
open-cut excavations are not practical. In this case, trench walls would have to be cut 
vertically, necessitating the use of shoring during construction. 
One possible method of shoring would be to use interlocking sheet pile shoring. 
The use of a sheet pile shoring system may also serve as a cut-off wall to aid in the 
dewatering operations, which is further discussed in the “Dewatering” section herein. The 
sheet piles should be driven with a suitable hammer to a sufficient depth to reduce the 
potential for areal ground subsidence and to reduce the amount of dewatering within the 
excavations due to the relatively permeable nature of the subsoils. It should be noted that 
the excavations will likely encounter very soft/loose soil deposits at the bottom of the 
excavation. Therefore, there is potential for bottom heave in these very soft/loose soil 
conditions. The contractor should carefully evaluate the potential for bottom heave and 
design the shoring system accordingly. 
It is important to install adequate sheeting prior to the excavation and to maintain 
it tightly against the excavation walls with proper bracing during excavation. The properly 
braced sheeting is essential to reduce appreciable lateral movements of the adjacent 
ground into the excavation, which may result in potential settlements or distress to 
adjacent structures or other improvements, such as roads or utilities. 
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable 
safety requirements. The contractor should be solely responsible for trench excavation 
and the adequacy and safety of the shoring installation. His/her representative, who 
should be required to be continuously present on-site during excavation and construction 
work, will have the best opportunity to promptly observe changing conditions during 
construction, such as unforeseen subsoils, unexpectedly high groundwater table,

DEWATERING 
Dewatering of excavations will be necessary where the existing groundwater level 
is above the bottom of the proposed excavation. Groundwater was encountered at depths 
ranging from about 1.6 to 3.8 feet below the existing ground surface in the borings drilled 
during our field exploration. Based on the groundwater levels anticipated and the 
permeable nature of the granular soils expected to be encountered in the excavations, 
dewatering will be required at the planned excavations of the project construction. 
Therefore, dewatering provisions will need to be included in the contract documents for 
the proposed construction. Since the excavation will involve the discharge of 
groundwater, an NPDES permit will be required for this discharge. Groundwater sampling 
and testing should be performed for the project in support of the NPDES permit 
application. The results of the baseline groundwater testing will be provided in the 
geotechnical engineering report. 
It is anticipated that the granular soils encountered are relatively permeable. 
Therefore, dewatering of the excavation may involve moderate to large volumes of water. 
Dewatering by means of a well point system along the outside of the excavations is not 
recommended. The resultant areal depression of the natural groundwater table could 
induce consolidation of the compressible subsoils resulting in potential ground 
settlements, which could affect nearby existing structures and utility lines. The potential

groundwater control requirement should be specified in the contract documents. 
The contractor is responsible for dewatering for construction. The contractor 
should select equipment and methods of dewatering, and the contractor should be aware 
that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during construction, 
depending on the conditions encountered. The dewatering method selected should have 
minimal impact on the groundwater level surrounding the proposed excavation. The 
dewatering operations should be coordinated with the trench shoring support such that 
the stability of the excavations is not jeopardized. The operations should be carried out 
without softening the bottom of the excavations. 
It is our opinion that the definition of "Dewatering" in the contract documents should 
be written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural groundwater table 
and/or to exclude water from the excavations to allow construction of the proposed water 
mains under safe and dry conditions. These works or systems may include, but are not 
limited to, pumping, grouting, cut-off walls, cofferdams, tremie plugs to resist buoyant 
forces or any combination of the above or other possible methods.

Dewatering Considerations 
We suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in the selection of 
a suitable method of dewatering: 
a. 
The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or damage to 
existing buildings, roads, and the environment. 
b. 
The dewatering method should maintain the stability of, and also provide 
safe, and dry working conditions in the excavation. 
c. 
The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow modifications 
to accommodate various ground conditions.

Dewatering Precautions and Monitoring 
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural 
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to dewatering. The 
contractor should retain a qualified geotechnical engineer to design and evaluate the 
dewatering system used. 
The contractor should be solely responsible for the impact and safety of the 
dewatering operations. His/her qualified representative, who should be continuously 
present on-site during dewatering activities, will have the best opportunity to promptly 
observe the effects of dewatering during construction and to implement, as soon as 
possible, necessary precautionary or remedial measures including, but not limited to, 
slowing down or stopping the dewatering operations. 
Where encountered at the bottom of excavations, permeable granular subsoils 
may be susceptible to piping and "quick" conditions. The dewatering operations should 
be carried-out without creating a "quick" condition or softening at the excavation bottoms. 
Therefore, the project dewatering operations should be performed without pumping out 
soil fines (pumping clear water only) and should be coordinated with the shoring 
installation such that the excavation stability is not adversely affected. Excessive 
pumping, which removes soil fines, may result in "blowing" or heaving of the excavation 
bottom or sides. 
Groundwater drawdown outside the excavation will cause additional settlement 
resulting from the consolidation of the soft/loose compressible soils. Therefore, the use 
of a deep well system outside the excavations to draw down the groundwater level should 
not be allowed. 
Special caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering existing utility trenches 
connected to the excavations. If this occurs, the granular bedding and/or backfill in the 
utility trenches could act as subdrains and cause significant areal groundwater drawdown. 
Significant areal groundwater drawdown would result in ground settlements and potential 
damage to utility lines and/or other existing adjacent structures.

PIPE CUSHION 
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the 
load-supporting capacity of the pipe. Therefore, the pipe cushion is an important design 
consideration. Based on our field exploration, we anticipate that the new water main will 
be bearing on the relatively loose fill, beach deposits, and lagoonal deposits encountered 
in our borings. Therefore, a stabilization layer consisting of 24 inches of open-graded 
gravel, such as No. 3B Fine gravel wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or

TRENCH BACKFILL 
In general, the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the Water 
System Standards, State of Hawaii 2002. The first zone of backfill extending from the 
cushion material to at least 12 inches above the top of the pipes should consist of select 
free-draining granular material (ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation) to reduce the compaction 
effort required and resulting stresses on the pipe. In addition, it is critical to use this 
free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches 
of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench 
backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the pavement subgrade should consist of the excavated on-site surface fill soils and 
beach deposits or imported fill materials that are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, 
organic material, and clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum 
dimension. It should be noted that the fill requirements presented herein are intended as 
guidelines only and may be modified based on Geolabs additional laboratory testing and 
field observations on the available fill materials. 
Imported fill materials used for trench backfill should be well-graded from coarse 
to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension. The material should 
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material 
should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher and a swell 
potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. Geolabs 
should test the imported fill materials for conformance with these recommendations. 
As an alternative, to facilitate the backfilling and reduce the time required for 
applying compactive effort, Lightweight Cellular Concrete (LWWC) may be used to 
backfill the trenches. The LWWC shall be Class III and designed to be tremied underwater 
and have an in-place density between 65 and 85 pcf, and a strength between 150 and 
250 psi. The use of LWWC would also reduce the potential for future settlement of the 
trench backfill under the repeated wheel loading from traffic on the roadway.

TRENCH BACKFILL COMPACTION AND PLACEMENT REQUIREMENTS 
Backfill materials consisting of the on-site surface fill soils and beach deposits 
should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not 
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction. The upper 3 feet below the pavement grade should be compacted 
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
Imported fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture, placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Aggregate base course materials should be 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not 
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by 
dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or 
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or ponding 
should not be allowed to compact the excavated surface on-site fill soils or imported fill 
materials. 
Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during backfilling to 
assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper 
moisture content. Where compaction is less than required, additional compactive effort 
should be applied with adjustment of moisture content as necessary to obtain the 
specified compaction.  
BELOW-GRADE STRUCTURES 
We understand that below-grade structures, consisting of manholes, are planned 
for the project. Based on the planned invert elevations (about 5 to 6 feet below the existing 
ground surface), we anticipate the new below-grade structures may bear on the 
underlying compressible lagoonal deposits encountered during our field exploration.  
We recommend the bottom of the below-grade structures bear on a bedding layer 
consisting of 6 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) over a 
stabilization layer. To reduce the potential for settlement of the below-grade structures, 
we recommend over-excavating the soft/loose deposits a minimum depth of 2 feet below 
the bottom of the bedding layer or until firm material is encountered. The over-excavation 
should be backfilled with a stabilization layer consisting of No. 2 Coarse aggregate 
wrapped in a woven geotextile (Mirafi RS580i or equal). In addition, the 24-inch-thick 
stabilization layer should extend at least 2 feet beyond the edges of the structures.

THRUST BLOCKS 
We envision that thrust blocks will be required at some locations to provide resistance 
to the thrust forces due to the pressurized pipes. The thrust force acting on the pipes may 
be resisted by passive earth pressure acting against the face of the thrust block. We 
anticipate that most of the planned inverts will be about 4 to 5 feet below the existing 
pavement surface.  
Based on our field exploration, very loose to loose sands and gravels were generally 
encountered throughout the depths explored. We recommend using a lateral bearing 
pressure of 500 pounds per square foot (psf) for the design of thrust blocks bearing in the 
very loose to loose sands and gravels underlying the site. 
SETTLEMENTS 
Primary settlement of the water mains is normally caused by the difference in the 
unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted backfill 
material placed over the pipes. The net increase in loading will cause settlement of the 
underlying subsoils below the trench invert. Based on our engineering analyses, 
settlement on the order of less than 1 inch is anticipated for the project. 
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good 
workmanship will be engaged during construction. Additional settlement could occur if 
improper trench support is used.
