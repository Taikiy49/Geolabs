SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered a pavement structure consisting of about 
4.5 to 7 inches of asphaltic concrete overlying 5 to 11 inches of granular base material. The 
pavement section on the western half of Paiko Drive was generally underlain by fill material 
consisting of loose sands extending to a depth of about 5 feet. We encountered loose to 
medium dense beach deposits consisting of coralline sand extending to depths of about 3 to 
5 feet on the eastern half of Paiko Drive. Beneath the fill and beach deposits, lagoonal 
deposits were encountered, extending to the maximum depth explored of about 21.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The lagoonal deposits generally consisted of very loose 
to loose silty/gravelly sand and silty gravel, and very soft sandy silt. 
Groundwater was encountered in the drilled borings at about 1.6 to 3.6 feet below 
the existing ground surface, corresponding to elevations of about +0.1 to +1.7 feet MSL. 
Due to the proximity of the project site to the Pacific Ocean, groundwater levels are expected 
to vary with tidal fluctuation. In addition, groundwater levels may change due to storm surge 
conditions, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
We anticipate that the majority of the water main inverts will be on the order of about 
4 to 5 feet below the existing pavement surface. Installation of the proposed water mains 
will involve excavation into the underlying fills, coralline material, and lagoonal deposits. In 
general, we anticipate that the in-situ soils along the proposed water main alignments may 
be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment. 
Where deep excavations (greater than 5 feet in depth) are planned, temporary 
shoring or sloping and benching should be implemented for the trench excavations. Based 
on our site observations, we believe that temporary cut slopes for open-cut excavations are 
not practical. In this case, trench walls would have to be cut near vertical necessitating the 
use of shoring during construction. One possible method of shoring would be to use 
interlocking sheet pile shoring. Use of a sheet pile shoring system may also serve as a 
cut-off wall to aid in the dewatering operations.  
Dewatering of excavations will be necessary where the existing groundwater level is 
above the bottom of the proposed excavation. Dewatering of the excavation may involve 
moderate to large volumes of water. Based on the results of our field permeability tests, the 
measured permeability of the on-site soils range from about 0.013 to 0.16 centimeters per 
second.  The actual subsurface soil permeability may range broadly and vary locally in terms 
of the orders of magnitude.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Paiko Drive 8-Inch Main project in the Hawaii Kai area of Honolulu on 
the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering 
analyses for the water main project. These recommendations are intended for the design 
of trench excavation, dewatering, backfilling, thrust block/vault design, corrosion 
potential, and pavement restoration only. The findings and recommendations presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from 
two shield volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to 
be middle to late Pliocene in age, and the Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene 
to early Pleistocene in age. 
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of the Koolau Range, and its 
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to the glacio-eustatic 
fluctuations of sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age).  The base of the 
stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt.  The Koolau volcanic shield was built 
by thinly bedded lava flows during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epochs.  The 
main shield-building activities ceased approximately 2½ million years ago. 
After a long period of cessation of the shield building, volcanic activity returned 
with the formation of a series of lava flows, cinder cones, and tuff cones, such as those 
associated with Koko Crater.  Many of the eruptions were rather explosive in nature due 
to the interaction of groundwater with the rising magma, resulting in steam explosions, 
which expelled large quantities of pyroclastic material, predominantly ash and cinder. 
Some explosive eruptions produced ash that cemented together to form a firm rock known 
as tuff, locally known as “mudrock.” 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation.  As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill 
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea.  When 
the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposition of lagoonal/marine sediments in 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs.  The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and

2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is located along Paiko Drive beginning near the intersection with 
Kalanianaole Highway and ending at a private road on the southeastern end. Based on 
our field observations, Paiko Drive is a relatively narrow, un-striped asphaltic concrete 
roadway. We observed street parked cars along either side of Paiko Drive and overhead 
utility lines on the north side of the road. 
Based on the topographic survey map provided, Paiko Drive slopes down gently 
from northwest to southeast with existing ground elevations of about +6 feet Mean Sea 
Level (MSL) near Kalanianaole Highway to about +2.2 feet MSL near the southeast 
terminus of the road.

Trench Excavation

invert levels will be about 4 to 5 feet below the existing pavement surface. Installation of 
the proposed water main will involve excavation into the underlying fills, beach deposits, 
and lagoonal deposits. In general, we anticipate that the in-situ soils along the proposed 
water main alignments may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment. 
Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal 
to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential for 
excessive ground movement. In addition, the excavated soils should be stockpiled at least

3.2 
Trench Excavation Support 
We anticipate excavation depths of up to about 4 to 8 feet below the pavement 
surface will be required to install the new water main (anticipated water main and manhole 
invert depths plus over-excavation for stabilization layers described in subsequent 
sections). Where deeper excavations (greater than 5 feet in depth) are planned, 
temporary shoring or sloping and benching should be implemented for the trench 
excavations. Based on our site observations, we believe that temporary cut slopes for 
open-cut excavations are not practical. In this case, trench walls would have to be cut 
near vertical, necessitating the use of shoring during construction. 
One possible method of shoring would be to use interlocking sheet pile shoring. 
The use of a sheet pile shoring system may also serve as a cut-off wall to aid in the 
dewatering operations, which is further discussed in the “Dewatering” section herein. The 
sheet piles should be driven with a suitable hammer as discussed below to a sufficient 
depth to reduce the potential for areal ground subsidence and to reduce the amount of 
dewatering within the excavations due to the relatively permeable nature of the subsoils. 
It should be noted that the excavations will likely encounter very soft/loose soil deposits 
at the bottom of the excavation. Therefore, there is potential for bottom heave in these 
very soft/loose soil conditions. The contractor should carefully evaluate the potential for 
bottom heave and design the shoring system accordingly. 
It is important to install adequate sheeting prior to the excavation and to maintain 
it tightly against the excavation walls with proper bracing during excavation. The properly 
braced sheeting is essential to reduce appreciable lateral movements of the adjacent 
ground into the excavation, which may result in potential settlements or distress to 
adjacent structures or other improvements, such as roads or utilities.

The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable 
safety requirements. The contractor should be solely responsible for trench excavation 
and the adequacy and safety of the shoring installation. His/her representative, who 
should be required to be continuously present on-site during excavation and construction 
work, will have the best opportunity to promptly observe changing conditions during 
construction, such as unforeseen subsoils, unexpectedly high groundwater table, 
inappropriate construction sequence or techniques, etc., which may affect shoring 
stability. 
However, it must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and 
the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation. 
Due to the complexity of the stress changes, it is difficult to accurately estimate the 
magnitude of movement. The magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship, such 
as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are installed, the subsoil 
conditions, the size of the excavation, and the rate of excavation. In addition, it should be 
noted that settlement of the existing ground may occur as a result of the vibrations 
generated during the extraction of the sheet pile shoring. Therefore, the contractor should 
give special attention during the sheet pile removal process to reduce the potential for 
appreciable ground settlement. 
A vibratory hammer for sheetpile installation and removal should not be used due 
to the potential for liquefaction to occur in the loose deposits. The use of an impact 
hammer should be considered. 
It is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed properly 
and as early as practical, if necessary, and that the adjacent ground should be 
continuously monitored for cracks, dips, and/or other indications of movements with 
instruments.

is above the bottom of the proposed excavation. Groundwater was encountered at depths 
ranging from about 1.6 to 3.6 feet below the existing ground surface in the borings drilled 
during our field exploration. Based on the groundwater levels anticipated and the

dewatering will be required at the planned excavations of the project construction. 
Therefore, dewatering provisions will need to be included in the contract documents for 
the proposed construction. Since the excavation will involve the discharge of

It is anticipated that the granular soils encountered are relatively permeable. 
Therefore, dewatering of the excavation may involve moderate to large volumes of water. 
Dewatering by means of a well point system along the outside of the excavations is not 
recommended. The resultant areal depression of the natural groundwater table could 
induce consolidation of the compressible subsoils resulting in potential ground 
settlements, which could affect nearby existing structures and utility lines. The potential 
impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural groundwater table 
must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to dewatering. 
It is our opinion that a cut-off wall system, such as interlocking sheet piles, should 
be considered to aid in dewatering the excavation. However, sumps will be needed to 
collect water that percolates up into the base of the excavation or infiltrates through the 
sheet piles. The sheet piles should be driven to a sufficient depth to reduce the potential 
for areal ground subsidence and to reduce the amount of dewatering within the 
excavations in areas underlain by granular subsoils with generally high permeability. The 
use of an interlocking sheet pile shoring support system with a tremie concrete plug is 
relatively watertight, which should allow the groundwater levels outside the excavations 
to be maintained close to the original pre-construction levels. Therefore, some type of 
groundwater control requirement should be specified in the contract documents. 
The contractor is responsible for dewatering for construction. The contractor 
should select equipment and methods of dewatering, and the contractor should be aware 
that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during construction, 
depending on the conditions encountered. The dewatering method selected should have

 Dewatering Considerations 
We suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in selection of a 
suitable method of dewatering: 
a. 
The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or 
damage to existing buildings, roads, and environment. 
b. 
The dewatering method should maintain stability of, and also provide 
safe and dry working conditions in, the excavation. 
c. 
The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow 
modifications to accommodate various ground conditions. 
 Dewatering Precaution and Monitoring 
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural 
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to 
dewatering. The contractor should retain a qualified geotechnical engineer to 
design and evaluate the dewatering system used.

Where encountered at the bottom of excavations, permeable granular subsoils

should be carried-out without creating a "quick" condition or softening at the

performed without pumping out soil fines (pumping clear water only) and should

adversely affected. Excessive pumping, which removes soil fines, may result in 
"blowing" or heaving of the excavation bottom or sides. 
Groundwater drawdown outside the excavation will cause additional settlement 
resulting from the consolidation of the soft/loose compressible soils. Therefore, the 
use of a deep well system outside the excavations to draw down the groundwater 
level should not be allowed. 
Special caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering existing utility trenches 
connected to the excavations. If this occurs, the granular bedding and/or backfill in 
the utility trenches could act as subdrains and cause significant areal groundwater 
drawdown. Significant areal groundwater drawdown would result in ground 
settlements and potential damage to utility lines and/or other existing adjacent 
structures.

3.6 
Pipe Cushion 
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the 
load-supporting capacity of the pipe. Therefore, the pipe cushion is an important design 
consideration. Based on our field exploration, we anticipate that the new water main will 
be bearing on the relatively loose fill, beach deposits, and lagoonal deposits encountered 
in our borings. Therefore, a stabilization layer consisting of 24 inches of open-graded 
gravel, such as No. 2 Coarse Aggregate (ASTM C33, No. 4 Gradation) wrapped in a 
non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) should be provided. Above the 
stabilization layer, a minimum of 6 inches of compacted granular cushion material, such 
as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation) should be provided below the pipe 
for uniform support. 
The granular cushion layer should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width 
of one-fourth (¼) of the outside pipe diameter, or trench width, whichever is greater, and 
should extend 12 inches above the pipe. Before the placement of cushion material, a

3.7 
Trench Backfill 
In general, the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the BWS Water 
System Standards, State of Hawaii 2002 (amended version). The first zone of backfill 
extending from the cushion material to at least 12 inches above the top of the pipes should 
consist of select free-draining granular material (ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation) to reduce 
the compaction effort required and resulting stresses on the pipe. In addition, it is critical 
to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the 
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper 
trench backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the pavement subgrade should consist of the excavated on-site surface fill soils and 
beach deposits or imported fill materials that are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, 
organic material, and clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum 
dimension. It should be noted that the fill requirements presented herein are intended as 
guidelines only and may be modified based on Geolabs additional laboratory testing and 
field observations on the available fill materials. 
Imported fill materials used for trench backfill should be well-graded from coarse 
to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension. The material should 
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material 
should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher and a swell 
potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. Geolabs 
should test the imported fill materials for conformance with these recommendations. 
As an alternative, to facilitate the backfilling and reduce the time required for 
applying compactive effort, Lightweight Cellular Concrete (LWCC) may be used to backfill 
the trenches. The LWCC shall be Class III and designed to be tremied underwater and 
have an in-place density between 65 and 85 pounds per square foot per foot of depth 
(pcf), and a strength between 150 and 250 pounds per square inch (psi). The use of

3.8 
Trench Backfill Compaction and Placement Requirements 
Backfill materials consisting of the on-site surface fill soils and beach deposits 
should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not 
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction. The upper 3 feet below the pavement grade should be compacted 
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
Imported fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture, placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Aggregate base course materials should be 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not 
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by 
dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or 
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or ponding 
should not be allowed to compact the excavated surface on-site fill soils or imported fill 
materials. 
Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during backfilling to 
assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper 
moisture content. Where compaction is less than required, additional compactive effort 
should be applied with adjustment of moisture content as necessary to obtain the 
specified compaction.

3.10 Below-Grade Structures 
We understand that below-grade structures, consisting of manholes, are planned 
for the project. Based on the planned invert elevations (about 5 to 6 feet below the existing 
ground surface), we anticipate the new below-grade structures may bear on the 
underlying compressible lagoonal deposits encountered during our field exploration.  
We recommend the bottom of the below-grade structures bear on a bedding layer 
consisting of 6 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) over a 
stabilization layer. To reduce the potential for settlement of the below-grade structures, 
we recommend over-excavating the soft/loose deposits a minimum depth of 2 feet below 
the bottom of the bedding layer or until firm material is encountered. The over-excavation 
should be backfilled with a stabilization layer consisting of No. 2 Coarse aggregate 
wrapped in a woven geotextile (Mirafi RS580i or equal). In addition, the 24-inch thick 
stabilization layer should extend at least 2 feet beyond the edges of the structures. 
Based on our engineering analyses, it is our opinion that the net increase in bearing 
pressure on the underlying soil deposits resulting from the below-grade structure 
construction would be minimal. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,000 pounds per 
square foot (psf) may be used for the design of the below-grade structures bearing on the 
stabilization layer. The bearing value recommended is for dead-plus-live loads and may 
be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic 
forces. If the below-grade structures are designed and constructed in accordance with 
the recommendations presented herein, we estimate that settlements on the order of 
1 inch or less may be anticipated for the below-grade structures.

3.12 Settlements 
Primary settlement of the water mains is normally caused by the difference in the 
unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted backfill 
material placed over the pipes. The net increase in loading will cause settlement of the 
underlying subsoils below the trench invert. Based on our engineering analyses, 
settlement on the order of less than 1 inch is anticipated for the project. 
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good 
workmanship will be engaged during construction. Additional settlement could occur if 
improper trench support is used.

Design of metallic substructures, such as waterline pipes or other metallic piping,

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling four borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, to depths of about 16.5 and 21.5 feet below the 
existing pavement surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 
through 2.3. We used a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers to 
advance the borings. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-4. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, Penetration 
Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration 
sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler 
the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Five Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. 
Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on Plate B-1. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested.  Results of the direct 
shear tests are presented on Plates B-2 and B-3. 
Two sets of corrosivity tests, pH (Method 9045C), Minimum Resistivity 
(SM 2510B), Chloride Content (EPA 300) and Sulfate Content (EPA 300), were 
performed by TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on selected soil samples obtained from our 
field exploration. Also, two additional Minimum Resistivity (ASTM G57) tests were 
performed by Geolabs, Inc. The test results are presented on Plate B-4. 
One Modified Proctor compaction test (ASTM D1557B) was performed on bulk 
sample of the near-surface soils and potential borrow material to evaluate the dry density 
and moisture content relationships. The test results are presented on Plate B-5. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTMD1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-6 and B-7.

Field Permeability Tests 
The permeability of the near-surface materials at the project site was evaluated by 
performing constant and falling head permeability tests in each boring at different depths at 
the approximate locations shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 through 2.3. The tests were 
performed at about 5, 10 and 15 feet below the existing ground surface in Boring Nos. 1, 3 
and 4.  In Boring No. 2, the tests were performed at about 5.5, 10.42, 15.5 and 20.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. 
During the constant head test, a water pump was used to pump clear water into the 
boring. The flow rate was recorded by timing and calculating the volume as a relatively 
constant flow rate was achieved. Water could not be pumped slow enough to establish a 
constant head for Boring No. 1 at 5, 10 and 15 feet, Boring No. 2 at 5.5, 15.5 and 20.5 feet, 
Boring No. 3 at 5 feet, and Boring No. 4 at 5, 10 and 15 feet.  
During the falling head test, the test hole was initially saturated. After saturation, 
the falling head percolation test was performed by filling up the test boring with clear 
water. Time intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which 
calculation of the permeability values were based.  
The constant head and falling head permeability test data are presented on 
Plates D-1 through D-17.

Narrative
Receipt 
The samples were received on 3/21/2023 9:40 AM. Unless otherwise noted below, the samples arrived in good condition, and where 
required, properly preserved and on ice. The temperature of the cooler at receipt was 20.6º C.
Receipt Exceptions
The samples were received outside of required temperature criteria at 20.6º C. There were no cooling media present.
General Chemistry 
Method 300.0: The matrix spike duplicate (MSD) recoveries for analytical batch 320-666942 were outside control limits for sulfate. 
Non-homogeneity is suspected because the associated laboratory control sample (LCS) recovery was within acceptance limits.
Method 300.0: The following sample in analytical batch 320-666942 was diluted to bring the concentration of target analytes within the 
calibration range: B1 RS3 5-6.5FT (320-97957-1) and B3 RS3 5-6.5FT (320-97957-2). Elevated reporting limits (RLs) are provided.
Method 300.0: The following samples in analytical batch 320-666942 were received after the holding time had expired: B1 RS3 5-6.5FT 
(320-97957-1) and B3 RS3 5-6.5FT (320-97957-2). As such, the laboratory had insufficient time remaining to perform the analysis within 
holding time.
No additional analytical or quality issues were noted, other than those described above or in the Definitions/Glossary page.
