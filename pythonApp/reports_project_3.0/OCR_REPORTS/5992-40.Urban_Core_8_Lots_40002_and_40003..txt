REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. 
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene 
(Ice Age) in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island.  As the volcanic 
activity in Waianae Volcano ceased, lava flows from Koolau Volcano banked against its 
eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau, now known as the Schofield Plateau.  The 
project site is located on the southwestern flank of the Waianae Volcano. 
Physical and chemical weathering was followed by erosion of Schofield Plateau 
generated sediments, which were transported to the coast.  In the project vicinity and to 
the south, these sediments accumulated and are interbedded with marine sediments and 
coral/algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge.  The thickness of the 
sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H-1 Highway to 
over 1,000 feet at Ewa Beach.  This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the 
confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu.  
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation.  As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill 
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea.  When the 
glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet 
waters protected by fringing reefs.  The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys 
in the sediments and reef deposits.  Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous 
materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the calcareous

SITE DESCRIPTION 
The proposed Urban Core Roads are in the Kapolei area on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii. The work under this project involves construction of the proposed street extension 
for Road “B” as well as the site grading work at Lots 40002 and 40003. 
Based on our field observations, Lots 40002 and 40003 generally consists of 
vacant land moderately vegetated with small trees, tall grasses, and some soil stockpiles. 
Dry and loose soils were exposed at the existing ground surface with potholes and 
trenches caused by soil shrinkage in the surface clayey soil. 
The northern corner of Lot 40003 is presently occupied by a drainage retention 
basin that is moderately vegetated with tall grasses, shrubs, and small trees.  The 
retention basin is surrounded by a chain-link fence with a double swing gate secured by a 
key padlock.  Based on the grading plans provided, the bottom of the retention basin is at 
an elevation of approximately +44 feet MSL.  The existing ground elevations across the 
remaining project site generally range between about +52 and +70 feet MSL. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Our field exploration program at the project site consisted of excavating and 
sampling six test pits, designated as Test Pit Nos. 1 through 6, extending to depths of 
about 2.5 to 9 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate excavated test pit 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain 
by variable subsurface conditions ranging from soft to stiff sandy clays, soft to medium stiff 
clays, medium dense silty sands, to medium hard to hard coral formation from the existing 
ground surface extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 9 feet below 
the existing ground surface. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the shallow excavated test pits at the time of 
our field exploration. However, groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, 
seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. Detailed descriptions of the 
field exploration methodology are presented in Appendix A of this report. Descriptions and 
graphic representations of the materials encountered in the test pits are provided on the 
Logs of Test Pits, Plates A-1 through A-6.

Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site. Soft 
and/or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill should be over-excavated to expose firm material, and the 
resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fills. The excavated 
soft and/or organic soils should be properly disposed of off-site.  Based on our field 
exploration results, we envision that soft/loose soils on the order of about 2 to 3 feet 
thick may be encountered at the bottom of the retention basin and along the 
drainage swales at the project site. A Geolabs representative should be present 
during the site grading operations to determine the amount of over-excavation 
required due to soft/loose subgrade soil conditions.

Our field observations indicate that shrinkage cracks and ground depressions are 
present at the existing ground surface across the project site. In general, shrinkage 
cracks will develop in the ground due to the lack of maintenance of the soil moisture 
content as the surface soils start to dry out under the sun and dry weather 
conditions. The development of shrinkage cracks in the ground was apparently 
intensified by the prolonged dry climatic conditions characteristic of the Kapolei 
area resulting in large fluctuations of the soil moisture content. 
If the existing ground cracks are left unattended for an extended period of time and 
are allowed to penetrate deeper below the surface, the ground cracks could 
adversely affect the performance of the future improvements. The ground cracks 
could serve as conduits for water to percolate into the building and pavement 
subgrade soils and cause loss of strength of the subgrade soils resulting in 
potential settlement of the building and pavement structures. Therefore, remedial 
work to correct or mitigate the observed ground cracks should be implemented prior 
to fill placement at the project site. 
After clearing and grubbing, the exposed subgrades should be proof-rolled with a 
minimum 10-ton vibratory drum roller for a minimum of six passes to assist in 
detecting and collapsing near-surface voids and loose zones created by the 
shrinkage cracks and ground depressions. The vibratory drum roller should be 
operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute.  The exposed subgrades should 
be proof-rolled without moisture-conditioning of the subgrade soils (at the 
pre-existing moisture contents). Yielding areas, loose areas, or cavities disclosed 
during the proof-rolling operation should be backfilled with general fill material 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. The proof-rolling 
operations should be conducted under the near-continuous observation by our 
representative. 
Subsequent to the proof-rolling operations, the subgrades should then be scarified 
to a minimum depth of 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above 
the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed 
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water content 
(percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Where shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade, we 
recommend scarifying the soils and preparing again as recommended above. 
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement 
weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and 
replacement with well-compacted fill. Contract documents should include additive 
and deductive unit prices for over-excavation and compacted fill placement to 
account for variations in the over-excavation quantities.

We envision the initial site grading work for this project will involve excavation down

exhibit high swelling potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations.  Therefore,

Fills and Backfills 
As mentioned above, the on-site clayey soils generally exhibit high swelling 
potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations.  Therefore, we believe the 
excavated on-site clayey soils (without deleterious materials and/or over-sized 
materials) should be used in backfilling the deeper portions of the retention basin 
and new fill areas. In addition, we anticipate the need for a significant amount of 
imported fill materials for the project. Imported general fill materials should consist 
of materials with particle sizes of 3 inches or less in maximum dimension and 
should be free of deleterious materials. The material should have a laboratory 
California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 12 or higher and a swell potential of 
1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883. 
The excavated on-site coralline materials, where encountered, should be reused as 
a source of non-expansive, select granular capping fill materials over the on-site 
expansive clays and imported general fill materials, provided they are processed to 
meet the following requirements. Non-expansive, select granular fill materials 
should consist of crushed coral or basalt. The material should be well graded from 
coarse to fine with particles no greater than 3 inches in largest dimension. The 
material should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the 
No. 200 sieve. In addition, the material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or 
higher and a swell potential of not more than 1 percent when tested in accordance 
with ASTM D 1883. Geolabs should test the imported fill materials a minimum of 
7 days prior to being transported to the project site for the intended use. 
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
General fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture 
content, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction.  The 
non-expansive select granular fill materials should be placed in level lifts of about 
8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, 
and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction.  Relative compaction 
refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum 
dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557.

Fill Slope Design 
Permanent fill slopes for the project may be designed with a slope inclination of 
two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. Fills to be placed on existing slopes 
with an inclination steeper than 5H:1V should be benched into the existing slope to 
provide stability of the new fill embankment against sliding. The filling operations 
should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers 
in accordance with the fill placement recommendations presented in the “Fill 
Placement and Compaction Requirements” subsection of this report. Fill slopes 
should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio to 
obtain a well-compacted slope face. The fill slope face should be free of voids, 
which would allow erosion and migration of fines to occur. 
Excessive surface water runoff should be diverted away from the tops of slopes, 
and slope planting should be provided as soon as the fill slopes are completed to 
reduce the potential for significant erosion of the finished slopes. Construction of 
earth berms, interceptor drainage ditches, and the use of geotextile fabrics over the 
fill slope face should be considered to reduce the potential for significant erosion, 
thus enhancing the long-term stability of the planned fill slopes.

Cavities and Voids 
Native coralline materials and coral formations generally are highly variable in terms 
of hardness, weathering, and fracturing. In addition, cavities and/or voids are 
encountered commonly in coral formations within the project vicinity. In many 
cases, cavities and voids in the coral formation may be filled with loose coralline 
deposits and/or clayey alluvial soils. In areas where coralline formation is 
anticipated near the ground surface, special attention should be given to the 
subgrade preparation to reduce the potential for distress to the future 
improvements, such as building slabs and pavements. 
In areas where the excavated subgrade exposes coral formation, the excavated 
subgrade should be proof-rolled with a minimum 10-ton vibratory drum roller to help 
detect the presence of potential near-surface cavities, sinkholes, or loose zones. 
The vibratory drum roller should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet per 
minute. The proof-rolling operations should be performed in the presence of a 
Geolabs representative.  
If the proof-rolling operations detect a near-surface cavity or loose zones, the area 
should be ripped open, cleaned of the debris, and then backfilled with compacted

Coral Excavation 
The coral formation in the project area appears to be moderately to highly 
weathered and medium hard in nature. It is anticipated that the majority of the coral 
formation may be excavated with heavy excavation equipment, such as the large 
backhoe excavator. However, coral formations typically contain localized hard and 
crystallized zones. Therefore, we anticipate some difficult excavation conditions 
may arise in localized areas during construction. It is possible that the excavation 
for a small portion of the utility line trenches will require the use of hoerams or 
chipping. The contractor must exercise care to avoid over-ripping, which would 
disrupt the structure of the coral formation, resulting in a potential loss of bearing 
strength for improvements in the vicinity. 
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the materials are based on our 
experience in the vicinity and visual observation of the coral formation encountered 
in the excavated test pits. Contractors should be encouraged to examine the site 
conditions and the subsurface data to make their own reasonable and prudent 
interpretation.

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration at the project site consisted of excavating and sampling 
six test pits, designated as Test Pit Nos. 1 through 6, extending to depths of about 2.5 to 
9 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate test pit locations are shown on 
the Site Plan, Plate 2. The test pits were excavated using a CAT 416D Hoptoe. 
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the test pits by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Rock 
materials were described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as 
shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2. The Rock Description System is based on 
the publication “Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in 
Rock Masses” by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977).  Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Test Pits, 
Plates A-1 through A-6.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) determinations were performed on selected 
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties. The test results 
are presented on the Logs of Test Pits at the appropriate sample depths. 
Three one-inch ring swell tests were performed on remolded samples to evaluate 
the swelling potential of the near-surface soils.  The test results are summarized on 
Plate B-1. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. Test results 
are summarized on the Logs of Test Pits at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic 
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-2. 
Two Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 & C 136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plate B-3. 
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test (ASTM D 1883) was performed on a 
bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils.  Results of the CBR test were also used to evaluate the quality of the material 
tested for use as fills.  The test results are presented on Plate B-4.
