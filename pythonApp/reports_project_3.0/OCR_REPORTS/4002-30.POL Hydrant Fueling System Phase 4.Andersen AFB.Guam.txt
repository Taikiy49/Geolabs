2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Guam is the largest and southernmost of the Mariana Island
archipelago which is an island arc parallel to the trend of the subduction zone forming the
Mariana Trench The Mariana Trench is part of a belt of trenches which extend from
Japan to the Palau Islands and include the Japan Trench Mariana Trench Yap Trench
and Palau Trench
The Island of Guam can be broadly divided into three geologic provinces
the southern mountains the central mountains and the northern plateau The formation
of the island started during the Eocene Epoch by the eruption of basaltic rhyolite lavas
The mountainous central portion of the island is composed of these Eocene volcanic rocks
and conglomerates tuffs and limestones of Eocene and Oligocene in age Lava flows
and limestone formations of the Miocene Epoch make up the majority of the southern
one third of the island
The plateaus of the northern portion of the island were formed while this part of the
island was still beneath the sea level During the Oligocene and Miocene Epochs a
complex series of coral and algal reefs formed an elongated platform of limestones and
related deposits Tectonic activity has lifted these coral algal limestones above sea level
to form the northern Guam Plateau The project site is situated in a relatively level portion
of the northern plateau which forms the main portion of Andersen Air Force Base
Weathering and solution working of the limestone has partially re crystallized the
calcium carbonate of the limestone forming massive limestones in some areas and poorly
to well cemented detrital limestones in others Typically the detrital limestones are highly
variable in strength characteristics and are frequently karstic or cavernous in nature
In addition the limestone formations are generally porous Except during heavy storms
the majority of the precipitation naturally percolates through the formation producing little
or no surface water runoff

plateau is very thin generally less than 50 mm thick
2 2 Geologic and Seismic Hazards Evaluation
Guam is designated as Seismic Zone 3 by the Uniform Building Code UBC 1994
However we understand that Seismic Zone 4 criteria will be used for design of this project
Therefore we conducted an evaluation of possible geologic and seismic hazards that may
impact the development of the proposed project An evaluation of geologic and seismic
hazards typically addresses surface fault rupture ground shaking ground failure
liquefaction lateral spreading and ground lurching tsunamis and seiches and
seismically induced flooding
As discussed previously Guam is situated adjacent to the subduction zone that
forms the Mariana Trench The island is tectonically active and has experienced
approximately 20 earthquakes exceeding a magnitude of 7 0 since 1902 Of these events
fifteen occurred prior to 1950 and five have occurred subsequent to that date Several
large faults and fault zones that traverse across the Island of Guam include the following
Pago Bay Adelup Point Fault River Valley Fault Zone
Tamuning Barrigada Fault Pugua Fault and Mount Santa Rosa Horst Block
The proximity of the project site to faults and fault zones is a major consideration
when evaluating geologic and seismic hazards Of the major faults and fault zones
mentioned above the trace of the Pugua Fault lies closest to the project site at
approximately 8 kilometers to the west of the project site The Pugua Fault stretches from
off shore near Uruno Point and extends south to about Taguac Several minor faults
located about 3 kilometers south of the project site are also present
Based on our evaluation of the project site we do not believe that the geologic and
seismic hazards described above are significant considerations for this project With the

2 3 Climate
The Island of Guam is located in the North Pacific Ocean between 144 degrees
36 minutes and 144 degrees 58 minutes longitude and between 13 degrees 13 minutes
and 13 degrees 40 minutes latitude as shown on the Project Location Vicinity Map
Plate 1 In general the climate of Guam is warm and humid with little variation in
temperature from month to month Based on information obtained from the National
Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration NOAA Guam s mean annual temperature is
about 27 degrees Celsius throughout the year Typically annual rainfall averages from
about 2 000 to per year The rainy season is during the months of July through
November when monthly rainfall can average over 300 mm per month
Tropical storms and typhoons often pass through or near the Island of Guam
Tropical storms are defined as closed pressure systems with wind speeds ranging from
60 to 120 kilometers per hour Typhoons are similar phenomena with wind speeds
exceeding 120 kilometers per hour During the rainy season tropical storms and typhoons
are common in the vicinity of Guam Tropical storms and typhoons bring high intensity
rainfall when their zone of influence is near Guam In extreme events in the past a
typhoon has produced winds of over 280 kilometers per hour over 3 5 meters of
inundation and 650 mm of rainfall in one day

finished subgrade elevations would provide suitable foundation support for the proposed
fuel tanks and pump house Therefore we believe that a shallow foundation system

3 2 Foundation Probing
Based on our experience in the area voids and or cavities may be present in the
limestone formations encountered at the project site To reduce the potential for loss of
foundation support resulting from collapse of cavities below a footing we recommend that
a program of cavity probing and grouting be conducted during construction for the
proposed fuel tanks considering the critical nature of these tanks
For estimating purposes we recommend that probeholes be drilled along the
perimeter ring footing spaced at approximately 3 meters on center In addition we believe
that a series of probeholes with a spacing of about 3 meters on center should be drilled
Starting at the center of the tank and radiating out to the perimeter in about 3 meter
increments in radius The probeholes should be at least 75 mm in diameter and should
extend to at least 3 meters below the planned bottom of footing elevation If cavities
and or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operation additional
probeholes should be drilled at closer spacing to help delineate the vertical and lateral
extent of the cavity and or void The probeholes and cavities discovered should be
backfilled with sand cement grout injected at low to moderate pressures A typical
sand cement grout should have a slump of about 150 to 200 mm

3 3 Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed pump
house building In general the near surface clayey soils within the building footprint
should be removed generally less than 200 mm to expose the underlying limestone
materials and backfilled with structural fill materials The over excavated subgrades
should then be scarified to a depth of at least 200 mm moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to not less than 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by
dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
We consider the slabs on grade for the pump house building to be industrial type
Slabs Therefore we recommend that the concrete slabs on grade be supported on a
minimum 150 mm cushion fill to provide uniform support under the slabs The cushion fill
layer should consist of aggregate subbase course compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction For design of structural slabs bearing on the aggregate subbase
course a modulus of subgrade reaction of per square meter per

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Controlled and structural fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not
exceeding 200 mm in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by
ASTM Test Designation D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Excavation
The proposed project will involve the installation of approximately 2 500 lineal
meters of stainless steel pipes We understand that the average fuel line invert will
generally be located approximately 1 5 meters below the existing ground surface
Therefore we anticipate that excavations of up to about 3 meters in depth may be
necessary for installation of the new fuel lines
Based on our field exploration the Phase 4 project site is underlain by limestone
formation near the existing ground surface The limestone formation encountered
in our borings appeared to be severely fractured and medium hard in nature Our
boring information indicated that the limestone formation generally breaks down
into gravelly sands and sandy gravel It is anticipated that the majority of the
limestone formation may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment
such as excavating with large backhoe excavators However limestone formations
typically contain localized hard and crystallized zones Therefore we anticipate
that difficult excavation conditions may arise in localized areas during construction
It may be necessary to excavate the trenches in some areas by using hoe rams or
by chipping

Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings it appears that
most of the cut slopes would expose the existing fills consisting of silty clays and
clayey silts with coralline gravel or limestone formations In general we believe that
cut slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to one vertical
2H 1V or flatter Permanent fill slopes constructed of controlled fill or structural fill
should also be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter
Fills to be placed on slopes with inclinations steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed
and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding
The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level
horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement
recommendations Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back
to the design slope ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face Water should be
diverted away from the tops of slopes and slope planting should be provided as
soon as possible to reduce the potential for significant erosion of the finished
slopes

3 7 Drainage
The finished grades outside the new structures should be sloped to shed water
away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also
advised that gutter systems be installed around the building structures and that the
discharge be diverted away from the foundation and slab areas Excessive landscape
watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to
foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the
potential for excessive water infiltration into the subsurface
The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab
edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for excessive water
infiltration In addition drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and
should be maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the foundations and slabs

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight determinations ASTM D 2937
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the logs of borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Eight sets of and Minimum Resistivity tests were
performed by Oceanic Analytical Laboratory Inc on selected soil samples obtained from
our field exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of the in situ soils to buried metallic
substructures In addition four sets of Chloride Contents and Sulfate
Contents were performed by Oceanic Analytical Laboratory Inc to evaluate
the corrosivity of the in situ soils to concrete in contact with the soils The results of these
tests are summarized on Plate B 1
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a bulk sample of the
near surface soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits Graphic presentation of the test
results is provided on Plate B 2
Eight Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on
Plates B 3 through B 5
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test ASTM D 1883 was performed on a
bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
the soils The test results are presented on Plate B 6
One Modified Proctor Compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationship The test results are presented on Plate B 7
