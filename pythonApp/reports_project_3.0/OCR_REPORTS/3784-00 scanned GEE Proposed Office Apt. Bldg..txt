INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed office apartment building at 1109 Young Street in the Makiki
area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input
only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed project site is located at 1109 Young Street in the Makiki area of
Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The proposed project site is approximately
rectangular in shape with a lot size of approximately 7 092 square feet It is proposed to
demolish the existing one story house and garage structures occupying the site in order
to construct a new 6 story office apartment building on the property The ground floor
level of the proposed 6 story building will be near the existing ground surface elevation
The proposed building will consist of ground level parking office space on the second
floor and one bedroom apartments from the third through sixth floors
At the time of our field exploration the existing structures at the site have been
demolished Based on the relatively level ground at the site we anticipate that only minor
site grading will be required to achieve the finished grades

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion
incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series
The project site is located near the southern flank of Punchbowl Hill The
Punchbowl Hill Puowaina is a tuff cone near the center of Honolulu built against the end
of a spur of the Koolau Range The tuff consists of mostly brown palagonitized vitric ash
and lapilli with scattered fragments of coral limestone and Koolau basalt
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
and coral reefs accumulated at higher elevations

SITE DESCRIPTION
Based on our field observations and the topographic survey map provided the
project site is relatively flat with existing ground elevations between about 14 4 and
15 6 feet Mean Sea Level MSL The project site is rectangular in shape with plan
dimensions of approximately 51 by 139 feet The total lot area is about 7 092 square

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling three borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of approximately 25 6 to
26 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are

coralline deposits and coral formation were encountered below the cinder sands

ground surface
Groundwater was encountered in the drilled borings at depths of approximately
12to 14 feet below the existing ground surface corresponding to an elevation of
approximately 2 feet MSL It should be noted that groundwater levels may be
influenced by seasonal precipitation storm surge conditions and other factors

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration at the subject site generally encountered a surficial fill layer
consisting of medium dense sandy soils overlying medium dense cinder sand deposits to
depths of approximately 7 to 10 feet below the existing ground surface The cinder sands
were underlain by dense to very dense coralline deposits and coral formation extending
to the maximum explored depth of 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface Based on
the relatively dense sandy subsoil conditions encountered in our borings we believe that
a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings bearing on
the recompacted near surface fill soils may be used to support the proposed building
Due to the potential for presence of cavities and or voids in the coralline
deposits formation and the relatively heavy structural loads of the building we
recommend that a program of probing and grouting the footing locations be implemented
during construction A representative from Geolabs Hawaii should observe the probing
and grouting operation to evaluate the presence and extent of cavities where
encountered Therefore special attention should be given to the Foundation Probing
section of this report Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical
engineering recommendations are presented in the following sections of this report
Building Foundations
Based on the relatively dense subsoil conditions encountered at the project site
we believe that shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used to support the
proposed 6 story office apartment building We understand that the column loads for the
proposed 6 story building will be on the order of about 150 to 400 kips per column
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered an allowable bearing pressure of up to
4 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be used for the design of foundations bearing
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3784 00 Page 7
on the recompacted near surface fill soils or compacted engineered fill This bearing
value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third for transient loads
such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
The foundation subgrade soils should be recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Based on the borings drilled at the
site pockets of soft loose materials may be encountered at the footing subgrade
elevation If soft loose soils are encountered at the footing subgrade elevation the
soft loose materials should be over excavated to expose dense competent materials
The over excavation should be backfilled with engineered fill compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom may be extended down to the
underlying competent material
Because of the potential for encountering soft loose materials at the footing
subgrade elevation we recommend that unit prices for footing over excavation and
backfill with engineered fill be included in the construction bid documents In general the
over excavated granular soils may be reused as a source of backfill material provided that
they are free of deleterious materials or not too wet Due to the potential for presence of
soft loose materials and the relatively large footings required we recommend that the
footing excavations be observed and tested by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii during
construction to confirm the bearing materials and to evaluate the need for footing
over excavation
Because of the potential for presence of cavities and or voids in the coralline
deposits formation and the relatively heavy structural loads a program of probing and
grouting the footing locations is recommended during construction This requirement is
further discussed in the Foundation Probing section of this report

In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grades If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements
they should be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane
extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of
the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement New footings to be located adjacent to existing structures on neighboring
properties should be lowered to the same depths as not deeper than the existing
building footings to avoid surcharging the existing foundations
We estimate that foundation settlements under the anticipated design loads for
footings bearing on the recompacted near surface fill soils or compacted engineered fill
as recommended herein to be less than 1 inch total with differential settlements on the
order of inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive
earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system
A coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted
near surface fill soils or compacted engineered fills Resistance due to passive earth
pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square
foot per foot of depth p c f This assumes that the soils around the footings are
relatively undisturbed or well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the
passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
Foundation Probing
As previously indicated our field exploration encountered coralline deposits which
are susceptible to the formation of cavities and voids Due to relatively heavy column
loads and the potential for presence of cavities and or voids in coralline deposits we
recommend a program of probing and grouting of the footing locations be conducted
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3784 00 Page 9
during construction to reduce the potential of loss of foundation support resulting from
collapse of cavities and or voids below a footing
For estimating purpose we recommend that probes be drilled at each spread
footing location and on 10 foot centers for continuous footings For large spread footings
a minimum of one probe should be drilled for each 100 square feet of foundation bearing
area The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at
least 20 feet below the planned bottom of footing If cavities and or voids are
encountered or suspected during the probing and grouting operations additional probes
should be drilled at closer spacing to help delineate the vertical and lateral extent of the
cavity and or void The probe holes and any cavities discovered should be backfilled with
sand cement grout We recommend that a low strength sand cement grout mixture with
an approximately 6 to 9 inch slump range pumped at low to moderate pressures be
utilized for the grouting operation
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids we recommend
that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during bidding In
addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and grouting
operation is complete Since there is a potential for encountering cavities and or voids
the contractor should be made aware that a longer lag time between probing grouting
operation and foundation construction should be allocated in the construction schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs Hawaii This would allow our firm to monitor the presence
of cavities and to allow additional recommendations to be made if excess grout in take
and or changed conditions are observed
Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the stair well elevator
pit and or mechanical room located on the ground floor level In general we anticipate
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3784 00 Page 10
that the slabs on grades will be supported by the granular soils encountered near the
surface in our borings In general we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion
layer consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be placed below
the slab to provide more uniform support of the slab and to serve as a capillary moisture
break To reduce future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to any floor
coverings or mechanical equipment an impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a
plastic membrane is recommended on top of the gravel cushion layer Two inches of
moist fine sand should be placed above the plastic membrane to provide protection of the
membrane and to aid in curing of the slab concrete
Where slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as driveways a 6 inch layer
of aggregate base course compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction is
recommended in lieu of the No 3B Fine gravel cushion layer The 2 inch moist fine sand
and vapor barrier may be omitted under these slabs Exterior concrete walkways may be
required for the proposed project We recommend that concrete walkways be supported
directly on the on site granular soils compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Contraction joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the
walkways with expansion joints at right angle intersections
Slab subgrades should be prepared by scarifying the subgrade soils to a depth of
about 8 inches moisture conditioning the materials to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Slab
subgrades should be kept moist until covered by concrete Where shrinkage cracks are
noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soils be thoroughly
moistened to close all cracks or re prepared Saturation and subsequent yielding of the
exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted engineered fill at
no additional cost to the owner The area adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled
tightly against the slab with non expansive impervious soil and graded to divert water
away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the foundation

Site Preparation
We envision that minimal site grading work will be required for the proposed
project construction In general the following guidelines should be used for site grading
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly
cleared All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed
and disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing
below any areas designated to receive fill or future improvements should be
over excavated to expose firm or dense material and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft and or organic soils
should be properly disposed of off site Contract documents should include additive and
deductive unit prices for over excavation and engineered fill placement to account for
variations in the over excavation quantities
Slabs and foundations of the existing structures that have been demolished should
be completely removed Any over excavation resulting from the demolition should be
backfilled with compacted fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned should be
removed and the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with the on site
granular fill material moisture conditioned to above optimum moisture placed in 6 to
8 inch loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Utilities that are to be abandoned in place under the proposed structures should be
backfilled by pumping lean concrete under low pressure
After clearing and demolition all areas at finished subgrade or designated to
receive fill should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction In areas where the finished grades will be subjected to vehicular traffic the
compaction requirement should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3784 00 Page 15
ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage
by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close all cracks Saturation and
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor
drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted engineered fill at no additional cost to the owner
In general the on site granular soils encountered in the borings may be reused as
a source of fill materials provided that the materials are less than 3 inches in largest
dimension and are free of deleterious materials Imported fill materials if required should
consist of non expansive granular material such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or
cinder sand The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles
larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent
particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of
20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less Imported fill material
should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for conformance with these recommendations prior
to delivery to the project site for its intended use
Site preparation should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii It is
important that a representative from our office observe the site preparation to evaluate
whether any undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and
scarification process and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those
encountered in our field exploration

Drainage
The finished grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away

the subsoils

saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations

the concrete to reduce water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling three
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 to depths of approximately 25 6 to
26 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drilling
rig equipped with continuous flight and hollow stem augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Spilt Barrel Sampling of Soils
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the logs of
borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of coral formation materials encountered at the site were obtained
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the coral formation The material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of
the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals
