INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the new parking areas and retaining wall replacement project at Kuhio
Homes in Kalihi on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our design level geotechnical engineering recommendations derived
from our analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for
design input only and may not be appropriate to form the basis for construction cost
estimates nor for construction bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed project site is located in the Kalihi area on the island of Oahu
Hawaii It is proposed to construct five new parking areas and to replace an existing
retaining wall at the subject site
Based on our previous experience in the area the project site may be underlain
by adobe soils over basalt formation at shallow depths Particular attention was given to
the swelling potential and shear strength properties of the near surface adobe clay soils

geotechnical engineering design recommendations pertinent to the design of the new

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is approximately bounded by Owene Street to the east Linapuni
Elementary School to the south Rose Street to the west and Kamehameha IV Road to
the north The site is currently occupied by twenty one 2 story buildings and three
parking areas with Ahonui Street running through the project site
The terrain at the project site slopes down slightly from the north towards the south
with existing ground surface elevations ranging from 102 feet Mean Sea Level MSL at
the north to 85 feet MSL in the south

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration indicates that the site is underlain by medium stiff to stiff
clayey silts and silty clays over basalt formation for the depths explored Laboratory tests
performed on the surficial silty clay soils indicate that the material exhibits moderate to
high shrink swell potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations Unless new
pavements supported by these soils are properly designed there is a potential for future
distress resulting from shrinking and swelling of these underlying clayey soils due to
changes in the moisture content Details of our pavement design recommendations are
discussed in the following sections of this report
Pavement Design
Due to the presence of the expansive clay subgrade soils encountered at the
project site we recommend that the clayey subsoils beneath the future pavement areas
be over excavated to a minimum depth of 18 inches below the bottom of the base course
layer and replaced with select granular fill If zones of soft loose or saturated soils are
encountered at the over excavated subgrade level deeper over excavation may be
required to remove these soils to the underlying firm material The resulting over
excavation should also be backfilled with compacted non expansive select fill material
The clayey material resulting from the over excavation should be removed and disposed
of off site
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3197 00 Page 6
The over excavated subgrade surface should be moisture conditioned to at least
2 percent above optimum moisture content and lightly compacted to about 85 to 90
percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with the procedures described in ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
Select granular fill material needed for the 18 inch capping layer should be placed
as soon as possible after the over excavation and recompaction of the existing clayey
subgrade soils The prepared new select granular fill and the underlying subgrade soil
should be wetted and kept moist prior to final placement of asphaltic concrete Select
granular fill should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
Select granular fill material should consist of imported granular material such as
crusher waste crushed coral basalt gravel or cinder sand The material should be well
graded from coarse to fine with no particles greater than 3 inches in largest dimension
The material should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 20 or
higher and a swell potential of not more than 1 percent when tested in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1883 It should contain less than 20 percent passing the
No 200 sieve
The base course cushion between the asphalt concrete and the underlying select
granular fill should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to not less than 95
percent relative compaction Our discussions and recommendations for pavement
section design are summarized below

Where rigid pavements will be used we recommend that pavement joints and joint
sealants be provided Joint sealants should be used to provide a seal to reduce potential
infiltration of water and to provide a compressible joint Sufficient joints should be
provided in rigid pavements for the following reasons
1 To reduce potential curling and warping stresses in the pavement caused
by temperature and moisture gradients across the pavement slabs
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3197 00 Page 8
2 To reduce and control cracking due to volume changes in the concrete
3 To facilitate construction
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all
surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during
or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or
adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend the curbs be extended a minimum of
2 inches into the subgrade soils below the base course aggregate to reduce migration
of landscape water into the pavement section Alternatively a subdrain system is
recommended to collect excessive water from landscaping irrigation For long term
performance we believe that a subdrain system should be considered adjacent to
paved landscape areas
Retaining Wall Design
We understand that the existing retaining wall behind Building No B 16 has
exhibited signs of tilting and will need to be replaced by a new wall The following
guidelines may be used for the preliminary design of the new retaining wall
1 An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 p s f may be used for the
design of wall footing foundations bearing on the underlying basalt
formation anticipated at shallow depths
2 For lateral earth pressures the following values may be used
Walls unrestrained atthe top 40 s f equivalent
active condition granular fluid pressure
level backfill per foot of depth
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3197 00 Page 9
Walls restrained at the top 60 s f equivalent
at rest condition granular fluid pressure
level backfill per foot of depth
The surcharge effect from loads adjacent to retaining structure should also
be included in the design of the wall A rectangular distribution over the
height of the wall with a pressure equal to 35 and 50 percent of the
surcharge load is normally used for active and at rest conditions
respectively
3 For lateral resistance design a passive earth resistance of 300 p s f per
foot of depth may be used for wall footing on level areas A base friction
factor of 0 4 may also be used to evaluate the resistance against sliding of
the wall bearing on basalt rock
4 Wall foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded a minimum
depth of 2 feet below the lowest adjacent finish grade Over excavation of
the wall footing subgrades may be required to expose the underlying basalt
formation for foundation support
5 Wall foundations located next to sewer drainage easements should be
embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the
bottom edge of the utility tench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines
6 A subsurface drainage system subdrains or weepholes should be utilized
behind the wall to reduce potential build up of hydrostatic pressures A
typical drainage system should consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of No 3B
Fine gravel material immediately adjacent to the structure with a perforated
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3197 00 Page 10
pipe with perforations down at the base of the structure discharging to a
storm drain or other discharge facility
7 Backfill between the No 3B Fine gravel and the excavated face should
consist of select granular borrow fill material such as crusher waste or
crushed coral The upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of non
expansive impervious material to reduce water infiltration behind the wall
A choking layer is recommended below this impervious capping layer to
reduce the potential for migration of fines into the underlying No 3B Fine
gravel material
We recommend that the project geotechnical engineer Geolabs Hawaii be
retained to provide the field observation and monitoring services during the over
excavation and backfilling operations at the subject project in order to observe
compliance with our recommendations and if necessary to provide additional
consultation

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based in part
upon information obtained from field borings Variations of subsoil conditions between
the borings may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not become
evident until construction is underway If variations then appear evident it will be
necessary to re evaluate the recommendations provided in this report
The locations of the field borings indicated in this report are approximate having
been estimated by taping from features shown on the Topographic Map undated
provided by R M Towill Corporation on March 17th Elevations of the borings were
estimated by interpolation from the elevation points shown on the same map The
physical locations and elevations of the borings should be considered accurate only to
the degree implied by the methods used
The stratification lines shown on graphic representations of the borings depict the
approximate boundaries between soil types and as such may denote a gradual
transition

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content unit weight and Atterberg Limits determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Nine one inch ring swell tests were performed on selected samples to evaluate the
swelling potential of the subsoils The test results are summarized on Plates B 1 and B 2
Two Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected samples to evaluate their
liquid and plastic limits The test results are presented on Plate B 3
One California Bearing Ratio test was performed on a bulk sample to evaluate the
pavement support characteristics The test results are also presented on Plate B 3
