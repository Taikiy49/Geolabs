PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project involves preparation of a redevelopment plan at the Kaimuki Station 
Wells in the Kaimuki area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The project location 
and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.   
The proposed redevelopment consists of installing four new wells.  The Board of 
Water Supply would like to determine whether the existing piping, which was installed in 
the early 1900’s, can be used in the system when the new wells are installed.   
We understand that in-situ testing of the pipe is planned to estimate the remaining 
life of the pipe.  The in-situ testing of the pipe consists of broadband electromagnetic and 
ultrasonic testing.  The entire circumference of the pipe will be exposed to conduct the 
broadband electromagnetic testing.  The existing pipe to be tested is a 16-inch pipe 
located at the south corner of the site.  Based on the available information, the pipe is 
about 14 to 16 feet below the existing pavement surface.  Since in-situ testing of the pipe 
will require a deep excavation, a trench shoring plan will be prepared prior to the in-situ 
testing.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from 
the Waianae and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to 
be middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene

W.O. 6895-00

Hawaii • California 
to early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Volcano, volcanic activity returned with a 
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are 
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. Most of the coastal plains were developed during the 
Pleistocene Epoch when the sea levels fluctuated significantly. As the glaciers grew and 
advanced, less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell 
below the present stands of the sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess 
of water became available such that the sea levels rose to above its present level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations. 
The erosional processes are dominated by detachment of soil and rock masses 
from the valley walls which are transported downslope toward the axis of a valley 
primarily by gravity as colluvium.  Once these materials reach the stream in the central 
portion of a valley, alluvial processes become dominant, and the sediments are 
transported and deposited as alluvium. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3. The borings drilled were 
advanced to depths of about 30 to 30.1 feet below the existing ground surface.  The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Boring No. 1, drilled at the north side of the site, generally encountered medium stiff 
silty clay fill extending to a depth of about 2.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  The 
fill was underlain by alluvium consisting of stiff clayey silt extending to a depth of about 
5 feet below the existing ground surface.  Basalt rock, which was generally medium hard, 
was encountered below the alluvium and extended to the maximum depth explored of 
about 30 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Boring No. 3, drilled at the east side of the site, generally encountered fill consisting 
of loose clayey gravel and sand extending to a depth of about 11 feet below the existing 
ground surface.  Below the fill, basalt rock was encountered to the maximum depth 
explored of about 30.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  The basalt rock was 
generally very hard. 
Boring No. 2, drilled near the proposed excavation, generally encountered about 
5 inches of asphaltic concrete underlain by medium dense sand to a depth of about

DISCUSSION 
We understand that two types of shoring systems are being considered to shore 
the excavation for the in-situ testing.  A steel cylindrical casing system and the trench 
shield system.  The steel cylindrical casing system will be fabricated by the excavation 
contractor and the trench shield is a pre-engineered structure.   
Steel Cylindrical Casing 
We understand that this system would consist of steel cylindrical casing sections 
that are welded together to extend down to the bottom of the excavation.  The steel 
casing would need to be designed to resist the lateral pressures surrounding the steel 
cylindrical casing.   
Based on our field exploration near the proposed excavation, the soils 
anticipated in the excavation may consist of hard silts and clays down to a depth of 
about 14 to 16 feet below the existing pavement surface.  Based on the anticipated 
subsurface conditions, the lateral pressure distribution for a braced excavation was 
determined.  The apparent pressure diagram is provided on Plate 3. 
Trench Shield 
The trench shield is a pre-engineered steel structure that consists of two steel 
plates that are held up with spreader pipes.  The steel plates line the excavation 
sidewalls.  The type of trench shield used can be determined from the classification of 
the soil in accordance with Appendix A of Chapter 132, Excavations, Trenching, and 
Shoring of “Title 12, Department of Labor and Industrial Relations, Subtitle 8, Division of 
Occupational Safety and Health.” 
Based on our field exploration near the proposed excavation, the soils 
anticipated in the excavation may consist of hard silts and clays down to a depth of 
about 14 to 16 feet below the existing pavement surface.  The pocket penetrometer test 
result indicates an unconfined compressive strength of greater than 3.0 tons per square

LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from our field borings. Variations of subsurface conditions between 
and beyond the borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may 
not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it 
will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations provided herein.  
The field boring locations indicated herein are approximate, having been taped 
from features shown on the Approximate Well Locations Kaimuki Pump Station Plan.   
The physical locations of the borings should be considered accurate only to the degree 
implied by the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict 
the approximate boundaries between the soil and/or rock types and, as such, may 
denote a gradual transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the 
times shown on the graphic representations and/or presented in the text herein. These 
data have been reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. 
However, it must be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in rainfall, 
temperature, and other factors.
