Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to
early Pleistocene in age As volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano ceased lava flows
from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau
now known as the Schofield Plateau The project site is located on the southwestern flank
of the Waianae Volcano
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of Schofield Plateau
generated sediments which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project
site and to the south these sediments accumulated and are interbedded with marine
sediments and coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge The thickness of
the sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1 Highway
to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the
confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present

During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea
When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposited lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet
waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys
in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the calcareous
reef materials Placement of man made fills and commercial development of the area
within the last 20 years have brought the Ko Olina area to its present form
Surface and Subsurface Conditions
As mentioned previously the project site is located within the Ko Olina Resort in the
District of Ewa on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project site Lot 33 is bounded by the
Ko Olina Golf Course to the north south and east Lot 16 is located to the west of Lot 33
as shown on the General Site Plan Plate 2
Due to time constraints a field exploration program consisting of drilling borings
was not conducted for this due diligence study Therefore the surface and subsurface
conditions were evaluated based on available in house information and past experience
The project site generally slopes down slightly from east to west Based on our research
and review of available in house information the northern portion of the project site has
been generally mass graded during the initial development of the Ko Olina Resort Based
on our site reconnaissance the ground surface is tiered down by approximately 8 to
10 feet from the mass graded elevation along the northern portions of the project site The
majority of the remaining project site appears to be near the original ground surface
elevation The approximate limits of the mass graded area is shown on the Site Plan
Plate 3
Based on the site reconnaissance and a review of available in house information
fills of up to about 10 feet in height may have been placed in the northern portions of
Lot 33 Mass grading at the northeastern corner of the project site may have consisted of
cuts of up to about 2 feet in depth The native soils at the project site generally consist of
stiff silty clay alluvium In some areas the native soils may consist of coralline sands and
gravel Based on the available information bedrock consisting of basalt formation is
underlain at shallow depths especially along the northern portion of the project site near
Farrington Highway

In general the fill materials placed during the initial mass grading operations for the
resort consisted of on site materials generated from excavations at other areas of the
Ko Olina Resort Therefore we anticipate that the near surface materials may consist of a
mixture of alluvium and coralline deposits In areas with deeper fills rock fills consisting of
coralline and basaltic boulders and cobbles were also placed
It should be noted that numerous stockpiles of soil were observed in the
southeastern portions of the project site The stockpiles generally consisted of mounds of
coralline and alluvial soils mixed with cobbles and boulders These stockpiles were
generally about 4 to 6 feet in height In addition some outcrops of basaltic boulders were
also observed in the northeastern portion of the project site
The vegetation at the project site is relatively light with the majority of the vegetation
consisting of shrubs and wild grasses Based on the topographic survey map provided
the ground surface elevations at the project site range from about 23 feet Mean Sea
Level MSL at southwestern portion of the project site to about 72 feet MSL at
northeastern portion of the project site
In general we envision that the groundwater levels will be near sea level ranging
from about 1 to 1 foot MSL However due to the close proximity of the project site to the
Pacific Ocean groundwater levels can fluctuate depending on tidal fluctuations seasonal
precipitation groundwater withdrawal and or injection and other factors

Stockpiled Materials
As mentioned previously an extensive amount of stockpiles of soil mixed with
cobbles and boulders are present at the project site In general the stockpiles range from
about 4 to 6 feet in height Due to the uncompacted condition of these soil materials the

In addition our field observation indicated that the stockpiled materials contained
numerous cobbles and boulders In general we envision that the majority of the

Cobbles and Boulders
As mentioned previously rock fills consisting of coralline and basaltic cobbles and
boulders were placed during the initial mass grading operations of the resort In general
we believe that rock fills at the project site were limited to the deep fill areas only
Therefore the presence of cobbles and boulders should be anticipated at the mass
graded areas along the northern portions of the project site Excavations for building
foundations and or utility lines may encounter some difficulties due to the presence of
these potential obstructions in the subsurface
Expansive Soils
Based on our experience in the vicinity of the project site the on site alluvial soils
may exhibit moderate shrink swell potential wnen subjected to moisture fluctuations As
mentioned previously the native alluvial soils were observed at the project site in some
areas In addition alluvial soils may have been used as fill materials during the initial
mass grading operations for the Ko Olina Resort development
The expansive potential of the alluvial soils at the site should be evaluated by
performing laboratory swell tests during the design phase Where expansive soils are
encountered over excavation of the alluvial soils may be necessary under foundations
slabs on grade and pavements We estimate that the over excavation depth below the
foundations slabs on grade and pavements may be on the order of about 2 to 3 feet in
areas underlain by the expansive soils The resulting over excavation would need to be
backfilled with non expansive select granular fill
Site Preparation
The proposed grading plans were not available at the time this letter report was
prepared In general the excavated on site materials may be used as a source of fill
materials provided that they are processed to meet the gradation requirements of the
various fill types Due to the potential for presence of expansive soils special attention
should be given to the site preparation aspect of the proposed construction

Over excavation of the subgrades under structures and roadways may be necessary
depending on the expansive nature of the on site soils Fill areas may require placement
of non expansive select granular material below future improvements such as building
slabs sidewalks and or pavements
Boulders i e rock fragments larger than 12 inches in maximum size but less than
2 feet in largest dimension encountered during site grading may be used in fills provided
that several provisions are followed In general the boulders should be placed a minimum
of 4 feet below the finished subgrades and at least 6 feet laterally away from slope faces
In addition fills consisting of cobbles and boulders should be properly choked off at the
surface to reduce the potential for migration of fines through the voids between the
boulders
Basalt Formation
As indicated previously the native soils are generally underlain by basalt formation
at the project site based on our available in house information We envision that the depth
to the basalt formation would generally be shallower along the northern portions of the
project site Excavations for building foundations and or utility lines along the northern
portions of the project site may encounter some difficulties due to the presence of basalt
formation A field exploration program consisting of drilled borings should be conducted
during the design phase to evaluate the depths to basalt formation
Foundations
We understand that details of the proposed structures have not been determined at
the time this letter report was prepared However we understand that the structures will
be limited to 30 feet in vertical height due to the current zoning restrictions Therefore we
envision that the new houses would generally consist of one and two story structures
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the project site we believe that the
proposed one to two story structures may be supported on a shallow foundation system
consisting of spread and or continuous footings provided that several provisions are
followed
As indicated previously over excavation of the foundation materials may be
required due to the variable nature of the fill materials and potentially expansive nature of
the alluvial soils Furthermore we believe that column loads for the new structures
supported on shallow foundations should be limited to about 300 to 400 kips at this time
The potential need for over excavation under the structure foundations should be
further evaluated based on the actual subsurface conditions at the structure locations In
addition the limits and extent of the surface fill alluvium and coralline deposits should be
delineated as much as possible Therefore we recommend that the subsurface conditions
be evaluated by conducting a field exploration program consisting of drilling and sampling
