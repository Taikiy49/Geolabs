SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Castle Cooke Aviation Parking Lot Improvements project
adjacent to Lagoon Drive in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project location
and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of new pavement
construction and light pole foundations only The findings and recommendations presented
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The proposed project consists of the construction of a new parking lot for
Castle Cooke Aviation Parking Lot Improvements in three vacant parcels
and 45 adjacent to Lagoon Drive at the Honolulu International Airport
in Honolulu Oahu Hawaii Details of the vehicular traffic loading were not available at the
time this report was prepared However it is anticipated that the majority of vehicular traffic
at the site will consist of passenger vehicles and light trucks
Structural elements for this project will be minimal consisting primarily of
foundations for light standards and electrically operated access gates
1 3 Purpose and Scope
The scope of our work was to obtain an overview of the surface and subsurface
conditions at the site to formulate geotechnical engineering recommendations for the
design of the new pavement for the proposed parking lot The work was performed in
general accordance with our fee proposal dated Our scope of work generally
included the following tasks and work efforts

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Koolau Volcano reached the
end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago
The Honolulu International Airport area which includes Lagoon Drive is on the
southern flank of Koolau and its geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly
related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the
genesis of Pearl Harbor The base of the stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes
occurred as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world The
higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of sediments in the heads of the old bays
accumulation of reef deposits at corresponding higher elevations and lagoonal marine
sediments in the quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused
streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef deposits Renewed erosion deposited
alluvial soils in the lagoons and bays
The geologic history of the area has been further complicated by the deposition of
recent pyroclastic materials and lava flows from eruptions of the vents of members of the
Honolulu Volcanic Series including the Aliamanu Salt Lake Makalapa vents These events
were contemporaneous with the Pleistocene sea level fluctuations The deposits of
pyroclastics have consolidated to form volcanic tuff locally referred to as mudrock These
eruptions caused a diversion of Moanalua Stream from its original course to its current
location
The site is situated at the western side of Keehi Lagoon which is a drowned stream
channel at the mouth of Kalihi and Moanalua Streams The area is generally underlain by

2 2 Existing Site Conditions
The project site is on the ewa makai side of the intersection of Lagoon Drive and
Kapalulu Place at Honolulu International Airport in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii
as shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 The site consists of three parcels that will
be consolidated to create a large parking lot The Ewa side parcel is presently occupied
by several warehouse workshop type structures The central portion of the site is vacant
except for two stripped light aircraft carcasses and some aviation equipment The
Diamond Head side lot is used for the temporary storage of new automobiles The ground
surface is generally bare with a basaltic gravel surfacing
2 3 Subsurface Conditions
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling six borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 inclusive The borings were extended to depths
between about 6 5 and 7 0 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring
locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The borings generally encountered man made fill to the maximum depth explored of
about 7 feet below the existing ground surface The fills generally consisted of dense
sands and sandy silts with gravel We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the
time of our field exploration However groundwater levels at the site are subject to
fluctuation in response to tides storm surges and periods of heavy precipitation
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered in the borings are presented on
the attached Logs of Borings Plate A 1 through A 6 of Appendix A Results of the
laboratory tests performed on selected soil samples are presented in Appendix B

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
We anticipate that site grading for construction of the proposed parking lot will be
minimal Based on the subsurface soil conditions encountered at the project site and the
assumption that traffic will consist of light vehicles we believe that a light duty asphaltic
concrete pavement section will be appropriate
Structural elements for this project will be minimal consisting primarily of
foundations for light standards and electrically operated access gates Based on the
subsurface soil conditions at the project site we believe that shallow spread footing
foundations may be used for support of the proposed structural improvements An
allowable bearing pressure of 2 500 pounds per square foot psf may be used for

3 1 Pavement Section
Design traffic loadings for the paved areas at the site were not available at the time
this report was prepared However as previously discussed it is anticipated that the
majority of vehicular traffic at the site will consist of passenger vehicles and light trucks For
this traffic condition the following light pavement section is recommended

The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches
moisture conditioned to above optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density
determined by ASTM D 1557 AASHTO T 180 Optimum moisture content is the water
content percentage by dry weight needed to achieve the maximum dry density
One of the primary mechanisms causing pavement distress is pumping of the
pavement section as a result of saturation of the aggregate base course and or subgrade
soils Therefore the pavement surfaces should be sloped to drain to appropriate drainage
structures or discharge areas Surface water ponding should not be allowed on site during
or after construction The recommended pavement sections assume that good drainage
will be provided for the paved areas
3 2 Foundations
Based on the anticipated subsurface soil conditions at the project site we believe
that shallow spread footing foundations may be used for support of the proposed structural
improvements An allowable bearing pressure of may be used for foundations
bearing on properly compacted structural fill The bearing value is for dead plus live loads
and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind
or seismic forces
In general the bottom of spread footings should be embedded a minimum of
24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grades If spread footing foundations are next
to utility trenches they should be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V
imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility
A coefficient of friction of 0 4 may be used for footings bearing on the compacted
structural fills Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This
assumes that the soils around the footings are well compacted Unless covered by
pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be
neglected

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly Vegetation debris deleterious material and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft
and or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas
designated to receive fill should be over excavated to expose firm material and the
resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fills The excavated
soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site and or used in
landscape areas where appropriate Contract documents should include additive
and deductive unit prices for over excavation and compacted fill placement to
account for variations in the over excavation quantities
The subgrades and areas designated to receive fills should then be scarified to a
minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in

3 4 Underground Utility Lines
We anticipate that underground utilities for the proposed parking lot development
will primarily consist of irrigation electrical lines and drainage Good construction practices
should be utilized for the installation and backfilling of the trenches for the proposed
utilities The contractor should determine the method and equipment used for excavation
subject to practical limits and safety considerations In addition the trench excavation
should comply with the applicable local state and federal safety requirements The
contractor should be responsible for trench shoring design and installation Trench shoring
and bracing should also conform to the appropriate health and safety requirements
In general we recommend placing granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation under pipes for support of the utility
lines The initial trench backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of
free draining granular backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for pipe
damage from compaction of the backfill It is critical to use a free draining granular material
the initial trench backfill to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches
of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Use of clayey soils as
initial trench backfill directly around utility pipes is not recommended
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to the
finished subgrade should consist of the approved on site soils or general fill materials with
a maximum particle size of 6 inches or less The backfill material should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence The upper 3 feet of the

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling a
total of six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 extending to depths of
approximately 6 5 to 7 0 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring
locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 We used a truck mounted drill rig equipped
with continuous flight augers
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field He monitored the drilling operations on a near continuous
basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory
Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System as
shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials encountered in the borings are
presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the
field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive
strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
One Sieve Analysis test ASTM C 117 C 136 was performed on a selected soil
sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate B 1
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test ASTM D 1883 was performed on a
bulk sample of in situ soil to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils The
test result is presented on Plate B 2
