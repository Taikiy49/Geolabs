Background Information
The existing residence is a split level wood framed double wall
construction with stucco exterior finish It is built on a moderate
slope with lower level facing west on Kaeleku Street A swimming pool
is situated at the back of the house A concrete decking surrounded
the pool and extended to the back of the house
A small concrete lined drainage ditch rums along the southern
property boundary
Based on a visual observation of the surface soil it appears that
the subsoil might consist of expansive brown to gray brown silty clay
with rock fragments Some of these soil appear to be mixed fill
materials resulted from the original site grading operation

and distorted door frames

Conclusion
Judging from the cracking patterns observed on the entire house
we believe that the damages may be attributed to two main causes
First settlement of retaining wall backfill second heaving of
subgrade soils underneath the slabs The areas judged to be thus
affected are shown on the attached plan
The more significant areas of settlement are along the back of
retaining wall in the den bedroom 2 and to a less extent in the
dining room area These settlements are probably caused by subsequent
settlement of retaining wall backfill along Lines 5 and 6
The retaining wall in the back of the garage exposed two
horizontal cracks which seem to indicate that some tilting of the
retaining wall may have occurred However the top of the wall was
restrained by the slab in the back A more complex cracking pattern
was noted next to the water heater tank under stair no 3 landing
This may be due to restraining effect of retaining wall C that keeps
the retaining wall along Line 5 from even a minor tilting
It is important that the existing subdrain lines behind the
retaining walls be located to assure that they are functioning
properly The outlets of the subdrain should be located and daylighted
to provide drainage relief

As shown on the original design drawings the slabs are tied to
the top of the wall Severing the connection at this time may result
in the causing the retaining wall to tilt more The repair work should

The distortion to the entry door frame was probably due to heaving

Remedial Measures
We believe that the slab heaving detected at the house is caused
by expansive subsoils swelling upon absorbing additional moisture which
may have come from rain run off or watering around the house It could
also have been caused by underground seepage of unknown source which is
believed to be very difficult to control
To control heaving of the floor slabs after construction is very
difficult It is important that every effort be exercised to prevent
changes in the moisture content of the subsoil For such a purpose we
recommend that the following steps be taken It should be understood
that as long as the underlying expansive soil is not removed a
potential for slab heave still exists although the following measures
would minimize external source of moisture change

A buried cut off curtain wall of about 3 feet deep should be
provided along both the northern and southern sides of the
house to minimize additional entry and escape of surface water
infiltration to the slab subgrade along the perimeter of the
house
All joints between the exterior wall and the curtain walls or
the swimming pool decking should be sealed watertight
All cracks on exterior stucco wall should be sealed watertight
All buried utility lines both water and sewer under the slabs
should be checked for leakage If any leakage is found it
should be repaired
The subdrain system behind the retaining wall should be
independent and any rock backfill over subdrain should be
covered by at least 12 inches of impervious soil backfill
It is important that the downspouts for rain rum off should not
be tied into the subdrain system The run off should be
discharged away from the house
The drainage around the house should be improved by sloping the
ground away from the house and providing swales to prevent any
ponding of water around the house
