2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The Waianae and Koolau Volcanic Shields
were built during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epochs by thinly bedded
basaltic lava flows The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the western third of
the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern two thirds
of the island The main shield building activities ceased approximately 3 5 to 2 5 million
years ago It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active and
its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in Central Oahu
The project site is located along the southern flank of Koolau The
geomorphology and subsurface conditions at the site are directly related to the
glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the
genesis of Pearl Harbor and the Honolulu Coastal Plain The subsurface conditions at
the project site are dominated by fossil Pleistocene age alluvial soil deposits and related
deposits laid during high stands of the sea that are intercalated with terrigenous
sediments The base of the stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt
Evidence from wells drilled indicate that the Island of Oahu has subsided by as much as
1 200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic activity Macdonald and Abbott
1970
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded and melted
global sea levels rose because more water was available The landmass of Oahu
remained essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in
nature These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both
higher and lower relative to the present sea level of Oahu

The higher sea level stands resulted in the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine sediments in the
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The concurrent growth of reefs and lagoonal
sediments may have also resulted in deposits of coral algal limestone and marls within
the predominantly lagoonal sedimentary units
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef
deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of the upper areas of
the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy
conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal
sediments About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred During
that rise the deep valleys in the project area were submerged Within the last
10 000 years the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine
sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal
environments resulting in thick deposits of soft harbor sediments along the coast in
areas that were formerly valleys and drainage ways Along the northern fringe of
Pearl Harbor brackish water marshes developed resulting in thick layers of organic
sediments in a lacustrine environment Land development and reclamation projects
within the last 100 years have brought the area to its present form These projects
involved the placement of man made fills over the thick lagoonal and marsh deposits

Soil Stabilization Layer
Typically the soil stabilization layer method consists of a 24 to 30 inch thick layer of
open graded crushed rock ASTM C 33 No 4 gradation wrapped in a non woven
filter fabric Mirafi 180N or equivalent below the 6 inch thick granular bedding layer
of the sewer pipes
The use of a stabilization layer below the sewer pipes is probably the most
economical of the pipe support methods mentioned earlier However this method
of pipe support may still result in differential settlement of the sewer pipes due to
the weight of the new backfill material above the proposed sewer line in areas
where significant amounts of soft soils are encountered In addition heavy
equipment and vibratory action of the compactor may exert high pressures onto the
pipes and supporting soils during the trench backfill operations resulting in
settlement of the soft soils and the proposed sewer line during construction
Inadequate overlapping of the non woven filter fabric in the stabilization layer may
also result in migration of the open graded gravel through possible openings in the
filter fabric and mix with the underlying soft soil deposits This will result in
settlement of the sewer pipe due to the loss of stabilization material into the
underlying soils Therefore it is recommended that filter fabrics placed below the
water level should have the seams sewn instead of just overlapping to ensure
containment of the open graded fill materials
Jet Grouted Column Foundations

consolidation of the soft soil deposits as a result of the weight of the new backfill
material above the proposed sewer line consideration may be given to the support
of the sewer line on jet grouted columns This construction approach would
essentially have less total and differential pipe settlement as compared to the sewer
pipes being supported on a 24 to 30 inch thick stabilization layer We envision that
the jet grouted column operations would be hindered significantly by the narrow

Micropile Foundations
Another alternative that would result in less total and differential settlement of the
sewer pipes is a micropile foundation system Micropiles are desirable because
they can be installed readily in access restrictive environments and in numerous
soil types and ground conditions The installation of the micropiles generally
causes minimal disturbance to adjacent structures the adjacent soils and the
environment As discussed in the previous subsection difficulties during column
construction and reduced integrity of jet grout columns may occur due to the
potential groundwater movement within the jet grout columns during installation as
a result of higher artesian water pressure Therefore the main advantage of using
a micropile foundation system to support the new sewer line at the project site is
the potential groundwater movement due to the higher artesian water pressure will
have less effect on the micropile foundation system as with a jet grout columns
We believe that a deep foundation system consisting of micropiles may be used In
general a micropile foundation system consists of a small diameter pile usually
less than 12 inches drilled and grouted with reinforcing The micropile foundation
typically is constructed by drilling a borehole placing steel casing and reinforcing
steel in the hole and grouting the borehole
The micropiles should have a minimum diameter of 6 inches and should be
installed into the underlying stiff medium dense alluvial deposits and residual soils
Permanent steel casing should be used within the very soft soils in the upper
portion of the micropile The steel casing will facilitate micropile construction
through the very soft soil deposits increase the corrosion resistance of the

Micropiles should be spaced a maximum of about 12 feet center to center along
the alignment of the proposed sewer line A detail of the sewer pipes supported on
a micropile foundation system is shown on the attached Concrete Beam on
Micropile Foundation Plate 3 In addition one micropile should be installed at each
corner of the proposed sewer manholes
Methods Pipe Bursting
As mentioned previously some portions of the proposed sewer line traverse
narrow utility easements located adjacent to private property The easements are in
close proximity to existing homes and other residential improvements These areas are
at the dead end on Awamoku Street and the area extending to Awanei Street These
areas may not be feasible to use conventional open trench methods Therefore
trenchless methods may be required to install the sewer pipes The pipe bursting

method may be the most feasible trenchless method to install the new sewer line
between residential areas
Pipe bursting consists of winching a pneumatic powered bursting tool through an
old section of pipe breaking and cracking the old pipe as it progresses A new pipe
section is pulled into place behind the bursting tool replacing the old pipe section High
Density Polyethylene HDPE pipe is typically used with pipe bursting
Damage to existing utilities in close proximity of the bursting tool may occur for
existing utilities generally within 3 feet of the bursting tool Based on the existing utility
plan some utility lines are within this zone In these areas the utility crossings may be
exposed and the crossing utility moved further away from the bursting tool during the
pipe installation to reduce the potential for damage
For conditions where the bursting tool is close to the existing ground surface
surface heave may occur For the conditions at the project site we estimate
approximately 5 feet of ground cover should be provided to reduce the potential for
significant ground heave
In summary we believe that pipe bursting is a viable option in areas providing
the minimum 5 feet of ground cover If the minimum ground cover is not attainable
then alternative methods should be considered

Soft soils were encountered along the proposed sewer line alignment between

be located within the limits of the existing roadways therefore sloping of the sides of
the trenches may not be possible due to the limited space Due to the above mentioned
conditions shoring of the sides of the trenches will be necessary to facilitate the
proposed trench excavations and to protect personnel working in the trench

Excavation Method
It is anticipated that conventional excavation techniques may be used for the sewer
line trench excavations In general the method and equipment to be used for
excavation should be determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and
safety considerations In addition the excavated soils from the trench excavation
should be stockpiled no closer than a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the
excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential for appreciable
ground movement
Excavation Support
In general the typical shoring method used for trench excavation is interlocking
sheet pile shoring system Typically the interlocking sheet piles are installed and
extracted by use of a vibratory hammer Differential settlements of the proposed
sewer line and existing underground utilities may occur due to the vibrations
generated from the installation and extraction of the sheet pile shoring system The
vibration from the installation and extraction of the sheet piles may also result in
Structural distress to the nearby residential structures along the sewer line
alignment
Adequate sheeting should be installed prior to the excavation and maintained tight
against the excavation walls with proper bracing during excavation The properly
braced sheeting is essential to reduce the potential for appreciable lateral
movements of the adjacent ground into the excavation which may result in
potential settlements or distress to adjacent structures or other improvements such
as the residential structures roads or utilities
It must be noted that some movements of the shoring system and the adjacent
ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation Due to
the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to accurately estimate the
magnitude of movement The magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship
such as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are installed the
subsoil conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of excavation

Use of a sheet pile shoring system may also serve as a cut off wall to aid in the

Dewatering
The field exploration indicated that groundwater levels were encountered at
depths of about 2 4 to 10 6 feet below the existing ground surface Therefore we
believe that dewatering of the trench excavations will be required for installation of the
sewer pipes
Dewatering by means of a well point or deep well system along the outside of the
excavations is generally not recommended The resultant areal depression of the
natural groundwater table could induce consolidation of the compressible subsurface
soils resulting in potential ground settlements which could affect the existing nearby
structures
Subsurface Permeability
The in situ permeability of the underlying subsurface soils at the project site was
evaluated by performing four falling head permeability tests in selected borings
The calculated permeabilities of the tested subsurface soils were on the order of
about 3 x 10 to 2 x 10 centimeters per second Details of the field permeability
tests and the test data are summarized in Appendix C
It is our opinion that the above calculated permeability value should be used with
caution due to the normally heterogeneous nature of the subsurface soils The
actual subsoil permeability may range broadly and also vary locally in terms of
orders of magnitude

SECTION 3 DICUSSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Dewatering Considerations
We suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in selection of a
suitable method of dewatering
a The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or
damage to existing buildings roads and environment
b The dewatering method should maintain stability of and also
provide safe and dry working conditions in the excavation
The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow modifications to
accommodate various ground conditions
Dewatering Precautions
The dewatering operations should be carried out without creating a quick
condition or softening at the excavation bottoms Therefore the project dewatering
operations should be performed without pumping out soil fines pumping clear
water only and should be coordinated with the shoring installation such that the
excavation stability is not adversely affected Excessive pumping which removes
soil fines may result in blowing or heaving of the excavation bottom or sides
Groundwater drawdown outside the excavation will cause additional settlement
resulting from consolidation of the very soft compressible soils Therefore the use
of a deep well system outside the excavations to draw down the groundwater level
should not be allowed
Special caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering utility trenches connected
to the excavations If this occurs the granular bedding and or backfill in the utility
trenches could act as subdrains and cause significant areal groundwater
drawdown Significant areal groundwater drawdown would result in ground
settlements and potential damage to utility lines and or other existing adjacent
structures

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
five borings extending to depths ranging from about 100 to 104 feet below the existing
ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings
were drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers
and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer or geologist who observed the drilling operations
on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual
observation and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation
of the materials encountered is presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 5
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and
drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer and torvane shear tests were performed on selected
cohesive soil samples retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an
indication of the unconfined compressive strength of the sample The torvane shear
test provides a quick estimate of the undrained shear strength of the soil Results of the
pocket penetrometer and torvane shear tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Eight Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification
Results of the test are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depth Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
Eight Sieve Analysis tests were performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate the gradation characteristics and to aid in soil classification The tests were
performed in accordance with ASTM Test Designations C 117 and C 136 Graphic
presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plates B 2 and B 3
Five Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the shear strength characteristics Results of the Direct Shear
tests are presented on Plates B 4 through B 8
One Consolidation test with time rates ASTM D 2435 was performed on a
sample of the potentially compressible soil to evaluate the compressibility
characteristics of the materials encountered Results of the consolidation test are
presented on Plate B 9
One Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression test ASTM D 2850 was
performed on a selected sample of the soil to evaluate the undrained shear strength of
the soils The results of the unconsolidated undrained triaxial compression test are
presented on Plate B 10

APPENDIX C
Permeability Testing
APPENDIX C
Permeability Testing
The permeability of the in situ soils was evaluated by performing two Falling
Head Permeability tests and two Constant Head Permeability tests at selected depths
within the borings The tests were performed at depths ranging from about 14 5 to
15 feet below the existing ground surface
The Falling Head Permeability test was performed by pumping water into the
borehole up to a reference level where the pumping of water was stopped The drop in
water height with time was then measured During the constant head testing the
borings were injected with water The flow rate was recorded by timing and calculating
the volume as a relatively constant flow rate was achieved The field permeability test
results are presented on Plates C 1 through C 4
