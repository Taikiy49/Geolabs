SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
In general our field reconnaissance indicated that the Ewa side of the subject site
appears to be underlain by colluvial deposits consisting of weakly to moderately cemented
conglomerate of cobbles and boulders with a matrix of grayish brown to brown clay silty clay
and silty sand between the larger clasts The colluvial deposit represents material that has
been eroded from the adjacent upper valleys and transported by gravity to the lower slopes
and valley floor The cemented colluvium generally has good strength both in place and as
fill although it often exhibits a moderate shrink swell potential when excavated and placed
as fill It is generally considered suitable for foundation support and as a source of fill
material if provisions are made to limit the swelling potential of the soil
The Diamond Head side of the subject site appears to be underlain by slightly to
moderately weathered basaltic bedrock deposited as lava flows Originating from the
southeast rift of the Koolau Volcano and subsequently exposed by erosion This material
has good strength characteristics and little or no shrink swell potential when excavated and

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field reconnaissance at the subject site generally encountered dense colluvial
deposits on the Ewa side to hard basalt rock formation on the Diamond Head side Based
on the relatively competent subsurface conditions observed during our field reconnaissance
and the anticipated light structural loads we believe that shallow foundations bearing on the
recompacted colluvial soils and or basalt rock formation may be used for support of the
proposed new residence It is our opinion that concrete slabs may be constructed on grade
provided that the subgrade is prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented
in the Slabs On Grade section of this report Detailed discussion and recommendations
are presented in the following sections of this report
House Foundations
Based on the generally competent subsurface conditions encountered at the site we
believe that shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the
proposed new residence An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square
foot psf may be used for the design of foundations bearing on the recompacted
near surface colluvial soils For footings bearing on the hard basalt rock formation the
allowable bearing pressure may be increased to if desired These bearing values
are for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third for transient loads such as
those caused by wind or seismic forces
The bottom of the footing excavations should be recompacted where possible to
provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to placement of reinforcing steel
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of footing excavations they
should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation
should be backfilled with select granular material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction or the footing bottom may be extended down to the underlying
competent material
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grades If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements
they should be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane
extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the
utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
Structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth

Slabs On Grade
We understand that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the garage area of the
proposed new residence To reduce the potential for structural distress of the slabs on grade
resulting from the swelling of the near surface colluvial soils we recommend that the
subgrade soils underneath the slab areas be moisture conditioned to between 2 and
5 percent above the optimum moisture and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction
The slab subgrades should be kept moist prior to placement of concrete Where
shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soils
be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or the subgrades be prepared again as
recommended herein Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to
inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and
replacement with well compacted engineered fill
For the garage slab and driveway area subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend
that a minimum 4 inch thick base course cushion be placed below the slab to provide more
uniform support of the slab The base course should consist of basalt aggregates compacted
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
The area adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly against the slab with
relatively impervious soil and graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce the
potential for water ponding around the foundation

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic pressures
A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of permeable
material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation immediately
around a perforated pipe perforations facing down at the base of the wall
discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a prefabricated
drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used instead of the
drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically
connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of the on site excavated
soils or select granular fill material Unless covered by concrete slabs the upper
12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively impervious soils to reduce the
potential for water infiltration behind the walls
Excavations
We envision that the majority of the site grading work would consist of excavations
into the existing hillside to create sufficient level areas for the house construction Based
on our field reconnaissance the site is partially underlain by weathered volcanic rock ranging
from medium hard to very hard at shallow depths In general the method and equipment to
be used for excavation should be determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and
safety considerations
It appears that the near surface colluvial soils and volcanic rock may be excavated
with normal heavy excavation equipment such as ripping with bulldozers where the rock is
near the existing ground surface However excavations into the deeper formations may
require the use of hoe rams chipping or blasting
It is our opinion that temporary cut slope inclinations up to 4H 1V or flatter may be
used for excavations in the weathered basalt rock formation during construction For cut
slopes in the dense colluvium a flatter cut slope ratio of is recommended unless
special stabilization measures are incorporated into the cut Slope design The temporary
excavations should be cleaned of loose materials and should conform to the requirements
of local state and federal regulations

Imported fill materials if required should consist of non expansive granular material
such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded
from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should
have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent
or less Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for conformance with
these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its intended use
Select granular fill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 10 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to
the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the
same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density

scarification process and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those
encountered in our field exploration
