PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project initially consisted of the installation of two retaining walls at the
northwestern end of Aalapapa Way in Lanikai on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project
site is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 One of the walls is a 30 foot long by 9 foot high
CRM wall with 6 foot long wingwalls that will be installed near the top of the slope The
second wall will be installed near the lower part of the slope and consists of a 15 foot long
by 9 foot high CRM wall The second wall is planned above the existing CRM wall near
the bottom of the slope The new walls were being installed because of concerns
regarding the existing slope erosion and potential hazard to vehicles that travel on the
roadway at the top of the slope
We understand that it is desired to develop remedial alternatives to address the
slope erosion concern for the project In addition we understand that the installation of a
guardrail system at the top of the slope may be suitable for the project

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene

Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island As volcanic
activity in Waianae Volcano ceased lava flows from Koolau Volcano banked against its
eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau known as Schofield Plateau Both
Waianae and Koolau Ranges have lost most of their original shield outlines and are now
long narrow ridges shaped largely by erosion
The rocks of the Koolau Volcano are divided into the Kailua Volcanic Series and the
Koolau Volcanic Series The lava of the Kailua Volcanic Series has been altered by
hydrodynamic action of rising steam within the ancient Koolau caldera The lava of the
Koolau Volcanic Series lies outside the caldera
The project area is generally composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of
lavas of the Kailua Volcanic Series In situ chemical weathering of the lava flows has
occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years The weathering process has formed a mantle of
residual and saprolitic soils
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 to depths of about 20 2 and 32 feet below
the existing ground surface The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2
Our field exploration generally encountered a surface fill layer about 5 5 to 6 feet
thick underlain by residual and saprolitic soils to about 6 to 15 5 feet deep The residual
and saprolitic soils were underlain by a basalt rock formation extending to the maximum
depth explored of 32 feet below the existing ground surface
The fill layer consisted of soft to stiff clayey silt and sandy gravel The residual and
saprolitic soils were comprised of very stiff to hard silty clay and clayey silt with weathered
gravel The basalt rock formation was soft to very hard and moderate to slightly
weathered
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration
It should be noted that groundwater levels at the project site may fluctuate depending on
seasonal precipitation and other factors Detailed descriptions of the materials
encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2 in
Appendix A Generalized geologic cross sections are presented on Plates 3 and 4
Laboratory tests were performed on selected soil samples and the results are presented
in Appendix B Photographs of the core samples are presented in Appendix C

Existing Conditions and Potential Cause of Slope Erosion
The existing slope was covered with grass light brush and small trees at the time
of our site reconnaissance It appears that some areas on the slope have been eroded
with bare soil exposed at the slope surface The existing slope ranges from about
two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V to inclination There are isolated areas on
the slope with steeper inclination due to the erosion An unpaved roadway is at the top of
the slope It appears that the surface runoff water from the areas above the roadway flows
down to the roadway and onto the slope face Based on our site observations and field
exploration we believe that the slope erosion is being caused by the surface water
entering from the top of slope area and traveling down the slope face

In general foundations should be embedded a minimum of 2 feet below the lowest
adjacent finished grades Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should
be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the
bottom edge of the utility trench or they should extend to a depth as deep as the
inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging
adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the
potential for appreciable foundation settlement
Based on a service limit state bearing pressure of 3 500 pounds per square
foot psf we estimate that foundation settlements under the anticipated design
loads for foundations bearing on the stiff to very stiff clays and silts to be less than

Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges line loads and point loads within a

Dynamic lateral earth forces due to seismic loading may be estimated

Remedial Alternatives Near Top of Slope
At the top of the slope the following alternatives to reduce the potential hazard to
vehicles near the top of slope may be considered in lieu of the initial CRM retaining wall
option
L Shaped Concrete Retaining Wall with Barrier Face
Because of the limited space at the top of the slope and the close proximity to the
slope face the installation of an L shaped concrete retaining wall with barrier face
would be required to resist the vehicle impact to the barrier Because of the large
lateral load the heel of the wall footing is estimated to extend close to the property
line Therefore the existing roadway would need to be closed for construction of
the new wall Retaining wall recommendations provided above may be used
Concrete Barrier Wall Supported by Micropiles
The use of a concrete barrier wall supported by micropiles may be considered to
resist the vehicle impact force on the wall In general a micropile foundation system
consists of a small diameter usually less than 12 inches drilled and grouted pile
with steel reinforcing The micropile foundation typically is constructed by drilling a
borehole placing steel casing and reinforcing steel in the hole and grouting the
borehole Micropiles are desirable because they can be installed readily in access
restrictive environments and in numerous soil types and ground conditions In
addition installation of the micropiles generally causes minimal disturbance to
adjacent structures the adjacent soils and the environment The advantage of
utilizing micropiles is that the footing is significantly reduced and therefore the
amount of excavation required for footing construction is significantly reduced
Guardrail Supported on Post Foundation
We understand that the use of guardrails supported on post foundation at the top of
the slope is acceptable to the City and County of Honolulu It should be noted that
this alternative does not resist the vehicle impact force like the alternatives above
This alternative will be less costly compared to the above remedial alternatives

Installation of Grouted Rubble Paving GRP
To reduce the potential for further slope erosion the installation of GRP on the
slope face may be considered Prior to installation of the GRP loose material on
the slope face should be removed The GRP should be keyed at the top and
bottom of the slope
Installation of Permanent Turf Reinforcement and Geogrid
The installation of permanent turf reinforcement mats and geogrids may be
considered to reduce the amount of slope erosion We recommend that the
permanent turf reinforcement mats consist of North American Green C350 or
equivalent In addition Mirafi BX1200 or equivalent should be used for the
geogrids The turf mats and the geogrids should be anchored at the top and
bottom of the slope and with pins staples along the face of the slope After
installation of the turf mats and geogrids the slope face should be hydro seeded to
establish vegetation on the slope face
Surface Water Drainage
In addition to the remedial measures above the surface water on top of the
roadway should be diverted away from the top of slope to prevent further slope erosion
The diverted surface water should be properly disposed of to prevent erosion to adjacent
properties These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
remedial measures recommended in this report

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 to depths of about 20 2 and 32 feet below
the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight
augers and coring equipment
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Soil samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 84 and D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the
sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 to 24 inch drive are shown on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113 Diamond
Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a
hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit is
mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Pocket Penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides and indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
