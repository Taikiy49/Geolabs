PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of this investigation was to evaluate the subsurface
conditions at the project site in order to develop data for site
planning and design recommendations for grading and foundation
construction

Due to the lack of available excavating and or drilling equipment on
the island of Tutuila our investigation was limited to 12 shallow
seismic refraction surveys visual observations of the surface
conditions at the site and visual observations of operations at the
nearby Government of American Samoa Quarry
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located in the Tafuma area of Tutuila Island
American Samoa approximately 2000 feet west of Pago Pago International
Airport at the intersection of Airport Road and the FAA Receiver site
access road The site is rectangular in plan view with dimensions of
300 feet by 600 feet and encompasses approximately 4 acres in area
The ground surface is hummocky and slopes very gently towards the
east The topography generally reflects the original surface of
basaltic lava flows with minor modification from possible previous
grading work
The site has moderately heavy vegetative cover of tropical trees
weeds ferns and vines such as haole koa
and wedelia Some evidence of previous
habitation was observed in several low stone walls noted on the site
Samoan names in parenthesis

It appears that portions of the southern half of the site were
previously graded possibly as borrow site for clinker There were
also several piles of rubbish and debris in various areas of the
site The only existing structure on the site is an abandoned concrete
bunker in the central portion of the site near the southern boundary
Access to the site for seismic surveys and visual reconnaissance was
by hand cut trails through the vegetative cover
The elevation of the site is on the order of about 25 feet above sea
level

It is anticipated that the main building and equipment shed will be of
steel framed construction and will be lightly loaded single story
structures
The access roads and parking areas will be paved with asphaltic

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The island of Tutuila was built during the Pliocene and early Pleis
tocene by basaltic lavas extruded from four main eruptive centers
Following the cessation of this main shield building activity there
was a long period of erosion accompanied by the growth of fringing
coral reefs During the late Quaternary vulcanism was renewed and
the Leone volcanics consisting of pyroclastics and lava flows
covered an extensive coral reef on the southern end of the island
forming the Leone Plain Stearns 1941
The project site is located on the eastern edge of the Leone Plain
and is underlain by basaltic lava flows of the Leone formation The
lavas are relatively fresh and unweathered despite the high rainfall
experienced in the area approximately 200 inches per year
Basaltic lava flows generally occur in two forms pahoehoe and a a
Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth ropy or billowy surface and
frequently contains voids or lava tubes A a is characterized by a
rough clinkery surface and has a dense inner core
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1182 00 Page 5
The lava flows observed at the project site are at a transitional
phase between the two forms and have characteristics of each i e
much of the surface is rough blocky clinker however some openings
leading to lava tubes were observed at the surface
Field Exploration
Our field exploration consisted of visual observations of the project
site and its vicinity observations of excavation operations at the
nearby Government Quarry and the performance of 12 shallow seismic
refraction surveys on the site
The seismic surveys were performed using a Bison Model 1570 C
Refraction Seismograph receiving seismic signals generated by the
impact of a 10 pound sledge hammer The results were recorded and
interpreted by a geologist from our firm
Soil Conditions
Our field explorations encountered volcanic formation from the ground
surface to about 19 feet the maximum depth detectable by our seismic
refraction survey methods The volcanic formation consists generally
of medium hard to hard gray vesicular basalt with a surficial
layer of medium dense gravel and cobbles clinker Surface openings
into voids lava tubes up to 2 feet in diameter were observed in
several areas of the site

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The field exploration disclosed basically competent foundation bearing
basaltic formation at the proposed project site From a soil engin
eering point of view the site is suitable for the proposed develop
ment
Shallow spread or continuous foundations may be used for the proposed
structures
The volcanic formation is non expansive therefore Slab on grade
construction may also be used
Site Grading
Relatively shallow cuts and fills are anticipated for the site
The site should be cleared of vegetation debris and other deleterious
material prior to the commencement of earthwork These materials
should be disposed of properly off site
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1182 00 Page 7
Prior to the placement of structural fill the existing ground surface
should be track rolled with a heavy dozer D8H or larger for a
minimum of 4 passes This will serve to densify any loose surface
materials and to collapse any near surface voids
Fill and backfill should be placed in 10 inch loose horizontal lifts
and compacted to obtain 95 of its maximum dry density as determined
by ASTM D 1557
Fills placed on slopes steeper than five horizontal to one vertical
5 1 should be keyed and benched into firm natural ground
Two horizontal to one vertical 2 1 fill slopes are recommended Cut
slopes of one horizontal to one vertical 1 1 may be utilized
Based on previous experience with similar material it is our opinion
that the on site volcanic formation is generally rippable by a D9G
dozer or its equivalent however isolated ledges of very hard rock
may be encountered in localized areas These ledges may require
special excavating techniques such as hoeram or blasting The
excavated rock material can for the most part be broken down to
workable size by dozer tracking
GEOLABS HAWAII
Excavated on site volcanic material generally less than 12 inches in
size may be used as fill material In this case the rocky fill may
be placed in 18 inch loose horizontal lifts and track rolled with a
heavy dozer until the required density is attained usually seven to
nine passes Due to the coarse granular nature of this material and
the resultant difficulty in taking density tests we strongly recommend
that the soils engineer continuously monitor the placement of all rock
fills
If imported material is required it should consist of well graded
crushed rock material generally less than 6 inches in size Other
materials such as volcanic cinder or fine grained soils less than 1
swell according to the CBR Swell test method may be utilized provided
that they are submitted to the soils engineer for testing and approval
a minimum of 20 working days prior to their intended delivery to the
project site
Foundations
Spread or continuous foundation are recommended for the proposed
structures
The bottom of all footing excavations should be compacted to 95
maximum dry density prior to the placement of reinforcing steel
except when the footing will bear directly on in situ rock
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1182 00 Page 9
For the light loading anticipated an allowable bearing value of
5000 p s f is recommended for footings bearing on in situ volcanic
formation For footings bearing on compacted structural fill a
bearing value of 2500 p s f may be used to design the footings The
above values are for total dead plus live loads and may be increased
one third when temporary wind and seismic loads are considered
A minimum footing width of 18 inches should be utilized and the bottom
of all footings should be placed in minimum of 12 inches below the
lowest adjacent grade In general the outside heel of the footing
should be set back a minimum distance of 5 feet away from the nearest
outside slope face This set back may require deepening of the
footing embedment to maintain the above specified minimum set back
For footing on rock the set back requirement may be reduced to a
minimum of 3 feet away from the slope face
To reduce the possibility of footing settlement due to possible large
cavities or lava tubes encountered near the footing bottom elevation
in the underlying lava rock drilled probes are recommended below
every major footing or on 10 foot centers for continuous footings
The probe holes which could be drilled with a track percussion drill
should have a minimum diameter of 2 inches and extend below the bottom
of the footing a depth equal to two times the footing width or a
minimum of 6 feet
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1182 00 Page 10
If a cavity is suspected below a footing additional probe holes
should be made to delineate the horizontal extend of the cavity
Voids greater than 6 inches in diameter should be exposed or grouted
on the recommendations of the soils engineer The probing and
grouting operations should be monitored by the soils engineer to see
that the aforementioned recommendations are carried out properly and
to evaluate other solutions to properly backfill potential voids which
May occur near the footing elevations
Surface water should be diverted away from the building foundations
Anticipated settlements are expected to be relatively small on the
order of less than one half of an inch for structures bearing on rock
or on compacted structural fill
Lateral Resistance
To resist lateral loads a coefficient of friction of 0 5 between the
foundations and underlying rock materials is recommended
In addition a passive soil resistance of 300 p c f per foot depth
may be used For footing against neat rock excavation the passive
soil resistance of 3000 p s f may be used
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1182 00 Page 11
Slab On Grade
Slabs should be placed over a 4 inch cushion layer of gravel generally
less than three fourths of an inch and larger than one fourth of an
inch in size
Slabs on grade on structural fill should have perimeter footings
extending down a minimum of 8 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
to reduce moisture infiltration below the slab
The slab subgrade should be kept moist and protected from drying prior
to concrete placement in order to minimize slab cracking due to
dehydration Subgrade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 of the
maximum dry density established by ASTM D 1557 78
Pavements
Based on observation of the on site materials and our previous
experience with these types of materials the preliminary pavement
design is as follows
2 inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 inch Base Course
8 inch Total Pavement Thickness
CBR tests should be performed on the actual subgrade materials as they
are encountered in the field during construction particularly if fine
grained soil or other imported material is used The above design
section may be revised on the basis of the tests
GEOLABS HAWAII
The use of volcanic cinder as base course is not recommended as past
experience has shown that this material tends to deteriorate rapidly
under traffic load conditions in wet climates
Trenching
It is anticipated that any utility trenching will encounter in situ
volcanic formation Shallow trenching may be possible with the use of
conventional excavating equipment although some areas of hard excava
tion may be encountered Trenches deeper than about 5 feet may
require special excavating techniques such as hoeram jackhammer or
blasting
We do not anticipate that shoring or bracing will be required for
trenches less than 5 feet however this decision should be left to
the contractor to determine the safety of his own workers
Additionally the contractor should follow local practices and
regulations regarding these matters
Design Review
Plans for site grading should be forwarded to the soils engineer for
review and written comments prior to construction to see that the
intent of our recommendations is included in the project plans and
specifications
GEOLABS HAWAII

CONSTRUCTION MONITORING
It is recommended that the grading operations be observed by the soils
engineer during construction This is to see that adequate compaction
has been obtained and the subsurface conditions are consistent with
the findings of our subsurface investigation The recommendations
given in this report are contingent upon the above inspections

of excavation operations at the nearby Government Quarry and

the site

generated by the impact of a 10 pound sledge hammer The
surveys were laid out on 100 foot length spreads hand cut
through the dense vegetation on the site The signal enhance

free signal as the result of repeated blows of the sledge
hammer The results which were recorded and interpreted
by a geologist from our firm are presented on Plates A 1
