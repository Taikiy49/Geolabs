SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our previous overview study, the anticipated subsurface conditions at the 
project site consist of medium dense calcareous sediments and hard coralline limestone 
and coral formations.  Alluvial deposits consisting of brown clayey soil may be interbedded 
in the calcareous sediments and coralline limestone and coral formations. Based on our 
site reconnaissance, a thin surficial layer of sandy silt was observed in some areas of the 
project site.  We anticipate that the surficial layer of sandy silt may be up to 1 foot thick.  
Based on the existing ground surface elevations, groundwater is generally not anticipated 
to be a consideration for this project. 
Based on the available information, deep fill areas on the order of about 15 to 
30 feet high are planned at the south end of Area 5D and at the central area of Area 5H.  
To fill these deep fill areas, we understand that expansive soil materials generated from 
the Harborside Channel borrow site, which is between Kalaeloa Boulevard and Kalaeloa 
Harbor, will be used as fill material.  We believe that these expansive clayey soils may be 
used as general fill for this site, provided that the clayey soil fill is placed at least 2 percent 
above the optimum moisture content, compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction, and capped with at least 3 feet of structural fill.  The 3-foot thick layer of 
capping fill should be placed over the entire area of the project site containing the 
expansive clayey soil fill, since the locations of the future improvements have not been 
finalized and there may be a significant lag time between the mass grading work and 
construction of the future improvements. 
The Kapolei area is generally considered a dry climate area; therefore, the potential 
for the clayey soil fill to dry out during the mass grading work is high.  Shrinkage cracks will 
develop in the clayey soil fill if these soils are allowed to dry.  To reduce the potential for 
the development of shrinkage cracks, we recommend watering the clayey soil fill surface 
to maintain the in-situ moisture of at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content.  
Care should be taken to not over-water the clayey soil fill to avoid saturating and softening 
of the clayey soil fill.    
If shrinkage cracks develop at the clayey soil fill surface, we recommend preparing 
the fill surface by scarifying the surface a minimum of 12 inches, moisture-conditioning the 
clayey soils to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture, and recompacting the 
clayey soils to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.   
In areas requiring fill on the order of about 20 feet thick, we anticipate ground 
settlements will occur.  Based on the results of our exploration, the anticipated ground

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was mainly built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western portion of the island.  
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene 
(Ice Age) in age and forms the bulk of the northern and eastern portions of the island.  
As the older Waianae volcanic activity ceased, younger lava flows from Koolau 
continued to bank against Waianae’s east facing slopes forming a broad upland, known 
as the Schofield Plateau. The Koolau Volcano reached the end of its main 
shield-building phase approximately 2 million years ago.   
Chemical weathering and physical erosion of the volcanic uplands generated 
copious terrestrial sediments, which were transported down slope toward the coastline. 
The terrestrial and embedded marine sediments accumulated to comprise the low 
topographic gradient coastal plain known as the Ewa Plain.  In the project vicinity 
(Ko Olina) and further towards the east and northeast (East Kapolei), the terrestrial 
sediments accumulated and became interbedded with other marine sediments and 
hardened coral-algal and coral limestone reef formations to form a thick sedimentary 
wedge of material fronting the mountain foothills.  The thickness of the collective 
sedimentary wedge ranges from nearly absent in the area underlain by the Interstate 
Route H-1 Highway to over 1,000 feet at the shoreline near Ewa Beach.  This wedge of 
sedimentary and calcareous reef materials comprising the Ewa Plain serves as the 
confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu.  
Deposition of recent or Holocene age sediments has continued from earlier geologic 
time through the present.   
During the Pleistocene Epoch, the global sea level fluctuated in response to 
cycles of continental glaciation.  As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of 
the sea.  When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available 
such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level.

The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of

Site Grading 
Based on the conceptual grading plan provided, cuts on the order of about 15 to

generated at the borrow site, the clayey soils have a high swell potential when 
subjected to moisture fluctuations; therefore, we believe that a minimum 3-foot thick 
layer of structural fill should be used up to the finished subgrade elevations.  The 3-foot 
thick layer of structural fill should be maintained below future structures (concrete 
flatwork and pavements).

3.1.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly.  Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and 
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off-site.  Soft 
and/or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill should be over-excavated to expose firm materials, and 
the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fills.  The 
excavated soft and/or organic soils should be disposed properly off-site and/or used 
in landscape areas, where appropriate.  Contract documents should include 
additive and deductive unit prices for over-excavation and compacted fill placement 
to account for variations in the over-excavation quantities. 
Cavities and/or voids are encountered commonly in coralline limestone and coral 
formations. In areas where coralline limestone and coral formations are exposed at 
the finished subgrade in cut areas or areas to receive fill, special attention should 
be given to the subgrade preparation to reduce the potential for distress to the 
future improvements. 
The finished subgrade in cut areas or areas to receive fill that expose coralline 
limestone or coral formations should be proof-rolled with a minimum 10-ton

3.1.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, we believe that the excavated soil materials may be used as a source of 
fill materials provided that the materials are properly processed and placed in 
accordance with the “Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements” subsection 
herein.  The excavated on-site soils to be used as fills should be free of vegetation, 
deleterious materials, and rock fragments greater than 6 inches in maximum

soils are allowed to dry.  We recommend watering the clayey soil fill surface to 
maintain the in-situ moisture of at least 2 percent of the optimum moisture content.  
Care should be taken to not over-water the clayey fill to avoid saturating and 
softening of the clayey fill.

3.1.5 Cut and Fill Slopes 
It appears that most of the cut slopes would expose the reef formation consisting of 
medium dense calcareous sediments and hard coralline limestone and coral 
formations.  In general, we believe that cut slopes may be made at an inclination of 
two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter.   
Fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter.  If the 
existing ground surface is steeper that 5H:1V, fills should be keyed and benched 
into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding.  The filling 
operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal 
compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations. 
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope 
ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. Track-rolling of slopes should not be 
allowed.  In the event that over-cutting of a slope occurs, keying and benching 
requirements should be implemented instead of backfilling the slope to the design 
grade with sliver fills.  Water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and 
slope planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for 
erosion of the finished slopes.

3.2 
Ground Settlements 
Ground settlements are anticipated when fill of about 20 feet thick or more are 
placed over the existing ground to raise the site to the finished elevations.  These 
settlements will affect earthwork quantity estimates for the project.  Based on the results 
of our exploration, the anticipated ground settlements are primarily the result of the 
following: 
compression of the fill material under its own weight 
compression of the in-situ soils induced by the new fill loads 
Based on our analyses, total settlement from compression of the fill material 
under its own weight is on the order of about 3 inches.  We believe that a settlement 
waiting period of about 8 to 12 weeks should be incorporated into the construction 
schedule for the project.  The settlement waiting period should commence after 
completion of the mass grading work.  It should be recognized that it is difficult to 
accurately predict the exact time required for the filled ground to settle, because the 
settlement rates will be affected by variations of the subsoil structure. 
It is possible that the estimated settlement period could vary by as much as 50 to 
100 percent from the actual settlement period.  Therefore, the actual settlement rates 
should be monitored, and a settlement monitoring program should be established to 
evaluate the magnitude and rate of the estimated settlements during the settlement 
waiting period prior to construction of future improvements.  In addition, provisions 
should be made for potential delays in the construction schedule if a longer settlement 
waiting period is required. 
To monitor the actual settlement rate, we recommend installing settlement 
gauges at areas requiring fill of about 20 feet thick or more.  Settlement gauges should 
be installed 12 inches below the finished grades after completion of mass grading work.  
A qualified professional surveyor should optically read the settlement gauges, and 
should transmit the readings in a timely manner to Geolabs for review.  The locations 
and number of settlement gauges should be determined once the filling schedule is 
determined or when the fills approach the finished grade.

3.3 
Manhole/Retaining Structures 
We envision that manholes and some retaining structures may be required. In 
general, we recommend using shallow foundations bearing on structural fill or the 
on-site reef formation to support these planned structures. The following general 
guidelines may be used for design of the manholes and retaining structures. 
3.3.1 Foundations 
Based on subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend using an 
allowable bearing pressure of 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) to design the 
foundations. In general, we recommend embedding the foundations for the 
retaining structures a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished 
grade. If the footings are designed in accordance with our recommendations, total 
settlements of the structures are estimated to be on the order of 1 inch or less. 
Where foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements, the foundation 
should be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from 
the bottom edge of the utility trench, or the footing should be extended to a depth 
as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This requirement is necessary to avoid 
surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with additional structural loads and to 
reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement. 
Lateral loads acting on the foundations may be resisted by friction between the 
base of the foundations and the bearing materials and by passive earth pressure 
developed against the vertical faces of footings and/or walls. A coefficient of 
friction of 0.40 may be used for foundations bearing on structural fill or the on-site 
reef formation. Resistance to lateral loads due to passive earth pressure may be 
calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot 
of depth (pcf). These values assume that the concrete for footings is cast neat 
against the surrounding soils or the surrounding soils will be well compacted. 
Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in the upper 
12 inches below the finished grade should be neglected. 
It is important that a Geolabs representative observe the footing excavations to 
evaluate the competency of the bearing materials. If unsuitable materials are

3.3.4 Drainage 
Retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic 
pressures.  A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide zone of 
permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), 
placed directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at the base of 
the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes.  As an alternative, a 
prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used 
instead of the drainage material.  The prefabricated drainage product should also 
be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the structure. 
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of compacted on-site 
materials or granular compacted fills, where specified by the designer.  Unless 
covered by concrete slabs, the upper 12 inches of backfill should also consist of 
low-expansion, relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential for excessive 
water infiltration behind the walls.

3.5 
Utility Trench 
It is anticipated that the utilities for the proposed development will primarily 
consist of water, sewer, and drain lines. In general, good construction practices should 
be utilized for the installation and backfilling of the trenches for the proposed utilities. 
The contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used for excavation, 
subject to practical limits and safety considerations. The excavation should comply with 
all applicable local, state, and federal safety requirements. The contractor should be 
responsible for trench shoring design and installation. Trench shoring and bracing 
should conform to the appropriate health and safety requirements. 
In general, we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) to support the utility lines. The initial 
trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes should consist of free-draining 
backfills, such as No. 3B Fine gravel, to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes 
from compaction of the backfill. It is critical to use a free-draining granular material to
