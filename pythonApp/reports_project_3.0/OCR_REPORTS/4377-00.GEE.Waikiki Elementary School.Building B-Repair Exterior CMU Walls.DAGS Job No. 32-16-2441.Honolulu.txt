INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Waikiki Elementary School Building B Repair Exterior CMU Walls
The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for
the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input only and are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

The project site is located within the existing Waikiki Elementary School in the Waikiki
area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii as shown on the Project Location Map

Monsarrat Avenues in the Waikiki area

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the
western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern
two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still
active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in Central Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of Koolau and its geomorphology and
subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea levels
during the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain The
subsurface conditions in the Waikiki area are dominated by fossil Pleistocene Age coral algal
reef deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of the sea which are intercalated
with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau
Basalt
During the later Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result
of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world These glacio eustatic
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower relative to the
present sea level of Oahu About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in the sea level
occurred During that rise the valleys in the project area were immersed In the last
10 000 years or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine
sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments
resulting in thick deposits of soft loose sediments along the coast in the areas which were
formerly valleys and drainageways

Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have brought
the Waikiki area to its present form Many of the resulting fills are of poor quality in terms of
supporting heavy structural loads

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered a surficial layer of fill consisting of very stiff
to hard silty clays extending to depths of about 5 5 to 6 5 feet below the existing ground
surface The surficial fill layer was underlain by very dense silty sands extending to depths
of about 7 5 to 8 5 feet below the existing ground surface The silty sands were underlain
by medium dense to very dense coralline sands and gravel extending to the maximum
depths explored of about 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface The boring drilled at
the existing exterior concrete walkway encountered about 3 5 inches thick of concrete
underlain by 6 inches of granular fill material consisting of medium dense gravelly sands
The granular fill was underlain by stiff silty clay fills extending to the maximum depth explored

of about 4 5 feet below the existing ground surface Groundwater was encountered at a
depth of about 16 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration
Our laboratory tests indicate that the surficial silty clay fills exhibit high swelling
potential when subjected to moisture fluctuation Based on our observations the existing
CMU walls along the corridor of Building B are constructed on the existing concrete slabs on
grade In addition poor drainage was observed at the ground surface adjacent to the
existing exterior concrete walkway slab We believe that the distress of the CMU walls is
likely due to swelling of the near surface silty clays beneath the existing concrete slab The
swelling of the near surface silty clay fills is likely caused by the increase in moisture content
due to the poor drainage adjacent to the exterior slabs
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe that
the on site silty clays should provide suitable foundation support for the new CMU walls We
recommend that a shallow foundation system consisting of continuous footings be used to
support the new CMU walls The soils encountered at the footing subgrade level should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the
footing subgrade level the materials should be removed to expose the underlying firm
materials and replaced with compacted fill material
We envision that some new concrete slabs on grade will be constructed within
Classrooms B 2 and B 3 as part of the repair work Due to the high swelling potential of
the near surface silty clays when subjected to moisture fluctuations there is a potential for
distress to the future concrete slabs on grade when subjected to moisture fluctuations To
reduce the potential for structural distress to the future slabs on grade resulting from the
potential swelling of the near surface silty clays we recommend that the new slabs on
grades be supported on at least 18 inches of non expansive select granular fill materials

CMU Wall Foundations
As previously mentioned the existing CMU walls along the corridor of Building B are
constructed on the existing concrete slabs on grade We believe that the distress to the
CMU walls is likely due to swelling of the near surface silty clays beneath the existing
concrete slab Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we
believe that the silty clay fills encountered at the project site should provide suitable
foundation support for the new CMU walls along the corridor of Building B In general we
recommend that a shallow foundation system consisting of continuous footings be used to
support the new CMU walls An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per
square foot psf may be used for design of footings bearing on the recompacted on site silty
clays This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3
for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
Due to the nature of the on site soils footings should be embedded a minimum of
36 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grades Footings located adjacent to existing
footings should be embedded deep enough to avoid surcharging the existing footings If
foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be embedded
below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility
trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid
surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce
the potential for appreciable foundation settlement
The soils at the footing subgrade level should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete Relative compaction refers to the in place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the
water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the footing subgrade level they
should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation
GEOLABS INC
W O 4377 00 oo Page 9
should be backfilled with select granular fill material compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for footings
bearing on the recompacted in situ soils as recommended herein to be less than 1 inch with
differential settlements on the order of about 0 5 inches
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 30 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted soils Resistance due
to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds
per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soils around footings are well
compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper
12 inches of soil should be neglected
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of Geolabs
prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the foundation bearing
conditions and the required embedment depths
Slabs On Grade
We envision that some new concrete slabs on grade will be constructed within
laboratory tests indicate that the surficial silty clays encountered in our borings exhibit high
potential for swelling when subjected to moisture fluctuations To reduce the potential for
structural distress to the slabs on grade resulting from the swelling of the clayey soils we
recommend that the subgrade soils underneath the slab areas be properly prepared and
capped with at least 18 inches of non expansive select granular fill material capping fill
below the slab cushion
GEOLABS INC
W O 4377 00 Page 10
The non expansive select granular fill material should be placed as soon as possible
after the subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented
in the Site Preparation section of this report The prepared capping fill and the underlying
subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist until the final placement of concrete
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that
the soils be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or prepared again Saturation and
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage
may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted fill
In addition to the 18 inches of non expansive select granular fill a minimum 4 inch
thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
should be placed below the slabs The open graded gravel cushion fill would serve as a
capillary moisture break and would provide for uniform support of the slabs To reduce the
potential for future moisture infiltration through the slab and subsequent damage to floor
coverings an impervious moisture vapor barrier such as plastic membrane is recommended
on top of the No 3B Fine gravel cushion layer Two inches of moist sand may be considered
above the plastic membrane to provide for protection of the membrane and to aid in curing
of the slab concrete Provision of the 2 inches of fine sand above the vapor barrier will be
left up to the discretion of the project structural engineer
In addition we envision that the exterior concrete walkways within the exterior corridor
of Building B fronting Classrooms B 2 and B 3 will be constructed to replace the existing
concrete walkways We also recommend that the concrete walkways be supported on at
least 18 inches of non expansive select granular fill In addition crack control joints should
be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways and at right angles The
thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at least
12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade Areas adjacent to the slab should be
backfilled tightly against the slab edges with relatively impervious soils and graded to divert
water away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the foundations

Site Preparation
We envision that minimal site grading work will be required for the proposed project
construction In general the following guidelines should be used for site grading work At
the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly
cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be
removed and disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas encountered during
clearing below areas designated to receive fill or future improvements should be
over excavated to expose firm or dense material and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with well compacted fill The excavated soft and or organic soils should be
properly disposed of off site Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit
prices for over excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the over
excavation quantities
Slabs and foundations of existing structures to be demolished should be completely
removed Over excavations resulting from the demolition work should be backfilled with
compacted fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the
resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with select granular fill material The fill
material should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in
6 to 8 inch loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
After clearing and demolition areas beneath future slabs on grade should be
over excavated where necessary to provide the minimum 18 inch thick capping layer of
non expansive select granular fill material below the bottom of the cushion fill layer Prior
to placement of the non expansive select granular fill the subgrade soils should be scarified
to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density

that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or that the soil be prepared again
as recommended above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due

Drainage
As previously mentioned the existing grades outside the edge of the existing concrete
walkway appears to provide for poor drainage Because of the high swelling potential of the
on site silty clays due to moisture fluctuations the finished grades outside the structures
should be sloped to shed water away from foundations and slabs and to reduce the
potential for ponding Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should
also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms
and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils
GEOLABS INC
W O 4377 00 Page 13
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be properly
backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce
the potential for water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible
and should be maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the foundations and slabs

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths of approximately 16 5
feet below the existing ground surface In addition a boring was drilled at the exterior
concrete walkway adjacent to the classrooms The approximate locations of the borings are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 Boring Nos 1 and 2 were drilled using a truck mounted drill
rig equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers Boring No 3 was drilled using
concrete coring machine and a hand auger
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Four 1 inch ring swell tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the
swelling potential of the near surface clayey soils The test results are presented on
Plate B 1
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of
the test are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
