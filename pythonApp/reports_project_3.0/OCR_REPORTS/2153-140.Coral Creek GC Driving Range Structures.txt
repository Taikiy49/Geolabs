INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed restroom kiosk and driving range structures to be located at
the Coral Creek Golf Course in the Ewa area on the island of Oahu Hawaii The
general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 7
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field reconnaissance previous field explorations
previous mass grading construction observations and engineering analyses These
recommendations are intended for the design of foundations for the proposed restroom
kiosk driving range tee off structures and driving range light poles only The findings
and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of
this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age As volcanic activity in Waianae ended lava flows from Koolau
banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as the
Schofield Plateau or Central Oahu
The forces of weathering and erosion attacked this plateau generating sediments
which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and further south
these alluvial sediments accumulated and interbedded with marine sediments and
coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge ranging in thickness from
zero feet in the area of H 1 Freeway to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms
the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal
aquifers of southern Oahu Deposition of these sediments has continued from earlier
geologic time through the present

Light Pole Loading Conditions
Based on the information provided we understand that the proposed driving range
lights will be supported by tapered cylindrical hollow galvanized steel poles
approximately 25 to 50 feet in height above the ground surface The pole is
tapered from 24 inch diameter at the base to 12 inch diameter at the top The
poles will be connected to the shaft foundation by means of fitting the hollow steel
poles over a solid prestressed reinforced concrete base of about 24 inches in
diameter The reinforced concrete base will extend to about 3 inches above the
bottom of the drilled shaft and then concrete will be poured in the annulus The
vertical overlap of the steel pole and the concrete base will be about 4 to 5 feet
and the concrete base will extend above the ground surface by about 2 feet The
pole assembly is estimated to range in weight from about 2 500 to 3 500 pounds
The poles will be relatively light but they will be subjected to high ground line
overturning moment torsional moment and lateral forces at the foundation level
These loading values are based on a combination of dead live and wind loads
estimated for a 25 to 50 foot high pole with 10 to 20 lights at the top manufactured
W O 2153 140 Page 9
by Musco Sports Lighting Inc and for 100 mph sustained winds in an Exposure C
Zone The lights are assumed to be arranged in two rows The assembly with
10 lights is estimated to have a height of 11 feet a width of 22 feet and a surface
area of about 182 square feet The assembly with 20 lights is estimated to have a
height of 11 feet a width of 44 feet and a surface area of about 363 square feet

Light Pole Torsion Resistance
In general pole foundations will be subjected to torsional moments resulting in
torsion on the shaft foundations Torsion may be resisted by the side shear along
the shaft surface and adjacent soils In our foundation analyses we have
evaluated the shafts subjected to the torsional moments computed using the
lateral force acting upon the light pole and light fixtures Based on our analyses
we believe that the 48 inch diameter shafts extending to the length recommended
should be capable of resisting the torsional moments without significant
movements
However for additional lights beyond a total of 20 based on a two row
arrangement of lights the recommended foundation depth will increase

significantly or a two pile foundation system will be necessary to handle the
torsion
We wish to emphasize that the sides of the holes should be relatively free of loose
materials and the concrete for the shaft should have close contact with the in situ
soils after the shaft has been constructed to provide the torsional resistance
required
Light Pole Construction Considerations
The performance of shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor s method
of construction and construction procedures As a result we recommend that a
representative from Geolabs Hawaii be present to observe the installation of
excavated and drilled shafts during construction In our opinion the following may
have a significant impact on the effectiveness and cost of the shaft foundations
To reduce the potential for caving in of the drilled holes temporary casing of the
drilled holes may be needed during construction Installation of the casing may be
by vibration driving or twisting to advance the casing In addition care should be
exercised during removal of the temporary casing to reduce the potential for
necking of the drilled shaft Therefore a minimum 5 foot head of concrete should
be maintained above the bottom of the casing and or groundwater level during
removal of the casing
The load carrying capacity of drilled shafts depends to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in
drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete in the holes The bottom of all
shafts should be cleaned of loose or disturbed material prior to constructing the
foundation The bottom should also be fairly level The concrete should be
poured neatly against the excavation sidewalls Backfill against the shaft
foundations shouid not be allowed
W O 2153 140 Page 13
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids is generally not recommended
Groundwater conditions are anticipated for shaft bottoms below an elevation of
about 5 feet MSL therefore placement of concrete by wet construction methods
using a tremie pipe may be required Wet construction methods are applicable
when a column of water within the hole is greater than 12 inches A low shrink
concrete mix with high slump 6 to 9 inch slump range should be used to provide
close contact between the shafts and the surrounding soils The concrete should
be placed in a suitable manner to reduce the potential for segregation of the
aggregates from the concrete mix and to displace disturbed material from the
bottom of the drilled shaft
Placement of concrete using a tremie pipe will likely cause some groundwater and
soil to discharge out of the top of the drilled shaft The Contractor should be made
aware that this fluid should be properly disposed of Since the water will be turbid
from soil particles direct disposal into storm drains will not be acceptable The
Contractor should also be made aware that a Nationa Pollutant Discharge
Elimination System NPDES permit may be necessary depending on the
proposed method of water disposal The Contractor should consult their
independent consultant or the State of Hawaii Department of Health for the latest
regulations and information pertaining to the permit application

Slabs On Grade
Our field exploration disclosed that the near surface silty clay soils have a
moderate swelling potential Unless floor slabs are properly designed there is a potential
for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from shrinking and
swelling of these soils due to changes in the moisture content To reduce the potential for
W O 2153 140 Page 18
structural distress resulting from the swelling of the subgrade soils we recommend that
the subgrade soils be properly prepared and capped with at least 12 inches of select
granular fill material If the on site corailine material is exposed prior to reaching
18 inches below the bottom of the slab elevation 12 inches of select granular fill plus
4 inches capillary break material plus 2 inches curing sand the over excavation may be
terminated at such elevation and filled with select granular fill
The area adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly against the slab and
graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding
around the foundation
The select granular fill should be placed immediately after the
moisture conditioning and compaction of the clayey subgrade soils The prepared select
granular fill and the underlying subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist prior to
final placement of concrete Subgrade soils should be prepared in accordance with the
recommendations presented in the Site Grading section of this report Compaction
requiremenis for the subgrade soils and select granular fill are also discussed in the same
section of this report
For building slabs we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill
consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be placed below the
slabs to serve as a capillary break and to provide more uniform support of the slabs To
reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to the floor
coverings an impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is
recommended on top of the gravel cushion layer Two inches of moist fine sand or
Basaltic Termite Barrier BTB may be considered above the plastic membrane to provide
protection for the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab concrete Flexible floor
coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered since they can better
mask minor slab cracking It is also recommended that interior walls be designed to
incorporate some flexibility to accommodate a small amount of possible ground
movements

reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials
Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the resulting excavation
should be properly backfilled with select granular fill material placed in 6 to 8 inch level
loose lifts and compacted to a minimum 90 percent relative compaction Utilities to be
abandoned in place under the proposed structure should be backfilled by pumping lean
concrete under low pressure
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas
designated to receive fill should be over excavated to expose firm natural material and
the resulting excavation should be backfilled with properly compacted fill material
For the restroom site areas designated to receive fill should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 6 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 to 4 percent above of
optimum and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction For the kiosk
and driving range structures areas designated to receive fill should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 6 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above of optimum
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction For the restroom kiosk
and driving range sites finished grades should be moisture conditioned to within
2 percent of optimum and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade
due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft
areas and replacement with properly compacted fill material

Select granular fill should consist of non expansive material such as crushed
coral mudrock basait or cinder sand The material should be well graded from coarse
to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension should contain between
10 and 30 percent passing the No 200 sieve and should be free of organics The
material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum
swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1883 The on site silty coral gravel and silty coral sand may be used as
select granular fill provided they meet the above maximum particle size and are free of
organics Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for conformance with
these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its intended use
Select granular fill materials should be moisture conditioned to within 2 percent of
the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density

In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used
under the pipes The initial backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of
free draining backfill material such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for
damaging the pipes from compaction of the backfill It is critical that a free draining
granular material be used to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes The use of
on site soils as backfill immediately around utility pipes is not recommended
Soft or loose soils encountered at the bottom of trenches should be

Drainage
The finished grades should be sloped to shed water away from the slabs and
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive landscape watering near
the foundations and slabs should be avoided Planters next to foundations and slabs
should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water
infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation and slab recommendations since ponded water could cause
subsurface soil saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation
excavations should be properly backfilled against the walls or slabs immediately after
setting the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration In addition drainage
swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain all
surface run off away from slabs and foundations
