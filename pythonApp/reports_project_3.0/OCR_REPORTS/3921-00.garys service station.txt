INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Gary s Service Station project located in Lihue on the island
of Kauai Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommenda
tions resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses
These recommendations are intended for the design of foundation site grading and
pavement sections only The findings and recommendations presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

and removed The new construction will include a Foodmart pump islands with a

Gary s Service Station Final grading plans were not available to us at the time this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene
Epoch more than 2 million years before present Following the cessation of this
main shield building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of
basaltic lavas of the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition
of alluvial sediments of the Palikea Formation
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the island of
Kauai including Lihue These rocks are generally characterized as thick flows of dense
basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in north south trends at various locations
Associated with the vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low cinder
cones at the vent
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
growth and retreat of the great continental glaciers As the glaciers advanced less water
was available to fill the ocean basins and sea levels fell below the current sea level As
the glaciers melted and retreated more water became available thus the sea level rose
and the streams aggraded their floors to form fills When the sea level fell the streams
removed part or all of the fills resulting in superimposed complex valley in valley deposits
of gravel boulders and mud
The project area is composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of the lavas
of the Koloa Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows of
jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers The weathering
process has formed a mantle of residual soils near the surface In general residual
soils are composed mainly of clayey and silty materials which are typical of the tropical
weathering of volcanic rocks The residual soils generally grade to saprolite and
basaltic rock formation with depth

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The proposed Gary s Service Station project is located at the corner of Poinciana
Street and Kuhio Highway in Lihue on the island of Kauai Hawaii The project site is
bounded by a warehouse complex to the north Kuhio Highway to the east Poinciana

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
In general the proposed project site is underlain by residual soils consisting of stiff
to very stiff silty clay Based on our field exploration and laboratory testing we believe
that a shallow foundation system bearing on the near surface residual soils may be used
to support the proposed new structures
Our laboratory test results indicate that the near surface residual soils have a slight
to moderate potential for shrink swell when subjected to changes in the moisture content
Therefore special attention should be given to the subgrade preparation requirements
presented in the Slabs On Grade section of this report to reduce the potential
shrink swell effects on floor slabs
We anticipate that minor site grading consisting of cuts and fills less than 1 foot will
be needed for the proposed construction in addition we anticipate that excavations of
up to about 15 feet and subsequent backfilling will be required for the removal of existing
underground fuel storage tanks and construction of the two new underground fuel storage
tanks
Detailed discussions and recommendations for design of foundation site grading
excavation and other geotechnical aspects of the project are presented in the following
sections of this report

Foundations
The proposed construction will consist of a Foodmart car wash pump islands with
a canopy and two underground fuel storage tanks Based on the subsurface conditions
encountered in our exploration we believe that a shallow foundation system consisting of
spread and or continuous footings may be used to support the proposed structures An
allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 p s f may be used for the design of footings
bearing on the residual soils This allowable bearing pressure is for dead plus live loads
and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as temporary wind
and or seismic forces Footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the
lowest adjacent finished grade We estimate that total footing settlements under the
anticipated design loads to be on the order of less than 1 inch with differential settlements
of approximately inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive
earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings
A coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used between the base of the footings and the
residual soils encountered in our borings Resistance due to passive pressure may be
calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of
depth p c f This assumes that the soil around footings is well compacted Unless
covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below
finished grade should be neglected
We anticipate that the pump island canopy will be subjected to uplift forces Uplift
loads may be resisted by a combination of the following 1 dead weight of the structure
2 weight of backfill material above the footing where applicable and 3 side shear
between the sides of the footings and surrounding soils Contribution of dead weight from
the backfill may be estimated using a unit weight of 105 p c f For side shear between the
sides of the footings and the adjacent soils a shear value of 150 p s f may be used

Where footings are located adjacent to below grade structures or utilities the
footings should extend to a depth below an imaginary 45 degree plane projected upward
from the bottom of the below grade structure or utility This requirement is necessary to
avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for foundation settlement
Footing excavations should not be allowed to dry before placing concrete If
shrinkage cracks appear in the footing excavations the soil should be thoroughly
moistened to close all cracks and recompacted to provide a firm base prior to concrete
placement
Footing excavations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii
prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to evaluate the competency of the
bearing materials and embedment depths If unsuitable materials are encountered in the
footing excavations these materials should be over excavated to expose firm soils and
replaced with compacted engineered fill
Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed
structures such as the Foodmart car wash and pump islands The near surface fills
encountered at the proposed site consist of residual soils which exhibit slight to moderate
expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations Therefore to reduce the
potential of structural distress to lightly loaded slabs we recommend that the subgrade
soils be properly prepared and a minimum of 6 inches of non expansive capping fill
material be provided below concrete slabs on grade The non expansive capping fill
should extend a minimum of 2 feet laterally beyond the edge of the slab The subgrades
for slabs on grades should be _ scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted
to not less than 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the
in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3921 00 Page 9
same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum
dry density
The non expansive capping fill recommended below the floor slabs should consist
of imported granular material such as crushed coral basalt gravel or cinder sand The
material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in
largest dimension The material should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR
value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 It should contain between 10 and 30 percent
particles passing the No 200 sieve All select granular fill materials should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to at least
90 percent relative compaction The slab subgrade should be kept moist until covered by
the cushion fill or base course
To provide more uniform support of interior slabs not subject to vehicular traffic we
recommend that the concrete slab on grade be supported on a minimum 4 inch thick
cushion fill above the capping fill layer The cushion fill layer should consist of
open graded gravels such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation which
will also serve as a capillary moisture break The slab cushion fill should be place as
soon as possible after the placement and compaction of the capping fill as recommended
above For slabs on grade with moisture sensitive surfacing we recommend that an
impermeable membrane be placed over the cushion fill to reduce potential migration of
moisture vapor through the concrete slab In order to promote more uniform curing of the
slab concrete and to provide protection of the vapor membrane placement of a 2 inch
layer of moist fine sand above the membrane prior to placing the slab concrete may be
considered If the sand layer is desired the sand should be moistened slightly prior to
placing concrete Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be
considered for the Foodmart since they can better mask minor slab cracking

Where concrete slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as the car wash
and pump islands we recommend that the slab be supported on a 6 inch layer of base
course compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction in lieu of the 4 inch
cushion fill layer The base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate
For design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per
cubic inch p c i may be used for the compacted base course The thickened edges of
slabs adjacent to any unpaved area should be embedded at least 12 inches below the
lowest adjacent grade
Site Grading
Final grading plans were not available to us at the time this report was prepared
However we anticipate minor grading with cuts and fills on the order of less than 1 foot
and excavation and backfill of up to about 15 feet relative to the existing ground surface
may be expected for the proposed project Site grading operations should be observed
by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii It is important that a representative from our
office observe whether the exposed soil conditions during excavation are similar to
those encountered in our exploration The following recommendations are intended to
provide guidelines for the design of site grading work
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared All debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed
and disposed of off site Slabs and foundations of the existing structures that are to be
demolished should be completely removed Any soft weak or yielding areas disclosed
during clearing operations or any over excavation resulting from the demolition should be
backfilled with well compacted engineered fill
Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the resulting excavation
should be properly backfilled with well compacted engineered fill Utilities that are to be
GEOLABS HAWAII

abandoned in place under the proposed structures should be backfilled by pumping lean
concrete under low pressure
After demolition and clearing the exposed subgrade or areas to receive fill should
be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches properly moisture conditioned to at least
2 percent above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction For subgrades of improvements subjected to vehicular
traffic such as the car wash pump islands parking and driveway areas the compaction
requirement should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density The finish subgrade should be kept
moist until covered by fill materials If shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of
the subgrade we recommend that the subgrade soil be moisture conditioned and
recompacted as recommended above to close all cracks
Based on our field exploration we believe that the excavated on site soils may
be reused as a source of general fill material up to the bottom of the non expansive
capping fill layer provided that they are moisture conditioned and compacted as
recommended herein However due to the slightly to moderately expansive nature of
the on site soils the excavated on site soils should not be used as the non expansive
capping fill recommended below slabs In general the on site clayey material used as
general fill should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
If imported fill materials are required the imported fill and or backfill material should
consist of well graded granular materials less than 3 inches in maximum dimension The
material should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 15 or more and

ASTM Test Designation D 1883 In addition it should contain between 10 and
30 percent passing the No 200 sieve Imported fill materials should be tested by

ones we anticipate that excavations on the order of about 15 feet below the existing
ground surface will be required for the project construction In general conventional
earthmoving equipment may be used to excavate the near surface fills and underlying
residual soils encountered in our borings The method and equipment to be used for
excavation should be determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety
considerations The slopes and sides of the excavations must comply with all federal
state and local requirements

Based on our field exploration the project site is underlain by stiff to very stiff
residual soils Therefore if construction space is available an open cut excavation may
be used for the excavation of the proposed underground fuel storage tanks A slope
inclination of one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V or flatter may be used for temporary
cut slopes provided that the exposed sides consist of the stiff to very stiff residual soils

Where an open cut excavation is not possible the excavation for the installation
of the tanks will need to be adequately shored to reduce the potential for adjacent
ground movement The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with
all applicable safety requirements
Appropriate loads due to surcharge equipment traffic or sloping ground conditions
adjacent to the excavations should be added to the design lateral earth pressures In
addition stock piling of excavated on site and or imported materials within a horizontal

should be avoided to reduce the potential of ground movement or subsidence toward the
excavation
The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His representative who should be required to be
continuously present on site during excavation and construction work will have the best
opportunity to promptly observe any changing conditions during construction such as
unforeseen subsoil conditions unexpectedly high groundwater table inappropriate
construction sequence or techniques etc which may affect shoring stability

Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines may be required for the proposed project In
general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding consisting
of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under the
pipes The backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of free draining
materials such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes
from compaction of the backfill
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select granular material or the
on site soils The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not
less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground
subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 2 feet of the trench
backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent
relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Also it is advised that gutter
systems be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations
and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils

noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in rainfall tides temperature and other

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling five
borings to depths ranging from 10 to 30 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 5
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive or the middle 12 inches of a 24 inch
drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected soil layers in the field The
pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive strength
of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on selected soil
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Four one inch ring swell tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate
the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results of the tests are summarized
on Plate B 1
Two Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results
of the tests are summarized on the Logs of Boring and are graphically presented on
Plate B 2
