Our borings generally encountered residual soils and saprolite extending to the 
maximum depth explored of about 26.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The 
residual soils encountered generally consisted of medium stiff to hard silty clay and clayey 
silt. Saprolite consisting of stiff silty clay was encountered in three of the drilled borings at 
depths of about 22 to 26.5 feet below the existing ground surface. We did not encounter 
groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. However, it should 
be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, 
seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
The results of our field exploration suggested that the near-surface clayey soils 
generally exhibit low to moderate shrink/swell characteristics when subjected to 
fluctuations in the soil moisture contents. To reduce the potential for appreciable structure 
distress resulting from shrinking and swelling of the subgrade soils, we recommend 
properly preparing the subgrade soils prior to fill placement. In addition, we recommend 
providing a minimum 12 inches of non-expansive select granular fill materials below the 
building pad and slabs-on-grade. 
In general, we anticipate the excavations during mass grading likely will encounter 
residual soil and saprolite. However, some of the excavations may encounter boulders 
commonly present within the residual and saprolite. It is anticipated that most of the 
material may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment. However, deep 
excavations and boulder excavations may require the use of hoerams. 
In general, the excavated on-site soils should be suitable for use as general fill and 
backfill materials, provided that they are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, and

finished grade in areas subjected to vehicular traffic should be compacted to a minimum 
of 95 percent relative compaction. Cut and fill slopes should be designed with a maximum 
inclination of two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
Of interest to our geotechnical engineering analysis are the subsurface materials 
encountered at the project site, the engineering properties of the materials encountered, 
and the variability of the subsurface conditions across the project site. Therefore, the 
following sections provide a description of the geologic setting of the project site and the 
surface and subsurface conditions encountered at the site. 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. The Waianae Volcano became 
extinct while the Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried 
below the Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank. These banked or ponded lavas 
formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau. 
The project site is located within the Schofield Plateau. The Schofield Plateau was 
formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded against the already eroded slopes 
of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene Epoch. The dips of the lava beds are near 
horizontal (between 3 to 5 degrees from horizontal). The lava flows on the plateau have 
undergone in-situ weathering to depths of 50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the 
reddish color of the soil. 
This in-situ weathering of the lava has formed a mantle of residual soils that grade 
to saprolite with increasing depth. In general, saprolite is composed mainly of silty 
material, while residual soils are more clayey. Both residual and saprolitic soils are typical 
of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks. The residual and saprolitic soils grade to a 
basaltic rock formation with increased depth. Spheroidally weathered boulders within the 
residual and saprolitic soils are typical in the Schofield Plateau.

3.1.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, roots, debris, deleterious materials, 
existing structures, and other unsuitable materials should be removed and 
disposed of properly off-site. 
Loose and/or soft soils, where encountered, should be over-excavated to expose 
firm and/or dense materials, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
well-compacted fills. The excavated loose and/or soft soils should be disposed of 
properly off-site and/or used in landscape areas, where appropriate. 
After clearing and grubbing, the cut subgrades and areas to be filled should be 
scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent 
above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by

3.1.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, the excavated on-site soils should be suitable for use as general fill 
materials, provided that the maximum particle size is less than 3 inches in the largest 
dimension. The on-site materials generated from the excavations may be used as a 
source of general fill or backfill materials, provided that they are screened of the 
over-sized materials and/or processed to meet the above gradation requirements 
(less than 3 inches in largest dimension). 
Based on the additional exploration recently performed by our firm at the Koa Ridge 
Makai Off-Site Detention Basin No. 1 borrow site, import borrow material from the 
Koa Ridge Makai Off-Site Detention Basin No. 1 borrow site may be used as general 
fill materials up to 12 inches below the finished subgrade.  
Structural fill and select granular fill should consist of non-expansive select granular 
fill material, such as crushed coralline and/or basaltic materials. The materials should 
be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no greater than 3 inches in the 
largest dimension. In addition, the materials also should contain between 10 and 30 
percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The materials should have a CBR value 
of 20 or higher and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance 
with ASTM D1883. 
3.1.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
General fill materials consisting of the excavated on-site soils or import borrow should 
be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content, 
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-
place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of 
the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the 
water content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

3.2.3 Backfilling Drainage Structures 
The drainage structure excavations will need to be properly backfilled to reduce 
the potential for settlement at the ground surface. The excavated on-site soils or

3.5 
Field Infiltration Testing 
Infiltration tests were conducted at three boring locations drilled at the site to 
evaluate the infiltration characteristics of the subsurface materials. The tests were 
performed in general accordance with the procedures in Appendix D of the State of 
Maryland Department of the Environment “Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I and II” 
(rev. 2009). These procedures are consistent with the other states’ procedures and 
generally may be considered an industry standard. 
The field infiltration tests were performed by drilling borings to test depths of about 
1.5 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface. Upon reaching the test depth, a 4-inch 
inside diameter PVC solid casing was set to the bottom of the drilled hole to allow 
infiltration only through the soil exposed at the bottom of the boring. A falling head 
infiltration test was performed at each infiltration test location to determine the infiltration 
rate of the underlying subsurface materials. The test consists of four trials of filling the 
casing with about 24 inches of water and taking periodic readings over a 1-hour trial 
period or until the hole drains completely. The infiltration rates are then calculated based 
on the results of the fourth and last trials.  Due to the shallow depth of the drilled holes, 
initial infiltration rates are fast due to water escaping through the annular space between the 
drilled borehole and the PVC casing. Therefore, the tests were modified to consist of two 
trials of filling the casing with about 24 inches of water and taking periodic readings over a 
2-hour trial period. The infiltration rates are then calculated based on the results of the 
second and last trials. The results of the final infiltration rates are presented in the table 
below. Detailed information on the tests is presented in Appendix C.

3.7 
Corrosion Potential 
Design of metallic substructures, such as waterlines, electrical conduits, or other 
metallic piping, should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the 
substructure. Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil 
characteristics regarding the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, 
the lower the resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic 
structure. Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to the 
corrosion of metallic objects. 
Based on the result of the resistivity and pH Value testing performed by our office 
on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration, the subsurface soils at the 
project site exhibit minimum resistivity values of approximately 10,000 to 13,000 ohm-cm, 
and pH Values of about 5.5 to 6.29, indicating that the subsurface soils may be considered 
moderately to severely corrosive (Corrosion Category A based on the Board of Water 
Supply, City and County of Honolulu Water System External Corrosion Control 
Standards, dated 2021) to buried metallic structures. Therefore, we recommend properly 
designing metallic substructures, such as piping, for protection against the potential for 
corrosion.   
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent on the pH and the chloride and sulfate contents found in the soil. 
Based on the chloride and sulfate content tests performed on the on-site soils, the tested 
chloride and sulfate content values ranged from 15 to 21 mg/kg and 24 to 39 mg/kg, 
respectively. It may be appropriate to consult with a professional corrosion engineer to 
review the test results and provide detailed recommendations for corrosion protection.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
17 borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 10, R-1 through R-4, and L-1 through 
L-3, extending to depths of about 1.5 to 26.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In

The borings were drilled using a track-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight 
auger. The boring and bulk sample locations are presented on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologists classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing 
in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Eight Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations of the test 
results are provided on Plate B-1. 
Three Sieve Analysis (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil samples to 
evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. Graphic 
presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on Plate B-2. 
Ten one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils. The test 
results are summarized on Plate B-3. 
Five 
Unconsolidated-Undrained 
Triaxial 
Compression 
(TXUU) 
tests 
(ASTM D2850) were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear 
strength of the silty and clayey soils encountered. The approximate in-situ effective 
overburden pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the relatively 
“undisturbed” soil sample. The test results and the stress-strain curves are presented on 
Plates B-4 through B-8. 
Four Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested.  Results of the direct 
shear tests are presented on Plates B-9 and B-12. 
Five laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-13 through B-17. 
Three sets of Corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0) and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), were 
performed by our laboratory and Eurofins TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on selected soil 
samples obtained from our field exploration.  Results of the corrosivity tests are presented 
on Plate B-18.

Field Infiltration Tests 
As part of our field exploration program, we performed three infiltration tests in Boring 
Nos. L-1 through L-3 at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. These 
tests were performed in general accordance with the procedures in Appendix D of the State 
of Maryland, Department of the Environment “Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I and II” 
(rev. 2009). These procedures are consistent with other state’s procedures and may 
generally be considered an industry standard. 
The field infiltration tests were performed by advancing the borings to the selected 
test depths of about 1.5 and 5 feet below the existing ground surface. Upon reaching the 
test depth, a solid casing was set to the bottom of the drilled hole to allow infiltration only 
through the soil exposed on the bottom of the boring. Falling head infiltration tests were 
performed to determine the infiltration rate of the underlying subsurface materials. Each 
test consisted of four trials of filling the casing with 24 inches of water and taking 
periodic readings over a one-hour trial period or until the water in the casing is gone. Due 
to the shallow depth of the drilled holes, initial infiltration rates are fast due to water escaping 
through the annular space between the drilled borehole and the PVC casing. Therefore, the 
tests were modified to consist of two trials of filling the casing with about 24 inches of water 
and taking periodic readings over a 2-hour trial period. The infiltration rates are then 
calculated based on the results of the second and last trial. The test results are presented 
on Plates C-1 through C-3.
