INTRODUCTION
The proposed new sewage system for the Turtle Bay Resort will
consist of a pump station to pump the wastewater to the wastewater
treatment plant The treated effluent will then be used for irrigation of
the golf courses nearby Any excess effluent will be disposed by means of
subsurface injection wells
The purpose of this investigation is to provide subsurface soil
permeability data for use in the design of the proposed injection wells
for the proposed waste water treatment plant Separate reports will be
issued for the wastewater treatment plant and pump station
The results of our field exploration and tests which were used in
the analysis that formated our conclusions and recommendations are
presented in the appendices of this report
The injection well site location in relation to the wastewater
treatment plant site and pump station locations at Turtle Bay Resort is
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 The approximate boring
locations at the injection well site are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
This report summarizes our findings and recommendations

SITE _ DESCRIPTION
The project site is located on the north shore of the island of Oahu
in the Kahuku area near the northern side of Punahoolapa Marsh The site
is approximately 1000 by 130 feet in plan dimensions and is bordered to
the southeast by the existing golf course fairway The Turtle Bay Resort
Hotel is about miles west of the site The ground surface
elevations vary from about 4 feet to about 5 feet
The site is generally flat and mostly covered with brush and small
trees The northerly end of the site was formally an airfield runway
PROPOSED CONSTRUCTION
The proposed injection well site covers an area of about 3 acres
1000 by 130 feet in plan It is recommended that a miltiple well field
be designed for the area since the wells will be located close to the
shoreline where ground elevations are low and the depth to water is
shallow Therefore injection head build ups of only a few feet may cause
well overflows
Also fluctuations in water levels from tidal effects and storms may
further reduce the allowable injection head build up
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The project site is located on the north flank of the Koolau
Volcano This volcanic shield formed a salient for the growth of coral
reefs on the seaward fringes of northern Oahu

During the Pleistocene sea level fluctuated widely as the result of
glacio eustatic changes When the sea stood at higher levels than the
present the coral reefs grew to elevations above present sea level The
coral formations encountered at the project site have been assigned to the
Waimanalo stand of the sea at elevation
In the low areas of the site the Waimanalo reef material is
generally mantled by a thin layer of recent alluvium or recent organic
marsh soils
Some amount of fill was encountered on the project site which was
probably placed during the site s former use as a military installation
Soil Conditions
Boring 1
The exploration at this site encountered 2 inches of asphaltic
concrete underlain by 3 feet of man made fill consisting of dense
brown sandy gravel Below this the drilling encountered loose or
soft tidal marsh deposits consisting of clayey sand and organic silt
to a depth of 8 feet below the existing ground surface
Underlying the marsh deposit was dense coralline reef rock to about
33 feet followed by a 5 foot thick zone of medium dense coralline
gravel then another stratum of dense reefrock to a depth of 52
feet Below this was a sequence of loose to medium dense coralline
sand and gravel down to 88 feet Underlying the coralline deposits
was stiff alluvial soils USC MH to 92 feet then a dense layer of
weathered basalt to 101 5 feet the depth drilled

Groundwater level was encountered in the borings at depths of 3 6 to
3 8 feet below the existing ground surface These depths correspond to
elevations of 4 5 to 5 0 feet above MSL
Permeability
In general permeability is a function of the grain size of the
soil Coarse grained soils such as sands and gravels readily transmit
water Fine grained soils such as silts and clays generally have low
permeability
The interbeds of granular marine sediments encountered at the site
have relatively good permeability and appear to be practical for use in
underground disposal of treated effluent

The two test wells were drilled utilizing truck mounted rotary
drilling equipment Drive samples were taken in the unconsolidated
deposits and the reef deposits for material classification

After the drilling of the test wells they were developed by

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATTONS
The test wells which were drilled at the project site generally
encountered coralline reef formation In general coralline materials are
quite permeable and can readily transmit clear water for injection wells
Injection Well Permeability
The field data for the gravity injection at Boring 1 showed a head
to a calculated coefficient of permeability k of 1 34 feet per minute
For Boring 2 the head build up was 0 6 feet for an injection rate of 85
to 90 G P M and a calculated k value of 0 22 feet per minute For
comparison purposes permeability tests done recently for another project
nearby in similar coralline materials indicated a coefficient of
permeability of 1 89 feet per minute

Therefore it appears that the value of k varies as much as 6 times
over a horizontal surface distance of about 900 feet The boring data
from Boring 1 appears to favor a higher injection rate as was indicated
from the injection tests
For a schematic diagram of injection testing refer to Plate C 3
It should be noted that these values were obtained from testing open
wells using clear water and no air entrainment Lower values are
anticipated under the proposed use
Injection Well Design
By extrapolation of the field test data our calculations indicated
that disposal wells in this area with a diameter of 1 5 feet and a depth
of 100 feet could theoretically accommodate a gravity injection from 0 5
injection average would be between 1 to 2 M G D with a 2 foot head
build up
The calculated relationship between discharge and head build up for
different sizes and depths of wells is presented on Plate c 4
Typically water injected into disposal wells contains suspended
solids micro organisms and other matter which may reduce the potential
permeability Here in Hawaii there have been numerous failures of
disposal wells injecting into coral algal formations These failures have
been attributed to numerous causes e g lack of maintenance
accummilation of solids and formation caving
In view of this past history of failures we recommend that the
injection system be overdesigned to allow for reduction in capacity or
well failure In addition provisions should be made in the facility
design for a maintenance program to extend the useful life of the wells

The injection wells could be constructed with a removable screen or
perforated casing to protect against formation caving and to prevent
migration of accummulated solids into the injection formation The
screens could be removed at regular intervals to be cleaned and emptied of
accumulated solids Upon completion of this maintenance procedure the
screens should be replaced and the wells redeveloped
It is our opinion that underground disposal of treated effluent
could be accomplished at the site by utilizing a series of production
wells to minimize the build up of accummulated solids and to maintain
effluent percolation in the event of reduced injection capacity or peak
flows during maintenance or construction of other wells
Provision should be incorporated in the development scheme for
additional production wells to be installed as the original wells reach
the end of their effective service life
