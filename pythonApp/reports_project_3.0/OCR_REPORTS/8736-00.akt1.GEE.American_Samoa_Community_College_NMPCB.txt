SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration encountered loose to medium dense sandy surficial fill 
underlain by alluvial soil deposits. Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings 
at the time of our field exploration. It should be noted that groundwater levels are subject 
to change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and 
other factors. 
Considering the presence of concrete rubbles and rebar observed in the upper 
surficial fill and marginal for support of the new building, we recommend removing the 
upper 3 feet of the in-situ soils and replacing it with imported structural fill. Because the 
on-site soils are too wet for reuse as fill on the project, imported structural fill material 
should be used for fill under the building pad. 
We recommend using shallow spread and/or continuous footings to support the 
new multi-purpose building.  An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,000 pounds per 
square foot (psf) may be used to design the shallow foundations bearing on the newly 
placed structural fill. 
The structural fill materials should consist of soil materials with particles no larger 
than 3 inches in the largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent 
particles passing the No. 200 sieve with a laboratory CBR value of greater than 20 and 
swell of less than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed in support of the New Multi-Purpose Classroom Building at American Samoa 
Community College project in the Mapusaga area on the Island of Tutuila, American 
Samoa. The project location and general site vicinity are shown on the Project Location 
Map, Plate 1.  
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses. These findings 
and geotechnical recommendations are intended only for the design of foundations, 
slabs-on-grade, retaining structures, site grading, and pavement. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report.  
1.1 
Project Considerations 
In general, the project consists of constructing a new two-story building as part of 
the community college improvements. Details pertaining to the structural design and site 
grading plans were not available at the time this report was prepared. 
The project site was located in an open leveled area previously occupied by an old 
classroom building that was recently demolished. The project site is adjacent to the 
existing cafeteria, which will be tied to the new building development. We envision the 
new building site grades will be raised about 2 to 3 feet to match the adjacent grades.  
Based on the information provided, we understand that the new building site is 
adjacent to a cut slope extending to the natural rainforest to a high elevation. To protect 
the new building from potential rockfall hazards, we understand that a slope hazard 
assessment will be conducted as part of the development. The results of the slope hazard 
assessment will be included in a separate report.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Territory of American Samoa is an island archipelago approximately 2,300 
miles toward the south of Honolulu at about 14 degrees south latitude from the equator. 
The total land area of American Samoa consists of about 76 square miles and includes 
five volcanic islands (Tutuila, Aunu'u, Ofu, Olosega, Ta'u) and two remote atolls (Rose, 
Swains). The Samoan archipelago was formed by submarine volcanic activity that built 
up to form the volcanic mountains, which emerged from the ocean about 1.5 million years 
ago during the late Pliocene Epoch (Stearns 1944). 
Tutuila is the largest island of the archipelago with a land area of about 53 square 
miles and a maximum elevation of just under about +3,000 feet Mean Sea Level. The 
Island of Tutuila consists of an elongated east-west trending island shape with a 
prominent sharp-crested mountain ridge spine spanning the length of the island. The 
primary mountain slopes are very steep with limited flatter terrain encompassing the 
island, with the exception of the coastal regions and the vicinity of the lava terrace 
identified as the Tafuna-Leone Plain.  The Island of Tutuila was formed by the 
coalescence of five volcanoes situated on two separate submarine volcanic rift zones. 
The bulk of the Island of Tutuila is composed of basaltic and andesitic lava flows and 
volcanic tuff and cinder deposits. Other volcanic rocks make up a small fraction of the 
geology, and coralline reef and sand deposits contribute to the coastal lagoons and 
shoreline areas. 
The American Samoa island archipelago formed as an elongated island chain that 
grew as the Pacific Plate moved westward in relation to a nearly stationary “hot spot” of 
thermal activity located deep in the earth's mantle.  As the island archipelago moves 
westward on the Pacific Plate, it interacts with the adjacent Australian Plate at a junction 
located approximately 100 miles toward the south of American Samoa.  This proximal 
plate interaction is believed to be responsible for some earthquakes and renewed 
volcanic activity in the area.

2.4 
Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the International Building Code (2018 Edition), the project site may be 
subject to seismic activity, and seismic design considerations should be addressed. The 
following subsections provide discussions on the seismicity, soil profile type for seismic 
design and the potential for liquefaction at the project site. 
2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused largely by 
shifts in the tectonic plates. Tutuila is situated near the Kermadec-Tonga 
subduction zone, which is the most linear, fastest converging, and seismically 
active subduction boundary on Earth. The Pacific Plate subducts westward 
beneath the Australian Plate along the Tonga-Kermadec Ridge. 
On average, 49 earthquakes with a magnitude of 4 or greater occur within 186 
miles of American Samoa yearly. Since 1900, there have been ten earthquakes 
with a magnitude of 6 or greater. The largest in recorded history occurred in 2009 
with a magnitude of 8.1. 
Based on the International Building Code (2018 Edition), the project site should be 
evaluated for the potential for soil liquefaction. Soil liquefaction is a condition where 
saturated cohesionless soils near the ground surface undergo a substantial loss 
of strength due to the build-up of excess pore water pressures resulting from cyclic

stress applications induced by earthquakes. When the loose saturated sand 
deposit is subjected to vibration (such as during an earthquake), the soil tends to 
densify and decrease in volume, causing an increase in pore water pressure. If 
drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up of pore water 
pressure, the effective stress (internal strength) of the soil is reduced. Under 
sustained vibrations, the pore water pressure build-up could equal the overburden 
pressure, essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to 
behave as a viscous fluid. During liquefaction, the soil acquires a mobility sufficient 
to permit both horizontal and vertical movements, and if not confined, will result in 
significant deformations. 
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained 
sands and loose silts with little cohesion. The major factors affecting the 
liquefaction characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows:

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered surficial fill underlain by medium stiff 
to stiff alluvial soils extending to a depth of 10 feet below the existing grade. Groundwater 
was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. It should be 
noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal 
precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
Considering the presence of concrete rubble and rebar encountered at shallow 
depths at the project site, we recommend the removal of the upper 3 feet of the in-situ soils 
and replacing with the imported structural fills within the new building footprint. The structural 
fill materials should consist of soil materials with particles no larger than 3 inches in the 
largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the 
No. 200 sieve with a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of greater than 20 and 
swell of less than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. 
We recommend an allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,000 pounds per square 
foot (psf) to be used for the design of the shallow foundations bearing on the newly placed 
structural fill supporting the new multi-purpose classroom building. 
In general, the on-site soils generated from the cut areas will be too wet for reuse 
as a source of general fill material. Detailed discussions of these items and our 
geotechnical engineering design recommendations are presented in the following 
sections. 
3.1 
Building Foundations 
Based on the marginal subsurface soil conditions, we recommend excavation of 
the in-situ soils to a depth of 3 feet and replacement with structural fill material.  In 
addition, we envision the building site may be raised by 2 to 3 feet to match the adjacent 
grades.  Imported structural fill should be used to raise the building pads to the desired 
height. We recommend using shallow spread and/or continuous footings to support the 
new multi-purpose building.  An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,500 pounds per 
square foot (psf) may be used to design shallow foundations bearing on the engineered

This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third 
(⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. In general, we 
recommend embedding the footings a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent 
grades. 
For footings bearing on the engineered fills, the bottom of the footing excavations 
should be recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction to provide a relatively 
firm and smooth bearing surface prior to the placement of reinforcing steel and/or 
concrete. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with 
ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
The bottom of footings located adjacent to slopes or on slopes should be 
embedded to a sufficient depth to provide a minimum horizontal setback distance of at 
least 6 feet measured from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope. 
Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a 
45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench, 
or the footings should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. 
This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with 
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation 
settlement. 
If the foundations are designed and constructed as recommended herein, we 
estimate total settlements of footings supported on the recompacted on-site soils and/or 
new compacted fills to be on the order of about 1 inch or less. Differential settlements 
between adjacent footings supported on similar materials should be on the order of about 
0.5 inches or less.

resistance between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive 
earth pressure developed against the near-vertical faces of the embedded portion of the 
footings. A coefficient of friction of 0.35 may be used for footings bearing on the structural

3.2 
Slabs-On-Grade 
We envision that concrete slabs-on-grade will be used for the new structure. We 
anticipate that the slabs-on-grade will bear on structural fills. We recommend placing a 
minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, 
No. 67 gradation) below the slabs. The open-graded gravel cushion fill would provide 
uniform support for the slabs and would serve as a capillary moisture break. To reduce 
the potential for appreciable future moisture infiltration through the slab and subsequent 
damage to floor coverings, we recommend placing an impervious moisture barrier is 
recommended on top of the open-graded gravel cushion layer. 
The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at 
least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade. Areas adjacent to the slab 
should be backfilled tightly against the slab edges with the on-site soils and/or select 
granular fill and graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential for 
appreciable water ponding around the slabs. 
An experienced geotechnical engineer should monitor slab-on-grade foundation 
excavations prior to placing the reinforcing steel and/or concrete to confirm the foundation 
bearing conditions and the required embedment depths and observe backfill placement 
to evaluate the compaction quality of the fill material. Observation of the foundation 
excavations should be designated as a “Special Inspection” item in accordance with 
International Building Code (2018).

Retaining Walls 
The following discussions and recommendations are intended to provide general 
guidance for the design and construction of retaining walls.  
3.3.1 Retaining Wall Foundations 
In general, retaining wall foundations should be designed in accordance with the 
"Building Foundations" section presented herein. In addition, retaining wall 
foundations should be at least 18 inches wide and should be embedded a minimum 
of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grades. Wall footings oriented parallel 
to the direction of the slope should be constructed in stepped footings.

In general, an active condition may be used for gravity retaining walls and walls 
that are free to deflect by as much as 0.5 percent of the wall height. If the tops of 
the walls are not free to deflect beyond this degree or are restrained, the walls 
should be designed for the at-rest condition. These lateral earth pressures do not 
include hydrostatic pressures that might be caused by groundwater trapped behind 
the walls. 
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges, line loads, and point loads within a 
horizontal distance equal to the depth of the wall should be considered in the 
design. For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the wall, a 
rectangular distribution with a uniform pressure equal to 33 percent of the vertical 
surcharge pressure acting on the entire height of the wall, which is free to deflect 
(cantilever), may be used in the design. For walls that are restrained, a rectangular 
distribution equal to 50 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure acting over the 
entire height of the wall may be used for the design. Additional analyses during 
design may be needed to evaluate the surcharge effects of point loads and line 
loads. 
The retaining structures should be well-drained to reduce the build-up of 
hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide 
zone of permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation), placed directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at 
the base of the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes. As an 
alternative, a prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, 
may be used instead of the drainage material. The prefabricated drainage product 
also should be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall. 
The backfill from the bottom of the wall to the bottom of the perforated pipe or 
weephole should consist of relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential 
for significant water infiltration into the subsurface. In addition, the upper 12 inches 
of the retaining structure backfill should consist of relatively impervious materials 
to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration behind the retaining structure 
unless covered by concrete slabs at the surface.

3.4.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site. 
Based on our field exploration, the presence of concrete rubble and rebar was 
encountered at shallow depths at the project site. Therefore, after the clearing and 
grubbing operation, the upper 3 feet of the surficial soils below the existing ground 
surface should be removed and disposed of properly off-site (or used in landscaped 
areas, if appropriate) because they contain an appreciable amount of construction 
debris. The removal of the upper 3 feet of surficial soils below the ground surface 
may be considered as part of the clearing and grubbing operations and may be 
classified as “unsuitable” materials that should not be used as fill for this project. 
The removal of the upper 3 feet of surficial soils should be replaced with structural fill 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Prior to replacing the 
structural fill material, the bottom of the excavation should be moisture-conditioned 
to above the optimum moisture content and recompacted to 90 percent relative 
compaction. The compaction requirement should be increased to a minimum of 95 
percent relative compaction in areas subject to vehicular traffic.

3.4.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, the on-site soils generated from the cut areas will be too wet for reuse as 
a source of general fill material. Imported structural fill materials should consist of soil 
materials with particles no larger than 3 inches in the largest dimension and should 
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve to prevent 
the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass. The material should have a 
laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent 
or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. 
3.4.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. In areas where the 
finished grades will be subjected to vehicular traffic, the compaction requirement of 
the subgrade should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or 
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or 
ponding should not be allowed to compact the fills. 
Where compaction is less than required, additional compactive effort should be 
applied with adjustment of moisture content, as necessary, to obtain the specified 
compaction.

3.4.4 Fill Slopes 
If permanent fill slopes are constructed using the on-site materials (or imported fill 
materials), these may be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to one 
vertical (2H:1V) or flatter.  Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed 
and benched into the existing slope to provide stability for the new fill against sliding.  
The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level 
horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement 
recommendations. Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back 
to the design slope ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face.  In addition, slope 
planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for erosion 
of the finished slopes. 
3.5

Flexible asphalt concrete pavements may be used for the parking areas and 
driveways. In general, we anticipate the vehicle loading for the pavements will primarily 
consist of passenger vehicles, light pick-up trucks, and occasional heavy trucks.  We have 
assumed that the pavement subgrade will consist of both in-situ soils and imported 
structural fill.  Based on the above assumptions, we recommend using the following 
pavement sections for preliminary design purposes: 
Flexible Pavements

ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight)

3.6 
Utility Trenches 
We anticipate that underground utility connections will be required for the project. 
In general, good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and 
backfilling of the trenches for the proposed utilities. The contractor should determine the 
method and equipment to be used for excavation, subject to practical limits and safety 
considerations. The excavation should comply with all applicable local, state, and federal 
safety requirements. The contractor should be responsible for trench shoring design and 
installation. Trench shoring and bracing should conform to the appropriate health and 
safety requirements. 
In general, we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) for support of the utility lines. The 
initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes should consist of free-draining 
backfills, such as open-graded gravel, to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from 
compaction of the backfill. It is critical to use a free-draining granular material to reduce 
the potential for the formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide 
adequate support for the sides of the pipes.

The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to

optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness,

concrete structure and soil backfill under continuous vehicular traffic dynamic movement.

may be extended to a horizontal distance beyond the edge of the manhole footprint with 
a ratio of the manhole depth. 
3.7 
Drainage 
The finished grades outside the building structures should be sloped to shed water 
away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding. Surface 
water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after construction. It is highly 
recommended to install gutter systems around the buildings and to divert discharge away 
from the foundation and slab areas. Excessive landscape watering near the foundations 
and slabs (within 3 feet of the building perimeter) should be avoided. Planters next to 
foundations also should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the 
potential for significant water infiltration into the subsoils. 
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the 
building foundations and slabs-on-grade because ponded water could cause subsurface 
soil saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength. The foundation excavations 
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting the 
concrete to reduce the potential for appreciable water infiltration into the subsurface. In

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture content (ASTM D2216) and unit weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Test Pits at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Six Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Test Pits at the appropriate sample depths, and graphic 
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Six Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) was performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plates B-2 and B-3. 
One California Bearing Ratio (ASTM D1883) was performed on a bulk sample of 
the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils. The 
test results are presented on Plate B-4. 
One Consolidation test (ASTM D2435) was performed on a remolded sample of 
the potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the 
materials encountered. Results of the consolidation test are presented on Plate B-5. 
One set of Corrosion test, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), was 
performed by our office and Eurofins TestAmerica on a selected soil sample obtained 
from our field exploration. The test results are summarized on Plate B-6.
