at Honolulu Harbor on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project involves various
improvements within the Harbors Division property however our scope of work only
included the replacement of an existing 30 inch diameter pipe culvert with 5 foot 4 inch
private driveway It is anticipated that trench shoring dewatering and installation of a
stabilization layer under the new box culverts will be required for the construction
One boring was drilled to a depth of approximately 20 feet below the existing
ground surface using a truck mounted drill rig Soil samples were collected using
standard split spoon samplers We anticipate that the site is underlain by surficial fill
materials of silty sands and gravel overlying loose to medium dense lagoonal deposits
consisting of silty sand and gravel and very soft to stiff silty clay with fine sand

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger volcano Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae Volcano became extinct while
Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas
in Central Oahu
The Koolau Volcanic Shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by
thinly bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities ceased
approximately 2 5 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicate that the Island
of Oahu has subsided by as much as 1 200 feet since the cessation of this early
volcanic activity Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of the Island of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The
growth of the reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of
lagoons formed behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments
accumulated in the lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the later Pleistocene Epoch ice Age there were many sea level
changes as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As
the great continental glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell since there was less
water available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or
melted global sea levels rose because more water was available The land mass of
Oahu remained essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were
eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that
were both higher and lower relative to the present sea level of Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine sediments in the
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by a thin
surface fill crust over soft lagoonal deposits Based on the poor soil conditions shoring
and dewatering will be an important consideration in the design and construction of the
project Additionally the construction will be done in phases to allow traffic flow through
the construction area A detailed discussion and our geotechnical engineering design
recommendations are presented in the following sections of this report
Concrete Box Culvert
Based on the current design concepts we understand that the existing 30 inch
diameter drain pipe will be replaced with a concrete box culvert In order to maintain
traffic over the site and allow continued water flow two new box culverts will be installed
and the construction will be phased First a 2 foot 8 inch wide by 3 foot deep box
culvert will be installed next to the 30 inch drain pipe in two phases Then the water
flow will be diverted into the new box culvert while the 30 inch drain pipe is removed
and a new 5 foot 4 inch wide by 3 foot deep box culvert is installed in two phases

Foundations
Soft harbor sediments are anticipated at the bottom of the new culvert structures
In order to provide adequate support to the new culverts in this area a
stabilization layer consisting of 24 inches of No 3B Fine gravel wrapped in a
non woven filter fabric such as Mirafi 18ON or equivalent would be required for
uniform bearing support An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 000 pounds
per square foot psf may be used on the stabilization layer We estimate that
footing settlements bearing on the stabilization layer as recommended herein to
be on the order of 2 inches Additional settlements could occur due to improper
dewatering or excessive vibrations during sheet pile installation or removal
This bearing value provided is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by seismic forces
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by
passive earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation
system A coefficient of friction of 0 40 may be used for footings bearing on
recompacted on site soils Resistance due to passive earth pressure on the
downstream side may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of
300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This passive earth resistance
should be reduced to 150 pcf under water However the passive earth pressure
resistance on the upstream side should be neglected due to potential erosion

Excavation Support
We anticipate that excavation depths up to about 10 feet may be required for the
project construction Based on the subsurface soil conditions encountered in the
borings we believe that shoring of the sides of the excavations will be necessary to
protect personnel working in the excavations Shoring of the excavations using
steel sheet pile shoring especially since dewatering of the excavations will be
necessary should be considered
Use of a sheet pile shoring system may also serve as a cut off wall to aid in the
dewatering operations which is further discussed in the Dewatering section of this
report The sheet piles should be driven with a suitable hammer to a sufficient
depth to reduce the potential for areal ground subsidence and to reduce the
amount of dewatering within the excavations The contractor should carefully
evaluate the potential for bottom heave and design the shoring system accordingly
It is important that adequate sheeting be installed prior to excavation and
maintained tight against the excavation walls with proper bracing during excavation
The properly braced sheeting is essential to reduce the potential for appreciable
lateral movements of the adjacent ground into the excavation which may result in

settlement
Dewatering
Dewatering of excavations will be necessary since the invert level of the culverts
will be below sea level Therefore dewatering provisions will need to be included in
the contract documents for the proposed construction Since the excavation may
involve discharge of groundwater a National Pollutant Discharge Elimination
System NPDES permit may be required for this discharge The contractor should
consult their independent consultant for the latest regulations and information
pertaining to the permit application
Dewatering by means of a well point system along the outside of the excavations is
generally not recommended The resultant areal depression of the natural
groundwater table could induce consolidation of the compressible subsurface soils
resulting in potential ground settlements which could affect the existing structures
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to dewatering
Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the contractor The selection of
equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor and
he she should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be
required during construction depending on the conditions encountered The

Backfill
The backfill around the culvert structures should consist of structural fill with a
maximum particle size of less than 3 inches and with less than 15 fines The
backfill should be compacted to a minimum of 90 relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density Compaction should be
accomplished by suitable compaction equipment
Drainage
The headwall structures should be well drained to reduce the build up of
hydrostatic pressures A typical drainage system should consist of a permeable
material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C33 No 67 gradation placed near
the bottom and along the length of the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or
weepholes The weepholes should be spaced not more than 8 feet apart
