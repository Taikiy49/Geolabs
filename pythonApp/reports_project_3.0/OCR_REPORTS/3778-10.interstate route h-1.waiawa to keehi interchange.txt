INTRODUCTION
In accordance with the project specifications the drilled shaft construction for the
sign poles and the directional drilling for the electrical utilities were monitored during
construction The drilled shaft construction began on March 10 1998 and was completed
July 1998 The directional drilling construction began in February 1998 and was
completed in May 1998 This report presents the results of our field observations during
the drilled shaft construction and the directional drilling for the Interstate Route H 1
Waiawa to Keehi Interchange Contraflow and Shoulder Lane Project on the island of
Oahu Hawaii
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Contraflow project site is approximately 14 miles in length stretching along
Interstate Route H 1 from about one half mile west of Waikele Bridge to Keehi
Interchange on the south side of the island of Oahu The project consisted of the
creation of an additional lane for Contraflow inbound traffic In general one of the
two existing outbound lanes is being utilized for the Contraflow lane and the other lane is
being used as a buffer between the Contraflow lane and the opposing traffic A specially
designed movable traffic barrier and vehicle is being used to partition the Contraflow lane
known as the Zipper lane on a daily basis
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3778 10 Page 2
There are four crossover areas to allow traffic to enter and exit the Contraflow
Zipper lane two are near Waikele the third is at the Interstate Routes H 1 H 2
Interchange and the fourth is near the Pearl Harbor Interchange to allow the Contraflow
Zipper lane back onto the inbound shoulder area In the Waiau area the existing
shoulder area adjacent to the median barrier was limited Therefore the inbound lanes
required re striping along with the creation of additional traffic lanes on the mauka side of
the highway This also involved paving of the existing mauka shoulder
In support of the Contraflow Zipper lane project sixteen electric powered signs
two of which are variable message signs or VMS and twenty one non powered signs
were constructed along the Contraflow Zipper lane route The various sign support
schemes are as follows

In order to provide electricity to power the signs electrical lines extending below
the pavement structure and crossing beneath the highway was required Due to the
heavy traffic along the Interstate Route H 1 Highway most of the electrical lines were
installed by directional drilling
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our observation of the drilled shaft construction and directional
drilling was to observe compliance with the intent of the design concepts specifications
and recommendations The purpose was also to expedite suggestions for design
changes that were required when subsurface conditions differed from those considered in

our geotechnical engineering exploration report Our scope of work for this project
included the following tasks and work efforts
1 Providing a field engineer on a near full time basis to observe the drilling
and construction of the 13 drilled shafts
2 Providing a field engineer on a part time basis to observe the directional
drilling and utility installation
3 Providing the necessary engineering support and consultation for problem
resolution during construction
4 Preparing this geotechnical construction observation report summarizing
our field observations

_ DRILLED SHAFT MODIFICATIONS
The overall pedestal height is 6 5 feet However the height of top of pedestal
above ground varies depending on the ground slope On level areas the height of top of
pedestal is approximately 6 inches above the ground surface The design assumed the
pedestal height to be 2 7 feet above the ground surface for all cases
GEOLABS HAWAIl
W O 3778 10 Page 7
During construction it was determined that some of the pedestal heights above
the ground surface would be increased The above grade pedestal heights for the
five drilled shafts with pedestals located on concrete barriers ranged from 2 7 to 4 1 feet
Also it was determined that the above grade pedestal heights for the on grade drilled
shafts would range from 0 1 to 0 5 feet The increase in the pedestal height resulted in
an increase in the overturning moment on the drilled shaft Following analysis with the
increased overturning moment it was determined that no modifications to the drilled
shafts were necessary
Drilling through the rock was found to be difficult at both sides of the sign bridge on
the Interstate Route H 1 Highway at Station 512 93 At these locations the drilled shafts
were not drilled to the original designed depths Due to the harder rock encountered it
was determined that the drilled shafts could be shortened to about 17 feet
Drilling through a boulder near the surface and through the rock near the originally
designed bottom elevation was observed to be difficult at the cantilever sign on the
Interstate Route H 2 Highway at Station 456 00 The basalt rock was observed to be
hard at about 2 feet above the original designed depth Due to the harder rock
encountered and based on the short above grade pedestal height it was determined that
the constructed drilled shaft was adequate
The drilled shaft excavation at the cantilever sign on the Interstate Route H 1
Station 35 70 was shortened after encountering the underlying rock material Due to the
near surface firm materials the drilled shaft was terminated in the underlying rock
material
The drilled shaft on the right side of the sign bridge on the Interstate Route H 1
Highway at Station 105 30 was drilled to a shorter depth than originally designed Due to
the short above grade pedestal height increased pedestal embedment it was
determined that the constructed drilled shaft was adequate
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3778 10 Page 8
Due to the difficulty in creating a neat cut for the pedestal with conventional
construction equipment aside from over reaming with a drill rig the following options
were offered during construction
1 Pour the entire excavation and pedestal in one pour This would require a
floating form for the portion of the pedestal that extends above the ground
2 Pour the entire length of the pedestal in one pour then pour the annulus
area in a later pour This would allow the contractor to use one form from
the bottom to the top of the pedestal without the need to float the form
Lean concrete or flowable concrete with a minimum compressive strength
of 350 psi may be used within the annulus Backfilling of the annulus with
soil materials was not permissible
DRILLED SHAFT OBSERVATIONS as
The drilled shafts were drilled without the use of temporary casing as the
sidewalls of the hole held up relatively well The drilled shafts were drilled using a
Watson 2500 track mounted drill rig equipped with the following
e 36 inch diameter 3 foot long rock auger with bullet type teeth on bottom
and on flights
e 36 inch diameter 5 foot long soil auger
e 24 and 36 inch diameter core barrels with bullet type teeth
e 24 and 36 inch diameter core barrels with saw tooth type teeth
e 23 and 35 inch diameter clean out buckets
e Rock hammer
The drilled shafts were constructed using pre tied steel rebar cages and
4 500 psi concrete supplied by Ameron Hawaii and Hawaiian Cement The rebar cage
was lowered into the hole with a hydraulic crane Bricks tied onto the rebar cage were
used as spacers at the bottoms and sides The concrete was placed into the shaft
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3778 10 Page 9
through a drop chute as no groundwater was encountered The exception is the shaft
at Station 35 70 where the height of the rebar cage above the ground due to error in
the shaft depth precluded placing concrete from above the rebar cage The concrete
was then mixed with a concrete vibrator
The sides of the drilled shafts were cleaned of loose material with various drilling
tools The bottoms of the drilled shafts were cleaned of loose material and debris with
the clean out buckets when deemed necessary The bottoms were found to be fairly
level and the vertical alignment plumb of the shafts were checked by the contractor
with the use of a level The concrete slump generally ranged from 6 to 9 inches The
exception was at Stations 35 70 and 105 30 Right Pole of Sign Bridge where the
concrete was observed to have a slump of about 5 to 6 inches

RE ANALYSES OF DRILLED SHAFTS
The constructed drill shafts were reanalyzed for the adjusted moments due to
changed above grade pedestal heights shorter drilled shaft lengths and different soil
conditions Based on our re analyses of the constructed drilled shafts we determined
that the as constructed drilled shaft lengths were adequate for the design loads and
moments Foundation analyses were conducted based on a combination of axial and
lateral loads in addition to ground line moments using the computer software program
LPILE plus for Windows In addition the drilled shaft foundations were also checked
for the torsional moments induced by the signs The analyses were based on a
free head condition and a maximum lateral deflection of not more than one fourth inch
at the top of the shaft

DIRECTIONAL DRILLING OBSERVATIONS
Directional drilling was utilized to install the electrical line crossings near Waikele
and the Pearl Harbor Interchange Directional drilling was also utilized to install the
electrical lines within the paved median areas from Station 90 05 to Station 35 70 near
the Pearl Harbor Interchange Conventional trench and backfill methods were utilized to
install the electrical lines within unpaved median areas
In general the directional drilling was performed at about 1 7 to 6 7 feet below the
pavement grade However in some instances the directional drilling was performed to
depths ranging from 12 to 18 4 feet below the pavement grade The depths ranged as
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3778 10 Page 15
the holes were drilled with some curvature The holes were drilled to accommodate up to
two 1 to 3 inch diameter electrical conduits within a high density polyethylene HDPE
sleeve The sleeve was installed by pulling it through the hole during the retracting of the
drilling equipment
The directional drilling was performed with a cutting bit with 10 foot long rods
Bentonite slurry was utilized to keep the hole open The bentonite slurry was
re circulated after the suspended excavated soil and rock materials were removed from
the slurry
In general the directional drilling encountered stiff clayey materials medium dense
silty gravel soft basalt rock and soft volcanic tuff Difficult drilling occurred near the
Waikele area in a few short runs into what we believe to be hard basalt rock and
runs into what we believe to be isolated hard areas within the volcanic tuff These difficult
drilling areas were determined by the advancement rate of the 10 foot long rods during
drilling In general the material is assumed to be medium hard to hard rock or boulders if
the advancement rate is slower than about 0 25 to 0 5 feet per minute
