GEOTECHNICAL ENGINEERING EXPLORATION
POLICE EOC EMPLOYEE PARKING ROAD
Dear Mr Tomita
In accordance with our fee proposal dated we have conducted
a geotechnical engineering exploration for the Police EOC Employee Parking Road project
This letter report summarizes our work on the project and presents our geotechnical
engineering recommendations pertaining to the design of the pavement section site
preparation and storm water run off and sewer disposal system design for the subject
project
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
Based on information provided the proposed project will consist of the construction
of a new roadway located within the Lihue area on the Island of Kauai Hawaii as shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1 The proposed roadway is about 25 feet wide by about
2 000 feet in length and extends from Hoolako Street to the proposed Kaana Street
Extension Cuts and fills on the order of 1 to 3 feet are anticipated for the new roadway
construction
REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene Epoch
more than million years before present Following the cessation of this main shield
building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of the
post erosional Koloa Voicanic Series and the concurrent deposition of alluvial sediments of
the Palikea Formation
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of
Kauai including Lihue These rocks are generally characterized as thick flows of dense
basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in north south trends at various locations

Associated with the vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low cinder cones at
the vent
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
growth and retreat of the great continental glaciers As the glaciers advanced less water
was available to fill the ocean basins and sea levels fell below the current sea level As the
glaciers melted and retreated more water became available thus the sea level rose and the
streams aggraded their floors to form fills When the sea level fell the streams removed part
or all of the fills resulting in superimposed complex valley in valley deposits of gravel
boulders and mud
The project area is composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of the lavas of the
Koloa Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows of jointed dense
vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers The weathering process has formed a
mantle of residual soils near the surface In general residual soils are composed mainly of
clayey and silty materials which are typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks The
residual soils generally grade to saprolite and basaltic rock formation with depth
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is located west of Kapule Highway in the Lihue area on the Island of
Kauai Hawaii The project site is bounded by the proposed Kaana Street Extension on the
north Kapule Highway on the east and Vidinha Stadium on the south as shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2
The project site is situated on an abandoned sugar cane field Based on the
topographic survey by M E Pacific Inc dated the existing ground
surface generally slopes up to the north with elevations ranging from 168 to 184 feet Mean
Sea Level MSL
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsoil conditions at the subject site were explored by drilling and sampling six
borings ranging in depths from about 2 to 4 8 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our field exploration indicate that the project site is generally underlain by residual
soils consisting of medium stiff to very stiff clayey silt to the maximum depths explored
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings at the time of the field exploration
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered in the borings are presented on the
Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6 Results of the laboratory tests performed on
selected soils samples are presented on Plates B 1 through B 4

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The field exploration generally encountered medium stiff to very stiff residual soils
near the existing ground surface Our laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR tests
indicated that these near surface soils generally have reasonable pavement support
characteristics
Cuts and fills on the order of about 1 to 3 feet are anticipated for the project The
on site residual soils may be used for general fills Detailed geotechnical engineering
recommendations regarding pavement design site grading and storm water run off and
sewer disposal system design are presented in the following sections
Pavement Design
We understand that asphalt concrete pavement is planned for the new roadway We
anticipate that the vehicle loading for the proposed roadway for this project will consist
primarily of passenger vehicles buses and maintenance vehicles Therefore we have
assumed medium traffic loading conditions with a 24 hour truck traffic of 8 percent and a
truck traffic distribution of 25 percent of 2 axle trucks and 75 percent of 3 axle trucks for
pavement design purposes Based on our laboratory testing the on site silty soils appear
to have a CBR value of 19 with an expansion value of 1 2 percent Based on the above
assumptions we believe that the following pavement section may be used for preliminary
design purposes

The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to
not less than 90 percent relative compaction The compaction requirements for the
pavement subgrades have been reduced due to the high rainfall in this area CBR and
density tests should be performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered during
construction to confirm the adequacy of the above sections
Aggregate base course and aggregate subbase materials required for the pavement
sections should consist of crushed basalt aggregate and should conform to Sections 703 06
and 703 17 of the State of Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road Bridge and Public
Works Construction 1994 HSS The aggregate base course and aggregate subbase
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should maintained to carry
surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during
or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or adjacent
to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs be extended a minimum of 2 inches
into the subgrade soil below the base and subbase course layers to reduce the migration of
landscape water into the pavement section Alternatively a subdrain system could be
constructed to collect excessive water from landscaping irrigation For long term
performance we recommend that a subdrain system be constructed adjacent to
paved landscaped areas

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to reduce potential
contamination of the excavated materials Scattered loose boulders lying on the
GEOLABS INC
M E Pacific inc Page 5

existing ground surface at the finished subgrade elevation should be removed to
expose firm ground Soft loose and or yielding areas disclosed during clearing and
subgrade preparation operations should be backfilled with well compacted fill
Finished subgrades and areas to receive fill should be scarified to a depth of about
8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to
the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to
the maximum dry density
Fill Materials and Compaction Requirements
In general the fill and backfill materials to be used as general fill material up to the
bottom of the aggregate base course or aggregate subbase may consist of the
excavated on site materials provided that they are free of vegetation deleterious
materials and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension
If imported general fill materials are required the imported materials should consist
of materials with a low to moderate expansion potential and with particle sizes of
3 inches or less in maximum dimension The material should have a laboratory
California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 19 or higher and a swell potential of not more
than 2 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883
Imported fill materials should be tested by Geolabs for conformance with these
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use
Based on our field exploration and laboratory test results it appears that the in situ
soils exist in a relatively moist state with high moisture contents Therefore drying
and aeration of the excavated materials may be necessary prior to their use as
engineered fills Aeration to lower the soil moisture and more compaction effort with
thinner fill lifts to achieve the specified compaction may be necessary and would
generally reduce the rate of fill placement for this project Compaction should be
accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers or other types of acceptable compaction
equipment Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed to compact the
on site silty soils General fill materials should be moisture conditioned to at least
2 percent above the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding
8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction
Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings it appears that the
cut slopes would expose soils consisting of stiff clayey silt In general we believe that

the cut slopes planned may be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to
one vertical 2H 1V or flatter
Permanent fill slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fills
placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the existing
slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations should

should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the
finished slopes
Storm Water Run Off Disposal Systems
We understand that the use of detention basins for storm water run off disposal are
being contemplated for the project but the basin size has not yet been determined It is our
understanding that a temporary detention basin is planned at the future parking area and a
permanent detention basin is planned above the police station area The detention basins
will have a maximum depth of 0 7 feet A total of three field permeability tests were
performed at three locations Boring Nos P 1 P 2 and P 3 as shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The field permeability tests were conducted to evaluate the infiltration characteristics
of the subsurface materials The permeability tests were performed in 3 5 inch diameter bore
holes drilled to 4 5 to 4 8 feet depths Results of the tests and the calculated permeability
are presented on Plates C 1 to C 3
Based on the field test results a coefficient of permeability of 2 x 10E 6 centimeters
per second was calculated for the upper subsoil For a detention basin measuring
approximately 200 feet long 100 feet wide and 0 7 feet in deep a disposal capacity of
approximately 870 gallons per day was calculated

We understand that seepage pits will be utilized for wastewater disposal and that prior
to placement into the seepage pits the wastewater will have undergone some primary
treatment For preliminary design purposes a percolation rate of 40 minutes per inch may
be used Subsequent to our field percolation testing the calculated percolation rate will be

Corrosion Control
Chemical analyses including pH and minimum resistivity were performed on selected
samples obtained during our field exploration for corrosivity evaluation The results of these
tests are presented on Plate B 1
The resistivity tests were performed in general conformance with ASTM Test
Designation G 57 95 for laboratory resistivity tests with a Miller box The resistivity tests were
also performed in general conformance with the requirements provided in the Water System
External Corrosion Control Standards Volume 3 dated 1991 The pH tests were performed
in general conformance with ASTM Test Designation D 2976 for laboratory pH tests and in
general conformance to the requirements provided in the Water System External Corrosion
Control Standards Volume 3 dated 1991
Based on the tests performed for the corrosion study Corrosion Ratings 2 and 3
should be used for the pipes
Construction Observation
Construction observation of the site grading work during construction is an integral
part of the geotechnical engineering recommendations provided herein The items of
construction monitoring that are critical requiring Special Inspection include observation of
the site preparation fill placement and compaction and pavement construction Other
aspects of earthwork construction should also be observed by a representative from
Geolabs This is to observe compliance with the intent of the design concepts
specifications or recommendations and to expedite suggestions for design changes that may
be required in the event that subsurface conditions differ from those considered in this report
The recommendations provided in this letter report are contingent upon such observations
If the actual exposed subsurface conditions encountered during construction are
different from those assumed or considered in this report then appropriate modifications to
the design should be made
LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this letter report are based upon
visual observations made during our field exploration and laboratory testing of the soil
samples recovered Variations of subsurface conditions between and beyond the sampling
points may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until
construction is underway If variations then appear evident Geolabs Inc should be
contacted to re evaluate the recommendations presented in this report
