REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Molokai was formed by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from 
two primary shield volcanoes during the early to middle Pleistocene Epoch. The shield 
volcanoes are known as the East Molokai Mountain and the West Molokai Mountain. The 
West Molokai Mountain, comprising the western end of the island, is a low profile volcanic 
dome compared to the higher profile volcanic dome of the East Molokai Mountain which 
comprises the eastern end of the island of Molokai. A broad upland plain known as the 
Hoolehua Plain comprises the central portion of the island where the two shield volcanoes 
coalesced.  It should be noted that a compact, younger, and short-lived volcano erupted in 
the late Pleistocene at Kalaupapa to form the Kalaupapa Peninsula lowland along the 
north coast of the eastern volcano. 
The older West Molokai Mountain consists almost entirely of thin-bedded a`a and 
pahoehoe lavas with limited late stage cinder and spatter cones. The volcanic deposits are 
referred to as the West Molokai Volcanic Series. The existing ground surfaces are 
generally covered by extensive lateritic soil development indicating a long ago volcanic 
extinction while the East Molokai Mountain remained active. Western Molokai is generally 
arid with few water supply resources. 
The younger East Molokai Mountain dome is mainly composed of thin-bedded 
tholeiitic a`a and pahoehoe lavas referred to as the lower member of the East Molokai 
Volcanic Series. Most of the East Molokai dome is veneered with alkali basalts and 
trachyte which erupted from large cones and bulbous domes atop the lower member 
deposits. These deposits are referred to as the upper member of the East Molokai 
volcanic series. 
The tall and broad eastern volcanic dome is deeply incised by large drainage 
valleys that cut into the softer caldera filling lavas comprising the central portion of the East 
Molokai Mountain. The large valleys generally drain towards the northern coastline. 
Faulting and erosion by ocean wave energy attack is believed to have created the steep 
and tall cliffs bounding the majority of the northern shoreline of the island. The uplands of 
Eastern Molokai are generally forested and high enough to readily tap incoming moisture 
from the predominant trade wind flow; thus, groundwater resources in East Molokai are 
generally plentiful.

During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), a variety of sea level changes occurred as 
a result of widespread glacial advance and retreat in the worldwide continents. As the 
great continental glaciers advanced and accumulated, the level of the ocean fell due to a 
lower quantity of water contained in the oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers 
receded/melted, global sea levels rose due to the increase in meltwater. The land mass of 
the Island of Molokai remained essentially stable in terms of uplift and settlement during 
these changes; therefore, the fluctuations of sea level were eustatic with respect to the 
landmass elevation.  These glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that 
were both higher and lower relative to the present sea level on Molokai. Thus, in the 
present time, ancient marine deposits may be exposed on the land surface and ancient 
terrestrial deposits may be encountered buried in today’s offshore depositional 
environment. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were also affected by these 
glacio-eustatic sea level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the landmass erosional 
base level was correspondingly lower, and deep valleys were carved to depths below the 
present sea level.  When the sea level was high, the erosion base level was raised such 
that sediments accumulated at higher elevations. 
In the mountainous regions of the Island of Molokai, the erosion processes are 
dominated by mass wasting, which is the detachment and fall by rockfall and landslide 
activity, of various soil and rock materials from the steep-sided valley walls. These 
mobilized and mixed colluvial deposits are then transported by gravity down slope toward 
the axis of a valley.  Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a 
valley, alluvial transport processes become dominant, and the sediments are further 
mobilized and deposited as alluvium accumulations. 
In general, many Hawaii stream flows are intermittent and flashy, such that the 
discharge flows may transmit very large volumes of water for very short duration.  
Because of this, transport of sediments is intermittent, and the bulk of the stream's 
hydraulic load consists of a poorly-sorted mixture of boulders, cobbles, gravel, sands, and 
fines.  When the landmass erosional base level changes, these sediment loads and 
deposits are either scoured and remobilized or buried in place as alluvial and colluvial 
deposits. 
When alluvial and colluvial deposits are buried in place for long periods of time, 
chemical processes begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or 
further weathering of the material. Chemical processes also cause induration, or 
cementation, of the sediment resulting in a poorly to moderately consolidated sedimentary 
rock, such as siltstone, sandstone and conglomerate. Simultaneously, erosion may 
continue in the upland areas above the valley floors. This continued upland erosion 
generates additional unconsolidated materials, which are then transported down slope to 
further bury the older consolidated alluvial and colluvial deposits. 
Depending on the local base level and rate of transport, these younger alluvial and 
colluvial sediments are generally transient in terms of geologic time and space. In addition,

The project site is adjacent to the eastern portal of the Molokai Irrigation Tunnel. 
The tunnel extends approximately 5 miles between Waikolu Valley and the North Fork of 
Kaunakakai Gulch on the Island of Molokai, Hawaii, as shown on the Project Location

Catwalk to Diversion/Intake No. 1 Structure 
The existing catwalk leading to the Intake No. 1 structure consists of a steel-framed 
catwalk with fiberglass grates covering the walking surface. The elevated catwalk is about 
30 inches wide and straddles the 20-inch diameter iron water transmission pipeline leading 
from Intake No. 1 towards the irrigation tunnel portal. We observed that the pipeline and 
concrete block footings for the catwalk bear on a slope bench that appears to have been 
cut into the side of the steep rock slope. The catwalk is generally level to very slightly 
inclined and is about 15 to 30 feet above the adjacent valley floor. The catwalk measures 
about 380 linear feet in total length between the irrigation tunnel portal and the Intake 
No. 1 structure. 
The slope bench providing the foundation support for the existing pipeline and 
catwalk appears to be constructed in a cut condition with generally medium hard to hard 
rock formation extending down slope toward the valley floor elevation. The slope bench 
ranges between about 5 and 8 feet in total width. Existing open space between the inner 
pipeline edge and the upper slope face varies between about 1 foot and 3.8 feet along the 
catwalk. Existing open space between the outer pipeline edge and the lower slope face 
varies between about 1.5 and 3 feet. The top of the existing catwalk surface above the 
bench surface averages about 3 feet. 
The pipeline and catwalk traverse across a section of steep rock slope standing at 
a typical inclination of about one-fourth horizontal to one vertical (0.25H:1V). The slope 
extends for several hundred feet in height above the valley floor before merging into the 
main valley wall which extends further to the western rim of the valley. Based on our 
observations, the steep natural mountain slope above the northern portion of the walkway 
appears to have a slight break in slope inclination at a height of about 40 to 50 feet above 
the catwalk. In contrast, the steep natural mountain slope extending above the southern 
portion of the walkway appears to rise steadily in elevation for about 100 feet or greater in 
height before a slight break in the slope inclination.

Zone No. 1: Rock Slope Instability and Catwalk Damage 
We understand that a landslide occurred two years ago above the catwalk at a 
location about 60 feet southerly from the irrigation tunnel portal. The landslide completely 
demolished about 36 linear feet of the catwalk structure extending between about 
Sta. 0+59 and Sta. 0+95. Based on our drone reconnaissance, the landslide appears to 
have initiated from high on the slope and swept down a small incised ravine. The incised 
ravine appears to terminate and become indistinguishable about 40 feet above the 
catwalk. The landslide discharge appears to have triggered some additional rockfall from a 
height of about 25 feet above the catwalk. The localized rockfall scar on the lower slope 
remains visible but the landslide source scar on the upper slope has since healed. 
The landslide mass had demolished a 36-foot section of the steel-framed catwalk 
structure. We observed that the landslide impact and scour may have caused some 
localized ground loss at the outer edge of the slope bench adjacent to the pipeline. 
Undermining of the pipe was not observed; however, the bench surface extends only 
about 6-inches beyond the outer edge of the pipe where the ground loss was observed. 
We did not observe overt visible damage to the pipeline. The ground loss appears to be 
confined to a short segment of the damaged catwalk where an alignment deflection occurs 
as the catwalk follows the slope face contour. 
Based on our observations, the rock slope above the damaged catwalk is densely 
vegetated with a near-complete ground cover and some scattered shrubbery. Observation 
of the localized exposure of the slope indicates that some poorly-supported blocks of 
fractured rock, consisting of boulder size slabs, remain precariously perched on the slope 
above the damaged catwalk. As a result of the previous landslide occurrence and the 
potential for additional slides to occur via a similar slope instability mechanism, we 
recommend the slope stabilization and pipeline protection measures as a priority for a 
Phase 1 remediation.  
Upon the rock slope being cleaned by scaling and stabilized by anchored mesh in 
the vicinity of the landslide, the catwalk may be restored with segmental precast concrete 
boxes over the existing pipeline to provide protection from potential future rockfall and 
landslide hazards.

Zone No. 2: Potential Rock Overhang Instability 
We observed a potential rockfall/rockslide hazard condition related to a section of 
overhanging cut slope along another section of the catwalk leading to Intake No. 1. This 
section of overhanging rock cut slope begins at about 135 feet beyond the end of the Zone 
No. 1 catwalk damage as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The observed overhanging rock 
condition extends a distance of about 91 feet in length further towards the south along the 
catwalk. 
Based on our observations, it appears that the original pipeline/catwalk construction 
involved the excavation of a concave cut into the rock slope to provide the bench surface 
for the pipeline construction. The concave slope cut appears to provide a natural 
overhanging shield against potential falling rock and debris that could emanate from higher 
on the slope. The length of the continuous concave rock overhang is about 91 feet and the 
cut depth into the slope relative to the overhanging slope ranges on the order of about 5 to 
8 feet. The typical height of the rock overhang above the catwalk surface is about 6 to 
7 feet. There is some open space present on both the inside and outside of the existing 
catwalk throughout the length of Zone No. 2. The available bench floor space averages 
about 1 to 3 feet on the cut slope side of the catwalk and about 1.5 to 3 feet on the stream 
side of the catwalk. 
In general, we observed widespread open fractures within the moderately fractured, 
hard basaltic rock comprising the roof of the rock overhang. We observed loose pieces of 
rock that could be moved by hand and frequent vertical-oriented rock fractures extending 
up into the overhanging rock mass that could facilitate the release of rock mass 
components generally consisting of slab-shaped pieces to boulder sized hard rock 
materials. We observed some empty pockets within the exposed rock face of the 
overhang that indicate some boulders and blocks of rock previously released from the 
overhang roof. Therefore, we recommend installing the segmental precast concrete boxes 
over the pipeline, as recommended for pipeline protection at Zone No. 1. 
Based on our observations, it appears that Zone No. 2 may be exposed to the 
potential release of individual rock (boulders). Due to the widespread vertical roof 
fracturing, the overhang may be subjected to a larger rockslide release that could 
encompass larger portions of the overhanging rock mass. Rockfall and slope stabilization 
at Zone No. 2 may be considered a proactive measure to protect the infrastructure from 
possible falling rock hazards. Amongst other factors, the history of rockfall events and/or 
damage events at the catwalk should be considered to assess the critical need for the 
protection improvements.

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The existing catwalk leading to Intake No. 1 within Waikolu Valley is exposed to 
natural falling rock hazards from the steep and tall mountain slopes bordering and 
extending well-above the existing catwalk at the project site. Falling rock hazards may 
involve the mass wasting release of individual boulders and/or blocks of hard rock and 
rockslides that encompass larger masses of soil and broken rock material.  
Because the catwalk is constructed near the toe of a steep and tall mountain slope, 
rocks falling from high on the slope are more likely to have higher velocity, outward 
bouncing trajectories that should direct most high elevation falling material away from the 
catwalk and further out towards the valley axis. In contrast, falling rocks emanating from a

Rock slope stabilization work conducted within the overhanging section of the slope 
should be focused on stabilizing the overhanging rock mass. Slope stabilization efforts 
other than rock safety scaling above the overhang may not be necessary since the 
overhang feature provides natural protection from rockfall emanating from above the 
overhanging topography. 
Because of the expected high cost for slope stabilization and pipeline protection 
work at Waikolu Valley, the improvements are preferred to be conducted in a phased 
approach, to accommodate restrained construction budgets. We recommend that slope 
stabilization and pipeline protection be conducted at Zone No. 1 as a priority because of 
the destroyed section of the catwalk and the potential for future landslide and rockfall 
activity to affect the area.  Remedial work conducted at Zone Nos. 2 and 3 may be 
considered as a proactive mitigation effort to protect the pipeline from possible future 
rockfall and landslide activity.  
We recommend installing segmental precast concrete boxes over the water 
transmission pipeline and under the existing steel-framed catwalk structure. The 
segmental reinforced concrete to be designed as a protective cover should withstand 
rockfall and landslide activity better than the steel-frame and fiberglass deck system 
currently in-place.  The segmental concrete boxes may be airlifted to the project site from 
a helicopter airlift staging area (Waikolu Valley Overlook) at the valley rim.

Other Construction Considerations 
The cost of the recommended construction is expected to be high due to the 
remote location of the Waikolu Valley project site on the Island of Molokai. The limited 
options for site access via either helicopter or the irrigation tunnel are difficult with its own 
limitations. In addition, the hazardous slope work and helicopter airlifting are dependent on 
the weather conditions and may be delayed due to unsafe conditions including rainfall, 
precipitation runoff, low visibility and wind. 
We envision that helicopter airlifting of various equipment and materials will be 
needed for the slope stabilization work and the pipeline protection work. It appears that a 
viable helicopter airlift and materials staging area could be established at the nearby 
Waikolu Valley Overlook.  Access and permitting issues should be carefully evaluated for 
the use of this important close-proximity helicopter airlift and materials staging area. 
We believe the on-site slope stabilization work could be performed with limited 
mechanized equipment which includes the use of a slope anchored wagon or bencher 
drill, colloidal grout mixer and bobcat loader. Heavy lift and light lift helicopters would likely 
be needed to mobilize and lay the slope mesh, import grouting supplies and to mobilize 
and place the segmental precast concrete components.

vegetation clearing and rock debris generated from the rock slope scaling will require

applicability of the findings and preliminary recommendations contained in this letter report
