exposed in test pits excavated by workers employed by Makani Kai Marina
on The approximate locations and sketches of these

Test Pit at Footing C
The test pit at Footing C was about 3 by 3 feet in size and about 2 5
feet below the existing grade The top of footing was found to be
rough and sloped down southeasterly off from the footing pedestal
stem The bottom of the corrugated metal pipe casing was about 6
inches above the top of the footing The edge of footing extended
beyond the test pit and could not be determined The backfill
material over the footing consisted of brown stiff silty clay
containing small coral fragments
We were unable to determine the foundation soil condition from this
test pit excavation
Test Pit at Footing D
The test pit at Footing D was somewhat smaller due to the nearness of
the sewer line to the east and a lined open ditch to the south The
test pit measured about 2 by 3 feet The top and one side of the
footing were exposed The bottom of the corrugated metal pipe casing
was about 6 inches above the top of the footing The side of footing
was neither parallel nor normal to the eastern wall line of Building
20 but it had large angle about 45 to 60 degrees to the east wall
line running generally in northeastern direction intersecting a
normal line to the southern edge of footing pedestal at about
feet from the edge of corrugated metal pipe casing

The depths to the top of footing varied from 5 feet 1 inch to 5 feet 4
inches within the test pit All depth measurements refer to the top
of footing pedestal Mr McCants reported that the edge thickness of
footing was 10 inches measured by scraping with a bolt attached to
end of an 8 foot long steel pipe An open ended 2 inch diameter
steel pipe was used to sample the soils from the bottom of the test
pit from 7 to 8 feet below the top of the footing pedestal The
material below the footing level consisted of medium stiff brown silty
clay containing coral fragments This material appeared to be fill
Water was encountered at 7 5 foot level that seeped into the small
hole made by the 2 inch steel pipe The water and soil had a septic
odor

Conc lusions
The results of test pit at Footing C are not conclusive as to the
actual foundation soil condition below Footing C However if the
footing size as implied by the test pit is realistic foundation soil
bearing would be substantially small and may not be a problem This
point should be reviewed by the original soils engineer
The results of test pit at Footing D and cross section measurements
indicate that the footing appears to be in fill and above the invert
of the adjacent sewer line These conditions apparently are not in
accordance with the following criteria as previously stipulated by
others
1 Division of Sewers required the footings extending into the
sewer easement be placed below the invert of sewer line per
letter of by Gray Rhee Associates
2 The original soils engineer recommended that footing excavations
be deepened to bottom entirely in residual soil per letter of
by Harding Lawson Associates
We noted that the fill below the footing in the Footing D test pit was
poorly compacted This is in general agreement with the fill
condition described in the handwritten record dated May 29 1978 by
of Harding Lawson on a test pit observation
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1202 00
Page 5
In general a poorly compacted fill has a higher potential to settle
under a footing load and or on saturation Therefore it is probable
that Footing D may have settled and could still settle more in the
future
Corrective measures may consist of deepening the bottom of Footing D
into the undisturbed firm residual soil below the invert of the sewer
line as recommended by the original soils engineer This will entail
underpinning the footing excavating below the bottom of footing
inspecting the soils conditions performing additional trimming as
deemed necessary placing concrete backfilling and restoring the site
The water encountered in the Footing D test pit can best be explained
by a sewer line leak or leaks It is felt that the sewer line or
lines should be checked for leakage and be repaired if so found If
seepage still persists such as during the underpinning excavation
work for Footing D a subdrain may need to be installed This new
subdrain should intercept the bottom of sewer line trench and daylight
out on the lower portion of the slope to bleed off the water and
reduce a potential seepage pressure build up in the slope The
construction of the subdrain may be similar to that recommended by the
original soils engineer except that it need not be located under the
surface lined ditch

Cautions on the potential instability were mentioned in the letter of
May 12 1978 by the original soils engineers They also stipulated
that a soil slippage would continue to occur Therefore it may be
appropriate to monitor the movements of the footings and the slope by
some means We believe that the simplest monitoring procedure may
consist of periodically taking photographs of potential areas of
cracks throughout the building and checking the spaces between the
casing and the footing pedestal at set points Other instrumentation
procedures to monitor movements may include installing strain gauges
across the existing cracks and making elevation surveys on fixed
points Strain gauges are extremely sensitive and the data developed
may be very confusing Vertical surveys are relatively insensitive
and require that a relatively significant movement to occur to be
detected by the readings Thus these other instrumentation proce
dures may not be practical for the project
