DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our literature research site reconnaissance and field exploration
performed for the Phase portion of the proposed Kaumualii Highway Widening project
we anticipate that the proposed highway alignments generally will traverse weathered
volcanic rock and alluvial soil deposits The majority of the project site is underlain by
medium stiff to very stiff residual soils and saprolite highly weathered volcanic rock
materials Basalt rock formations also were encountered at various depths in several
of the borings drilled The basalt formations generally range from extremely weathered
to relatively unweathered and from soft to hard Because the proposed alignment will
traverse numerous streams and drainageways soft alluvial deposits likely will be
encountered at many of these areas and at other alluvial flood plains
Several geotechnical considerations that may impact the project construction
and future highway maintenance were identified in the field during our site
reconnaissance and field exploration program These geotechnical considerations
include slope raveling or failure in the steep cut slopes settlement of the new fill
embankments due to the soft soil deposits seepage of groundwater and flooding
associated with high rainfall storm events Therefore special attention should be given
to the recommendations for cut and fill slope design soft ground stabilization drainage
design and erosion control measures during the design and construction to reduce the
impacts of these geotechnical considerations on the project
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated along the proposed alignments
we believe that a slope inclination of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V may be used
for the design of the proposed cut and fill slopes Where steeper slope inclinations are
desired due to limited extent of right of ways reinforced soil slopes in fill areas and
steepened cut slopes with special erosion control measures in cut areas may be
considered In addition to construction of earth berms V ditches and slope planting
other erosion control measures such as flexible geosynthetic matting may offer a viable
solution for slope erosion control problems by promoting mulching reducing water
runoff velocity and establishing temporary and permanent erosion control on the slope
face
We anticipate that the proposed highway alignment will traverse soft loose
and or unstable ground conditions such as swamps gullies and stream crossings with
recent alluvial soils In general the soft and or loose soil deposits encountered should
be removed where practical and replaced with compacted fills Otherwise we
recommend that a working platform or a drainage blanket consisting of a woven
geotextile and a gravel layer be established at these soft ground areas prior to fill

materials were found to have relatively high moisture contents Groundwater
levels observed in some of the borings drilled indicate that perched groundwater

weathered materials earthwork cuts may expose permeable layers responsible

potentially unstable cut faces Therefore provisions should be made in the
construction documents to provide for subsurface drainage at select areas in the

Flooding by Rainfall
Based on a review of available topographic maps and reconnaissance of the site
the Island of Kauai contains numerous rivers streams and drainage paths as a
result of the high volume of tropical rainfall Extended periods of heavy rainfall
may swell drainage pathways to their capacity Based on our site
reconnaissance many of the existing culvert crossings and bridges along the
Kaumualii Highway are partially choked with alluvial sediments and or debris
Therefore the drainage structures may not have the capacity to handle extreme
storm events We wish to emphasize that adequate modeling of the storm water
runoff and scour potential should be performed for the design of the proposed
culvert and bridge structures
Shoring and Dewatering
We anticipate that some relatively deep excavations will be required for
construction of some of the drainage structures Open cut excavation may be
desirable provided that the excavation may be setback away from the existing
on grade and below grade structures and utility lines However the deep
excavations will require installation of shoring systems such as cofferdams
and or steel sheet pile shoring
We envision that some of the excavations will be made in areas where the

Soft Ground Stabilization
Based on our site reconnaissance and field exploration performed for the
proposed Kaumualii Highway Widening project we anticipate that the proposed
highway alignment will traverse soft and or unstable grounds such as at swamps and
stream crossings There are several ways to mitigate potential instability and ground
settlements associated with soft soils
General discussions and preliminary recommendations for soft ground
stabilization are discussed below The items of soft soil stabilization that are addressed
in the subsequent subsections include the following
Remove and Replace
Blanket Stabilization
Vibro Replacement Stone Columns
Jet Grouting
Remove and Replace
Ideally the soft soils encountered within the limits of the proposed highway
alignment should be removed and replaced with suitable materials prior to new
embankment fill placement and or pavement construction Based on our field
exploration the majority of the soft soils encountered in the borings drilled are
limited to less than about 10 feet from the ground surface In areas where soft
soil deposits are limited near the ground surface we believe that the soft soils
may be removed and replaced with compacted fills In general complete
removal and replacement should be implemented in other areas with limited
amounts of soft soil deposits such as at drainage crossings for some of the small
pipe culverts
However where soft soil deposits are anticipated at greater depths we believe
that complete removal and replacement of the soft soil deposits would not be
cost effective In addition some construction difficulties may be anticipated due
to shoring and dewatering considerations Therefore alternate schemes utilizing
soft ground stabilization may be more appropriate than complete removal and
replacement where soft soil deposits are anticipated at greater depths
Blanket Stabilization
Because the surface soils in the soft ground areas would be marshy and wet we
believe that it would not have sufficient strength to support the weight of heavy
construction equipment Therefore a working platform or drainage blanket
should be established prior to filling of the area The working platform may
consist of a combination of a woven geotextile fabric and a gravel layer The
woven geotextile fabric would reduce the need for strict gradation requirements

of the drainage blanket because it would act as a filter medium to reduce the
potential for gravel loss into the soft ground In addition the woven geotextile
also would prevent the mud and fine soil particles from contaminating the
granular drainage blanket material It also provides tensile reinforcement to the

The drainage blanket should consist of granular crushed rock materials such as

the soft materials The controlled rate of filling should be maintained to allow the
consolidating soils to gain sufficient strength to support the next lift The actual
filling rate in the soft areas should be established in the field based on settlement
gauge readings and adjusted to meet field conditions
The stability of the new fill slopes should be evaluated for potential local and global
slope failure In areas where slope stability becomes a concern due to extensive
deposits of soft soils and or appreciable fill heights alternative methods of soft soil
stabilization may be required We envision that blanket stabilization may be
applicable where the thickness of the soft soil deposits is less than about 10 feet
vertically and or the new fill heights is less than about 15 feet vertically
Vibro Replacement Stone Columns
Vibro replacement methods may be preferred to mitigate soft soil problems when
soft soil deposits are encountered and extend deep into the subsurface
Vibro replacement methods consist of inserting a vibrating probe into the soft soils
placing crushed rock in the void created by the probe and compacting the crushed
rock to form a stone column Stone columns typically have diameters of about
2 5 to 3 feet and are placed at about 4 to 10 feet on center depending on the soft
soil thickness and height of fill embankment Closer stone column spacings
generally are used for the more heavily loaded cases
The stone columns act to reinforce the soft soils and provide strength and stability
to the surrounding mass of soil In addition the stone columns will provide a
means to dissipate pore pressures generated when new fills are placed thus
increasing the rate of consolidation of the soft soil deposits We envision that soil
stabilization by vibro replacement would be more cost effective at areas containing
soft soil deposits at deeper depths

Jet Grouting
Jet grouting is a ground modification method in which thin high pressure jets of
cement grout are discharged sideways into the borehole wall to simultaneously
excavate and then mix with the soil The result is a column of modified soil with low
permeability and improved strength This technique is effective across the widest
range of soil types including granular soils such as sands and gravel and
cohesive soils such as silts and clays
Jet grouting generally will provide greater strength to the improved soil than
vibro replacement However jet grouting is generally significantly more costly than
vibro replacement In addition there are quality control and quality assurance
issues with obtaining adequate size of the treated soil because the soil
improvement takes place below ground In general we believe that jet grouting
may be considered as an alternative to vibro replacement especially in areas
where significant improvement in soil strength is required

Geogrids are generally polymer grid structures with a tensile strength
comparable to steel It generally provides a cost effective solution to slope stability
problems which may include the following insufficient right of way high surcharge
loads poor quality fills high seismic forces steep slopes or difficult landslide repairs
When geogrids are placed in soil the grid geometry interlocks with the adjacent soil
creating a soil geogrid composite with greatly enhanced engineering properties
Different grid configurations are available to provide optimum soil grid interaction for a
range of soil types and slope reinforcement applications Reinforced slope geotextiles
work in a similar manner to reinforced slope geogrids The construction methodology is
briefly described as follows
As the slope is constructed near horizontal layers of geogrids are placed in the
compacted fill at predetermined levels The lengths of the geogrid layers are designed
to anchor potential failure zones into stable interior sections of the embankment or
hillside As forces develop within a soil mass the high modulus geogrids are

Steepened Cut Slopes
We understand that the realigned Hoomana Road will require cut slopes on both
sides of the new alignment Based on the information provided cut slope heights of up to
about 40 feet will be required to achieve design grades As mentioned previously we
recommend that a cut slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter may be used for the design of
the planned cut slopes exposing medium stiff to very stiff residual soils and saprolite
However due to the limited right of way of the new Hoomana Road we understand that it
is desired to construct a steeper cut slope or build a retaining wall to accommodate the
elevation difference Based on the relatively high cut slope planned we recommend that a
steepened cut slope with special erosion control measures be considered at this location
In general we believe that a cut slope inclination of up to about 1H 1V may be
achieved with the provision of special erosion control measures The special erosion
control measure for the steepened cut slope should consist of an anchored wire mesh with
grouted soil anchors Provision of an anchored wire mesh cover over the cut slope face
should assist in the retention of the near surface soil horizon on the steepened cut slope
face We believe that the anchored wire mesh should provide positive erosion control
measures for the steepened cut slope
The anchored wire mesh system is an active slope stabilization system where an
alloyed high tensile steel wire mesh is anchored onto the slope face by applying a defined
torque force in the grouted soil anchors and spike plates The anchored wire mesh
system should be designed to withstand the gravity forces from the near surface soil
horizon to reduce the potential for the soils to move under the anchored wire mesh
system Based on our evaluation we recommend that a 10 foot by 10 foot nail spacing
arranged in a triangular grid pattern with 14 foot deep nails be used for the preliminary
design of the anchored wire mesh system at the project site In general the following
provides a brief outline of the installation process for the anchored wire mesh system
