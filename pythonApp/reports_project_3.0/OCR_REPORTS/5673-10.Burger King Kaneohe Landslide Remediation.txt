performed for the Landslide Remediation Adjacent to the Burger King Kaneohe project
in Kaneohe District on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project location and general
vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our

intended for design and construction of segmental retaining walls excavation and
backfill placement only The findings and recommendations presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island After a
long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion incised deep valleys into the
Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder
and tuff cone formations These late series eruptions are referred to as the Honolulu
Volcanic Series The Koolau Volcano reached the end of its main shield building phase
about 2 million years ago
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stand
When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that
the sea levels rose to above its present level
Glacio eustatic sea level fluctuations affected the processes of erosion and
deposition When the sea level was low the erosional base level was correspondingly
lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level When the sea
level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments accumulated at
higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and transport downslope
toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these materials
reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become dominant
and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium

In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the
stream flows transmit large volumes of water for very short durations Because of this
the transport of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load
consists of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When
the erosional base levels change these sediment loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes
begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the
material Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the
coarse grained portion of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or
conglomerate Simultaneously erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors
and upstream in headwaters This continued erosion generates material that is
transported downslope covering the older alluvial deposits Depending on the local base
level and rate of transport these newer sediments are generally transient in terms of
geologic time In addition their consistency and density are generally less than those of
the older partially consolidated deposits
The lower portion of the project site is at the northeastern margin of a broad
alluvial basin encompassed by multiple stream channels and underlain by thick deposits
of older alluvium Some highly weathered basaltic rock materials representing younger
volcanic deposits of the Honolulu Volcanic Series may be encountered in close
proximity to the upper portion of the project site

Retaining Walls
In general the segmental retaining wall system is a composite wall system
which utilizes high density polyethylene or other reinforcing elements to reinforce the
backfill zone and improve the shear strength of the reinforced soil zone This composite
system essentially forms a gravity wall structure with an ability to tolerate significant
total and differential settlements In addition segmental retaining walls are also
desirable due to the flexibility of the wall ease of construction high load carrying
capacity and economy Because of the above the installation of a segmental retaining
wall system for the stabilization of failed slope is recommended
Based on the current grading concept the construction of the segmental
retaining walls will require excavating about 35 to 40 feet into the existing slope toe to
create a reinforced soil zone and filling about 25 to 30 feet high to tie into the existing
ground at the top of the slope

Construction Considerations
As previously mentioned construction of the segmental retaining walls will require
excavating up to 40 feet into the existing slope toe to provide a sufficient reinforced
soil zone Due to constraints of the available narrow construction parcel we
envision that a near vertical temporary cut slope with an inclination of up to 1H 8V
would likely be constructed
Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal to
the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential
for excessive ground movement We envision that construction access may be
either from the top of the slope or from the bottom of the slope provided a
construction easement is permitted from the private owner
We have performed slope stability analyses using undrained shear strength to
model the existing stiff residual and saprolite materials as retaining and foundation
soils The factor of safety for 40 foot high temporary cut slope with an inclination of
about 1H 8V is at least 1 5
It should be noted that the temporary cut slope was modeled to simulate undrained
shear condition Therefore it is critical to prevent the moisture loss and shrinkage
cracking of the retaining soils during wet and dry weather cycles We recommend
applying shotcrete on the face of the temporary cut slope to reduce the risk of
potential moisture loss and shrinkage cracking As a precaution the contractor
should establish survey points along the top of the slope to monitor the ground
movement
As previously mentioned we encountered seepage water in the boring To reduce
hydrostatic pressure built up behind the shotcrete we also recommend placing a

recommended to accommodate these possible extra costs

APPENDIX A

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling one boring designated as Boring No 1 using a truck mounted drill rig three
borings designated as Boring No 2 through 4 using portable hand auger and two
probes designated as P 1 and P 2 using Dynamic Cone Penetrometer DCP The
borings generally extended to the maximum depth drilled of about 45 feet below the
existing ground surface and were drilled at the approximate locations shown on the Site
Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Portable Dynamic Cone Penetrometer DCP was used for probing by driving an
8 mm diameter 60 steel cone tip with a 8 kg hammer falling 575 mm in vertical height
The blow counts were recorded per every or near 40 mm penetration and converted to
standard penetration resistance using correlation between
Penetration Index Pl and SPT developed by Sowers and Hedges and later Livneh
and Ishai Both PI and SPT blow counts are shown on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Two 1 inch Ring Swell tests were performed on samples of the remolded soils to
evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The test results are presented
on Plate B 1
Three Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial compression TXUU tests
ASTM D 2850 were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained
shear strength of the silty and clayey soils encountered The approximate in situ
effective overburden pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the
relatively undisturbed soil sample The test results and the stress strain curves are
presented on Plates B 2 through B 4
