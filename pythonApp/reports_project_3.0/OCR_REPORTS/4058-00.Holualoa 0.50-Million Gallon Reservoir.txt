REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is situated on the southwestern flank of Hualalai Mountain which
makes up the west central part of the island and northwest of Mauna Loa The lava
formation encountered at the site appears to consist of pahoehoe flow which spread and
ponded as it approached the ocean Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or
billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly
encountered in pahoehoe lavas Cavities are formed when the lava is still in a molten state
and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such as blisters and
pockets Lava tubes are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a
hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow
Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted through
the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and along
clinker layers The permeability of the fractures and cavities is high therefore they serve as
the major water transmission features

Reservoir Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe that
the on site basalt rock formation would provide suitable foundation support for the
proposed reservoir structure We anticipate that the basalt rock formation at the foundation
elevation will likely vary from the severely fractured basait scoria basalt formation to the
slightly fractured basalt formation To provide more uniform support for the shallow
foundation system we recommend that the subsurface materials below the bottom of the
tank floor and foundations be over excavated and be replaced with at least 12 inches of
base course The 12 inches of compacted base course should extend a minimum of 2 feet

laterally beyond the limits of the tank structure The base course should consist of crushed
basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91
For design of foundations bearing on the 12 inch base course placed over basalt
rock formation we recommend that an allowable bearing pressure of up to 6 000 pounds
per square foot psf be used This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces For design of the tank floor a modulus of subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per
square inch per inch of deflection pci may be used for the compacted base course
Foundations and thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be
embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade If the
foundations are located adjacent to a slope sloping down away from the foundations the
foundations should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum 8 foot setback
distance measured horizontally from the outside edge of the foundations to the slope face

foundations should extend to a depth below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V

Foundation Probing
Based on our site reconnaissance and previous experience in the vicinity cavities
and or voids may be present in the basaltic lava flows encountered at the project site To
reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of the cavities
below foundations we recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting at the
foundation locations be conducted during construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that probes be drilled at each interior
foundation location and at 10 foot on center for the perimeter wall foundation The probe
holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below the
planned bottom of foundation elevation If cavities and or voids are encountered or
suspected during the probing operation additional probes should be drilled at closer spacing
to help delineate the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes and
cavities discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected at low to moderate
pressures A typical sand cement grout should have a slump of about 6 to 9 inches

basalt formations We anticipate that the majority of the clayey silt soils will be removed
after grading to the design finished subgrade elevations f soft and or loose materials are
encountered at the pavement subgrades over excavation may be necessary to remove
the soft and or loose materials The over excavation should be backfilled with compacted
structural fill As an alternative the exposed clayey silt soils may be over excavated by
6 inches and replaced with 6 inches of compacted select borrow subbase The select
borrow subbase should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
The subgrade soil under the pavement areas should be scarified to a minimum depth
of 8 inches where possible moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction CBR tests and or field observations
should be performed on the actual subgrade soil samples during construction to confirm that
the above design sections are adequate The base course should consist of crushed basalt
aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction The recommended
section considers only light traffic conditions and assumes that good drainage will be
provided adjacent to paved areas
Reconstructed Pavement Design
As previously mentioned the pavement for the existing road leading to the reservoir
site is in relatively poor condition Therefore it is desired to reconstruct or rehabilitate the
pavements in the vicinity of the new reservoir site For long term performance it is
recommended that the pavements be reconstructed in accordance with the

recommendations presented in the New Pavement Design section of this report
However we understand that it is desired to rehabilitate the existing pavements without
complete removal and replacement with a new pavement section
It is assumed that the existing roadway pavement consists of about 2 inches of
asphalt concrete over at least 6 to 8 inches of base and subbase course materials
combined Based on the desire to rehabilitate the existing pavements for generally light
traffic conditions we recommend that the existing asphalt concrete pavement be removed
to expose the existing granular base material The exposed granular base material should
then be scarified to a depth of about 6 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content and recompacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction The
finished surface of the recompacted base should be firm and unyielding when proof rolled
by a 10 ton smooth drum vibratory roller A 2 inch thick layer of new asphalt concrete
should then be placed on top of the recompacted base material
Water Lines
We envision that new water lines will be required for the development of this project
It is anticipated that most of the trenches for the waiter lines will be excavated in the medium
hard scoria basalt formation to hard basalt rock For water lines located outside the
reservoir limits granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 1 inch minus granular fill materials
or base course is recommended below the pipes The 1 inch minus granular fill materials
should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide
adequate support around the pipes The 1 inch minus granular fill materials used for bedding
and initial backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in
level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and mechanically compacted to at least
90 percent relative compaction
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of well graded materials less than
6 inches in maximum dimension structural fill material The backfill should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in about 12 inch level loose lifts

W O 4058 00 Page 19
and mechanically compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Where trenches are
located in paved areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade
should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
For water lines located below the footprint of the reservoir tank the water line trenches
should be backfilled with concrete or lean concrete up to the bottom of the 42 inch thick
base course layer The 12 inch base course layer recommended below the tank floor and
foundations should be placed above the concrete to provide more uniform support for the
tank foundations The concrete around the water lines below the reservoir tank structure
should be placed neat against the excavation In addition the concrete backfill around the
water line should extend laterally beyond the perimeter of the tank a minimum of 3 feet
Since basaltic rock formation was encountered at the surface the trench work for the
water line installation will likely involve excavation in the basaltic rock formation and may
require some use of the hoeram or chipping Care must be exercised by the contractor to
avoid over ripping especially within the reservoir tank footprint which would disrupt the
structure of the rock formation resulting in a potential loss of bearing strength for foundations
in its vicinity As previously indicated the trench excavations within the tank structure
footprint should be backfilled with concrete or lean concrete placed neat against the sides
of the trench excavation up to the bottom of the 12 inch thick base course layer below the
tank

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 to depths of about 23 to 33 feet below
the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped
with continuous solid stem augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained by using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in

fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
