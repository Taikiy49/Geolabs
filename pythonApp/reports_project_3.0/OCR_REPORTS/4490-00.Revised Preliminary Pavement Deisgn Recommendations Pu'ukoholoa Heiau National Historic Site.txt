Design Subgrade Conditions
Based on our visual observations of the exposed subsurface conditions from the
on going construction work at the project site we anticipate the pavement subgrade soils
will likely consist of silty cobble and boulder fill materials overlying in situ basalt rock
formation Based on our experience with similar subgrade materials we believe that the
granular fill materials and in situ basalt rock formation should exhibit a Kentucky California
Bearing Ratio CBR value equivalent to that of an aggregate subbase material Therefore
a design Resistance R value of 60 was adopted in our pavement analyses for the
subgrade materials
Although the 1973 Soil Conservation Service Soil Survey describes the soils in the
project area as silt loam and loam overlying Pahoehoe rock we understand that the

Based on our site reconnaissance and our experience in the project area we
anticipate that the subgrade material below the new pavements should consist of relatively
permeable granular fill materials We believe that groundwater or surface runoff trapped
below the pavement section should dissipate fairly easily through the granular subgrade
material Therefore it is our opinion that the incorporation of a permeable base course
would not be necessary for the proposed roadway improvement project Due to the heavy
traffic flow in the project area we envision that the progress of the roadway construction
would be of critical concern In order to reduce the potential for disturbance to vehicular
traffic asphalt concrete base ACB is generally used as the base material to facilitate the
pavement construction work to be performed in a timely manner Therefore the use of
aggregate base course was not considered in our pavement design analyses

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program for this portion of the project consisted of drilling and
sampling one boring designated as Boring No 5 extending to a depth of about 5 feet below
the existing ground surface In addition the subsurface information obtained from the drilled
boring Boring No 4 performed for the intersection improvement project was also used in our
pavement analyses The approximate locations of these drilled borings are shown on the Site

In general our field exploration disclosed that the project area is generally underlain
by silty sands and gravel fill materials overlying relatively competent in situ basalt rock
formation Evidence of shallow or near surface voids was not observed during our site
reconnaissance In addition our field exploration at the boring locations did not encounter
voids and or cavities throughout the depths explored

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The design procedures used in determining the new flexible pavement section follow
those described in Chapter 3 of the Draft Pavement Design Manual prepared by the State
of Hawaii Department of Transportation The design procedures are based on the
Hveem Stabilometer method developed and used by the California Department of
Transportation
Design Traffic Loading Conditions
Detailed information pertaining to the vehicle type and operation frequency were not
provided for our engineering analyses Therefore we have assumed 100 percent truck
traffic with a 70 and 30 percent distribution of 2 and 3 axle trucks respectively In addition
we have assumed that the pavements will be subjected to five passes per direction of the
truck traffic on a daily basis over a design life of 20 years
New Flexible Pavement Design
Our preliminary pavement design assumed that the pavement subgrade soils will
consist of the recompacted near surface granular soils described in the logs of borings If
site grading exposes soils other than those assumed Geolabs should be contacted to
perform additional tests to confirm or revise the recommended pavement section for actual
field conditions If imported fills is required during the pavement construction the subgrade
on which the pavement structural section is to be placed will need to have a R value of 60

or more and should have a maximum swell value of 1 percent or less Based on the above
assumptions and a properly prepared subgrade we recommend that the following new

New Flexible Pavements
2 5 Inch Asphalt Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
8 5 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on a Compacted Granular Subgrade
The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to not less
than 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density
of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined
in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 AASHTO T 180 Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
The aggregate base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate and should
conform to Section 703 06 of the Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road Bridge and
Public Works Construction 1994 Aggregate base course should be moisture conditioned
to near the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction We
recommend that CBR and field density tests be performed on the actual pavement
subgrade materials encountered during construction to confirm the adequacy of the
recommended pavement section
Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the aggregate base course and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement
surface should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface
water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water ponding should
not be allowed on the site during or after construction Where landscaping is planned
adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that a subdrain system be constructed to
collect excessive water from landscaping irrigation and to reduce the potential for migration
of landscape water into the pavement section The recommended pavement section
assume that good drainage will be provided for the paved areas

INTRODUCTION
This pavement justification report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering
analyses for the design of new roadway pavements and rehabilitation of the existing
pavements for the proposed National Historic Site Spencer Beach Road
and Park Maintenance Road Intersection Improvements project in the District of South
Kohala on the Island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for the design and
rehabilitation of pavements only The findings and recommendations presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
Based on the information provided it is desired to improve two intersections along a
portion of Kawaihae Road State Route No 270 near the intersection with Queen
Kaahumanu Highway in the District of South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii The first
intersection is the entrance to National Historic Site which is also known
as the Spencer Beach Road Intersection The second intersection is the Park Maintenance
Road Intersection Both intersections are shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 1 and 2 2
In general the proposed roadway improvement project will be performed from about
to along the roadway baseline The improvement work may include
but is not limited to the following construction of left turn deceleration and storage lanes
reconstruction of existing shoulders to include bike lanes intersection lighting utility
adjustments signage and striping roadway resurfacing commercial driveway construction
and slope cut back to improve site distance Due to the availability of funding the proposed
roadway improvement work may be divided into three phases

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes which include the following Kohala which has long been extinct Mauna Kea
which has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and
Mauna Loa and Kilauea which are still active
GEOLABS INC
W O 4490 00 Page 5
The project site is underlain by basaltic lava flows of the Hamakua Volcanic Series of
Mauna Kea which were deposited during the Pleistocene Epoch Stearns and Macdonald
1946 The Hamakua series is typically composed of basaltic lava flows which are mantled
by a relatively thin layer of palagonitized ash locally referred to as Pahala Ash The ash soil
at the project site differs from Pahala Ash found in other portions of the Island of Hawaii in
that the in situ moisture content is generally lower and the soil exists in a dry friable state
The soils at the site have been subjected to wind erosion which has resulted in the
formation of a desert pavement on the surface of the site This desert pavement consists
of a surface layer of cobbles and gravel which were left behind as the finer ash was blown
away In the area of the project site the lava flows are in the a a form The a a flow is
generally characterized by a rough spiny or rubbly surface of fragmental clinker underlain
by a dense core of massive basalt which frequently has a thinner less continuous layer of
clinker below the massive core

Design Subgrade Conditions
Our field exploration program along the proposed roadway intersection improvement
project consisted of drilling and sampling four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through
4 extending to depths of about 1 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate locations of the drilled borings are shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 1 and 2 2
Based on our field exploration and visual observations of the exposed subsurface
conditions from previous construction work at the project site we anticipate that the
pavement subgrade soils will likely consist of silty sands and gravel fill materials overlying
relatively competent in situ basalt rock formation Evidence of shallow or near surface voids
was not observed during our site reconnaissance In addition our field exploration at the
boring locations did not encounter voids and or cavities throughout the depths explored
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field
exploration However groundwater levels are expected to vary with seasonal rainfall and
time of year Detailed descriptions of the field exploration methodology are presented in
Appendix A of this report Descriptions and graphic representation of the soils encountered
in the drilled borings are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Based on our field exploration and the current design concept we anticipate the
pavement subgrade soils will likely consist of compacted 3 inch minus granular fill materials
imported and or in situ basalt rock formation for the proposed intersection improvement
project Based on our experience with similar subgrade materials we believe that the 3 inch
minus granular fill materials and the in situ basalt rock formation would exhibit a
Resistance R value equivalent to that of an aggregate subbase material Therefore a
design R value of 60 was adopted in our pavement analyses for the subgrade materials
GEOLABS INC
W O 4490 00 Page 8
Although the 1973 Soil Conservation Service Soil Survey describes the soils in the
project area as silt loam and loam overlying pahoehoe rock we understand that the
majority of these surface silt loam and loam soils have been removed during the earthwork
construction of the existing roadway The underlying pahoehoe rock condition used in our
pavement analyses was confirmed by our field exploration and site reconnaissance
conducted at the project site However we recommend that provisions be made in the
contract documents that in the event pockets of silt loam and loam soils are encountered
at the pavement subgrade during construction these soils should be removed and replaced
with compacted select granular material with a minimum R value of 60 Contract
documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and
compacted fill placement as a contingent item to account for the potential presence of silt loam
and loam soils at the pavement subgrade level

As discussed later in the Rehabilitation of Existing Pavements section of this report
an AC overlay on the order of about 2 5 inches thick will be required for the rehabilitation of
the existing pavements located adjacent to the new pavements planned for the proposed
roadway intersection improvement project In order to provide a better finished product and
to aid in the construction and rehabilitation of the new and existing pavements it would be
desirable to perform the AC overlay in conjunction with the AC placement for the new
pavements Since there is a difference of 0 5 inches between the AC overlay thickness and
the AC thickness of the new pavements this would result in construction difficulties in placing
the remaining 0 5 inches of AC in the new pavement areas Since there is only a slight
GEOLABS INC

the AC thickness with a corresponding 0 5 inch increase in the ACB thickness wouid not

2 5 Inch Asphaltic Concrete

10 0 inch Total Pavement Thickness on a Compacted Granular Fill or
Basalt Rock Subgrade
As previously mentioned we envision that the new pavements will be constructed
either on 3 inch minus granular fill materials along fill areas or underlain by the in situ basalt
rock formation in cut areas It is our opinion that the 3 inch minus granular fill materials
should provide adequate drainage for the new pavements due to the generally permeable
nature and elevated grades For new pavements underlain by the in situ basalt rock formation
in cut areas we believe that the potential accumulation of water would not adversely affect the
performance of the subgrade materials Therefore it is our opinion that the inclusion of an
Untreated Permeable Base UTPB layer for subdrainage purposes would not be necessary
for the subject project
Due to the heavy traffic flow in the project area we envision that the progress of the
roadway construction would be of concern In order to reduce the potential for disturbance
to vehicular traffic asphalt concrete base ACB is generally used as the base material to
facilitate the pavement construction work to be performed in a timely manner Therefore
pavement sections incorporating the use of aggregate base course were not considered
in our pavement design analyses
Rehabilitation of Existing Pavements
As part of the project construction we understand that it is also desired to rehabilitate
the existing pavements without reconstruction In general the type and severity of the
distresses exhibited in the pavement areas would determine whether rehabilitation or
reconstruction of the existing pavements should be performed Existing pavements with
GEOLABS INC
W O 4490 00 Page 10
minor distresses of low severity are generally rehabilitated with an AC overlay with or
without cold planing
Based on our field observations the overall roadway in this area appears to be flat
with no rutting or depressions There is moderate severity alligator cracking at the
approach and tail ends of the bridge and other localized areas In addition bumps were
observed at the end of the bridges Patches were noted on the mauka side of the road
which may be attributed to the installation or repair of the existing waterline In summary
the existing pavements within the roadway improvement limits were observed to be in fair
to moderate condition with minor pavement distresses
For the AC overlay design the strength of the existing AC layer has been reduced
to that of asphalt concrete base ACB based on the fair to moderate conditions of the
existing pavements observed at the subject roadway Based on the results of the
AC overlay analyses it appears that an AC overlay on the order of about 2 5 inches thick
will be required under the anticipated future traffic conditions for the existing pavement
section based on a design life of 10 years Results of our overlay design are presented
on Plates B 6 and B 7 of this report
Subgrade Preparation
Based on the information provided we anticipate that the proposed roadway
intersection improvement project will involve site grading consisting of minimal fills of less
than one foot and cuts of up to about 3 feet thick In general the area within the contract
grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed at the on set of earthwork
Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed
of properly off site or in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the
excavated materials Soft and yielding areas such as the silt loam soils encountered during
clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill or future improvements should
be over excavated to expose firm or stiff material and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with well compacted granular fill The excavated soft and or organic soils should
be properly disposed of off site
GEOLABS INC
W O 4490 00 Page 11
As previously mentioned we understand that the majority of the surface silt loam
and loam soils have been removed during the earthwork construction of the existing
roadway In the event pockets of silt loam and loam soils are exposed at the pavement or
fill subgrade levels the subgrades should be over excavated to provide a minimum of 3 feet
of well compacted granular fill material below the bottom of the asphalt concrete base layer
Because silt loam materials may be encountered in scattered areas it is imperative that
contract documents include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and
compacted fill placement as a contingent item to account for potential presence of these
soils
If the subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade preparation the subgrade soils
may be stabilized by cement treatment to reduce the potential for pumping subgrade
conditions As a guide one sack of cement may be used for 25 square feet of subgrade
area Alternatively the subgrade may be proof rolled and a woven reinforcing fabric such
as Mirafi 500X or equivalent should be placed on the subgrade prior to placement of fill
material Alternately the pumping areas may be further over excavated to expose firm
natural material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted
granular fill Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or the subgrade should
be re scarified moisture conditioned and recompacted Saturation and subsequent yielding
of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over
excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted granular fill
After clearing and grubbing the future pavement areas should be excavated where
necessary to the pavement subgrade level i e bottom of the asphalt concrete base layer
In areas where the pavement subgrades expose dense clinker materials or basalt rock
formation the subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 12 inches where possible
and proof rolled with a minimum 15 ton vibratory roller for a minimum of eight passes to help
detect and collapse near surface cavities If the subgrade exposes dense basalt rock
formation the scarification process may be omitted however the basalt rock subgrade
should be proof rolled as recommended Loose areas or cavities disclosed during clearing
GEOLABS INC
W O Page 12
and proof rolling operations should be backfilled with well compacted granular fill materials
The proof rolling operation should also be performed at the finished subgrades in cut areas
The vibratory drum roller should travel at a speed of about 200 to 300 feet per minute Due
to the potential for presence of cavities lava tubes blisters pockets etc and soft and or wet
soils contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over
excavation and structural fill placement to account for variations in the over excavation and
backfill quantities
If needed a leveling course consisting of aggregate base course 1 inch minus may
be provided on the prepared pavement subgrade prior to the placement of the ACB The
leveling course should be proof rolled to a dense and unyielding condition prior to placement
of the ACB The ACB material should consist of asphalt treated basalt aggregate placed in
two layers of equal thickness and compacted to a density of at least 91 percent of the
maximum theoretical specific gravity determined in accordance with AASHTO T 209 or
ASTM D 2041
Fill Materials
Based on the existing topography and current design concept we believe that minimal
fills will be required for the project construction In general fills placed within the upper 3 feet
of the finished pavement grade should consist of well graded granular materials less than
3 inches in largest dimension structural fill The structural fill material should have sufficient
fine materials sands and gravel to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass
Soft and or wet soils encountered should not be used as fill materials and should be
properly disposed of off site Excavated clinker materials if encountered may be used as
a source of fill material or blended with basalt rock fragments to provide a relatively well
graded fill material Cut materials generated from excavations into the underlying basalt
formation may be used as general or structural fill materials provided that they are screened
of the over sized materials greater than 3 or 6 inches in largest dimension depending on
the usage and processed to meet the above gradation requirements
GEOLABS INC

Imported fill material if required should consist of non expansive granular material
such as crushed coral basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded from
coarse to fine with no particles greater than 3 inches in largest dimension The material
should have a Kentucky CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell value
of 1 percent or less Imported fill materials should be tested for conformance with these
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use

Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the aggregate subbase and or subgrade soils Considering the permeable
nature of the subgrade materials granular fill materials or basalt rock pavement drainage
for this project is less critical As a minimum the pavement surface should be sloped and
drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to
appropriate drainage structures Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site
during or after construction Where landscaping is planned adjacent to the pavement areas
we recommend that a subdrain system be constructed to collect excess water from
landscaping irrigation and to reduce migration of landscape water into the pavement section
Subdrains should be provided in areas where seepage is observed during and after
grading or as recommended by the Engineer during construction Subdrains should conform
to the requirements of Section 605 Underdrains of the State of Hawaii Standard
Specifications for Road Bridge and Public Works Construction 1994 HSS As a minimum
the subdrain pipes should consist of 6 inch diameter perforated pipes conforming to
Subsection 706 12 of the HSS with the perforations facing down The subdrains should be
daylighted into appropriate drainage structures for proper discharge

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions along the proposed roadway intersection improvement
project were explored by drilling and sampling four borings designated as Boring Nos 1
through 4 extending to depths of about 1 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface using
portable drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers The approximate
locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 1 and 2 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using a 140 pound hammer falling
