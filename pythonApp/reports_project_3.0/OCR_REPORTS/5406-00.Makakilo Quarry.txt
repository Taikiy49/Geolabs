PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project study area consists of a series of southeasterly facing hillslopes located
between the H 1 Highway and the Grace Pacific Makakilo Quarry on the Island of Oahu
Hawaii as shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 Based on the information
provided we understand that a rockfall incident occurred in December 2004 which
involved a large boulder that rolled from the hillside onto the southbound lanes of the
H 1 Highway The rockfall incident involved a vehicle accident on the freeway The
boulder reportedly came loose from the upper hillside during some work activity involving
the operation of a bulldozer on an existing trail located at the top of the slope above the
freeway
Based on the information provided a bulldozer was working to re establish the
existing access trail located along the southwestern rim of the quarry pit when several
boulders embedded in the near surface of the hillside were destabilized and rolled
downslope Apparently the caterpillar tracks of the bulldozer initiated the rockfall
movement One boulder measuring approximately 5 feet in diameter passed down the
gently to moderately inclined hillside surface and fell over the highway cut slope onto the
highway Two other boulders of about 3 to 4 feet in dimension rolled and came to rest on
the gently inclined portion of the hillside above the highway cut slope

PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering consultation was to conduct a
geological reconnaissance at the project site to develop a generalized field data set to
support engineering analysis of potential rockfall behavior for the formulation of a
preliminary rockfall hazard assessment with possible mitigation measures In order to
accomplish these objectives an evaluation program generally consisting of the following
tasks and work efforts was performed
1 Review of available in house soil and geologic information from within the
vicinity of the project site location
2 Conduct site reconnaissance visual observations and mapping of the
surface conditions at accessible locations of the hillslopes by geologists from
our firm
3 Geological and engineering analyses of the field data including some
preliminary potential rockfall behavior modeling using the computerized
Colorado Rockfall Simulation Program CRSP to develop a statistical
rockfall hazard assessment with possible mitigation measures that may be
appropriate for implementation
The CRSP is a widely used computer program based on mathematical
probability models and field rockfall experiments The CRSP is used to
predict the statistical distribution of rockfall behavior which is used as an
engineering reference tool for the design of various rockfall mitigation
schemes
4 Preparation of this letter report presenting our preliminary findings and
recommendations
5 _ Coordination of our work on the project by a project geologist from our firm
6 Quality assurance of our overall work on the project and client design team
consultation by a principal engineer from our firm
7 Miscellaneous work efforts such as drafting word processing clerical
support and reproductions

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The
younger shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in
age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became
extinct while Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below
Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed
a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau
The project site is situated on the southeast facing slopes of the Waianae Volcano
in the vicinity of several preserved volcanic vents which at one time had erupted cinders
spatter lavas and dense basaltic lava flows Based on our observation of the existing
conditions the project site is underlain by weathered basaltic lava flows typified by layers
of hard rock interbedded with medium hard clinker materials
In general the existing ground surface conditions consist of relatively thin silty and
clayey surface residual soils which grade with depth to hard basalt rock material Some
generally localized surface rock outcroppings were observed as widely scattered
exposures of moderately fractured dense basaltic rock protruding about 2 to 5 feet above
the surrounding ground surfaces Other more widespread areas of surface rock
outcropping were observed to consist of low relief flattened surface exposures protruding
less than about 1 foot above the surrounding terrain
GENERAL SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is bounded by the H 1 Highway toward the southeast and the
Grace Pacific Makakilo Quarry toward the northwest in the Makakilo area on the Island of
Oahu Hawaii as shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 The project study area
encompasses a series of southeasterly facing hillslopes ridges separated by topographic
basins valleys bordering the H 1 Highway The length of the study area which resides
between the operating Grace Pacific Makakilo Quarry and along the H 1 Freeway is
approximately 3 000 lineal feet long in the north to south direction and about 200 to
400 lineal feet wide in the east to west direction as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The project study area encompasses sloping terrain identified by Grace Pacific as a
buffer zone for the existing quarry operations which are mainly confined to the pit area
located on the northwestern side of the sloping terrain The sloping terrain borders the
southbound lanes of the H 1 Highway As mentioned previously the hillside study area
ranges between about 200 and 400 lineal feet in width between the H 1 Highway and the
existing quarry pit rim There is an existing rough graded access trail of about 20 feet wide
located at the top of the hillside along the pit rim as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 In
addition there are some paved quarry roadways that are separated from the study
hillslopes by roadside berm features of about 5 to 10 feet in height

SITE RECONNAISSANCE
A team of two geologists traversed the hillslopes located within the established
study area in an effort to identify and map the approximate position of surface boulders
and rock outcroppings located on the hillside The purpose of the mapping effort was to
obtain an overview of the general location size and spatial distribution of the surface
boulders and rock outcroppings existing on the hillside In addition the field
reconnaissance was performed to evaluate probable boulder rolling paths that could affect
the highway located downslope from the project study area The field information was
also used to support the computer simulation and statistical evaluation of potential rockfall
behavior at the site using the CRSP engineering analytical tool

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
As previously discussed a site reconnaissance was performed to visually identify
and locate potential rockfall hazards such as loose surface boulders and fractured rock
outcroppings residing within the project study area The site reconnaissance also allowed
our geologist to estimate probable rolling paths based on the existing site topography and
ground surface features identified in the field Following the site reconnaissance the field
information was input to the CRSP to validate the estimations made in the field
The CRSP is a computer program that is a widely accepted engineering tool used
by experienced personnel to estimate potential rockfall behavior by simulating rockfall
activity based on input parameters that are assigned on a site specific basis The input
parameters for this project were assigned based on the observations and measurements
collected in the field Basically the program provides a statistical evaluation of potential

rockfall behavior based on the typical hillslope topographic profile and other input
information such as rock size shape and parameters used to describe the ground surface
irregularities Information obtained from the CRSP analysis includes the predicted falling
rock velocity bounce height kinetic energy and roll out distance The output information
is useful to assist in the site specific design of various rockfall mitigation schemes such as
rockfall impact barriers or other rockfall containment systems
Low to Moderate Potential Rockfall Hazards
Based on our field exploration and CRSP analysis we identified a hillside region of
moderate potential rockfall hazard located within the project study area In general the
region consists of moderate relief fractured rock outcroppings and some surface boulders
residing on sloping terrain inclined at about The region is located on Ridge 2 in
relatively close proximity to the highway as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our assessment of greater risk for Ridge 2 is based on the observed location of the
potential rock source area situated on moderately inclined terrain with few existing natural
buffers such as flatter slopes or topographic barriers located below the rock source area
In addition our CRSP analysis indicates that about 5 of 4 and 5 foot dimension boulders
rolling from the rock source area along the axis of Ridge 2 could reach the highway
elevation
The greater potential rockfall hazard of the region resides along the nose axis of
Ridge 2 leading directly down toward the highway where flatter slopes or topographic
basins are not present downslope from the potential rockfall source area The side slopes
on either side of the ridge nose appear to direct potential rockfall trajectories toward the
adjacent basins therefore the potential rockfall hazard decreases away from the ridge
axis A cut slope bench of about 10 to 15 feet wide traverses along the existing highway
right of way boundary The potential rockfall source area resides just upslope from the
existing cut slope bench
Based on our evaluation we recommend that a section of rockfall impact barrier
fencing be constructed just upslope of the existing highway easement spanning across the
lower portion of Ridge 2 A minimum barrier length of about 400 lineal feet is
recommended to reduce the risk associated with the potential rockfall hazards at Ridge 2
The approximate location of the recommended rockfall impact barrier spanning across
Ridge 2 is shown on the Rockfall Mitigation Plan Plate 5
Low Potential Rockfall Hazards
Based on our site reconnaissance and modeling of potential rockfall behavior using
the CRSP analytical tool surface boulders and rock outcroppings residing within the
sloping areas encompassed by Ridges 5 and 6 and the bordering side slopes of
Valleys 1 through 5 appear to be in generally stable ground settings located within
favorable topographic terrain which reduce the potential for possible rockfall hazards to
reach the highway elevation The anticipated low level hazard potential of these hillslopes

results from a combination of factors including the presence of flatter upslope inclinations
limited rock sources present on the locally steeper slopes and the presence of downslope
topographic basins which appear to be capable of collecting most potential falling rocks
emanating from the steeper upslope terrain Although the potential rockfall hazard is
considered to be lower for the hillside areas mentioned previously the potential for falling
rock to occur cannot be completely discounted since some rock source is present on the
sloping hillside terrain
Based on our CRSP analyses potential rockfall behavior models were run using
1 000 source boulders ranging from 2 to 5 feet in dimension rolling from the top of the
slope under the existing topographic conditions encountered at various positions of the
site Our CRSP analysis indicates that over 95 of simulated rockfall 5 foot dimension
boulders may stop on the hillslopes located above the highway Furthermore the
analysis indicates that the topographic basins bordering the ridge side slopes should
contain about 99 of simulated rockfall should the simulated rocks roll into the basin
areas from the adjacent side slopes Our CRSP analysis also indicates that over 99 of
potential rockfall involving boulders of about 4 feet or less in dimension would stop on the
flatter slope inclinations located above the highway cut slopes
With respect to the December 2004 rockfall incident one of the three boulders that
rolled from the top of the slope as a result of contact with a large bulldozer reached the
highway travel lanes The boulder that reached the highway was about 5 feet in
dimension while the other two boulders were of somewhat smaller dimension Thus the
observed rockfall behavior appears to validate the results obtained from our CRSP rockfall
behavior modeling The Cross Section B B presented on Plate 4 depicts the field
conditions where the rockfall incident had occurred
Based on our evaluation the potential for rockfall to reach the highway from most
areas of Ridges 5 and 6 and the side slopes of Valleys 1 through 5 is generally
considered to be relatively low However it must be emphasized that the simulation and
prediction of actual rockfall travel paths and rockfall rolling behaviors is based on the
available information obtained from visual observations of the typical conditions
encountered at the project site Furthermore the initiation of rockfall movement cannot be
accurately predicted because the movement initiation may be dependent on external
factors such as ground disturbance heavy surface water runoff and earthquake vibration
which cannot be accurately predicted Thus we are unable to conclude that rockfall
activity would absolutely not affect the highway for the primary reason that surface
boulders and rock outcroppings remain on the hillside

additional field reconnaissance be performed to identify a feasible alignment for the barrier
construction In addition we recommend that additional computer rockfall simulation be
performed in an effort to determine the rockfall barrier design energy Capacity and height
Finally because the barrier alignment will traverse undulating sloping ground surfaces we
recommend obtaining a detailed topographic survey of the proposed alignment to assist in
determining the barrier post layout and ring net dimensions to accommodate the variable
terrain conditions
Rockfall impact barriers are commonly used as an effective rockfall barrier system
constructed on hillsides where protection of downslope areas from falling rock is desired
The rockfall impact barrier is a specialized barrier fence designed to flex and absorb the
design impact energy of falling rock The barrier system essentially consists of a series of
steel posts linked by wire rope cables with cable or steel ring nets spanning between the
posts Rockfall impact barriers of varying capacity and height are fabricated based on
anticipated design loads rockfall impact energy and rock bounce heights Based on our
preliminary evaluation a medium capacity system with a minimum height of about 8 feet
may be an appropriate system for this project site
