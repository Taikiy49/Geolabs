INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our preliminary geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the proposed Waikele Family Housing Project located at the
Naval Magazine Lualualei Waikele Branch in Waikele Oahu Hawaii The general location
and vicinity of the site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and preliminary geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses The preliminary recommendations presented herein are intended for design
input of site slope stability site grading foundations pavements and infrastructure work
and may not be appropriate to form the basis for the preparation of construction cost
estimates nor for construction bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed project is located within the Naval Magazine Lualualei Waikele
Branch on the island of Oahu Hawaii It is proposed to provide 130 or more new units
of family housing consisting of townhouse type structures that could range up to three
stories high with associated access roads parking lots and utilities Conceptual design
drawings and grading plans were not available at the time this report was prepared

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The project site is located on a gently sloping plateau area formed by the extrusion
of Koolau basalts from vents along the Koolau Range The Koolau Volcanics flowed in
a southerly direction until banking against the older previously erupted Waianae Volcanics
of the Waianae Range forming the land mass which now encompasses the areas of Kunia
and Waikele
A period of deep erosion and down cutting by several major drainageways
originating in the Koolau Range formed the deep drainage gullies of Kipapa and Waikele
gulches Exposures created by the erosion of Kipapa Gulch provide a clear view of the
geologic strata and the eruptive history of the area Clearly visible within the canyon walls
are the surficial weathered soil products directiy overlying the massive basalt flows of the
Koolau Volcanic Series

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsoil conditions at the subject site were explored by drilling and sampling
seven borings to depths of about 21 to 39 feet below the ground surface In addition 23
test pits were excavated to depths of about 7 feet below the existing ground surface for
our preliminary exploration The approximate locations of the borings and test pits are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on the borings and test pits completed for the field exploration at the site
we generally encountered residual soils and saprolite consisting of reddish brown clayey
silts from the ground surface to depths of about 15 to 20 feet Basalt rock formation was
encountered below the saprolite to the maximum depth explored of about 39 feet The
consistency of the residual soils ranged from very stiff to hard and should be capable of
supporting a shallow foundation system for the proposed structures The residual soils
grade to saprolite partially to highly weathered rock with increasing depth
The near surface soils appear to be dry and grades to damp with increasing depth
Based on our laboratory tests the near surface soils appear to have low shrink swell
potential when subjected to changes in the moisture content
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings drilled at the time of our field
exploration However it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change
due to rainfall time of year and tidal fluctuations

DISCUSSION AND PRELIMINARY RECOMMENDATIONS
The recommendations provided in this report are preliminary in nature with the
intent of providing general geotechnical geological information for the proposed housing
development A design level geotechnical engineering exploration is considered
necessary to develop finalized foundation and grading recommendations The design
level exploration should consist of additional test borings and laboratory testing in order
to provide design level recommendations and should be performed following the
development of more specific information as to the site grading and type of structures
In general our field exploration indicates that the proposed project site is underlain
by a layer of very stiff to hard clayey residual soil to depths of about 7 to 18 feet below
the existing ground surface The preliminary geotechnical considerations for development
of the site are the stability of the slope along the margin of the bluff and prudent set back
limits for structures constructed near the bluff
Laboratory tests performed on the near surface clayey soils indicate that the
materials generally have a low shrink swell potential when subjected to fluctuations in the
moisture content Based on our field exploration and laboratory testing we believe that
a shallow foundation system bearing on the very stiff to hard residual soils and or on
compacted engineered fill may be used to support the proposed building structures
Detailed discussions and preliminary recommendations for foundation design site
grading pavements and other geotechnical aspects of the project are presented in the
following sections of this report

Slope Stability
Based upon the subsurface conditions encountered at the site and slope stability
analyses we believe that the existing slopes forming the bluff above Kipapa Gulch appear
to be relatively stable and could support the proposed housing structures and recreational
facilities provided that a prudent set back distance is maintained between proposed
structures and the top of slope Our slope stability analyses were based on the existing
slope configurations with minimal new fills
The test pits excavated along the margin of the bluff revealed generally competent
subsoils except in localized areas where thin fills up to 1 foot thick appeared to have
been placed along the margin of the slope near Test Pit Nos 12 and 13 Test Pit No 4
excavated adjacent to a buried drain pipe crossing beneath the existing road
encountered poor pipe backfill material consisting of loose coral gravel and cobbles It
is recommended that this loose pipe backfill material be removed and replaced with
compacted engineered fill as outlined in the Site Grading subsection of this report
For normal on grade construction of improvements we recommend that a
minimum horizontal set back distance of 5 feet from the top of slope be maintained for
structures planned near the edge of the existing slopes with slope inclinations of two
horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter For the slopes along the bluff which are
generally steeper than 2H 1V a set back distance of at least 10 feet should be
considered Structures which cantilever over the minimum set back distance may be
considered however the foundations will require deeper embedment depths to provide
adequate lateral stability
During site grading soft or loose soils encountered along the bluff margin should
be removed to expose the firm residual soils and backfilled with compacted engineered
fill Surface runoff water should be diverted away from the bluff margin to reduce the
GEOLABS HAWAIi
W O 3198 00 Page 11
potential for slope erosion New fill slopes to be located near the existing bluff will need
to be evaluated by a geotechnical engineer for stability and erosional concerns
The presence of underground storage tunnels which extend beneath portions of
the proposed development poses another geotechnical concern Subsidence of the
ground surface and the proposed development overlying these tunnels may occur in the
event that these tunnels were to collapse or partial collapse These tunnels are used
for the storage of military equipment and extend up to 300 feet in length The tunnels
were constructed in basalt rock formation with approximately 100 feet of overburden
material above the tunnel roof Based upon our visual observations of the interior of
several tunnels the tunnels appear to be in relatively good condition with little apparent
signs of distress Therefore based on our observations it appears that the likelihood of
tunnel collapse and ground subsidence is considered to be low
Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our exploration we believe that
a shallow foundation system may be used to support the generally light structural loads
generated by the proposed townhouse type structures Spread footings bearing on the
native residual soils and or on compacted engineered fill may be used to support the
proposed building structures
Allowable bearing pressures on the order of 3 000 to 3 500 pounds per square foot
p s f may be used for preliminary design of spread footings bearing on the stiff residual
soils and or on compacted engineered fill needed to raise the grades The allowable
bearing pressures are for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 4 for
transient loads such as wind and or seismic forces Typically footings should be
embedded a minimum of 18 to 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade for
footings constructed on level ground areas For footings constructed on sloping ground
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3198 00 Page 12
the footings should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal set back
distance of 8 feet measured from the edge of the footing to the face of the slope
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the spread footing and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings
A coefficient of friction of 0 35 to 0 4 may be used between the base of the footings and
the supporting residual soils Resistance due to passive pressure may be calculated
using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f
for footings embedded in the residual soils and or engineered fill These values assume
that the concrete for footings is placed directly against the footing excavations Unless
covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below
finished grade should be neglected
Where footings will be located adjacent to below grade structures or utilities the
footings should extend to a depth below an imaginary 45 degree plane projected upward
from the bottom of the below grade structure or utility line trench This requirement is
necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural
loads and to reduce the potential of foundation settlement
It is important that a representative of Geolabs Hawaii observe the footing
excavations to evaluate the competency of the bearing materials and the embedment
depths If unsuitable materials are encountered in the footing excavations these materials
should be over excavated to firm soils and be replaced with compacted engineered fill
Slabs On Grade
We anticipate concrete slabs on grade will be used for garage floors and other
structures within the proposed housing development We envision that the slabs on
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3198 00 Page 13
grade will be supported on the existing near surface soils and or on non expansive fills
needed to raise the existing grades where applicable Based on the laboratory results
the near surface residual soils encountered at the site consist of clayey silts which exhibit
low expansion potential Based on this concrete slabs on grade may be constructed on
grade with proper subgrade preparation The subgrades for slabs on grades should be
scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above
the optimum moisture and compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
For more uniform support of the slabs we recommend that concrete slabs on
grade be supported on a minimum 4 inch cushion fill The cushion fill layer should
consist of open graded gravels such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C33 No 67
gradation which will also serve as a capillary moisture break The slab cushion fill layer
should be placed as soon as possible after the scarification and recompaction of the
existing subgrade soils For slabs on grade with moisture sensitive surfacing we
recommend that an impermeable membrane be placed over the cushion fill to reduce
potential migration of moisture vapor through the concrete slab In order to promote
uniform curing of the slab concrete and to provide protection of the vapor membrane we
recommend that a 2 inch layer of moist fine sand be placed above the membrane prior
to placing the slab concrete The sand should be moistened slightly prior to placing
concrete
Where the slab will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as the driveways and
garage slabs we recommend that the slabs be supported on a 6 inch layer of base
course compacted to 95 percent of its maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test

The base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate
Retaining Wails
Because of the relatively steep slopes at the site construction of fill slopes will likely
extend beyond the property limits As a result retaining walls may be necessary to
support any new fills planned along the slope margins Retaining wall structures may also
be required for grade separation purposes Where needed we envision that low retaining
walls on the order of 10 feet or less may be proposed Preliminary design of foundations
for retaining walls should be in accordance with the recommendations presented in the
Foundations section of this report
All retaining structures should be designed to resist the lateral earth pressures due
to adjacent soils and surcharge effects The recommended active lateral earth pressure
for design of cantilever walls expressed in equivalent fluid pressure is 35 p c f for an
assumed level backfill Generally tops of walls which are free to deflect beyond about
0 5 percent of the wall height may be designed for the active condition
The value provided above is for on site soils or imported granular soils Backfills
behind retaining walls should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to between 90 and 95 percent relative
compaction Over compaction of the wall backfill should be avoided The top 2 feet of
backfill should consist of non expansive impervious material to reduce water infiltration
behind the walls This lateral earth pressure does not include hydrostatic pressures that
might be caused by groundwater trapped behind the walls
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 foot to 2 foot wide zone of
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3198 00 Page 15
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately adjacent to the wall with a perforated pipe perforations down at the base
of the wail discharging to a discharge facility A filter fabric such as Mirafi 140N or
equivalent should be placed between the drainage material and the compacted backfill
material As an alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or
EnkaDrain may be used instead of the drainage material with filter fabric The
prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated
pipe at the base of the wall
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges line loads and point loads within a
horizontal distance equal to the height of the wall should be considered in the design
For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the wall a rectangular
distribution with uniform pressure equal to 30 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure
acting on the entire height of the wall may be used in design of cantilever walls
Additional analyses during design may be needed to evaluate the surcharge effects of
point loads and line loads

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to reduce
potential contamination of the excavated materials The existing fills encountered
in the test pits should be completely removed and replaced with compacted
engineered fill
After clearing and grubbing the exposed subgrade should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 8 inches properly moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil ex
pressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined
in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the
water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
Any soft weak or yielding areas disclosed during clearing operations should be
over excavated to expose firm ground and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with engineered fill compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Prior to placement of the backfill the over excavated subgrade
should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least
2 percent above the optimum moisture content and compacted to at least 90
percent relative compaction The excavated soft and or organic soils should be
properly disposed of off site Contract documents should include additive and
deductive unit prices for over excavation and engineered fill construction to
account for variations in the over excavation quantities

Fill materials should be tested and approved for its intended use by Geolabs

and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent loss of strength of the near surface soils The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting
of the concrete to reduce water infiltration
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain
all surface run off away from the slab footing foundations and the edges of slopes

The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling seven
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 7 at the approximate locations shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2 The depths of our borings ranged from about 21 to 39 feet below
the existing ground surface The seven borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill
rig equipped with continuous flight augers and advancing rotary coring tools as
appropriate
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Engineer and Geologist who monitored the drilling
operations on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed
visually and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with
the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A of Appendix A Graphic
presentation of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
