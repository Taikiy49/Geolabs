SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling four borings within the 
planned development. Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered surficial 
fills overlying very stiff to hard silty and clayey alluvial soils extending to the maximum depth 
explored of about 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  
Our laboratory tests indicate that the near-surface clayey soils exhibit moderate to 
high shrink-swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents. 
Considering the lightly loaded structures planned for the subdivision development, we 
believe that structural distresses could occur to the new residential/commercial buildings 
and pavements due to the moderate/high shrink-swell potential of the on-site soils unless 
special subgrade preparations are implemented during mass grading construction. 
Therefore, special attention should be given to the site grading preparation and foundation 
design to reduce the risk of structural distresses due to the moderately expansive soil 
conditions. 
We recommend implementing the following site grading preparation procedures to 
create a reworked soil layer within the entire grading limits at the project site. 
1. 
Removal of 12 inches of the near-surface cultivated soils after the clearing 
and grubbing operation. These soils should be considered “unsuitable” 
because they contain an appreciable amount of organic materials. These soils 
should be disposed of properly off-site or used in landscaping areas, if 
appropriate. 
2. 
Over-excavate and remove additional 12 inches of soil and moisture-condition 
to between 2 and 4 percent above the optimum moisture content for use as 
general fill, provided the soils are free of vegetation, debris, deleterious 
material, and other unsuitable materials. 
3. 
Prior to replacing the moisture-conditioned soil as general fill, the bottom of 
the over-excavation should be scarified to a depth of 12 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to between 2 and 4 percent above the optimum moisture 
content, and recompacted to a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction. 
The intent of the above site preparation is to create a 2-foot thick reworked soil 
layer within the entire grading limits. The reworked soil serves to close up the shrinkage 
cracks observed throughout the site. It would reduce the future soil swelling/heaving 
potential by preparing the clayey soils with elevated moisture contents and reduced 
compaction requirements. In addition, it decreases the affinity of the clayey soil for water 
and creates a barrier to reduce water infiltration into the underlying dry clayey soil. It

SECTION 2. SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene 
to early Pleistocene in age. As the volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano ceased, lava 
flows from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad 
plateau now known as the Schofield Plateau. The project site is on the southeastern flank 
of the Waianae Volcano. 
Physical and chemical weathering, followed by erosion of the Schofield Plateau, 
generated sediments that were transported to the coast. In the project vicinity and to the 
south, these sediments have accumulated and are interbedded with marine sediments 
and coral/algal reef formations, forming a sedimentary wedge. The thickness of the 
sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H-1 Highway to 
over 1,000 feet at Ewa Beach. This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the 
confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu. 
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill 
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When 
the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the 
calcareous reef materials.

Existing Site Conditions

Shrinkage cracks have been known to occur in expansive soils in the Ewa/Kapolei 
area during prolonged dry weather conditions. Shrinkage cracks adjacent to the building 
foundation are potentially detrimental because water could readily infiltrate into the 
subsurface through the crack openings and soften the subsurface soils around the

3.1.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site to reduce 
the potential for contamination of the excavated materials. After the clearing and 
grubbing operation, the upper 12 inches of the cultivated soils below the existing 
ground surface should be removed and disposed of properly off-site (or used in 
landscaped areas, if appropriate) because they contain an appreciable amount of 
organic materials. The removal of the upper 12 inches of cultivated soils below the 
ground surface may be considered as part of the clearing and grubbing operations 
and may be classified as “unsuitable” materials that should not be used as fill for this 
project. 
After clearing and grubbing, and removal of the upper 12 inches of cultivated soils 
below the existing ground surface, the normally dry and friable soils should be 
over-excavated by at least another 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to about 2 to 
4 percent above the optimum moisture content, and replaced with general fill 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Prior to replacing the 
moisture-conditioned soil as fill, the bottom of the over-excavation should be scarified 
to a depth of at least 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to about 2 to 4 percent above 
the optimum moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 85 percent relative 
compaction. It should be noted that the intent of reducing soil compaction with 
elevated moisture contents is to reduce the future soil swelling/heaving potential. 
Details of the subgrade preparation recommendations are presented on Plate 3.

Due to the hard, dry and friable in-situ clayey soils, the materials generated from the 
over-excavation and scarification process and/or from cut areas would likely contain 
large clumps. To moisture-condition the soils effectively, the clayey clumps should 
be thoroughly broken down and mixed by compaction equipment, such as large 
sheepsfoot compactors and/or large dozers until a relatively uniform moisture 
condition is achieved. Contractors bidding on this project should anticipate additional 
processing of the on-site soils to break down the hard clayey clumps and achieve a 
relatively uniform moisture content. 
The 12 inches of over-excavated soils (not including the 12 inches of cultivated soils 
removed as part of the clearing and grubbing) may be reused as a source of general 
fill materials, provided they are free of vegetation, debris, deleterious material and 
other unsuitable material and are moisture-conditioned to about 2 to 4 percent above 
the optimum moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction. It should be noted that the 2-foot thick reworked soil layer should also 
be constructed below the finished subgrade and pad grade in cut areas, as shown 
on Plate 4. 
The intent of the above recommended site preparation procedures would serve to 
accomplish the following: 
1. 
Close up the existing shrinkage cracks observed and anticipated 
throughout the site. 
2. 
Reduce the future soil swelling/heaving potential by preparing the 
clayey soils with elevated moisture contents and reduced 
compaction requirements. 
3. 
Decrease the affinity of the dry clayey soil for water. 
4. 
Create a barrier to reduce water infiltration into the underlying dry 
clayey soil. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry

3.2 
Building Foundations 
Based on our field exploration, we believe the planned building structures may be 
supported on a shallow Wafflemat foundation system bearing on the 2-foot reworked soil 
layer, or general fills placed below the finished pad grade. The Wafflemat is a 
post-tensioned slab-on-grade foundation system. This proprietary system utilizes a series 
of recycled plastic boxes, “Waffleboxes”, which are placed with the open side down on 
the soil. The Waffleboxes are arranged to form deep ribs or footings throughout the floor 
system, creating a very stiff foundation. A post-tensioned concrete slab is monolithically 
poured over and between the boxes. The empty space beneath the boxes allows for soil 
expansion, reducing the uplift of the slab.

3.6.5 Site Preparation Below Pavement Areas 
The near-surface clayey soils at the project site are dry and friable and exhibit 
relatively moderate to high shrink/swell characteristics when subjected to 
fluctuations in the soil moisture contents. Prior to placing the 18-inch thick layer of 
non-expansive select granular fill, we recommend that the subgrade be properly 
scarified to a depth of at least 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to about 2 to 
4 percent above the optimum moisture content, and recompacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction. 
Where minor shrinkage cracks are observed after subgrade preparation, we 
recommend thoroughly moistening the soil to close the cracks prior to 
recompacting. Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due 
to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft 
areas and replacement with well-compacted fill.

3.6.6 Fill Materials Below Pavement Areas 
Select granular fill should consist of non-expansive granular material, such as 
crushed coralline and/or basaltic materials. The material should be well-graded 
from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in the largest dimension 
and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 
sieve. The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should 
have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with 
ASTM D1883. 
Aggregate base course and subbase course should meet the material 
requirements for Base Course and Subbase Course as specified in 
Sections 31 and 30, respectively, of the Standard Specifications for Public Works 
Construction, City & County of Honolulu, September 1986. Geolabs should test 
imported fill materials for conformance with these recommendations prior to 
delivery to the project site for the intended use. 
3.6.7 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
The non-expansive, select granular fill should be placed in level lifts of about 
8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, 
and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The compaction 
requirement for the final lift of the select granular fill should be increased to at least 
95 percent relative compaction. Aggregate base course and subbase course 
should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
As an additional check for stability and uniform compaction, we recommend 
proof-rolling the non-expansive, select granular fill material prior to placing the 
aggregate base course (for flexible pavement section) or the aggregate subbase 
course (for rigid pavement section) materials using a pneumatic-tired vehicle with

3.6.8 Pavement Drainage 
The recommended pavement sections assume that adequate drainage will be 
provided for the paved areas, which should be sloped and drainage gradients 
maintained to carry surface water off-site. Surface water ponding should not be 
allowed on-site during or after construction. When concrete curbs are used to 
isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas, we recommend 
extending the curbs a minimum of 2 inches into the soils below the aggregate base 
or subbase layer to reduce the potential for migration of excessive landscape water 
into the pavement section. Alternatively, a subdrain system could be constructed 
to collect the excess water from landscaping irrigation. For long-term performance, 
we recommend constructing a subdrain system adjacent to the paved/landscaped 
areas. 
3.7 
Corrosion Potential 
Three sets of laboratory corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum 
Resistivity (ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), 
were performed (by our office and TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc.) on selected soil 
samples obtained from our field exploration. The results of the corrosivity tests are 
presented in Appendix B. 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower 
the resistivity the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
SECTION 3. DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
W.O. 7885-50(A) 
GEOLABS, INC. 
Page 31 
Hawaii • California 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to the corrosion 
of metallic objects. 
On the basis of the laboratory resistivity and pH results, the subsurface soils at the 
project site have resistivity values ranging from approximately 1,000 to 2,800 ohm-cm 
and pH values of between 7.0 and 8.0 within the upper 5 feet of soils, corresponding to a 
corrosion rating of 1 to 2 (Extremely Corrosive to Very Corrosive) based on the guidelines 
provided by the City & County of Honolulu – Board of Water Supply. Therefore, we 
recommend properly designing near-surface metallic substructures (such as piping) for 
protection against the potential for corrosion. 
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent, in part, on the chloride content and sulfate content found in the 
soil. In general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm), 
sulfate content of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH greater than 5.0 may be considered 
“non-corrosive” to underground concrete pipelines and structures. 
Based on the relatively low values of chloride content and sulfate content tested 
on the in-situ materials, we believe that the near-surface soils at the project site may be 
considered “non-corrosive” and either Type I or Type II (Type I/II) cement may be used 
for the concrete in contact with the ground. It may be appropriate to consult with a 
professional corrosion engineer to review the test results and provide detailed 
recommendations for corrosion protection.

In general, we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) for support of the utility lines. The 
initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes should consist of free-draining 
backfills, such as open-graded gravel, to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from

adequate support for the sides of the pipes. The use of on-site clayey soils as backfill 
directly around the utility pipes is not recommended and should not be allowed. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the finished subgrade may consist of approved on-site soils or select granular fill material. 
The granular or on-site soil backfill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content or between 2 and 4 percent above the optimum moisture 
content, respectively, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for 
appreciable future ground subsidence. The finished subgrade of the trench backfill below 
the pavement grade should be compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction. 
For the deep manhole structures, we recommend the backfill compaction to be 
increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for 
appreciable future ground subsidence due to the stiffness difference between the 
concrete structure and soil backfill under continuous vehicular traffic dynamic movement. 
The limits of the increase compaction backfill around the concrete manhole structure may 
be extended to a horizontal distance beyond the edge of the manhole footprint with a ratio 
of the manhole depth.

3.10 Infiltration Tests 
We envision that the project may require on-site stormwater runoff management. 
We conducted falling head infiltration tests at two selected locations in vicinity of proposed 
detention basins at the site to evaluate the infiltration characteristics of the subsurface 
materials encountered. The tests were performed in general accordance with the 
procedures in Appendix D of the State of Maryland, Department of the Environment 
“Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I and II” (rev. 2009). These procedures are 
consistent with other state’s procedures and may generally be considered an industry 
standard. 
The field infiltration tests were performed by drilling boreholes utilizing a 4-inch 
solid-stem auger to the selected test depths of about 5 and 10 feet below the existing 
ground surface. Upon reaching the test depths, a 4-inch diameter PVC solid casing was 
set to the bottom of the drilled borehole to allow infiltration only through the soil exposed 
at the bottom of the borehole. Falling head infiltration tests were performed to determine 
the average infiltration rates of the underlying subsurface materials. Each test consisted 
of four trials of filling the casing with 24 inches of water and taking periodic readings until 
the water drained out or up to one hour. The infiltration rates are then calculated based 
on the results of the fourth and/or last trial for each test location. The calculated infiltration 
rates at each test location are summarized in Table 5.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
four borings, designated as Boring Nos. 201 through 204, extending to the maximum depth 
explored of about 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, two boreholes, 
designated as I-201 and I-202, was drilled to a depth of about 5 to 10 feet below the ground 
surface for the infiltration testing access. Two bulk samples of the near-surface soils, 
designated as Bulk-201 and Bulk-202, were obtained to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the near-surface soils. The approximate boring locations are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-6.  
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Seven Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations of the test 
results are provided on Plate B-1. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM C117 & C136) was performed on a selected soil 
sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. 
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate B-2. 
Six one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and/or 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the on-site soils. The test results 
are summarized on Plate B-3. 
Four One-Dimensional Swell or Settlement Potential tests (ASTM D4546 Method 
A) were performed to evaluate the swell and/or compressibility characteristics of the 
remolded soil samples under different surcharge pressures. A soil sample is remolded to 
approximately 90 percent relative compaction (based on ASTM D1557) to simulate the 
anticipated field conditions. The remolded soil sample is allowed to settle until it has 
stabilized under a surcharge pressure. Water is then added to the soil sample to evaluate 
its collapse or swell potential in a saturated condition. The test results are presented on 
Plates B-4 through B-7. 
Three Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-8 through B-10. 
Two Consolidation tests with time rates (ASTM D2435) were performed on 
selected samples of the potentially compressible soil to evaluate the compressibility 
characteristics of the materials encountered. Results of the consolidation tests are 
presented on Plates B-11 and B-12.
