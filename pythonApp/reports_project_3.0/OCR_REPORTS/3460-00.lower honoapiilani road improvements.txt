INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Lower Honoapiilani Road Improvements Phase III project
in the Kapalua area on the island of Maui Hawaii The general location and vicinity of
the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommenda
tions resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses
These recommendations are intended for the design of pavement structures site
grading and utility trenches only The findings and recommendations presented herein
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located in the Kapalua area of the District of West Maui on the
island of Maui Hawaii The proposed project consist of improvements to the Lower
Honoapiilani Road from approximately Mahinahina Stream to about Hoohui Road as
shown on Plate 1 Based on the information provided the proposed construction will
consist of widening a portion of the existing roadway installation of new utility lines and
construction of new sidewalks and parking stalls In addition resurfacing of the
roadway is also considered for the portions of the existing asphalt pavement of the
roadway Based on the proposed roadway improvements we envision site grading to
be minimal with minor cuts and fills on the order of about 1 to 2 feet relative to the
existing ground surface Excavations on the order of about 3 to 5 feet and subsequent
backfilling may be anticipated for installation of the proposed drainage structures

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Maui the second largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago was formed by the
activity of two volcanoes East Maui or Haleakala and West Maui The broad gently
sloping plain connecting the two volcanoes known as the Maui Isthmus was formed
when lavas of the Haleakala Volcano banked against the already existing West Maui
Volcano
The East Maui Volcano or better known as the Haleakala Mountain makes up
the eastern portion of the island Haleakala Mountain is considered to be dormant with
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3460 00 Page 5
the last eruption taking place on the southerly side of the mountain around 1790
Haleakala Mountain consists largely of the Honomanu Volcanic Series that built the
mountain from three rift zones probably during the Pliocene and early Pleistocene
Epochs
The volcanic rocks of the West Maui have been divided into three volcanic
series The Wailuku Volcanic Series is the oldest and comprises of the basaltic lava
flows and associated pyroclastic and intrusive rocks that built the major shield volcano
The Wailuku Volcanic Series was covered by a thin discontinuous frosting of
andesitic and trachytic flows domes and pyroclastic deposits known as the Honolua
Volcanic Series After a long period of inactivity where erosion incised deep valleys
into the volcanic flows volcanic activity returned with the eruptions that produced the
flows and cones of the Lahaina Volcanic Series
The project site is situated on the western flank of West Maui mountain The
processes of erosion and deposition caused the top of the basaltic lava flows to be
highly weathered and mantled by residual soils The coastal portions of the West Maui
mountain are flanked by fringing reefs and alluvial deposits Land development and
reclamation projects within the last century have brought the project site to its present
form

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration and laboratory test results the project site is
underlain by layers of stiff silty clays and clayey silts over medium hard to hard
weathered basalt at relatively shallow depths At the location of Boring No 5
approximately 7 feet of soft to medium stiff clays were encountered below the near
surface stiff clayey silts Generally the soils encountered at the site are competent and
should provide adequate support for the proposed new pavements improvements and
utility lines planned for this project The near surface clayey soils encountered exhibit
a slight expansion potential when subjected to fluctuations in the moisture content
Therefore special attention should be given to the moisture conditioning of the
subgrade soils and fill materials for this project In addition medium hard to hard
GEOLABS HAWAII

surface Therefore it should be expected that trenching for utilities may encounter the
basalt formation at localized areas Recommendations addressing the above consider

sections of this report
Pavement Design
We understand that asphaltic concrete are planned for the new pavements for
the proposed project Based on the information provided to our office we understand
that the vehicle loading for this project will consist primarily of passenger vehicles and
light trucks with occasional heavy trucks We understand that the design traffic for the
proposed roadway will have an average daily traffic ADT of about 9 500 vehicles per
day for two directions with 2 5 percent heavy truck traffic
We have made our preliminary pavement design assuming the pavement
subgrade soils will generally consist of the stiff silty and clayey soils as described in our
boring logs Based on our laboratory tests we anticipate the subgrade soil to have a
minimum CBR value of 8 with a maximum expansion value of about 1 percent Based
on the above assumptions we recommend the following flexible pavement sections be
used for preliminary design purposes
For the new Lower Honoapiilani Road travelway the following pavement
section may be used for design of the flexible pavement
3 0 Inch Asphalt Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 percent Relative Compaction
6 0 Inch Aggregate Subbase Course 95 percent Relative Compaction
15 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on a Compacted Subgrade

The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of at least 6 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction The subbase and base
course materials should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with AASHTO
Test Method T 180 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density

Based on the results of our laboratory tests the near surface soils exhibit a slight
expansion potential when subjected to changes in the moisture content Therefore we
recommend that all subgrades be properly prepared and kept moist prior to placement
of subbase and base course material If shrinkage cracks appear on the subgrade prior
to placement of subbase course the subgrade soils should be scarified to a minimum
depth of 6 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture and recompacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
We understand that sidewalks are planned along the alignment of the proposed
roadway improvements To reduce the potential for cracking of the concrete due to
expansion of the slightly expansive subgrade soils and to provide more uniform support
we recommend that a minimum of 4 inches of select borrow be incorporated below the
sidewalks The subgrade soils should be scarified to a minimum depth of 6 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted
to not less than 90 percent relative compaction The select borrow base should also
be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Construction joints
should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints
at right angle intersections
We recommend that CBR and density tests be performed on the actual
pavement subgrade soils encountered during construction to confirm the adequacy of
the recommended sections The recommended sections assume that good drainage
will be provided for the paved areas
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all
surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed anywhere
during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in
or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs extend a minimum
GEOLABS HAWAII

migration of landscape water into the pavement section For long term performance
we recommend that a subdrain system be constructed adjacent to paved landscape

Pavement Overlay
We understand that pavement overlays are also considered for the proposed
roadway improvement project For asphalt concrete overlay we recommend a
minimum overlay thickness of at least 2 inches Prior to asphalt concrete overlay the
existing asphalt concrete surface should be thoroughly swept to remove all loose
materials A tack coat and pavement reinforcing fabric should be placed on the existing
asphalt concrete to bond the new asphalt concrete overlay to the existing pavements
Pavement reinforcing fabric should be aligned and placed with no wrinkles that lap If
manual laydown methods are used the fabric should be unrolled aligned and placed
in increments of approximately 30 feet Adjacent borders of the fabric should be lapped
between 2 and 4 inches Pavement reinforcing fabric should be manufactured from
polyester polypropylene or polypropylene nylon material The fabric should be non
woven and heat treated on at least one side Guide specifications for the pavement
reinforcing fabric are presented in Appendix C of this report

Fills and Backfills
Generally the on site materials may be reused as a source of general fill and
backfill materials provided that they contain no lumps or fragments greater than
3 inches in maximum dimension Structural fill material aggregate subbase
course base course and asphalt treated base course material should meet the
material requirements as specified in Section 700 of the Standard Specifications
for Road Bridge and Public Works Construction Department of Transportation
State of Hawaii 1994
If imported fill materials are required as general fill and backfill materials the
imported fill and or backfill material should consist of well graded granular
materials of less than 3 inches in maximum dimension The material should
have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 15 or more and should
have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1883 In addition it should contain between 10 and
30 percent passing the No 200 sieve Imported fill material should be tested by
GEOLABS HAWAII

Geolabs Hawaii for conformance with our recommendations prior to delivery to
the project site for its intended use
All permanent fill slopes should have slope inclinations of 2H 1V or flatter All
fills placed on slopes steeper than 4H 1V should be keyed and benched
Benching should be of sufficient width to permit operations of placing and
compacting equipment Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling past the
design fill slope and then cutting back Surface water run off should be diverted
away from the top of slopes and slope planting should be utilized to reduce the
potential for erosion
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
The fill and backfill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding
8 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction The fill and backfill material within the upper 3 feet below the
pavement finish grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction The pavement finish subgrade should be kept moist until covered
by the aggregate subbase course
Observations and soil density tests should be performed during grading to assist
the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper
moisture content on each fill lift Where compaction is less than required
additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture
content as necessary to obtain the specified compaction

Utility Trench
Installation of utility lines are anticipated for the proposed roadway improvements
project In general good construction practices should be utilized for the installation
and backfilling of the trench for the proposed utility lines The method and equipment
to be used for excavation should be determined by the contractor subject to practical
limits and safety considerations The excavation should comply with all applicable
local state and federal safety requirements
Based on the results of our field exploration it appears that in general
conventional excavation methods may be utilized for trenching of shallow utility lines
at the site depending on the locations The clayey and silty soils encountered at the
site are generally stiff to very stiff However it should be noted that medium hard to
hard weathered basalt formation was encountered at depths of about 5 to 13 feet
below the ground surface in the borings Therefore some excavations will likely
encounter the medium hard to hard weathered basalt formation as described in the
Logs of Borings requiring more difficult excavation methods
In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used
under the pipes Where soft and or loose soil deposits are encountered near the pipe
invert elevations we recommend that the utility pipe be supported on an additional
18 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation wrapped in a geotextile
fabric Mirafi 180N or equivalent The backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes
should consist of free draining backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the
potential for damaging the pipes from compaction of the backfill It is critical that a free
draining granular material be used to reduce the potential for formation of voids below
the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Use
of the on site clayey and silty soils as backfill around utility pipes is not recommended
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3460 00 Page 16
We recommend that a minimum cover of 3 feet be provided between the top of
utility pipes and the finished grade to facilitate construction of the pavement structure
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to 3 feet
below the finish grade should consist of approved on site soils or select granular
material The backfill material should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
future ground subsidence The upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement
finish grade should consist of select granular material and should be compacted to not
less than 95 percent relative compaction
Mechanical compaction equipment should be used to compact the materials at
the project site Water tamping jetting or ponding may not be used to compact the
backfill materials
Manhole Structures
In general we believe that the bottom of the manhole structures will bear on the
very stiff clayey and silty soils or medium hard to hard basalt formation To provide
more uniform support for the manhole structures we recommend that the manhole
structures be supported on a 6 inch layer of cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel
to provide for more uniform bearing conditions For these foundation conditions an
allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be used
for design of the manhole structures bearing on the stiff soils or basalt formation
Where manhole structures are to be located near Boring No 5 soft soil deposits may
be encountered at the foundation level of the manhole structures For the manhole
structures bearing on the soft soils we recommend that the allowable bearing pressure
be reduced to 1 500 pounds per square foot p s f In addition a cushion layer
consisting of 18 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation wrapped
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3460 00 Page 17
in a geotextile fabric Mirafi 180N or equivalent should be used below the manholes
in lieu of the 6 inches cushion fill to provide more uniform support and as a working
platform Settlements on the order of 1 inch are anticipated for the manhole structures
The lateral earth pressures acting on the manhole structures will depend on the
type of backfill used the extent of backfill and the compactive effort on the backfill
material around the structures We recommend that the new manhole structures be
designed for an equivalent fluid pressure of 55 pounds per cubic foot p c f above the
groundwater level and 85 p c f below the groundwater level The above values
assume that the backfill around the manhole structures is properly placed and
compacted
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges traffic loads line loads and point
loads within a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the structure should be
considered in the design For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the structure
a rectangular distribution with uniform pressure equal to 55 percent of the vertical
surcharge pressure acting over the entire depth of the structure may be used in design
Additional analyses during design may be needed to evaluate the surcharge effects of
point loads and line loads

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
eight borings to depths of about 9 to 16 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using
a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the
materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 8
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was formulated to aid in evaluating pertinent
engineering properties of the soil and materials sampled
Moisture content ASTM Test Method D 2216 is used as an aid to soil classifica
tion and to aid in evaluating certain properties such as relative saturation The
moisture content expressed as a percentage of the dry weight of the tested sample
and the unit weight are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Two one inch ring swell tests were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results are summarized
on Plate B 1
One Atterberg Limits test was performed on a selected sample of the soils to
evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test results are
summarized on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depth Graphic presenta
tion of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Two California Bearing Ratio CBR tests were performed on samples of the
near surface soil to evaluate the strength characteristics for pavement subgrade
support Results of the CBR tests are presented on Plates B 3 and B 4
