Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene
to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
Schofield Plateau was formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded
against the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene
Epoch The dips of the lava beds generally range from 3 to 5 degrees near horizontal
The lava flows on the plateau have weathered to depths of 50 to 100 feet and are
characterized by the red colors of the soil The deep narrow canyons such as
Waikakalaua Kipapa and Kaukonahua are a result of this weathering The brown and
reddish brown soils generally consist of ancient alluvial deposits and residual soils
overlying the weathered rock

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the
tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or
during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not
erupt The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but
most are low intensity quakes that can only be detected by sensitive instruments
However some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause
minor to moderate damage

Liquefaction Potential
Based on the International Building Code the project site may be subjected to
seismic activity and the potential for soil liquefaction at the project site will need to
be evaluated
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils near the ground
surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess pore
water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes In
this process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration
such as during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume
causing an increase in pore water pressure If drainage is unable to occur rapidly
enough to dissipate the build up of pore water pressure the effective stress
internal strength of the soil is reduced Under sustained vibrations the pore water
pressure build up could equal the overburden pressure essentially reducing the
soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid During
liquefaction the soil acquires mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal and
vertical movements and if not confined will result in significant deformations
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion It is generally acknowledged that

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we
recommend supporting the planned shear walls on a foundation system consisting of
continuous spread footings bearing on the very stiff clay encountered at the project site
The footings should extend down to a depth of 3 5 feet 42 inches below the finished
floor elevation to match the existing footing depth For the new elevator shaft a mat
footing is recommended In general the bottom of footing excavations should be
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior to the placement of
reinforcing steel and concrete
Soft and or loose materials if encountered at the bottom of footing elevations
should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm or dense materials The
resulting over excavation should be backfilled with select granular fill materials
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction In lieu of backfilling the
over excavation with compacted fills the footings may be extended down to bear
directly on the firm or dense materials
Based on our laboratory tests the clayey silt soils at the site exhibit low to
moderate 2 6 potential for shrinking and swelling when subjected to fluctuations in
moisture content To reduce the potential for future distress to lightly loaded
slabs on grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of the surface clayey silt soils we
recommend scarifying the subgrade soils underneath the slab areas to a depth of about
8 inches moisture conditioning to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and
compacting to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction In addition the slab
subgrade should be capped with at least 1 foot 12 inches of non expansive select
granular material The non expansive select granular fill material should be compacted
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction

foundation is typically constructed by drilling a borehole placing reinforcing steel in
the hole and grouting the borehole
Micropiles are desirable because they can be readily installed in access restrictive
environments and in numerous soil types and ground conditions In addition
installation of the micropiles generally causes minimal disturbance to adjacent
structures the adjacent soils and the environment

Micropile Load Test Program
It should be noted that the bond stress between the grout bulb and the soil is highly
dependent on the drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed by the
contractor installing the micropile Therefore the bond stress between the grout
bulb and the soil may vary considerably between different contractors and micropile
foundation systems In order to determine whether the contractor s methods of
micropile installation are adequate and to determine the ultimate grout to soil bond
stress we recommend performing pre production load tests on sacrificial
micropiles Considering the smaller size of the micropile it may be difficult to
maintain the compressive test loading centralized during the load test Therefore it
is our opinion that the tensile load test may be performed to evaluate the developed
grout to soil bond stress In general the purpose of the pre production load test on
a sacrificial micropile is to fulfill the following objectives
To examine the adequacy of the methods and equipment proposed by the
contractor to install the micropiles to the depths required
To confirm or modify the estimated minimum depths of the micropiles by
determining the ultimate grout to soil bond stress
To assess the contractor s method of drilling and grout injection
In general the pre production load test should be performed in accordance with
ASTM D 3689 Based on our experience we believe that the load test should be
conducted no earlier than 7 days after completion of the micropile installation

allowable design load in tension Each load increment should be about 10 percent

It should be noted that the micropile foundation system provides low lateral load
resistance due to the relatively small diameter of the micropile Therefore the
lateral load resistance contributed by the micropiles should be neglected Lateral
loads imposed on the building structure may be resisted primarily by passive
pressure resistance against the vertical faces of the pile cap wall footing and grade
beams and by shear acting along the sides of the pile caps and grade beams

Construction Considerations
In general corrosion protection is one of the concerns for the micropile foundation
system Depending on the micropile foundation system used there is a potential
for having inconsistent grout cover around the reinforcing steel of the micropile The
potential for inconsistent grout cover could be significantly reduced if good
installation practices and good quality control measures are implemented We
believe that a permanent steel casing for the micropile may provide sufficient
corrosion protection A permanent steel casing with a minimum diameter of
5 5 inches should be installed for at least the top 5 feet of the micropile In addition
a grout with water cement ratio of about 0 40 to 0 45 should be used
As previously mentioned the performance of the micropile foundations is highly
dependent on the drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed by the
contractor The consistent implementation of production quality control measures
will result in a better micropile product We believe that a metering device to
measure and record the flow rate of the grout pumped into the micropile should be
provided and working at all times Grout should be produced with high speed
high shear mixers to reduce the potential for segregation of the neat cement grout
The grouting equipment should be equipped with a pressure gauge to monitor grout
pressures The grouting equipment should be capable of thoroughly mixing and
producing a grout free of lumps and un dispersed cement and be able to pump the
grout in a continuous operation In addition quality control measures should ensure
that the grout has a water cement ratio that range between 0 40 to 0 45 not to

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings
at the appropriate sample depths
Four Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
Two Unconfined Compressive Strength tests ASTM D 2166 were performed on
selected soil samples to obtain an indicative measure of the shear strength of the
foundation soil The test results are presented on Plates B 2 and B 3
Four Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the friction angle and indicative shear strength characteristics of the in situ
soils The test results are presented on Plate B 4 through B 7
Four one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples
of the near surface soils to evaluate the swelling potential The test results are
presented on Plate B 8
