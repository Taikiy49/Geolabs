REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to
GEOLABS INC
W O 2819 50 H Page 5
be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age As volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from Koolau banked
against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau known as Schofield Plateau
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of this plateau generated
sediments which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and to the
south these sediments accumulated and interbedded with marine sediments and coral algal
reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge The thickness of the sedimentary wedge
ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1 Highway to over 1 000 feet at Ewa
Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation or caprock
over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu Deposition of sediments has continued
from earlier geologic time through the present Agricultural development within the last
100 years and recent mass grading work have brought the Ewa Villages area to its present
form

Drilled Shaft Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site and our
experience with similar developments we believe that drilled shaft foundations should be
used to support the proposed one story plantation style residential structures planned for the
subject development Based on the field exploration results and our experience in the project
area we recommend that an allowable skin friction of 300 pounds per square foot psf be
used for the in situ clayey soils in the design of the proposed drilled shaft foundations
Contribution of skin friction from the upper 5 feet of subsoils should be neglected due to the
presence of shrinkage cracks and potential saturation of the upper subsoils resulting from
surface water runoff and landscape irrigation For the end bearing contribution of the in situ
clayey soils an allowable bearing pressure of may be used Both the skin friction
and end bearing components of the drilled shaft may be used in combination to support
structural loads
Based on these design parameters and the anticipated structural loads we
recommend that drilled shaft foundations with a minimum diameter of 14 inches and an
GEOLABS INC
W O 2819 50 H Page 9
embedment length of not less than 10 feet below the design finished grades be used for
support of the house structures An allowable compressive load capacity of up to 6 5 kips
per shaft may be used for the recommended 14 inch diameter drilled shaft of approximately
10 feet deep This value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by 50 percent for
transient loads such as wind or seismic forces An uplift capacity of up to 6 5 kips may be
used for the drilled shaft foundation based on skin friction and weight of the drilled shaft
foundation
Lateral loads imposed on the drilled shaft foundations may be resisted by resistance
of the shaft soil system to deflect Lateral load resistance of the drilled shaft is a function of
the stiffness of the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable deflection at the top
of shaft and induced moment in the shaft Based on our foundation analyses the
recommended drilled shaft foundations may be designed with a lateral load capacity of 2 kips
per shaft The lateral load would induce an associated maximum moment of about
12 kip feet occurring at a depth of about 6 feet below the top of the shaft for the free head
boundary condition Our lateral load analyses indicated that the estimated deflection at the
top of the drilled shaft should be less than 0 5 inches
The anticipated settlements of the drilled shaft foundations are estimated to be on the
order of less than 1 5 inches In addition we estimate that differential settlements between
adjacent foundations supported on drilled shafts to be on the order of 0 75 inches We
believe that a significant portion of the settlements is elastic and should occur as the loads
are applied Due to the exposed nature of the foundations the homeowners should be made
aware of provisions to protect the foundations of the residences to reduce the potential for
settlement and or heave of the foundations associated with saturation of the subsoils
Consiruction Considerations
Performance of the drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor s
method of construction and construction procedures In our opinion the following conditions
would have a substantial effect on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft foundations

W O 2819 50 H Page 10
Since the drilled shafts are designed with an end bearing component in addition to
skin friction it is important that the bottom of the shaft holes be relatively free of loose
cuttings to provide clean contact between the bottom of the drilled hole and the concrete
shaft
Widened tops or mushroom caps could provide a bearing surface for the underlying
expansive clay soils to exert uplift pressure which could result in foundation movement
Therefore care must be exercised during the drilled shaft foundation construction to avoid
the formation of mushroom caps at the surface
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the contact
between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper construction
techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in drilling the shaft holes and
in placing concrete into the holes
A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 6 to 9 inch range should be used to
provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soil The concrete
should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce segregation of the aggregates from the
concrete mix A representative of Geolabs should be present to observe the installation of
drilled shafts during construction to confirm the subsoil conditions and embedment depths

Shrinkage Cracks
Our field observations indicated that a substantial amount of shrinkage cracks is
present at the existing ground surface across the subject site In general shrinkage cracks
will start to develop in the ground due to the lack of maintenance of the soil moisture content
as the soils start to dry out under the sun and dry weather conditions The development of
shrinkage cracks in the ground was apparently intensified by the prolonged dry climatic
conditions in the Ewa area resulting in large fluctuations of the soil moisture content
Detailed discussions of the shrink swell behavior of the on site soils and the mechanism of
shrinkage crack development are presented beiow
Shrink Swell Behavior of On Site Soils
Based on the laboratory tests performed for the subject project the in situ clayey soils
generally have a high shrink swell potential when subjected to fluctuations in the
GEOLABS INC
W O 2819 50 H Page 13
moisture content and may be classified as expansive soils In addition the clayey
soils will lose substantial strength when saturated and will shrink appreciably when
subjected to drying resulting in shrinkage cracks In general the mechanism of
shrink swell in expansive soils is a complex phenomenon and is influenced by a
number of factors including soil characteristics and environmental factors
Expansive soil characteristics can generally be measured by engineering properties
such as soil plasticity and density to evaluate the shrink swell potential of soils In
general the soil plasticity reflects the capacity of the clay minerals in the soil to retain
large amounts of water while maintaining a coherent structure Therefore a highly
plastic soil such as those found at the subject site will have a greater potential for
volume change shrink and swell due to the loss or absorption of moisture under
cycles of drying and wetting In addition increased soil densities through compaction
or natural depositional history such as desiccation generally have greater
shrink swell potential
Environmental factors comprise of the initial moisture content of the soil and moisture
variations over time The initial moisture content generally affects the potential of a
soil to absorb or expel water from the soil system In general a dry soil will have a
higher capacity to absorb water and swell while a wet soil will lose water more readily
upon exposure to dry conditions and will shrink more Moisture variations can
generally be caused by climate surface drainage vegetation permeability etc
Shrinkage Crack Development Mechanism
We believe that the moisture content of the on site soils was kept moist and relatively
constant during the mass grading phase As a result development of large shrinkage
cracks might not be evident during the site grading operations Due to the high
plasticity of the on site clayey soils shrinking of the near surface soils started to
develop as soil moisture was evaporated from the soil system under the prolonged
dry climatic conditions characteristic of the Ewa area These shrinkage cracks may
close up to a certain degree upon wetting as the soils expand during periods of wet
GEOLABS INC
W O 2819 50 H Page 14
weather or heavy rains However shrinkage of the on site soils and subsequent
cracking of the ground surface will recur when the dry season returns Therefore the
development of shrinkage cracks will recur when the on site clayey soils are subjected
to prolonged dry climatic conditions which causes large fluctuations in the moisture
content unless some measures are taken to maintain the soil moisture content
relatively constant
In areas where water ponding occurs the wet ground will have a higher potential for
shrinkage cracks to develop since the wet soils will lose water more readily upon
exposure to dry conditions and will shrink more Therefore it should be recognized
that this phenomenon would recur and continue unless measures are taken to
maintain the soil moisture content relatively constant in order to reduce the
shrink swell behavior of the on site clayey soils due to moisture fluctuations
Recommendations During Site Preparation
If the existing ground cracks are left unattended for an extended period of time and
are allowed to penetrate deeper below the surface the ground cracks could adversely
affect the performance of the future house foundations The ground cracks could
serve as conduits for water to percolate into the foundation soils and cause loss of
strength of the foundation soils resulting in potential settlement of the house structure
In addition migration of the foundation soils into the openings of the shrinkage cracks
could reduce the frictional contribution of the foundation system and result in potential
settlement of the house structure Therefore remedial work to correct or mitigate the
observed ground cracks should be implemented prior to construction of the proposed
houses
Based on our field measurements shrinkage cracks up to about 6 inches wide and
3 to 4 feet deep were observed across the project site Therefore we recommend
that as a minimum measure the areas outside the building and slab on grade limits
be scarified to a depth of about 12 inches and recompacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction
GEOLABS INC
W O 2819 50 H Page 15
It should be noted that a proper site drainage system is critical to the performance of
the foundation system and reduction in the development of shrinkage cracks
Therefore we recommend that the drainage swales be properly established and the
house pads be graded to provide positive drainage away from the house foundations
Special attention should also be given to the recommendations presented in the
Drainage section of this report

Long Term Maintenance Measures
As discussed above the phenomenon of shrinkage crack development will recur
depending on climatic conditions unless measures are taken to maintain the soil
moisture content relatively constant thereby reducing the shrink swell behavior of the
on site clayey soils due to moisture content fluctuations These long term solutions
may include landscaping installation of sidewalk and or concrete aprons around the
house structure and or placement of non expansive fills in the yard areas
Based on our experience with similar soils house lots without landscaping have
experienced varying degrees of soil erosion during heavy rains It is our opinion that
landscaping of the house lots should be given serious consideration by the
homeowners since it would generally achieve the following objectives
1 Landscaping along with proper irrigation will serve to maintain the soil moisture
content relatively constant due to controlled regular irrigation which reduces
the shrink swell behavior of the soils and consequently reduces the potential
for development of shrinkage cracks
GEOLABS INC
W O 2819 50 H Page 16
2 Development of a root system from landscaping would reduce the potential for
erosion and for altering the drainage patterns
However the future homeowners should be advised or cautioned about the following
conditions which may occur or would be potentially detrimental to their property
1 Ground cracks may develop in the landscaped areas if there was a ration on
the use of irrigation water due to prolonged dry seasons As the root system
is deprived of the irrigation water it would start to draw water out from the
surrounding soil system thus accelerating the drying out of the soils and
resulting in faster development of shrinkage cracks
2 To ensure proper performance of the foundation system the homeowners
should be strongly cautioned against over irrigation or landscaping within 3 feet
around the house structures as this may introduce irrigation water into the
subsoils around the drilled shaft foundations which can soften the subsoils
and affect the foundation performance In lieu of landscaping we recommend
that sidewalks and or concrete aprons be constructed within this zone to
reduce the potential for direct water infiltration into the subsoils along the
perimeter of the house foundation Where provided the sidewalks and or
concrete aprons should be sloped to shed water away from the house
foundations and carport slab In addition the sidewalks and or concrete
aprons should be supported on 2 feet of non expansive select granular fill
material
Based on the above discussion it is our opinion that the yard area may be scarified
to a depth of 12 inches recompacted and landscaped with 6 inches of topsoil
Alternatively the yard area may be capped with a minimum 8 inch layer of
non expansive or low expansion capping soils The 8 inch capping fill would serve
as a moisture barrier to maintain the moisture content of the underlying highly
expansive soils in a relatively constant state The capping fill should consist of low
expansion silty or clayey soils with low permeability It should be noted that the
recommended capping fill for the yard areas should not consist of relatively
permeable materials such as the select granular fill recommended below the carport
and driveway slab areas
GEOLABS INC
W O 2819 50 H Page 17
The capping fill should be moisture conditioned to near the optimum moisture content
and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Prior to placement of the
capping fill the on site soil subgrade should be scarified to a depth of about 12 inches
and recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction The yard area should
be landscaped as soon as the grading work is completed Even with the use of an
8 inch capping fill layer constant watering of the yard areas would be needed to
maintain the soil moisture contents relatively constant
It should be noted that it would not be possible to eliminate the ground cracks
completely due to the nature of the highly expansive soils encountered at the site and the
severe dry weather conditions in the Ewa area The recommendations provided above are
intended only to reduce the potential for recurring of large ground cracks
Site Preparation
Based on our field observations the project site has been mass graded as part of the
Ewa Villages mass grading work Therefore we anticipate that grading work for the
proposed self help residence construction will consist of minor cuts and fills on the order of
generally less than 1 to 2 feet in thickness
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials
should be removed and disposed of properly off site The near surface clayey soils
encountered in cut areas may be stockpiled and reused as a source of general fill material
provided that they meet the requirements for general fill materials
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas
designated to receive fill should be over excavated to expose firm natural material and the
resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill The excavated soft and or
organic soils should be properly disposed of off site and or used in landscape areas where
appropriate

After clearing and grubbing the areas outside the building and slab on grade limits
should be scarified to a depth of about 12 inches and recompacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction This serves to close up some of the large shrinkage cracks
which may become a potential tripping hazard to future homeowners As previously
recommended the yard area may be over excavated to provide an 8 inch layer of
non expansive or low expansion capping soil
Areas within the carport and driveway limits will need to be over excavated by a
minimum of 3 feet 2 feet below the sidewalks concrete aprons where necessary to provide
the non expansive select granular fill as recommended in the Slabs On Grade section of
this report
The over excavated subgrades should be scarified to a minimum depth of 12 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to
at least 90 percent relative compaction Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction
of the subgrade we recommend that the soil be prepared again as recommended above
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and
poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted fill at no additional cost to the owner The need for over excavation should
be evaluated by a representative of Geolabs in the field
General fill materials required to raise the subgrades to the bottom of the
non expansive select granular fill may consist of the on site excavated soils provided that
they are free of vegetation deleterious materials and rock fragments greater than 3 inches
in maximum dimension Additional general fill needed for site filling may consist of imported
materials with a low to moderate expansion potential The imported general fill material
shouid consist of materials with particle sizes of 3 inches or less in maximum dimension The
material should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 8 or higher and a
swell potential of not more than 2 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1883

W O 2819 50 H Page 19
General fill materials should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to
the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the
same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
In general the 3 foot thick non expansive select granular fill required under and
beyond the slabs on grade should consist of non expansive select granular material such
as crushed coral basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded from coarse to
fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension It should also contain
between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have
a laboratory CBR value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested
in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D1883 Imported fill material should be tested
by Geolabs for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site
for the intended use
Based on the information provided we understand that imported ash rock material
may be used for the 3 foot thick non expansive select granular fill underneath the slab on
grade areas The ash rock material appears to be non expansive in nature but may not
meet all the requirements for the non expansive select granular fill material as specified
above Based on our evaluation we believe that the ash rock material may be used as a
source of non expansive capping fill material for the subject development provided that it is
properly compacted to the specified requirements It should be noted ponded water adjacent
to the slabs could cause softening of the ash rock and may result in subsequent heaving
or loss of strength which could lead to potentiai settiement Therefore special attention
should be given to the drainage requirements discussed in the Drainage section of this
report

Non expansive select granular fill materials should be moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The compaction requirements
for the non expansive select granular fill final 8 inches under the carport and driveway siabs
should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the clayey subgrade soils are
critical elements of earthwork observations and soil density tests should be performed by
Geolabs during site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of
compaction and the proper moisture content on each fill lift Where compaction is less than
required additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture content
as necessary to obtain the specified compaction It should be noted that the moisture
requirement of the on site fills and subgrades at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture below slabs on grades is an important requirement considering the moderately to
highly expansive nature of the on site clayey soils
Drainage
The finished grades outside the structures should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Surface water ponding should not be
allowed on the site during or after construction It is highly recommended that gutter systems
be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from the foundation
and driveway areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs within
3 feet of the building perimeter should be avoided Planters next to foundations should also
be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration
into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent heaving or loss of strength In addition drainage swales should be provided as
soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the
foundations and slabs

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
ten borings designated as Boring Nos 101 through 110 extending to depths of about
19 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the test borings are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling
equipment equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 10
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as
the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the
field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive
strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Ten one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and remolded
samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results of these
tests are summarized on Plates B 1 and B 2
Four Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test results are
presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentation
of the test results is provided on Plate B 3
Two direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested Results of the direct
shear tests are presented on Plates B 4 and B 5
