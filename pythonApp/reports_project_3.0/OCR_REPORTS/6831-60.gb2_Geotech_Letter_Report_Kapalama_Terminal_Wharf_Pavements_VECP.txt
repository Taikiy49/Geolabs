In accordance with our revised fee proposal dated August 10, 2023, we have 
provided geotechnical consultation services in support of the Value Engineering Change 
Proposal (VECP) for the Kapalama Terminal Wharf project in Honolulu on the Island of

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located at the western end of Honolulu Harbor in Honolulu on 
the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project consists of two phases – new container terminal 
and new piers and wharves. We understand that the new container terminal phase has 
been constructed with pavements consisting of 14.5 to 16.5 inches of Portland Cement 
Concrete (PCC) placed over 6 inches of aggregate base course. 
The new piers/wharves phase also will consist of new PCC pavements that tie into 
the PCC pavements constructed during the container terminal construction and extend to 
the face of the new wharves. The current PCC pavement section designed for the new 
piers/wharves phase consists of 21 inches of PCC placed over 6 inches of aggregate 
base course. 
We understand that it is desired to re-evaluate the pavement section designed for 
the new piers/wharves phase. The re-evaluation consisted of performing plate bearing

FINDINGS AND DISCUSSIONS 
The design procedures used in determining the new Portland Cement Concrete 
pavement section is based on the “Design of Heavy Industrial Concrete Pavements” 
developed by the Portland Cement Association. Detailed discussions of our field testing 
and geotechnical recommendations for design of the PCC pavement structural section 
are presented in the following subsections. 
Design Subgrade Parameters 
Eight plate bearing load tests were conducted at six locations in accordance with 
ASTM D1196, to determine the modulus of soil reaction (k-value) of the subgrade soils, 
which will be used in our pavement analysis. The approximate test locations, designated 
as Test Areas 1 through 6, are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, the test areas were prepared by placing about two lifts of on-site fill 
material consisting of silty coralline sand and gravel compacted to at least 95 percent 
relative compaction per ASTM D1557. The preparation of Test Areas 1 and 2 consisted 
of installing Tensar Interax NX850 Geogrid across about one-half of the test area. The 
Tensar Interax NX850 Geogrid was installed prior to placing the on-site fill material. Plate 
bearing load testing was conducted on the test section areas with geogrid and without 
geogrid of Test Areas 1 and 2 to evaluate potential improvements to the modulus of 
subgrade reaction with the use of geogrids within the compacted on-site fill material.

Design Pavement Section 
Due to the number of containers that will be handled annually at the container 
terminal, we have assumed an unlimited number of repetitions for the Hyster H1150XD-
CH for the PCC pavement in our pavement design. Based on the above assumptions and 
the design k-value, we recommend using the following pavement section for the project.  
Heavy Industrial Portland Cement Concrete Pavement Section 
16.0-Inch Portland Cement Concrete (Minimum Flexural Strength of 750 psi) 
  6.0-Inch Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction) 
22.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Subgrade 
It should be noted that the above pavement section was calculated based on a 
minimum concrete flexural strength of 750 psi when tested in accordance with 
ASTM C78, which is higher than the conventional flexural strength of 650 psi used in the 
Hawaii concrete industry. 
Subgrade Preparation Below Pavement Section 
In general, the on-site soils consisting of silty coralline sand and gravel may be 
used to raise the existing ground surface to the pavement subgrade level. Fills should be 
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction.

Prior to placing the fill material, the existing ground surface should be scarified to 
a depth of at least 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, 
and compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction 
refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry 
density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture 
is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry 
density. 
The plate bearing load testing indicated that the subgrade support characteristics 
and strength of the on-site soils vary appreciably across the project site. To provide more 
uniform support across the pavement area, we recommend placing a layer of Tensar 
Interax NX850 Geogrid above the compacted subgrade (below aggregate base course 
layer) after compaction of the pavement subgrade. 
In the areas where stone columns were installed, which generally is between the 
landside and waterside crane rails, the stone columns could act as hard spots below the 
pavement due to the variable thickness and consistency of the soft soil conditions at the 
site. To evenly spread the wheel loads and to provide uniform subgrade conditions, we 
recommend placing a second layer of Tensar Interax NX850 Geogrid at about 12 inches 
below the top geogrid layer. The geogrid layer should extend a minimum of 10 feet beyond 
the stone column limits. 
As mentioned previously, relatively thick fills were placed in the area formerly 
known as Snug Harbor. Surcharge fill is planned in this area. After removal of the 
surcharge fill, residual ground settlements could occur in this area. Therefore, we 
recommend placing a second layer of Tensar Interax NX850 Geogrid at about 12 inches 
below the top geogrid layer to reduce the potential for significant pavement distress due 
to differential settlements. The geogrid layer should extend a minimum of 10 feet beyond 
the limits of Snug Harbor. 
Pavement Joints 
Due to the rigidity of the Portland Cement Concrete pavements, significant 
stresses may develop in the concrete from variations in temperature and moisture 
content. In order to relieve the elevated level of stress and to reduce the potential for 
cracking, an adequate number of joints should be provided in the Portland Cement 
Concrete pavement. We understand the concrete pavement will be constructed using the 
slip-form paving method. Paving lanes of up to 30-foot wide are being considered. The 
following describes the various types of joints and general guidelines that should be 
implemented in the design of concrete pavements. 
Crack Control Joints (Contraction Joints) 
In general, a decrease in temperature or moisture content, such as that during 
concrete curing, will cause the concrete to contract and possibly crack. Crack 
control joints are used to provide controlled cracking due to volume changes and

relieve the stresses caused by curling or warping. Crack control joints, such as 
saw cut joints or formed grooves, are provided to a depth of generally one-fourth 
(¼) the slab thickness. These saw cut joints or formed grooves (crack control 
joints) provide a weakened plane, which will likely crack through the full depth as 
the concrete shrinks during the curing process. 
If 30-foot wide paving lanes are being considered, the spacing of the crack control 
joints should be limited to a maximum of 15 feet. Smaller joint spacing should be 
used if the concrete pavement will be subjected to extreme temperature gradients 
during the placement and/or life span of the pavement. The crack control joints 
should be provided in both longitudinal and transverse directions. The transverse 
joint spacing should not be less than the longitudinal joint spacing or more than 
25 percent of the longitudinal joint spacing. 
Expansion Joints (Isolation Joints) 
Expansion joints typically are used to relieve compressive stresses developed at 
critical locations due to fluctuations in temperature. Expansion joints are cut to the 
full depth of the concrete slab, and compressible filler materials are placed in the 
cut. These joints allow for isolated lateral movement of the slabs and reduce the 
potential for damage to the adjoining concrete (spalling of the edges of the 
concrete slabs). 
Expansion joints should be used at pavement intersections and at intersections of 
pavements with fixed structures, such as the light pole structures. Because 
expansion joints generally do not provide for load transfer across the joint, 
placement of dowels with lubrication on one end of the dowel should be installed 
for load transfer across the expansion joint, where required. 
Construction Joints 
Construction joints generally are required when two abutting slabs are placed at 
different times. Therefore, construction joints usually form the edges of each day’s 
work. For the relatively thick concrete slab planned for this project, construction 
joints should consist of a butted joint with a dowel. In general, construction joints 
should be aligned with the crack control or expansion joints to reduce the number 
of pavement joints required.
