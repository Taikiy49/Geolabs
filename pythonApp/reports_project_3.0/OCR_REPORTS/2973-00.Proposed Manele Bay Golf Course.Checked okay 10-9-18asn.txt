INTRODUCTION
We have conducted a geotechnical engineering reconnaissance in support of the
preparation of civil engineering plans for the proposed golf course development
This report summarizes our work on the project and presents our preliminary
findings pertinent to the proposed development of the site The work was performed in
general accordance with our fee proposal to Castle Cooke Lanai Company Inc dated

PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our reconnaissance was to obtain an overview of the subsurface
soils and rock conditions within the proposed golf course development area in order to
provide data for the formulation of preliminary geotechnical engineering recommendations
pertinent to the proposed development of the site as a golf course with related facilities
including grading roadway and associated infrastructure installation
The findings and recommendations presented herein are of a preliminary nature
Therefore they are not sufficient for final civil design nor should they be considered
sufficient for detailed cost analyses of the subject project or its elements
The scope of our reconnaissance consisted of the following tasks and work efforts
1 Review of previous geotechnical engineering exploration reports by others
and review of published literature pertaining to the soils and geological
conditions at the site and in its vicinity
2 Mobilization of a refraction seismograph with operators to and from the
project site
3 Performance of four 4 geophysical surveys seismic refraction at selected
locations within the proposed golf course development area
4 Excavation and logging of four 4 shallow test pits within the accessible
portions of the site using a bulldozer provided by Castle Cooke Lanai
Company
5 Detailed site reconnaissance coordination of the field work and logging of
the test pits by a Senior Geologist from our firm

GENERAL SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is situated northerly of Manele Bay on the southern coast of the
island of Lanai Currently the site is raw undeveloped land with a thin to moderate cover
of dryland vegetation such as keawe and ilima
The ground surface elevations range from about 85 to about 505 feet above Mean
Sea Level The terrain generally slopes in a southerly direction at a ratio of about ten
horizontal to one vertical 10H 1V and drainage is in a southerly direction along
somewhat poorly defined drainage paths
The surface of the site is characterized by a thin cover of soil with abundant
boulders and cobbles Outcrops of in situ volcanic rock formation is exposed throughout
the site The presence of isolated strands of fencing indicate that the site may have
formerly been used for grazing of livestock

W O 2973 00 Page 4
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to develop the project site as an eighteen hole golf course in
conjunction with resort development in the Manele Bay area It is anticipated that the golf
course project will include site improvements such as golf course playing areas a
clubhouse complex access roads a driving range and a maintenance facility
The current plans for the proposed golf course grading indicates that the earthwork
for the project will involve cuts and fills on the order of 20 to 30 feet in depth and
thickness
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Soil Conditions
The site and its vicinity were previously mapped by the U S Department of
Agriculture Soil Conservation Service as part of an overall soil survey of the island of
Lanai In this survey the Soil Conservation Service mapped the site as having the
following land types
Very Stony Land rVS This land type consists of areas where 50 to 90 percent
of the surface is covered with stones and boulders The slopes range from 7 to
30 percent
On Molokai and Lanai this land type consists of stones and boulders underlain by
weathered rock In a few places there is a thin clayey soil among the stones and
boulders
Rock Land rRK This land type is made up of areas where exposed rock covers
25 to 90 percent of the surface Slopes range from nearly level to very steep

Previous geotechnical explorations were conducted by others for the adjacent hotel
development for wastewater pump stations and for portions of the water system As part
of our reconnaissance effort we have reviewed the information presented in the reports
from those previous explorations
In general those previous reports indicate that the site and its vicinity is underlain
by a thin surface layer of soil consisting of boulders and cobbles with some silty clay or
clayey silt between the boulders This surface layer is generally on the order of about
three feet 3 or less in thickness
Under the surface soil the previous explorations generally encountered in situ rock
formation The previous explorations indicate that this rock formation is generally
competent and has good strength characteristics

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our review of the available data pertaining to the site and its vicinity our
field reconnaissance and analyses of the data it is our opinion that development of the
proposed golf course is feasible from a geotechnical engineering point of view
The volcanic formation encountered at the project site should provide adequate
bearing for the proposed site improvements provided that our recommendations are
followed The dry friable reddish brown clayey silt soil encountered at the surface
throughout most of the site is about one foot 1 thick The other surface soil
encountered is dark brown silty clay which generally exhibits high swelling potential and
is less susceptible to erosion Modification to standard grading design and construction
procedures will be required for this project due to the rocky nature of the underlying
formation and very thin surface soil deposits
Whenever possible the clayey soil should be stockpiled for use as topsoil rather
than used as embankment fill material Rock fill procedures are required to construct the
majority of the embankment consisting of the more competent excavated basalt and
clinker materials
The excavated clay soils may be utilized as part of the capping fill near the finish
grade
Site Preparation
Prior to commencement of grading the areas to be graded should be cleared of
all vegetation and deleterious materials The resulting grub and spoil material should be
disposed of properly off site or stockpiled for use in landscaping
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2973 00 Page 8
Clayey soils generally have relatively low strength and high shrinkage and swelling
potential Therefore it is our recommendation that the clay soil should not be used as
subgrade fill in roadway and embankment slope areas The clayey soil may be either
stockpiled for use as topsoil or thoroughly blended into rock fill material as discussed
below
Soft or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below any areas
designated to receive fill should be over excavated to firm natural material and the
resulting depression backfilled with well compacted engineered fill
Excavation
The field reconnaissance and visual observations made at the site and in its vicinity
indicate that the site is underlain by competent basalt formation at relatively shallow
depths
It is anticipated that some hard excavation and or ripping may be encountered for
any excavation greater that about three feet 3 in depth below the existing ground
surface Very hard excavation may be encountered in localized cemented areas or when
ledges are encountered
Cemented clinker and ledge basalt formation will most likely require some hard
excavation such as heavy ripping hoerams or blasting
Generally the excavation method should be left to the contractor Excavation by
ripping is dependent upon the jointing of the formation the configuration of the rippers
and the skill of the operator Blasting should be considered to expedite the excavation
process Generally consideration should be given to the economics of blasting to initially
reduce the size of the excavated rock material and to facilitate excavation as opposed to
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2973 00 Page 9
extended periods of heavy ripping at high equipment rates However care should be
exercised to avoid overshooting which could disrupt the clinker or rock formation resulting
in a loss of strength
Fill Placement
Whenever possible the clayey soil should be stockpiled for use as topsoil rather
than used as embankment fill material Efforts should be made to construct the majority
of the golf course fill embankment of the more competent excavated basalt and clinker
materials
In general the excavated rock materials may be considered to be suitable for use
as fill material The material if reduced to less than three inches 3 in size should be
adequate as a source of trench and structural backfill if needed imported material if
required should be free of boulders organic debris or other deleterious materials and be
non expansive
The excavated rock material may not contain sufficient intermediate fragments or
fines to produce a well graded fill material therefore a choking layer should be provided
at the top of the rock fill to minimize future soil fine migration into the underlying voids
between large rock fragments The stockpiled clayey surficial soii could be used near the
top of the rock fill to provide a capping fill layer
Slopes
Provided that the above recommended rock fill placement procedures and basaltic
materials are utilized design fill slopes of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter
are recommended For fills constructed of on site clayey soils a generally much flatter
Slope ratio of four horizontal to one vertical 4H 1V is recommended Rock and soil fill
slopes should generally be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope
ratio

For cut slopes the following ratios are recommended
a Clay Soil 3 1 H V
c Dense Basalt 1 1 H V
Any cuts or fills greater than 25 feet in height should have a bench at the midpoint
of the slope
It is recommended that upper portion of cut slopes exposing clayey soils be
immediately protected by planting or other means to minimize the potential of erosion due
to soil shrinkage of the exposed soils
Erosion Control
The clayey soils at the surface of the project site are dry and friable In this
condition they are somewhat more susceptible to soil erosion and appropriate erosion
control procedures are recommended during earthwork construction
Due to the dry and friable soil site conditions watering and moisture conditioning
of fill materials along with proper compaction of the surface soils will be necessary as part
of the earthwork operations This watering and moisture conditioning will aid in
minimizing erosion hazards
A review of the civil engineering plans for the project indicates that additional
erosion control measures will be implemented in the grading and earthwork procedures
These measures include the use of detention basins and silt fences to minimize the
transport of sediment It is our belief that these engineering controls represent good
professional practice and should serve to further minimize erosion hazards

Infrastructure Installation
It is anticipated that underground utilities will be installed as part of the
development work Based on the results of our test pit excavations and seismic surveys
it is anticipated that conventional excavation will be encountered in the upper clayey soils
and weathered rock formation for utility line trenching grading to more difficult as the
underlying rock layers are encountered Very hard excavation is anticipated in the dense
basalt formation
Foundation Support
The rock formation encountered at the project site has good strength
characteristics Rock fill material generated from the excavation of the rock formation will
also have good foundation support characteristics It is our opinion that relatively shallow
spread or strip footing foundations may be used to Support proposed structures within
the development area
Drainage
It is anticipated that some form of drainage system will be required to handle runoff
flows within the development area A common practice in Hawaii is to construct drywells
to intercept and dispose of the flows
Based on observations made during our reconnaissance it is believed that the
rocks underlying the site have rapid permeability characteristics and will serve as a good
formation for disposal of the runoff water
Where the rocks are overlain by soil or where ledges of dense basalt occur at
shallow depths it may be necessary to penetrate deeper to encounter more permeable
material

Permits to construct and to operate the drywells will be required under the UIC program

Additional Exploration
This reconnaissance was performed on a preliminary basis in support of the
permitting for the proposed golf course development project Our field reconnaissance

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by the performance of four 4
seismic refraction surveys at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The seismic refraction surveys were conducted using a single channel signal
enhancement seismograph with a 12 pound sledge hammer as a seismic energy source
Refraction seismography is based on the transmission of elastic energy
compressional waves through geologic materials Generally the denser or more
consolidated a material is the faster the transmission of the compressional waves These
waves are refracted at interfaces between materials
The refracted waves are received by geophones on the surface and recorded by
the operator The recorded data is inputted into a desktop computer for processing
which involves the interpretive selection of the arrival times of the refracted waves
After the interpretive selection of arrival times the technique permits mathematical
calculations which will yield approximate depths or thicknesses of materials below the
survey and give their approximate compressional wave veiocities The velocities are
averaged along the travel path of the compressional waves The calculated velocities may
be empirically related to the excavation characteristics of the materials The following
legend explains the terms shown on the Seismic Refraction Survey Results Plates A 2 1
through 5 2
Velocity Compressional wave velocity in feet per second
Dip Dip of the top of the layer in degrees
Depth Depth below ground surface in feet
In addition to the geophysical surveys the subsurface conditions at the site were
explored by the excavation of four 4 test pits to depths ranging between 4 0 and 6 5 feet
below the existing ground surface The test pits were excavated using a Komatsu D 355
bulldozer with single shank ripper provided by Castle Cooke Lanai Company
The materials encountered in the test pits were examined and classified in general
accordance with ASTM Test Method D 2488 84 Description and identification of Soils
Visual Manual Procedure by a geologist from our firm The logs of the test pits are
shown on Plate A 1 1

Laboratory Testing
Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
reconnaissance A laboratory testing program was developed and appropriate tests were
scheduled to aid in determining pertinent engineering properties of the soil and or rock
materials sampled
Classification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Atterberg Limits tests ASTM Test Method D 4318 84 are used to determine the
plasticity of the fine grained portion of soils The Plasticity Index of the material is used
to classify the soil under the Unified Soil Classification System The results of these tests
are shown on Plate B 1
Physical Property Tests
Tests are performed to develop a correlative index of certain physical parameters
of a soil These physical parameters may be used in engineering analyses pertaining to
the performance or suitability of a soil
Proctor tests ASTM D 1557 78 was used to determine the moisture density
relationships for soils when compacted California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883
