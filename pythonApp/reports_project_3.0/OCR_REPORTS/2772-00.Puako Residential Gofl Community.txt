PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our reconnaissance was to obtain an overview of the subsurface
soils and rock conditions within the proposed development area in order to provide data
for the formulation of preliminary geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to
concerns raised in the Environmental Impact Statement for the proposed project The
exploration was limited to the portions of the site accessible to four wheel drive vehicles
The findings and recommendations presented herein are of a preliminary nature
They are not intended for civil design nor should they be considered sufficient for detailed
cost analyses of the subject project or its elements
The scope of our reconnaissance consisted of the following tasks and work efforts
1 Mobilization of a refraction seismograph with operators to and from the
project site
2 Performance of eight 8 geophysical surveys seismic refraction at
selected locations and three 3 geophysical traverses ground penetrating
radar within the accessible portions of the proposed development area
3 Excavation and logging of twenty 20 shallow test pits within the accessible
portions of the site using a combination loader backhoe
4 Detailed site reconnaissance coordination of the field work and logging of
the test pits by a Senior Geologist from our firm

5 Engineering analyses of the field data for the formulation of preliminary
geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to development of the
project site

GENERAL SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is situated in the District of South Kohala on the island of Hawaii
to the south of Kawaihae Harbor The site is bounded by Queen Kaahumanu Highway
to the west the Waikoloa Development area to the south and east and vacant land to the
north Most of the proposed development area is vacant land which has been used for
pasturage of livestock
The ground surface elevations range from about 200 feet to about 680 feet above
Mean Sea Level The terrain generally slopes in a westerly direction at a ratio of about
five horizontal to one vertical 5H 1V and drainage is in a westerly direction along
relatively well defined drainage paths The terrain in this area generally reflects the
surface of aa lava flows which have been mantled with a relatively thin layer of aeolian
soil
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2772 00 PAGE 4
The northern portion of the site is generally vegetated with a thin cover of grasses
with scattered thickets of algaroba Keawe trees Under the grass cover the ground
surface is covered with boulders and cobbles creating a very rugged surface In the
southerly portion of the site the ground surface is generally exposed aa lava flows with
some grasses and isolated algaroba trees
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to develop the project site as a residential and recreational
community It is anticipated that the development will include site improvements such as
residential subdivisions golf course areas and community centers
During the Environmental Impact Statement portion of the project concerns were
raised about the potential contamination of groundwater below the site by the use of
pesticides and fertilizers on the golf course and in other areas of the development
Groundwater modelling of the potential contaminant transport is currently being
done by others To supplement their modelling it is desired to perform a reconnaissance
of the site and to conduct geophysical surveys at the site in an attempt to look for
geophysical anomalies which could be contaminant pathways such as lava tubes and to
provide supplemental data on the approximate thickness of soil at the site
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
_ The project site is situated on the western flank of Mauna Kea which is the second
oldest of the five 5 shield voicanoes forming the island of Hawaii Kohala Mountain was
built during the very late Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epochs by basaltic lava flows
from the Hamakua Volcanic Series
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2772 00 PAGE 5
Typically the lava flows of the lower member of Hamakua Series are composed
of tholeiitic olivine basaltic lava flows grading to oceanite Generally such flows are
characterized as thinly bedded with a high degree of porosity
During the later stages of the active phase of Mauna Kea vents of the upper
member of the Hamakua Volcanic Series opened These events extruding alkalic lavas
with a composition closer to andesite These andesitic rocks generally form thicker and
denser flows
During our field reconnaissance it was noted that in the northerly portion of the
site there is a surface layer of ash soil mantling the site This ash soil is blanketed with
many exposed cobbles and boulders It is believed that this surface ash the boulders
and cobbles are airfall deposits from a large explosive eruptive event which may have
occurred on either Mauna Kea or Mauna Loa This surface layer of ash was not present
in the southerly half of the site
It was also noted that all of the lava flows observed during our field reconnaissance
were in the aa form i e surface layer of spiny clinker overlying a dense core of rock
Generally these observations were substantiated by the data obtained from the
geophysical surveys and traverses
Soil Conditions
The ash soil at the project site differs from Pahala Ash found in other portions of
the island of Hawaii in that the in situ moisture content is somewhat lower and the soil
exists in a friable state as opposed to the plastic soil found in more humid areas such as
along the Hamakua Coast and the mauka areas of North and South Kona
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2772 00 PAGE 6
It appears that the ash was lain in a relatively uniform layer over the hummocky
surface of the lava flows in the northerly portion of the site After the ash was deposited
the forces of wind and water erosion redistributed the soil so that the soil became thinner
on the tops of the hummocks and accumulated in the lower areas between the
hummocks Therefore the thickest soil is found in the local topographic lows It was
also noted that the thickest soil layers were generally found in the areas with thickets of
keawe trees
Although it could not be confirmed by our field reconnaissance and geophysical
surveys it is believed that the ash was also deposited in the southerly portion of the site
and then subsequently buried by later lava flows
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by the excavation of twenty
20 shallow test pits by the performance of eight 8 geophysical surveys seismic
refraction and the performance of three 3 geophysical traverses along the existing
improved trails within the site
In general our exploration encountered a surface layer varying between one 1
to about eight 8 feet in thickness of reddish brown to brown silt Pahala Ash grading
to weathered basalt formation with increasing depth Below this surface layer our
exploration indicated in situ volcanic formations consisting of interbedded basalt and
clinker to the maximum depths explored It was noted that in most areas the clinker
material below the ash had been relatively weli cemented by a carbonate material similar
to caliche forming a hardpan at relatively shallow depths

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our reconnaissance and analyses it is our opinion that development of
the site is feasible from a geotechnical engineering point of view
The volcanic formation observed at the project site should provide adequate
bearing for any proposed site improvements However this observation is preliminary in
nature and may be modified by subsequent detailed geotechnical engineering exploration
The dry friable ash soil encountered at the surface throughout most of the site
generally has low strength characteristics and is susceptible to erosion Therefore some
modification to standard grading design and construction procedures will be required for
this project
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2772 00 PAGE 8
Whenever possible the ash soil should be stockpiled for use as topsoil rather than
used as embankment fill material Efforts should be made to construct the majority of the
embankment of the more competent excavated basalt and clinker materials if reduced
to less than 12 inches in size
The excavated ash soils may be utilized as fill provided that special consideration
is given to its placement
Subsurface Conditions
Based on our reconnaissance and geophysical work it appears that surface soils
only occur in the northern portion of the site Generally it appears that the surface ash
soils average about three 3 to four 4 feet in thickness
Underlying the surface soils our geophysical work indicated that there is volcanic
rock formation generally consisting of aa clinker with a thickness on the order of about
ten 10 feet overlying dense basalt rock It was noted in the test pits that the upper
portions of the clinker horizon are cemented
Our limited visual observations of the southern portion of site indicate that there is
only a minimal surface soil layer in this area
It should be noted that all of the volcanic rock formation observed was in the aa
form
Site Preparation
Prior to commencement of grading the areas to be graded should be cleared of
all vegetation and deleterious materials Due to the relatively high potential for erosion
of exposed ash soils clearing and grubbing should be scheduled as closely as possible
GEOLABS HAWAI
W PAGE 9
ahead of the grading work i e leaving the vegetation in place in given areas until shortly
before the grading in that area is to commence The resulting grub and spoil material
should be disposed of properly off site or processed and stockpiled for use in
landscaping
Ash soils generally have relatively low strength and if the in situ moisture contents
are high enough they are thixotropic i e they lose strength when remolded Therefore
it is our recommendation that the ash soil should not be used as embankment fill or in
roadway and structural areas The ash soil may be either stockpiled for use as topsoil
or thoroughly blended into rockfill material as discussed below
Due to the dry and sometimes windy site conditions moisture conditioning of fill
material will be necessary It is advisable to institute a dust control program during
grading operations Generally it is suggested that the soil be moistened as it is exposed
and excavated to minimize the occurrence of fugitive dust
Permeability Characteristics
The ash soil encountered at the site is a fine silt The estimated hydraulic
conductivity of this material is on the order of about 0 1 gallons per day per square foot
By comparison fresh clinker is equivalent to a gravel soil and has an estimated hydraulic
conductivity on the order of about 10 000 gallons per square foot per day The clinker
observed at the site is somewhat weathered and in northern portion of the site has been
cemented into a hardpan which would reduce the permeability of material in an
undisturbed condition
It should be noted that the formations observed at the site were aa lava flows
Transport of water through aa flows is primarily through the clinker beds Conduits such
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2772 00 PAGE 10
as lava tubes or large open fractures are associated with pahoehoe flows and are rarely
found in aa flows
It should also be noted that the interior core of aa flows consists of dense rock
which has very low hydraulic conductivity values e g on the order of 0 001 gallons per
day per square foot These cores can act as a aquitards by limiting the vertical flow of
water and dispersing the flow over a wider area
Drainage
It is anticipated that some form of drainage system will be required to handle runoff
flows within the development area The current practice in the County of Hawaii is to
construct drywells to intercept and dispose of the flows
It should also be noted that drywells are currently regulated as injection wells under
the Hawaii State Department of Health s Underground Inject Control UIC program
Permits to construct and to operate the drywells will be required under the UIC program

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based in part upon
information obtained from field data points such as geophysical surveys and test pits
Variations of conditions between the field data points may occur and the nature and
extent of these variations may not become evident until construction If variations then
appear evident it will be necessary to re evaluate the recommendations given in this
report
The locations of the field data points were approximately determined by
triangulation and measurement from reference points shown on the topographic plans
provided by Nansay Hawaii Incorporated The physical location of the field data points
should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the method used
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the field data points
depict the approximate boundaries between soil types and as such may denote a
gradual transition The strata lines shown on profiles or cross sections are based upon
interpolation between field data points and may not represent actual subsurface
conditions

APPENDIX A
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by the performance of eight
8 seismic refraction surveys at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate
2 The seismic refraction surveys were conducted using a 12 channel signal
enhancement seismograph with a 12 pound sledge hammer as a seismic energy source
Refraction seismography is based on the transmission of elastic energy
compressional waves through geologic materials Generally the denser or more
consolidated a material is the faster the transmission of the compressional waves These
waves are refracted at interfaces between materials
The refracted waves are received by geophones on the surface and recorded by
the seismograph The recorded data is downloaded to a portable computer either for
processing in the field or for storage and later processing The processing involves the
interpretive selection of the arrival times of the refracted waves
After the interpretive selection of arrival times the technique permits mathematical
calculations which will yield approximate depths or thicknesses of materials below the
survey and give their approximate compressional wave velocities The velocities are
averaged along the travel path of the compressional waves The calculated velocities may
be empirically related to the excavation characteristics of the materials The following
legend explains the terms shown on the Seismic Refraction Survey Results Plates A 1
through A 16
Velocity Compressional wave velocity in feet per second
Dip Dip of the top of the layer in degrees
Depth Depth below ground surface in feet
In addition to the geophysical surveys the subsurface conditions at the site were
explored by the excavation of twenty 20 test pits to depths ranging between 1 0 and 7 0
feet below the existing ground surface The test pits were excavated using a Caterpillar
416 rubber tired backhoe mobilized to the site
The materials encountered in the test pits were examined and classified in general
accordance with ASTM Test Method D 2488 84 Description and Identification of Soils
Visual Manual Procedure by a geologist from our firm The logs of the test pits are
shown on Plates A 17 through A 23
