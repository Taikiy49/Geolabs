Dear Mr. Arana: 
In accordance with the project design requirements, a static compressive pile load 
test was conducted on an augered cast-in-place (ACIP) test pile (Test Pile No. 4) on 
August 8 and 9, 2016.  This letter report presents the results of our field observations, test 
data, engineering analysis, conclusions, and our recommendations. 
In general, the Kahului Consolidated Car Rental (ConRAC) Facility involves 
construction of a new multi-story consolidated rental car facility at Kahului Airport on the 
Island of Maui, Hawaii.  The project site generally is bounded by Aalele Street to the 
west, an air cargo facility to the southeast, and Keolani Place to the northwest. The site 
is also traversed by Lanui Circle, Hemaloa Street, and a large box culvert oriented in a 
northwest-southeast direction.  The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
We provided geotechnical engineering exploration services previously and 
recommended using ACIP pile foundation to support the new building structure. Our 
recommendations were presented in the report entitled, “Geotechnical Engineering 
Exploration, Consolidated Car Rental (ConRAC) Facility, Kahului Airport, Kahului, Maui, 
Hawaii” dated June 10, 2016. 
The pile load test was conducted on a 24-inch diameter ACIP pile with a length of 
approximately 43.6 feet below the existing ground surface.  The ACIP pile was installed by 
Hawaiian Dredging Construction Company (HDCC) using a Bauer BG-28 drill rig equipped 
with automatic monitoring equipment providing the drilling information, such as drilling 
depth, drilling rate, auger torque, stroke count, etc., on a near-continuous basis. HDCC 
used continuous flight augers to drill the pile.  As the augers were removed, the hole was 
grouted from the bottom upwards.  After sound quality grout (based on visual observation)

To provide reaction support for the load test, ten 24-inch diameter ACIP reaction 
piles, with lengths of about 32.2 to 32.6 feet long, were installed in similar fashion as the 
test pile with the exception of using 3-inch diameter center bars in lieu of rebar cages.  The 
load test pile was completed on July 27, 2016, while the reaction piles were completed on 
July 28 and 30, 2016. 
To better understand and predict the load transfer mechanism at the pile-soil 
interface, the load test pile was instrumented with six vibrating wire embedment strain 
gages (Model No. 4200A-2) manufactured by Geokon, Inc.  The strain gages were 
connected using a multiplexer to a Geokon Micro-10 data acquisition system to record the 
strains during the compressive load test.  The embedment strain gages were installed in 
the load test pile at the following depths below the existing ground surface.

The ACIP piling subcontractor, HDCC, provided load testing equipment, including a 
reaction beam, hydraulic jack, hydraulic pump, reference beams, and other accessories. 
Geolabs, Inc. provided the load cell, digital dial gauges, survey level equipment, and a 
spherical bearing. The testing loads were measured by a Model 3000, 500-ton capacity 
load cell manufactured by Geokon. 
Geolabs provided the data acquisition system including a multiplexer, data-logger, 
and data acquisition software program to collect the strain gage readings during the load 
testing. The strain gage readings were further used in the analyses and evaluation of the 
static compressive load transfer. Displacements at the top of the load test pile were 
measured and recorded by the digital dial gauges.

The pile load test was conducted in general accordance with the Quick Load Test 
Method of ASTM D1143 and modified by our office to suit the design intents.  In general, 
the test pile was loaded to about 1,200 kips in approximately 70.0-kip load increments. 
Each load increment was held for at least 15 minutes or until the rate of the pile head 
movement has generally stabilized. In addition, the static load test was held for 
approximately 2 hours at the 560-kip and 840-kip load increment, and 3 hours at the 
1,120-kip load increment to evaluate the effects of creep. After reaching the maximum test 
load of 1,200 kips, the load test pile was unloaded in approximately eight decrements and 
held for at least 5 minutes at each decrement. The results of the compressive pile load test 
are summarized below.

The load test readings and the load-displacement curve are presented on Plates 4 
and 5, respectively. Based on the shape of the load-displacement curve and our 
interpretation of the load transfer mechanism of the compressive load test, it appears that 
the 24-inch diameter ACIP load test pile underwent minimal creep displacement over a 
3-hour period at 200% of the design load of about 1120 kips. In addition, the 24-inch 
diameter ACIP load test pile did not fail at the maximum compressive test load of about 
1,200 kips.  
It should be noted that the load test pile was extended down to the anticipated hard 
basalt formation and was terminated due to refusal conditions, defined as very hard drilling 
with no advancement. The favorable test results confirmed our design parameters for the 
24-inch diameter ACIP piles terminated in refusal conditions bearing on hard basalt 
formation.
