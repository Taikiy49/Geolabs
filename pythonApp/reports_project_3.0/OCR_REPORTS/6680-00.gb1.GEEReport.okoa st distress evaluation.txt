Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas
banking against its eastern flank
The project area is on the southern flank of Koolau and is composed of basaltic
rock built by the extrusion of the lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks are
generally characterized by flows of jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin
clinker layers For the last 1 to 2 million years in situ chemical weathering of the
Koolau lava flows has occurred forming a mantle of residual soils which grade to
saprolite with depth In general saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is
typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite grades to basaltic rock
formation with depth Grading operations for the developments in the area have
brought the site to its present form

Slope Stability Analyses
The Reinforced Slope Stability Analysis computer program was
used in our slope stability analyses Detailed calculations of the slope stability analyses
are presented in Appendix C The Bishop s simplified method of limit equilibrium was
used to evaluate the balance forces and moments acting within the slope to determine
the factor of safety for the slope based on the subsurface soils encountered at the site
and the shear strength parameters The Bishop s simplified method of slope stability
analysis assumes a circular slip surface which is divided into slices with finite mass

Based on our slope stability analysis the embankment fill slope prior to the Kiholo
Bay earthquake has a minimum factor of safety of 2 0 This indicates that the
embankment fill slope would have been stable if a seismic event did not occur
Seismic
Slope stability of the embankment fill during a seismic event also was evaluated
Based on available information a ground acceleration of 0 20g was used for the
slope stability analysis Because seismic condition is considered a transient event
a short term slope stability analysis using total stress parameters was used to
evaluate this condition As mentioned previously we believe that the Kiholo Bay
earthquake may have initiated movement of the embankment fill slope Our
analysis consisted of a parametric analysis by varying the undrained shear strength
of the fill to determine the undrained shear strength that would result in movement
of the embankment fill slope during the Kiholo Bay earthquake
Based on our parametric analysis the undrained shear strength which would result
in movement of the embankment fill slope is about 230 pounds per square foot
psf Our borings and laboratory testing did not indicate that the fill material would
have an undrained shear strength of 230 psf or less However two conditions may
have resulted in low shear strength values within the embankment fill
The first condition may have been that the fill placed on the existing ground may not
have been keyed and benched in the east west direction If the fill was not keyed
and benched it creates a weak plane at the interface of the fill and residual soil
resulting in a zone that would simulate low shear strength Typically fills are keyed
and benched into existing slopes with an inclination of five horizontal to one vertical
5H 1V or steeper Based on the mass grading plans the existing slope inclination
in the east west direction is about 10H 1V therefore keying and benching was
probably not performed in the east west direction In a static condition the slope
would be stable without keying and benching but we believe that the seismic
loading during the Kiholo Bay earthquake may have been enough to initiate
shearing at the interface of the fill and residual soil resulting in movement of the
embankment fill slope

Reinforced Concrete Wall With Tiebacks Anchor
The advantage of this option is that the lateral excavation to construct a
conventional wall foundation is reduced significantly The tieback anchors would
help to provide lateral earth pressure resistance which would likely result in
narrower wall foundation A typical detail for a reinforced concrete wall with tie back
anchor is provided on Plate 4
Tieback anchors are generally near perpendicular to the wall Tieback anchors are
constructed by drilling a borehole installing a steel tendon or reinforcing bar into the
borehole and grouting the borehole The basic components of a grouted permanent
tieback anchor consist of the anchorage and the bonded and unbonded lengths of
the steel tendon or reinforcing bar In general the unbonded length of the tieback
anchor is the portion of the steel tendon or reinforcing bar that does not have direct
contact with the grout allowing the tendon or reinforcing bar to elongate elastically
and transfer the resisting force from the structure to the bonded length The bonded
length of the tieback anchor is the portion of the steel tendon or reinforcing bar that
is directly bonded to the grout allowing it to transfer the applied load into the ground
Based on the location of the slope failure plane the minimum tieback anchor
lengths would be about 20 to 25 feet

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to a depth of about
20 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring locations are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight augers
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1
through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected soil samples retrieved in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Three Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples obtained from the borings to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic presentations of the test results are provided on Plate B 1
Three Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested The test results are
presented on Plates B 2 through B 4
One unconsolidated undrained Triaxial Compression test ASTM D 2850 was
performed on a selected sample to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the soils
The test results are presented on Plate B 5
