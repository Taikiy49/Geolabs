Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, we recommend using a deep
foundation system consisting of driven piles to support the new structure. The driven piles
should consist of 12-inch square and/or 16.5-inch octagonal precast, prestressed concrete
piles, which may be designed with allowable compressive load capacities of up to 50 tons
(100 kips) and 75 tons (150 kips) per pile, respectively. These allowable compressive load
capacities are for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient
loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces.
The driven piles would derive support by end bearing on the coral ledge
encountered at about 20 to 28 feet below the existing ground surface. To reduce the
potential for appreciable foundation settlement and over-stressing of the coral ledge, we
recommend spacing the piles a minimum of 4 feet from center-to-center. In addition, we
also recommend increasing the pile spacing as appropriate to keep the bearing pressures
(column load divided by the pile cap area) on the coral ledge to about 12 kips or less per
square foot.
To develop the recommended compressive load capacity from end bearing on the
coral ledge, preliminary pile lengths for piles supporting the new building should be on the
order of about 30 to 35 feet. Due to the variation in thickness and hardness of the coral
ledge, we believe that some of the driven piles intended for end-bearing purposes may
“punch through” the coral ledge during the pile driving operations. In the event that this
occurs, these piles will need to be spliced and driven deeper (total length of about 110 and
130 feet for the 12-inch square and 16.5-inch octagonal piles, respectively) to derive
support from skin friction by embedment into the underlying coralline detritus materials.
We envision that about 20 percent of the total number of piles required may “punch
through” and will need to be spliced and driven deeper.

2.1. Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes - Waianae and Koolau. The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger Koolau Volcano forms the majority of
the eastern two-thirds of the island. It is believed that Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in
Central Oahu.
Thinly bedded basaltic lava flows built the Koolau Volcanic Shield during the
early Pleistocene Epoch. The main shield-building activities ceased approximately
2% million years ago. Evidence from drilled wells indicates that the Island of Oahu has
subsided by as much as 1,200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic activity
(Macdonald and Abbott, 1970).
During that period of subsidence, coral-algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across the mouth of the bays. The
growth of the reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence. A series of
lagoons formed behind the barrier reefs, and both terrigenous and marine sediments
accumulated in the lagoons (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970).
During the later Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great
continental glaciers advanced, the level of the ocean fell since less water was available
to fill the oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded or melted, global sea
levels rose because more water was available. The landmass of Oahu remained
essentially stable during these changes, and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature.
These glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea, which were both higher
and lower relative to the present sea level of Oahu.
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at

2.4 Seismic Design Considerations
Based on the 1997 Uniform Building Code (UBC) (current code adopted by the
City and County of Honolulu), the Island of Oahu is within Seismic Zone 2A and has an
associated peak ground acceleration of 0.15g. Therefore, the project site may be
subject to seismic activity and seismic design considerations will need to be addressed.
The following sections provide discussions on the seismicity of the Island of Oahu, the
soil profile for seismic design at the site, and the potential for liquefaction at the site.
2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the
tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to
volcanic activity; therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or
during volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not
erupt. The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but
most are so small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments.
However, some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause
minor to moderate damage.
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii
(over 90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the southern
portion of the Island of Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous
earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not
confined only to the Island of Hawaii.
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced numerous earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have
occurred in the County of Maui. The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Islands

2.4.3 Liquefaction Potential
Based on the 1997 UBC, the project site may be subject to seismic activity and
should be evaluated for the potential for soil liquefaction. Based on the subsurface
conditions encountered and a design peak ground acceleration of 0.15g,
liquefaction may occur resulting in appreciable settlements to the ground surface.
Therefore, the effects of potential liquefaction should be taken into consideration
when designing the new storage facility.
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils near the ground
surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build-up of excess pore
water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes.
When the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration (such as during an
earthquake), the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an increase
in pore water pressure. If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate
the build-up of pore water pressure, the effective stress (internal strength) of the soil

Hawaii + California

is reduced. Under sustained vibrations, the pore water pressure build-up could
equal the overburden pressure essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero
and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid. During liquefaction, the soil acquires a
mobility that is sufficient to permit both horizontal and vertical movements and if not
confined will result in significant deformations.
Soils consisting of loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained sands and loose silts with
little cohesion are most susceptible to liquefaction. It is generally acknowledged that
liquefaction will not occur if the deposit is greater than 40 to 50 feet below the
ground surface. In deeper deposits, the greater overburden pressure is generally
sufficient to prevent liquefaction. The following are major factors affecting the
liquefaction characteristics of a soil deposit:
1. Grain Size Distribution - Fine and uniform sands and silts are more
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well-graded sands.
2. Initial Relative Density - Loose sands and silts are most susceptible
to liquefaction. Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to
relative density.
3. Magnitude and Duration of Vibration - Liquefaction potential is
directly proportional to the magnitude and duration of the
earthquake.
In general, the subsurface information obtained from the borings indicates that the
site is underlain by very loose/very soft lagoonal deposits overlying coral formation
and coralline detritus. The lagoonal deposits consisting of very loose silty sands
and very soft sandy silts with little cohesion are potentially liquefiable during a
seismic event of Magnitude 6 with an associated peak ground acceleration of
0.15g.
Based on our analyses, it appears that the loose lagoonal deposits below the
surface fill have a factor of safety of 0.6 to 1.0 against liquefaction. Therefore, our
opinion is that the project site could be subject to appreciable ground settlements
(on the order of about 3 to 6 inches) in the event of liquefaction during a moderate
earthquake (M6+).

3.1.1 Downdrag Loads
The lagoonal deposits at the project site may be susceptible to liquefaction during a
seismic event producing a horizontal peak ground acceleration of about 0.15g at
the project site. In the event of seismically induced liquefaction, settlement of the
lagoonal deposits will occur at the project site. Lagoonal deposits moving
downward relative to the stationary pile will result in negative skin friction forces
(generally known as downdrag forces) acting on the pile shaft. The magnitude of
the downdrag force depends on the thickness of the liquefiable layer, relative
density of the potentially liquefiable lagoonal deposits, and the pile perimeter.
Due to the potential for liquefaction of the very loose/very soft lagoonal deposits at
the project site, settlement of the lagoonal deposits will result in downdrag loading
of the deep foundations extending through the potentially liquefiable materials.
Therefore, the allowable compressive load capacities of the piles have been
reduced by the expected downdrag loads in the pile foundation analyses. Based on
our foundation analyses, we estimate that the downdrag loads imposed on the

3.1.5 Pile Foundation Settlements
Settlements of the pile foundations will primarily result from elastic compression of
the pile member and subgrade response. We estimate the total settlement of the
pile-supported foundations to be less than 0.75 inches with differential settlements
between columns supported on piles not exceeding about 0.5 inch. We believe that
these settlements are essentially elastic and should occur as the loads are applied.
3.1.6 Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering energies ranging
about 25,000 to 35,000 foot-pounds. The hammer should be equipped with an
energy level control such that driving within the loose/soft soils may be controlled at
a lower energy to reduce tensile stress development, which could damage or cause
cracking of the piles.
“Matching” of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be driven should be
considered when determining the type of driving equipment to use. This will result
in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy to the top of the pile
and also in the reduction of pile damage. Geolabs should review the proposed pile
driving equipment to evaluate whether they are capable of installing the piles to the
recommended capacity. The contractor should forward pile and driving equipment
data forms to Geolabs for evaluation prior to construction. Geolabs should review
the submittal and assess the driveability of piles with the use of the selected
hammer.

with a maximum particle size of 3 inches. To reduce the potential for appreciable future

pavement areas, the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling six borings designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2 and Boring Nos. 101 through
104, to a maximum depth of about 154.0 feet below the existing ground surface. The
borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight auger
and rotary coring tools. The approximate locations of the borings drilled for the project
are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-6.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings in general
accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition,
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths.
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937)
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties. The results of these tests are presented on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
Seven Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 & C 136) were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is presented on
Plates B-1 and B-2.
Five Unconfined Compression tests (ASTM D 2938) were performed on selected
core samples to evaluate their unconfined compressive strength of the coral formation
encountered. Results of the unconfined compression tests are presented on Plate B-3.
