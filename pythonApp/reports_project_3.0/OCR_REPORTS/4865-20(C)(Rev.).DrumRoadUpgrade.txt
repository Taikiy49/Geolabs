This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the FYO5 MCA Drum Road Upgrade project from Helemano to Kahuku on
the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1

findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at
the end of this report

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was formed by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The
younger shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in
age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became
extinct while Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below
Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank The project site is generally located at
the west to northwest flanks of the Koolau
Koolau was built during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epoch by the
accumulation of thinly bedded basaltic lava flows extruded from vents along a fissure
line over 30 miles in length which roughly paralleled the trend of what is now the Koolau
Range The main shield building activity ended in the early Pleistocene approximately
2 5 million years ago In situ chemical weathering of the Koolau lava flows has
occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years The weathering process has formed a mantle
of residual and saprolitic soils In general saprolite is composed mainly of silty material
and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite grades to
basaltic rock formation with increased depth
After the end of the main shield building activity the forces of erosion began to
cut deep valleys into the volcanic dome During the middle to late Pleistocene the Ice
Age sea levels fluctuated eustatically in response to the cycles of continental
glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill the ocean
basins thus sea levels fell to stands below the present sea level When the glaciers
melted and retreated an excess of water became available thus sea levels rose to
elevations above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacioeustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level

When the sea level was high the base level was raised so that sediments accumulated
at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
the detachment of soil and rock mass from the slopes and movement of the material
downslope toward the axis of the valley by gravity to form colluvial deposits Once the
colluvium reaches streams in the valley alluvial processes become dominant and the
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium Large areas of alluvium can be
found in the lowland areas and bordering streams
When alluvial and colluvial deposits are left in place or buried for long periods of
time chemical processes begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a
breakdown or weathering of the material The chemical processes also cause
induration or cementation of the coarse grained portion of the sediment resulting in a
poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate Simultaneously erosion
continues in areas above the valley floors and upstream in headwaters This continued
erosion generates material which is transported downslope covering the older alluvial
deposits
2 2 Site Description
The project site is located along the existing Drum Road in the Helemano and
Kahuku areas on the Island of Oahu Hawaii Drum Road is currently a dirt road
approximately 37 8 kilometers long Drum Road begins from the Helemano Military
Reservation and extends to the Kahuku Training Area The project site is located in a
relatively high rainfall region in a humid tropical subtropical climate setting The
proposed roadway alignment is shown on the General Site Plan Plate 2

consisting of multiple large gulches and smaller stream valleys separated by mountain
ridgelines and some broad upland plateaus The mountain terrain is part of the Koolau
Mountain Range which formed from the Tertiary age volcanic eruption of layered
basaltic lavas from the Koolau Shield Volcano

Residual Soils
Residual soils are completely weathered volcanic rocks that have lost all visible
rock texture characteristics Residual soils are typically brown to reddish brown in
color and may be mottled with various colors Residual soils are comprised mainly
of clayey and silty materials with stiff to very stiff consistency Typically residual
soils usually occur overlying saprolitic soils or weathered basalt rock formation
Some relict boulders of hard rock may occasionally be encountered in residual
soils

Saprolitic Soils
Extremely weathered rock is referred to as saprolitic material and represents rock
that has been reduced by weathering to soil like components with decomposed
rock fragments However the material retains the remnant rock texture such as
layering vesicularity and some fracture patterns Saprolitic soils are typical of the
tropical weathering of volcanic rocks and are composed mainly of silty materials
Saprolitic soils generally consist of brown to reddish brown mottled with various
colors The coloring scheme of the saprolitic soils depends on the type of parent
volcanic rock from which the soils are derived and the degree of chemical
weathering that the material has experienced In general saprolitic soils have a
stiff to very stiff consistency Relict boulders of hard rock and hard rock ledges
are commonly encountered in saprolitic soils
Colluvial Talus Deposits
Talus deposits are materials that have detached from the slope face and
transported down slope by gravitational forces The transportation methods of the
detached material from the slope include falling or sliding rolling washing of loose
materials by sheet or rill runoff of rain soil creep and landsliding Talus deposits
are often found at the base of cliffs in mountainous regions Talus deposits
characteristically contain large unsorted rock fragments ranging in size from a few
centimeters to a few meters in diameter There is no orderly arrangement of the
fragments and no bedding or layering The abrupt break in the slope is usually

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by shifts
in the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before
or during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not
erupt The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but
most of the earthquakes are so small that they may only be detected by sensitive
instruments However some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and
a few cause minor to moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii
over 90 percent of earthquakes are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic
activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the
Island of Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5 M5 however earthquakes are not confined only to the
Island of Hawaii
To a lesser degree the Island of Maui has experienced numerous earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5 Therefore moderate to strong earthquakes have
occurred in the County of Maui The effects of earthquakes occurring on the
Islands of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu For example
several small landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui
Earthquake of In addition some houses on the Island of Oahu were
reportedly damaged as a result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 M7

Subbase and Base Preparation
As previously mentioned the in situ soils have high moisture contents 30 to
40 percent We believe that compaction of the pavement subgrade soils to a minimum
of 95 percent relative compaction may be difficult without significant aeration of the
pavement subgrade soils Because the project site is located in a high rainfall
environment we believe that it may be difficult to aerate the pavement subgrade soils
Therefore we recommend that the compaction requirement for the pavement subgrade
be reduced from 95 percent to not less than 90 percent relative compaction Prior to
compaction of the pavement subgrade the soils should be scarified to a depth of
200 mm and moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of the soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by
dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Because the pavement subgrade compaction requirement has been reduced to
not less than 90 percent relative compaction we believe that it would also be difficult to
achieve a relative compaction of not less than 100 percent for the aggregate base and
subbase course materials below the flexible pavement section Therefore we
recommend that the compaction requirement for the aggregate base and subbase
course materials be reduced from 100 percent to not less than 95 percent relative
compaction

3 11 Pavement Drainage System
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due
to saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils Based on our experience
W O 4865 20 C GEOLABS INC Page 54
Hawaii California
SECTION 3 DICUSSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
with projects having similar soil conditions there is a strong tendency for water to
accumulate at the interface between the granular subbase material and the underlying
silty and clayey pavement subgrade soils In addition free water voids in the pavement
section repeated heavy loads and an erodible base can also cause pumping Unless
the pavement subgrade soils are properly prepared and adequate drainage is provided
the accumulated water will likely soften the silty and clayey subgrade soils and cause
premature failure of the new pavement Therefore paved areas should be sloped and
drainage gradients maintained to carry surface water off the site Surface water
ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after construction
In general the primary source of free water in the pavement section is surface
water infiltration through cracks and joints in the pavement A major source of infiltrated
moisture is the longitudinal pavement shoulder interface Water also enters the
pavement section from shallow ditches To reduce moisture infiltration into the
pavement structure pavement joints and cracks should be sealed to reduce water
infiltration into the pavement section and underlying subgrade soils Considering the
high rainfall in this area longitudinal edge drains should be considered along the
alignment with relatively flat gradients and transverse drains should be provided at low
spots
As mentioned earlier pavement drainage is generally caused by poor surface
and subsurface drainage conditions along the roadway alignment Therefore we
recommend that longitudinal edge drains along the sides of the roadway alignment be
considered where the roadway gradient is less than 2 percent Where needed a
longitudinal edge drain collector system with outlet pipes should be provided for positive
drainage The outlets must discharge into appropriate drainage outlet structures such
as drainage ditches culverts or catch basins
The typical edge drain would consist of a perforated pipe with
perforations facing down surrounded by permeable backfill material such as No 3B
Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation The bottom of the edge drain trench should
be at least 300 mm below the bottom of the subbase course layer A filter fabric such

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
45 borings along the roadway extending to depths ranging from about 4 6 to 6 6 meters
below the existing ground surface and 48 borings at the 16 stream crossings extending to
depths ranging from about 6 6 to 17 5 meters below the existing ground surface The
approximate locations of the borings are shown on the General Site Plan Plate 2
Enlarged site plans are provided on Plates 3 1 through 3 21 The borings were drilled
using truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers
and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologists who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered is presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 93
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils by driving a 75 mm OD Modified California sampler with a 63 5 kilogram
hammer falling 760 millimeters In addition some samples were obtained from the
drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration
Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 50 mm OD standard penetration
sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the
sampler the second and third 150 mm of an 450 mm drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket
penetrometer tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths

presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on Plates B 3 through B 6

APPENDIX D ROCKFALL HAZARD MITIGATION
1
Introduction
A geotechnical engineering exploration was conducted for the Drum Road Upgrade
project which included an assessment of the existing potential rockfall hazards that could
affect the use and safety of persons traveling on Drum Road The Drum Road alignment
traverses rugged mountainous terrain consisting of steep forested slopes containing areas
of surface boulders and weathered basalt rock outcroppings The steep slopes which
contain potential rockfall hazards are located directly upslope from portions of the Drum
Road alignment particularly within the deeper gulches traversed by the roadway The
Drum Road alignment has very limited shoulder area available for rockfall catchment and
the steep inclined slopes adjacent to the roadway have the potential to generate falling
rock and rolling boulders that could directly impact upon the roadway
The natural weathering and erosion of the steep mountain slopes have the potential
to generate generally unpredictable rockfall activity Rockfall in the Koolau Range is a
naturally occurring erosional process typified by the mass transfer of unstable slope
materials down toward the valley floor under the influence of gravity Hillslope areas may
be identified as having a strong potential for generating rockfall hazards however the
current state of engineering practice is not able to identify when and where specific rockfall
activity may occur
Because the roadway is intended for use by occasional heavy military vehicle
traffic which may produce some ground vibrations that could exacerbate rock slope
instability it was desired to evaluate the slopes with respect to rockfall hazard mitigation in
an effort to substantially reduce the potential for rockfall activity to cause injury and
property damage Due to the extensive remote forested slopes encountered and other
project constraints rockfall hazard mitigation for this project will be limited to slope scaling
to remove identifiable rockfall hazards on the lower slopes adjacent to the roadway and
the installation of rockfall impact barriers at selected locations where widespread upslope
potential rockfall hazards exist
Our evaluation of the potential rockfall hazards for the project consisted of the
performance of a ground surface reconnaissance of the roadway alignment and adjacent
hillslopes to identify areas of significant rockfall hazard potential Based on the information
obtained from our field reconnaissance computer aided simulation of rockfall activity was
performed to evaluate the potential behavior of rockfall emanating from the selected slope
areas
2
General Site and Rockfall Hazard Conditions
The project site is located within steep mountainous terrain underlain by layered
basaltic volcanic rock and other weathering derived in situ soil products saprolitic
materials The saprolitic materials are generally encountered along the higher elevation
slopes of the mountainous terrain The majority of the Drum Road alignment traverses
these higher elevation slopes composed of saprolitic materials The saprolitic materials

Potential rockfall hazards were identified located in two primary field settings along
Drum Road The first field setting Condition 1 encompasses the existing cut slope and
exposed bare slope faces located adjacent to the roadway where localized blocks of rock
or boulders hard rock erosional remnants are embedded in the saprolitic materials In
addition other limited areas of layered basaltic rock outcropping were encountered along
some existing roadway cut slopes The potential rockfall hazard from the cut slopes and
exposed slope surfaces may consist of existing loose unsupported or highly fractured rock
outcroppings that could fall onto the roadway surface The cut slope faces observed along
the majority of the roadway alignment generally expose deeply weathered basaltic rock
consisting of saprolitic soil materials containing some embedded boulders and localized
outcropping of hard rock formation as previously mentioned Based on our observations
the undisturbed natural hillslope areas located upslope from the existing saprolitic cut
slopes appear to have limited low relief surface rock outcroppings Thus the potential
rockfall hazard is believed to be lower within the natural saprolitic slopes located above the
existing developed cut slopes Based on our observations potential rockfall hazards
associated with the saprolitic materials is believed to be limited to the cut slope faces and
bare slopes where localized embedded boulders have been partly exposed by on going
slope surface erosion Since new slope cuts and grading which could mitigate the
existing rockfall hazards is limited for this project we believe that slope scaling is the most
efficient method of reducing the potential rockfall hazards from the existing cut slopes and
bare slope exposures that will not be affected by grading
The second field setting Condition 2 identified for potential rockfall hazards
encompasses some extensive hillside slopes located above the existing steep rock cut
slopes where numerous large blocky surface rock outcroppings are located The upslope
areas harboring potential large block rockfall hazards appear to be limited to portions of
some of the deeper gulches located at the project site Based on our observations the
potential rockfall hazards consist of larger high relief outcroppings of dense blocky rock
material and surface boulders residing on steep slopes extending approximately 30 to
100 meters in height above the roadway surface The potential rockfall hazard from these
slopes may consist of rolling tumbling and bouncing blocks of dense rock material which
could fall from a significant height above the roadway Mitigation of this type of rockfall
hazard typically consists of individual block stabilization demolition removal or
construction of a rockfall impact barrier to protect downslope property Due to the
widespread occurrence of potential rockfall hazards located within the remote and difficult
access terrain block stabilization and block removal are not considered to be viable
options for rockfall hazard mitigation on the upper natural slopes located above the
roadway cut slopes Therefore the installation of high capacity rockfall impact barriers
along the existing cut slope brows should reduce the potential for high energy rolling
boulders to impact the roadway

Rockfall Simulation
In order to develop a basis of design for the rockfall barrier system capacity an
analysis of the potential rockfall behavior emanating from the upper natural slopes was
performed using the Colorado Rockfall Simulation Program CRSP The Colorado
Rockfall Simulation Program CRSP is a computer program that is a widely accepted
engineering tool used by experienced personnel to estimate potential rockfall behavior by
simulating rockfall activity based on input parameters that are selected on a site specific
basis The input parameters for this project were selected based on the field observations
Basically the program provides a statistical evaluation of potential rockfall behavior based
on the hillslope topographic profile and other input information such as rock size rock
shape and other parameters used to characterize the ground surface irregularities
Information obtained from the CRSP analysis included the predicted falling rock velocity
bounce height kinetic energy and roll out distance The output information is useful to
assist in the site specific design of various rockfall mitigation schemes such as rockfall
impact barriers or other rockfall containment systems
The CRSP analysis for this project was conducted by statistically modeling rockfall
behavior emanating from moderate relief forested slope inclinations of 1H 1V and
Slope heights of about 30 60 and 90 meters were used to model the general
rock source area conditions observed in the field Spherical rock block sizes of 4 5 and
6 foot dimension rolling from random initiation points on the slope were used as input
parameters for the analysis One hundred to 1 000 source boulders were utilized for each
simulation run to develop a statistical analysis for computing the estimated boulder bounce
height and rolling kinetic energy Selected information obtained from the available
topographic survey data which was used to assist in evaluation of the possible extent and
slope inclination condition of the upslope rockfall source area is provided on the Rockfall

Rockfall Impact Barrier
The Rockfall Impact Barrier RIB is a specialized barrier fence structure designed
to flex and absorb the design impact energy of falling rock and rolling boulders The
barrier system essentially consists of a series of steel posts linked by wire rope cables with
steel ring or cable nets and wire mesh materials spanning between the support posts
Lateral and upslope wire rope anchors that are installed in drilled holes and grouted in
place with cement grout restrain the barrier posts In a rockfall event the kinetic energy is
primarily absorbed by the barrier net and braking ring system Secondarily the remaining
energy is transmitted through the posts and the support ropes into the ground Rockfall
impact barriers of varying capacities and heights are fabricated based on the anticipated
design loading rockfall impact energy and probable rock bounce heights Therefore it is
critical to carefully evaluate the existing site conditions and perform statistical simulation of

the rockfall behavior to assist in the design of an appropriately sized rockfall impact barrier
system
Rockfall impact barriers are designed to non destructively deform to absorb and
dissipate the impacting rockfall energy The barrier may sustain multiple small events
without the need for debris removal or maintenance However following a large rockfall
event the entrained debris should be removed to restore capacity and the system
inspected for signs of wear and distress Monitoring and maintenance of the barrier
system is an absolute requirement to ensure the desired level of safety System safety
could be diminished through repeated rockfall events natural disasters inadequate
dimensioning or failure to use standard components and original parts In addition
corrosion caused by the environment or other external factors may reduce the system
factor of safety
Rockfall events are sporadic and generally unpredictable in nature Slope
disturbance heavy storm runoff earthquakes and ground vibration are all common cause
of rockfall events The complex interaction of possible causes and changes due to slope
weathering may all contribute to produce rockfall events therefore the guarantee of the
safety of persons and property is not an exact science which can be completely
controlled However the risk of injury and property damage may be significantly reduced
with appropriate calculations and the application of sound engineering principals in the
design and construction of rockfall impact barriers in identified potential risk areas
Based on our CRSP analyses a high capacity rockfall barrier system with a height
of about 4 meters located along or near the existing cut slope brow appears to be an
effective protection system for the roadway based on the input parameters used for this
project We recommend the construction of a rockfall barrier system having a minimum
design capacity of about 1 000 Kilo Joules kj with some additional reserve capacity To
facilitate the rockfall barrier installation on the difficult access sloping terrain we
recommend that the selected rockfall barrier system utilize a hinged base plate assembly
In addition to further facilitate the barrier installation we recommend the selection of a
barrier system that maximizes the support post distance spacing and minimizes the use of
upslope post restraining ropes Based on our research of available rockfall barrier
systems it appears that the or equivalent would be an acceptable
rockfall impact barrier system In addition the capacity CAN C rockfall barrier
distributed by Mountain Management Inc may be considered since the containment
system places all support ropes and support posts on the downslope side of the ring
netting eliminating them from possible direct impact on the upslope side of the ring nets

that could potentially lower the effectiveness of the system the planned barrier alignment
was shifted upslope to areas suggested by the LIDAR survey as being feasible for barrier
installation while maintaining the system effectiveness

consist of saprolitic and highly weathered basaltic rock materials Occasional moderately
weathered hard basaltic rock materials comprise some of the cut slopes exposed within
the deeper drainage gulches Most of the cut slopes range in height from about 5 meters
to about 20 meters in height with an average height on the order of about 12 meters
Based on our estimation there is approximately 3 700 lineal meters of slope length where

It appears that some of the original cut slopes were originally of lower height
however continued surface erosion and shallow slide activity acting upon the steep slopes
have generated additional bare slope scars extending above the original cut slope height
On going erosion appears to have resulted in the exposure of localized boulder
corestones hard rock erosional remnant material embedded within the surrounding
saprolitic materials In addition active erosion of the exposed slopes has resulted in
overhanging cut slope brows generally composed of loose rocks and soils with exposed
organic root material
In an effort to stabilize the existing cut slopes where construction of new cut slopes
are not feasible to construct it is desired to perform slope scaling to remove the potentially
unstable earth materials and partly embedded potential rockfall hazards In addition slope
areas residing below the planned RIB alignments contain loose fractured rock and boulder
materials capable of producing rockfall hazards
Rockfall mitigation specialty contractors typically perform slope scaling Slope
scaling consists of experienced crews employing manual labor and hand tools to remove
loose rocks and precariously positioned rock fragments from the slope face The scaling
crews performing the rock slope scaling should be experienced in the performance of rock
slope scaling by rappelling and or working from crane baskets Based on the estimated
area of the slope face designated for scaling we envision that the slope face areas could
readily accommodate multiple 4 man crews working simultaneously on the slopes We
envision that two 2 slope scaling crews may be deployed for the Drum Road project
slope scaling effort Slope scaling work typically proceeds by starting from the top of the
slope and working downward in a controlled and orderly manner in conjunction with the
combined progress of all the scaling crews working on the slope face Slope scaling is
typically performed on slopes with a slope inclination steeper than about 2H 1V
In general each slope scaling crew would consist of a scaling supervisor who
should be a specialist in slope scaling and three experienced slope scalers For the
additional slope scaling crews we anticipate that a scaler foreman in lieu of the scaling
supervisor and three 3 slope scalers would make up each additional scaling crew The
scaling supervisor should have a minimum of five years of high slope scaling experience
with a minimum of 2 000 hours of demonstrated experience supervising slope scaling
The scaling supervisor should have completed the American Red Cross basic first aid
course or equivalent and should have experience or training in the use of emergency
remote rescue techniques
The scaler foreman is an individual who is directly in charge of and responsible for
the safety and work performed by a crew of three slope scalers The scaler foreman
Appendix D Continued
Rockfall Hazard Mitigation

JULY 2005 Page D 11
should be actively engaged at the site location and should be engaged in actual slope
scaling activities for at least 50 percent of the slope scaling crew time The scaler foreman
should have a minimum of three years of high slope scaling experience with a minimum of
2 000 hours of demonstrated experience performing slope scaling Each scaler foreman
should have completed the American Red Cross basic first aid course or equivalent and
should have experience or training in the use of emergency remote rescue techniques
Each slope scaler is an individual who is engaged in accessing the slope face and
removing loose rock and materials from the slope face using a variety of hand tools at
locations that require modified rock climbing techniques for the safe execution of the work
Each slope scaler should have a minimum of two years experience with a minimum of
1 000 hours of demonstrated experience performing similar slope scaling work
The scaling crews would be supported by an ancillary ground crew such as a
general contractor that is responsible for cleanup of the removed loose materials
deposited at the base of the slopes
Prior to the start of the rock slope scaling a series of eyebolt anchors may need to
be installed along the top of the slope The eyebolt anchors will be used as rappelling
anchors The eyebolt anchors may also be used to anchor other slope scaling apparatus
such as a grouser or other spider type platform These platforms may be used to
supplement the manual slope scaling to loosen remove topographic overhangs and
ledges The scaling platforms are essentially dragged up and down the slope face to
abrade and remove weakened ledges and overhangs that may be too difficult to be
removed by hand and or manual methods
It should be noted that slope scaling work is considered hazardous work and
should be classified as high risk work Visibility is the single most essential attribute to
the successful performance of rock slope scaling by the rock slope scaling crews
Therefore night work for rock slope scaling at the site should be discouraged for safety
reasons of the scaling crews In addition the difficulty in maintaining proper and
complete illumination of the work site makes night work for rock slope scaling undesirable
Furthermore it should be noted that night work for slope scaling should be avoided
because of potentially poor visibility and shadow conditions which may reduce the ability
of the rock slope scalers to properly assess the slope conditions and the presence of loose
materials and precariously located rocks
The rock slope scaling will require closure of the roadway during the time that rock
is being scaled or removed from the slope We recommend that off of the rock slope
scaling work be conducted in the during daylight hours It is envisioned that the rock slope
scaling work would be conducted on an 8 hour work shift We envision that access for
emergency vehicles would be provided at all times during the scaling work
We do not recommend the use of backhoe or other mechanized equipment for
excavation and scaling at the project site Due to the presence of interbedded layers of
weak clinker saprolitic soil materials scaling with the use of a backhoe may be too

aggressive to control the breakage of rock along existing fractures In addition scaling
with the use of a backhoe may unnecessarily over excavate clinker and saprolitic soil
materials and cause potential new slope instability Therefore we believe that the use of
backhoe or other mechanized equipment to assist in the excavation and scaling work
in an effort to speed up the scaling work should be discouraged and disallowed
Finally due to the extensive length of the overall roadway alignment which
traverses mainly steep hilly terrain we envision that some additional slope areas may be
encountered that would benefit from slope scaling to remove unstable earth materials
which cannot be reached by traditional excavation equipment These additional areas of
slope scaling may be identified during construction
