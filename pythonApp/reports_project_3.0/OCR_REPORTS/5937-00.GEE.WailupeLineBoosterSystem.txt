2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Koolau
Volcano reached the end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago
When the building of the Waianae and Koolau Volcanoes came to an end only a slight
embayment existed on the southern coast of Oahu
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of the Koolau Mountain
Range and its geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to the
glacio eustatic fluctuations of sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age The
base of the stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt The Koolau Volcanic
Shield was built by thinly bedded lava flows during the late Pliocene and early
Pleistocene Epochs The main shield building activities of the volcano ceased
approximately 272 million years ago
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea
When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits
at correspondingly higher elevations and deposition of lagoonal and or marine
sediments in quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused
streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef deposits depositing alluvium
Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused consolidation of

2 4 Seismic Design Considerations
Based on the International Building Code IBC 2003 the short period spectral
acceleration S for the project site is 0 61g and the long period spectral acceleration
S1 is 0 178g Based on the above the project site may be subjected to seismic
activity therefore seismic design considerations should be addressed The following
sections provide discussions on the seismicity of the Island of Oahu and the site
classification at the project site
Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the
tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before
or during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not
erupt The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but
most are so small that they can only be detected by sensitive equipment However
some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to
moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii over
90 percent are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic activity and degree of
ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of Hawaii The
Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5
M5 however earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of Hawaii

Discussions and Recommendations
Based on our engineering experience and as built plans it is our opinion that the
building foundations did not experience significant foundation settlements and
differential settlements We understand that fills were placed underneath the
building slabs during construction We believe that continuous vibration of the
motor pumps may have facilitated soil densification of the fills and resulted in
differential settlements of the existing building slabs Therefore we recommend
demolishing the existing building slabs and excavating the existing fills The
excavation should be backfilled with flowable fill to prevent fill settlements in the
future
3 2 New Building Extension Foundations
Based on the field exploration the site is underlain by medium stiff to stiff
coralline sandy silt and clay Based on the relatively competent subsurface conditions
encountered at the project site we believe that shallow spread and or continuous
footings may be used to support the new building extension structure An allowable
bearing pressure of 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of
foundations bearing on the compacted subgrade
The bottom of the footing excavations should be compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or concrete
Soft and or loose materials encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations should
be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavated area
may be backfilled with new fill material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction or the bottom of footing may be extended down to bear on competent
materials at a greater depth
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the
lowest adjacent finished grades Foundations next to utility trenches or easements
should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the
bottom edge of the utility trench or embedded at least as deep as the inverts of the
utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging the adjacent

Drainage
In general retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the potential for build up
of hydrostatic pressures
For site retaining walls the typical drainage system may consist of 1 cubic foot of
permeable material such as open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
wrapped with non woven filter fabric placed at each of the weephole locations The
weepholes should be spaced no more than 6 feet apart As an alternative a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used
instead of the permeable drainage material The prefabricated drainage product
should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the
retaining wall
Unless covered by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of
relatively impervious material well graded materials to reduce the potential for
significant water infiltration behind the retaining structure
Backfill Materials
The backfill material for the retaining walls should consist of imported select
granular materials The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with
particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension The material should also
contain less than 15 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material
should have a CBR value of 25 or higher and a swell potential of 1 percent or
less when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883
Backfill materials should be placed in level loose lifts not exceeding 8 inches in
loose thickness and be compacted to at least 95 percent of the maximum dry
density established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 at moisture contents above
the optimum

materials with a maximum particle size of 6 inches or less The backfill material should
be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence The upper
3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to no less
than 95 percent relative compaction Mechanical compaction equipment should be used
to compact the backfill materials at the project site Compaction efforts by water
tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed at the site

Therefore the finished grades outside the structure should be sloped to shed water
away from the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised to

We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling

Core samples of coralline materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113 Diamond
Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which uses a
hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of the
bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description

California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
