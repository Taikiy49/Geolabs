SUMMARY
A geotechnical exploration program was carried out to provide
pertinent soil and rock mass classification data to aid in the design and
construction of the structures for the H Tunnel portals
and the exploratory tunnel in North Halawa Valley Honolulu Oahu
Hawaii
The program included drilling five borings installing three
piezometers performing five field permeability tests and conducting
borehole orientations and laboratory soil testing
Results of the subsurface exploration indicate that the tunnel
portals will be constructed predominantly in weathered basalt of the
Koolau formation This formation consists of aa and pahoehoe flows that
are typically interbedded layers of moderately to closely jointed dense
to vesicular basalt and layers of highly weathered clinkers basalt
fragments
Groundwater levels recorded during the monitoring period were at
approximately elevation 1000 feet and had a range of fluctuation of 1 to
2 feet

2 0 PROJECT AND SITE DESCRIPTION
The H Tunnel is located in the upper North Halawa
Valley area of Honolulu and is within the Ewa District of Oahu Plates 1
and 2 When completed it will help to alleviate the increasing traffic
flow difficulties experienced by the existing Likelike and Pali Highways
The proposed H freeway is part of a project jointly
supported by the State of Hawaii and the U S Department of
Transportation
The proposed tunnel portals are situated in an undeveloped remote
area of Honolulu Hawaii The topography and various surface features
along the alignment are shown on Plates 1 and 2
The proposed H 3 tunnel traverses the foothills of basaltic lava
flow ridges Topographically the Halawa portal area varies in surface
elevation from approximately 1020 to 1310 feet above Mean Sea Level

3 1 Exploratory Program
The field exploration program consists of test borings and sampling in
five 5 locations installation of piezometers in three 3 boreholes
in situ permeability testing in three 3 borings to delineate the general
soil and rock conditions near the proposed tunnel portals and directional
borehole surveys in two angle holes
Continuous Sampling Methods
Continuous sampling procedures were adopted for the soil and rock
units by using a wireline triple tube rock coring system HQ3 with foam
drilling fluid advanced by a CMC 100 skid rig to obtain a continuous core
of the subsurface and to provide adequate sampling of geologic units
particularly at and near the tunnel level In the upper residual soil and
weathered rock sampling was carried out mostly using a 2 inch split spoon
sampler driven with a 140 lb hammer or 3 inch steel tube pushed into the
ground hydraulically
Details of the drilling and sampling methods and equipment used are
described in Appendix A of this report
Piezometers and Field Permeability
Piezometers were installed in 3 boreholes 2 and NH 4 to
allow long term monitoring of groundwater levels Details of the
piezometer installations are presented in Appendix A with the appropriate
boring logs Results of groundwater measurements are given in Appendix C

4 1 Geologic Setting
The entire Hawaiian Archipelago forms a southeast trending arc of
volcanic islands 1491 miles long The major Hawaiian Islands are located
along a 404 mile arc at the southeasterly end of the chain Oahu the
third largest of the eight major islands of the Hawaiian Archipelago
covers a land area of approximately 604 square miles The island was
formed by the volcanic activity of two large shield shaped volcanoes
4 1

The island of Oahu is divided into four main areas Waianae Range
Koolau Range Schofield Plateau and Coastal Plains
The chief topographic features of the island of Oahu are the two
elongated basaltic domes the Waianae Range on the west side and the
Koolau Range on the east side The Schofield Plateau is located where
the two ranges merge and is formed by ponded lavas of the Koolau
Volcano The Coastal Plains adjacent to the ocean are nearly level and
are formed by intercalated marine and terrigenous sediments The
Honolulu Plain extends easterly from Pearl Harbor and is mostly occupied
by the City of Honolulu
The Koolau Volcanic Shield was built during the late Pliocene and
early Pleistocene by the accumulation of thinly bedded basaltic lava
flows erupted from numerous vents along a fissure line over 30 miles long
that roughly parallels the mountain trends No evidence of a general
break in the deposition of these flows has been found The main Koolau
Shield building activity ended approximately 2 5 million years ago
The project site is located within the Ewa District which lies
about the midrange flank of the Koolau Range that forms the central
eastern portion of Oahu Plate 1
Basalt Flows of the Koolau Formation
The North Halawa tunnel portals will be excavated in basaltic lava
flows of the Koolau volcanics

Two types of lava flows are recognized aa and pahoehoe Aa is
the Hawaiian term for lava flow typified by a rough jagged spinose
clinkery surface and pahoehoe is the Hawaiian term for basaltic lava
flows typified by smooth billowly or ropy surface The flows generally
average 10 feet or less in thickness and the entire rock mass shows
exceptional petrographic uniformity Wentworth 1951 Although pahoehoe
and aa lava flows are common in the Koolau Formation there is a
preponderance of aa flows on the lower flanks of the mass Aa flows
include both clinker basalt fragments and dense interior components
Nearly all the Koolau lava flows are vesicular The pahoehoe flows have
a rather regular distribution of vesicles which often shows marked flow
lines The aa dense masses in some places have almost no vesicles but
more commonly have a few large irregular vesicles or vugs
The contacts between lava flows are irregular due to the broken
character of the pre existing surface formed by the chilling and increase
in viscosity of the advancing lava
Lava tubes are generally encountered in any extensive tunnel
excavated through the Koolau Formation Wentworth 1951 Commonly lava
tubes are 1 or 2 feet in width however some 20 foot wide tubes have
been reported by Wentworth 1951 Lava tubes may form in aa flows but
far greater numbers occur in pahoehoe flows MacDonald et al 1983
An important characteristic of the lava flow formation is its
generally high permeability This is due to contraction or shrinkage
joints normal to lava flow surfaces open spaces along most of the
contacts between flows lava tubes and the open structure of many of the
aa clinker layers The permeability of these rocks may decrease with
increasing degree of weathering see Section

The chemical composition of the Koolau basalts varies from normal
basalts to olivine basalt or hypersthene basalt from studies of rock
thin sections by previous investigators see Data Sources The
variation in lithology between flows a few feet apart laterally or
vertically may be as great as the variation between flows widely
separated
fresh groundwater is impounded by dikes in a northwest trending rift
zone at and near the Koolau crest and for some distance downgradient of
the crest and floats on saline groundwater as lenses outside the rift
zones The downgradient extent of the dike impounded reservoir is
difficult to define The western flank leeward slope of the Koolau
range has not been eroded as extensively as the windward side
consequently no dikes were noted in the study area However the
existence of dikes is believed to diminish significantly to the west of
the Koolau crest
Around the leeward margin of the Koolau Range of the Honolulu Pearl
Harbor area of Oahu a free water table exists at elevations between 1
and 25 feet above sea level Wentworth 1951 Based on the highest
groundwater levels recorded in the borings during the monitoring period
see Appendix C the groundwater appears to be at about elevation 1000
feet at the portal areas If this is true then the roadway tunnel may
intersect the groundwater level about 800 to 1000 feet east of the North
Halawa portals The location of water levels in the tunnel will depend
on the number of dikes encountered that affect reservoir storage
elevations

Weathered Basalt Clinker
Clinker zones encountered during the field exploration were commonly
weathered in situ The degree of weathering generally varies from
moderately to extremely weathered
Most clinker layers encountered in the borings are less than 3 feet
thick but Boring NH 3 encountered 10 to 11 foot thick zones Zones of
high core loss may consist of clinker or soil materials or both The
thick clinker layers may represent a succession of thin lava flows
comprised of thin dense interiors and overlapping clinker that make it
difficult to differentiate individual flow units Clinkery materials
recovered by coring mostly contain cobble gravel to sand sized basalt
fragments which are cemented together The apparent lack of fines in
some cored clinkery material and the occasionally very high core losses
may be a result of material being washed out by drilling fluids
Residual Material
The presence of residual soil at the Halawa site is limited due to
the narrow ridges and steep sideslopes The soil on the ridge forms a
sort of perched cap that is thicker at the top and overlaps down the
sides to a point where erosion keeps pace with weathering in the
drainageways Because of vigorous plant growth the layer may be gray or
black Beneath the soil layer is a zone of weathered rock This rock is
soft in place and exhibits the characteristic structures of lava flows
The weathered Koolau rock presents a complete range from slightly
weathered rock through deeply weathered rock to structureless residual
soils

Recent Sediments
Alluvium This unit is composed of terrestrial sediments deposited
by the movement of water and is mostly silts and clay with gravel and
cobbles Some gravels are uniformly graded and contain cobbles Partly
because of the dominance of chemical weathering the fine grain size of
the parent rocks the steep slopes short streams and imperfect sorting
the erosion of Hawaiian land areas results in sediments that are
predominantly of fine grain and are poorly sorted
In the active stream channels the loose bouldery and pebbly
detritus is classified as recent alluvium
Colluvium This is the mass of detrital mantle rock that overlies
the basalt bedrock and consists of soil and rock detritus that has moved
by gravity from its original position Lower down on the side hills the
mantle may not exist and bedrock outcrops are exposed In some places
the mantle rock is fairly strongly cemented and moderately compacted
Shallow avalanches of colluvium usually only a few feet thick often
occur after heavy rains

Subsurface explorations for the proposed H Portals at
North Halawa Valley were conducted by drilling five 5 borings ranging
in depth from 120 to 280 feet below the existing ground surface The
borings were drilled by a skid mounted drill rig that
was airlifted to the drill sites by helicopters a Hughes 500 C anda
Bell 204
Most of the soil portions residual and alluvium of the borings
were sampled by using a 2 inch split spoon or 3 inch thin wall tube
Four inch 4 diameter steel casing was used to clean out the borings
after each split spoon or thin wall tube sampling interval
Rock coring was carried out using the Longyear HQ3 triple tube
wireline rock coring system for all of the borings
To facilitate better recovery of the soil rock samples a mixture of
air fresh water foaming agent airquick foamer and a co polymer clear
mud was used for the drilling fluid system to conserve water and to
lessen the erosive action on the sample while coring
The wireline HQ3 core barrel was used successfully to obtain rock
core samples in highly weathered basalt lava flows and in most clinker
layers The occasionally very high core loss experienced in some borings
may be a result of the friable and silty nature of the clinker matrix
Three 3 single level piezometers were installed in different
boreholes to allow long term monitoring of groundwater levels Graphical
presentations of each piezometer installed including depths and
different types of backfill materials are presented on Piezometer
Details in Appendix A Periodic readings of the piezometric levels were
made and the readings are summarized on Plate C 1 in Appendix C
The two angle holes NH 1 and NH 3 were surveyed with a PAJARI
directional borehole instrument to measure the inclination dip and
azimuth horizontal direction of the borehole at different depths
These readings are summarized on Plates E 1 and E 2 in Appendix E
Materials encountered in the field explorations were described and
classified in the field by an engineering geologist The classifications
were further reviewed by visual inspection and testing in the
laboratory All soils were classified in general conformance with the
Unified Soil Classification System A graphic presentation of the
materials encountered is presented on the Boring Logs Plates A 1 1
through A 5 6

APPENDIX D
Laboratory Testing
The moisture content unit weight specific gravity Atterberg Limit
and gradation tests were performed on selected samples in general
accordance with ASTM standards as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties In accordance with ASTM testing
procedures the Atterberg Limits were performed only on the portion of
the sample passing a No 40 sieve
Bulk specific gravity tests were performed on the core pieces in
accordance with ASTM D 1188 This method was deemed more appropriate
than ASTM C 97 since the bulk specific gravity would reflect differences
in the vesicularity of the cores
Forty four 44 unconfined compression tests were performed on
selected rock core samples in general accordance with ASTM standard
D 2938 79 The ends of the rock core samples were cut with a diamond saw
parallel to each other and at right angles to the longitudinal axis of
the core The axial lengths of the samples were 2 to 2 5 times its
diameter The load was applied continuously at a rate of 0 05
inches minutes until failure occurred The stress at failure and the
failure mode are recorded in the summaries on Plates D 1 through D 23
Sketches of the failures are available on the original laboratory
worksheets
Two 2 direct shear tests were performed on selected soil samples
in general accordance with ASTM standard D 3080 72 Each test specimen
was consolidated under a different normal load A lateral shear force
was then applied to the top half of the specimen while the bottom half
was restrained Three specimens were tested each under a different
normal stress to determine the increase in shear resistance with normal
stress The purpose of the tests was to evaluate the shear strength
parameters of the soils The test results are presented on Plates D 24
and D 25
The Unified Soil Classification system has been used to classify the
laboratory tested samples Grain size distribution and Atterberg limits
vary but the residual materials and saprolite when disaggregated
generally classify as highly plastic silts MH grading to silty sands
SM The results of grain size distribution or sieve tests are highly
dependent on the length of time the samples are tested When the sieves
are vibrated longer more disaggregation takes place and therefore a
greater percent of particles in the clay and silt size result

Directional Borehole Survey
Sa ee mi a LN SH A SE
A survey to verify the azimuth horizontal angle and inclination
dip of two angle holes NH 1 and NH 3 was done using a PAJARI
directional borehole instrument
The readings were taken at selected depths usually at 20 to
30 foot intervals using the HQ wireline technique Non magnetic rods
are attached to the inner tube to pass the instrument through the drill
bit beyond the magnetic influence of bit and drill rods
The survey data are summarized on Plates E 1 and E 2
The calculated borehole location based on the inclination and
azimuth measurements are presented on Plate E 3
