SECTION 1.  
INTRODUCTION 
The project site is located along Kawailoa Road near the Kailua Beach Park on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii. The existing Kawailoa Road Bridge carries a roadway with one traffic lane in each 
direction. In addition, pedestrian walkways are present on both sides of the bridge. We 
understand that the existing bridge is supported on spread footing foundations and the potential 
for scouring to occur beneath the footings is a concern. Therefore, remedial measures to address 
the scour concerns are desired. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. 
The objective of the geotechnical exploration program is to collect information to 
characterize the geologic and hydrogeologic conditions at the existing bridge structure. The 
collected data will be used to support the remedial design of the bridge.  
This Geotechnical Investigation Work Plan (GIWP) serves as a general plan for our field 
explorations consisting of drilling and sampling. The proposed boring locations are presented on 
the Site Plan, Plate 2.

SECTION 2.  
FIELD EXPLORATIONS 
The proposed boring locations are presented on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The exploration will 
be observed by a field representative from Geolabs. The representative, who will generally be an 
engineer or geologist, will log the exploration and collect geotechnical soil and rock samples in a 
consistent manner. 
2.1 
Drilling and Exploration Methods and Considerations 
The drilling equipment for the project would generally consist of a truck-mounted drill rig, 
a flatbed support vehicle for supplies and equipment, a water truck (as necessary), and a pick-up 
truck for the engineer/geologist. The type of drilling method(s) used at each boring location will be 
selected based on the anticipated subsurface conditions from our knowledge and experience in 
the vicinity and will be adjusted/modified during the actual drilling operation.   
Borings will be advanced with a combination of 4-inch continuous-flight solid-stem augers 
and continued with HQ or PQ wireline coring methods to advance the HQ or PQ-size casing. In 
general, auger drilling will be performed in areas where stiff to hard cohesive soils exist and in 
locations where rock coring is not anticipated and will be limited to above the water table. In areas 
where soft soils and/or high groundwater levels are present and in locations where rock coring is 
anticipated, rotary wash boring drilling techniques will be used. Drilling will be performed using a 
truck-mounted drill rig. 
Our drilling over water will consist of utilizing an outer and inner casing and mud pan to 
reduce the potential for drill water to enter the stream. The outer casing will contain the drill 
water should the drill water overflow from the inner casing. As an added protection, a floating silt 
curtain in the water will be placed around the boring location. A schematic diagram of the above is 
shown on the Schematic Diagram of Drilling Over Water, Plate 3. A photograph of our typical drill 
rig is presented on Plate 4. 
2.1.1 Solid-Stem Auger 
Geolabs will use a solid-stem auger (SSA) if auger drilling is accomplished. Solid-stem auger 
drilling incorporates 4-inch diameter continuous-flight augers that mechanically cut and

remove soil from the borehole. Solid-stem auger produces a moderate amount of easily 
obtained cuttings, and little or no fluid is required in the drilling process. This type of 
drilling is usually limited to use in dense or stiff soils. Split-barrel soil sampling operations 
are accomplished after the augers are removed from the hole. Drilling will switch over 
from SSA to HQ or PQ-size Wireline Coring if needed. 
2.1.2 HQ or PQ-Size Wireline Coring 
Borings accomplished by Geolabs typically will be advanced with a combination of 4-inch 
diameter continuous-flight solid-stem augers and continued with HQ or PQ-size wireline 
coring methods to advance the HQ or PQ-size casing. Sampling of the soils will be 
performed using the modified California spilt-barrel sampler (ASTM D3550) and the 
split-spoon sampler (ASTM D1586). In the softer soils, “relatively undisturbed” and 
“undisturbed” samples will be obtained for testing by driving a ring-lined modified 
California split-barrel sampler (ASTM D3550) or pushing a 3-inch outside diameter (OD) 
thin-walled steel Shelby tube (ASTM D1587) into the soil formation. 
Where the borehole will be advanced utilizing the HQ or PQ core barrel in the soil 
formations, samples of the soils recovered from the HQ or PQ core barrel also will be 
obtained and retained in core boxes for visual observation. In some cases, the borehole will 
be advanced by utilizing the HQ or PQ casing only as a rotary wash drilling method; 
therefore, there will be no soil samples retrieved between the soil sampling points (soil 
sampling by modified California split-barrel sampler or the split-spoon sampler for the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) at maximum 10-foot intervals). The borings will be 
advanced into the rock formations with HQ or PQ wireline coring methods. The HQ or PQ 
wireline coring methods will produce rock core samples that are 2.5 and 3.35 inches in 
diameter, respectively.

The bridge/pedestrian walkway deck will be cored prior to the drilling and sampling 
operations. The coring operations may be accomplished by the driller or by a specialty 
coring contractor, depending upon the type and thickness of the deck. Upon completion of

2.2 
Soil Sampling 
Soil sampling will be accomplished in the boreholes at 5-foot depth intervals. The sampling 
technique will consist of driving split-spoon samplers every 5-foot depth along the borehole. 
Thin-walled steel tube samples also may be retrieved instead of the disturbed driven samples. 
Depending upon the nature and consistency of the soils, the thin-walled tube samples may be 
replaced with a modified California sampler fitted with internal rings or brass liners driven with a 
140-pound hammer. Pocket penetrometer readings generally will be obtained and recorded by 
the field representative on retrieved cohesive soil samples where appropriate. Geolabs field 
representatives will observe the drilling, prepare a field log of the borings, collect soils samples, 
and transport the soil and/or rock samples to the sample storage facility.  
2.2.1 Grab Sampling 
Grab samples may be obtained from the soil cuttings and will be stored in plastic bags or 
jars, depending on the proposed geotechnical testing. 
2.2.2 Spilt-Spoon Sampling 
Split-spoon soil samples will be collected from each boring. The sample generally will be 
collected in association with the SPT. Soil samples also may be collected with a lined 
modified California split-barrel sampler using penetration test methods. 
Geotechnical soil samples will be collected from the split spoons and placed into plastic 
bags or jars. Multiple sample containers may be used for each sample, depending upon the 
nature and volume of the soil collected. The geotechnical samples will be stored in boxes 
or containers and transported to the sample storage facility.

2.2.2.a 
Standard Penetration Test Sampler 
Disturbed samples will be collected in association with the SPT using a 2-inch OD 
split-spoon sampler driven with a 140-pound hammer, free falling 30 inches. The 
sampler will be driven 18 inches with blows recorded for each 6 inches of 
penetration. If refusal is encountered, defined as 50 blows for 6 inches or less of 
penetration, a total of 100 blows are obtained, or 10 successive blows produce 
no advancement, the sampling operation will stop without advancing the full 
18 inches.  
2.2.2.b Modified California Sampler 
In granular soils that contain numerous gravels and cobbles, a modified 
California split sampler may be used to improve sample recovery. In addition to 
the coarse-grained soils, the modified California split sampler may be fitted with 
internal rings or brass liners and used in the hard, cohesive soils to collect soil 
samples for laboratory strength and consolidation testing. This generally would 
be accomplished if the cohesive material is hard and/or contains gravel such that 
using thin-walled tube sampling is not practical. 
The modified California sampler (3-inch OD, 2.42-inch inside diameter [ID]) will 
be driven with a 140-pound hammer, free falling 30 inches. The sampler will be 
driven 18 inches with blows recorded for each 6 inches of penetration. If refusal 
is encountered, defined as 75 blows for 6 inches or less of penetration, a total of 
150 blows are obtained, or 10 successive blows produce no advancement, the 
sampling operation will stop without advancing the full 18 inches. 
2.2.3 Relatively Undisturbed Sampling 
Relatively undisturbed samples will be collected within the soft soil formation. Collection of 
Shelby tube samples (3-inch OD, 2.87-inch ID) will be attempted in the soft silt and clay if 
encountered. In the very dense or hard cohesive soils, samples will be collected primarily 
using a modified California sampler driven with a 140-pound hammer.

2.2.4 Borehole Designations and Sample Numbering 
We have established a borehole designation and a soil sample numbering system for 
consistency in the project. The borings will generally be designated with an alphanumeric 
label such as “B-X” where: 
B – designates the exploration as a boring 
X – designates the boring number (1- to 3-digits) 
Drive samples will be numbered in sequential order for each boring, from the top down. 
When multiple samples are collected from a split spoon, the containers will be numbered 
with the sample number and then the container number (e.g., Sample SS-6, 1 of 3, 2 of 3, 
and 3 of 3). If a geologic discontinuity is noted in a split spoon, then the different soil types 
will be collected in separate containers and labeled with “A” and “B” (e.g., Samples SS-6A 
and SS-6B). The following designations will be used and recorded on the field log 
depending upon the sample type retrieved.

2.3.1 Field Coordination 
The following fieldwork will be coordinated before and during the exploration effort: 
1. 
Notify the client and affected parties prior to the start of borings. 
2. 
Once the borehole is located in the field, Geolabs will proceed with the utility 
clearance process with the Hawaii One Call Center. 
3. 
Coordinate with client to obtain access to boring locations. 
4. 
Coordinate and establish appropriate traffic control devices to assure maintenance 
of traffic and any necessary public right-of-way. 
5. 
Coordinate and perform deck coring operations, as required, prior to the drilling 
operations. The core hole will be covered with a secured, heavy steel plate to allow 
traffic mobility at the end of the workday if the boring has not been completed and 
backfilled. 
6. 
Upon completion of the drilling, the borehole will be properly backfilled and the 
deck will be patched.  
2.3.2 Health and Safety 
Prior to the start of drilling for the contract, the Project Manager will conduct a safety 
meeting with the field personnel, which would include the drillers, the drill helpers, field 
engineers, and geologists. The purpose of this meeting will be to identify potential 
site-specific hazards and to remind personnel to be aware of their surroundings. 
2.3.3 Best Management Practice 
Best Management Practice (BMP) planning serves to evaluate and address potential 
sources of sediment and other pollutants associated with the performance of the field 
geotechnical exploratory borings within the project site. It also provides controls that may 
minimize and/or prevent pollutant discharges to surface and groundwaters.   
The drilling will be confined within a steel casing to minimize and/or prevent discharge into 
the stream. In addition, a floating silt curtain device will be placed around the steel casing 
to provide secondary protection from discharge into the stream.

The drill rig will be surrounded by geotextile Snake Bags and/or BioSocks during drilling 
operations to contain the materials generated during the drilling and sampling operation. 
The geotextile Snake Bags and/or BioSocks will be monitored and maintained in good 
working order.  
2.3.4 Noise Control 
We do not anticipate that the proposed fieldwork will exceed the noise levels established 
by the Honolulu Noise Control Ordinance (NCO). If noise complaints are received from the 
public during the field activities, any or all of the following measures may be used in an 
attempt to further control noise: 
Reduce drill rig motor revolutions per minute (RPM). 
Place additional portable plywood sound walls around the drill rig, where feasible. 
Insulate the portable sound wall with noise blankets. 
If complaints continue to be received after implementing the above measures, the field 
coordinator will develop additional mitigation techniques. Noise monitoring, if performed, 
will use the dB(A) weighted range to evaluate the effectiveness of the noise control 
measures. 
2.3.5 Borehole Abandonment 
In general, each borehole will be backfilled with bentonite and/or grouted completely to 
near the ground surface. The open borehole within the bridge and pedestrian walkway 
deck will be backfilled with Quikrete.
