PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project sites are located along Mamalahoa Highway in the Waiohinu and
Naalehu areas in the District of Ka u on the Island of Hawaii The general location and
vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
Based on the information provided we understand that it is desired to improve the
existing drainage conditions at the Waiohinu approximately thru 65 3 and
Naalehu approximately areas along the Mamalahoa Highway The proposed
drainage improvements will consist of installing a drywell system for surface runoff
disposal constructing grated drop inlets and installing drainage culverts at these
locations In order to estimate the disposal capacity of the proposed drywell system
planned at each location in situ permeability testing will be conducted within a drilled
boring at depths of about 25 and 40 feet below the existing pavement surface

In general the basalt rock formation encountered at the sites may be considered
permeable rock and can transmit water quite readily in both the horizontal and vertical
directions Water is transmitted through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and
inter flow contacts cavities and along clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and
cavities is high therefore they serve as the major water transmission features However
it should be noted that the percolation rate in basalt rock formation will depend significantly
on the amount of fractures and the presence of clinker layers Therefore the actual
drywell disposal capacity may vary significantly from the calculated value since the
fracturing characteristics of the basalt rock formation may not be well represented in a
4 inch diameter borehole Therefore we strongly recommend testing the drywells during
construction to evaluate the actual disposal capacity of the installed drywells

Construction Considerations
Drywells on the Island of Hawaii are typically 5 feet in diameter and 20 feet in
depth The actual size and depth of the storm water runoff disposal system will depend on
the design quantity of the surface runoff Extension of the drywell from the planned 20 foot
depth may be required depending on the specific site conditions at the proposed drywell
locations Therefore we recommend obtaining unit prices for additional drilling during
bidding Provisions should be incorporated into the construction documents to extend the
drywell depths from the planned 20 feet to about 60 feet below the ground surface using a
minimum 15 inch diameter extension hole or 5 foot diameter drilling equipment The unit
prices for drywell extension should be obtained in 20 foot increments
In general the type of drilling operation may alter the hydraulic characteristics of the
formation materials at the edges of the borehole and may result in significant reduction of
the hydraulic conductivity of the drilled hole We envision that the drilling fluid may seal the
permeable surfaces of the drywell Therefore pressure washing or water jetting to remove
the seal should be included in the specifications if the drywells are to be installed by
drilling The contractor should forward the proposed excavation method and equipment to
be used on the project to Geolabs for evaluation and comments Geolabs should review
the submittal and assess the impact of drywell construction procedures on the permeability
characteristics
Due to the potential for encountering clinker materials during drilling consideration
should also be given to installing concrete rings in the clinker layers to reduce the potential
for collapse when saturated Unit prices for installing concrete rings should also be
obtained during bidding
The drywell installation and testing program should be conducted under the
observation of a Geolabs representative This would allow our firm to monitor the
subsurface conditions to evaluate the storm water disposal capacity and to allow for
additional recommendations to be made The drywell design recommendations presented
herein are contingent upon such observations and additional tests The owner client
should be aware that substantial variations in subsurface rock conditions may occur
Therefore a contingency fund is recommended to accommodate the potential extra costs
associated with these variations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the two highway drainage improvement sites were
explored by drilling and sampling two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2
extending to depths of about 40 feet below the existing pavement surface The
approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plans Plates 3 1 and
3 2 The borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with
continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered during drilling are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler or a 3 inch OD split barrel sampler with
a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6 inches a total of 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock formations encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and would
be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 foot core
run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
