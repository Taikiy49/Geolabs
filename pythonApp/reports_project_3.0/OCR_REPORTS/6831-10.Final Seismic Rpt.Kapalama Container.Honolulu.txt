1.0 INTRODUCTION
Earth Mechanics, Inc. (EMI) has performed a site-specific ground motion study for the New
Kapalama Container Terminal Wharf and Dredging Project in Honolulu Harbor, Hawaii. This
report documents the findings of the study and provides recommendations for seismic design of
the project.
A detailed seismic study was carried out for the project. The main objectives of this study were
as follows:
« Develop a site-specific seismic design criteria based on subsurface soil conditions
" Incorporate the next generation attenuation (NGA) ground motion prediction equations
« Evaluate the impact of the most recent seismic activity on the seismic hazard criteria
Based on the ASCE Seismic Standards for Piers and Wharves (ASCE 61-14) (ASCE, 2014), a
three-level seismic design criteria was developed for the project site. The criteria are based on
both probabilistic and deterministic seismic hazard evaluations. The analyses used the latest
available seismic source models and NGA relationships available for Hawaii.
The recommended seismic design criteria are presented as 5% damped acceleration response
spectra (ARS), adjusted for anticipated site soil conditions. In addition, peak ground acceleration
(PGA) values are estimated for each earthquake level. These values will be used in geotechnical
evaluations such as liquefaction potential, seismic settlement, lateral earth pressure, and seismic
slope deformations.
In addition to using three level seismic design criteria, the proposed bulkhead structures as part
of this project was also designed for the 2012 International Building Code (IBC 2012), as
directed by H-DOT. IBC 2012 is based on ASCE 7-10 (ASCE, 2011).
The seismic hazard analysis for the project is discussed in detail in the following section.
2.0 PROJECT LOCATION AND SEISMICITY
As part of The New Kapalama Container Terminal Project, the proposed improvements will
redevelop the former Kapalama Military Reservation property at Honolulu Harbor into a new
shipping container terminal to handle current and projected cargo volumes. The project site is
located in the Honolulu Harbor, on the south end of the island of Oahu. The approximately 90-
acre Kapalama site is bounded on the west by Sand Island Access Road, on the north by Auiki
Street, on the east by Young Brothers’ inter-island barge terminal, and on the south by the harbor
waters Kapalama Basin. Existing vehicular access is via Sand Island Access Road and Auiki
Street. Site coordinates are 21.3182° N, 157.8830° W, measured from Google Earth. Figure 1
shows the site location.
The project site is in a region with high seismic activity. The magnitude and rate of occurrence of
earthquakes in some parts of Hawaii Islands are comparable to the areas close to the largest
Geotechnical & Earthquake Engineering
faults in California. However, the mechanism of the earthquakes in Hawaii is different. The
largest earthquakes occur due to mobile active flanks of the volcanos on the island of Hawaii.
The south side of the island has the highest seismic/volcanic activity.
Historical earthquake epicenter maps show widespread seismicity throughout the Hawaii Islands.
The largest historical earthquakes in the islands were two earthquakes in 1868 with intensity-area
magnitude M; of 7.0 and 7.9, and a 1975 earthquake with teleseismic surface wave magnitude
Msg of 7.2. Figure 2 shows the locations of the largest earthquakes in the region. Since 1868
approximately fifty earthquakes with moment magnitude M,, of 6 or larger have been recorded.
Most of M,, 6 and larger earthquakes occurred on the south side of the island of Hawaii.
Seismicity decreases considerably along the island chain toward northwest of the island of
Hawaii.
As part of the National Seismic Hazard Map program, the United States Geological Survey
(USGS) has published seismic hazard estimates for various regions in the United States since
1996 (Frankel et al., 1996). The hazard maps are based on probabilistic seismic hazard analysis
(PSHA) methodology. Seismic hazard maps for the 48 conterminous states have been published
in 1996, and updated in 2002 and 2008 (Petersen et al., 2008). The latest update in 2008
incorporates NGA models in PSHA. For Hawaii, the maps were initially published in 1998
(Klein et al., 1998), and have not been updated since. The 1998 maps provide estimated peak
ground acceleration (PGA) and spectral accelerations at 0.2, 0.3, and 1.0 second, for 475- and
2,475-yr return periods. These maps were developed for firm-rock site condition, equivalent to
an average shear wave velocity in top 30 m (V,30) of 760 m/s.
Klein et al. (2001) paper on seismic hazard in Hawaii includes detailed discussion on seismic
source model, earthquake catalog and attenuation relationships used by USGS for developing
1998 maps. This study was performed using an earthquake catalog spanning from 1868 to 1997,
and mainly relied on attenuation equations from 1997 USGS national seismic hazard study.
The current study is based on the general methodology and the source model described by Klein
et al. (2001), however it incorporates a more recent earthquake catalog and the latest ground
motion prediction equations (GMPE) in the analyses.

3.0 SEISMIC HAZARD ANALYSIS
3.1 Recommended Seismic Design Criteria
The recommended design ground motion criteria for this project are consistent with the three
level seismic criteria specified in ASCE Standards for Seismic Design of Piers and Wharves
(ASCE, 2014), which is presently under preparation. The three earthquake levels are: Operating
Level Earthquake (OLE), Contingency Level Earthquake (CLE), and Design Earthquake (DE).
OLE and CLE are based on a probabilistic criteria; OLE has a 50% probability of exceedance in
50 years (72-yr. return period), and CLE has a 10% probability of exceedance in 50 years (475-
yr. return period). DE is consistent with ASCE 7-05 (ASCE, 2006), which is adopted in IBC
2009, the code adopted by the City and County of Honolulu.
DE response spectra for Site Class A to E conditions can be based on either the mapped
acceleration parameters or site-specific hazard evaluations. Mapped acceleration parameters are
determined from the 0.2 sec. and 1.0 sec. spectral accelerations provided in maps for the entire
U.S. in Chapter 22 of ASCE 7-05 (ASCE, 2006). However, ARS for Site Class F must be based
on site-specific evaluations, per Section 11.4.7 of ASCE 7-05 (ASCE, 2006). For this study, a
site-specific procedure in accordance with Chapter 21 of the ASCE 7-05 (ASCE, 2006) is used.
The procedure for developing DE ARS is as follows:
The site-specific DE ARS is defined as two-thirds of the Maximum Considered Earthquake
(MCE) spectral response acceleration, but not less than 80% of the acceleration response
determined for the same site class in accordance with Section 11.4.5 of the ASCE 7-05 (ASCE,
2006). The MCE response acceleration at each period is the lesser of probabilistic and
deterministic MCE response accelerations. The probabilistic MCE response acceleration
corresponds to a 5 percent damped acceleration response spectrum having a 2 percent probability
of exceedance within a 50-yr. period (2,475-yr. return period). The deterministic MCE response
acceleration at each period is 150% of the largest median 5 percent damped spectral response
acceleration calculated at that period for characteristic earthquake on all known active faults
within the region, but not less than the deterministic lower limit on the MCE ARS determined in
accordance with Figure 21.2-1 of the ASCE 7-05 (ASCE, 2006).
The recommendations in this study are based on a site-specific PSHA for the site. Seismic hazard
is evaluated as a 5% damped ARS at 72-yr., 475-yr., and 2,475-yr. return periods, adjusted for
site soil conditions. The 72-yr. and 475-yr. ARS are recommended for OLE and CLE levels,
respectively. The 2,475-yr. ARS is used as probabilistic MCE ARS, as defined in Chapter 21 of
the ASCE 7-05 (ASCE, 2006). The DE spectra was determined using the combination of
deterministic MCE ARS and ASCE lower limit ARS.
3.2. Probabilistic Seismic Hazard Analysis
The PSHA follows the standard approach first developed by Cornell (1968). This approach has
been expanded to more fully treat both the randomness (aleatory variability) and the scientific
uncertainty (epistemic uncertainty). The methodology used in this study is similar to the
procedure previously used by USGS (Klein et al., 1998 and 2001), with updated ground motion
prediction equations and earthquake catalog. .
The PSHA was performed using the computer program HAZ43, developed by Dr. Norm
Abrahamson (2013). The input parameters to PSHA, including earthquake sources, frequency-
magnitude distribution, site soil conditions, and GMPEs are discussed in this section.
3.2.1 Earthquake Sources
Earthquake sources in Hawaii are discussed in detail in Klein et al (1998 and 2001). Earthquakes
on Hawaii Islands are mainly generated by the following two mechanisms:
" Mobile flanks of the active volcanos Kilauea, Mauna Loa, and Hualalai: These volcanos
grow due to lava flow from caldera and rift zones and expand toward the sea. Mobile
flanks adjacent to active rift zones store the energy in form of compressive stress and
release it in seaward slips. These sources generate the largest earthquakes on Hawaii
Islands.
« Flexure of the lithosphere and volcanic pile under volcanic load: the entire island chain
produces earthquakes in this manner. Seismicity decreases with aging of volcano and
decrease in volcanic activity.
Using the earthquake catalog and aftershock data in combination with geometry of the flanks,
Klein et al. (1998 and 2001) developed a model for tectonic source zones on the islands. In this
model seismic sources are modeled as area sources with uniform or smoothed seismicity. Several
types of area sources have been used in this model, as shown in Table 1. The geographical
location of these area sources are shown on Figure 3.

3.2.2 Frequency-Magnitude Distribution
In order to perform a PSHA, the frequency-magnitude distribution of earthquakes in each
seismic source must be determined. Unlike shallow crustal earthquakes that follow a
characteristic distribution, the frequency-magnitude of earthquakes on Hawaii Island area
sources follows the Gutenberg-Richter distribution:
log(N) = a-—bM
where N is the number of earthquakes of magnitude greater than or equal to M. a- and b-values
for each region are estimated based on the number of previous earthquakes in that region. A
maximum likelihood or least square regression method is used to determine a- and b-values.
The frequency of larger earthquakes in some flank regions is larger than that predicted by a
linear Gutenberg-Richter magnitude distribution. In these regions, a bi-linear or kinked
magnitude-frequency distribution have been used, where each magnitude range has a different
set of a- and b-values. More details on this topic can be found in Klein et al. (2001).
For current study, the source parameters are generally identical to the values used by Klein et al.
(2001). Source parameters used in this study are shown in Table 2. Since the project site is
located within the NW region and the second closest source is more than 150 km away, it is
expected that the hazard is dominated by shallow earthquakes in NW region, and other sources
will have only minimal impact at the site. Therefore, for this analysis, source parameters for NW
region has been revised using the latest earthquake data, while for other regions source
parameters are identical to Klein et al. (2001) model with some simplifications. The revisions to
the source model of Klein et al. are as follows:
= a- and b-values for NW region are revised using an extended earthquake catalog. The 2001
model relies on 1868-1997 events. In this study 1868-2013 events were included.
= Ramped seismicity is implemented by dividing NW to 10 area sources with identical b-
values and decreasing a-values from southeast to northwest. These sources are identified
as R1 (southeast end) to R10 (northwest end) in the analyses.
" For other areas with smoothed seismicity in 2001 model (s1 to s7, and dl to d6), uniform

consideration for variation of seismicity in these regions unnecessary. The a-values for
the whole region as reported by Klein et al. have been used in the analyses.

3.2.3 Site Classification for Ground Motion
Site soils are classified as surface fill, lagoonal deposits,and coralline deposits. Liquefaction
hazard assessment at the site indicates that shallower granular soils at the site have potential for
liquefaction under CLE and DE conditions. However, ground improvement will be performed at
the container wharf site to mitigate liquefaction potential. Therefore, the site has been classified
for improved ground conditions, using SPT blowcounts. The average measured N-value in the
top 100-ft is about 30.
Therefore, for improved ground conditions, the site is classified as Type D with average shear
wave velocities of 270 m/sec (~900 ft/sec) over a depth of 30 m (#100 ft). This shear wave
velocity value was used in the PSHA.

GMPE which included Hawaii data in empirical regression was by Munson and Thurber (1997).

in subduction zones was used.
Since the USGS study, the PEER/Lifelines Next Generation Attenuation (NGA) project has
resulted in four new GMPEs by Abrahamson and Silva (2008), Chiou and Youngs (2008),
Campbell and Bozorgnia (2008), and Boore and Atkinson (2008). These new models are based
on a greatly expanded and improved empirical database. In particular, there is a great increase in
the number of recordings from large magnitude earthquakes resulting from the 1999 Kocaeli
(M7.5), 1999 Chi-Chi (M7.6), 1999 Duzce (M7.1), 2000 Hector Mine (M7.1), and 2002 Denali
(M7.9) events. Nowadays these NGA equations are widely used for shallow crustal earthquakes.
Atkinson (2010) evaluated the applicability of Boore and Atkinson (2008) equation to Hawaii
earthquakes. This study appears to be the most recent research on the subject of GMPEs for
Hawaii region. Atkinson developed a modified GMPE based on Boore and Atkinson (2008) for
both shallow and deep Hawaii earthquakes, and concluded that NGA equations for shallow
crustal earthquakes are applicable to shallow earthquakes, but deep events in Hawaii generally
have higher SA amplitudes than what predicted by NGA equations. Figure 5 compares the ARS
for shallow (h < 20 km) and deep (h > 35 km) events from Atkinson 2010 Hawaii GMPE to that
of NGA western US (Boore and Atkinson, 2008). The comparison is for a M 7.0 event, at
Joyner-Boore distance (Rj») of 10 km.
Based on this research, the following GMPEs have been used in the current project:
= Shallow sources (h < 20 km): The Hawaii GMPE by Atkinson (2010), as well as NGA
GMPEs by Abrahamson and Silva (2008), Chiou and Youngs (2008), and Campbell and
Bozorgnia (2008) are used. The Atkinson (2010) GMPE has a weight of 40%, and the
rest have a weight of 20% each.
= Deep sources (h > 20 km): The GMPE by Atkinson (2010) for deep earthquakes in Hawaii
is used exclusively.

3.2.6 Deaggregation of Seismic Hazard
Probabilistic seismic hazard deaggregation results are provided in Appendix A. For PGA, there

sources are M 5.0—7.0 earthquakes at distances of 10 to 500 km. For the 475-yr return period, the

3.3 Deterministic MCE ARS
The deterministic MCE response acceleration at each period is 150% of the largest median 5
percent damped spectral response acceleration calculated at that period for characteristic
earthquake on all known active faults within the region, but not less than the deterministic lower
limit on the MCE ARS determined in accordance with Figure 21.2-1 of the ASCE 7-05 (ASCE,
2006).

3.6 Seismic Hazard Levels per IBC 2012
In addition to using IBC 2009, the proposed bulkhead structures as part of this project were also
designed for IBC 2012, as directed by H-DOT. IBC 2012 is based on ASCE 7-10 (ASCE, 2011).
Per Section 11.8.3.1 of ASCE 7-10 (ASCE, 2011), if the site is improved to eliminate
liquefaction under MCE level earthquake, DE ground motion can be used in the determination of
dynamic seismic lateral earth pressure on bulkhead structures. However, if liquefaction is not
mitigated, the effects of liquefaction under MCE level earthquake must be accommodated in the
design.
Per Section 11.8.3.2 of ASCE 7-10 (ASCE, 2011), PGA values should be determined based on
either (1) a site-specific study or (2) the PGA adjusted for site class effects using site coefficient
provided in Table 11.8-1 in ASCE 7-10 (ASCE, 2011). Using the adequate site coefficient, the
PGA for improved ground conditions for MCE level is recommended to be 0.359g.
Corresponding equivalent earthquake magnitude is 7.5.
As defined, DE is two-third of MCE and the corresponding PGA for DE level based on ASCE 7-
10 (ASCE, 2011) is 0.239g. However, since the PGA for DE level based on ASCE 61-14
(ASCE, 2014) is 0.255g, we recommend using this value, instead of 0.239g, for the project as a
conservative measure.
