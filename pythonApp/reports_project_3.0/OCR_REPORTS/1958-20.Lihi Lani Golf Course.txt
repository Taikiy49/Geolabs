INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our preliminary geotechnical engineering
exploration and field percolation testing performed for the design of detention basins
drainage system and the golf course mass grading of the proposed Lihi Lani Golf Course
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
Our work on the project was performed in general accordance with our proposal
dated and our additional proposal dated March 17 1992 This report
summarizes the findings of our field exploration and percolation testing and presents our
preliminary geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from analyses of the
preliminary data Since our exploration was limited and conducted on a preliminary basis
subsurface soil conditions and percolation characteristics could vary The
recommendations presented herein are intended for preliminary design input and may not
be adequate to form the basis of preparation of final construction cost estimates or for
bidding purposes
This report supplements our progress report dated March 31 1992 which
presented our preliminary findings and design recommendations for the project Should

The purpose of our work on the project is to obtain a soil rock data set to form
the basis for formulation of geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the
design of the mass grading detention basins and drainage systems for the golf course

1 Obtaining an overview of the general soil conditions which will be
encountered during mass grading operations

the design of the detention basins and drainage systems

the absorption characteristics of the deeper subsoiis

GENERAL SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is situated in the Pupukea area on the north shore of the island of
Oahu Hawaii The site encompasses approximately 1 150 acres of land area
Ground surface elevations range from about 20 feet above Mean Sea Level MSL
at the westerly boundary along Kamehameha Highway to about 850 feet MSL in the
southeasterly portion of the site The majority of the land area is in the upper portion of
the site above Pupukea Bluffs and Paumalu Bluffs which are steep nearly vertical slopes
which rise from an elevation of about 50 feet MSL in the lower portion of the site to about
400 feet MSL
The upper portion of the site generally slopes towards the west at an average
slope of about ten horizontal to one vertical 10H 1V The upper area is traversed by
several drainageways with locally steep slopes approaching two horizontal to one vertical
2H 1V or steeper

The land has been used for agricultural purposes such as cultivation of sugar cane
and pasturage The current vegetation cover ranges from open pasture to dense brush
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regionai Geoiogy
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western one third 1 3 of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds 2 3 of the island
The project site is located on the northwesterly flank of Koolau and the
geomorphology of the area is related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of sea level during the
Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age
The Koolau volcanic shield was built during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene
Epochs by thin bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities ceased
approximately 2 5 to 3 5 million years ago
During the Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the oceans fell since there was less water available to
fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted giobal sea leveis
rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio
eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower
relative to present sea level at Oahu
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1958 20 Page 7
The higher sea level stands allowed wave action to attack higher elevations causing
headward erosion of steep sea cliffs Pupukea Bluffs and Paumalu Bluff are two 2 of
these old sea cliffs
The action of chemical weathering on the in situ basalts at the top of the cliff has
resulted in the formation of a deep layer of residual soil which grades with depth to
saprolite i e soil that retains the structure of the parent rock from which it is derived

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Generai
Based on our exploration and analyses it is our opinion that the development of
the proposed goif course project is feasible from a geotechnical engineering point of view
The soils encountered at the site are generally competent and should provide adequate
bearing for the site improvements which will form part of the proposed development The
excavated materials should provide a suitable source of general fill material Some hard
excavation and or ripping may be encountered if localized rock ledges are encountered
or when bouldery horizons are encountered
As discussed above permeability and percolation tests have been conducted in
excavated test pits and drilled borings to provide information for the design of detention
basins and for injection wells for the disposal of storm runoff
Permeability Testing
Our permeability testing indicates that the upper 15 feet of the surface soils
generally have moderate to low permeability In general our field testing indicated that
the permeability of the upper soil ranges from 10 to Based on these
results the upper soils may provide some initial absorption for the proposed shallow
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1958 20 Page 9
detention basins However continued saturation during prolonged periods of precipitation
may decrease their absorption capacity
Percolation Testing
Percolation testing conducted in the drilled borings indicates that the deeper soils
beyond fifteen 15 feet depth appear to have lower permeability values which range from
10 to Generally these values may be considered too low for satisfactory
performance of injection wells for the disposal of the quantities of water which would be
associated with storm runoff
We suggest that consideration be given to alternate shallow disposal methods such
as but not limited to infiltration trenches Based on a review of the U S Federal Highway
Administration Design Guidelines Manual for underground Disposal of Storm Water
Runoff it is believed that large infiltration trenches could be installed in open areas such
as the golf course fairways
These trenches would provide additional storage and infiltration capacity to
supplement the proposed detention basins A conceptual sketch of such an infiltration
trench is shown on Plate 4
These trenches should be installed in cut areas or in natural ground below fills
It is our opinion that such infiltration trenches should not be constructed in fill
embankments to minimize the potential of softening the fill
Ponds and Water Features
It is anticipated that the proposed golf course development may involve ponds and
water features The field permeability tests conducted in excavated test pits during the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1958 20 Page 10
preliminary field exploration indicate that the subsoils underlying the site have low to
moderate long term infiltration characteristics
Consideration may be given to using synthetic flexible membranes to reduce the
loss of water from the static levels of the ponds through infiltration into the soil
To reduce the potential of punctures in the flexible membranes a cushion layer of
sand is recommended for the bottoms and sides of the ponds and the water features
The cushion should be installed prior to the placement of the flexible membrane and
should be a minimum of six 6 inches thick
Although the flexible membranes are considered to be impervious there is a
possibility of seepage due to punctures in the liner or breaks in the seams Leakage
through the membrane could potentially cause piping losses or weakening of the
subgrade soils Piping losses could result in loss of subgrade support leading to liner
failure Weakening of the subgrade soils could cause loss of stability in impoundment
slopes potentially leading to slope failure and or liner failure Therefore we recommend
that a subdrain system be installed below the sand cushion to relieve potential leakage
water
Our experience with similar liner installations indicates that there is sometimes a
tendency for flexible liners to become detached at the top of the water feature or
impoundment It appears that this is may be the result of inadequate anchoring of the
top of the liner and insufficient flexibility of the liner material It is our recommendation that
the liner manufacturer be consulted regarding proper installation of the liners with
particular attention to proper construction detailing and anchoring of the liner to reduce
the potential for liner failure Effects of fluctuating water levels on the performance of the
impervious liner near the top anchoring should also be evaluated
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1958 20 Page 11
A technical representative of the manufacturer of the impervious liner should be
required to be present on site at all times during their installation to assure that proper
construction procedures and precautions are followed A sales representative of the
supplier may not have the technical expertise to adequately perform this function
Site Grading
In general based on the results of our exploration it appears that conventional
earthwork methods may be used for construction of the detention basins and for the golf
course grading work
Detailed grading plans were not available for the project at the time of preparation
of this report The following recommendations pertaining to site grading are of a general
nature and may need to be modified if significant amounts of grading are required for the
final site development plan
Site Preparation
Prior to the commencement of grading all areas within the grading limits should
be cleared and grubbed of the existing cover of trees shrubs and other vegetation
This material should either be disposed of properly off site or the material should
be shredded chipped and stockpiled for use in landscaping
During our field exploration it was noted that the near surface soils are relatively
dry Due to the dry and sometimes windy site conditions moisture conditioning
of fill material will be necessary Because of the silty soil conditions it is also
advisable to institute a dust control program during grading operations
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1958 20 Page 12
Following clearing and grubbing of the site in areas designated to receive fill the
ground surface should generally be scarified brought to near optimum moisture
content and proofrolled with a heavy vibratory roller
Soft or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing and or by the
proof rolling operation which are located below areas to receive fill should be over
excavated to firm natural material and the resulting depression backfilled with well
compacted engineered fill
Fill Placement
in general the excavated soil materials should be suitable for use as fill material
Any boulders or cobbles larger than twelve 12 inches in maximum dimension
should be separated from the excavated material and either disposed of off site or
be placed in the deeper fills as discussed below
The material should be adequate as a source of trench and structural backfill for
the upper portion a minimum of one foot above the utility pipes if needed Trench
and structural backfill should not contain any rock or lumps larger than six 6
inches in maximum dimension
Imported material if required should be non expansive with no particles greater
than six 6 inches in size and no more than fifteen 15 percent passing the 200
sieve It is recommended that if additional off site borrow is required it should be
tested by the geotechnical engineer to determine if it is suitable for its intended use
prior to its delivery to the job site
GEOLABS HAWAIl
W O 1958 20 Page 13
Large boulders or rock fragments should be buried in the deepest portions of the
fills provided that they are not nested and an adequate amount of compacted finer
grained soil material is placed between the boulders to reduce the potential of loss
of fines from the upper portion of the fill No boulder larger than five 5 feet in its
largest dimension should be placed in the fill It may be necessary to reduce the
size of larger boulders or dispose of them off site Care must be exercised to
avoid placement of any boulders in locations which would conflict with future utility
lines In general boulder or rock fragments larger than twelve 12 inches in size
should be placed at depths greater than four 4 feet below finish subgrade level
No boulders or rocks may be used in structural fill area and no boulder or rock
fragments greater than six 6 inches in size may be placed within two 2 feet of
finish grade in mass grading areas
Structural and trench fills or backfills required for the project site grading should
be placed in level lifts not exceeding eight 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent maximum dry density as determined in
accordance with procedures described by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 The
upper two 2 feet below finish subgrade of trench backfills in paved areas should
be placed in level lifts not exceeding eight 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent maximum dry density as determined in
accordance with procedures described by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
The compaction requirement for golf course grading may be modified Fills for
non structural golf course areas may be placed in level lifts not exceeding twelve
12 inches in loose thickness and should be compacted to a minimum of 85
percent maximum dry density as determined in accordance with procedures
described by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78

Slopes
In general design slope ratios of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V may be
used for the design of cut slopes and fill slopes compacted to 90 percent maximum dry
density In areas where the compaction requirements have been modified to 85 percent
maximum dry density a maximum slope ratio of three horizontal to one vertical 8H 1V
should be considered for the design of these fill slopes
Fills to be placed on slopes having ratios steeper than five horizontal to one vertical
5H 1V should be keyed and benched into firm soil or in situ rock formation to lessen
potential instability of the new fill see Plate 3 The filling operations should start at the
lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the
above recommendations for fill placement
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope
ratio Care should be exercised to avoid over cutting on cut slopes in order to avoid the
placement of sliver fills i e thin sloping fills on the slope faces in an attempt to repair the
over cut area
In the event over cutting occurs consideration should be given to leaving the slope
rather than attempting to backfill the slope to design grade If backfilling of an over cut
slope is required keying and benching requirements should be implemented
Silty soils are susceptible to erosion when exposed particularly in slope areas
Water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and slope planting utilized to
reduce the potential of erosion and or instability of the finished slopes
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1958 20 Page 15
In general midpoint slope drainage benches with a minimum width of eight 8 feet
should be provided for fill slopes and cut slopes steeper than three horizontal to one
vertical 3H 1V and having vertical heights greater than 20 feet
Excavation
General
The field exploration and visual observations made at and in the vicinity of the site
indicate that the upper portion of the site is most likely underlain by competent
residual soils and saprolites at relatively shallow depths
In general it is our opinion that conventional excavation methods may be used for
site grading However localized ledges or bouldery horizons may be present in
the residual soils and saprolites If these are encountered it is anticipated that
hard excavation and or ripping may be required to enable removal of the material
Generally hard excavation is considered to be techniques such as heavy ripping
hoerams or splitting In general the contractor should be given as much leeway
as possible in the choice of equipment and methods used for excavation

Bluffs indicated that the proposed access road would be excavated into existing
soil and rocks forming the bluffs
In our progress report entitled Progress Report Preliminary Geotechnical

1988 it was observed that the steepest portions of the bluff are in situ basaltic rock

It is anticipated that underground utilities such as water sewer drain and irrigation

trenching However bouldery horizons or isolated rock ledges may be encountered in
the deeper trenches
In general the subsurface materials encountered at the site are relatively
competent and would require minimal shoring However areas of loose sand or gravel
may be encountered Trench shoring design and installation should be made the sole
GEOLABS HAWAII

Foundation Support
Laboratory testing of selected representative samples of materials encountered
during our exploration indicates that the on site soils generally have moderate expansion
potential It is our opinion that relatively shallow spread or strip footing foundations may
be used to support structures Alternately thickened edge slabs on grade may be used
for lightly loaded structures For preliminary estimating an allowable bearing capacity of
2 500 p s f may be used for design of structure foundations
Further exploration and testing is recommended when structure locations have
been established Select non expansive cushion fill may be required to minimize the
potential of foundation distresses resulting from swelling of the moderately expansive
soils

Placement and Compaction of Fill
The fill shall be placed in horizontal layers not exceeding eight inches 8
in loose thickness Each layer shall be spread uniformly and blade mixed
to attain uniformity of material and water content within each layer
Additional fill material shall not be placed on any fill layer which has not
been properly compacted Subsequent removal of any unacceptable layer
shall be done at the Contractor s expense
Rocks greater than 12 inches in diameter shall be removed from the fill
unless specifically approved for placement in the fill by the Geotechnical
Engineer
All fill shall be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent of the maximum dry
density determined in accordance with procedures described in by ASTM
Test Method D 1557 78 unless otherwise specified in the Geotechnical
Engineer s Report
Boulders encountered in excavations may be used in the deeper fills
provided that the following are followed
1 Boulders shall be spread out and must not be nested together They
should be placed so that compaction equipment will be able to
compact around them
2 No part of the bouider should enroach any closer to finished
subgrade than the boulder s largest dimension and in no case be
closer than four 4 feet to finished subgrade

3 No boulder larger than 5 feet in its largest dimension should be
placed in the fill It may be necessary to reduce the size of larger
boulders or dispose of them off site
4 Care must be exercised to avoid placement of boulders in the
proposed utility alignments to prevent difficulty in the later excavation
of the utility trenches
5 No boulders or rocks may be used in structural fill area
