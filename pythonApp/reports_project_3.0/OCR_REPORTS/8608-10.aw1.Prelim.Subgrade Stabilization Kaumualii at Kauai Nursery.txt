The following contains our preliminary geotechnical engineering recommendations
for pavement subgrade stabilization for the Kaumualii Highway Intersection Improvements
at Kauai Nursery project on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii.
The preliminary geotechnical recommendations presented herein are based on our
recently completed field Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) tests, pending the results
from our ongoing laboratory testing program. A detailed summary of our findings and
recommendations will be contained in our geotechnical engineering letter report for the
project. The final letter report should be consulted when it becomes available.
Project Considerations
The project consists of widening the existing Kaumualii Highway in the vicinity of
the commercial operations of Kauai Nursery between Mileposts 2.40 and 2.57 on the
Island of Kauai, Hawaii. We understand it is proposed to widen the existing two-lane
undivided highway to include a center left turn and merging lane. In general, the
widened highway will have two 11-foot wide lanes with approximately 6-foot wide
shoulders on the sides of each travel direction and an 11-foot wide center left turn and
merging lane.

Transportation, Kauai Division email dated February 17, 2023, we understand that in
2020, an ADT of 29,000 was determined and used to develop a pavement section for
the project reflected in the current project plans consisting of the following:

We understand that the high in-situ moisture was encountered in the subgrade at 
the project site, and it is desired to evaluate the existing surface and subsurface conditions 
along the roadway widening project site and provide recommendations for pavement 
subgrade stabilization.  
Subsurface Conditions 
The subsurface conditions at the project site outside of the existing pavements 
were explored by performing seven DCP tests, designated as DCP Nos. 1 through 7, 
extending to depths ranging from about 4.3 to 8.9 feet below the existing ground surface. 
In addition, two bulk samples of the near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1 and Bulk-2, 
were obtained to evaluate the engineering characteristics of the near-surface soils. Lab 
testing was not completed at the time of preparation of this technical memorandum. 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is underlain by a relatively thin fill 
materials underlain by residual soils. The fill soils were not explored during our site 
investigation but are believed to be from the existing construction of Kaumualii Highway. 
The residual soils consisted of very soft to stiff silty clay with gravel and clayey silt. We 
believe that our DCP tests penetrated these soils to the maximum penetration depth of 
8.9 feet below the existing ground surface. We are not able to determine if groundwater is 
encountered during DCP testing. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are 
subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, 
and other factors. The approximate boring, DCP, and bulk sample locations are shown on 
the Site Plans, Plates 1.1 and 1.2.

Subgrade Strength Characterization 
Dynamic Cone Penetration tests were performed at seven locations in general 
accordance with ASTM D6951 to obtain indicative subsoil strength characteristics such as 
California Bearing Ratio (CBR). The tests were generally performed in the unpaved 
shoulder between depths of about 5.1 and 8.9 feet below the existing ground surface 
except for DCP-5, where a 3 feet deep pit was excavated for a bulk sample, and the DCP 
test was conducted in the bottom of the pit to a depth of 4.3 feet, for a total depth of 
7.3 feet below existing grade. 
The Dynamic Cone Penetrometer device consists of two 16mm-diameter stainless 
steel rods with the lower rod containing an anvil, a replaceable 60 pointed tip, and depth 
markings. The upper rod holds a 17.6-pound drop hammer with a 22.6-inch drop distance. 
At the beginning of a test, the cone is “seated” by dropping the hammer until the widest 
part of the cone is just below the testing surface at which point the initial penetration is 
recorded as zero. The operator then lifts and drops the hammer one or more times 
depending on the strength of the soil at the test location. Following each sequence of 
hammer drops, a penetration reading is taken. The process is continued until the desired 
depth of testing is reached or the full length of the lower rod is buried. 
Data from our DCP testing is presented as the penetration index, which is the 
penetration distance of the cone for each drop of the hammer expressed in inches per 
blow. The penetration indices obtained from our tests were correlated with a common 
pavement design parameter California Bearing Ratio (CBR). The penetration indices and 
correlated CBR values are presented on the attached DCP field logs in Appendix A. 
Based on the above results, the field exploration results appear to indicate that the 
on-site soils generally had relatively low to moderate CBR values between 1.2 and 24.9 
within a few feet below the proposed pavement structure.

Based on a design period of 30 years, the traffic volume, and the assumed truck 
traffic distribution, a Traffic Index (TI) of 13.5 has been determined for the analysis of 
the pavement subgrade stabilization. 
Based on the results of our field exploration, the pavement subgrades along the 
proposed new roadway alignments are generally underlain by very soft to stiff silty clays 
with gravel and clayey silts. Based on the correlated CBR test results from the field DCP 
tests, a design CBR value of 1.2 with a subgrade of resilient modulus (MR) of 
1,800 pounds per square inch (psi) or R-Value of 1.2 were adopted in our pavement 
analyses along the proposed roadway alignments. In addition, asphaltic concrete (AC) 
and asphaltic concrete base (ACB) thicknesses were not modified from the original 
flexible pavement design.

Our field exploration and laboratory testing indicated that the majority of the 
roadway alignment under consideration for this project is generally underlain by overly 
saturated clayey soils. As a result, the subgrade may not be able to compact into a 
dense mass to receive placement and compaction of the aggregate subbase layer of 
the recommended pavement reconstruction section. Based on our evaluation and these 
construction considerations, we recommend providing a subgrade stabilization layer 
consisting of a 14-inch-thick layer of No. 2 Rock (ASTM C33 No.4) underlain by Tensar 
InterAx Grid UX850 or equivalent wrapped in a permeable separator (Mirafi 180N or 
equivalent) on relatively undisturbed subgrade. In general, the triaxial geogrid will 
interlock with the aggregate fill, resulting in two benefits during initial construction and 
for the life of the project: 1) lateral confinement – increasing the modulus of the 
aggregate fills, and 2) subgrade bearing capacity enhancement.  
The exposed subgrade materials below the pavement subgrade stabilization 
layer should be excavated utilizing a flat bucket such that the resulting pavement 
subgrade is relatively smooth and drains to the shoulder of the pavement. A Geolabs 
representative should monitor the pavement subgrade preparation to observe whether 
undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation process and to confirm 
whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar to those encountered in our field 
exploration. The No. 2 Rock should be placed in lifts no greater than 12 inches and 
proof-rolled with a large smooth drum roller (minimum 10-ton static weight) a minimum 
of four passes without vibratory action prior to wrapping the non-woven geotextile around 
the stabilization layer. If the pavement subgrade stabilization layer continues to pump after 
the proof-rolling operation on the final lift of No. 2 Rock, an additional layer of Tensar 
InterAx Grid UX850 or equivalent should be installed between the revised flexible 
pavement design section and stabilization layer. 
Based on the full-scale performance test results and the white paper entitled 
“Evaluation of using Tensar InterAx® Geogrid for Improved Pavement Support 
Characteristics Below Paved Roads” by Tensar, we believe that the R-value of the 
stabilized subgrade may be enhanced from a value of 1.2 to 35 (conservative side). The 
referenced Tensar documents can be found in Appendix C. Based on this enhanced 
R-value of the subgrade soils, it is our opinion that the following revised flexible pavement 
design section may be used for the Kaumualii Highway Intersection Improvements at 
Kauai Nursery project. Detailed calculations are presented in Appendix B.

Aggregate subbase materials required for the project should consist of crushed 
basaltic aggregates and should conform to Subsection 703.17 of the State of Hawaii, 
Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction (2005). The aggregate 
subbase material should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture 
content, placed in lifts no larger than 8 inches, and compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with 
AASHTO T-180 or ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage 
by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Asphalt concrete base material should consist of asphalt-treated basalt 
aggregate, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and 
compacted to no less than 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity 
determined in accordance with AASHTO T-209 or ASTM D2041. Asphaltic concrete 
material should be constructed in accordance with Section 401 Asphalt Concrete 
Pavement of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge 
Construction (2005) and its amendments. 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to 
saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain, and drainage gradients should be maintained 
to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. Surface water 
ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development of good shoulder 
drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage structures is 
strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration or premature 
failure of the pavements.

Here was our RFI to the state for the Kauai Nursery soil: 
Due to high moisture content in the on-site soil proctor, GP is concerned that the dirt subbase will not meet 
be able to meet compaction. GP proposes to excavate an additional 24" before laying down geogrid and 
fabric. We would then fill back, either 24" with excavated materials to original 33" depth then the 21" 
subbase course per the pavement section details, or the entire 45" with subbase course, whichever the 
state approves. Are there alternate methods that the state is considering for this issue? 
Here was their response: 
We take no exception to your proposed method herein RFI 10 and/or as directed by the Engineer. The 
relative compaction of the material shall be in accordance with Subsection 203.03(C)(3) of the 2005 Hawaii 
Std Spec. As for payment, we prefer to utilize the existing pay item at the agreed unit price in which the 
additional verified quantities & amounts will be done by a Contract Change Order. Also, we request a trial 
section to observe your means and methods for the high moisture on-site soil to determine the most 
efficient and practical method at no additional cost to the State.
