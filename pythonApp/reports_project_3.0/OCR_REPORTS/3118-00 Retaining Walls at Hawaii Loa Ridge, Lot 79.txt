PRELIMINARY CONCLUSIONS RECOMMENDATIONS
We believe that a large part of the problem for this steeply sloped lot is due to
stormwater drainage from the street running off onto Lot 79 rather than emptying into a
storm drainage inlet on the street The lot itself lacks a system for surface drainage
collection and appropriate distribution to storm drains The lot is located downslope of

the outside of a relatively short radius steeply graded downhill curve on Pu u ikena Drive
According to the contractor s backhoe operator who was present on site during the
heavy rains of October 1992 the curb on Pu u ikena Drive in front of the lot is easily
overtopped by high velocity water flows travelling down the gutter during heavy rains
The existence of deep erosional gullies through the overcast material and water stains
over the curbs provide visual confirmation of this statement It is possible that prior to
clearing of Lot 79 the natural grassland vegetation was able to absorb and filter these
flows With the lot now basically cleared of vegetation water and eroded soil pond in the
right rear corner of the lot and the retaining walls function as dams during storm events
We recommend that the City and the Hawaii Loa Ridge Homeowners Association
be contacted and asked to correct the existing street drainage condition that is causing
storm water to flow over the curb onto Lot 79 We also recommend that the owner of Lot
79 retain the services of a registered civil engineer to analyze the surface water runoff
situation on the site and design a storm drainage collection and distribution system that
will alleviate the drainage problems observed on Lot 79
However we also conclude that the existing retaining wall backdrain system is
inadequately designed and constructed If weepholes are relied upon to relieve
hydrostatic pressure behind retaining walls then free draining materials should be placed
behind the walls in such a manner that groundwater at all locations along the wall can be
drained through the holes Therefore a continuous layer consisting of a one 1 to two
foot 2 wide zone of permeable material No 3B Fine gravel or ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation should be placed immediately adjacent to the entire wall not just behind the
weepholes If a well graded permeable material is placed behind the walls then filter
fabric will not be necessary If uniform size sand or drain rock is used this material
should be wrapped by a layer of filter fabric so that fine materials are not carried through
the weepholes A filter fabric such as Mirafi 140N or equivalent should be placed

between the permeable drainage material and the compacted backfill material The filter
fabric is not effective if simply placed on top of the drain rock as we observed in Test Pits
Nos 3 and 4
The upper 12 inches of retaining wall backfill should be a clayey cap material
placed to reduce the potential infiltration of surface drainage directly into the retaining wall
backfill Surface water should be directed away from the retaining walls to an appropriate
surface outlet
The existing retaining walls should either have effective functioning weepholes as
described above or should have backdrains A typical backdrain would consist of a one
1 to two foot 2 wide zone of permeable material No 3B Fine gravel or ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation immediately adjacent to the wall with a continuous perforated pipe
placed with the perforations facing down at the base of the wall discharging by gravity
to a storm drain facility A filter fabric such as Mirafi 140N or equivalent should be
placed between the permeable drainage material and the compacted backfill material As
an alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be
used instead of the drainage material with filter fabric The prefabricated drainage product
should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall
The stability of the existing walls will likely be adversely affected by continued
coverage of the weepholes Previously existing drainage patterns were altered by clearing
of the site and construction of the walls A drainage system for surface waters should be
designed and constructed as soon as possible If the walls fail due to buildup of
hydrostatic pressure then the stability of both your client s and the downslope neighbors
properties could be adversely affected
