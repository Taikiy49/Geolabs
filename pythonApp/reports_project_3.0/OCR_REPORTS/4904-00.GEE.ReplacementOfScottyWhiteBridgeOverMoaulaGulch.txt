2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes including the following Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea some activity
during recent geologic time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 and is considered to be
dormant and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both are still active The project site is situated
on the southeast flank of the Mauna Loa Shield Volcano
The volume of the Mauna Loa Shield Volcano extending from the ocean floor is
estimated to be on the order of about 10 000 cubic miles making it possibly the largest
volcanic mountain on earth Furthermore it is estimated from current lava production
that Mauna Loa may have been forming for the past 1 to 2 million years Mauna Loa is
actually composed of two separate shield volcanoes Mauna Loa and Ninole The
latter having been covered by Mauna Loa lavas left only a few localized surface
expressions visible at present The Mauna Loa shield has been built by volcanic
eruptions along two principal rift zones that extend southwestward and
east northeastward from the summit caldera
The majority of the surface rock exposures at Mauna Loa consist of lavas and
volcanic deposits belonging to the Kau Volcanic Series Pleistocene and Holocene age
and the older Kahuku Volcanic Series Pleistocene age In addition a regional ash
deposit identified as Pahala Ash Pleistocene age forms a soil mantle overlying and
interbedded within the rocks of the Kau Volcanic Series The ash deposits typically
occur as kipukas isolated deposits with some significant layer accumulations ranging
up to about 30 feet in thickness
Pahala Ash is a low plasticity silt derived from the weathering of ash cinder and
Pele s Hair Pahala Ash generally has low shear strength and when the moisture
content is high enough it loses significant strength when it is remolded becomes
thixotropic Therefore the typical Pahala Ash may be potentially liquefiable during
seismic events

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration at the bridge and culvert replacement sites generally
encountered a medium dense to dense surface fill layer extending to depths of about
2 to 9 8 feet below the existing ground surface The surface fill layer was underlain by a
hard to very hard basalt rock formation extending to a depth of about 45 feet the
maximum depth explored Layers of medium dense clinker were encountered within the
basalt formation at the site At the culvert site a 10 foot thick volcanic ash layer was
encountered near the existing ground surface Groundwater was not encountered in
the borings drilled at the time of our field exploration
In general the basalt formation encountered below the surface fill layer appeared
to be in a moderately fractured condition with Rock Quality Designations RQD of at
least 50 percent Based on the fracturing characteristics of the basalt formation
encountered at the site we believe that the basalt formation at the site may be
considered as a non erodible material and would not be considered susceptible to
additional stream scour
Based on our field exploration we believe that a shallow foundation system
bearing on the medium hard to hard basalt rock formation encountered in the borings
may be used to support the new abutment structures The footings for the new bridge
structure should be embedded into the basalt formation To reduce the potential for
loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of cavities below a footing we
recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting be implemented at the
foundation locations of the abutments during construction
Reinforced concrete sheetpile type retaining walls are planned for the new
culvert walls The culvert walls should be embedded a minimum of 6 feet into the

3 1 Seismic Design Considerations
We understand that the Scotty White Bridge Replacement project will need to be
designed based on a seismic acceleration coefficient of 0 80g Based on the
subsurface materials encountered in the borings drilled and the geologic setting of the
area the project site may be classified from a seismic analysis standpoint as a rock site
corresponding to a Type Soil Profile A seismic site coefficient of 1 0 may be used for
the project site for seismic analysis of the replacement bridge structure
To assist in the modeling of the behavior of the bridge structure during an
earthquake we believe that the abutment structure backfill provides a significant
amount of lateral load resistance in the form of passive pressure resistance The
abutment structure may be considered as a large footing bearing on the abutment
backfill soils for resistance of lateral loads generated during a seismic event in the
longitudinal direction Considering the type of structure backfill materials recommended
behind the abutment structures Type A Structure Backfill the stiffness of the abutment
fill soils will generally be mobilized prior to the lateral load resistance of the foundations
Provided that intimate contact exists between the backfill soils and the
abutments a soil stiffness spring equal to approximately 4 kips per square foot of
abutment wall per inch of deflection may be used in resisting lateral loads To reduce
the potential for failure of the abutment fill soils the lateral deflection should be limited
to 1 inch or less The structural engineer should check the structure displacement
and rigidity of the abutment wall required in order to engage the abutment fill soils for
lateral load resistance It should be noted that the stiffness of the abutment fill soils only
applies to resisting forces that cause the abutment wall to move into the backfill soil
longitudinal direction

generally consists of three components 1 Aggregation and Degradation 2 Contraction

3 4 Foundation Probing
Based on our experience in the area voids and or cavities may be encountered
in the basalt rock formation at the project site To reduce the potential for loss of
foundation support resulting from the collapse of cavities below a footing we
recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting at the foundation locations
abutments be implemented during construction Probing and grouting of the site
retaining wall foundations may be omitted due to the less critical nature of these
structures
For estimating purposes we recommend that a probe hole be drilled for every
100 square feet of foundation area or at 10 feet on center along the abutment
foundations The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should
extend to a depth of at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of footing elevation If
cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operation
additional probeholes should be drilled at closer spacing to aid in delineating the vertical
and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes and cavities discovered
should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected at low to moderate pressures We
recommend that a low strength sand cement grout mixture with a slump range of 6 to

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of
about 40 to 45 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using
truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers and coring
tools The approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1
through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and
drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Eight Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on the rock
core samples retrieved to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the
underlying basalt rock formation The test results are summarized on Plate B 1
Two California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of near surface soils to evaluate the strength characteristics for pavement
subgrade support Results of these CBR tests are presented on Plates B 2 and B 3
