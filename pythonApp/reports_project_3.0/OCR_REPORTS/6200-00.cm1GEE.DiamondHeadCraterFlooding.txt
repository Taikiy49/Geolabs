Our field exploration generally encountered a thin layer of AC over 7 to 8 inches of
base course underlain by brown clay to the maximum depth explored of about 11 5 feet
below the existing ground surface The existing pavement was deteriorated with signs of local
base failure
We anticipate that site grading for construction of the proposed parking lot will be

supporting capacity However based on the anticipated light traffic conditions and limited
pavement life required we believe that a light duty asphaltic concrete pavement section will

softening weakening of the soils and accelerate pavement failure Drainage improvements
should be initiated to reduce future flooding of the pavements

SECTION 1 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Pavement and Drainage Improvements at CSMS 1
Building 304 Diamond Head Crater in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The
project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and _ geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of new pavement
construction and light pole foundations only The findings and recommendations presented
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The proposed project is within the Hawaii Army National Guard facility in Diamond
Head Crater The Combined Field Maintenance Shops CSMS No 1 consists of a
T shaped warehouse type building surrounded by AC pavement The pavement is
generally in fair condition except for the northwestern corner where the pavement is in
poor condition with signs of localized base failure
It was reported that this northwestern corner of the facility floods during after heavy
rains Visual observations disclosed that this corner is a low point in the area Therefore a
major objective of this project is to correct improve the drainage and prevent flooding in this
area The geotechnical engineering scope of work was limited to the design of the
pavement repair in the northwestern corner of the parking lot
1 3 Purpose and Scope
The scope of our work was to obtain an overview of the surface and subsurface
conditions at the site to formulate geotechnical engineering recommendations for the
design of the new pavement for the parking lot The work was performed in general
accordance with our fee proposal dated March 20 2009 Our scope of work generally
included the following tasks and work efforts

SECTION 2 SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Koolau Volcano reached the
end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age many sea level changes occurred as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world The higher sea level
stands caused the accumulation of sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation
of reef deposits at corresponding higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits Renewed erosion deposited alluvial soils in the
lagoons and bays
The geologic history of the area has been further complicated by the deposition of
recent pyroclastic materials and lava flows from eruptions of the vents of members of the
Honolulu Volcanic Series including Diamond Head These events were contemporaneous
with the Pleistocene sea level fluctuations The deposits of pyroclastics have consolidated
to form volcanic tuff locally referred to as mudrock
The site is situated within the Diamond Head Crater which is comprised primarily of
volcanic tuff mudrock However the bottom of the crater is comprised of alluvial colluvial
soils mostly originating from tuff but weathered to a clay soil

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
We anticipate that the existing grades will be maintained therefore site grading for
construction of the proposed parking lot will be minimal Based on the assumption that
traffic will consist of light vehicles and the design life will be limited we believe that a light
duty asphaltic concrete pavement section will be appropriate
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering design
recommendations are presented in the following sections herein
3 1 Pavement Section
Design traffic loadings for the paved areas at the site were not available at the time
this report was prepared However we anticipate that the majority of vehicular traffic at the
site will consist of light trucks with occasional medium trucks For this traffic condition the
following light pavement sections are recommended
Flexible Pavement
2 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 Relative Compaction
6 0 Inch Aggregate Subbase Course 95 Relative Compaction
14 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Subgrade
Rigid Pavement Concrete Pad
6 0 Inch Portland Cement Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Subbase Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
12 0 inch Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Subgrade
The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches
moisture conditioned to above optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density
determined by ASTM D 1557 AASHTO T 180 Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight needed to achieve the maximum dry density
One of the primary mechanisms causing pavement distress is pumping of the
pavement section as a result of saturation of the aggregate base course and or subgrade

soils Therefore the pavement surfaces should be sloped to drain to appropriate drainage
structures or discharge areas Surface water ponding should not be allowed on site during

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
cleared thoroughly Soft and or yielding areas encountered at the pavement subgrade
level should be over excavated to expose firm material and the resulting excavation
should be backfilled with well compacted fills The excavated soft and or organic soils
should be properly disposed of off site and or used in landscape areas where
appropriate Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for
over excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the
over excavation quantities
The subgrades and areas designated to receive fills should then be scarified to a
minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage
by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
If shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade we recommend
preparing the subgrade soil again as recommended above Saturation and
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor
drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted fill A Geolabs representative should evaluate the need for
over excavation due to soft subgrade soil conditions

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Fill Materials
Aggregate base course and aggregate subbase materials required for the flexible
pavement section should consist of crushed basalt aggregate and should conform to
Sections 703 06 and 703 17 of the State of Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road
and Bridge Construction should test imported fill materials for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the
intended use
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Aggregate base course materials should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 6 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Because moisture conditioning and compaction are critical elements of the earthwork
Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during the site grading
operations to assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and
the proper moisture content on each fill lift Where compaction is less than required
additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture content as
necessary to obtain the specified compaction

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling a
total of two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths of
approximately 11 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring
locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 We used a truck mounted drill rig equipped
with continuous flight augers
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operations on a near continuous
basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory
Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System as
shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials encountered in the borings are
presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a _ 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the
field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive
strength of the sample Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
