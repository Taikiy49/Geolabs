INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our preliminary geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the proposed H 304 and H 305 Family Housing Project
located within the Marine Corps Base Hawaii MCBH at Kaneohe Bay on the island
of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and preliminary geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and
engineering analyses The preliminary recommendations presented herein are intended
for design input for site slope stability site grading foundations pavements and
infrastructure work and may not be appropriate to form the basis for preparation of
construction cost estimates nor for construction bidding purposes

The project site is located at the southern flank of Pu u Hill
within the MCBH in Kaneohe on the island of Oahu Hawaii The project site covers
an area of approximately 30 acres and is located on the lower slope of Pu u Hawaii
Loa as shown on Site Plans 1 and 2 Plates 2 and 3 respectively The project site

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be of
the middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be of the late Pliocene
to early Pleistocene Ice Age In age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3159 00 Page 6
which erosion cut valleys deep into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The site of the MCBH at Kaneohe Bay is located on Mokapu Peninsula which
was built by three volcanic vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series namely Ulupau Head
and Pyramid Rock These vents are believed to be among the oldest
of the series The pyroclastic deposits and lava flows from vents formed salients
permitting the growth of a coral reef that merged with the main body of the island of
Oahu
It is believed that Pu u Hawaii Loa was formed above the sea level before the
Kaena stand which is about 100 feet above the existing sea level During the Kaena
stand of the sea the slopes of Pu u Hawaii Loa may have been eroded by continuous
wave action The proposed family housing project generally overlies the surficial
residual soils which are the eroded remnants of the lava flow of Pu u Hawaii Loa

DISCUSSION AND PRELIMINARY RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration and engineering analyses the proposed project
site is underlain by residual soils consisting of stiff to hard silty clays extending to
depths of about 5 to 11 feet below the existing ground surface The residual soils are
underlain by saprolite and basalt rock formation with increasing depth Laboratory tests
performed on the near surface silty clay residual soils indicate that the materials
generally range from moderately to highly expansive Therefore the near surface soils
have moderate to high shrink swell potential when subjected to fluctuations in the
moisture content Some of the saprolite soils below the near surface residual soils
have also weathered to expansive clayey soils

Expansive Soils
Based on our laboratory test results the near surface silty clay residual soils
generally have moderate to high shrink swell potential when subjected to fluctuations
in the moisture content Therefore we recommend that proposed slabs on grade be
supported on a minimum of 24 inches of non expansive capping fill to reduce the
potential for distress resulting from shrinking and swelling of the near surface expansive
soils when subjected to moisture fluctuations
If post and beam construction is elected for support of structures we recommend
that the post footings be supported on shallow drilled piers Since minor grading is
anticipated we envision that the near surface soils below the proposed buildings will
consist of expansive silty clay soils We anticipate that the potentially expansive
subgrades for the proposed buildings supported on a post and beam foundation system
will be exposed to the environment such as winds and drying resulting in soil
shrinkage in the future due to loss of soil moisture Therefore the drilled piers
supporting the post footings should be embedded to a depth below the seasonal
moisture variation approximately 5 feet below finished grade In general frictional
support of the drilled pier from the upper 3 feet should be discounted in foundation
design due to potential soil shrinkage around the pier shaft near the surface

Cut slopes exposing the expansive residual soils at the slope face should be
protected from drying to reduce the potential for development of shrinkage cracks
Development of shrinkage cracks may lead to slope instability of the cut slope In
addition the potential for soil erosion would be significantly higher if shrinkage cracks
develop on the cut slope face
Fill slopes constructed of the recompacted on site silty clay soils would behave
similarly Therefore the faces of cut and fill slopes should be protected from drying
and development of shrinkage cracks during construction In addition adequate slope
protection should be provided to reduce the potential for erosion of the slope faces
Alternatively cut slopes may be buttressed with fill slopes constructed of non expansive
or low expansive fills
Special attention should be given to the recommended procedures for subgrade
preparation for slabs and pavements to reduce the potential shrink swell effects of the
near surface clayey soils Detailed discussions and preliminary recommendations

on most ridge slopes in Oahu Various methods of boulder barriers or catchments may
be used to reduce the hazard of falling boulders For this site some of the methods
which may be used to reduce the boulder hazard include the following
1 Boulder sweeps along the upper grading boundaries of the site could be

Shallow Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our exploration we believe
that a shallow foundation system may be used to support the relatively light structural
loads generated by the proposed low rise structures anticipated for the family housing
project Proposed mid rise structures may also be supported on a shallow foundation
system depending on the location final grades and foundation conditions
Allowable bearing pressures on the order of 2 500 to 3 000 pounds per square
foot may be used for preliminary design of isolated and continuous footings bearing on
the stiff residual soils and or on compacted engineered fill needed to raise the grades
It should be noted that the preliminary bearing values provided above assumes that the
proposed buildings are not located across cut and fill transitions with more than 5 feet
of differential fill thickness across the structure If this condition exists a qualified
geotechnical engineer should be consulted for supplemental recommendations The
allowable bearing pressures are for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one
third Vs for transient loads such as wind and or seismic forces
As previously indicated the site is generally underlain by moderately to highly
expansive clayey soils Therefore a perimeter wall footing is recommended around
the proposed structures to serve as a moisture barrier to reduce the potential drying
and subsequent wetting of the near surface expansive clayey soils As a minimum
footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
For footings constructed on sloping ground the footings should be embedded deep
enough to provide a minimum horizontal set back distance of 8 feet measured from the
edge of the footing to the slope face Footings should have a minimum width of at least
18 inches Total settlement for footings bearing on the stiff clayey soils and or
about inch

In general lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by a
combination frictional resistance between the base of the footing and the bearing

Slabs On Grade
Slabs on grade for the proposed project may consist of concrete floor slabs and
exterior flatwork To reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from swelling
of the on site expansive soils we recommend that a minimum of 24 inches of non
expansive capping fill material be provided below slabs on grade The non expansive
capping fill should extend at least 2 feet beyond the limits of the proposed structure
For exterior flatwork such as sidewalks and driveways the non expansive capping fill
may be reduced to a minimum of 12 inches
The non expansive fill material should provide a capping layer of moist soil
above the underlying potentially expansive subgrade soil thereby reducing the loss of
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3159 00 Page 17
drying and wetting of the subgrade soils should reduce the distress on the slab due to
potential heaving of the underlying expansive clayey soils when wetted
In general non expansive capping fill should consist of granular materials such
as crushed coral basalt crusher waste or cinder The non expansive capping fill
should be well graded with no particles greater than 3 inches in size should have less
than 35 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve and should have a laboratory
California Bearing Ratio CBR swell value not exceeding 1 percent when prepared
near the optimum moisture The non expansive capping fill should not contain organic
or other deleterious materials and should have a plasticity index less than 12 when
tested by ASTM Test Designation D 4318 In general we recommend that the non
expansive capping fill be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
and compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D
1557 91
The non expansive fill should be placed as soon as possible after the
scarification and compaction of the underlying subgrade soils Soil subgrades should
be kept moist prior to placement of non expansive capping fill and or concrete If
shrinkage cracks appear in the subgrades the soil should be thoroughly moistened to
close all cracks and recompacted prior to capping fill or concrete placement

borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 14 and 101 through 108 at the

The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural

testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic presentation of the materials

nia sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to

Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left
in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on selected soil
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample depths
Four California Bearing Ratio CBR tests were performed on selected bulk
samples collected for pavement design The test results are summarized on Plate B 1
Two Sieve Analysis tests ASTM Test Method D 422 63 were performed on
samples of the granular materials encountered to evaluate the grain size distribution
The test results are summarized on Plate B 2
Three one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the soils in the
natural state The results of the tests are summarized on Plate B 3
Thirteen one inch Ring Swell tests were also performed on remolded and
compacted samples The samples were remolded to approximately 90 percent relative
compaction near and above the optimum moisture content Results of the swell tests
on the remolded samples are summarized on Plates B 4 and B 5
Five Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate
their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification A summary of the test
results is presented on Plate B 6
Two Compaction Tests ASTM D 1557 91 were performed on bulk samples of
the near surface soils to evaluate the moisture content and dry density relationship
Graphic presentation of compaction test results is provided on Plates B 7 and B 8
