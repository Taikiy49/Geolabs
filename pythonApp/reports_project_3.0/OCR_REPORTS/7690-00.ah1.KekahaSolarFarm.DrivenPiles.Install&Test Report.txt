Dear Mr. Haughey: 
This letter report summarizes our geotechnical engineering observation and testing 
services performed for the test pile program at the Kekaha Solar Farm Development 
project in the Kekaha area on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. In general, this letter report 
presents our field observations and the results of testing performed for the driven test piles 
installed and load tested from October 22 through 26, 2018. Our work was performed in 
general accordance with the requirements outlined in the Pile Load Test – Scope of 
Work - Revision 6.0, dated October 15, 2018 submitted by AES Corporation for the 
project. 
A geotechnical engineering exploration was performed by Geolabs for the project, 
and the results are presented in the report entitled “Geotechnical Engineering Exploration, 
Kekaha Solar Farm Development, Kekaha, Kauai, Hawaii” dated August 3, 2018.  The 
purpose of our geotechnical observation and testing was to monitor the geotechnical 
aspects of the test pile installation and testing, and to verify/modify the pile design 
recommendations presented in our report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
We understand it is proposed to develop a 19-MW DC photovoltaic power plant in 
the Kekaha area on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. The general vicinity of the Kekaha Solar 
Farm Development is presented on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. The development 
will be constructed on approximately 140 acres of open land, consisting of solar panels 
installed on ground-mounted tracker systems; various pads for electrical equipment such 
as switchgear, transformers, and inverters; and overhead or underground electrical 
circuits. We understand the wide flange beams, such as W8x10, W8x13 and W8x18, are 
considered for the foundation support of the new solar arrays. 
As part of the test pile program, W8x10, W8x13 and W8x18 wide flange beams 
were driven to selected embedment lengths below the existing ground surface at a

DRIVEN PILE INSTALLATION AND TESTING 
Driven Pile Installation 
Test pile installation was performed by others, between October 22 and 25, 2018. A 
total of thirty-two (32) test pile locations were driven using a track-mounted GAYK HRE 
4000 hydraulic ram.  
The pile driving operations were conducted under the near-continuous observation 
of our engineer. During the pile driving operations, logs were maintained for each test pile, 
to document information pertaining to pile number, date driven, driving time, driving rate, 
and observations made during the pile driving operations. Records of the pile driving 
operations are presented in Appendix A – Pile Driving Logs. The driven pile installation is 
summarized in the Table 1 below. 
Based on the design load capacities provided and our experiences with load testing 
behavior, we anticipated the pile length would likely be governed by the tensile load 
resistance due to only friction bearing component to be mobilized. Therefore, in an effort of 
expediting the testing program, we prioritized determining the embedment length by 
performing the tensile load tests first and then verifying the embedment lengths with the 
lateral and saturation compressive tests.  
Sacrificial W8x10, W8x13 and W8x18 wide flange beams were driven to an initial 
specified embedment length of 6 feet below the ground surface at the pre-selected test 
location. When the pullout failure occurred, the test piles were removed and re-driven to 
2 feet deeper than the initial embedment length for additional tensile load testing. This 
sequence was repeated until meeting the tensile load capacities specified. Additional test 
piles, then, were driven for lateral and saturation compressive load testing, to confirm the 
load capacities specified. 
Upon the determined pile embedment length, groups of test piles (three W8x10, 
one W8x13 and one W8x18) were driven at the remaining test locations, to confirm the 
load bearing consistency throughout the development site.

Driven Pile Testing 
A minimum of one (1) tensile, one (1) lateral and one (1) saturation compressive 
pile load tests were performed on each wide flange beam type at Test Location No. 5 in 
support of determining the embedment length having sufficient load capacities. It should 
be noted that the pre-selected test location was moved from Test Location 1 to Test 
Location 5 for adequate space of the testing program. A summary of the design loads 
specified for each pile type are presented in the Table 2 below. The lateral testing load 
was applied up to 150 percent of the design load whereas the tensile and compressive 
testing loads were up to 160 percent of the design loads or beyond if the maximum of pile 
displacement is less than 1 inch during the load testing.

2.  If the pile did not reach the maximum displacement of 1 inch at 160 percent of design load, the test

Tensile pile load tests were conducted on Pile Nos. 5.1, 5.2, 5.3, 5.4, 5.5, 5.10 and 
5.11 in general accordance with ASTM D3689. In general, the test piles were loaded in 
tension to 60, 80, 100, 120, 140 and 160 percent of the design loads. If the pile did not 
reach the maximum displacement of 1 inch after the application of the 160 percent of the 
design load, the testing load was increased in 1-kip increments until failure or the 
mechanical limitations of the testing device. Each load increment was held for at least 
1 minute or until the rate of the pile head movement had generally stabilized. The results 
of the tensile pile load tests are presented in the Appendix B – Tensile Load Test Results. 
It should be noted that Pile Nos. 5.1, 5.4 and 5.5 with an embedment length of 
about 6 feet below the ground surface were not able to maintain the maximum test load at 
about 160 percent of design load without excessive displacement, resulting in a pullout 
failure. Based on the tensile load tests and our field observations, we extended the 
embedment length from 6 to 8 feet below the ground surface and retested the piles. One 
(1) W8x10, one (1) W8x13 and one (1) W8x18 wide flange beams, designated as 
Pile Nos. 5.2, 5.10 and 5.11, were driven to an embedment length of about 8 feet below 
the ground surface and maintained at the maximum testing load of about 160 percent of 
the corresponding design load without pullout failure.

Therefore, we recommend extending the embedment length from 6 to 8 feet below 
the ground surface for the remaining test piles and the production piles. 
A tension load test was conducted on a W8x10 wide flange beam with a 10-foot 
embedment length at Pile No. 5.3. The test pile was loaded in tension to about 9.7 kips 
(160 percent of the design load) and then in 1-kip increments up to the maximum testing 
load of about 13.7 kips. The tensile load test was terminated at the maximum load of about 
13.7 kips due to mechanical limitation of the testing equipment. 
To verify the pile tensile resistance in other areas, additional tensile load test was 
performed on Pile No. 3.2 (W8x10) at Test Location No. 3. The tensile test load was 
applied on this test pile up to the mechanical limitations of the testing equipment of 
10.2 kips, equivalent to about 168 percent of the design load without pullout failure. 
Lateral pile load tests were conducted on Pile Nos. 5.6, 5.7 and 5.8 in general 
accordance with ASTM D3966. The lateral load was generally applied at about 40 inches 
above the ground surface, and deflections were measured with a digital dial gauge placed 
at about 6 inches above the ground surface. In general, the test piles were laterally loaded 
to 60, 80, 100, 120 and 150 percent of the design load. Each load increment was held for 
at least 1 minute or until the rate of the pile head movement had generally stabilized. The 
results of the lateral pile load tests are presented in Appendix C – Lateral Load Test 
Results. 
Saturation compressive pile load tests were conducted on Pile Nos. 5.9, 5.10 and 
5.11 in general accordance with ASTM D1143. Prior to conducting the compressive load 
test, the ground was saturated with at least 150 gallons of water in a circumference of 
about 3.5 feet around the pile. In general, the test piles were loaded in compression to 60, 
80, 100, 120, 140 and 160 percent of the design load. If the pile did not reach the 
maximum displacement of 1 inch at the 160 percent of the design load, the pile was 
completely unloaded and then reloaded to 60, 80, 100, 120, 140 and 160 percent of the 
factored design load. The factored design load is 1.6 times of the compressive design 
load. Each load increment was held for at least 1 minute or until the rate of the pile head 
movement generally stabilized. The results of the saturation compressive pile load tests 
are presented in the Appendix D – Saturation Compressive Load Test Results. 
Based on our review of the load testing results, we believe the pile embedment 
length of 8 feet below the ground surface may provide sufficient load capacities meeting 
the design requirements, except in the western portion of the project site with a pile 
embedment length of 10 feet. Based on our observation of the pile driving, it is our 
professional opinion that the proposed pile driving equipment is capable of installing the 
wide flange piles to the above embedment depth of 8 feet below the ground surface. 
Selected photographs taken during the pile installation and load testing are presented in 
Appendix E - Site Photographs. 
It should be noted that relatively lower pile driving rates of about 2 to 4 seconds per 
foot penetration were encountered during the test pile driving at Test Location No. 1. We

PRODUCTION DRIVEN PILE INSTALLATION CRITERIA 
Based on the above results and the anticipated subsurface conditions, we 
believe that the proposed pile driving equipment (GAYK HRE 4000 hydraulic ram) is 
capable of installing the specified W8x18, W8x13 and W8x10 wide flange beams to the 
desirable embedment depths, meeting the design lateral, tensile and compressive load 
capacities per each pile type, provided they are installed meeting the following driving 
criteria. 
The pile should be installed to a minimum of 10-foot embedment depth in 
the vicinity of Test Location No. 1 and a minimum of 8-foot embedment 
depth in the remaining areas. 
The pile driving rate should be no less than 6 seconds per foot for the last 1 
foot of pile driving. 
If the production pile is not able meet the above driving criteria, we recommend that 
a tensile proof load test be conducted to verify the tensile load capacity for acceptance 
and/or modification. 
Due to the specialized nature of the driven pile construction and potential variation 
of the bearing material stiffness, a Geolabs representative should be present to observe 
the geotechnical aspects of the driven pile foundation construction and testing. This is to 
observe compliance with the intent of the design concepts or recommendations and to 
expedite suggestions for design changes that may be required in the event the subsurface 
conditions differ from those anticipated at the time this report was prepared. The 
recommendations provided herein are contingent upon such observations. If the actual 
subsurface conditions encountered during construction are different from those assumed 
or considered in this report, then appropriate modifications should be made.
