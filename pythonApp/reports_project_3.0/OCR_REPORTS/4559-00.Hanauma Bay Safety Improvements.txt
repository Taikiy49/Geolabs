SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The project involves the rock hillslope that is located adjacent to
the existing access road and footpath which leads down to the
shoreline at the Hanauma Bay Nature Preserve There are
some concerns that periodic falling rocks from the steep cut
bank located adjacent to the access road may pose a hazard to
vehicles and pedestrians utilizing this road
Based on our reconnaissance of the affected hillslope fractured
and potentially unstable bodies of volcanic tuff rock were
identified The purpose for this project is to improve the safety
of the hillslope condition while also preserving the natural
character of the highly visible rock slope
Based on our evaluation of the site conditions with respect to the
purpose of the project we recommend that the hillslope
improvements consist of the localized removal of potentially
unstable rock by means of scaling only the fractured rock bodies
from the exposed face of the cut bank Due to the visibility of
the subject hillslope the scaling effort should be carefully
controlled to achieve the desired results
In addition a sweep of loose surface stones and boulders from
the flatter hillslope located above the cut bank is recommended
The boulder sweep would be performed by manual labor The
intent of the boulder sweep is to reduce the potential for rockfall
from these upper slopes
The text of this report should be referred to for more detailed
discussions and specific recommendations

INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering evaluation performed
for the rockfall safety improvements at the Hanauma Bay Nature Preserve located in
Maunalua on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site
are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering
recommendations for the limited slope grading and removal of loose rocks at the subject site
The findings and recommendations are a result from our field reconnaissance and
engineering analyses These recommendations are intended to support the plans and
specifications prepared for slope grading and the removal of loose rocks at the subject site
The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at
the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Hanauma Bay Nature Preserve is located within the confines of a series of
volcanic craters that comprise the Koko Head area of the southeastern portion of the Island
of Oahu For the development of the park access some steep slope cuts were made into
the volcanic tuff formation mudrock in order to construct the existing access road which
leads from the upper parking lot down to the beach We understand that on occasion loose
rocks fall from the steep bank cuts or roll down from farther up the slope onto the access
road creating a potential hazard to pedestrians and vehicles This project is to minimize the
hazard from falling rocks while retaining the natural beauty of the site Therefore some
conventional solutions such as retaining walls fences and bolted slope meshes are not
viable alternatives The area of concern is limited to the rock slope located between the
existing upper parking lot and the subject beach access road The limits of the project are
delineated on the General Site Plan Plate 2
Our evaluation of the site consisted of a geologic reconnaissance to observe the
existing rock fracture planes and overall condition of the steep slope in order to propose
appropriate cuts for removal of the potentially unstable rocks Based on our evaluation the

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
GEOLABS INC
W O 4559 00 Page 4
middle to late Pliocene in age 3 5 to 3 9 million years old and forms the bulk of the western
third of the Island of Oahu The younger volcano Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene
to early Pleistocene in age 1 7 to 2 5 million years old and forms the majority of the eastern
two thirds of the Island of Oahu The Waianae volcano became extinct while the Koolau
volcano was still active therefore lava flows from Koolau banked and ponded against the
eastern flank of Waianae to form the broad upland plateau referred to as the Schofield
Plateau of central Oahu
After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time weathering and stream
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield eruptive activity rejuvenated on the Island
of Oahu with a series of lava flows followed by eruptions of cinder and volcanic tuff These
most recent volcanic eruptions are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series In general
the vents and lava flows are believed to be on the order of about 100 000 to 410 000 years
old The lava flows and ash materials of the Honolulu Volcanic Series contain higher
contents of sodium and potassium with less silica compared to the older volcanic products
of Oahu Many of the volcanic vents have erupted below and through the established coral
reefs that surrounded the southern shoreline of the Island of Oahu The explosive eruptions
formed prominent cinder and tuff cones that include the features of Diamond Head Koko
Head including Hanauma Bay and the Punchbowl and Salt Lake Craters
The Hanauma Bay Nature Preserve is located on the southeastern tip of the Island
of Oahu between the prominent Koko Crater and Koko Head landmarks The site is
underlain by products of the Honolulu Volcanic Series which include consolidated cinder and
volcanic tuff materials The materials were erupted from multiple vents aligned along the
Koko fissure which exploded through the older submerged coral reef and Koolau basalt rock
to produce bedded pyroclastic deposits that contain inclusions of coral reef and basalt rock
fragments Hanauma Bay is believed to represent a crater that was breached on the
southeastern side by ocean wave activity
GEOLABS INC
W O 4559 00 Page 5
The hill slopes composing Hanauma Bay consist of layered deposits of volcanic ash
that were consolidated to produce volcanic tuff rock also locally referred to as mudrock In
general the brownish colored rock consists of cemented sand and silt components with
some embedded gravel and occasional cobble to boulder sized fragments The components
of the tuff rock were deposited as fallout from explosive eruptions therefore the materials
essentially had draped the preexisting terrain surrounding the vents As a result the bedding
planes formed by the layered ash fallout possess a wide range of orientations with some
bedding horizons changing orientation and inclination over relatively short distances The
depositional bedding planes represent horizons of preferred fracture orientation Other
secondary post depositional fracture planes have developed as a result of weathering and
rock stress relief on the exposed surfaces
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is located at the Hanauma Bay Nature Preserve Park located at
Koko Head on the southeast portion of the Island of Oahu Hawaii The subject hill slope
is located within the park along the north side of the beach access road The limits of the
rockfall project site include the existing access road which bounds the lower slope limit
and the existing viewing lookout and administration building which bounds the upper slope
limit The lateral limits of the project site include the northeastern and southwestern ends
of the beach access road In plan view the subject hill slope is approximately 635 lineal
feet in length and ranges from about 120 feet wide at the southwestern end to less than
about 15 feet wide at the northeastern end of the site The location of the project site limits
is shown on the General Site Plan Plate 2
The southeasterly facing hill slope extends upward from the sloping access road
toward the main parking lot view overlooks and the existing visitor center administration
buildings The beach access road traverses the hillside and extends down toward the
southwest to provide access to the shoreline area The elevation of the project site ranges
from about 120 feet Mean Sea Level MSL at the western corner of the site to about
20 feet MSL at the toe of the slope at the southwestern end of the project site

Slope Condition Failure Mechanism
The subject slope consists of a steep cut bank which is located adjacent to the road
with generally flatter slopes that are located at higher elevations above the cut bank The
existing steep cut bank appears to stand at inclinations of about one horizontal to
eight vertical 1H 8V to 1H 4V The primary bedding planes were measured to dip
backward and into the cut slope face therefore the risk for landslide along the bedding
planes is believed to be low
The rockfall hazard is primarily due to the development of secondary fractures
related to stress relief and weathering of the exposed rock surfaces These secondary
fractures appear to trend roughly perpendicular to the primary bedding planes thus the
falling rock tends to result from the dislodgment of generally isolated tabular rock bodies
The secondary fractures are believed to slowly widen with the passage of time due to
percolating rainwater rock stress strain relief and other factors such as the prying effect
from migrating tree roots As bodies of rock fall from the steep slope face they potentially
undermine the support of rock bodies that are located above the failure site thus
potentially creating a new zone of rock instability Past falling rock from the cut bank is
evidenced by fresh exposures of previously covered rock material and the presence of
newly developed overhanging rock bodies
Several banyan trees were observed to be growing along the subject cut bank and
appear to contribute to some of the rockfall with an invasive root system that pries into
existing rock fractures and accelerates the weathering processes Otherwise most of the
cut bank is not vegetated
GEOLABS INC
W O 4559 00 Page 7
The more gentle slopes located above the cut bank exist as generally smoother
surfaces with few rock outcropping forming positive relief The slopes are generally devoid
of soil accumulation and are vegetated with haole koa brush and kiawe and banyan tree
growth
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
We understand that a potential safety hazard exists as a result of falling rocks from
the slopes located adjacent to the beach access road Rocks such as cobbles and
tabular shaped boulder fragments have been reported to periodically loosen and fall from the
steep roadside cut slopes The falling rock poses a potential hazard to daily vehicular and
foot traffic utilizing the narrow beach access road
Secondarily it is believed that another source of potential rockfall may exist from the
flatter upper slopes that are located at the southwestern portion of the site directly below the
bus parking lot This source area is believed to be of lower risk due to the flatter slope
presence of vegetation which reduces erosion and generally smooth planar rock surfaces
which limit rolling
As part of the proposed park improvements it is desired to reduce the falling rock
hazard by removing the existing loose fractured and potentially unstable rock components
of the cut bank face Because the park is a nature preserve and visitor attraction it is desired
to limit the construction impact of slope grading to preserve the natural look and beauty of
the volcanic tuff rock formation Therefore it has been emphasized that the methods used
to cut the fractured rock from the slope face should have a minimal scarring impact and an
irregular finished slope face is most desired
Rock Face Scaling
Based on our reconnaissance of the project site and in consideration of the objectives
for this project we believe that the proposed slope grading should consist of rock removal
achieved by scaling the loose and fractured bodies of rock from the slope as opposed to
conventional slope grading such as cutting and flattening Based on our observations there
are particular segments of the steep cut bank that should be scaled to provide for a more

competent exposed rock surface For the purpose of discussion in this report scaling refers
to the removal of isolated blocks of rock that are bounded by open fractures such that
minimal force would be required to dislodge the rock body from the exposed rock face
The available topographic map which was prepared primarily for other park
improvements was used for our mapping of the unstable rock features However the
topographic map lacks sufficient detail to depict the subtle topographic expression of the
overhanging blocks of fractured rock and other pertinent features observed at the site We
have delineated by taping and pacing the approximate location s of unstable rock features
on the topographic base maps without additional survey support The base maps showing
the location of unstable rock as individual slope segments are provided as a series of
Site Plans identified as Plates 2 1 though 2 5 of this report The Site Plans show the
approximate location and identification of slope segments that should be scaled of loose and
fractured rock
As a supplement to the Site Plans generalized sketches of five typical existing slope
conditions are provided to assist in evaluating the site conditions with respect to the type of
unstable rock condition that is observed and proposed for removal by rock scaling The five
slope conditions are identified as Typical Slope Scaling Conditions A B C D and E
and are provided in Appendix A of this report It should be noted that the sketches are
approximations and that actual site conditions may vary The contractor is encouraged to
visit the site and carefully review the site conditions to determine the most appropriate means
to conduct the rock slope scaling effort
The intent of the slope scaling effort is generally limited to the relatively small scale
removal of bodies of rock that appear unstable and are bounded by open fractures greater
than about 1 8 to inch in width Fractured overhanging or loose bodies of rock are the
primary intended target for removal by slope scaling Rock bodies that protrude from the
slope face but are firmly attached to the slope and are not bounded by open fractures
should be left in place We understand that it may be difficult to control the volume of rock
that becomes loose with the use of mechanized equipment therefore it is crucial for the
contractor to exercise judgment and good workmanship in the selection and operation of

Boulder Sweep
In addition to the scaling of the existing cut bank located adjacent to the beach access
road we recommend that a sweep of loose surface rock such as stones and boulders that
are not firmly embedded in the surrounding ground surface be conducted The location of
the areas identified for the proposed boulder sweep are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 6
The hillslope area for the proposed boulder sweep effort has been categorized into
two generalized conditions that have separate criteria that were used to define the proposed
work The Boulder Sweep Condition No 1 encompasses the ground surfaces that have a
limited growth of vegetation We believe the hazard associated with rocks rolling from these
sloping surfaces is greater due to the lack of a vegetation buffer which serves to reduce
erosion We recommend that all loose rocks greater than 4 inches in diameter stones and
boulders be swept from the exposed slopes to reduce the rockfall hazard from these slopes
In addition widely scattered small scale rock outcroppings were observed that contain some
highly fractured rock The highly fractured rock that can be moved by hand or by slight
leverage force should also be swept from the hilislope The location of the hillslopes
evaluated as Boulder Sweep Condition No 1 are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 6
The Boulder Sweep Condition No 2 encompasses the ground surfaces that are
covered with vegetation and have loose surface boulders and some highly fractured rock
outcroppings These vegetated slopes were observed to contain pockets of loose boulders
and fractured rock outcroppings that may be capable of producing rockfall The slopes
identified as Condition No 2 typically contain banyan tree growth lighter vegetation
undergrowth and scattered rocky scarps that consist of some highly fractured rock material
We recommend that all loose surface boulders greater than 12 inches in diameter including
the fractured rock outcroppings capable of producing rock greater than 12 inches in diameter
GEOLABS INC
W O 4559 00 Page 11
be swept from the ground surfaces More specifically if the fractured rock outcropping forms
positive relief against the surrounding ground surface and can be moved by hand or by slight
leverage force the material should be swept from the slope to reduce the potential for
rockfall The location of the hillslopes evaluated as Boulder Sweep Condition No 2 are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 6
Other ground surfaces located on the flatter hillslope above the access road were
evaluated and determined to be densely overgrown with kiawe and haole koa brush The
general location of the densely vegetated areas is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 6 The
vegetated terrain is located southwesterly of the vegetation demarkation line approximately
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 6 Loose boulders and fractured rock outcroppings were
observed however the thick and intertwined growth of tree limbs and branches were judged
to be an effective buffer against erosion and actually serve to reduce the potential for rolling
rock to reach the access road In addition significant cutting and clearing of the overgrowth
would be necessary to permit access and allow for a workable means to move the loose rock
material downward toward the road for disposal For these reasons the hillslope was not
included in the proposed boulder sweep operation
We envision that the boulder sweep may be conducted by a team of manual laborers
prior to the scaling of the cut bank located downslope Loose boulders and rock fragments
fitting the criteria previously described should be pushed or leveraged downhill toward the
roadway located below for collection and proper disposal Consideration should be given to
marking the location of the target boulders in advance of the boulder sweep effort
Embedded boulders and in situ rock outcroppings situated above the surrounding ground
surface that cannot be moved by hand or slight leverage force may be left in place These
firmly attached rocks are believed to be of lower potential for rockfall hazard considering the
steepness of the hillslope and generally thicker vegetation growth
