REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late 
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which 
time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a 
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are referred 
to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
The geologic conditions underlying the site are the result of a complex series of 
depositional and erosional events due to glacio-eustatic sea level changes and 
post-erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch. The Koolau Volcanic 
Shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows. 
Following the cessation of activity of the Koolau Volcano, alluvial soil materials 
were deposited in an estuarine and deltaic environment in the Pearl Harbor area. The sea 
level fluctuated several times, receding perhaps 300 feet below and rising about 100 feet 
above the present sea level.   
During the Recent Epoch, as the sea level rose to its current stand, the area was 
covered with additional sediments consisting primarily of soft and loose lagoonal marine 
deposits.  Land development and reclamation projects in the past 40 to 60 years have 
added man-made fills, bringing the site to its present configuration.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the near-surface conditions along the new walkway by collecting 
four bulk samples, designated as Bulk-1 through Bulk-4, of the near-surface soils to 
evaluate the subgrade conditions along the new walkway. Our previous field exploration 
explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling four 
borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, extending to depths of about 24 to 29 
feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate bulk sample and previous boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The bulk samples generally consisted of fill material composed of sandy clay, silty 
sand, and sand with traces of gravel. Our previous borings generally encountered a 
surface fill layer about 3.5 to 5.5 feet thick. The surface fill layer was underlain by coralline 
detritus and shallow marine deposits, lagoonal deposits, beach sand, and coral formation, 
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 29 feet below the existing ground 
surface. 
The surface fill layer consisted of loose to medium dense silty sand and sandy 
gravel, and stiff clayey silt with sand.  The coralline detritus and shallow marine deposits 
were comprised of very loose to medium dense silty sands and gravels. The lagoonal 
deposits consisted of very soft silty clay with sand.  The coral formation ranged from soft 
to medium hard.  
Groundwater was encountered in the previous borings at depths of about 3.8 to 
4.8 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration. The 
groundwater depths correspond to elevations from about +1.2 to +2 feet Mean Sea Level 
(MSL). It should be noted that groundwater levels at the project site may fluctuate 
depending on the tide, seasonal precipitation, and other factors. 
Results of the laboratory testing performed on selected bulk samples are 
presented in Appendix A. Boring logs from our previous field exploration in our report 
dated August 26, 2012 are presented in Appendix B. 
WALKWAY SLABS-ON-GRADE 
Concrete slabs-on-grade for the new walkway are planned for the project. Based 
on the results of our field exploration and laboratory testing, the near-surface clayey soils 
exhibit a low to moderate expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations. 
To reduce the potential for changes in the moisture content of the slab subgrade soils, 
we recommend capping the slab subgrade with a minimum 6-inch thick layer of 
non-expansive, select granular fill below the slabs-on-grade planned for the new walkway. 
Unless slabs are properly designed and constructed, there is a potential for future 
distress to the lightly loaded slabs-on-grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of these 
soils due to changes in the moisture content. To reduce the potential for structural distress 
resulting from the shrinking and swelling of the subgrade soils, we recommend keeping

We recommend scarifying the subgrade soils to a depth of about 8 inches,

Control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways 
with expansion joints at right-angle intersections. The thickened edges of slabs adjacent 
to unpaved areas should be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent 
grade. It should be emphasized that the areas adjacent to the slab edges should be 
backfilled tightly against the edges of the slabs with relatively impervious soils. These 
areas should also be graded to divert water away from the slabs and to reduce the 
potential for water ponding around the slabs. 
REVETMENT 
The installation of a revetment system is planned in front of the existing concrete 
seawall and retaining wall at the project site.  Typically, a revetment system consists of a 
layer of large and heavy armor stones underlain by a layer of smaller stones about 
6 to 12 inches in size. At the time of this report, the specific size and weight of stones for 
the revetment system were not available and are being designed by others.  
To provide protection of the revetment toe, the toe of the revetment should be 
buried below the design scour depth.  A revetment slope of one horizontal to one vertical 
(1H:1V) or flatter may be used for the anticipated boulders and cobbles revetment in front 
of the existing wall structures. 
To reduce the potential for loss of fines from the subgrade material into the 
revetment system, we recommend using a filter fabric (Mirafi FW403 or equivalent) 
between the subgrade material and the smaller stones below the armor stones.  
SITE GRADING 
We anticipate site grading for the project will involve cuts and fills less than 2 feet 
to achieve the finished grades.  At the onset of earthwork, areas within the contract grading 
limits should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed. Vegetation, debris, and other unsuitable 
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site to reduce the potential for 
contamination of the excavated materials designated to be reused as fill and/or backfill. Soft 
and/or yielding areas encountered during site preparation operations should be 
over-excavated to expose firm and/or dense materials, and the resulting excavation should

be backfilled with granular fill materials (well-graded 3-inch minus materials) compacted to 
a minimum 90 percent relative compaction. Saturation and subsequent yielding of the 
exposed subgrades due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require 
over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with compacted fill material. The 
excavated soft and/or organic soils should be properly disposed of off-site and/or used in 
landscape areas where appropriate. 
After clearing and grubbing, the existing ground surface should be scarified to a depth 
of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry 
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil 
established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content 
(percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.  
In general, the near-surface sandy and gravelly soils encountered during our field 
exploration should be suitable for use as general fill materials, provided that the maximum 
particle size is less than 3 inches in the largest dimension.  
Imported materials to be used as select granular fill should be non-expansive 
granular material, such as crushed coral or basalt. The select granular fill should be 
well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in the largest 
dimension. The material should have a California Bearing Ratio value of 20 or higher and a 
swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. The 
material should also contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. 
Imported fill materials should be tested for conformance with these recommendations prior 
to delivery to the project site for the intended use. 
Fills and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, 
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to at least 
90 percent relative compaction. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with 
ASTM D1557 test procedures. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, 
multiple-wheel pneumatic-tired rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction equipment. 
Where compaction is less than required, additional compactive effort should be applied with 
adjustment of moisture content as necessary to obtain the specified compaction.

LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from the bulk samples and previous field borings. Variations of the 
subsurface conditions between and beyond the bulk samples and previous field borings 
may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until 
construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to 
re-evaluate the recommendations provided herein. 
The bulk sample and previous field boring locations indicated herein are 
approximate, having been taped from existing visible features at the project site. The 
physical location of the bulk samples and previous field borings should be considered 
accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict 
the approximate boundaries between the soil and/or rock types and, as such, may denote 
a gradual transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown 
on the graphic representations and/or presented in the text of this report. These data have 
been reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. It should be noted 
that the groundwater levels are subject to change due to tidal fluctuation, rainfall, 
temperature, surface runoff and other factors.
