2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was formed by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas
banking against its eastern flank
The project site is located along the northeast flank of the Koolau Mountain In the
mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by detachment
of soil and rock mass from the slopes and movement of the material downslope toward
the axis of the valley by gravity to form colluvial deposits Once the colluvium reaches
streams in the valley alluvial processes become dominant and the sediments are
transported and deposited as alluvium Large areas of alluvium can be found in the
lowland areas and bordering streams
When alluvial and colluvial deposits are left in place or buried for long periods of
time chemical processes begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a
breakdown or weathering of the material The chemical processes also cause
induration or cementation of the coarse grained portion of the sediment resulting in a
poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate Simultaneously erosion
continues in areas above the valley floors and upstream in headwaters This continued
erosion generates material which is transported downslope covering the older alluvial
deposits

Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the control tank and control
building locations we recommend that the new structures be supported on shallow
foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings The footings should
have a minimum width of 12 inches and should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches
below the lowest adjacent finished grade We recommend that the control tank floor and
foundation subgrades be over excavated a minimum of 12 inches and be replaced with
compacted select borrow
Our laboratory test indicates that the near surface clayey soils exhibit a moderate to
high potential for shrinking and swelling when subjected to moisture fluctuations To
reduce the potential for future distress to the slabs on grade we recommend that the
subgrade soils below the slab areas be properly prepared in accordance with the
recommendations presented in the Site Preparation section of this report The areas
under the slab should also be capped off with a 12 inch thick layer of select borrow
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction

3 1 Foundations
Based on the subsurface soil conditions encountered at the site we believe that
shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be used to
support the proposed structures planned for the project An allowable bearing pressure of
up to 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the foundation design of the
proposed new structures This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces

To provide more uniform support for the control tank we recommend that the
subsurface materials below the bottom of the tank floor and foundations be
over excavated and replaced with at least 12 inches of select borrow compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 If soft and or loose
soils are encountered at the bottom the footings or floor subgrades the soft and or
loose soils should be over excavated to the underlying firm or dense materials The
resulting over excavation should be backfilled with the well compacted fill
In general footings should have a minimum width of 12 inches In addition
footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished
grades Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded
below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility
trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to
avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and
to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system Coefficients of
friction of 0 35 and 0 4 may be used for footings bearing on the clayey soils and the
compacted select borrow respectively Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be
estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of
depth pcf This assumes that the soils around the footings are well compacted Unless
covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil
should be neglected

3 2 Slabs On Grade
Based on our field exploration the contro building site is generally underlain by
near surface clayey soils with a moderate to high expansion potential when subjected to
moisture fluctuations To reduce the potential for future distress to the slabs on grade
resulting from shrinking and swelling of the clayey soils due to changes in the moisture
content we recommend that the subgrade soils be properly prepared and capped with at
least 12 inches of select borrow compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction
The select borrow should be placed as soon as possible after the subgrade soils
are prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented in the Site Preparation
section of this report The select borrow and the underlying subgrade soils should be
wetted and kept moist until final placement of concrete Where shrinkage cracks are
noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soils be thoroughly
moistened to close the cracks or the subgrade soils be prepared again Saturation and
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage
may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted fill
Exterior concrete slabs and walkways are not as sensitive to movements and
may be supported directly on a 6 inch thick layer of select borrow or non expansive
select granular fill material The select borrow or select granular fill material should be

reduce the potential of undermining of the pipe support

firm materials are exposed at the bottom of the trench or if the bedding should be
extended deeper as recommended above
Backfill
In general the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the Water
System Standards State of Hawaii 1985 Volume 1 The first zone of backfill
extending from the bedding material to at least 12 inches above the top of the pipes
should consist of select free draining well graded granular material to reduce the
compaction effort required and resulting stresses on the pipe The granular
material should be well graded less than 1 inch in maximum size and should have
W O INC Page 17

less than 10 percent passing the No 200 sieve Compaction by water ponding or
jetting may be allowed provided the granular material is self draining such as
No 3B Fine gravel
The trench backfill from 12 inches above the top of the pipes to the finished
subgrade may consist of the excavated on site clayey gravelly and sandy soils
provided that they are free of deleterious materials vegetation and are screened
of particles greater than 6 inches in largest dimension The backfill should be
placed in thin lifts normally 8 inches in loose lift thickness with each lift
mechanically compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of
the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1557 A typical section of the trench is presented on
Plate 4 It should be noted that boulders were encountered in our borings
Boulders encountered in the excavated soils should not be re used as trench
backfill
Trenches should be kept free of water during backfilling operations Should
backfilling below the groundwater level be necessary free draining granular
material such as No 3B Fine gravel should be used to reduce the potential of
future settlement of the trench backfill Where the free draining granular materials
are used a filter fabric such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent should be used to wrap
the free draining materials

Settlements
Primary settlement of the new water main construction is normally caused by the
difference in the unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier
compacted backfill material placed over the pipes The net increase in loading will
cause settlement of the underlying subsoils below the trench invert Based on our
calculations primary settlement on the order of less than 1 inch is anticipated for
the project
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good
workmanship will be engaged during construction Additional settlement could
occur if improper trench support or dewatering procedures are used Therefore
we believe that the proposed transmission main should be designed with
sufficient flexibility to accommodate possible settlements

Field Resistivity Survey
Geophysical survey equipment i e resistance meter was mobilized to the site on
September 14 and 19 2001 for the field survey In general our field work was
performed in sunny conditions with generally dry surface conditions
Seventy five soil resistivity surveys were conducted at about 300 foot intervals at
24 locations designated as R 1 through R 24 along the transmission main
alignment The soil resistivity surveys were performed by using the in situ Wenner
4 Pin Method and a resistance meter Nilsson Model 400 manufactured by Nilsson
Electrical Laboratory Inc The method utilizes four surface probes or pins
electrodes to evaluate the average resistivity an indication of corrosivity of the
underlying soil mass The average resistivity values of the soil mass extending
from the existing ground surface to the test depth was assessed by using this
method at the approximate survey locations as shown on the General Site Plan
Plate 2 Enlarged site plans are shown on Plates 3 1 through 3 7
The soil resistivity surveys were performed at the existing ground surface along the
proposed alignment of the 12 inch diameter transmission main The average soil
resistance along the proposed alignment was evaluated at each survey location
with the electrodes aligned at equal spacing of generally 3 to 5 feet at the existing
ground surface The spacing of the electrodes were generally increased to 6 to
8 feet at the stream drainage crossings where deeper invert elevations are
anticipated The depth of penetration of a resistivity sounding is a function of the
electrode spacing
The resistance meter Nilsson Model 400 impresses a voltage between the outer
electrodes causing an electrical current to flow between them The voltage drop
between the inner electrodes is then measured by a sensitive built in voltmeter As
a result the soil resistance in ohms can be found directly on the dial The soil
resistivity in ohm cm which is directly related to both resistance and spacing can
then be computed

Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects In general the
lower the resistivity the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic
structure Conversely the higher the resistivity the less likely the soil will contribute
to corrosion to metallic objects
In general our test results indicate that the subsurface materials along most of the
12 inch diameter transmission main alignment have corrosion ratings between

3 8 Drainage
The finished grades outside the new structures should be sloped to shed water
away from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive landscape
watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to
foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the
potential for water infiltration into the subsoils Drainage swales should be provided as
soon as possible and maintained to drain surface run off away from the slabs and
foundations
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent loss of strength and or heaving of the near surface clayey soils The
foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after
setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
One one inch ring swell test was performed on a relatively undisturbed sample to
evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results of the test are
summarized on Plate B 1
Four Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results
of these tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Three direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested Results of the direct
shear test are presented on Plates B 3 through B 5
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed
on bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 6 and B 7

APPENDIX C
Soil Corrosion Study
APPENDIX C
Soil Corrosion Study
Seventy five soil resistivity surveys were conducted at about 300 foot intervals at
twenty four locations designated as R 1 through R 24 along the proposed transmission
main alignment The soil resistivity surveys were performed by using the in situ Wenner
4 Pin Method and a resistance meter Nilsson Model 400 manufactured by Nilsson
Electrical Laboratory Inc The results of the soil resistivity surveys are summarized on
Plates C 1 through C 7
In addition a laboratory analyses study was performed on 10 selected soil
samples The soil samples were tested by Oceanic Analytical Laboratory Inc The
laboratory tests included the pH value EPA SW9045B chloride content and
sulfate content The results of the soil analyses are summarized on
Plate C 8
