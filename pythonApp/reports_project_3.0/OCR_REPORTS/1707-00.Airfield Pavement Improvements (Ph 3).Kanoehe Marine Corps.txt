Geology
Oahu the third largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers
approximately 600 square miles The island was formed by the activity of
two large shield shaped volcanoes These volcanoes were Waianae which
GEOLAEBS HAWAI

was the first shield to emerge from the ocean and Koolau which emerged
after Waianae After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which
erosion cut canyons several thousand feet deep into the Koolau shield
volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder
and tuff cone formation These series are collectively referred to as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series
The site of the MCAS at Kaneohe Bay is located over the Honolulu
series The three volcanic vents on Mokapu Peninsula are believed to be
among the oldest of the series These vents were the salients permitting
the growth of coral reef that merged with the main island of Oahu
After lowering of the sea level sand from emerged coral reefs was
blown inland by the prevailing northeast trade winds to form sand dunes
along the northern portion of the airfield An extensive deposit of
unconsolidated calcareous marine sediments which is chiefly beach sand
made up of water worn coral shells and marine organisms covers the
western tip of Mckapu Peninsula Reclamation fills consisting of marine
muds shells coral fragments and marine organisms dredged from the ocean
floor have been used to build up the area around the southern and western
shoreline of Mokapu Peninsula
The beach sand deposit and dredged fill material are located along
the western one half of the runway taxiways and parking aprons
In general the subgrade materials at the site consists mainly of
silty coral gravel and sand

Design Modulus of Subgrade Reaction
In situ tests for modulus of subgrade reaction K value were
carried out in each of the ten test pits and in general accordance to the
ASTM D 1196 64 test method
Detailed descriptions of the testing procedure and data analysis are
presented in Appendix B The test equipment and its typical set up are
shown on Plate B 1 The test pits were excavated to depths of 4 0 to 4 5
feet below the existing pavement surface by using a hoptoe In situ plate
load tests were performed on the natural soil conditions exposed by
excavation at different selected depths for analyzing the variation of
strength properties with depths It is believed that the natural soil
condition would be disturbed by excavation Therefore the plate load
tests were repeated one or two times to evaluate the change of K values
upon densification due to repeated loading of the subgrade soil and to
eliminate most of the inelastic soil deformation In situ plate load
tests were also performed on backfilled and recompacted soil conditions in
order to obtain strength properties for the recompacted subgrade
condition The resulting load deflection curves are included in Plates
B 3 to B 18 and a summary of the K values obtained are presented in Plates
B 19 and B 20
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 1707 00 Page 10
Dk No 19
The K values for the natural soil condition ranged from 78 to 198
pci with an average of about 100 pci The low K values are most probably
Que to disturbances by excavation and would be too conservative for
pavement design There is no specific trend of variation between the
K values and depths The average K value for natural soil conditions with
depths at or less than 18 inches is 132 pci while that for depths greater
than 18 inches is 119 pci The difference between the above two average
K values is small and therefore no sound conclusion can be drawn on the
relationship between soil strength properties and depths This agrees
with our field observations that there is no apparent distinction between
subgrade and base course materials below the existing pavement
As shown in Plates B 19 and B 20 the subgrade moduli obtained by
repeating the plate load test at the same test location were found to
increase significantly with resulting K values ranging from 230 to 750 pci
and an average K value of 350 pci This increase appears to reflect
elimination of inelastic deformation and densification of the subgrade
soil by the repeated plate load tests The undisturbed in situ subgrade
modulus is believed to have a value somewhere between the K value of 350
pci obtained from repetitive tests under the natural soil condition and
the K value of 120 pci from the initial test results on the excavated
soil For pavement design purposes we believe the undisturbed in situ
K value of 230 pci can be used
Using the same soil excavated from the test pits for backfill
K values were found to range from 112 to 310 pci on backfilled and
recompacted subgrade The 6 inch thick layer of dark brown silty sand
beneath the test area in Test Pit 1 is believed to be the cause for a low
value of 112 pci The average for the rest of the K values is about 250
pei For pavement thickness design purposes a subgrade modulus of 250
pei was used since the subgrade soils would be scarified and recompacted
in the pavement construction

Design Subgrade California Bearing Ratio CBR
Laboratory CBR tests were performed in accordance to ASTM D 1883
test method on bulk samples obtained from the test pits In order to
obtain the design CBR the procedure outlined in Section 3 of the Navy
Design Manual DM 21 3 Flexible Pavement Design for Airfields of August
1978 was followed
Since the subgrade soil type is similar across the project area
bulk samples obtained from the various test pits were combined to obtain
composite samples for two complete series of laboratory CBR tests
Results from the first series of tests with samples from test pits TP 1 to
TP 5 are summarized on Plates C 25 and C 26 A design CBR of 36 was
obtained for a minimum compaction of 95 and moisture content between 7
and 15 percent
A second series of tests were then performed on composite samples
from test pits TP 6 to TP 10 in order to confirm the above design CBR and
to account for soil variability Results from the tests are summarized on
Plates C 27 and C 28 with a design CBR of 32 obtained for a minimum
compaction of 95 and moisture content between 10 and 14 percent
The average design CBR from these two series of tests is 34 A
smaller design CBR value of 30 was selected for use in our analysis to
account for some localized soil variability From the approximate
relationship between CBR and subgrade modulus Figure 3 of NAVFAC DM 5 4
October 1979 a CBR of 30 correlates approximately to a subgrade modulus
of 300 pci which is slightly larger than the design subgrade mcedulus of
250 pci obtained from the field plate load tests on the compacted soil
It is our opinion this confirms a relatively uniform subgrade soil
type condition

Pavement Joints
Joints in concrete pavements should be provided to 1 allow
contraction and expansion caused by temperature and moisture changes in
the pavement 2 relieve warping and curling stresses caused by
temperature differential within pavements 3 prevent irregular or random
cracking of pavements from unequal subgrade support or other causes 4
control shrinkage and cracking and 5 expedite construction
NAVFAC Type Specification TS 02614 latest revision Joints
Reinforcements and Mooring Eyes in Concrete Pavement should be referred
to for joint details and construction requirements Joint sealing
compounds used should be jet fuel resistant and blast resistant and should
follow the recommendations of the same specification TS 02614
Pavement Drainage
Surface runoff from the project and pavement areas should be
collected and drained to a suitable discharge point Water should not be
allowed to pond immediately adjacent to the pavement areas The site
should be graded to provide positive drainage away from the pavement
sections

Soft spots encountered at the subgrade level should be removed and

The subgrade soil disclosed by our exploration program was composed
of tan and white silty sand and coralline gravels The maximum dry
densities determined by ASTM D 1557 78 on bulk soil samples from test pits
varied from a low value of 99 pef for silty sand with traces of coralline

results of our recent field density tests taken on the compacted with
small compaction equipment backfill at the test pits it appears that the
subgrade soil can be compacted to 100 of its maximum dry density
established by ASTM D 1557 78 without unusual difficulties
From field and laboratory tests the recompacted subgrade was found
to have a relatively high design K value of 250 pci and design CBR of 30
Therefore cement or asphalt stabilization of the subgrade soils is not
recommended

High groundwater level may affect subgrade compaction criteria since
it is difficult to compact saturated silty sand to its maximum dry
density Groundwater level observed in our borings varied from 4 0 to 7 2
feet below the existing ground surface No groundwater level was
encountered in all of the ten test pits which were excavated to depths of
4 0 to 4 5 feet below ground surface Since the highest groundwater level
encountered during our investigation is about 4 0 feet below the ground
surface which corresponds to the bottom of the 90 compaction zone Plate
D 8 we believe the water level should not significantly affect the
subgrade compaction
From the grain size distribution study and rough correlation between
capillary rise and effective grain size the anticipated height of
capillary rise would be on the order of one foot or less The capillary
effect combined with the highest groundwater level may create temporary
compaction difficulty If this is encountered it is recommended that the
compaction be carried out during low tide as indicated on the tide
calendar in order to attain adequate subgrade compaction
Due to the granular nature of subsoil the subgrade soil can be
classified as permeable estimated coefficient of permeability ranges from

In each of the ten 10 test pits excavated in situ density and
plate load tests were performed at selected depths below the existing
ground surface The test pits were excavated to depths of 4 0 to 4 5 feet
below the ground surface with a hoptoe The soil conditions exposed by
excavation for in situ tests were treated as natural and undisturbed
The test pits were then backfilled and compacted with a hand operated
compactor to the original ground elevation In situ tests were also
performed on the backfilled and recompacted soil conditions in order to
determine soil properties for a compacted subgrade condition Plate load
tests on the natural soil conditions were repeated one to two times Test
Run Nos 2 and 3 in order to determine the relative amount of inelastic
deformation in the first test Test Run No 1 and to reduce the effects
of disturbance caused by the excavation
In situ plate load tests for determination of the modulus of
subgrade reaction K value were carried out in general accordance with
ASTM D 1196 64 test method The test equipment and its typical set up are
shown on Plate B 1 A trailer truck with approximately 10 tons of dead
weight on its bed was used for the load reaction The truck was placed
over the test pit so that one of the I beams in its bed was located
directly above the center of the test pit A hydraulic jack was then used
to jack against this I beam with four 1 inch thick steel plates stacked on
the bottom of the jack as support The steel plates had diameters of 12
18 24 and 30 inches and were stacked in this sequence with the largest
plate at the bottom Two dial gages graduated in units of 0 001 inch
were used to measure the deflections near the circumference of the 30 inch
diameter plate A capacity proving ring was used to measure
the applied load
An initial load of psi was used to seat the loading
system and bearing plates This seating load was also considered to be
the zero load Then without releasing the seating load loads were
applied in increments of psi with each load increment held
until the rate of deformation was less than 0 001 inch per minute
Readings of the dial micrometers at the end of each load increment were
recorded Following the completion of the 10 psi load
increment the average deflection was determined by averaging the total
movement between the zero and 10 psi increment for each dial The
modulus of subgrade reaction K value was taken as the value of 10 psi
divided by this average deflection Following recommendations by the

Military Standard 621A Method 104 Modulus of Soil Reaction computed
K values larger than 200 pci were corrected for poor seating of the plates
and or nonlinear load deformation relations The correction consists of
drawing a straight line parallel to the straight line portion of the
plotted curve through the origin of the load deformation relation The
deflection at 10 psi indicated by this straight line was used in computing
K values The resulting K values larger than 200 pci were further
corrected for plate bending effects by using the correction curve shown in
Plate B 2 The effects of plate bending on K values less than 200 pci are
small and negligible
Results obtained from the plate load tests are plotted and included
in Plates B 3 to B 18 A summary of the K values is included in Plates

Tests for density of in situ soil in the test pits were carried out
after excavation or backfilling by the sand cone method following the
procedure detailed in the ASTM D 1556 82 test method in order to determine
the in situ density and the compaction characteristics of the sandy
subsoil The results are summarized on Plates B 21 and B 22 In general
there was no difficulty encountered in the compaction of the backfill of
the test pits and the subgrade to a minimum of 95 of its maximum dry
density established by ASTM D 1557 78

APPENDIX C
LABORATORY TESTING
APPENDIX c
Laboratory Testing
Ss sh i Re ee
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on
selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Log of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Twenty nine 29 sieve analysis tests were performed on samples of
the underlying sandy soils to evaluate its grain size distribution The
test results are summarized on Plates C 1 through C 6 and are plotted on
Plates C 10 through C 20 for visualization and comparison purposes
To further evaluate the index properties of the soil samples four
4 Atterberg Limit tests and three 3 specific gravity tests were
performed and the test results are summarized on Plates C 7 and C 8
respectively
Four 4 moisture density determinations were performed on selected
samples to determine the compaction characteristics of the subsoils
These test results are summarized on Plate C 9 and plotted on Plates C 21
through C 24
Two 2 laboratory CBR tests were performed on bulk samples of the
subsoil to evaluate the suitability of the soil for pavement support The
test results are summarized on Plate C 8
Two full series CBR tests were performed on composite bulk samples
obtained from test pits in accordance with the method outlined in Section
3 of the Navy Design Manual DM 21 3 in order to evaluate the relationships
between the moisture density and CBR value of the compacted soil and to
estimate the design CBR value for use in flexible pavement analysis The
test results are plotted on Plates C 25 through C 28
Two 2 consolidation tests were performed on selected undisturbed
samples to evaluate the settlement characteristics of the subsoil The
curves are presented on Plates C 29 and C 30
