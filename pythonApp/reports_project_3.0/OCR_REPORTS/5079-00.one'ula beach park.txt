2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is comprised largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of
the eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in
central Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea
When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits
at correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in
the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials Land development and reclamation projects
within the last century have brought the project site to its present form

2 3 Subsurface Conditions
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling three borings designated
as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths ranging from about 2 8 to 15 8 feet
below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by
surface fills on the order of about 3 feet thick consisting of very stiff silty clays and
medium dense coralline sands and gravel Below the surface fills coral formation was
encountered and extended to the maximum depth explored of approximately 15 8 feet
below the existing ground surface The coral formation generally appeared to be
moderately weathered and medium hard in nature It should be noted that the strength
characteristics of coral formation are highly variable The depositional nature of the
formation may create localized areas of hard crystalline coral intermixed with soft
severely fractured detritus It should be noted that coral formation is frequently
cavernous or contain pockets of voids Although cavities were not encountered in our
drilled borings the potential for the presence of cavities and or voids exists at the
project site
Groundwater was encountered at a depth of about 6 5 feet below the existing

groundwater level encountered in the boring generally corresponds to approximately

such as seasonal precipitation and storm surge conditions

3 1 Building Foundations
Based on the design concept and the subsoil conditions encountered at the site
we believe that spread and or continuous footings may be used to support the proposed
bathhouse planned at the site An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds
per square foot psf may be used for design of footing foundations bearing on the
recompacted in situ soils or new compacted fills This bearing value is for
those caused by wind or seismic forces
For footings bearing on the in situ stiff dense soils the bottom of the footing
excavations should be recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction to
provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to placement of reinforcing
steel or concrete If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the
footing excavations the materials should be over excavated to expose the underlying
firm materials The over excavation may be backfilled with on site soils compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom may be extended
down to the underlying competent materials For footings bearing on new compacted
fills the bottom of the footing excavations should also be recompacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
W O INC Page 8
Hawaii California

ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by
dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the
lowest adjacent finish grades If foundations are located next to utility trenches or
easements they should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for
footings bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or new compacted fills as
recommended herein to be less than 1 inch total with differential settlements on the
order of one half 2 inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive
earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A
coefficient of friction of 0 3 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted in situ
soils or new compacted fills Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be
estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of
depth pcf This assumes that the soil around footings is well compacted Unless
covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil
should be neglected
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative from
Geolabs prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation
bearing conditions and the required embedment depths

In general we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer consisting
of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be placed below the slab to
provide more uniform support of the slab Due to the nature of the bathhouse we
believe that the floor slab will be subjected to water infiltration Therefore it is our
opinion that the moisture barrier commonly placed over the gravel cushion may be
omitted for the bathouse slab
Exterior concrete walkways are planned for the proposed project We
recommend that concrete slabs on grade for these areas be supported on at least
6 inches of non expansive select granular fill or aggregate subbase Control joints
should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways and at right angle
intersections The area adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly against the
slab with low expansion relatively impervious soil and graded to divert water away from
the slab to reduce the potential of water ponding around the foundation
Slab subgrades should be prepared by scarifying the subgrade soils to a depth of
about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton vibratory drum roller to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction Slab subgrades should be kept moist until covered by concrete
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend
that the soils be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks and recompacted to provide a
firm base prior to concrete placement Saturation and subsequent yielding of the
exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted fill

3 5 On Site Sewage Disposal System
Based on the information provided we understand that on site sewage treatment
and disposal system using septic tank and leach field is planned for the new bathhouse
facility Therefore it was desired to conduct field percolation tests at the future leach
field area in order to obtain data for preliminary design of the sewage disposal system
Field percolation testing was performed in general accordance with the State of
Systems and Chapter 10 of the Ten States Standards to evaluate the permeability of
the subsurface soils for wastewater disposal The percolation tests were performed by
inch diameter boreholes to depths of about 3 and 4 feet below the existing
ground surface

Hawaii California

Approximately one inch of gravel was then placed at the bottom of the boreholes
to protect the bottom of the holes from scouring and sediments Based on the results of
the trial runs the holes were presoaked with 12 inches of water for at least 4 hours and
then permitted to swell for at least 12 hours before testing During testing the holes
were carefully filled with water to a minimum depth of 6 inches above the gravel pack
The time intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which the
calculation of the percolation tests was based
Based on the percolation tests conducted we believe that a percolation rate
ranging from 10 to 30 minutes per inch may be used for the subject site Based on
Table II in the Manual of Septic Tank Practice by the US Department of Health
Education and Welfare this corresponds to a maximum rate of sewage application of
about 0 9 to 1 6 gallons per square foot per day Due to the highly variable nature of
the coral formation localized loose pockets and cemented zones underlying the project
site the absorption capacity of the disposal system should be confirmed by additional
percolation tests during construction Results of the individual percolation tests are

APPENDIX A

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths ranging from
about 2 8 to 15 8 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the
borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using
truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of coral rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the coral rock formation The coral rock material
left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of soil rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of coral rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the

APPENDIX C

APPENDIX C
Field Percolation Testing
The percolation rates of the in situ soils were evaluated by performing two
percolation tests at two selected locations designated as P 1 and P 2 at the approximate
locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The percolation tests were performed by inch diameter boreholes to
depths of about 3 and 4 feet below the existing ground surface Approximately one inch of
gravel was then placed at the bottom of the boreholes to protect the bottom of the holes
from scouring and sediments Based on the results of the trial runs the holes were
presoaked with 12 inches of water for at least 4 hours and then permitted to swell for at
least 12 hours before testing During testing the holes were carefully filled with water to a
minimum depth of 6 inches above the gravel pack The time intervals and water drops
were then recorded to provide data upon which the calculation of the percolation tests was
based The test results of the field percolation tests are presented on Plates C 1 and C 2
