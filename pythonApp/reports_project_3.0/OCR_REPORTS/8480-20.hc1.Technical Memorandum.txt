Kohala Shoreline Subdivision Development 
North Kohala, Island of Hawaii, Hawaii 
This technical memorandum summarizes our understanding of the project and 
presents our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations for the drywell 
design of the proposed Kohala Shoreline Subdivision Development project in the North 
Kohala area on the Island of Hawaii, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity 
are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. It should be noted that geotechnical 
engineering recommendations for the design of foundations, slabs-on-grade, retaining 
structures, site grading, pavements, and underground utility lines are provided in a 
geotechnical engineering exploration report prepared under a separate cover. 
It should be noted that our findings and recommendations presented herein are 
based on our recently completed borings and field percolation tests. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
memorandum. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located along the shoreline on the west side of the Kohala area 
on the Island of Hawaii, Hawaii. The project site is located southwest of Akoni Pule 
Highway, near where Kohala Ranch Road intersects with Akoni Pule Highway. 
We understand that this project consists of the following elements: 
1. 
Improvements along Akoni Pule Highway to accommodate roadways into 
the project site. We understand that Akoni Pule Highway is under the 
jurisdiction of the State of Hawaii Department of Transportation (HDOT). 
Therefore, the new pavements, guard rails, and water and electrical utilities 
along Akoni Pule Highway need to be designed per the HDOT standards. 
2. 
Installation of the North Access roadway and parking area will be performed 
at this time. We understand that the South Access Road within the 
right-of-way of Akoni Pule Highway will also be constructed at this time.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 and 102, were drilled to depths of 
about 51.5 and 52.5 feet below the existing ground surface for the drywell design 
purposes. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, the borings encountered a surface clinker layer, about 6 feet thick, 
consisting of very dense silty sand with some gravel, cobbles, and boulders underlain by 
interbedded layers of basalt formation and clinker materials extending to the maximum 
depth drilled of 52.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Basalt formation encountered 
generally consisted of soft to hard, severely to moderately fractured, and extremely to 
slightly weathered basalt.  
Our borings did not encounter cavities and/or voids. However, it should be noted 
that lava tubes, blisters and/or cavities are commonly encountered in basalt formations. 
Therefore, the potential for presence of lava tubes, blisters, and/or cavities exists at the 
project site.  
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
explorations. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change 
due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
Detailed descriptions of the field exploration methodology are presented in 
Appendix A. Descriptions and graphic representations of the materials encountered in the 
borings are presented on the Logs of Borings in Appendix A. Laboratory tests were 
performed on selected soil and rock samples and the test results are presented in 
Appendix B. Photographs of core samples are presented in Appendix C.

Field Permeability Testing 
Constant Head permeability tests were performed in the borings drilled at the 
project site to assess the average k-Value within an open interval of rock material. In 
general, the borings were drilled using HQ wireline coring tools advanced to the scheduled 
test depths. Upon reaching the desired test depth, the HQ casing was pulled up to expose 
an open interval of basalt rock formation and/or clinker. The diameter of the open borehole 
was 4.0 inches. Constant head tests were performed at open rock/clinker depth intervals 
of about 31.5 to 41.5 feet and 41.5 to 51.5 feet below the existing ground surface for 
Boring No. 101 and at depth intervals of about 32.5 to 42.5 feet and 42.5 to 52.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface for Boring No. 102, respectively. The constant head 
tests were conducted using the discharge hose of a water truck, which could provide a 
maximum flow rate of up to about 68.9 gallons per minute (gpm).  
It should be noted that after approximately 10 to 15 minutes of continual pumping, a 
hydrostatic head could not be established in the constant head tests. Therefore, the 
k-values for the selected test intervals may be greater than the values presented herein. 
Due to site and equipment constraints, the pumping capacity during the test could not be 
increased.  
The field data for the constant head tests were analyzed using the 
Well Point - Filter in Uniform Soil equation presented in “Construction Dewatering and 
Groundwater Control, New Methods and Applications,” 3rd Edition by Powers, Corwin, 
Schmall, and Kaeck (2007). The following table summarizes the k-values calculated from

Stormwater Treatment System  
Clogging can be a major factor in the loss of capacity and ultimate failure of 
drywells and similar disposal systems. Clogging can result from sediment and debris 
entering the drywell and either blocking the permeable features, such as pores and 
fractures, or filling the interior of the drywell. 
To reduce clogging in the final drywells, we recommend including a stormwater 
treatment system in the overall drywell system design. There are several commercially 
available systems that could be incorporated into the stormwater disposal systems for 
the new drywell sites.

LIMITATIONS 
The findings and discussions submitted herein are based, in part, upon information 
obtained from the field borings. Variations of subsurface conditions beyond the borings 
may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until 
construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to 
re-evaluate the findings and discussions provided herein. 
The field boring locations indicated herein are approximate, having been estimated 
by using a hand-held Global Positioning System (GPS) device. Elevations of the borings 
were estimated based on the contours shown on the same Site Plan provided by Belt 
Collins Hawaii dated June 1, 2018. The physical location and elevation of the borings 
should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on graphic representations of the borings depict the 
approximate boundaries between soil types and, as such, may denote a gradual transition. 
Water level data from the boring were measured at the times shown on the graphic 
representations and/or presented in the text herein. These data have been reviewed and 
interpretations made in the formulation of this report. However, it must be noted that 
groundwater level changes may occur due to seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, 
and other factors.
