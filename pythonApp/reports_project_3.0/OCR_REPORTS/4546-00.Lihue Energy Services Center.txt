REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lava of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene Epoch
more than 2 million years ago Following the cessation of this main shield building phase
there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lava of the post erosional
Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the alluvial sediments of the Palikea
Formation
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series are generally characterized by thick flows of dense
basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various locales
Associated with the vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low cinder cones at
the vent Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of
Kauai
During the Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental glaciers
accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to fill the oceanic
basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels rose because more
water was available The land mass of Kauai remained essentially stable during these
changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic fluctuations
resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower relative to the present sea
level on Kauai
GEOLABS INC
W O 4546 00 Page 6
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of terrigenous
sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at correspondingly higher
elevations and lagoon marine sediments in the quiet waters protected by fringing reefs
Most of the soils in the project area consist of residual and saprolite soils derived from the
in situ weathering of igneous rocks and alluvial soils that were deposited from streams

In general the project site is underlain by alluvial and residual deposits consisting of
hard to very hard silty clays and clayey silts grading to very stiff clayey silts extending to the
maximum depths explored of about 11 5 to 41 5 feet below the existing ground surface

Shallow Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site the structures
proposed for the KPP and switchyard and the booster pump station may be supported on
shallow foundation systems consisting of spread footings continuous footings and or mat
foundations
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grades If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements
the foundations should be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V
imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as
the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent
below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for
foundation settlement In addition footings adjacent to existing slopes should extend deeper
to obtain a minimum 8 foot setback distance measured horizontally from the outside edge
of the footing to the face of the slope
The soils encountered at the footing subgrade level should be moisture conditioned
to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to not less than 90 percent
relative compaction If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the footing subgrades
the materials should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The
resulting over excavation should be backfilled with select granular materials and compacted
to not less than 90 percent relative compaction

Drilled Shaft Foundations
In lieu of shallow foundations the deadend towers at the switchyard site may be
supported on drilled shafts to resist the anticipated uplift loads Based on the structural loads
provided and the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we recommend that
drilled shafts with a diameter of 30 inches and an embedment length of about 19 feet below
the design finished grade be used to support each post of the deadend towers The
cast in place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support from the adhesion between
the shafts and the surrounding soils An allowable compressive load capacity of up to
180 kips per shaft may be used The compressive load is for dead plus live loads and may
be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces In general uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the dead weight of the
drilled shaft and by shear along the shaft surface and the adjacent soils Considering that
the drilled shafts are designed based on adhesion between the shaft and the surrounding
soils we recommend that an uplift load capacity of 80 kips be used to resist uplift loads
The uplift load capacity provided include the weight of the drilled shaft The structural
capacity of the shaft member in tension should be checked by the project structural
engineer
Lateral loads imposed on the foundations may be resisted by a combination of the
following 1 resistance of the shaft soil system to deflect and 2 passive resistance on the
vertical faces of shaft caps where applicable Lateral load resistance of the drilled shaft is
a function of the stiffness of the surrounding material the stiffness of the shaft allowable

deflection at the top of shaft and induced moment in the shaft Based on the information
provided each drilled shaft will be subjected to a lateral load of about 15 kips and an

Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations
Drilled shafts are desirable for the deadend towers because of the significant
increase in lateral and uplift load capacities when compared to shallow foundations
However the performance of the drilled shafts will depend upon the contractor s
method of construction and construction procedures In our opinion the following
conditions would have an effect on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft
foundations
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the frictional
resistance between the shaft and the surrounding Therefore proper construction
techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in drilling the shaft
holes and in placing concrete into the drilled holes The drilled shafts are designed
to be embedded into the very stiff to very hard clayey soils encountered in the borings
Some boulders should be expected in the clayey soil horizon given the geologic
conditions at the site
Based on the estimated lengths of the drilled shafts groundwater is not expected in
the drilled holes during the shaft installation In general placement of concrete may
be accomplished by free fall methods provided that the concrete is placed in a
GEOLABS INC
W O 4546 00 Page 14
suitable manner that reduces the potential for segregation of aggregates from the
concrete mix However due to factors such as seasonal rainfall and perched water
groundwater may be encountered in the drilled holes In addition there is the
potential for water seepage into the drilled holes considering the relatively wet
environment in this area If groundwater is encountered in the drilled holes then
concrete should be placed by tremie methods A low shrink high slump 7 to 9 inch
slump range concrete mix should be used to provide close contact between the
drilled shafts and the surrounding soils The concrete should be placed promptly after
drilling within 24 hours to reduce the potential for softening of the sides of the drilled
holes
It is imperative that a representative from Geolabs be present at the site to observe
the drilling and installation of drilled shafts during construction Drilling by methods
utilizing drilling fluids is not recommended because use of drilling fluids may reduce
the frictional resistance between the drilled shaft and surrounding soils
Slabs On Grade
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by near surface
clayey soils with a low expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations
However to reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade
resulting from shrinking and swelling of the clayey soils due to changes in the moisture
content we recommend that the subgrade soils be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to
not less than 90 percent relative compaction ASTM D 1557
The underlying subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist until the final
placement of slab concrete Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the
subgrade we recommend that the soils be prepared again as recommended Saturation and
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage
may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted fill
GEOLABS INC
W O 4546 00 Page 15
In general we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill be placed
below interior slabs such as the office and control building areas to provide the slabs with
more uniform support The cushion fill should consist of open graded gravels such as
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation which would also serve as a capillary
break To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor
coverings an impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is
recommended on top of the cushion layer Two inches of fine sand may be considered
above the plastic membrane to provide for protection of the membrane and to aid in curing
of the slab concrete Provision of the 2 inches of fine sand above the vapor barrier will be
left up to the discretion of the project structural engineer Flexible floor coverings such as
carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered since they can better mask minor slab cracking
Where the slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic or equipment operation we
recommend that a 6 inch layer of aggregate subbase compacted to not less than 95 percent
relative compaction be provided in lieu of the 4 inch thick cushion layer The 2 inch moist
fine sand and vapor barrier may also be omitted for these slabs The aggregate subbase
should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to not less than 95 percent relative
compaction For the design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of about
200 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection pci may be used for the compacted
aggregate subbase Where slabs are intended to function as rigid pavements a minimum
slab thickness of 6 inches may be used for preliminary design purposes In addition
provisions should be made for proper load transfer across the slab joints that will be
subjected to vehicular traffic
We envision that some exterior concrete walkways will be required for the proposed
project We recommend that concrete walkways be supported on at least 6 inches of
aggregate subbase The aggregate subbase should be compacted to not less than
90 percent relative compaction Control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the
width of the walkways with expansion joints provided at right angle intersections

Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic pressures
A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of permeable
material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation placed
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations facing down at the base of the
wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used
instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also be
hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of the on site clayey silt or
select granular fill material Unless covered by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches
of backfill should consist of low expansion relatively impervious soils to reduce water
infiltration behind the walls

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site The excavated clayey
residual and alluvial soils in the cut areas may be reused as a source of general fill
material provided that they meet the requirements for general fill presented in the
Fills and Backfills subsection of this report
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below any areas
designated to receive fill should be over excavated to expose firm natural material
and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill The
excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site and or
used in landscape areas where appropriate Contract documents should include
additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and engineered fill placement
to account for variations in the over excavation quantities
After clearing and grubbing finished subgrades and areas designated to receive fills
should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least
2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to not less than 90 percent
relative compaction The compaction requirement should be increased to not less
than 95 percent relative compaction in areas subjected to vehicle traffic Where
shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the
soil be thoroughly moistened to close all cracks or prepared again as recommended
above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to
inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas
and replacement with well compacted fill The need for over excavation should be
evaluated by a representative from Geolabs in the field

Fills and Backfills
General fill material may be used to raise the existing grades to the finished
subgrades General fill materials needed to raise the existing grades to the
proposed finished subgrades may consist of the excavated on site soils generated
from the excavations provided that the materials are free of vegetation deleterious

It should be noted that boulders were encountered in our borings Therefore some
over sized materials should be expected in the excavated materials and should not
be used as general fill materials It should also be noted that some of the on site
soils consist of high in situ moisture contents on the order of about 30 to 40 percent
moisture contents Therefore appreciable compactive effort and or drying of the
soils may be necessary to achieve the compaction requirements with some of the
high moisture soils
Imported fill materials if required should consist of select granular material such as
crushed coral basalt gravel or cinder sand The material should be well graded from
coarse to fine with no particles greater than 3 inches in largest dimension and should
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material
should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell
of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D1883

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other
types of acceptable compaction equipment Water tamping jetting or ponding should
not be allowed to compact the on site clayey soils General fill materials should be
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to not
less than 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density

Percolation Testing
Five percolation tests were performed at various locations of the project site to
evaluate the infiltration characteristics of the underlying soils Our borings drilled for the
storm drainage facilities encountered hard to very hard silty Clays extending to depths of
about 6 to 10 feet below the existing ground surface The hard to very hard silty clays were
underlain by stiff to hard clayey silts extending to the maximum depths explored of about
11 5 to 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface
The falling head method was used for the percolation testing After the boring was
drilled water was injected into the boring To obtain a steady state percolation rate the soil
was allowed to soak in the water for at least four hours After the initial soaking period water
was readjusted and drop of the water level in the boring was measured along with time
The tests indicate that the subsurface silty clay and clayey silt soils
have low infiltration capability with coefficient of permeability k in the range of 2 x 10 to
5x 10 feet per second

Drainage
Since the project site is relatively level and located in a high rainfall environment
special attention should be given to site drainage during and after construction The finished
grades outside the structures should be sloped to shed water away from foundations and to
reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems be installed around
the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from the foundation and pavement
areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be
avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and
drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be properly
backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of the concrete to
reduce water infiltration In addition drainage swales should be provided as soon as
possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away from slabs and
foundations

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Two one one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples
to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results of these tests are
summarized on Plate B 1
Three Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test results are
presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentation
of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Two direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples of the
soils to evaluate shear strength characteristics of the soils Graphic presentation of the test
results are presented on Plates B 3 and B 4
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the
soils The test results are presented on Plates B 5 and B 6
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 91 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates B 7 and B 8

APPENDIX C
Field Percolation Testing
Percolation tests were performed by using the falling head method in five of the
borings drilled Boring 9 13 and 14 The test borings have or had a diameter of
4 inches and depths of 5 5 to 13 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of testing
Water was injected into the test borings and was allowed to soak for at least 4 hours to
achieve a steady state percolation rate Water was again injected into the test borings and
the drop in water level with respect to time was recorded The water levels within the test
borings were monitored by an electric water level sounder The results of the percolation
tests are presented on Plates C 1 through C 5
