INTRODUCTION
In general accordance with the Revised Amendment No 2 issued by PACDIV
dated August 24th and our revised fee proposal dated September 6th we have
completed our geotechnical engineering evaluation of the boulder survey and potential
rockfall hazard on the northeast slope of Pu u Hawaii Loa at the proposed family
housing project site within the Marine Corps Base Hawaii at Kaneohe Bay Hawaii
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed family housing project site is located within the Kaneohe Marine
Corps Base Hawaii on Mokapu Peninsula in Kaneohe on the island of Oahu Hawaii
It is proposed to provide 300 new units of enlisted personnel family housing on about
27 acres of land on the lower slopes of Pu u Hawaii Loa
During our preliminary geotechnical engineering exploration of the subject site
boulder accumulations were observed within a portion of the proposed development
which are believed to be associated with past boulder fall events generated from a
rocky source area beyond the project development limits
It was desired to further evaluate the extent of the boulder deposits source
area s and mitigative measures which may be appropriate to reduce the potential for
boulder fall hazard on the proposed development

W O 3159 10 Page 2
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our additional geotechnical engineering evaluation was to obtain
a more detailed overview of the surface conditions to evaluate the potential hazards
related to the boulder accumulations at the northeast portion of the proposed
development In addition a boulder survey was conducted to provide numerical data
pertaining to the boulder accumulations to aid in the evaluation of the potential rockfall
hazard and possible mitigative measures General recommendations on mitigative
measures which may reduce the potential of boulders rolling into the development are
also provided We conducted a preliminary geotechnical engineering exploration for
the subject site and the results are presented in our report entitled Preliminary
Geotechnical Engineering Exploration FY93 H 304 and H 305 Family Housing Project
Marine Corps Base Hawaii Kaneohe Bay Kaneohe Oahu Hawaii dated June 20th
Our work was performed in general accordance with our revised fee proposal dated
September 6th The scope of work included the following tasks and work efforts
1 Literature research and consultation to evaluate possible mitigative
measures for boulder fall hazards
2 Detailed field surveys and reconnaissance of the subject site in the vicinity
of the heavy boulder accumulations and potential source areas
Mapping and field flagging of boulder deposit concentrations and
boundaries as well as other significant features relating to the potential
boulder fall hazard Features staked in the field during our recon
naissance will be professionally surveyed and transferred to the project
site plan and or topographic maps

Boulder survey to record the approximate number and dimensions of
boulders located within a surveyed grid pattern of the site The grid

3 Engineering analyses and compilation of the field data for the presentation

mendations pertinent to the mitigation of boulder fall hazard

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The subject site is located on the eastern through northeastern slope of Pu u
Hawaii Loa at the site of the proposed family housing development within the Marine
Corps Base Hawaii Kaneohe Bay The area of the boulder survey encompasses the
hillslope within and upslope of the development limits of the proposed family housing
project The boulder survey area is bounded by Moffett and Reeves Roads to the east
and north respectively and extends upslope to the top of Pu u Hawaii Loa including
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3159 10 Page 4
the two rocky knolls referred to as Knoll Nos 1 and 2 as shown on the Site Plan
Plate 1 The entire survey area was divided into a grid pattern for the boulder counting
survey and is also shown on Plate 1
Elevations at the site range from about 120 feet U S Navy Datum 100 feet
is Mean Low Water at the bottom of the hillslope to about 326 feet at the summit of
the two rocky knolls which form the top of the eastern portion of Pu u Hawaii Loa The
natural ground surface within the project limits varies in slope inclinations from about
one and one half horizontal to one vertical 1 H 1V to about ten horizontal to one
vertical 10H 1V In general the ground surface within the project limits is covered by
a near surface clayey soil with weathered gravels and scattered or clustered groups of
boulders
Above the development limits the slope inclination varies from near vertical at
the summit of Knoll No 1 to about 1 H 1V immediately below Knoll Nos 1 and 2 The
ground surface above the development limit consists mainly of boulder deposits and
scattered boulders on clayey and gravelly ground surface Some areas were observed
to be covered with stacked up layers of boulders deposited by historic colluvial
processes and rockfall events
The entire site is heavily vegetated with tall grasses Haole Koa and Kiawe
trees Haole Koa trees with stem dimensions of less than about 2 to 3 inches in
diameter and tall dry land grasses dominate the intermediate and upper slopes of the
boulder survey area Some larger trees of the Banyan variety exist within the heavy
boulder accumulation zone These trees with their extensive surficial root system aid
in the stability of portions of the heavy boulder accumulation zone

Boulder deposits of varying concentrations were observed to occur throughout
the site The heavier boulder concentrations were found to exist on the intermediate
and upper slopes of the hillside directly below Knoll Nos 1 and 2 A typical stack of
boulders within the heavy boulder accumulation zone is shown on Photograph No 1
Plate 4 1 The knolls are believed to be the source area of the boulder deposits
observed on the intermediate and lower slopes and within the proposed housing project
limits A boundary between the heavy and moderate boulder accumulations below
Knoll No 1 was staked and surveyed in the field and is shown on Plate 1 The heavy
and moderate boulder accumulation boundary was observed to coincide approximately
with the break in slope between the more gentle lower slope and the steeper
intermediate slope of the hillside The lower margin of these heavy boulder
accumulations encroach within the upper development limits of the housing project In
general concentrations of boulders were observed to decrease downslope from the
source area and laterally away from the source area
In general the size of the accumulated boulders varied from about 2 to 12 feet
in maximum dimension The smallest boulder size recorded in the survey was a 1 by
1 by 2 foot boulder The shape of the boulders were observed to be generally angular
with irregular dimensions The boulders generally were observed to decrease in size
from the knoll downslope toward the housing project Generally the larger size
boulders were observed at Knoll Nos 1 and 2 and within the heavy boulder
accumulation zone shown on Plate 1
The source area of the boulder accumulations is believed to be the two
prominent rocky knolls Knoll Nos 1 and 2 making up the summit of the eastern half
of Pu u Hawaii Loa at an elevation of about 320 feet The knolls are comprised of
topography of the Pu u as shown in Photograph No 2 on Plate 4 1 In general the

knolls contain some large blocks of deeply fractured rock in excess of about 15 feet in
dimension with accumulations of smaller boulders mainly in the size range of about
3 feet in dimension
Many of the fractured basalt blocks and boulders appeared to be precariously
situated above near vertical slope faces as shown in Photograph Nos 3 and 4 on
Plate 4 2 The unstable and precarious positions of large boulders at the source area
was especially prevalent at Knoll No 1 In addition the large blocks at Knoll No 1 are
highly fractured as shown in Photograph No 5 on Plate 4 3 In contrast Knoll No 2
appeared to contain fewer number of unstable blocks and instead appeared to contain
a greater number of smaller relatively more stable boulders The condition at Knoll
No 2 is shown in Photograph No 6 on Plate 4 3 Although it appears that Knoll No 2
is relatively more stable than Knoll No 1 some areas exist at Knoll No 2 which have
potential for rockfall
BOULDER SURVEY
A boulder counting survey was performed from August 23rd through 27th
subsequent to our initial reconnaissance which included flagging the extent of heavy
and moderate boulder deposits The boundaries staked in the field during our initial
reconnaissance were professionally surveyed to locate the boundaries which represent
our initial attempt to delineate zones of heavy moderate and light concentrations of
boulders The surveyed boundaries between the heavy and moderate boulder
accumulations below Knoll No 1 are shown on the Site Plan Plate 1 These initially
surveyed boundaries correspond relatively well with the boulder count totals recorded
for each grid
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3159 10 Page 7
The northeast portion of Pu u Hawaii Loa was divided into a two dimensional grid
pattern consisting of grids of approximately 100 by 100 feet as shown on Plate 1 The
shape and area of the grid blocks vary as the study area wraps around the hillside A
total of 100 grids were initially delineated However due to time constraints for the
completion of the survey only critical grids of interest were surveyed and staked in the
field The individual grid blocks are referenced by columns A through N and by rows
1 through 8 as shown on Plate 1 The grid blocks which were not surveyed remain
blank while the surveyed grid blocks contain a circled number corresponding to the
number of boulders counted estimated within the grid
Due to time constraints to complete the boulder count survey a total of 56
separate grid blocks along with the two source knolls were surveyed to record the
estimated number and dimensions of surficial boulders located within the grid blocks
Efforts were made to count and measure each of the boulders observed along
traverses within each grid block In areas where heavy concentrations of stacked up
boulder layers were observed estimating techniques were utilized to approximate the
quantities and dimensions of the boulders Many of the boulders were partially
obscured below the ground surface therefore the third dimension of the boulder size
had to be estimated In addition boulders buried below the ground surface or rock
occurring as in situ outcrop were not included in the survey In general only boulders
with dimensions of 1 by 1 by 2 feet or greater were included in the survey
The numerical boulder counts for each of the grids surveyed are summarized on
Plate 1 Based on our estimation of the boulder dimensions weights of the boulders
were estimated based on an average basalt rock unit weight of 150 pounds per cubic
foot We also separated the boulders into four categories by weight pounds
15 000 pounds These four categories were selected based on our visual observations
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3159 10 Page 8
of the boulder size distributions at the site A table summarizing the number of
boulders in each of the four weight categories is presented on Plates 2 1 and 2 2 In
addition due to the smaller number of boulders below Knoll No 2 we have divided the
boulders counted from grids A through G into nine categories to aid in selecting a
boulder size to use as a basis of design for rockfall containment structures The
boulder counts in the nine categories for grids A through G are summarized on
Plate 3 1
The relatively short time frame allowed to complete the survey combined with the
extensive boulder accumulations required that we implement some amount of
estimation during our survey Tall grass heavy tree growth rugged slopes and the
heavy stacked up layers of boulders of varying sizes limited our ability to count each
and every boulder present at the site Therefore the data presented in this report
represents our best attempt at quantifying the number and size of boulders located at
the subject site in the short time frame provided
Estimates of the number and size distribution of boulders present at the two
source areas Knoll Nos 1 and 2 are provided but it should be noted that these
figures are based on estimation due to the complex layered distribution of the boulder
deposits and their association with in situ rock outcrop
FINDINGS
Based on our boulder survey the total number of accumulated boulders
counted estimated within the 56 grid blocks was about 18 242 boulders excluding the
two source knolls The estimated number of boulders existing at the source knolls was
about 7 585 boulders Therefore the estimated total number of boulders counted
estimated was 25 827 boulders The dominant weight category for all recorded
boulders included in the survey is Category to 7 000 pound category This
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3159 10 Page 9
weight category makes up about 56 percent of all total boulders surveyed A summary
of the data is presented on Plates 1 through 3 1
The heaviest concentration of accumulated boulders occurs downslope of
Knoll No 1 in rows 2 through 4 columns H through K This corresponds well with our
initial reconnaissance where boundaries were defined between heavy and moderate
boulder concentrations based on visual observations There appears to exist an area
downslope and between the two knolls where the boulder accumulation declines before
increasing again immediately below either source knoll In general the accumulations
decrease as one progresses downslope below row 5
General trends in the relative distribution of the boulder accumulations can be
established by analyses of the boulder count data The boulders located in the highest
concentration areas referred to as heavy boulder accumulation were observed to be
angular well graded and ranged in size froni maximum dimensions of about 2 to
12 feet The heavy accumulations of boulders were stacked to depths in excess of
about 8 feet in some areas Photograph No 1 on Plate 4 1 shows the typical
occurrence of the heavy boulder accumulation Many of the boulders were observed
to be pre cariously situated and considered to be unstable Based on comparison of
the boulder count data with our visual observations the heavy accumulations may be
defined by boulder counts in excess of about 400 boulders per grid These heavy
accumulations appear to be confined to the upper and intermediate slopes of the Pu u
especially in areas immediately downslope of the two source areas The heavy boulder
accumulation below Knoll No 1 was staked and surveyed and found to encroach within
the development limits of the project
The boulder concentrations referred to as moderate may be defined by recorded
boulder amounts in the range of 50 to 400 boulders These moderate accumulations
generally exist on the lower gentle slopes of the Pu u in between the two source knolls
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3159 10 Page 10
and adjacent to the steep slopes immediately below the source knolls In general the
moderate concentrations were observed to occur as one layer of boulders or as
clustered groupings of lightly stacked angular boulders of varying sizes The size of
the majority of the boulders were observed to be about 3 to 5 feet in maximum
dimension
Boulder concentrations referred to as light may be defined by recorded boulder
counts less than about 50 boulders per grid These boulder accumulations were
observed to be located at the lower slopes of the project area and at the periphery of
the survey area In general the boulders occurred as isolated or lightly grouped
accumulations of boulders are typically less than about 3 feet in maximum dimension
DISCUSSIONS
The source knolls were surveyed to estimate the number dimensions and
character of the boulders and blocks existing in those areas The boulder count
estimate of Knoll No 1 was about one half of the boulder count estimate recorded for
Knoll No 2 This is likely due to the observation that Knoll No 1 appeared to contain
fewer but larger blocks of deeply fractured outcrop The large blocks appear to be
erosional remnants of the original basalt formation at the site
From the top of Knoll No 1 a near vertical slope face of about 10 to 15 feet high
was observed to exist on the northeastern portion of the knoll This near vertical slope
face increases the potential for rockfall hazard into the lower slopes of the proposed
development below In addition the heavy boulder concentrations observed directly
below the source knolls may increase the bounce height of boulders which may
become dislodged from the source outcrop
The lower boulder counts recorded downslope of Knoll No 2 in comparison to
the accumulations present below Knoll No 1 suggest that there are fewer incidents of
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3159 10 Page 11
rockfall from Knoll No 2 in the past Based on our observations of Knoll No 2 the
character of the outcrop appeared more flattened with a lower occurrence of deeply
fractured blocks Some portions of Knoll No 2 appeared to have been disturbed
possibly from the construction activity for the underground tank adjacent to Knoll No 2
In contrast to Knoll No 1 with a near vertical slope face of about 10 to 15 feet steep
slope faces around Knoll No 2 were observed to be on the order of less than 5 feet
In addition the boulders counted in our survey appear to be fewer and smaller than
those observed below Knoll No 1 These observations lead us to believe that a
smaller size boulder may be used as a basis of design for rockfall containment
structures below Knoll No 2
Based on our field observations of the boulder distribution and the results of the
boulder counting survey areas of relatively higher risk for potential rockfall hazard may
be defined Based on historic evidence the area of highest risk at the site appear to
This area of highest risk is likely due to the existence of the unstable and near vertical
slope face at Knoll No 1 relatively steep slopes with a lack of natural barriers and the
existence of a rock covered ground surface which may aid in boulder travel downslope
Based on historic evidence rockfall hazards have occurred downslope of Knoll
No 2 therefore the area downslope of Knoll No 2 has potential for rockfall hazard
Based on our observations the area immediately downslope of Knoll No 2 is thought
to have a lower potential of rockfall than Knoll No 1
MITIGATION ALTERNATIVES
Based on our observations and the results of the boulder survey we believe that
there is a high potential for rockfall hazard downslope of Knoll No 1 and a moderate
potential for rockfall hazard downslope of Knoll No 2 Therefore we recommend that
mitigative measures be incorporated into the proposed family housing project to reduce
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3159 10 Page 12
the potential for rockfall hazard from affecting the proposed housing development
Several alternatives to reduce the potential for rockfall hazard were evaluated including
off site and on site mitigation alternatives
Off site mitigation measures which are to mitigate the source areas considered
in our evaluation are as follows
1 Remove and or dislodge a large portion of precariously positioned
boulders and or blocks
2 Place welded wire mesh or netting around the source knolls to keep the
boulders blocks in place
3 Grout the fractures and void spaces between the boulders blocks to
increase the cohesive strength of the source knoll
In general it appears that off site mitigation alternatives are not feasible due to
the large areal extent of the source knolls which will require mitigation In addition due
to the terrain and steep slopes off site mitigation at the source areas will present
significant difficulties during construction pose a significant risk of rockfall hazard
during construction and likely would not be cost effective It is also our understanding
that archaeological and cultural constraints would likely pose difficulties in implementing
any off site mitigation measure In addition mitigation of the source areas may not
reduce the potential of rockfall from the heavy boulder accumulation zone on the upper
and intermediate slopes as shown on Photograph No 1 on Plate 4 1
It is our opinion that mitigative measures should concentrate on reducing the
potential for boulders entering the proposed family housing project in the event of a
rockfall occurrence As evidenced by the boulder survey and observation of the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3159 10 Page 13
boulder deposits rockfall has occurred at the site and should be expected to continue
in the future Mitigation alternatives such as rockfall barriers will likely need to be
constructed within the development limits of the proposed family housing project
Various on site mitigation measures such as different types of rockfall barriers have
been researched for use at the subject site The on site rockfall barriers evaluated
include the following
Flexible barriers with energy absorbing capability
Fence barrier for smaller design boulders
Geosynthetically reinforced earth berm barrier
oN
Natural barriers such as trees and vegetation
In our opinion the most viable barrier appears to be the flexible barrier with
energy absorbing capabilities These flexible barriers are specifically designed to
capture errant boulders of particular sizes especially if the design boulder is of a large
size The flexible barrier would require design for anticipated boulder sizes which may
be derived from analyses of the boulder survey This alternative would be most viable
for the barrier downslope of Knoll Nos 1 and 2 The fence barrier for smaller boulders
is only feasible if the design boulder is of a small size This may be feasible downslope
of Knoll No 2 depending on the size of the design boulder downslope of Knoll No 2
In our opinion another viable alternative would be a geosynthetically reinforced
earth berm barrier However construction of the earth berm will require substantial
grading of the intermediate slopes therefore the cost effectiveness of this alternative
will need to be evaluated The earth berm alternative would consist of an earthen
barrier of approximately 10 feet above the ground surface The thickness of the earth
berm typically should be about 80 percent of the height or about 8 feet in thickness
he earth berm would need to be embedded a minimum of about 2 to 3 feet below the
ground surface and should be constructed of soil compacted to a minimum of 90
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3159 10 Page 14
percent relative compaction To reduce the materials needed to construct the earth
berm the earth berm would be reinforced with geosynthetics such as geotextile or
geogrid to provide a near vertical face for the upslope and downslope faces of the
earth berm
Geosynthetics are recommended for use as reinforcements to allow the use of
fine grained soils which are more readily available to construct the earthen barrier
Metallic reinforcing may be used as an option however the soil used in the earth berm
will need to be select granular material to reduce the corrosion potential on the metallic
reinforcing
An outer facing will be needed for the earth berm as a protective cover over the
geosynthetic reinforcements In general an outer facing such as shotcrete or gabions
may be used to reduce the potential damage of the geosynthetic reinforcing from fire
or natural weathering
In our opinion use of natural barriers consisting of trees will require multiple rows
of relatively large trees to be effective in stopping large boulders from rolling into the
housing project The uncertainty in the location and effectiveness of the natural barrier
consisting of trees make this mitigation alternative less desirable
In summary we believe that an on site mitigation measure such as the flexible
barrier with energy absorbing capabilities downslope of Knoll No 1 and 2 is the most
viable alternative for reducing the potential for rockfall hazard to the proposed family
housing project We also believe that a smaller less substantial barrier may be used
to reduce the potential for rockfall hazard downslope of Knoll No 2 based on a smaller
design boulder

PRELIMINARY RECOMMENDATIONS
Geotechnical engineering parameters will be needed for the design of the flexible
barrier with energy absorbing capability Additional borings should be performed in the
area where the flexible barriers are planned to provide the geotechnical parameters for
design of the flexible barriers We wish to emphasize that the subsurface information
from the borings drilled at the lower slopes should not be extrapolated to the upper
slopes as the subsurface conditions may vary considerably
However due to time constraints we are providing some general guidelines for
the design of the flexible barrier based on visual observations The preliminary values
presented herein should be confirmed by the contractor in the field In general we
believe that footings for the flexible barrier posts may be supported on shallow footings
bearing on the near surface soils at the site Footings may be designed with an
allowable bearing pressure of 2 000 pounds per square foot p s f This bearing
pressure is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third for transient
loads such as wind or seismic forces Footings should be embedded a minimum of
2 feet below the lowest adjacent ground
We understand that anchors for the flexible barrier with energy absorbing
capability will be subjected to a tensile force of approximately 20 tons 40 kips per
anchor The tensile force may be resisted by the pullout resistance per unit area of
grout soil interface contact known as frictional resistance The frictional resistance is
variable depending on the type of soil and grout the overburden stress and the
construction procedures used in installing these grouted anchors For a rough
estimate an average frictional resistance of 800 p s f may be used for the clayey soil
observed near the surface The frictional resistance will need to be reduced if
cohesionless soils are encountered The frictional resistance used in the design will
need to be confirmed in the field during construction
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3159 10 Page 16
It should be noted that weathered rock and or basalt formation may be
encountered near the existing ground surface If basalt formation is encountered the
frictional resistance may be increased
