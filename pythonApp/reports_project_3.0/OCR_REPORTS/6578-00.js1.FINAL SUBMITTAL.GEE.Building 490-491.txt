Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The
Koolau Volcano reached the end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years
ago
The geologic conditions underlying the Pearl Harbor area and the project site are
the result of a complex series of deposition and erosional events brought on by
glacio eustatic sea level changes and post erosional volcanic activities during the
Pleistocene Epoch Following the cessation of volcanic activity of the Koolau Volcano
alluvial soil materials were deposited in a deltaic or estuarine environment in the
Pearl Harbor area
The sea level fluctuated several times receding perhaps 300 feet below and
rising about 100 feet above the present sea level During the Recent Epoch as sea
level rose to its current stand the area was covered with soft and loose lagoonal marine
deposits These deposits were subsequently covered by man made fills placed during
the middle portion of this century for land reclamation projects during construction of the
existing Pearl Harbor area

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic
plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic
activity therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during
volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most are so
small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments However some of
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to moderate
damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii Because the majority of earthquakes in Hawaii
over 90 percent are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic activity and
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of
Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than
Magnitude 5 M5 however earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of
Hawaii
To a lesser degree the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5 Therefore moderate to strong earthquakes have

The renovation and repair of Buildings 490 and 491 will consist of removing and
replacing the existing interior asphaltic concrete and concrete slabs with a new

deposit may not experience a large vertical stress increase which would lead to
additional substantial ground settlements
In general consolidation takes place in two phases comprised of Primary and
Secondary Consolidation Primary consolidation takes place after an increase in vertical
stress is applied to the soil During primary consolidation excess pore pressures build
up within the soil and will slowly dissipate resulting in settlement Secondary
consolidation occurs after primary consolidation when excess pore pressure has
dissipated During the secondary consolidation stage soil particles within the soil
structure will slowly yield and compress The rate at which secondary consolidation
occurs largely depends on the soil type Typically consolidation of a lagoonal deposit
may take a substantial amount of time due to its low permeability characteristics
Based on the length of time the existing fill and structural loads have been in
place we believe that the thick lagoonal deposits have reached the end of the primary
consolidation phase and heading into secondary consolidation Due to the low in situ
strength characteristics and the relatively large thickness of the lagoonal deposits a
small amount on the order of 2 inches of secondary consolidation may occur over the
life span of the buildings

Field Exploration
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling six borings
designated as Boring Nos 101 through 106 extending to depths between about
17 5 feet and 76 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring
locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 We used a track mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools to drill the borings
Our geologist observed the drilling operations on a near continuous basis and
classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and textural examination in
the field These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation and testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths Undisturbed samples of the soft soil formation were obtained by
pushing a 3 inch OD thin walled stainless steel Shelby tube into the soil formation in
general accordance with ASTM D 1587 Standard Practice for Thin Walled Tube
Sampling of Soils for Geotechnical Purposes The range of hydraulic pressures needed
to advance the sampler through a 24 inch push is shown as the Penetration
Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Boring at the
appropriate sample depths
Four Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths The
test results are provided on Plate B 1
Three fines content tests were conducted on selected soil samples to evaluate the
percent passing the No 200 sieve The tests were performed in accordance with
ASTM D 1140 The test results along with those obtained from the sieve analysis tests
are summarized on Plate B 2 and on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Four Consolidation tests ASMT D 2435 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the on site soils The test results are
plotted on Plates B 3 through B 6
Two Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 136 D 1140 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils
and to aid in soil classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are
provided on Plate B 7
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates B 8 and B 9
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were
performed on bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 10 and B 11
