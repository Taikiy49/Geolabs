SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered a layer of surface fill/alluvium soils
overlying coral formation extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately
21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The surface fill/alluvium soils generally
consisted of silty clay/clayey silt with coralline gravel and sand on the order of about 0.5 to
7 feet thick. The coral formation encountered in our borings were generally moderately
weathered and medium hard. Groundwater was encountered at a depth of about 12 feet
below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration.
Based on the subsurface materials encountered, we believe that a shallow
foundation system may be used to support the new structures planned at the site. An
allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for the design of spread
and/or continuous footings bearing on the recompacted in-situ soils or new compacted
fills. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3)
for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. In general, footings
should be embedded a minimum depth of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished
grade.
Since cavities and/or voids are commonly encountered in coral formations, we
recommend that a program of probing and grouting at the building foundations be
conducted during construction to reduce the potential for possible loss of foundation
support from the collapse of underground cavities. In addition, the excavated subgrades
underneath future slabs should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches,
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and proof-rolled with a minimum
10-ton vibratory drum roller to detect the presence of near-surface cavities, sinkholes, or
loose zones. Special attention should be given to the subgrade preparation requirements
presented in the "Site Preparation" section of this report to reduce the potential for future
distress on the floor slabs and pavements.
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific
recommendations for design of the project.

SECTION 1.0 - GENERAL
1.1. Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Ewa Beach Elementary School project located in the District of Ewa
on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The general location and vicinity of the project site are
shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project. These recommendations are intended for the design
of building foundations, slabs-on-grade, and site preparation only. The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report.
1.2 Project Considerations
The project involves the construction of a new one-story building at
Ewa Elementary School in Ewa on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The proposed building
will accommodate six classrooms and will be connected to the existing Building “B” by a
sidewalk. The proposed building location is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. Structural
details were not available at the time this report was prepared. In addition, a new
grasscrete firelane is also proposed on the southwestern side of the campus.
1.3. Purpose and Scope
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the surface and
subsurface conditions at the project site. The subsurface information obtained was
utilized to develop a soil/rock data set for the formulation of geotechnical engineering
recommendations pertaining to the design of building foundations, slabs-on-grade, and
site preparation for the proposed classroom building. In order to accomplish this, we
conducted an exploration program consisting of the following tasks and work efforts:

SECTION 2.0 - SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2.1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau. The older volcano, Waianae, is estimated
to be middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age. As volcanic activity in Waianae Volcano ceased,
lava flows from Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a
broad plateau, known as Schofield Plateau.
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of this plateau generated
. sediments, which were transported to the coast. In the vicinity of the project site and to
the south, these sediments accumulated and interbedded with marine sediments and
coral/algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge. The thickness of the
sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H-1 Highway to
over 1,000 feet at Ewa Beach. This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the
confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu.
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present.
Development within the last 100 years has brought the project area to its present form.
2.2 Site Description
The project site is located within the Ewa Beach Elementary School campus on
Papipi Road in the Ewa Beach area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The proposed
building site is located to the north of the existing Buildings A and E, as shown on the
Site Plan, Plate 2.
The project site is currently vacant except for several trees located on site. A
jungle gym was also observed on the project site at the time of our field exploration.
Based on our field observations and the topographic survey map provided, the existing
project site is slightly sloping up from east to west, and the ground elevations over the
majority of the property range from about Elevation +11.5 to +13.5 feet Mean Sea Level
(MSL).

2.3. Subsurface Conditions
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling four borings, designated
as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, extending to depths ranging from about 20.3 to 21.5 feet
below the existing ground surface. The approximate locations of the borings are shown
on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by
surface fills/alluvium, on the order of about 0.5 to 7 feet thick, consisting of soft to
medium stiff silty clay/clayey silt with coralline gravel and sand. Below the surface soils,
coral formation was encountered and extended to the maximum depth explored of
approximately 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The coral formation
generally appeared to be moderately weathered and medium hard in nature. It should
be noted that the strength characteristics of coral formation are highly variable. The
depositional nature of the formation may create localized areas of hard, crystalline coral
intermixed with soft, severely fractured detritus. It should be noted that coral formation
is frequently cavernous or contain pockets of voids. Although cavities were not
encountered in our drilled borings, the potential for the presence of cavities and/or voids
exists at the project site.
Groundwater was encountered at depths of about 12 feet below the existing
ground surface in the borings drilled at the time of our field exploration. It should be
noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to seasonal precipitation,
surface water runoff, and other factors. In addition, it should be noted that water
seepage is commonly encountered in coral formations due to infiltration of surface
water.
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered from our field exploration are
presented in Appendix A. Laboratory tests were performed on selected soil samples,
and the test results are presented in Appendix B.

SECTION 3.0 - DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered surface
fills/alluvium overlying coral formation extending to the maximum depth explored of
approximately 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Since cavities and/or voids
are commonly encountered in coral formations, we believe that the primary geotechnical
considerations for the proposed construction will include the following: (1) probing and
grouting for potential cavities and/or sinkholes beneath the building foundations; and
(2) proof-rolling of future slab subgrades to detect the presence of potential
near-surface cavities/sinkholes or loose zones.
Based on the relatively competent subsoil conditions encountered in our borings
and the anticipated design structural loads, it is our opinion that spread and/or
continuous footings may be used for support of the proposed buildings planned at the
project site. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf)
may be used for design of footing foundations bearing on the recompacted in-situ soils
or new compacted fills.
To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of
the cavities and/or sinkholes below foundations, we recommend that a program of
cavity probing and grouting at the foundation locations be implemented during
construction. In addition, we recommend that the excavated subgrades underneath
future slabs be proof-rolled with a minimum 10-ton vibratory drum roller to detect the
presence of near-surface cavities/sinkholes or loose zones. Special attention should be
given to the subgrade preparation requirements presented in the "Site Preparation"
section of this report to reduce the potential for future distress on the floor slabs.
Detailed discussions and recommendations pertaining to these items are further
presented in the following sections of this report.
3.1. Building Foundations
Based on the design concept and the subsoil conditions encountered at the site,
we believe that spread and/or continuous footings may be used to support the proposed

buildings planned at the site. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be
used for design of footing foundations bearing on the recompacted in-situ soils or new
compacted fills. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by
one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces.
For footings bearing on the in-situ stiff/dense soils, the bottom of the footing
excavations should be recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction to
provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to placement of reinforcing
steel or concrete. If soft and/or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the
footing excavations, they should be over-excavated to expose the underlying firm
materials. The over-excavation may be backfilled with on-site soils compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction, or the footing bottom may be extended
down to the underlying competent materials. For footings bearing on new compacted
fills, the bottom of the footing excavations should also be recompacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete.
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 test procedures. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by
dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.
In general, footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the
lowest adjacent finish grades. Footings located adjacent to the planned retaining walls
should be embedded deep enough to avoid surcharging the retaining wall foundations,
or the retaining walls should be designed to resist the additional structural loads.
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements, they should be
embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge
of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This requirement is
necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with additional
structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement.

We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for
footings bearing on the recompacted in-situ soils or new compacted fills as
recommended herein to be less than 1 inch total with differential settlements on the
order of one-half (¥2) inch.
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance

3.2 Foundation Probing
Our experience suggests that cavities and/or voids are commonly encountered in
the coral formations underlying the project site. To reduce the potential for loss of
foundation support resulting from collapse of the cavities and/or sinkholes below
foundations, we recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting at the
foundation locations be implemented during construction.
For estimating purposes, we recommend that probe holes be drilled at each
spread footing location (one probe hole for every 50 square feet of footing area) and at
10 feet on-center along wall foundations. The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in
diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of foundation
elevation. If cavities and/or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing
operation, additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacing to aid in delineating

3.3 Slabs-On-Grade
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by
near-surface clayey soils with moderate expansion potential. To reduce the potential for
structural distress resulting from the swelling of the subgrade soils, we recommend that
the subgrade soils be properly prepared and capped with non-expansive select granular
fill material.
In general, we recommend a minimum of 18 inches of non-expansive, select
granular fill be provided below the building slabs. The non-expansive, select granular fill
should be moisture-conditioned to above optimum moisture content and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.
In addition, we recommend that the subgrade soils be scarified to a depth of
8 inches, and moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture,
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior to the placement
of the select granular fill. Optimum dry density refers to the moisture content
(percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

3.4 Fire Lanes
Emergency fire lanes were proposed to serve the new classroom building. We
understand that the new fire lanes will be constructed using the Grasspave System.
Installation of the Grasspave System includes excavating for base course, applying
hydrogrow mixture, laying out Grasspave mats consisting of rings, filling rings with clean
concrete sand, laying turf over the rings, and rolling sod with heavy roller.
Our laboratory tests performed on the surface silty clay encountered at the
proposed fire lane area indicated a CBR value of 28 with a swell value of 2.5 percent.
Considering the traffic conditions anticipated for the proposed project, we recommend
that the proposed fire lane area be prepared properly according to our
recommendations to reduce future distresses.
We recommend that the subgrade soils for the new fire lane areas be scarified to
a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content,
and compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction.
We also recommend that 6-inch base and 6-inch subbase with a maximum grain
size of % inches be placed under the Grasspave System. The aggregate base course
and subbase should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95
percent relative compaction. CBR and field density tests should be performed on the
actual subgrade soils encountered during construction to confirm the adequacy of the
above section.
In general, the fire lane areas should be graded properly to avoid surface water
ponding at low spots. In the event of low spots, a subdrain system is recommended to
collect the excess water, and drain the water away from the fire lane areas.

3.5 Site Preparation
Based on the grading plan provided, we anticipate that the majority of the site
preparation work for the project will consist of minor cuts and/or fills on the order of
about 2 feet thick to achieve the design finish grades. As previously mentioned, the
project site may be underlain by cavities and/or sinkholes near the existing ground
surface. Therefore, special attention should be given to the subgrade preparation for
the project development.
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed. Vegetation, organic debris, construction debris,
deleterious material and other unsuitable materials, should be removed and disposed of
properly off-site or in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the
excavated materials with the cleared and grubbed spoils. Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing should be over-excavated to expose firm
natural material, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted
fill. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be properly disposed of off-site.
Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for
over-excavation and engineered fill placement to account for variations in the
over-excavation quantities.
The excavated subgrades underneath future slab areas should be scarified to a
depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content,
and proof-rolled with a minimum 10-ton vibratory drum roller to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction to detect the presence of potential near-surface cavities, sinkholes,
or loose zones. If the proof-rolling operations detect a near-surface cavity or loose
zone, the area should be ripped open, cleaned of debris, and then backfilled with
compacted fill material or lean concrete.
Fill materials required for the project construction should consist of
non-expansive select granular material, such as crushed coral and basalt. The material
should be well-graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in
largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the

A granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33,

such as No. 3B Fine gravel should also be used for the trench backfill up to about
12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce
the compaction effort on the backfill, thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes.
It is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of
voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the
pipes. Improper backfill material and placement could result in settlement of the backfill
and damage to the pipes.
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of on-site soils or select
granular fill material. The backfill should be placed in maximum 8-inch level loose lifts
and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce
the potential for future ground subsidence. Where trenches are below pavement areas,
the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.
Based on our field exploration, the project site is underlain by coral formation
near the existing ground surface. The coral formation encountered in our borings
appeared to be moderately weathered and medium hard in nature. It is anticipated that
the majority of the coral formation may be excavated with normal heavy excavation
equipment, such as excavating with large backhoe excavators. However, coral
formations typically contain localized hard and crystallized zones. Therefore, we
anticipate that difficult excavation conditions may arise in localized areas during

3.7 Drainage
The finished grades outside the new buildings should be sloped to shed water
away from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding. It is also advised that
gutter systems be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted
away from the foundation areas. Excessive landscape watering near the foundations
and slabs should also be avoided. Planters next to foundations should be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the
subsoils.
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent loss of strength. The foundation excavations should be
properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration. Drainage swales should be
provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface runoff away
from the slab and footing foundations.

APPENDIX A

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling four borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, extending to depths
ranging from about 20.3 to 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The
approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The
borings were drilled using truck-mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous
flight augers.
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer, who monitored the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-4.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer
falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586, Penetration Test and
Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
