PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is situated in the Kamakaipo area on the west coast of the island
of Molokai Hawaii The proposed lagoon will be a marina and water feature for a future
resort development
The proposed lagoon area is a relatively low area along the shore with ground
surface elevations ranging from about sea level to approximately 30 feet above Mean Sea
Level It is proposed to excavate and dredge the area to an elevation of about 10 feet
below Mean Sea Level Previous geophysical exploration by others indicated that there
may be hard rock materials below the site
It was desired to further explore the site by the drilling and sampling of borings to
obtain data pertaining to the excavation characteristics of the materials below the site
Other areas of consideration are stability of the sidewalls of the proposed lagoon the
potential need for erosion protection along the sides and bottom of the lagoon and
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2648 10 Page 3
preliminary foundation recommendations for future site improvements such as marina
piers
Since the project is in the master planning stage details of the proposed site
grading the configuration of the proposed lagoon and the design of structural
improvements such as marina walls were not available for use in our exploration

GENERAL SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is situated on the western end of the island of Molokai Hawaii
westerly of the town of Maunaloa The site forms a low basin along the shore
Presently the site is under lease to Molokai Ranch but it appears that the land is
generally not used as pasture Most of the site is densely vegetated with keawe lantana
and other dryland vegetation
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2648 10 Page 5
In general the topography of the site slopes gently up towards the east from the
shore Localized areas of the site are rugged with sharp vertical relief and surface
exposures of boulders The elevations at the site range from sea level to about 30 feet
above sea level along the upslope boundaries of the site
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Molokai was built during the early to middle Pleistocene Epoch by
basaltic lava flows from two shield volcanoes East Molokai Mountain and West Molokai
Mountain The project site is situated on the western flank of West Molokai Mountain
overlying rocks of the West Molokai Volcanic Series The West Molokai Volcanic Series
consists of exceptionally thinly bedded basaltic lavas
Geomorphologically the site is in a shallow basin formed by the confluence of
three intermittent streams In previous work by Stearns and Macdonald 1947 the area
was mapped as being underlain by alluvial soils
The geomorphology and subsurface conditions at the site are directly related to
glacio eustatic fluctuations of sea level during the Pleistocene The base of the
stratigraphic section in this area is West Molokai Volcanic Series basalt
During the Pleistocene ice Age there were many sea level changes as a result
of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to
fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Molokai remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio
eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of sea which were both higher and lower relative
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2648 10 Page 6
to present sea level on Molokai The available evidence indicates that sea level in West
Molokai was as high as about 250 feet above and over 300 feet below the present level
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef
deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of upper areas of the
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments at corresponding higher elevations in areas protected by fringing
reefs and beach strands
Along the shoreline of the project site wave action has deposited littoral sands
In many areas these littoral sands have become cemented into a moderately strong
sandstone
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward
About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred During that
rise the shallow stream valleys in the project area were drowned In the last 10 000 years
or so sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous sediments have continued
to accumulate in the area behind the now consolidated beach strand in what were
formerly valleys and drainageways
Based on the previous geophysical work and our current exploration it appears
that the alluvial soils form a relatively thin mantle overlying in situ basaltic rock formation
and its weathering products It also appears that channels were eroded through the rock
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2648 10 Page 7
formation by the action of the streams and that these channels have been buried by
subsequent alluvial action
Subsurface Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by the drilling and
sampling of fourteen 14 borings to depths ranging between 25 0 and 37 0 feet below
the existing ground surface The drilling and sampling operations were monitored by a
Field Geologist Engineer from our firm A detailed description of the field exploration
methodology along with the Boring Logs is presented in Appendix A of this report
Soil Conditions
In general the drilled borings encountered about three 3 to about seven 7 feet
of very stiff silty clay and clayey silt soils overlying weathered in situ basalt formation
These stiff soils are generally alluvial soils deposited in the old valleys and drainageways
forming the basin
The underlying basalt formation ranged from clayey silt residual soils and sandy
saprolites derived from in situ weathering of the West Molokai basalts to moderately
weathered basalt rock Basalt in the moderately weathered stage is generally easier to
excavate than unweathered rock
Groundwater Conditions
Groundwater levels were measured in the borings at depths ranging between about
10 5 and 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface during our field exploration Based
on the information provided to us by the project surveyor these depths are approximately
equivalent to elevations ranging between about 4 5 to 4 7 feet Mean Sea Level It is our
opinion that the extreme low measurements resulted from readings taken prior to the
static level adjusting after drilling It is our further opinion that the extreme high levels
GEOLABS HAWAIi
W O 2648 10 Page 8
reflect localized perched or confined conditions and that they are not indicative of
conditions which could be significant from a water resources point of view
Due to the proximity of the ocean and the estimated rapid permeability
characteristics of the basalt formation underlying the site it is anticipated that the
groundwater levels within the proposed lagoon dredging area will fluctuate due to tidal
influences storm surges along the adjacent shoreline and precipitation at and upslope
of the site Therefore a high groundwater level at 2 5 feet MSL is recommended for
use in the project design
DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
It is our opinion that the proposed lagoon project is feasible from a geotechnical
engineering point of view However since the project is in the planning phase and details
of the proposed development were not available detailed site specific recommendations
pertaining to structural improvements could not be developed Therefore the
recommendations presented herein pertain mainly to site grading or dredging of the
lagoon and are general in nature
Site Grading
It is anticipated that the site grading for the lagoon project will involve excavation
and dredging of a basin to an elevation of about 10 feet below Mean Sea Level 10 MSL
In a mass excavation or dredging project the ease of excavation is generally an
important economic consideration for the project Generally hard excavation i e
adverse ripping hydraulic chipping or breaking and blasting is more costly than
conventional excavation i e high production ripping or the use of trenchers and
excavators
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2648 10 Page 9
However the actual economics are usually dictated by the contractor s ability to maintain
production
Based on the data obtained from the borings drilled for our exploration it appears
that both conventional and hard excavation or dredging methods will be required for the
project This is based on visual examination of the rock cores and the rate of auger
penetration into the in situ rock formation
In general it appears that most of the materials encountered above the elevation
of 0 MSL may be excavated by conventional earthmoving equipment However hard
excavation may be encountered in localized areas above this elevation
Harder excavation or dredging will be encountered in most areas below the
elevation of O MSL Based on the data cbtained from the borings drilled for our
exploration it is our opinion that most of this material would be rippable by large
bulldozers such as the Caterpillar D 9 and D 10 series or similar machines Some scil
horizons may be encountered within this portion of the proposed excavation
However it is our opinion that ripping excavation and dredging below water level
is difficult to control and that the continued working of the equipment could generate
excessive amounts of mud and turbidity There is also a possibility of the equipment
bogging down when working in the water In general the use of conventional
earthmoving equipment below water level would be highly inefficient
It is our understanding that some bulldozers have been modified to allow remote
control operation at shallow underwater depths However it appears that the use of
these machines is also costly and inefficient
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2648 10 Page 10
Generally the method of excavation should be left to the discretion of the
contractor within the applicable safety regulations and in accordance with good
workmanship However it is our opinion that the use of blasting followed by excavation
or dredging with clamshell dredges dragline dredges or large backhoes sucn as
Caterpillar 235 and 245 series or similar would be preferabie to ripping and excavating
with bulldozers in terms of excavation efficiency and elevation control
A possible scenario for the progress of work would be to first excavate in a
conventional manner to about two 2 to four 4 feet above the groundwater level This
would form a working table for the dredging and hauling equipment
The excavation contractor should exercise caution when digging along the edges
of the working table When excavating below water the actual excavation is obscured
and there is a potential for undermining tne excavation equipment creating a safety
hazard
To reduce the potential of siltation in the offshore waters fronting the site we
recommend that any channels be excavated in the last stages of the dredging To further
reduce the potential of siltation the dredging operations could be temporarily suspended
prior to the excavation of the channels and the silts allowed to settle The basin could
then be cleaned with hydraulic or suction dredges
Dewatering Considerations
It is anticipated that it may be necessary to dewater portions of the dredged area
to allow construction of certain structural improvements to be conducted under dry
conditions In general the lower basalt materials appear to be moderately to rapidly
permeable It is anticipated that dewatering of deep excavations if required will be
difficult and may require efforts such as cofferdams with tremie concrete seals

Generally the method of dewatering should be left to the discretion of the
contractor within the applicable safety regulations and in accordance with good
workmanship It is recommended that additional exploration including field permeability
testing be conducted on a site specific basis for any structural elements which would
require dewatering for construction
Slope and Bottom Protection
Based on the information obtained during our exploration it is anticipated that
portions of the lagoon side slopes and portions of the lagoon bottom will be in soil
materials
It is our understanding that the proposed lagoon will be used for recreational
swimming and that portions of the lagoon will be used for boating Water clarity is an
important aesthetic consideration for the lagoon project Due to the fine grained nature
of the cohesive soils encountered at the site it may be necessary to protect the lagoon
sides and bottom from erosion and to reduce the potential of turbidity resulting from
disturbance of the soils For this protection we recommend the use of riprap underlain
by a geotexitile filter fabric such as Mirafi 700X or similar to reduce losses of the soils
due to piping or surge action Schematic illustrations of the riprap and fabric placement
are shown on Plate 3 The manufacturer of the filter fabric should be contacted for
specific details of the installation
For areas of the proposed lagoon which will be subject to relatively high energy
conditions a composite concrete and geotextile armor such as Armorform or similar may
be considered as an alternate to the riprap protection Manufacturer s information on this
product is presented in Appendix C This information is presented for information only
and does not constitute an endorsement of a particular product or manufacturer

For extremely high energy conditions such as channel entrances the slopes and
bottoms may need to be protected by stone armor constructed in accordance with
recommendations of the ocean engineering consultant In this situation we believe that
an underlayment of geotextile would aid in reducing the potential of piping or ercsion
losses below the stone armor
Use of Excavated Materials
Since the overall development project is in the master planning phase it is not
possible to develop specific recommendations for the use of the excavated materials
However we are presenting these discussions for informational purposes to assist in the
master planning efforts
It is Our opinion that the excavated materials may be utilized far fill materials in
other portions of the overail development project The upper clayey alluvial sails generally
have a moderate to high swell potential are prone to erosion due to the dry climatic
conditions and have only moderate structural strength if allowed to become saturated
trom externai sources such as landscape irrigation The use of these ciayey soils should
be limited to non structural fills such as golf course embankment or general yard fill
Therefore efforts should be made during the excavation and dredging work to segregate
the upper clayey soils from the underlying saprolite soils and rock materials
Although testing of the permeability of the soils was not within our scope of work
it should be noted that clayey soils generally have low permeability values Consideration
could be given to using the clayey soils for lining of golf course water features or selling
these soils for use as sanitary landfill liner and cover material It is recommended that
if these uses are considered permeability tests should be conducted on sampies of the
clayey soils to verify their suitability for these specific purposes

The saprolite soils and weathered basalts appear to be generally acceptable for
use as select fill material In general the basalt has been weathered and is not suitable
for use as aggregate
The following recommendations are general guidelines for fill placement using the
excavated materials and are subject to modification based on subsequent exploration or
site specific requirements for structural improvements
Prior to commencement of grading all areas within the proposed grading limits
should be cleared of all vegetation and deleterious materials The resulting grub and spoil
material should either be disposed of properly off site or else the material should be
shredded chipped and stockpiled for use in landscaping
Due to the dry and sometimes windy site conditions moisture conditioning of fill
material will be necessary The State and Federal governments have enacted stringent
regulations pertaining to the release of fugitive dust from construction sites Therefore
it is also advisable to institute a dust control program during grading operations to
minimize the amount of airborne dust
Soft or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below any areas
designated to receive fill should be overexcavated to firm natural material and the resulting
depression backfilled with well compacted engineered fill
Any boulders or cobbles larger than twelve 12 inches in maximum dimension
should be removed from the excavated material and either disposed of off site or be
placed in the deeper fills as discussed below
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2648 10 Page 14
Large boulders or rock fragments should be buried in the deepest portions of the
fills provided that they are not nested and an adequate amount of compacted finer
grained soil fills the space between the boulders to reduce the potential of loss of fines
from the upper portion of the fill Care must be exercised to avoid placement of any
boulders in locations which would conflict with future utility lines In general boulder or
rock fragments larger than twelve 12 inches in size should be placed at depths greater
that four 4 feet below finish subgrade level
Generally fills required should be placed in level lifts not exceeding twelve 12
inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 maximum dry density as
established by ASTM D 1557 78 Trench and structural backfills should be placed in level
lifts not exceeding eight 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of
90 maximum dry density per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
Any fills to be placed on slopes having ratios steeper than 5 1 horizontal to
vertical should be keyed and benched into firm soil or in situ rock formation to lessen
potential instability of the new fill see Plate 4 The filling operations should start at the
lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the
above recommendations for fill placement

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by the drilling and sampling
of fourteen 14 borings to depths ranging between 25 0 and 37 0 feet below the existing
ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings
were drilled by a truck mounted rotary drill rig advancing hollow stem continuous flight
augers or rotary core drilling tocls as appropriate
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts to drive the
sampler the last 12 inches are shown on the boring logs at the appropriate sample
depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material leit in
the annulus of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination classification and testing
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 and would be shown on
the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material that is in excess of four 4 inches in length without any discontinuities
discounting any drilling induced fractures or breaks if 2 5 feet of sound material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 and would be shown on the
Boring Logs as REC 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative
quality based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was developed to schedule appropriate tests
to aid in determining pertinent engineering properties of the soil and or rock materials
sampled
Classification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Moisture Content ASTM Test Method D 2216 80 and Unit Weight Determinations
are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid in determining certain properties such
as relative saturation The moisture content expressed as a percentage of the dry weight
of the tested sample is shown at the appropriate location on plates summarizing test
results and at the appropriate sample depths on the Boring Logs Plates A 1 1 through
A 14 1
Atterberg Limits tests ASTM Test Method D 4318 84 are used to determine the
plasticity of the fine grained portion of soils The Plasticity Index of the material is used
to classify the soil under the Unified Soil Classification System
Sieve Analysis tests ASTM Test Method D 422 63 are performed to determine the
grain size distribution of the soil The grain size distribution is used for classification in
the Unified Soil Classification System and to provide indices on other engineering
properties
Physical Property Tests
Tests are performed to develop a correlative index of certain physical parameters
of a soil These physical parameters may be used in engineering analyses pertaining to
the performance or suitability of a soil
Direct Shear tests ASTM Test Method D 3080 72 are used to determine the angle
of internal friction and the coefficient of cohesion in a soil sample These data are used
in various engineering calculations
Expansion tests are performed to evaluate the swelling potential of soils under
structural loads These tests aid in evaluating the materials for use as foundation support
and embankments

Three dimensional erosion control mats are used to estab
lish a reinforced turf in ditches channels and slopes The
mat entangies with the root and stem network of vegetatior
to greatiy enhance its resistance to flow velocity
Erosion mats used for turf reinforcement have a strong
stable three cimensional structure with adequate porosity
to retain soil while allowing roots and stems to grow through
Correct installation requires pinning the mat to the ground
and burying mat edges and ends Top soil cover may be
used to enhance temporary erosion protection and early
vegetative growth
5 Of Erosion Control Under Rip Rap Armor
s Geotextiles are used between soil and rip rap to prevent
the erosion of soil through the armoring layer The geotextile
is used in lieu of a conventional graded aggregate filter In
this application large armor stone or rip rap or in some
cases flexible concrete mattresses are placed to protect
the soi against erosion and wave attack
The use of a geotextile in this application has been shown
to provide substantial savings over conventional aggregate
filter systems with far greater control during construction
particularly in underwater applications Typical geosynthe
tics used for this application are medium weight and
Strength nonwoven fabrics monofilament woven geotex
tiles and some multifilament or fibrillated woven fabrics
Two key criterions must be met for this application First
the geotextile must have sufficient permeability to permit
the passage of water thus relieving hydrostatic pressure
ree behinc the armor layer Second the geotextile must be
Nicest oe a aes es i capable of retaining the soil particles under the armor Con
ventional filter criteria can be used for design with some
modifications required for the geotextile properties
