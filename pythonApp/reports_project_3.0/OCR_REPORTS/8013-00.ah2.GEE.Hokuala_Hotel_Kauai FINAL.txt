SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered stiff and medium dense fills overlying 
medium stiff to hard residual soils and very stiff/dense saprolites at greater depths. We did 
not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. It should 
be noted that groundwater levels may vary depending on rainfall, time of year, seasonal 
precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
Based on the subsurface conditions at the site and anticipated structural loads, we 
envision that the proposed hotel building and minor structures may be supported on shallow 
foundations consisting of spread and/or continuous strip footings bearing on the 
recompacted on-site soils and/or new compacted fills placed to achieve the design finished 
grades. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for the design of 
footings bearing on the recompacted on-site soils and/or new compacted fills placed to 
achieve the design finished grades. 
We envision concrete slabs-on-grade will be used for the new structures constructed 
near the existing grades or over about 5 feet of new fill. Based on our field exploration, we 
believe the near-surface silty and clayey soils at the project site exhibit a low to moderate 
potential for shrinking and swelling when subjected to moisture fluctuations. To reduce the 
potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs-on-grade resulting from shrinking and 
swelling of the silty and clayey soils, we recommend the silty and clayey subgrade soils be 
properly prepared and capped with a 12-inch layer of non-expansive select granular fill 
materials extending a minimum of 2 feet beyond the edges of the new structures.

project. Based on the information provided and a design CBR value of 20 for the subgrade

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed in support of the design of the proposed Hokuala Hotel project in Lihue on the 
Island of Kauai, Hawaii. The project location and general site vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1.  
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses. These 
recommendations are intended for the design of building foundations, slabs-on-grade, 
retaining structures, site grading, pavements, underground utility lines, and drainage only. 
The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted 
at the end of this report.

Project Considerations 
The project consists of the construction of a new hotel at Lot 8 within the Hokuala 
Kauai development in the Nawiliwili area of Lihue on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii.

constructed near the existing golf clubhouse. The proposed hotel building will connect to

located between the existing golf clubhouse and existing tennis courts. The proposed

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the 
extrusion of lava of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene Epoch 
(more than 2.5 million years ago). Following the cessation of this main shield building 
phase, renewed volcanic activity occurred with the extrusion of basaltic lava of the post 
erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the alluvial sediments 
of the Palikea Formation. 
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of 
Kauai. These rocks generally are characterized as thick flows of dense basalt extruded 
from groups of vents aligned in north-south trends in various locales. Associated with the 
vents are pyroclastic materials that usually form low cinder cones at the vent. 
The basaltic rock formations built by the extrusion of lavas of the Koloa Volcanic 
Series generally are characterized by flows of jointed dense vesicular basalt with 
interbedded thin clinker layers. The weathering process has formed a mantle of residual 
soils, which grade to saprolite with increased depth. In general, saprolite is composed of 
mainly silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks. The 
saprolite grades to a less weathered basaltic rock formation with increased depth. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, many sea level changes occurred as a result of 
widespread continental glaciation. As the great continental glaciers accumulated and 
advanced, the level of the ocean fell because there was less water available to fill the 
oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded or melted, global sea levels rose 
because more water was available. The land mass of Kauai remained essentially stable 
during these sea level changes, and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. These 
glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower 
relative to the present sea level of Kauai. Placement of man-made fills for the existing 
Hokuala Kauai development within the last 30 years has brought the project area to its 
present form.

Existing Site Conditions 
The proposed Hokuala Hotel project site is within Lot 8 of the existing Hokuala 
Kauai Development in the Nawiliwili area of Lihue on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. Lot 8 of 
the Hokuala Development is located in the western portion of the Kauai Lagoons. The 
project site is approximately bounded by Ho`olaulea Way to the north, Kalapaki Circle to 
the west, and an existing golf course and lagoon (Lot 2A) to the east, as shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, the project site is relatively level with the exception of the existing golf 
course uphill of the development along the eastern boundary. The ground surface slopes 
down from the existing golf course to the proposed hotel development at a gradient of 
about four horizontal to one vertical (4H:1V) or flatter. The majority of the project site is 
on the level ground adjacent to the golf course with portions of the proposed development 
extending into the slopes. Based on the topographic survey provided, the existing ground 
elevations range from about +85 to +110 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). 
2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions across the proposed hotel development 
site by drilling and sampling six borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 6, extending 
to depths of about 5 to 41.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  In addition, two bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils were collected for CBR tests. The approximate boring 
and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our field exploration suggests that the project site is generally underlain by surface 
fills overlying residual soils and saprolites at greater depths. The fill materials generally 
consisted of stiff to very stiff silty clays and medium dense silty gravel extending to depths 
between about 1.5 and 5 feet below the existing ground surface. Beneath the surface fills, 
our borings encountered residual soils consisting of medium stiff to hard clayey silts and 
silty clays extending to a depth of approximately 35 feet below the existing ground 
surface. The residual soils were underlain by saprolites consisting of very stiff to hard 
sandy silts and very dense silty sands extending to the maximum depth explored of about 
41.5 feet below the existing ground surface.

2.4 
Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the International Building Code (2012 Edition), the project site would be 
subjected to seismic activity, and seismic design considerations will need to be 
addressed, as well as evaluation for potential soil liquefaction. 
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils near the ground 
surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build-up of excess pore water 
pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes. Soils most 
susceptible to liquefaction are loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained sands and loose silts 
with little cohesion. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration, the 
geology in the area, and our engineering analyses, we believe the potential for soil 
liquefaction at the project site is nonexistent due to the presence of stiff cohesive soils

3.1 
Shallow Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we envision 
that the proposed hotel building and minor structures may be supported on shallow 
foundations consisting of spread and/or continuous strip footings. An allowable bearing 
pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for the design of footings bearing on the 
recompacted on-site soils and/or new compacted fills (minimum 90 percent relative 
compaction) placed to achieve the design finished grades. This bearing value is for dead 
plus-live loads and may be increased by 50 percent for transient loads, such as those 
caused by wind or seismic forces.  
In general, the bottom of the footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches 
below the lowest adjacent finished grade. For interior column locations, the bottom of the 
footings should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished 
grade. Footings constructed near the tops of slopes or on sloping ground should be 
embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal setback distance of 6 feet 
measured from the outside edge of the footings (base of footings) to the face of the slope. 
Foundations next to other subsurface structures, including but not limited to existing 
foundations, utility trenches, and/or easements, should be embedded below a 45-degree 
imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the subsurface structure, or 
the proposed footings should extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. 
This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent subsurface structures with 
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation 
settlement. 
The bottom of the footing excavations should be scarified to a depth of at least 
8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture, and 
recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction to provide a relatively firm and 
smooth bearing surface prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or concrete. Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557.

Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density. 
If soft and/or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of footing excavations, 
the soft and/or loose materials should be over-excavated until dense and/or stiff materials 
are exposed in the footing excavations. The over-excavation should be backfilled with 
select granular fill materials moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, 
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Alternatively, the bottom of the footing may 
be extended down to bear directly on the underlying competent materials.  
If the foundations for the proposed hotel structures are designed and constructed 
in strict accordance with our recommendations, the total settlement of the foundations is 
estimated to be approximately 1 inch or less with differential settlements on the order of 
about 0.5 inches. 
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction between the base 
of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth pressure developed 
against the near-vertical faces of the embedded portion of the foundation. A coefficient of 
friction of 0.3 may be used for foundations bearing on the recompacted on-site soils or 
compacted fill. Resistance to lateral loads due to passive earth pressure may be 
calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of 
depth (pcf) for foundations embedded in the on-site soils and/or compacted structural fill 
materials. The passive earth pressure value assumes that the concrete for the footings is 
cast neat against on-site materials or compacted fills. Otherwise, the soils around the 
footings should be well-compacted (minimum of 90 percent relative compaction). Unless 
covered by pavements or slabs, the passive pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches 
below the finished grade should be neglected.

coverings, we recommend providing an impervious moisture barrier on top of the gravel 
cushion layer.

slabs-on-grades with No. 4 reinforcing bars design, but it should be noted there will be a

Retaining Structures 
Based on the 50 percent Design Drawings provided, we understand new retaining

3.3.1 Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions, we believe retaining wall foundations may be

3.3.4 Drainage 
The backfill immediately behind the retaining wall structure should be well-drained 
to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would 
consist of a 12-inch wide zone of permeable material, such as open-graded gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), wrapped with geotextile fabric (Mirafi 180N or 
equivalent) placed directly around a perforated pipe (perforations down) at the 
base of the retaining structure discharging to an appropriate outlet or weep holes. 
As an alternative, a prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or 
EnkaDrain, may be used in lieu of the drainage material. The prefabricated 
drainage product also should be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at 
the base of the wall.  
Unless covered by concrete slabs or pavements, the upper 12 inches of backfill 
should consist of relatively impervious material to reduce the potential for 
excessive water infiltration behind the walls. In addition, the backfill below the 
drainage outlet (or weep holes) should consist of relatively impervious materials to 
reduce the potential for appreciable water infiltration into the footing subgrade. The 
relatively impervious material should be compacted to no less than 90 percent 
relative compaction.

It should be noted that some fills associated with the construction of the existing 
golf course, and the abandoned construction site are within the grading limits of 
the proposed Hokuala Hotel project site. Therefore, the development of the new 
hotel structures will require the removal of the undocumented fills to expose the 
underlying native ground and replacement with compacted fills to support the new 
hotel structures on a shallow foundation system. 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site. 
Undocumented fills within the hotel structure footprints should be removed to expose 
the underlying native ground.  Soft and/or yielding areas encountered during clearing 
and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill and/or future improvements

of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil 
established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content 
(percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. After the 
subgrade soil is moisture-conditioned and recompacted, it should be protected to 
retain the moisture by keeping it moist or immediately capped with new fill.

3.5.4 Cut and Fill Slopes 
We envision that the cut slopes planned for the proposed development will generally 
expose the stiff to hard clayey silts encountered in the borings and the undocumented 
fill underlying the existing golf course. In general, cut slopes and fill slopes 
constructed of the on-site soils may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or 
flatter. Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into 
the existing slope to provide stability for the new fill against sliding. The filling 
operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal 
compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations. 
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope 
ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. In the event that over-cutting of a slope 
occurs, keying and benching requirements should be implemented instead of 
backfilling the slope to the design grade with sliver fills. Water should be diverted 
away from the tops of slopes, and slope planting should be provided as soon as 
possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes. 
3.5.5 Excavation 
It is anticipated that the on-site fill materials, residual soils, and saprolites may be

encountered within the undocumented fills underlying the existing golf course and 
abandoned construction site. Therefore, excavation for subsurface infrastructure 
construction may encounter cobbles/boulders and construction debris, which may 
require the use of hoerams.

Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-6. 
Some soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance 
with ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance 
with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD modified 
California sampler with the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the 
sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Four Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths, and a 
graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Four one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and/or 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the on-site soils. The test results 
are summarized on Plate B-2. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-3 and B-4. 
One Consolidation test with time rates (ASTM D2435) was performed on a sample 
of the potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the 
materials encountered.  Results of the consolidation test are presented on Plate B-5. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on a 
selected bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the soils at 25 and 56 blows per layer. The test results are presented 
on Plates B-6 and B-7. 
One Modified Proctor compaction test (ASTM D1557 Method A) was performed 
on a selected bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and 
moisture content relationships. The test results are presented on Plate B-8.

Field Infiltration Tests 
Two field infiltration tests were conducted within two borehole locations, Boring 
Nos. 1 and 6, at the site to evaluate the infiltration characteristics of the subsurface 
materials encountered. Infiltration tests in Boring Nos. 1 and 6 are approximately located 
at the north and south ends of the proposed development, respectively. The approximate 
field infiltration test locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. These tests were 
performed in general accordance with the procedures in Appendix D of the State of 
Maryland, Department of the Environment “Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I and II” 
(rev. 2009). These procedures are consistent with other state’s procedures and may 
generally be considered an industry standard. 
The field infiltration tests were performed by drilling boreholes utilizing a 4-inch 
inner diameter solid-stem auger to the selected test depths of about 5 feet below the 
existing ground surface. Upon reaching the test depth, a 4-inch diameter PVC solid casing 
was set to the bottom of the drilled borehole to allow infiltration only through the soil 
exposed at the bottom of the borehole. Falling head infiltration tests were performed to 
determine the infiltration rate of the underlying subsurface materials. Each test consisted 
of four trials of filling the casing with 24 inches of water and taking periodic readings until 
the water drained out or up to one hour. The test results are presented on Plates C-1 and 
C-2.
