Preliminary Geotechnical Recommendations
File / Original
The following contains our preliminary geotechnical recommendations for the site
grading, foundation and pavement designs of the Franciscan Vistas Development in the
Ewa Villages on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. A detailed summary of our findings and
recommendations will be contained in our geotechnical engineering report. The final
report should be consulted when it becomes available.
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project consists of the design and construction of the residential subdivision,
comprised of housing units and infrastructure development in the vicinity of the
intersection of Renton Road and O’ohao Street. The new development will encompass
approximately 14 acres, involving construction of 72 units in 11 multi-family apartment
buildings and 72 single-family houses. Site development will also include construction of
roadways; installation of utility lines; and construction of parking areas. Details for the
proposed structures and site development were not available at the time this
memorandum was prepared.
Geolabs provided geotechnical consultation services for the original 23-acre
Franciscan Vistas development under other developer in 2006. The mass grading
operation was conducted in 2010 within the entire 23 acres of the property. Consequently,
150 units in 7 multi-family apartment buildings and a community center were constructed
within approximately 7-acre graded area. Remaining 14 acres of the mass graded
property was left vacant from 2010 to 2018.

SITE GRADING
A grading plan was not available at the time this memorandum was prepared.
Based on the existing topography, we anticipate site grading work will generally consist
of minimal cuts and fills of less than 5 feet thick to achieve the design finished grades for
the project. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site and our experience
in the project vicinity, we recommend implementing the following special site grading
procedures during the earthwork construction.
Site Preparation
At the on-set of earthworks, the areas within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off-site or disposed in a
designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials.
After clearing and grubbing, the normally dry and friable soils should be
over-excavated by at least another 12 inches and replaced with general fill compacted to
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Prior to replacing the moisture-conditioned
soil as fill, the bottom of the over-excavation should be scarified to a depth of at least
12 inches, moisture-conditioned to between 2 and 4 percent above the optimum moisture
content, and recompacted to a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction.
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight)
corresponding to the maximum dry density.
Due to the hard, dry, and friable in-situ clayey and silty soils, the materials
generated from the over-excavation and scarification process would likely contain large
clumps. To moisture-condition the soils effectively, the hard, clayey clumps should be
thoroughly broken down and mixed by the compaction equipment, such as sheepsfoot
and/or large dozer, until achieving a relatively uniform moisture condition.
The 12 inches of over-excavated soils may be re-used as a source of general fill
materials, provided they are free of vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other
unsuitable material as well as moisture-conditioned to between 2 and 4 percent above
the optimum moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction.

The intent of the above site preparation procedures recommended will serve to
accomplish the following.
1. Close-up the existing shrinkage cracks observed throughout the site.
2. Reduce the future soil swelling/heaving potential by preparing the clayey
soils with elevated moisture contents and reduced compaction
requirements.
3. Decrease the affinity of the clayey soil for water.
4. Create a barrier to reduce water infiltration into the underlying dry clayey
soil.
It should be noted that the 2-foot thick reworked soil layer should also be
constructed below the finished subgrade and pad grade in cut areas.
We strongly recommend that a Geolabs representative is present during and after
the clearing and grubbing operations to evaluate the exposed ground conditions prior to
site preparation operations.
Soft and/or yielding areas encountered at the bottom of the over-excavation below
areas designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over-excavated to
expose stiff and/or dense materials. The resulting excavation should be backfilled with
well-compacted general fill materials. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be
disposed properly off-site or used in landscaped areas, if appropriate. Contract
documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over-excavation and
compacted fill placement to account for variations in the over-excavation quantities.
After the soil is moisture-conditioned and recompacted, it should be protected to
retain the moisture by keeping it moist or immediately capped with new fill. If the
recompacted soil dries out and shrinkage cracks develop, it should be scarified, moisture-
conditioned, and compacted again.
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement
weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and
replacement with well-compacted fill at no cost to the owner. A Geolabs representative
should evaluate the need for over-excavation in the field.

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
General fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness, moisture-conditioned to between 2 and 4 percent above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The non-expansive
select granular fill materials should be placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose
thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted to at
least 90 percent relative compaction (or 95 percent relative compaction for finished
subgrades subjected to vehicular traffic).
Aggregate base course and subbase course materials should be
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction.
It should be noted that our recommendations require elevated moisture contents
and reduced compaction requirements to reduce the potential swelling/heaving of the
clayey soils encountered at the site. Therefore, we wish to emphasize that moisture
conditioning of the fill materials is an integral and critical part of the earthwork
recommendations provided herein. Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot
rollers, vibratory rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction equipment.
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the subgrade soils are critical
elements of earthwork, particularly for the elevated moisture content and reduced
compaction requirements, Geolabs should provide observations and perform soil density
tests during site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the appropriate degree of
compaction and the proper ranges of moisture content. Where moisture and/or
compaction are less than required, additional moisture-conditioning and/or compactive
effort should be applied with adjustment as necessary. Where the reduced compaction
requirements are applicable, it may be necessary to scarify the soil and re-compact it to
the proper range of relative compaction, if the required range of compaction is exceeded.

Excavations
It is anticipated that the on-site fill and alluvial soils may be excavated with
conventional heavy excavation equipment. However, our past experience in the project
area and the results of our field exploration indicates that isolated coralline
detritus/formation and cobbles/boulders could be encountered. Therefore, excavation for
subsurface infrastructure construction may encounter hard coral and/or cobbles/boulders
which may require the use of hoerams or trenching machines.
The above discussion regarding the rippability of the surface materials is based on
data obtained from the field borings. Contractors bidding on this project should examine
the site conditions and soil data to make their own interpretation.
Cut and Fill Slopes
We envision the cut slopes at the project site will generally expose the stiff to hard
silty and clayey soils encountered in the drilled borings. In general, cut slopes and
permanent fill slopes constructed of the on-site soils may be designed with a slope
inclination of two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. Fills placed on slopes steeper
than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the
new fill embankment against sliding. The filling operations should start at the lowest point
and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above
general fill placement recommendations.
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope
ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. Surface water should be diverted away from
the tops of slopes, and slope planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce
the potential for erosion of the finished slopes.
BUILDING FOUNDATIONS
Based on our field exploration, we believe the planned building structures may be
supported on a Wafflemat foundation system bearing on the 2-foot reworked soil layer or
general fills placed below the finished pad grade. The Wafflemat is a post-tensioned slab-
on-grade foundation system. This proprietary system utilizes a series of recycled plastic
boxes, “Waffleboxes”, which are placed with the open side down on the soil. The
Waffleboxes are arranged to form deep ribs or footings throughout the floor system,
creating a very stiff foundation system. A post-tensioned concrete slab is monolithically
poured over and between the boxes. The empty space beneath the boxes allows for soil
expansion, reducing uplift of the slab.
An allowable bearing pressure of up to about 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf)
may be used for design of the Wafflemat foundations bearing on compacted reworked
soil layer or general fills placed in accordance with the recommendations presented

SLABS-ON-GRADE
Generally, very highly expansive clayey soils are present at the project site. Concrete
slabs-on-grade constructed above these expansive soils should be properly designed and
constructed to reduce the risk of structural distress to the lightly loaded conventional
concrete slabs-on-grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of the clayey soils due to
changes in the moisture content.
For the conventional slab-on-grade foundation, we recommend providing a minimum
of 3 feet of non-expansive, select granular fill material below the slab, to reduce the potential
for appreciable structural distress resulting from expansive clayey soils. Prior to placing the
non-expansive select granular fill material, the clayey subgrade soils should be scarified to
a depth of 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to about 2 to 4 percent above the optimum
moisture content, and recompacted to a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction. The
non-expansive select granular fill should extend beyond the perimeter of the slab-on-grade
a minimum of 3 feet (or extend to the outside edge of the concrete sidewalk surrounding the
slab-on-grade, if applicable).
For interior building slabs (not subjected to vehicular traffic or machinery vibration),
we recommend placing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of
open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) below the slabs. The open-graded

CORROSION POTENTIAL
Three sets of laboratory corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum
Resistivity (ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0),
were performed by our office and Test America Laboratories, Inc. on selected soil
samples obtained from our field exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of the on-site soils.
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the
resistivity the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. Conversely,
the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of metallic objects.
The on-site soils encountered near the existing ground surface at the project site have
resistivity values ranging from approximately 700 to 870 ohm-cm, corresponding to a
corrosion rating of 1 (Extremely Corrosive) based on the guidelines provided by the City &
County of Honolulu — Board of Water Supply.
Based on the results of the laboratory corrosivity tests, we believe that a corrosion
rating of 1 (Extremely Corrosive) may be used for the proposed development. Design of
metallic substructures, such as water line pipes or other metallic piping, should consider the
effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure.
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and
structures is dependent on the pH value, chloride content, and sulfate content found in the
soil. In general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm), sulfate
content of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH of greater than 5.0 may be considered “non-
corrosive” to underground concrete pipelines and structures. Based on the pH values
generally greater than 7.5 and relatively low chloride and sulfate contents tested on the
in-situ soils, we believe that the near-surface soils at the project site may be considered
“non-corrosive”, therefore, either Type | or Type II (Type I/II) cement may be used for the
concrete in contact with the ground.

As mentioned previously, the near-surface clayey soils at the project site are dry
and friable and exhibit very high shrink/swell characteristics when subjected to
fluctuations in the soil moisture contents. Prior to placing the 18-inch the non-expansive,
select granular fill, we recommend that the subgrade be properly scarified to a depth of
at least 12 inches, moisture conditioned to between 2 and 4 percent above the optimum
moisture content, and recompacted between 85 and 90 percent relative compaction, as
specified in “Site Preparation” section herein.
Where minor shrinkage cracks are observed after subgrade preparation, we
recommend thoroughly moistening the soil to close the cracks prior to re compacting.
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather
and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted fill.
The 18-inch capping layer below the pavement sections should consist of
non-expansive granular material, such as crushed coralline and/or basaltic materials. The
material should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches
in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the
No. 200 sieve. The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should
have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883.
Aggregate base course and subbase course should meet the material
requirements for Base Course and Subbase Course as specified in Sections 31 and 30,
respectively, of the Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction, City & County
of Honolulu, September 1986. Geolabs should test imported fill materials for conformance
with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use.
The non-expansive, select granular fill should be placed in level lifts of about
8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The compaction requirement for
the final lift of the select granular fill should be increased to at least 95 percent relative
compaction. Aggregate base course and subbase course and should be compacted to at
least 95 percent relative compaction.

provided for the paved areas which should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained
to carry surface water off-site. Surface water ponding should not be allowed on-site during
or after construction. When concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or adjacent
to the pavement areas, we recommend extending the curbs a minimum of 2 inches into
the soils below the aggregate base or subbase layer to reduce the potential for migration
