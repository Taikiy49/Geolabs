2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers
an area of approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of
the following five shield volcanoes Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea activity during
recent geologic time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 1803 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea both still active
The project site is situated on the western flank of Hualalai Mountain which
makes up the west central portion of the island The lava formation observed at the
project site appears to consist of both a a and pahoehoe type lava flows at the ground
surface which spread and ponded as they approached the ocean Ava lava typically is
characterized by a porous rough and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged
accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders Pahoehoe is
characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of
vesicular porous rock
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas especially pahoehoe type
lava Cavities form when lava is in a molten state and represent both lava tubes
intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes form when
molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may
extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow Blisters and pockets are
generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water
quite readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is
transmitted through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and _ inter flow
contacts cavities and along clinker layers Clinker and cavities serve as the primary
water transmission features because of their high permeability

2 4 Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in
the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or
during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The Island of
Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most are so small that
they can only be detected by sensitive instruments However some of the earthquakes
are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on
the Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the
Island of Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii over 90 percent
are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground shaking
diminishes with increased distance from the active volcanoes located in the southern
portion of the Island of Hawaii

Fills and Backfills
In general we recommend capping fill areas with a minimum 2 foot thick layer of
structural fill materials Structural fill materials should consist of well graded
granular materials generally less than 6 inches in maximum dimension with
sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass In
addition structural fill materials should contain less than 30 percent particles
passing the No 200 sieve by weight In general the excavated on site materials
may be re used as a source of structural fill materials provided that the materials
are processed to meet the maximum particle size and gradation requirements
presented above
Fill and backfill materials below the minimum 2 foot thick structural fill layer may
consist of general fill materials General fill and backfill materials should consist of
well graded granular materials the majority of which are less than 12 inches with
an absolute maximum dimension of 18 inches Materials between 12 and 18 inches
in particle size should be limited to about 15 percent or less of the total volume of
general fill materials The excavated on site materials if less than 18 inches in
maximum dimension may be used as a source of general fill or backfill materials
except for trench backfill provided that they are processed to meet the above
gradation requirements for general fill If the excavated materials do not contain
sufficient fines to produce a well graded material off site borrow or on site rock
crushing of larger rock fragments or boulders should be considered to provide the
required gradation and particle sizes
Imported fill materials if required should be free of organics and deleterious
materials and should be suitable for the intended use based on the
recommendations for fill materials provided herein structural and general fill In
addition Geolabs should observe and or test imported fill materials a minimum of

Based on the limited depth and extent of fills anticipated materials consisting of

density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by using sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers
or other types of acceptable compaction equipment

3 2 Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the anticipated grading
activities we recommend using shallow spread and or continuous strip footings to
support the new structures planned As an alternative the foundations for support of the
new structures may also consist of thickened edge slab footings An allowable bearing
pressure of up to 4 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of
footings bearing on dense basalt formation or compacted structural fill This bearing
value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by 50 percent for transient loads
such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the
lowest adjacent grade Footings constructed near the tops of slopes or on sloping
ground should be embedded deep enough such that the horizontal distance measured
from the outside edge at the base of the footing to the face of the slope is no less than
6 feet
In addition foundations next to other foundations utility trenches easements or
other retaining walls should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane
extending upward from the bottom edge of the structure or utility trench or the footings
should extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is
necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional
structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement
Soft and or loose materials encountered at the bottom of footing excavations
should be over excavated until dense materials are exposed The over excavation
should be backfilled with select granular fill materials moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Alternatively the bottom of the footing may extend down to bear directly on
the underlying competent material

3 3 Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the floors of the new
structures For the interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic or sustained
vibrations we recommend providing a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill below
the slabs for uniform support The cushion fill should consist of open graded gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation and also would serve as a capillary moisture break
Where slabs are subjected to vehicular traffic or sustained vibrations a minimum of
6 inches of compacted aggregate base course should be provided below the slabs
in lieu of the 4 inch thick cushion fill layer The aggregate base course layer should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
To reduce the potential for excess moisture infiltration and subsequent damage
to floor coverings an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the gravel

3 8 Percolation Testing
Three constant head percolation tests were conducted in selected borings to
provide data that supports the design of the proposed storm water disposal systems
planned The percolation tests were performed at depths of about 20 to 30 feet below
the ground surface Results of the percolation tests are presented in Appendix C
Based on our field percolation tests the subsurface materials generally have
coefficient of permeability k of about 6 8 x 10 and 2 1 x 10 feet per minute which
correspond to about 3 5 x 10 and 1 1 x 10 centimeters per second at the locations
tested Based on the test results we believe that an average hydraulic conductivity of
about 0 1 feet per minute may be assumed in the subsurface materials encountered at
depths of about 20 to 30 feet below the existing ground surface The hydraulic
conductivity value roughly corresponds to an infiltration rate of about 1 100 gallons per
day per square foot of surface area 0 002 cubic feet per second per square foot
It should be emphasized that the hydraulic conductivity of the on site materials is
highly variable due to the presence or absence of significant voids in the lava or the
presence of embedded clinker seams which greatly affect the disposal capacity of a
drywell In general the greater the number of clinker seams and or voids encountered
by the drywell excavation the higher the overall permeability and disposal capacity

The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and

truck mounted drill rig equipped with rotary coring tools The approximate locations of

The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a

Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 9
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core
run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock

APPENDIX C
Percolation Testing
APPENDIX C
Percolation Testing
As part of our field exploration program we conducted three percolation tests in
selected borings by using the constant head method In general the borings were
4 inches in diameter and extended to depths of about 20 to 30 feet below the ground
level at the time of the test A water truck was used to pump clear water into the
boreholes for the constant head test method The flow rate was recorded by timing and
calculating the volume as a relatively constant flow rate was achieved Percolation test
results are presented on Plates C 1 through C 3
