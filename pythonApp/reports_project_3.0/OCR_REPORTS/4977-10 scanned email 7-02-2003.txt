As you know the two primary factors for determining the barrier dimensioning are a the expected kinetic energies at
the barrier location and b the expected rock bounce heights at the barrier location This determination is normally
made by using computer simulation models such as the Colorado Rockfall Simulation Program CRSP such as you
have done Once these factors have been well defined the choice of appropriate barrier to use will be at the discretion
of the owner or client using the attached Load Chart as a reference for determining the degree of protection needed
versus the costs of each Please be advised that barriers with much higher ratings than are shown on this chart are
also available To further help with evaluation of your site and determination of the appropriate barrier please also
refer to the Optus dimensioning documents in the binder gave you during my recent visit
Once we have an idea of the expected kinetic energies and bounce heights we will have a basis for providing better
pricing information specific sample specs and drawings and so forth
There are a number of determining factors in the project design above and beyond the preceding considerations First
a determination must be made regarding where the barrier will be placed which will depend on a variety of factors
including aesthetic considerations deflection of the barrier nets up to 1 3 meters or more depending on the energy
rating of the barrier practicality in terms of terrain etc Along the rockfall path there will be points where the rockfall
energy will be maximum or minimum and bounce heights will be maximum or minimum Obviously from a material
cost standpoint it will be most advantageous to place a barrier at the point on the slope where energy and bounce
heights are at a minimum However if this is high up the slope the material cost advantages can quickly be overtaken
by the increase in installation costs due to the more difficult access Conversely if kinetic energy and bounce heights at
a particular point along the path are at a maximum then a higher capacity barrier of greater height will be necessary
Other considerations in the design and spec writing include whether or not the steel materials will need to be Buy
America compliant or not generally yes if there is federal funding involved what degree of corrosion protection is
needed if any painting or colorization is needed whether or not there will be separate sections of barrier instead of one
continuous barrier the total length of each section the post spacing generally 20 or 25 feet with closer post spacing
involving more material and installation cost and wider spacing giving the barrier more flexibility and thus net
deflection ground conditions for anchoring and post foundations if there is terrain irregularity along the fenceline
exceeding 15 which necessitates special shaped nets etc
Beyond the above considerations the placement of the barrier will be mostly a constructability issue access terrain
irregularity ground conditions for anchors etc
