Our field exploration along the highway widening alignment generally encountered
surface fill materials underlain by soft to medium stiff alluvial deposits extending to depths
of approximately 7 to 20 feet below the existing ground surface. Below the alluvial
deposits, the borings generally encountered colluvial soils and dense basaltic cobbles and
boulders over medium hard to hard basalt rock formation extending to the maximum depth
explored of about 136 feet below the existing ground/pavement surface.
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site and the traffic data
provided for our pavement analyses, we recommend that new pavements extending from
Aholo Road to Prison Street consist of 4 inches of AC on 7 inches of ACB over 6 inches of
UTPB on 6 inches of ASB. New pavements extending from Prison Street to Lahainaluna
Road should consist of 4 inches of AC on 7.5 inches of ACB over 6 inches of UTPB on
6 inches of ASB. Based on our engineering analyses, we believe that rehabilitation of the
existing pavements between Aholo Road and Prison Street may be performed with an
AC overlay of about 2.0 inches in thickness. For the rehabilitation of existing pavements
between Prison Street and Lahainaluna Road, the AC overlay should be increased to
2.5 inches.
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the site, we believe that shallow
foundations bearing on the recompacted on-site soils or new compacted fills may be
utilized for the support of the new retaining walls planned for the project. The retaining
structures should be designed to resist the lateral earth pressures due to the adjacent soils
and traffic loads.
Based on the results of the permeability tests conducted at the site, the shallow
subsurface conditions appear to be unfavorable for the use of drainage drywells for the
disposal of storm water runoff. In general, a very low disposal capacity of about 0.1 cfs
may be anticipated for drywells embedding in the upper alluvial soils. A higher disposal
capacity of over 1 cfs may be anticipated for drywells extending into the basalt rock
formation underlying the site at depths of over 70 to 110 feet below the existing ground
surface.

2.1. Regional Geology
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes, the older West Maui
(Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala (Pleistocene
Epoch). The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain located between these
two volcanoes. The project site is located along the south and southwest flanks of West
Maui.
The volcanic rocks of the West Maui have been divided into three volcanic
series. The Wailuku Volcanic Series is the oldest and is comprised of the basaltic lava
flows and associated pyroclastic and intrusive rocks that built the major shield volcano.
The Wailuku Volcanic Series was covered by a thin, discontinuous “frosting” of andesitic
and trachytic flows, domes, and pyroclastic deposits known as the Honolua Volcanic
Series. After a long period of inactivity, when erosion incised deep valleys into the
volcanic flows, volcanic activity returned with the eruptions that produced the flows and
cones of the Lahaina Volcanic Series.
In-situ chemical weathering of the lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to
2 million years. The weathering process has formed a mantle of residual and saprolitic
soils. In general, saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is typical of the
tropical weathering of volcanic rocks. The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation
with increased depth.
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii, the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock mass from the slopes and movement of the material
downslope toward the axis of the valley by gravity to form colluvial deposits. Once the
colluvium reaches streams in the valley, alluvial processes become dominant, and the
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium. Large areas of alluvium can be
found in the lowland areas and bordering streams.
When deposits are left in-place for long periods of time, chemical processes
begin to alter the materials, simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the
W.O. 5107-00 GEOLABS, INC. Page 5

materials. Chemical processes also cause induration, or cementation, of the
coarse-grained portion of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or

and upstream in headwaters. This continued erosion generates material, which is
transported downslope covering the older alluvial deposits. Depending on the local
base level and rate of transport, these newer sediments are generally transient in terms

3.1.5 Subgrade Preparation Below Pavement Section
In general, the area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared
and grubbed at the on-set of earthwork. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials,
and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site.
The existing AC, underlying base and subbase materials should also be completely
removed and properly disposed of off-site. Soft and yielding areas encountered
during clearing below areas designated to receive the pavement section should be
over-excavated to expose firm or stiff material, and the resulting excavation should
be backfilled with well-compacted general fill. The excavated soft and/or organic
soils should be properly disposed of off-site.
After clearing and grubbing, the future pavement areas should be excavated, where
necessary, to the pavement subgrade level, i.e., bottom of the aggregate subbase
layer. The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of at least
8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
content, and recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with AASHTO T 180 or ASTM D 1557 test procedures. Optimum moisture is the
water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry
density.

3.5 Utility Trenches
We understand that the highway widening project may require the installation of
new drain lines connecting the drywells planned for the project. In general, good
construction practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling of the
trenches for the new utilities at the site. The method and equipment to be used for
trench excavation should be determined by the contractor, subject to practical limits and
safety considerations. In addition, the excavations should comply with the applicable
federal, state, and local safety requirements.
Based on the results of our field exploration, it appears that conventional
excavation methods may generally be utilized for utility line excavation at the site. The
upper fill soils encountered at the site are generally stiff to very stiff. However, it is
anticipated that utilities extending deeper than about 3 to 6 feet below the existing

3.6 Storm Water Runoff Disposal System
Based on the information provided, we understand that the proposed highway
widening project will result in an additional increase of surface storm water runoff of
12 cubic feet per second (cfs) based on a 25-year storm event. In order to handle the
additional storm water runoff, we understand that drywells for storm water runoff
disposal are planned at selected locations along the highway widening alignment.

A total of eight field permeability tests using the constant head method were
performed in Boring Nos. 1 through 3 at selected depths to evaluate the infiltration
characteristics of the subsurface materials. Results of our field permeability tests and
the calculated permeabilities are presented on Plates D-1 through D-8 of Appendix D.
The tests indicate that the subsurface soils have a relatively wide range of coefficient of
permeability, k, ranging from about 6.0 x 10° to 1.7 x 107 feet per minute. These
coefficients of permeability generally correspond to a water run-off disposal capacity
ranging from about 0.1 to 4 cubic feet per second (cfs) for a 2-foot diameter, 30-foot
deep drywell.
In general, a very low disposal capacity of about 0.1 cfs may be anticipated for
drywells embedding in the upper alluvial soils. A higher disposal capacity of over 1 cfs
may be anticipated for drywells extending into the basalt rock formation underlying the
site at depths of over 70 to 110 feet below the existing ground surface. Basalt is
considered to be relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite readily in both
the horizontal and vertical directions. Water is transmitted through the porous rock
matrix, along joints, fractures and inter-flow contacts, cavities, and along clinker layers.
The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high; therefore, they serve as the major
water transmission features. However, it should be noted that the percolation rate in
basalt rock formation will depend significantly on the amount of fractures and the
presence of clinker layers. Therefore, the actual disposal capacity of the drywell may
vary significantly from the calculated value since the fracturing characteristics of the
basalt rock formation may not be well represented in a 4-inch diameter borehole.
Drywells on the Island of Maui are generally 2 feet in diameter and about 30 feet
in depth. The actual size and depth of the storm water runoff disposal system will
depend on the design quantity of the surface runoff. Extension of the drywell from the
planned 30-foot depth may be required depending on the specific site conditions at the
proposed drywell locations. Therefore, we recommend that unit prices for additional
drilling be obtained during bidding. Provisions should be incorporated into the
construction documents to extend the drywell depths from the planned 30 feet to
100 feet below the ground surface using a minimum 15-inch diameter extension hole.

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions along the highway widening alignment were explored by
drilling and sampling six borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 6, extending to
depths ranging from about 20 to 136 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition,
three bulk samples, designated as Bulk-1 through Bulk-3, were obtained of the
near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics. The approximate
locations of the borings drilled and bulk samples collected are shown on the Site Plans,
Plates 3.1 through 3.4. The borings were drilled using truck-mounted drilling equipment
equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools.
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic representations of the
materials encountered during drilling are provided on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1
through A-6.
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by
driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler or a 3-inch OD split-barrel sampler
with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. The blow counts needed to drive the
sampler the second and third 6 inches (a total of 12 inches) of an 18-inch drive are
shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample. Results of the pocket
penetrometer tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths.
Core samples of the rock (coral) formations encountered at the site were
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Practice D 2113, Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core
drilling is a rotary drilling method, which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation.
The material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination
and classification.
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5-foot core run, the recovery would be 80 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 80%.

The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative quality of
rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material
in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any drilling-
induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5-foot core
run, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock,
based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals."

The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock, its
relative quality, brittleness, and fissile characteristics. A dense rock formation with a
high RQD would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous
methods of excavation.

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight determinations
(ASTM D 2937) were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties. The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
One Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D 4318) was performed on a selected sample
of the clayey soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification.
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. Results of the test are
also summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth.
Seven Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 136 & ASTM C 117) were performed on
selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in
soil classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on
Plates B-2 and B-3.
One unconfined compression test (ASTM D 2166) was performed on a selected
soil sample to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the on-site clayey soils.
The test results and stress-strain curve of the unconfined compression test are
presented on Plate B-4.
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D 3080) were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. The results of the
Direct Shear tests are presented on Plates B-5 and B-6.
Two Consolidation tests with time rates (ASTM D 2435) were performed on
samples of the potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility
characteristics of the materials encountered. Results of the consolidation tests are
presented on Plates B-7 and B-8.
Three laboratory Resistance (R) Value Tests (ASTM D 2844) were performed on
selected bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-9 through B-11.

APPENDIX D
Field Permeability Testing
The permeability of the in-situ materials was evaluated by performing eight
constant head tests at various depths within Boring Nos. 1 through 3. The constant
head test was performed by pumping water into the borehole until the water surface
Stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a period of time. The water height and the
rate of pumping were then measured to provide data upon which to base the calculation
of the subsurface material permeability. The results of the field permeability tests are
presented on Plates D-1 through D-8.
