INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our soil engineering
investigation performed for the subject project The purpose of this
investigation was to explore the subsurface soil and groundwater
conditions to provide recommendations for the design of the foundations
of the 0 5 M G reservoir and design of the proposed high cut slope The
approximate location of the project site and its vicinity is shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
The results of our field exploration and laboratory tests which
we used in the engineering analysis to formulate our conclusions and
recommendations are presented in the appendices of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The propesed project consists of the following
1 A 0 5 M G concrete reservoir structure approximately 72
feet in diameter and 20 feet high
2 Site grading involving cuts into the hillside of about 15
feet western side and up to about 45 feet high eastern
side are planned to create a level pad area for the
reservoir

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located along a ridge line in South Halawa
Valley on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The site is about 4000 feet
northeast of Halawa Jail and approximately 2000 feet west of Hawaiian
Electric Company s substation
An existing steel power pole is located about 130 feet east of the
center of the tank
The topography of this site is fairly steep Sloping down the
ridgeline in a westerly direction at about 3 1 H V with ridge faces
along the northern and southern sides with slope ratios of about 1
The site is inaccessible except by walking in from an A C paved
access road that runs along the valley floor
The site is overgrown with bushes and scattered trees A 15 foot
high basalt rock cliff is located about 80 to 100 east of the site and
about 30 feet south of the existing steel power pole

The ground elevations at the site vary from about 400 to 360 feet
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The site is located in the lower portion of South Halawa Valley
which has been brought to its present form by a series of geologic
events The original valley was a youthful valley cut into the Koolau
Dome during a lower stand of the sea Several subsequent stages of
alluviation have partially filled the valley floor with fluviatile
deposits
The small ridges are erosional remants of a large deposit of
mainly colluvial material derived from the erosion of the windward flank
of the Koolau Range Valleys cut by stream erosion tend to be filled
with alluvial sands gravels and some amount of boulders In their lower
reaches recent alluvium consisting of clays and silts has covered a
majority of the older sand and gravel in the valley bottom

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The subsurface exploration generally indicates that most of the
site is underlain at shallow depths by lava rock formations which could
provide adequate foundation support for the proposed structure
From a soils engineering point of view the site is feasible for
the proposed reservoir provided our recommendations are followed
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1431 20 Page 6
0 5 M G Reservoir Foundation
The subsurface exploration generally indicates that at the
proposed finished grade elevation 354 the lower edge of the
reservoir will be underlain by alluvial colluvial deposits while the
remaining portion will be underlain by lava rock formations
Foundations
Spread and continuous wall footings bearing on the lava rock is
recommended for the reservoir An allowable bearing value of 6000
p s f may be used for footings bearing on the rock formations
The allowable bearing value may be increased by one third for
temporary wind and seismic loads
Footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade Deep footing excavation will be required
for the lower portion of the tank wall foundation which is located
over the deeper alluvial sediments in order to bear on the rock
foundation
For footing located close to the edge of the Slope area the
horizontal set back distance measured from the outer edge of the
footing to the slope face should be a minimum of 15 feet or more
Prior to concrete placement the bottom of all footing excavations
and trench backfill should be compacted to a minimum of 95 of its
maximum dry density as established by ASTM D 1557 78
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1431 20 Page 7
Footings located next to utility trenches should extend down to
the bottom of the utility trench and bear on rocky materials or
structurally designed to span over the well compacted trench
backfill to minimize footing settlement
The reservoir slab on grade will bear on various materials The
alluvial soils above the rock formation were found to exhibit
moderate to high swelling potential Therefore at the lower
portion of the reservoir where rock is not encountered at finished
grade the soils should be removed for a depth of 3 feet below the
finished grade and backfilled with non expansive granular material
to reduce the swelling potential of the alluvium Also to
minimize a hard spot where the tank slab is partly on rock and
partly on soil or compacted fill the rock formation below the
finished subgrade of the slab should be overexcavated about 10
inches and replaced with select granular material
The site grading should be designed to divert surface water away
from the reservoir foundation

should also be relatively small if the tank is properly
constructed

Lateral Resistance
For passive earth pressure an equivalent fluid pressure of 300
p s f per foot of depth can be used A friction factor of 0 4 can
be utilized to determine the sliding resistance of the structure
and foundation
Site Grading
In general the site grading at the reservoir site will consist of
cutting the ridge about 15 to 45 feet Site filling is not anticipated
at the site
Site Preparation
The site to be graded should be cleared of all vegetation organic
matter debris and soft material
Backfill
Select granular material such as base course or select borrow is
recommended under the reservoir slab and for the backfill around
the deep wall footing the backfill should be compacted to a
minimum of 95 of its maximum dry density established by ASTM
D 1557 78

Slopes
For the proposed reservoir the hillside behind the tank will be
cut for a vertical height of about 45 feet We recommend that a
cut slope ratio up to H V may be used for the underlying
rock formation and H V used for the upper soil layer
Near the finished grade the slopes should be cut with extreme care
by the contractor We believe a backhoe should be used to provide
the final finishing of the cut slope surface to minimize over
cutting by a dozer Over cutting beyond the design slope should be
avoided since it is not possible to properly backfill and recompact
soil at such a steep slope
In general surface runoff water should be diverted as much as
feasible away fram the top of the slope Slope planting should be
incorporated on the soil slope as soon as practicable to reduce
erosion and prevent unravelling of the alluvial soil exposed on the
cut slope surface
Excavation
The field exploration indicates that the majority of the site is
underlain at fairly shallow depths by dense Aa basalt mantled
with a thin layer of alluvial soil mixed with cobbles and
boulders The western side of the reservoir site will be cut into
deep alluvial deposits

It is anticipated that the alluvium may be excavated with normal
excavating methods
The more massive basalt in the top half of the H V cut
slope will likely require special excavating procedures such as
hoe ram or blasting to make the proposed cut Much of the lower
fractured rock layers can be ripped with heavy equipment
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the subsurface
materials are based on our visual observations of the existing rock
outcrops in the area and interpretation of the boring core data
We recommend that all prospective contractors bidding on this site
should examine the rock outcrops and the boring data and to make
their own interpretation

Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the soils engineer be present during
earthwork construction particularly backfill operations slope cuts and
to monitor the contractor s grading operations
It is also recommended that the footing excavations be observed by
a soils engineer during construction to see that adequate bearing has
been obtained and that the subsurface conditions are consistent with the
findings of our investigation
Any imported material required should generally be non expansive
tested and approved by the soils engineer prior to hauling to the site
If conditions encountered during construction are different from
those presented in this report then appropriate modifications to the
design should be made
