REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking
against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad plateau referred
to as the Schofield Plateau Both Waianae and Koolau Ranges have lost most of their
original shield outlines and are now long narrow ridges shaped largely by erosion
The site is located in the lower portion of Halawa Valley which has been brought to
its present form by a complex series of geologic events The original Halawa Valley was a
youthful valley cut into the Koolau Dome during a lower stand of the sea Several
subsequent stages of alluvial depositional processes have filled the valley floor with fluvial
deposits
At an intermediate stage between two series of alluvial deposits pyroclastic
materials such as cinder were laid across the mouth of the valley by members of the
Honolulu Volcanic Series These deposits of volcanic material most likely originating from
Aliamanu and Salt Lake Craters caused a diversion of Halawa Stream to the west
Stream erosion and diversion of the course of Halawa Stream have shaped the area to its
present topography During the last few decades man has been an important force in
shaping Halawa Valley to its present form with the placement of extensive fills over much
of the older alluvial deposits

Based on the subsoil conditions encountered at the project site we believe that
shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings bearing on the stiff
on site soils or new compacted fills needed to achieve the design finished grades may be
used to support the proposed structures Due to the substantial amount of cobbles and
boulders encountered during our field exploration special attention should be given to the
foundation subgrade preparation recommendations especially where boulders are exposed
at the bottom of footing elevation
Based on our laboratory tests the near surface clayey soils exhibit moderate
shrink swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents
Therefore we recommend that the moderately expansive clayey soils be capped with a
minimum of 12 inches of non expansive select granular fill material below the building slab
areas In addition special attention should be given to the recommended procedures for
subgrade soil preparation below slabs and pavements in order to reduce the potential
shrink swell effects of the near surface clayey soils

Foundations
Based on our field exploration conducted for the proposed corporation yard the
project site is generally underlain by stiff to very stiff silty clays and clayey silts with varying
amounts of embedded cobbles and boulders In general we believe that shallow
foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings bearing on the stiff clayey soils
or new compacted fills may be used for support of the proposed one story buildings planned
at the project site An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot psf
may be used for the design of foundations bearing on the stiff clayey soils or new compacted
for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
For footings bearing on the stiff clayey soils the bottom of the footing excavations
should be recompacted to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to
placement of reinforcing steel or concrete If soft and or loose materials are encountered at
the bottom of the footing excavations they should be over excavated to expose the
underlying firm materials The over excavation may be backfilled with general fills
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom may be
extended down to the underlying competent materials For footings bearing on new
GEOLABS INC
W O 4079 00 Page 10
compacted fills needed to raise the site the bottom of the footing excavations should be
recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior to placement of
reinforcing steel or concrete Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Based on our field exploration cobbles and boulders may be encountered at or near
the design footing subgrade elevations If boulders are exposed at the footing bottom during
construction the boulders should be removed to a minimum depth of 12 inches below the
design footing subgrade elevation The resulting over excavation should be backfilled with
compacted general fills
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grades For footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping ground
conditions the footings should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal
setback distance of 8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the face of the
slope Footings located adjacent to the planned retaining walls should be embedded deep
enough to avoid surcharging the retaining wall foundations or the retaining walls should be
designed to resist the additional structural loads
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the
utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to
avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for foundation settlement

recommendations we estimate that total foundation settlements would be on the order of

Slabs On Grade
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by near surface
clayey soils with low to moderate expansion potential when subjected to moisture
fluctuations To reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade
resulting from shrinking and swelling of the clayey soils due to changes in the moisture
content we recommend that the subgrade soils be properly prepared and capped with at
least 12 inches of non expansive select granular fill capping fill material below the slab
cushion The capping fill should also extend beyond the new building footprint a minimum
of 24 inches Since cobbles and boulders were encountered at various depths below the
existing ground surface across the project site the 12 inch capping fill would also serve to
provide more uniform support for the building slabs on grade that might otherwise bear
directly on the cobbles and boulders
GEOLABS INC
W O 4079 00 Page 12
The non expansive select granular fill should be placed as soon as possible after the
subgrade soils are properly prepared The prepared capping fill and the underlying subgrade
soils should be wetted and kept moist until the final placement of slab concrete Where
shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soils
be prepared again as recommended Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed
subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the
soft areas and replacement with well compacted fill
In general we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill be placed
below the interior slabs to provide the slabs with more uniform support The cushion fill
should consist of open graded gravels such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation which would also serve as a capillary break To reduce the potential for future
moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture vapor
barrier such as a plastic membrane is recommended on top of the cushion layer Two
inches of sand may be considered above the plastic membrane to provide for protection of
the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab concrete Provision of the 2 inches of fine
sand above the vapor barrier will be left up to the discretion of the project structural engineer
Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered since they can
better mask minor slab cracking
Where the slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic or equipment operation we
recommend that a 6 inch layer of aggregate subbase compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction be provided in lieu of the 4 inch thick cushion layer The 2 inch moist
fine sand and vapor barrier may also be omitted for these slabs The aggregate subbase
should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction For the design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of about
250 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection pci may be used for the compacted
aggregate subbase Where slabs are intended to function as rigid pavements a minimum
slab thickness of 6 inches may be used for preliminary design purposes In addition
GEOLABS INC
W O 4079 00 Page 13
provisions should be made for proper load transfer across the slab joints that will be
subjected to vehicular traffic
We envision that some exterior concrete walkways will be required for the proposed
project We recommend that concrete walkways be supported on at least 6 inches of
non expansive select granular fill or aggregate subbase The select granular fill or
aggregate subbase should be compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Control
joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints

The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at

be backfilled tightly against the slab with the on site soil and graded to divert water away from
the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the slabs
Conventional Retaining Walls
Due to the sloping terrain at the project site we understand that several conventional
retaining structures will be required to provide grade separation along the sloping grounds
at the project site Consideration has been given to soil nail and tied back retaining wall
systems in cut conditions to reduce construction time and cost However the presence of
substantial amounts of cobbles and boulders as encountered during our field exploration
may pose construction difficulties in the installation of the soil nail and tied back retaining wall
systems Therefore the use of soil nail and tied back retaining wall systems may not be
economically viable for this site
Based on the current design concept a two tier retaining wall system consisting of
a lower conventional concrete retaining wall and an upper Keystone retaining wall with a
sloping backfill condition is planned for grade separation between the upper parking
lots and the lower building areas In addition several other conventional retaining walls such
as concrete masonry and or gravity rock retaining walls may be constructed for this project

Wall Foundations
In general we believe that retaining wall foundations may be designed in accordance
with the recommendations presented in the Foundations section of this report Due
to the substantial height of some of the retaining structures we recommend that the
wall footings bear on at least 12 inches of aggregate subbase material to increase the
sliding resistance of the foundation Where the wall foundations will bear on the
aggregate subbase a coefficient of friction of 0 45 may be used in the design The
aggregate subbase material should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction and extend a minimum of 12 inches beyond the edges of the
footing
The in situ soils below the 12 inch aggregate subbase material should be scarified to
a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction If soft
and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the aggregate subbase layer
the soft loose materials should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm
materials The over excavation should be backfilled with aggregate subbase material
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
In general wall footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches In addition wall
foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded a minimum depth of
24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade For sloping ground conditions
the footing should be extended deeper to obtain a minimum 8 foot setback distance
measured horizontally from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope
Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should be constructed in
stepped footings

surcharge pressure acting on the entire height of the wall which is free to deflect
cantilever may be used in design For walls that are restrained a rectangular
distribution equal to 50 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure acting over the
entire height of the wall may be used for design Additional analyses during design
may be needed to evaluate the surcharge effects of point loads and line loads

Keystone Reinforced Soil Retaining Wall
Based on the current design concept we understand that a Keystone Reinforced Soil
Retaining Wall KRSRW will be incorporated into the upper tier of the two tier retaining wall
system planned at the subject site The system combines the Keystone modular concrete
wall units with geogrid reinforcement and compacted soil to result in a reinforced soil mass
that supports the earth pressure and surcharge loads This composite material essentially
forms a gravity wall structure with an ability to tolerate significant total and differential
settlements In addition the KRSRW is also desirable due to the flexibility of the wall ease
of construction high load carrying capacity and economy
Design of the KRSRW will need to take into consideration both external and internal
stability of the structure In evaluating external stability the KRSRW structure must satisfy
four stability conditions 1 bearing failure 2 translational sliding 3 overturning stability
and 4 overall slope stability Geotechnical design parameters to evaluate these stability
conditions are presented in the following sections Some of the geotechnical parameters
necessary in evaluating the internal stability of the KRSRW are presented in the Reinforced
Fill and Backfill Materials subsection of this report The internal and external stability of the
KRSRW will need to be evaluated by the design engineer We have evaluated the overall
slope stability for the KRSRW and the results are summarized in the Overall Slope Stability
subsection

Lateral Earth Pressures
The retained soil behind the KRSRW structure will exert lateral earth pressures on the
KRSRW structure Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we recommend
that the horizontal and vertical component of earth pressures equivalent to a fluid
weighing 50 and 20 pcf be used to design the internal and external stability of the
KRSRW These values assume a sloping backfill at the top of the wall and
do not include surcharge effects Lateral earth pressures due to uniform surcharge
loads imposed on the sloping backfill should be taken as 33 percent of the vertical
surcharge pressure acting uniformly over the entire height of the KRSRW structure
The lateral earth pressure is based on a friction angle of 30 degrees and a retained
fill unit weight of 120 pcf
The lateral earth pressure values provided above do not include hydrostatic pressure
that may be caused by groundwater trapped behind the KRSRW structure A
subdrain is recommended at the base of the KRSRW structure to collect and
discharge excess water that may infiltrate through the wall structure A typical
subdrain should consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of permeable drainage material
such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation with a perforated pipe
with perforations down at the base of the KRSRW discharging to a proper discharge
facility

and long term stability of the KRSRW is at least 1 5 which is the minimum factor of
safety normally recommended

wall height ratio of at least 1 0 is recommended for the KRSRW with a

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
General fill materials should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The non expansive
select granular fill materials should be placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to
at least 95 percent relative compaction The aggregate subbase and base course
materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density Compaction
should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers multiple wheel
pneumatic tired rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment Water
tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed for compaction of the fills
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the clayey subgrade soils are
critical elements of the earthwork observations and soil density tests should be
performed by Geolabs during the site grading operations to assist the contractor in
obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper moisture content on each
fill lift Where compaction is less than required additional compactive effort should
be applied with adjustment of moisture content as necessary to obtain the specified
compaction It should be noted that the moisture requirement of the on site fills and
subgrades at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture is an important
requirement considering the moderately expansive nature of the on site clayey soils

Rock or boulder fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension
of the rocks or 3 feet whichever is less Placement should be by haul trucks
traversing previously placed lifts and dumping at the edge of the currently placed lift
Spreading of the rock fill should be performed by a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar
heavy equipment to facilitate seating of the rock The rock fill should be watered

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
22 borings extending to depths ranging from about 10 5 to 51 5 feet below the existing
ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous
flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer or geologist who monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 22
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barre Sampling of Soils by driving
a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler or a 3 inch OD split barrel sampler with a
140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last
12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the
field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive
strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Three sets of pH ASTM G 51 and minimum resistivity ASTM G 57 tests were
performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration The results of
these tests are summarized on Plate B 1
Eighteen one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils under different
surcharge loads The results of these tests are summarized on Plates B 2 through B 5
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are presented on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentations of the test results are
provided on Plate B 6
Three unconfined compression tests ASTM D 2166 were performed on selected
cohesive soil samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the on site clayey
soils The test results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths Individual stress strain curves of the unconfined compression tests are presented
on Plates B 7 through B 9
Four direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the shear strength parameters The test results are presented on Plates B 10
through B 13
Four laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 14 through B 17
Four Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates B 18 through B 21
