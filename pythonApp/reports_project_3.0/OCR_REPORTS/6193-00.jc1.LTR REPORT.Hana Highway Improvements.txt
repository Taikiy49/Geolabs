Project Considerations

Hana which is heavily used by residents and tourists This section of the existing
highway is a two lane undivided road with very steep cut slopes on one side and steep
natural slopes on the downhill side The area is heavily vegetated due to the high
rainfall experienced
Based on the information provided we understand that multiple sections of the
highway experienced lateral movements resulting in settlement and longitudinal cracks
of the highway pavements It was proposed to repairs these sections and upgrade the
existing highway
Five sections of the highway were considered for repair upgrade The proposed
general the distress consisted of on going creeping movement in vertical and lateral
directions linear longitudinal cracks and or shallow slope failures along the makai side of
the travel lane

Regional Geology
The Island of Maui is comprised of two major volcanoes the older West Maui
Tertiary Epoch and the more recent East Maui also known as Haleakala
Pleistocene Epoch The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow gently sloping plain between these
two volcanoes The project site is located on the northeastern portion of the island
The East Maui Volcano is represented by the older rocks of Honomanu Volcanic
Series buried beneath younger volcanic deposits The Honomanu Series is overlain by
the Kula Volcanic Series which generally consists of thick lava flows with appreciable
embedded pyroclastic ash and cinders materials Rocks of the Kula Series cover much
of the present day surface of the East Maui Volcano
A long period of deep erosion and sediment deposition occurred between the end
of the Kula Volcanic Series and the beginning of the Hana Volcanic Series The Hana
Series generally consists of the youngest lava flows and cinder cones presently
encountered at the East Maui ground surface Although the Hana lavas were generally
more fluid and the lava flows tended to be thinner and less massive than the preceding
Kula lavas thick accumulation of lava did occur in the valleys and valley heads
The erosional process of volcanic islands is dominated by the detachment of
weathered soil and rock materials from the interior steep mountain slopes and the
movement of the eroded materials down slope by gravity toward the lowland mountain
perimeter areas Once these eroded slope materials colluvium reach a valley stream
alluvial processes become dominant and the sediments are further transported by flowing
water and deposited as alluvium
In general mountain stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that
the stream flows may transmit large volumes of water for very short duration Because of
this the transport of sediment is intermittent and the bulk of a stream s hydraulic load may
consist of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fine soil
particles When the erosional base level of a land mass changes through long term
changes in sea level or the sinking raising of the island mass sedimentary deposits may
either by reactivated renewed incision and erosion or remain deposited in place as
unconsolidated alluvial and colluvial deposits

When unconsolidated sedimentary deposits are buried in place for long periods of
time chemical processes begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing additional
breakdown and weathering of the materials The continued upland erosion generates new
eroded materials which are transported downslope and may cover the older alluvial and
colluvial soil deposits Depending on the erosional base level and the rate of sediment
transport these newer sediments may be generally transient in terms of geologic time
The project site is along Hana Highway traversing steep slopes on the sides of
large drainage ravines Because the roadway sites are in steep terrain containing
excavated cut slopes surface alluvial and colluvial soil cover is anticipated to be limited at
the project sites

Discussion and Recommendations
Existing Hana Highway in the five specified areas mentioned above has
experienced continuous creeping movement resulting in linear longitudinal cracks
Multiple asphalt concrete overlays have been placed on the original pavement to
maintain the service level As evidence thickness of the asphalt concrete pavement
encountered in the borings drilled in the makai lane was generally greater than those in
the mauka lane
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site and our slope
stability evaluation we believe that critical slope failure wedge is generally located
within the makai lane of the highway The new retaining wall is required to be designed
to restrain the potential slope failure wedge and to support the highway traffic loads
Based on the current design concept we recommend using a soil nail ground
anchor system to provide the lateral support for new concrete retaining walls installed
along the steep or near vertical makai slope face The soil nail retaining wall could be
constructed using the top down technique serving both as temporary shoring of the
excavation and the permanent retaining wall
Soil Nail Retaining Walls
A soil nail retaining wall system consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars
grouted into drilled holes to stabilize an excavation or slope A shotcrete facing is
generally applied to the face of the excavation providing the appearance of a
conventional concrete retaining wall To provide the natural rock appearance the soil
nail installed may be capped with sculpture shotcrete The reinforcing bars provide both

Design Considerations
For corrosion protection we believe that the soil nails will require some form of
corrosion protection such as a fully encapsulated double corrosion protection
system due to anticipated chloride contents of the soils
The soil nail retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of
hydrostatic pressures A typical drainage system would consist of 2 foot wide
strips of a prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain
placed on the face of the excavation between the soil nails The prefabricated
drainage product should extend from the top to the base of the wall and should
be hydraulically connected to a weephole at the base of the wall as shown on
Plate 3 The weepholes should discharge to appropriate outlets
Soil Nail Testing
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated behind the soil nail retaining
walls we recommend performing a minimum of five 5 pre production
verification tests one per area on sacrificial soil nails The sacrificial soil nails
should be fully instrumented with vibration wire strain gauges and tested to
failure or up to rebar yield strength whichever is achieved first We also
recommend conducting the verification tests on a minimum of 15 production soil
nails three per area In addition proof tests should be performed on at least
10 percent of the production soil nails during construction to confirm the bond
stresses used in the design
The verification tests should be loaded gradually to 150 percent of the design
load for the creep test and subsequently to the maximum test load of about
200 percent of the design load In addition the load at every loading increment
should be held for at least 10 minutes and creep tests should be held for at least
60 minutes The proof tests should consist of subjecting the soil nail to at least
150 percent of the design loads and the load should be held for at least
10 minutes until stable The test nails may be incorporated into the permanent
soil nail retaining wall provided that they satisfy the test criteria Pullout tests and
creep tests on the soil nails are integral components of the design for the soil nail
retaining wall system Therefore we recommend conducting pullout tests and
creep tests in the presence of a Geolabs representative

Workmanship
Construction of the permanent soil nail retaining wall should be performed by a
specialty contractor experienced in the construction of soil nail retaining walls
Due to the specialized nature of the soil nail retaining wall construction a
Geolabs representative should be present to observe the geotechnical aspects of
the soil nail retaining wall construction and testing of soil nails
To reduce the potential for caving in of the drilled holes temporary casing of the
drilled holes may be required during the soil nail installation
It is common that some large boulder or cobble may be encountered in the alluvium
and colluviums materials Dense basalt rock would likely be encountered in the
drilling depths Therefore hard drilling conditions should be anticipated by the soil
nail contractor
Drilling fluids including mineral and polymer slurry should not be allowed for the soil
nail installation If utilizing air pressure during the drilling the contractor should be
cautious when drilling is advanced into the loose soil materials to prevent
undermining the roadway subgrade Undermining the roadway subgrade will further
cause ground settlement and loss of foundation support
The contractor should fully investigate the project site and make his her own
interpretation It is the contractor s responsibility to bid the projec6t accordingly with
sufficient contingency taking into account potential caving in hard drilling and
difficult access conditions
