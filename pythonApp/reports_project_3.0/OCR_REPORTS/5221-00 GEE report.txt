This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for Maunalaha Homesites Water System Improvements project in the
Makiki Tantalus area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location
and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for
the proposed project These recommendations are intended for the design of the new
water system improvements and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger shield Koolau Volcano is estimated to
be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity
during which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Volcano volcanic activity
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These
series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series The project site is located at the
southern flank of the Koolau Mountain Range
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of
the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available
such that the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii and in the heads of valleys the erosional
processes are dominated by detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls
and are transported downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as
colluvium Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley
alluvial processes become dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited
as alluvium

3 1 Corrosion Control
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil
characteristics with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects In
general the lower the resistivity the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried
metallic structure Conversely the higher the resistivity the less likely the soil will
contribute to corrosion of metallic objects
On the basis of the field resistivity test results a majority of the test results for the
subsurface soils along the proposed waterlines within the project site have a corrosion
rating of 5 at the invert depths This indicates the subsurface soils have mildly corrosive
to corrosive potential Isolated resistivity measurement with corrosion ratings of 3 4
and 4 were encountered at Maunalaha Road at Station 24 00 and 32 20
respectively This indicates the subsurface soils have corrosive to moderately corrosive
potential Results of the resistivity surveys are presented on Plates C 1 through C 4 of

3 3 Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the
load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the pipe bedding is an important design
consideration For pipes bearing on the cinder materials 6 inches of compacted
granular cushion materials such as natural sand manufactured sand coral and
crusher screenings S4C should be provided below the pipe Cinder sand is not
allowed
The trench width shall be at least 24 inches Before the placement of pipe
cushion materials the excavated trench bottom should be observed by a representative
from Geolabs to evaluate whether firm or dense materials are exposed at the bottom of
the trench or if the pipe cushion materials should be extended deeper due to the
exposed subsurface conditions

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 extending to depths of
about 6 to 16 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a
track mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers The
approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 1
through 2 3
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer or geologist who monitored the drilling
operations on a near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 6
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX C
Field Resistivity Survey
APPENDIX C
Field Resistivity Survey
Geophysical survey equipment i e resistance meter was mobilized to the
project site on March 10 and 11 2004 for the field survey
Forty two soil resistivity surveys were conducted at a maximum of 300 foot intervals
and a Nilsson Model 400 resistance meter manufactured by Nilsson Electrical Laboratory
Inc The method utilizes four surface probes or pins electrodes to evaluate the average
resistivity an indication of corrosivity of the underlying soil mass The average resistivity
values of the soil mass extending from the existing ground surface to the test depth was
assessed by using this method at the approximate survey locations shown on the
Site Plan Plates 2 1 through 2 3 Results of the resistivity surveys are presented on
Plates C 1 through C 4
The soil resistivity surveys were performed at the existing ground surface along
the shoulder area of the existing roadways of the proposed waterlines The average
soil resistance along the proposed alignment was evaluated at each survey location
with the electrodes aligned at equal spacings of generally 2 to 5 feet The depth of
penetration of a resistivity sounding is a function of the electrode spacing The pin
spacings closest to the pipe invert depths are highlighted in rating colors on Plates C 1
and C 2
The resistance meter Nilsson Model 400 provides a voltage between the outer
electrodes causing an electrical current to flow between them The voltage drop
between the inner electrodes is then measured by a sensitive built in voltmeter As a
result the soil resistance in ohms is obtained from the voltmeter dial reading The soil
resistivity in ohm cm which is directly related to both resistance and spacing can then
be computed The evaluated soil resistances the computed resistivity values and the
corresponding corrosion ratings based on the Water System External Corrosion
Control Standards Volume 3 1991 are presented on Plates C 1 and C 2 The
computed resistivity values with pin spacings closest to the pipe invert depths at the test
locations are connected with black lines in the graphs on Plates C 3 and C 4 It should
be noted that the soil resistivity may be influenced by temperature weather conditions
existing underground facilities and other factors
