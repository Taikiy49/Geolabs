Regional Geology
The Hawaiian Archipelago consists of an elongated island chain comprising the
eight major islands of the State of Hawaii and hundreds of small islets and atolls
extending for more than 1 000 miles to the northwest of the Hawaiian Island of Niihau
The age of the islands in the Hawaiian Archipelago increases progressing from the
southeast toward the northwest direction
The Hawaiian Archipelago is believed to have formed as a result of plate tectonic
movement working in combination with a relatively deep stationary focal point of
volcanic heat and magma lava generation The Pacific Plate containing the upper
earth s crust underlying the Central Pacific Ocean continues to slowly move in a
northwesterly direction while the nearly stationary hot spot of magma generation
continues to produce eruptive emissions beneath the plate As a result a continuous
chain of volcanic islands formed and has slowly moved off in the northwesterly direction
carried away from the active center of volcanism by plate movement The Island of
Hawaii is presently located adjacent to the deep seated hot spot region The most
distant volcanic island remnants located far to the northwest of Hawaii have been
reduced to submerged seamounts as a result of a long history of land erosion and
subsidence
The Island of Hawaii the largest and youngest island in the Hawaiian
Archipelago covers an area of approximately 4 030 square miles The Island of Hawaii
was formed by the coalescence of five primary shield volcano mountains The five
primary shield volcanoes in decreasing geologic age include the following
Kohala located at the northern tip of the island which is long extinct
Mauna Kea located at the northern central portion of the island which
has had some activity during recent geologic time
Hualalai located at the western portion of the island which last erupted
in 1801

Mauna Loa and Kilauea Volcanoes located at the southern and eastern
portions of the island both of which are still active
Submarine volcanic activity on the Island of Hawaii is believed to have
commenced in the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epoch about 1 to 2 million years
ago and continues today with eruptive activity at Kilauea Volcano It is believed that the
Island of Hawaii breached the ocean surface approximately 500 000 years ago Due to
the coalescence of the flanks of the volcanoes composing the Island of Hawaii the
older mountain flanks have been overlapped and buried by recent volcanic eruptions
therefore the evolution of the broad dome shape of the Island of Hawaii is visible today
The summit elevation of Mauna Kea Volcano resides at about 13 796 feet Mean
Sea Level MSL and the summit elevation of Mauna Loa Volcano resides at about
13 679 feet MSL The broad high elevation region of the Humuula Saddle is located
between the summits of the Mauna Kea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes The elevation of
the Humuula Saddle region is on the order of approximately 6 500 feet MSL
As an illustration of the recent volcanic activity on the Island of Hawaii over the
past 150 years or so Mauna Loa Volcano has averaged a caldera eruption
approximately every few decades Kilauea Volcano continues an on going active
eruption cycle which has spanned continuously since about 1983 Therefore the
southern and eastern portions of the Island of Hawaii remain volcanically active
whereas toward the north volcanism appears to be in its waning stages
A sixth volcano Loihi which is presently an active submarine volcano lies
offshore of the southeast coastline of the Island of Hawaii and may emerge some
hundreds of thousands of years in the future Due to the presence of ongoing volcanic
activity at and Loihi the Island of Hawaii is located in a zone of
active seismic influence The Island of Hawaii experiences frequent light to moderate
earthquakes associated with the movement of underground magma and shifting of the
earth s crust under the weight of the growing island formation Occasional large
earthquakes are usually less frequent based on recorded historic occurrence and may
involve geologic fault rupture and lateral offset

Local Site Geology
The Saddle Road East Side Alignment between Milepost 11 and Milepost 19 is
on the broad easterly facing mountain slopes formed by the coalescence of lava flows
which erupted from the Mauna Kea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes In the vicinity of the
proposed Saddle Road East Side Alignment the older weathered lava flows associated
with Mauna Kea Volcano are buried by younger lava flows that were erupted from
Mauna Loa Volcano in historic time and in the late Holocene Epoch These younger
lava flows generally extend from the summit region of Mauna Loa Volcano toward the
north and east directions The northerly flowing lavas banked against the southerly
facing flank of Mauna Kea Volcano and were diverted further downslope toward the
easterly direction where some of the historic flows approach the outskirts of Hilo The
exposed lava flows mapped at the ground surface along the project alignment are
shown on the Geology Map Plate 4
In sporadic locations where the youngest surface lava flows from Mauna Loa
Volcano are thin in coverage the older slightly more weathered lava rock formations
erupted from Mauna Loa may be exposed locally at the ground surface The existing
Saddle Road alignment between Milepost 11 and Milepost 19 generally was
constructed following the natural ground surface topography resulting from these recent
lava flows which extend east northeasterly down slope toward Hilo
Based on our geological literature review and geotechnical field exploration for
this project the Saddle Road East Side Alignment between Milepost 11 and
Milepost 19 traverses surface basaltic rock lava flows consisting mainly of pahoehoe
type lava with some localized a a type lava Our site reconnaissance and test boring
exploration did not encounter appreciable surface or buried soil deposits within the
depths explored of the proposed roadway alignment between Milepost 11 and
Milepost 19

A a lavas typically encompass thick rubbly and irregular clinker surfaces
consisting of loosely indurated welded basalt rock fragments cobble to small boulder
size and occasional embedded large blocks of dense rock The thickness of the typical
surface clinker layer encountered at the project site is estimated to average on the order
of about 5 to 15 feet with some clinker materials extending to depths up to about 20 feet
below the existing ground surface
Below the surface clinker rubble very dense and often massive basalt rock
locally termed as blue rock may be encountered The dense rock contains the solid
core of the a a lava flow which may range up to several tens of feet in thickness The
a a lava flows typically advance down slope at a slow pace due to the viscous character
of the thickened lavas therefore there typically is greater surface topographic relief
associated with the deposition of a a lava flows The surface relief takes the form of
irregular choppy terrain encompassed by steep sided elevated mounds of piled
rocks which may be located adjacent to topographic depressions The surface of the
a a lava flows generally is very porous due to the rubbly clinker character which
encompasses considerable void space between the piled rock fragments
In contrast pahoehoe lava flows typically encompass thinner less viscous lavas
that flow more rapidly as successive layers typically averaging about 3 to 10 feet in
thickness with only limited inter stratified clinker materials The pahoehoe lava flows
may be thicker where underlying topographic depressions were filled as a result of

Pahoehoe lavas typically contain internal flow cavities and or void spaces derived

as localized pockets or as elongated lava tubes that may extend for significant

may range in size from several feet to over 40 feet in diameter and may consist of tube
alignments that branch converge plunge and breach the ground surface The
supporting roof over the buried tube structure may be as thin as several feet and poses
as a potential geotechnical hazard for tube structure collapse and overlying ground

Climate
Over the ocean near Hawaii rainfall averages between 25 and 30 inches a year
The islands receive as much as 15 times that amount in some places and less than
one third of it in others This phenomenon is caused mainly by orographic or mountain
induced rains which form within the moist trade wind air as it moves from the sea over
the high terrain of the islands Precipitation amounts typically are greatest over the
upper mountain slopes and crests and are least in the leeward lowlands On the higher
mountain slopes the belt of maximum trade wind induced rainfall typically lies between
about Elevations 2 000 and 3 000 feet MSL and precipitation amounts usually
decrease rapidly with higher elevations except during cyclonic storm conditions
The project site spans between about Elevation 2 800 feet MSL near
Milepost 11 and about Elevation 5 000 feet MSL near Milepost 19 Based on a review
of the rainfall distribution map presented in the Atlas of Hawaii Third Edition the mean
annual rainfall in the vicinity of the project site ranges widely from about 80 to over
160 inches of rainfall per year depending on the elevation of the east facing mountain
slopes The wide range in rainfall amount is the result of the project site location with
respect to elevation changes and orographic induced rainfall

Lower precipitation often is experienced during the summer months of June and

relatively short distances below about Elevation 5 500 feet MSL as lower elevation
areas further toward the east are approached from the west
Based on a review of available Koppen climate information pertaining to the
project site and vicinity the project site resides in a temperate climatic region
characterized as Continuously Wet Warm Temperate Cfb
Based on our observations at the project site at various times of the year typical
rainfall generally was observed to be light to moderate in intensity consisting of fog drip
and misty rainfall at elevations greater than about 3 000 feet MSL Due to the close
proximity of the volcano summits and upland slopes afternoon heating of the landmass
may produce cumulus cloud buildups which result in locally heavier downpours that
generally terminate in the cooler early evening hours In addition the air temperatures
were noted to be generally cooler with lower humidity levels At elevations greater than
about 5 000 feet MSL evaporation of moisture was observed to be appreciably
greater
The average daytime air temperatures in degrees Fahrenheit range between
the 60s and 70s at the project site with abrupt temperature changes occurring with the
formation of afternoon clouds and rain showers Night time temperatures average in the
50s to 60s It should be noted that low cloud and fog formation is a common occurrence
especially between about Elevations 3 000 and 6 000 feet MSL

Geologic Material Type No 1
The Pahoehoe Basalt Rock Geologic Material Type No 1 typically consists of
more fluid lava that flowed more rapidly as successive flow layers averaging about
3 to 10 feet in thickness with only limited inter stratified clinker seams The lava
flows may be thicker where underlying depressions were filled as a result of ponded
lava or very thin where the lava streams flowed readily down steeper underlying
terrain Pahoehoe lava flows typically comprise flatter terrain containing lower
topographic relief Pahoehoe lava typically forms hummocky ground surfaces with
frequent ground pits and surface cracks and crevices derived from the deposition
and cooling of the molten rock layers The pahoehoe flow surface generally is hard
with a limited mantle of clinker material Pahoehoe lava flows generally are less
porous than the surface clinker comprising a a lavas however the multitude of
open surface cracks readily permits water percolation into the subsurface
Pahoehoe lavas typically contain internal flow cavities and or void spaces derived
from the transfer and cooling of molten rock within the flow core The cavities may
occur as localized pockets or as elongated lava tubes that may extend for
significant distances miles along the lava flow lobe Lava tubes represent the
drained conduit that supplied the advancing flow front with molten rock Lava tubes

Geologic Material Type No 2
The A a Basalt Rock Geologic Material Type No 2 typically consists of thick
rubbly and irregular clinker surfaces consisting of loosely indurated welded basalt
rock fragments cobble to small boulder size with layer thickness generally on the
order of about 5 to 20 feet Below the clinker rubble surface very dense and often

rock contains the core of the a a lava flow which may range to several tens of feet
in thickness The flows advanced down slope at a slower pace due to the thickened
viscous character of the lava therefore there typically is greater surface
topographic relief associated with the deposition of a a lava flows The surface relief
takes the form of rolling terrain encompassed by steep sided elevated mounds of
piled rocks located adjacent to topographic depressions The surface of the a a
lava flows generally is very porous due to the rubbly clinker character which
encompasses considerable void space between the piled rock fragments

was performed in accordance with ASTM D 1586 SPT sampling provides a measure of

12 inches with a 140 lb hammer after 6 inches of initial seating

Field Permeability Testing
The permeability of the in situ materials were evaluated by performing constant
and falling head tests in selected borings to evaluate the infiltration characteristics of the
subsurface materials The constant head tests were performed by pumping clear water
into the boreholes until the water surface has stabilized and remained at equilibrium for
a sufficient period of time Then the water height and the rate of pumping were
measured to provide data upon which to base calculation of the subsurface material
permeability
During the falling head test the boreholes were filled carefully with water to the
top of the boreholes Then the time intervals and water drops were recorded to provide
data upon which to base calculation of the permeability values of the subsurface
materials The field permeability test results are presented in Appendix C

Test Boring Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions along the segment extending from about
Milepost 11 to Milepost 19 of the Hawaii State Route 200 Saddle Road by drilling and
sampling 42 borings designated as Boring Nos B 101 through B 142 The borings
extended to depths ranging from about 14 to 27 feet below the existing ground pavement
surface using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary
coring tools The field exploration work was conducted between and
are shown on the Site Plans Plates 3 1 through 3 14
Soil borings were advanced with a combination of 4 inch OD continuous flight
solid stem augers and continued with HQ size wireline coring methods to advance through
the rock formations After the borings were advanced to the maximum depth of the
boreholes the bored holes outside the pavement areas generally were backfilled with the
soil and or rock cuttings Borings within the pavement areas were backfilled with the soil
cuttings and grouted with Quikcrete concrete mix in the upper 12 inches
Our geologists classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D 2487
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1 Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 42
Samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Standard Test Method for Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and
drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained
by using diamond core drilling techniques consisting of an HQ wireline coring system with
double tube core barrel core size of 2 5 inches in general accordance with
ASTM D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description Rock
cores were described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown
on the Rock Log Legend Plate A 0 2 The Rock Description System is based on the

Recovery REC may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the
relative quality of rock masses where appropriate Recovery is defined as the actual
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt
For example if 3 7 feet of material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
mechanical and handling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on
the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the
relative quality of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of
Rocks and Minerals by Robert S Carmichael 1989

Pavement Coring Exploration
Pavement coring was conducted at thirty two 32 selected locations designated as
P 01 through P 32 along the existing Saddle Road to evaluate the existing asphaltic
concrete AC thickness and the underlying subgrade conditions The field exploration was
conducted from August 25 through The approximate locations of
these pavement coring are shown on the Site Plans Plates 3 1 through 3 14
Each investigative location generally consisted of two shallow cores P 01A and
situated within the travel lanes and the shoulder areas of the existing Saddle Road for a
total of ninety six 96 pavement cores borings The cores borings generally extended to
depths ranging from about 0 8 to 8 feet below the existing pavement surface using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools
Cores of the existing AC ranging in diameter from 3 to 6 inches were obtained and
the thicknesses were measured and recorded The drilling coring and sampling
procedures below the existing AC were conducted in similar fashion as described in
Appendix A Test Boring Exploration Soil classifications and rock core descriptions also
followed the methods as described in Appendix A and as shown on the Soil and Rock Log
Legends Plates B 0 1 and B 02 Graphic representations of the materials encountered are
presented on the Logs of Borings Plates B 1 through B 96 Detailed information
locations and results of the pavement coring exploration are summarized in Tables B 1 1
through B 1 6 for ease of reference

Field Permeability Tests
The permeability of the in situ materials were evaluated by performing twelve 12
constant head and seven 7 falling head tests in selected borings Boring Nos B 106
depths ranging from approximately 7 to 23 5 feet below the existing ground surface
The constant head tests were performed by pumping clear water into the boreholes
until the water surface has stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a sufficient period of
time Then the water height and the rate of pumping were measured to provide data upon
which to base calculation of the subsurface material permeability
During the falling head test the boreholes were carefully filled with water to the top
of the boreholes Then the time intervals and water drops were recorded to provide data
upon which to base calculation of the permeability values of the subsurface materials The
field permeability test results are presented on Plates C 1 through C 12

Laboratory Tests
Samples recovered from our field exploration program performed for the segment
extending from Milepost 11 to Milepost 19 of the Hawaii State Route 200 Saddle Road
project were examined visually and classified in our laboratory in Honolulu Hawaii
Selected samples were tested to evaluate the physical characteristics and engineering
properties of the materials encountered
Forty five 45 Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 7012 Method C were
performed on selected rock cores to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the
basalt rock formation encountered The unconfined compressive strength test results are
presented on Plates D 1 through D 3
