INTRODUCTION
This report presents our preliminary geotechnical engineering recommendations
pertinent to the design of the reservoir foundations and site grading It is important to
note that the preliminary recommendations and opinions presented in this report are
based upon a visual reconnaissance of the site vicinity The accuracy of the findings and
recommendations are limited to the degree implied by the method used
Our visual reconnaissance of the site was performed in general accordance with
our proposal dated March 3 1992

PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering reconnaissance was to obtain an
overview of the surface soils and rock conditions in the vicinity of the proposed reservoir
site to formulate preliminary geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the
design of the grading and foundations of the proposed 1 0 M G reservoir
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to construct a 1 0 million gallon M G concrete potable water
reservoir as a portion of the Villages of La i opua Planned Community in North Kona on
the island of Hawaii The proposed reservoir is approximately ninety eight 98 feet in
diameter with a finish floor elevation of 575 feet above Mean Sea Level MSL The
proposed grading for the reservoir will also provide an area for a future second reservoir
of the same size adjacent to and north of the first reservoir The site grading will entail
excavation with cuts of up to twenty five 25 feet in depth Fills are anticipated to be
minimal with the exception of the entrance roadway where they will be on the order of
maximum of four 4 feet in thickness
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located downslope westerly of the Mamalahoa Highway and
upslope easterly of the Queen Kaahumanu Highway in the Kealakehe district of North
Kona on the big island of Hawaii
The area is characterized by topography which slopes slightly to moderately
downward toward the west The topography is characterized by the original structure of
the last volcanic lava flows which have covered the site In the vicinity of the project site
the volcanic rock formation is generally a a lava The area is moderately vegetated with
grasses uhi uhi and keawe trees

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The project site is underlain by prehistoric basaltic lava flows of the Hualalai
Volcanic Series which were most likely deposited during the late Pleistocene Stearns and
Macdonald 1946
At and in the area of the project site the lava flows are in the a a form which is
characterized by a rough spiny or rubbly surface of fragmental clinker underlain by a
dense core of massive basalt which frequently has a thinner less continuous layer of
clinker below the massive core
Visual observations of exposures of existing road cuts indicate that the clinker
layers may be as thick as ten 10 feet with discontinuous ledges of hard basalt
interbedded within the clinker
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
In general our field reconnaissance indicates that the proposed water reservoir site
is underlain at the surface or at very shallow depths by volcanic rock formation It is
anticipated that the basaltic lava formation will provide adequate bearing for the proposed
reservoir However a subsurface exploration should be undertaken to verify that the
conditions are as anticipated It is important to note that unforeseen conditions such as
perched groundwater soft deposits very hard layers or cavities may occur in localized
areas A subsurface exploration utilizing rotary core drilling and or ground penetrating
radar should be performed to attempt to verify the assumed subsurface conditions at the
site prior to the earthwork and foundation construction

We also recommend that a program of probing and grouting of the foundation
subgrades be conducted during construction This program is discussed in more detail

Site Preparation
Prior to the commencement of any earthwork all areas within the grading limits
should be cleared of any vegetation and deleterious materials The resulting stripped and
processed material should be stockpiled for use in landscaping and or general yard fill
Any voids or yielding areas disclosed during the clearing operations should be
overexcavated to firm ground and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with
well compacted engineered fill
Based on our reconnaissance it is anticipated that the majority of the cut and
materials generated from cuts will likely be volcanic rock materials including clinker
material The on site excavated rock materials should be suitable for use as engineered
fill if they are properly crushed and processed placed and compacted If additional
imported material is required the material should conform to the requirements stipulated
in Section 16 of the Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction Borrow
dated September 1986
If the volcanic rock materials encountered at the site are processed and reduced
to a relatively well graded select granular material with an average maximum size of about
six 6 inches and an absolute maximum size of twelve 12 inches the material may be
utilized as structural fill
Excavation
It is anticipated that much of the earthwork for the project will consist of excavation
for the reservoir pads The hardness and excavation characteristics of the materials

underlying the site should be determined by a subsurface exploration The site may be
underlain by materials ranging from loose clinker which may be readily excavated by
conventional means to massive basalt which may require hard excavation techniques
which may include but are not limited to chipping splitting and blasting
Generally the excavation method should be left to the contractor Blasting may
be considered to expedite the excavation process Generally consideration should be
given to the economics of blasting to initially reduce the size of the excavated rock
material and to facilitate excavation as opposed to extended periods of heavy ripping at
high equipment rates However care should be exercised to avoid overshooting which
could disrupt the clinker or rock formation resulting in a loss of strength
Blasting should be performed in accordance with the applicable statutes rules and
regulations and only by a qualified operator with a proven record We recommend that
contractors and blasting operators bidding on the project be prequalified if appropriate
If blasting is to be utilized for the earthwork we recommend a program of test
shots with instrumentation by seismographs or other appropriate means
Fill Placement
In areas to receive fills the existing surface should be prepared and then
proofrolled with a vibratory drum roller with a static weight of a minimum of 20 tons to
expose and collapse any near surface voids Any voids encountered should be exposed
and then backfilled with select granular fill material placed in twelve 12 inch loose level
lifts and compacted to 90 percent of the maximum dry density established by ASTM Test
Method D 1557 78
Generally one and one half 1 inch minus size granular fills required should be
placed in level lifts not exceeding twelve 12 inches in loose thickness and compacted

Method D 1557 78 Trench and structural backfills should be placed in level lifts not

maximum dry density per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 The upper two 2 feet of fills

Any fills to be placed on slopes having ratios steeper than five horizontal to one

Conventional compaction testing with sand cone or nuclear methods is generally
not practicable in fills composed of excavated volcanic material Instead a testing
program to determine the number of passes of a compactor needed to achieve the
desired level of compaction should be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the
project Based on this testing program the number of passes required may then be used
as the field criterion for adequate compaction In general we believe that a minimum of
four to five passes of a heavy roller over each fill location is be required

Infrastructure Installation
It is anticipated that underground utilities e g waterlines drainage and electrical
lines will be installed as part of the project
In general the loose clinker materials encountered at shallow depths below the
surface of the site may tend to unravel and slough in open trench excavations Therefore
trenches in clinker will require shoring or sloping back of the trench faces It is our
opinion that trench faces may be sloped temporarily to a maximum of one horizontal to
one vertical 1H 1V for stability considerations However it should be noted that the
increased width of the trench may result in additional stresses on the utility pipes We
recommend that this be considered in the design of the infrastructure systems
Trenches in competent basalt rock may be excavated at steeper slopes
Trench sloping or shoring design and installation should be left to the contractor
and must conform to the appropriate health and safety requirements
Trench backfills should consist of well graded granular material less than three 3
inches in maximum size placed in level lifts not exceeding eight 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent maximum dry density per ASTM

Reservoir Foundations
It is anticipated that the reservoir foundations will bear on in situ volcanic formation
ranging from moderately hard basalt rock to loose clinker Since conditions at the finish
floor elevations could vary special consideration should be given to subgrade preparation
at these locations
Where the reservoir structure will soan between differing subgrade conditions i e
hard basalt grading to loose clinker or compacted fill the slab and footing areas bearing
on hard basalt should be ripped and backfilled with select fill material to a depth of two
2 feet below the bottom of the slab or footing and recompacted to a minimum of 95
percent maximum dry density to provide uniform bearing support and to lessen the
potential for differential settlement across the structure On site materials after being
processed to form a well graded gravel with a maximum size of about six 6 inches may
be utilized as the select fill material The select material should be tested and approved
by the geotechnical engineer prior to placement
Provided that these recommendations are properly followed an allowable footing
bearing pressure of 4 000 p s f may be used for the foundation design This value may
be increased by up to one third 1 3 when temporary wind or seismic loads are
considered
Footings should be embedded a minimum of twelve 12 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade Footings located next to utility trenches should be extended
down to an elevation equal to or deeper than that of the trench bottom The trench
backfill which should be completed prior to foundation construction should be

Probing and Grouting
To reduce the possibility of footing settlement due to collapse of loose pockets or
cavities in clinker or lava tubes in the underlying lava rock we recommend that the footing
excavations be probed at approximately ten 10 feet on centers with a minimum of one
1 probe drilled at each spread footing location If a cavity is located below a footing
additional probe holes should be made to delineate the horizontal extent of the cavity
The probe holes should have a minimum diameter of three 3 inches and extend below
the bottom of the footing to a depth of at least ten 10 feet
The probe holes should be grouted from the bottom of the hole using low
pressures If the grout take exceeds two 2 times the volume of the drilled hole sand
should be added to the grout to create a stiffer mixture

Pavements
Asphaltic concrete flexible pavement is anticipated for the access roads to the
proposed reservoir Based on the anticipated traffic loads and the anticipated subgrade
conditions we recommend that the following pavement section be used for preliminary
design purposes
Flexible Pavement
2 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 Inch Base Course
8 Inch Total Pavement Thickness
on Compacted Moist Subgrade
CBR and density tests and or field observations should be performed on the actual
subgrade soils encountered during construction and the above section may be revised
if necessary The recommended section assumes no appreciable growth in traffic loads
and further assumes that adequate subgrade drainage will be provided in the paved
areas Weepholes could be provided in the drainage inlet structures in order to relieve
the seepage of water which may accumulate in the subgrade

Design Review
Preliminary and also final plans and specifications for the proposed construction
should be forwarded to the geotechnical engineer for review and written comment prior
to construction This review is needed to determine adherence to the earthwork and
foundation recommendations given herein If this review is not made the geotechnical
engineer cannot assume responsibility for misinterpretation of the recommendations
Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the geotechnical engineer be retained to provide
geotechnical engineering services during construction of the foundation phases of the
work This is to observe compliance with the design concepts specifications or
recommendations and to allow design changes in the event that subsurface conditions
differ from that anticipated prior to start of construction The recommendations given in
this report are contingent upon such observations
If actual exposed rock soil conditions encountered during construction are different
from those assumed or considered in this report then appropriate modifications to the
design should be made
