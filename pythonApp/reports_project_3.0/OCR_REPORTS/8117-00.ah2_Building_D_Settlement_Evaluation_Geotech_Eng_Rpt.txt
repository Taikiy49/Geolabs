Our field exploration suggests that the project site is generally underlain by surface 
fills overlying alluvium, saprolites and basalt formation at greater depths. Based on the 
subsurface conditions encountered by our field exploration, we anticipate the fills placed 
over the stiff alluvium deposits likely induced some ground settlements soon after the 
building was constructed in the early 1970s. At the northwest corner of the building, we 
estimate minimal settlement due to the cut condition during the original construction and 
the stiff soils encountered within our borings. 
Based on the Floor Elevations provided by ControlPoint Surveying, Inc. dated 
September 18, 2020, we believe the measured floor level data points indicate Building D 
is actually sloping from the northwest down to the southeast corner of the building (away 
from the cracked wall) with an elevation difference of about 6 to 7 inches. A follow up site 
visit was performed to confirm no steps nor ledges were causing misrepresentation of the 
floor level survey data. 
The as-built foundation plans of the original Building D construction indicate the 
building is constructed on two independent shallow foundation systems divided at the 
north and south sides of the building. The difference in floor elevations appears greater 
in the north half of Building D with differential settlement of up to 5.5 inches between the 
northwest corner and the central-east corner. The building as a whole could be settling, 
but the very stiff soil and hard rock conditions underlying the northwest corner of the 
building may have reduced the total settlement of this corner resulting in differential 
settlements and structural distresses. 
Based on our field exploration and the floor level survey data, we believe the 
structural stresses occurring within Building D likely occurred due to ground movements 
at the southeast corner of the building. We recommend performing the following actions 
to further investigate the ground settlements at Building D. 
1. 
Drill additional borings around the southeast corner of Building D to 
investigate the subsurface conditions. 
2. 
Perform long-term crack monitoring of the cracks at the northwest corner of 
the building and any other significant cracks.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed in support of the Building D Settlement Evaluation Study project for the Shidler 
College of Business within the University of Hawaii at Manoa Campus. The project 
location and general site vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.  
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses. These findings 
and geotechnical recommendations are intended to evaluate the probable causes of the 
observed building settlement. The findings and recommendations presented herein are 
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report.  
1.1 
History of the Project Site 
The Shidler College of Business was established in 1949 and was originally called 
the College of Business Administration. The college was originally located at Hawai`i Hall, 
but due to it’s expanding student population, the college moved to its current location at 
the corner of University Avenue and Maile Way in the early 1970s. In 2006, the college 
was renamed after the founder of the college, Mr. Jay H. Shidler.  
The college consisted of seven buildings (including Buildings A, B, C, D, E, F 
and G) surrounding a center courtyard. Shortly after the construction, Building F (located 
at the northwest portion of the college), significant settlements were observed resulting in 
the demolition of the building. Building F was never rebuilt and only six buildings remain 
today. 
1.2

conduct a structural assessment of the Shidler College of Business. In their report dated 
July 19, 2019, several areas with observable building distresses were reported.

the exterior wall of the northwest corner of Building D. According to the report, these 
particular cracks were first noted by the university’s staff in late 2018. At the time of KAI

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
Of interest to our geotechnical analysis for foundation remediation design of the 
building are the subsurface materials encountered at the project site, the engineering 
properties of the materials encountered, and the variability of the subsurface conditions 
across the project site. Therefore, the following subsections provide a description of the 
geologic setting of the project site, the surface and subsurface conditions encountered at 
the site, and a discussion on the items needed for seismic design, such as soil 
liquefaction, soil profile, etc. 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a series 
of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone eruptions. These later eruptions are referred 
to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of 
the sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available 
such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii, the erosion processes are dominated by 
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported downslope 
toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium. Once these materials reach

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions across the project site by drilling and 
sampling four borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, extending to depths of 
about 41 to 53.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations 
are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 3. 
Our field exploration suggests that the project site generally is underlain by surface 
fills overlying alluvium, saprolites, and basalt formation at greater depths. The fill materials 
generally consisted of loose sands and gravel with varying silt content and stiff sandy silts 
extending to depths between about 0.5 and 3 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Beneath the surface fills, our borings encountered alluvium consisting of medium stiff to 
very stiff silty clays and sandy silts, medium dense to dense clayey gravel, and very dense 
cobbles and boulders extending to depths between about 27 and 41.5 feet (the maximum 
depth explored for Boring Nos. 1 and 2) below the existing ground surface.  
Underlying the alluvium, Boring No. 2 encountered saprolite consisting of dense 
silty sand extending to a depth of about 34 feet below the existing ground surface. Below 
the alluvium and saprolite, our borings encountered hard basalt formation extending to a 
depth of 52 feet below the existing ground surface. It should be noted that Boring No. 3 
extended the deepest of the four borings and encountered clinker consisting of stiff sandy 
clay beneath the basalt formation from 52 feet to the maximum depth explored of about 
53.5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings drilled at the time of our field 
exploration. It should be noted that groundwater levels may vary depending on rainfall, 
time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors.

3.1 
Ground Settlement Evaluation 
Based on the information provided, we understand that the northwest corner of 
Building D has large, visible cracks on the outside surface of the building at about the 
ground level. We understand the cracks were believed to be associated with ground 
settlements at the college. To evaluate the subsurface conditions underlying this load 
bearing wall, we drilled four borings in the vicinity of the building to evaluate the underlying 
subsurface conditions. 
Our field exploration was performed between July 9 and 15, 2020 and it consisted 
of drilling and sampling of four borings in the vicinity of Building D. Based on our borings 
drilled at the project site, we understand the project site generally is underlain by surface 
fills overlying alluvium, saprolites and basalt formation at greater depths. It should be 
noted that Boring Nos. 1 and 2 generally encountered stiff (but potentially compressible) 
soils extending to the maximum depth explored of about 41.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface whereas Boring No. 3 (located about 4 feet west of the cracked wall) and 
Boring No. 4 (located near Building G as a control reference) encountered potentially 
compressible soils to depths of about 34 and 32 feet below the existing ground surface, 
respectively, over hard, incompressible basalt formation. 
Based on the as-built site plan provided and the results of our subsurface 
exploration, Building D of the Shidler College was constructed on up to about 3 feet of cut

3.3 
Micropile Foundations 
The existing building is a three-story concrete structure supported on shallow 
spread/continuous footings at the column/wall locations. To stabilize the portion of the 
building with tilting floors and distressed walls due to settlement, it may be desired to 
underpin the existing building to stabilize the existing building foundations to keep the 
foundation from settling further. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the 
borings and tight access conditions, we recommend using a deep foundation system 
consisting of micropiles to underpin the existing Building D foundations. We recommend 
the capacity of the micropile foundation be derived primarily from skin friction between 
the micropile shaft and the alluvium, saprolite and/or dense basalt rocks encountered in 
the borings. 
A micropile consists of a small diameter (usually less than 12 inches), drilled and 
grouted, pile with steel reinforcing. The micropile foundation typically is constructed by 
drilling a borehole, placing reinforcing steel in the hole, and grouting the borehole.  
Micropiles are desirable because they can be installed readily in access restrictive 
environments and in numerous soil types and ground conditions. In addition, installation 
of the micropiles generally causes minimal disturbance to adjacent structures, the 
adjacent soils, and the environment. 
Based on the availability of local material inventory, we envision that a micropile 
system with a minimum grout bulb diameter of 7 inches may be used. We recommend 
that each micropile be designed based on an allowable compressive load capacity of 
60  to 100 kips (30 to 50 tons). The allowable compressive load capacity for the micropiles 
is for supporting dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient 
loads, such as wind or seismic forces. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered 
at the site, we recommend that the micropiles extend through the potentially compressible

greater depths, as a bonded zone.

factor of safety of 2, we estimate that the 7-inch micropile would need to extend to a depth

bulb (bonded zone) and the adjacent soils. In general, an uplift load capacity of up

3.3.2 Lateral Load Resistance 
It should be noted that the micropile foundation system provides low lateral load 
resistance due to the relatively small diameter of the micropile. Therefore, the 
lateral load resistance contributed by the vertical micropiles should be neglected.  
Lateral loads imposed on the structure foundations may be resisted primarily by 
passive earth pressure resistance against the vertical faces of the pile cap and 
grade beams and by shear acting along the sides of the pile caps and grade 
beams. 
Passive pressure resistance against the vertical faces of the pile cap and grade 
beams may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per 
square foot per foot of depth (pcf). Additional resistance to lateral loads may be 
provided by shear resistance between the sides of the pile caps and grade beams 
and the adjacent soils. A side shear resistance of up to 150 pounds per square 
feet (psf) may be used for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces only. 
3.3.3 Micropile Foundation Settlement 
Settlements of the micropile foundations will result primarily from elastic 
compression of the pile member and subgrade response. We estimate the total 
settlement of the micropile foundations to be less than 0.5 inches.  We believe that 
these settlements are essentially elastic and should occur as the loads are applied. 
3.3.4 Micropile Load Test Program 
It should be noted that the micropile load capacity is highly dependent on the 
drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed by the contractor to install 
the micropile. Therefore, the micropile load capacity may vary considerably 
between different contractors and micropile foundation systems. We recommend

performing pullout (proof) tests on selected micropiles during construction to 
confirm the load carrying capacity of the installed micropiles. Pullout tests should 
be performed on 10 percent of the micropiles installed for the project (or at a 
minimum of 1 micropile). Each pullout test should consist of subjecting the 
micropile to at least 150 percent of the design load. The micropile should be loaded

design of the micropile foundation system. Therefore, we recommend that the 
micropile test program be conducted under the observation of a representative 
from Geolabs. 
3.3.5 Micropile Construction Considerations 
Typically, micropiles utilized for underpinning structurally connect at the existing 
footing. Based on the bottom of footing elevations provided in the as-built 
drawings, we anticipate excavations extending up to about 10 feet deep would be 
required to structurally connect micropiles to the existing foundations. We 
anticipate these excavations within the low overhead space of the Building D’s 
hallway will pose significant difficulties. 
It should be noted that the bond stress between the grout bulb and the soil is highly 
dependent on the drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed by the 
contractor to install the micropile. Therefore, the bond stress between the grout 
bulb and the soil may vary considerably between different contractors and 
micropile foundation systems. 
Installation of the micropiles should be performed by a specialty contractor 
experienced in the construction of a micropile foundation system (minimum of 
five projects).  Due to the specialized nature of the micropile foundation 
construction, observation and testing of the micropile foundation system should be 
designated a “Special Inspection” item.  Therefore, we recommend that a 
representative from Geolabs (Special Inspector) be present to observe the 
geotechnical aspects of the micropile foundation construction and testing.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling four exploratory borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, extending to 
depths of about 41 to 53.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled 
with a track-mounted drill rig equipped with 4-inch OD solid-stem augers and coring tools. 
The borings were drilled at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 3. 
Our geologist or engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by 
visual and textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, 
Standard Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling 
operations on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further 
reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general 
accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for 
Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log 
Legend (Plate A-0.1). Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with 
ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil Classification Log Key (Plate A-0.2). Rock 
materials were described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as 
shown on the Rock Log Legend (Plate A-0.3). The Rock Description System is based on 
the publication “Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in 
Rock Masses” by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (April 1977). Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1.1 through A-4.2. 
Some soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance 
with ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance 
with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD modified 
California sampler with the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the 
sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the harder and/or rock materials encountered at the project site 
were obtained by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with 
ASTM D2113, Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling

Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. 
For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any 
drilling, mechanical, and handling induced fractures or breaks. For example, if 2.5 feet of 
sound material is recovered from a 5.0 foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent 
and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is 
used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of 
Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals” by Robert S. Carmichael (1989).

Specimen breaks with difficulty after several "pinging" hammer blows.
Example: Dense, fine grain volcanic rock
Specimen breaks with some difficulty after several hammer blows.
Example: Vesicular, vugular, coarse-grained rock
Specimen can be broked by one hammer blow. Cannot be scraped by knife. SPT may penetrate by
~25 blows per inch with bounce.
Example: Porous rock such as clinker, cinder, and coral reef
Can be indented by one hammer blow. Can be scraped or peeled by knife. SPT can penetrate by
~100 blows per foot.
Example: Weathered rock, chalk-like coral reef
Crumbles under hammer blow. Can be peeled and carved by knife. Can be indented by finger
pressure.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Seven Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths, and 
graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Two Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distributions is provided on Plate B-2. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected soil sample to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested. The test results are 
presented on Plate B-3. 
Six Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests (ASTM D2850) were 
performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the in-situ 
soils. The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressure was used as the applied 
confining pressure for the relatively “undisturbed” soil sample. The test results and the 
stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-4 through B-9. 
Five Consolidation tests with time rates (ASTM D2435) were performed on 
samples of the potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility 
characteristics of the materials encountered. Results of the consolidation tests are 
presented on Plates B-10 through B-14. 
Four Uniaxial Compressive Strength tests (ASTM D7012, Method C) were 
performed on selected intact core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive 
strength of the boulders and basalt formation encountered. The test results are presented 
on Plate B-15. 
Two sets of Corrosion tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0) tests, were 
performed by our office and TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on selected soil samples 
obtained from our field exploration. The test results are summarized on Plate B-16.
