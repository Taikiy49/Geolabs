SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The project site is situated at the base of the Koolau Pali a high
cliff which extends along most of the windward side of the Koolau Range
The climate along the Pali is typified by a high to very high average
annual rainfall
Following the main shield building stage of the Koolau volcano in the
late Pliocene early Pleistocene stream erosion became the dominant
agent affecting the morphology of the area
The project site is located in a major amphitheater headed valley
along a ridge of a drainageway Along the ridge in situ chemical
weathering of the parent Koolau Basalt has formed deep residual soils and
Saprolites On the side slopes of the ridge and in the floor of the
drainageways mass transfer materials such as alluvium colluvium and
landslide debris have accumlated Some of these transported materials
are sufficiently old enough to have been subjected to in situ chemical
weathering

initial soft ground stabilization with a subsequent surcharge fill
would be more suitable than removal and replacement at the northern
soft ground area Soft Ground l

Fill Material and Compaction Requirements
Generally the on site soils should be suitable for use as engineered
fill provided the high in situ moisture contents can be substantially
lowered to achieve 85 compaction Since the project site is located ina
high rainfall area the on site materials have very high in situ moisture
contents of about 15 to 35 percent above optimum Therefore significant
drying of excavated materials will be required prior to their use as fill
and or backfill

Due to the high in situ moisture contents of the on site soils and
the high amounts of rainfall occurring in the area it is our opinion that
the compaction requirement for this residential project may be reduced
from the standard of 90 to a minimm of 85 maximm dry density as
established by ASTM D 1557 78 Even with this reduced 85 minimm
compaction requirement we believe substantial moisture reduction would
still be required along with the use of light compaction construction

placement at this site

Natural Hillside Stability
The terrain of the project site rises sharply toward the ridge on the
western side of the proposed residential project site This ridge is
steep with a slope ratio of about one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V
within the Conservation District above the proposed development area
Previous slope failures involving shallow slide or mud flows can be
observed above Lot Nos 5 and 6 It appears that the stability of a slope
adjacent to the existing slides could pose some concern on the future
development area in particular Lot Nos 7 and 8
In Hawaii one of the predominant forces in weathering and erosion is
the geologic process known as mass transfer This process is the
movement of material down the side slopes of valleys primarily under the
influence of gravity The process of mass transfer ranges from very slow
movement of less than one inch per year soil creep to very rapid
movement such as the landslides which occurred on New Year s Eve of
1987 1988 in eastern Oahu
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2393 00 Page 14
The following quotation from Macdonald and Abbot 1970 provides an
excellent discussion of soil avalanches
Normally one expects a valley wall with a Slope of 50
degrees or more to consist of solid bedrock but in the wet parts of
Hawaii such slopes commonly are covered with soil Vegetation holds
the soil together and binds it to the bedrock so that it remains on
slope much steeper than the angle of equilibrium in sparsely
vegetated areas
In the wet regions of Hawaii the principal agent of mass
transfer is a type of landslide known as a soil avalanche in which
the soil cover on a steep slope simply pulls loose from the
underlying bedrock and slides downward Sometimes the avalanche
travels all the way to the foot of the valley wall and adds to the
bulk of the colluvial apron which indeed has been almost wholly
built by slides of this sort At other times it travels only part
way down the wall before it is checked The bright red scars left by
these slides are very conspicuous on the green hillsides for a few
months after the avalanche occurs
Most soil avalanches are small commonly less than 100 yards
long downslope and less than 100 feet wide though rarely they may
be two or three times that large The mass of soil removed generally
is only 2 or 3 feet thick but rarely may be as much as 10 feet
The frequency of soil avalanches is closely related to heaviness
of rainfall Some are caused by nearly every exceptionally heavy
rain The additional weight of the water saturated soil possibly
together with some degree of lubrication by the water causes less
stable portions of the mantle to pull loose from the bedrock and
slide downhill Sometimes the actual tearing loose seems to be
initiated by a single large tree that is leaning outward and pulls
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2393 00 Page 15
the soil loose with its roots Following a heavy Kona storm in 1930
Stearns counted 14 new avalanches in the head of Nuuanu Valley
alone
As can be seen in the above discussion such soil avalanches are part
of the normal weathering and erosion processes in Hawaii Usually these
avalanches are generally small surficial slumps or skin tears of the thin
soil cover which occurs on steep slopes and are generally not indicative
of mass instability of the slope itself
During dry periods the soil covers on steep Slopes desiccate and
began to crack forming fissures for water to enter and accumulate Long
periods of rainfall allow for deep saturation of the soils and heavy
rainfall events add water faster than the soils allow percolation and
runoff This causes portions of the saturated soils to become overloaded
and to fail or slide
It appears that the existing slope failure above Lot Nos 5 and 6 is
an example of the rapid form of mass transfer which is usually referred to
as soil avalanche
For a particular slope it is current belief that the time of slide
occurrence and the potential volume of debris which may be involved are
not possible to predict since they are dependent on weather conditions
local soil variabilities and hydrogeologic settings Therefore although
our current reconnaissance of the hillside area above the development site
did not disclose obvious signs of potential instability there is no
guarantee that a debris flow could not occur if very intense and or
prolonged periods of rainfall occur This is an inherent risk with any
hillside lot
In light of the above there are some precautions which could help to
reduce the risk of damage to the subdivision from shallow debris flows

Fill Materials
Material for fill shall consist of on site soils or approved import
material Fill material shall contain less than 5 percent organic
matter and other deleterious material and shall not contain rocks or
lumps in excess of eight inches 8 in diameter
Representative samples of the materials to be used as compacted fill
shall be tested by the Geotechnical Engineer Samples of the
proposed materials shall be submitted to the Soils Engineer at least
seven working days prior to its intended jobsite delivery

Placement and Compaction of Fill
A
The fill shall be placed in horizontal layers which when
compacted will not exceed ten inches 10 in thickness Each
layer shall be spread uniformly and blade mixed to attain
uniformity of material and water content within each layer
Additional fill material shall not be placed on any fill layer
which has not been properly compacted Subsequent removal of
any unacceptable layer shall be done at the Contractor s
expense
Rock fragments or boulders less than eight inches 8 in
diameter may be utilized provided they are not concentrated in
any one area and that there is sufficient fine grained material
to fill the voids between the rocks
Rocks greater than eight inches 8 in diameter shall be
removed from the fill unless specifically approved for placement
in the fill by the Geotechnical Engineer
All fill shall be compacted to a minimm of 85 percent of the
maximum dry density established by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
unless otherwise specified in the Geotechnical Engineer s
Report
Boulders encountered in excavations may be used in the deeper
fills provided that the following are followed
1 Boulders shall be spread out and must not be nested
together They should be placed so that compaction
equipment will be able to compact around then

The compaction equipment may be sheepsfoot vibratory or
pneumatic tire rollers suited to the soil type and capable of
producing the specified results in an expedient and efficient
manner The equipment shall be in good working condition fully
ballasted and self cleaning
The compaction shall be accomplished in a systematic and uniform
manner over the entire fill area to produce a uniformly
compacted fill
