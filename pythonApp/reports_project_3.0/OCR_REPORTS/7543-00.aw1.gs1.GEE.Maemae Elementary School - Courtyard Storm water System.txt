Based on our subsurface exploration, the project site is generally underlain by stiff to 
hard silty clays and cobbles and boulders to about 6 to 11.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface.  Below the stiff to hard silty clays and cobbles and boulders, residual saprolite, and 
basalt rock formation were encountered extending to the maximum depth explored of 
approximately 16.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  The residual soil and saprolite 
consisted of very stiff clayey silt.  The basalt rock formation was soft.  We did not encounter 
groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. 
We understand that the invert for the new drainage inlet and manhole structures will

allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used. To provide more uniform bearing 
support, an 8-inch gravel cushion layer consisting of No. 3 Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation) should be provided below the bottom of the structures. 
Subsurface infiltration tests were performed in two of the drilled borings at a depth of 
about 6 feet below the existing ground surface.  Based on the infiltration testing, the 
infiltration rates ranged from about 0.13 to 1.25 inches per hour.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the proposed Courtyard Storm Water System, Ma’ema’e Elementary 
School project in the Honolulu District on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location 
and general site vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the proposed project. These recommendations are intended for the design of drainage 
structures, and excavation and backfill only. The findings and recommendations 
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site is at the Ma’ema’e Elementary School in the Honolulu District on 
the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  Ma’ema’e Elementary School is located at 319 Wylie Street. 
We understand that new storm water drainage improvements, consisting of new 
inlet and manhole structures, and subdrains are planned at the lower end of the Courtyard 
area near the Kitchen and Cafetorium.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene Epoch in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island.  Volcanic 
materials erupted from the Waianae Volcano are referred to is the Waianae Volcanic 
Series. 
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene 
(Ice Age) in age and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. Volcanic 
materials erupted from the Koolau Volcano are referred to as the Koolau Volcanic Series. 
The Waianae Volcano became extinct while the Koolau Volcano remained active; 
therefore, the Waianae Volcano’s eastern flank was partially buried below the younger 
Koolau lavas that banked against its eastern flank.  These banked or ponded lavas formed 
a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau of Central Oahu. The present-day 
form of the Island of Oahu is a result of stream dissection and mass erosion of the steep 
valley walls. 
During the history of the evolution of the Island of Oahu, multiple global fluctuations 
in sea-level occurred as a result of the advance and retreat of the great continental 
glaciers.  These sea-level changes occurred more substantially in the Pleistocene Epoch 
and had some significant effect on the geologic evolution of the Island of Oahu.  The 
changes in worldwide sea-level affected the erosional baseline of streams and caused 
local submergence and emergence of the coastal landforms with respect to the level of 
the sea. 
The end of volcanic activity associated with the Koolau Volcano was followed by a 
long period of erosion whereby deep valleys were carved into the Koolau Mountain 
Range.  The valleys were filled with thick accumulations of alluvial sediments as the island 
slowly sank under the weight of the new landmass. 
The project area is located on the southern flank of the elongated Koolau Volcanic 
Range, which forms the southern portion of the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The range was

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, the borings encountered stiff to hard silty clays and cobbles and 
boulders underlain by residual soil, saprolite, and basalt rock formation extending to the 
maximum depth explored of about 16.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  We did 
not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on our borings, the new drainage inlet and manhole structures will bear on

two of the drilled borings.  Detailed discussions of our findings and geotechnical

3.1 
Drainage Structures 
We understand that new drainage inlet and manhole structures will be constructed 
for the project.  In addition, we understand that the invert of the drainage structures will 
range from about 5 to 10 feet below the existing ground surface. Based on the subsurface 
conditions encountered in our borings, we anticipate that the bottom of the drainage 
structures will bear on very stiff silty clay/clayey silt soils.  
In general, we anticipate that the stiff silty clay/clayey silt soils encountered at the 
project site may be utilized for support of the reinforced concrete inlet and manhole 
structures.  An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) 
may be used for the design of the inlet and manhole structures bearing on the stiff silty 
clay/clayey silt soils. 
An 8-inch gravel cushion layer should be provided between the bottom of the inlet 
and manhole structures, and the underlying foundation soils to provide more uniform 
bearing support.  The gravel cushion layer should consist of No. 3 Fine gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation). The cushion layer should extend a minimum of 12 inches 
laterally beyond the edge of the drainage structures.  
Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a 
one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V) imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom 
edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines.  This requirement is

3.2 
Excavation and Backfill 
Based on the design invert elevations of the drainage structures, excavation 
depths on the order of up to about 10 feet below the existing ground surface and 
subsequent backfilling is anticipated for the project construction. 
 Excavation Method 
In general, the method and equipment to be used for excavation should be 
determined by the contractor, subject to practical limits and safety considerations.  
Based on our field exploration, excavation will involve cuts into the underlying very 
stiff to hard silty clay/clayey silt.  Cobbles and boulders and basalt rock formation 
were encountered in the borings.  It is anticipated that the very stiff to hard silty 
clay/clayey silt may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment where 
the material is near the existing ground surface.  However, deep excavations and 
excavations into the cobbles and boulders and basalt rock formation may require 
the use of hoerams. 
In general, the excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than the horizontal 
distance equal to the depth of the excavation measured from the outside edge of 
the excavation.  
 Excavation Support 
In order to install the new drainage structures, we anticipate that temporary 
excavation depths on the order of about 5 to 10 feet below the existing ground 
surface will be required for the project construction.   
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our borings and the depths of 
the excavations anticipated, we believe that shoring of the sides of the excavations 
may be necessary to protect personnel working in the excavations.  Excavations, 
shoring support, and construction works should conform to all applicable Federal, 
State, and other safety requirements.

 Subgrade Preparation 
In general, the bottom of the drainage structure excavations should be compacted 
to a firm and unyielding condition prior to the placement of the cushion layer.  If 
soft and/or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the excavations, the 
soft and/or loose materials should be over-excavated to expose the underlying firm 
materials.  The over-excavation to remove the soft and/or loose materials should 
be backfilled with select granular fill materials compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction.

 Backfills 
In general, the excavated on-site soils may be used as a source of general backfill 
materials, provided that deleterious materials such as vegetation are removed and 
over-sized materials greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension are screened. In 
the event that imported fill materials are needed for the project construction, imported 
fill materials should consist of select granular fill material, such as crushed coralline 
and/or basaltic materials. The materials should be well-graded from coarse to fine 
with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain 
between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should 
have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or more and should 
have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM 
D1883. 
 Backfill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
The backfill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture 
content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Where 
trenches are located below pavement areas, the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill 
below the pavement grade should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative 
compaction.   
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.  In addition, compaction by water 
ponding or jetting should not be allowed for this project. 
Site work operations should be observed by a Geolabs representative. It is 
important that our representative monitor the earthwork operations to evaluate whether 
undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and scarification process 
and to confirm whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar to those encountered 
in our field exploration.

3.3 
Subdrains 
We understand that a series of subdrains will be installed to collect subsurface 
water.  We anticipate that the subdrains will be at fairly shallow depths.  In general, we 
recommend providing granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of open-graded gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) below the pipes.  The granular bedding should extend 
beyond the sides of the pipe a minimum width of one-fourth (¼) the outside diameter of 
the pipe or 12 inches, whichever is greater.  The open-graded gravel materials should be 
used for the initial backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes. The open-graded gravel 
bedding and initial backfill should be wrapped with a filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equal). 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from 12 inches above the pipe to the top of 
the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on-site soils or select granular fill materials.  
The backfill material should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the 
optimum moisture, placed in maximum 8-inch horizontal loose lifts, and compacted to no 
less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future 
ground subsidence.

We understand that the project is considering a retention and disposal system for 
the on-site management of storm water run-off.  In order to obtain subsurface infiltration 
information in support of the storm water runoff disposal system design, we conducted 
falling head infiltration tests in Boring Nos. 4A and 5A. 
These infiltration tests were performed in general accordance with the procedures 
in Appendix D of the State of Maryland, Department of the Environment, “Stormwater 
Design Manual, Volumes I and II” (Rev. 2009).  These procedures are consistent with 
other state’s procedures and may generally be considered an industry standard.

infiltration only through the soil exposed on the bottom of the boring.  Falling head 
infiltration tests were performed to determine the infiltration rates of the underlying 
subsurface materials.  Each test consisted of four trials of filling the casing with 24 inches

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon 
information obtained from the borings. Variations of the subsurface conditions between 
and beyond the borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not 
become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be 
necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations presented in this report. 
The boring locations indicated in this report are approximate, having been taped 
from existing visible features at the project site that is shown on the existing site plan 
transmitted by Pacific Architects, Inc. on April 12, 2017. Elevations of the boring locations 
were interpolated from the spot elevation shown on the same plan. The boring locations 
and elevations should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
five exploratory borings, designated as Boring Nos. 4, 4A, 5, 5A, and 6, extending to depths 
of about 6 to 16.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled with a 
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. The borings were drilled at 
the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist/engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual 
and textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing 
in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard 
Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification 
System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil 
classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil Classification Log 
Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock samples were described in general accordance with the Rock 
Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-5. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the Standard 
Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Please note that the efficiencies of the safety hammers used for this project were 
measured in May 2015 and the resulting energy transfer ratios are shown on the Logs of 
Borings. The soil consistencies and densities presented on the logs of borings have been 
adjusted based on the energy transfer ratio, as appropriate.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. The pocket penetrometer test results are summarized 
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
