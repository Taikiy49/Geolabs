This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering evaluation 
performed for the Design-Build Request for Proposal (RFP) of NSA Kunia Perimeter Road 
Erosion Control project located in Kunia on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project 
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1 and Overall 
Site Plan, Plate 2. 
This letter report summarizes the findings and preliminary geotechnical 
recommendations derived from our field exploration and engineering analyses. The 
information presented in this report is preliminary and is not intended to be used for final 
design and construction. The selected design-build contractor should conduct an 
independent geotechnical exploration and analyses for their specific design. The findings 
and preliminary recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at 
the end of this report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project involves stabilization of eroded slope areas along the perimeter 
security road at the NSA Kunia facility. The purpose of this project is to provide erosion 
control measures to maintain the integrity and safety of the security road. The scope of 
work includes document preparation for solicitation of the design-build RFP for the 
erosion control project. 
Based on the information provided, four eroded slope areas were initially identified. 
Due to the construction budget restraint, two high prioritized areas (Location Nos. 1 and 3), 
as shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 and 3.2, were included in our study. The remaining 
two lower prioritized areas (Location Nos. 2 and 4) may be included as options to this 
project.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae and 
Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late 
Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early 
Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time erosion 
incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a series of lava 
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are referred to as the 
Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
Schofield Plateau was formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded against 
the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene Epoch. The dips 
of the lava beds generally range from 3 to 5 degrees. The lava flows on the plateau have 
weathered to depths of about 50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the red colors of the 
soil. The deep narrow canyons, such as Waikakalau, Kipapa, and Kaukonahua, are the 
result of this weathering.  
The northern portion of the Kunia area lies at the mouth of a broad amphitheater 
headed valley where colluvial and alluvial materials intercalate with and sometimes overlie 
the Koolau lavas.  These colluvial and alluvial materials weather to dark brown and reddish 
brown soils and generally have higher clay fractions than the more silty residual soils.  The 
brown and reddish brown soils generally consist of ancient alluvial deposits overlying 
weathered volcanic rock. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITION 
We anticipate that the subsurface conditions generally consist of stiff to hard 
residual/saprolite soils overlying weathered basalt rock formation at the greater depth. 
Based on the field observation, larger basaltic boulders and/or rock remnants were 
exposed on the slope surface.  
Detailed subsurface conditions will be presented in our Data Report.  
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions and our observation, we believe 
that the slope failures may be a result of saturation of the existing over steepened slopes. 
Although surface runoff may have contributed to the erosion problems, it appears that the 
source of much of the runoff is from off site areas. Therefore, control of surface runoff

under a separate project.  
Two areas, designated as Location Nos. 1 and 3, were previously identified as top 
priority for slope stabilization and erosion control. Based on the slope geometry and slope 
failure mechanism, we believe the slope stabilization may consist of soldier pile concrete 
lagging walls and/or segmental retaining wall with combination of erosion control matting 
and slope regrading. 
We understand that a steel sheetpile tieback or deadman anchor system was 
previously proposed by a contractor. Due to limited space between the slope and the 
property line, we believe that the proposed tieback or deadman anchor would likely extend 
beyond the property line at Location Nos. 1 and 3. In addition, installation of the sheetpile 
would likely encounter boulders and/or rock remnants that would prevent penetration to 
the desired depths. Predrilling to assist in the sheetpile installation would reduce the 
passive pressure resistance in front of the sheetpiles unless the predrilled holes were 
backfilled properly. For these reasons we feel that a sheetpile wall may not be appropriate 
for these slopes. Considering the high rainfall environment, exposed steel sheetpile would 
likely have potential corrosion concern.  
Detailed discussions and recommendations for slope stabilization and erosion 
control at each location are presented in the following sections. 
Location No. 1 (Priority #1) 
The slope at Location No. 1 is located behind the existing warehouse building 
near the entry gate, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 3.1. The security perimeter road is 
situated at the top of the slope adjacent to the property line and Kunia Road to the east. 
The slope height varies from approximately 40 to 70 feet with an overall gradient of 
about one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V). Based on the slope geometry and the 
slope failure mechanism, Location No. 1 may be divided into three areas for the 
stabilization and erosion control. 
Location 1.1 
This portion of the failure slope, exposing a near vertical failure scarp, is about 
60 feet long. Horizontal distance between the top edge of the failed slope and the 
nearest edge of the road is approximately 3 feet. To maintain the integrity and 
safety of the security road, we recommend installing a soldier pile concrete 
lagging wall, as shown on the Schematic Soldier Pile Concrete Lagging Wall 
(Location 1.1), Plates 4.1 and 4.2. 
The soldier pile concrete lagging wall is a cantilever retaining wall system, 
consisting of installing drilled shafts and HP steel piles with concrete planks as 
lagging. The drilled shafts and HP steel piles may be installed at closer spacing 
of about 6 to 8 feet along the failure slope. The concrete plank panels may be 
precast and installed between the HP steel piles within the cantilever portion. The

As an alternative, a segmental retaining wall system may be installed, to stabilize

Location 1.2 
A portion of the security road at Location No. 1, about 140 feet long, was 
originally constructed below the top of the slope, as shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 3.1. Soil/rock debris accumulate at the edge of the road from run-off/storm

sight distance, we recommend removal of the slope portion that is above the 
roadway grade, as shown on the Schematic Slope Stabilization and Erosion 
Control (Location 1.2), Plate 6. 
We also recommend installing erosion control matting on the finished grade of 
the level ground adjacent to the roadway and the existing slope below, to 
minimize continuous soil erosion from run-off/storm water, also shown on Plate 6. 
Location 1.3 
The remaining portion of the security road at Location No. 1 is about 180 feet 
long, adjacent to the tall slope, up to 70 feet high and with various gradients 
ranging from 1H:1V to 1.5H:1V, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 3.1. The 
majority of this portion of the roadway is located next to the slope within a 
horizontal distance of about 3 to 8 feet. Localized potholes/ruts caused by 
run-off/storm-water were observed in the roadway, indicating on-going erosion. 
To reduce the risk of continuous erosion extending into the slope, we 
recommend installing a soldier pile concrete lagging wall, as shown on the 
Schematic Soldier Pile Concrete Lagging Wall (Location 1.3), Plate 7. We 
recommend installing erosion control matting on the existing slope below the new 
soldier pile concrete lagging wall to minimize continuous soil erosion from 
run-off/storm-water. 
Similar to Area 1.1, the segmental retaining wall system may be installed as an 
alternative, to stabilize the entire slope, as shown on the Schematic Segmental 
Retaining Wall (Location 1.3), Plate 8.

Location No. 3 (Priority #2) 
The slope at Location No. 3 is about 100 feet long, above and to the east of the 
existing softball field and basketball courts, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 3.2. The 
total slope height is on the order of about 60 feet although the eroded portion is 
generally limited to the upper 15 to 20 feet. The slope erosion has affected the eastern 
edge of the security road. A section of the roadway edge was protected by a timber 
lagging wall due to slope failure. Other localized slope failure was also observed with a 
vertical failure scarp of about 6 to 8 feet deep. 
To repair the failed slope sections and reduce risk of continuous erosion 
extending into the remaining slope section, we recommend installing a soldier pile 
concrete lagging wall, as shown on the Schematic Soldier Pile Concrete Lagging Wall 
(Location 3), Plate 9.  
Similar to Areas 1.1 and 1.3, the segmental retaining wall system may be 
installed as an alternative to stabilize the upper slope above the bench, as shown on the 
Schematic Segmental Retaining Wall (Location 3), Plate 10. 
The erosion appears to be caused by runoff running down the security road 
adjacent to this area. Much of the runoff appears to miss an existing drain outlet near 
this area. Unless the drain inlet can be repaired/modified to intercept the runoff, a 
stabilized drain outlet should be installed to divert this runoff into the heavily vegetated 
area below the security road. 
Location No. 2 (Priority #3) 
This site is located at the northernmost end of the access road above a paved 
parking lot. The total height of the eroded slope is about 50 feet and the affected road 
length is about 120 feet.  
Previously described repair schemes using the soldier pile with concrete lagging 
or the segmental retaining wall system may be used at this location. Exploratory borings 
should be drilled at this site to determine appropriate soil parameters for use in the 
design. 
Location No. 4 (Priority #4) 
This site is located near a sharp bend in the road, above a parking lot and small 
building. The total height of the eroded slope is about 70 feet and the affected area is 
about 120 feet long.  
Both the soldier pile with concrete lagging system and the segmental retaining 
wall system appear feasible to stabilize the security road at this site. Exploratory borings 
should be drilled at this site to determine appropriate soil parameters for use in the 
design.

LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from field borings. Variations of subsoil conditions between and 
beyond the field borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not 
become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be 
necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations provided herein. 
The field boring locations indicated herein were taped with reference to the features 
shown on the Topographic Survey Map transmitted by AECOM, entitled “Utility and 
SATCOM Areas, Proposed NAVSECGRUA BEQ, United States Military Reservation 
Wheeler Field Extension, Upper Pouhala, Waikele, Ewa, Oahu, Hawaii”. Boring elevations 
were obtained by interpolating between the spot elevations shown on the same plan. The 
physical locations and field boring elevations should be considered accurate only to the 
degree implied by the method used. 
This report has been prepared for the exclusive use of AECOM for specific 
application to the NSA Kunia Perimeter Road Erosion Control project in accordance with 
generally accepted geotechnical engineering principles and practices.  No warranty is 
expressed or implied. 
This report has been prepared solely to assist the architects and engineers in the 
preparation of the design/build request for proposal (RFP) design documents for the NSA 
Kunia Perimeter Road Erosion Control project. Therefore, this report may not contain 
sufficient data, or the proper information, for final design to form the basis for construction 
cost estimates or contract bidding. A contractor wishing to bid on this project should retain 
a competent geotechnical engineer to assist in the interpretation of this report and/or 
performance of site-specific exploration for bid estimating purposes. 
The owner/client should be aware that unanticipated soil conditions are commonly 
encountered.  Unforeseen soil conditions, such as perched groundwater, soft deposits, 
hard layers or cavities, may occur in localized areas and may require additional probing or 
corrections in the field (which may result in construction delays) to attain a properly 
constructed project.  Therefore, a sufficient contingency fund is recommended to 
accommodate these possible extra costs. 
This geotechnical engineering evaluation conducted at the project site was not 
intended to investigate the potential presence of hazardous materials and/or 
archeological features existing at the site.  It should be noted that the equipment, 
techniques, and personnel used to conduct a geo-environmental exploration differ 
substantially from those applied in geotechnical engineering.
