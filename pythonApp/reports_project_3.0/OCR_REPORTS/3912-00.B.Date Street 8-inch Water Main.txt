REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western one third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of
the eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while
W O 3912 00 B Page 5
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in
central Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of the Koolau_ shield
Its geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic
fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu
Coastal Plain The subsurface conditions in the Waikiki area are dominated by fossil
Pleistocene Age coral algal reef deposits and related deposits which are intercalated with
terrigenous sediments laid during high stands of the sea The base of the stratigraphic
section in this area is Koolau Basalt
The Koolau volcanic shield was built during the early Pleistocene by thinly bedded
basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities ceased approximately 2 million
years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicate that the island of Oahu has subsided as
much as 1 200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic activity Macdonald and
Abbott 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of the
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed
behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the
lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell since less water was available to
fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers melted global sea levels rose
The land mass of Oahu remained essentially stable during these changes and the
fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands
of the sea which were both higher and lower relative to present sea level on Oahu
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3912 00 B Page 6
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the
sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of
upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high
energy conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal
sediments During these stable periods the growth of fringing coral algal reefs formed
distinct ledges of reef limestone at elevations roughly conforming to the level of the sea
during that period About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred
During that rise the valleys in the project area were submerged In the last 10 000 years
or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine sediments
have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting
in thick deposits of soft loose harbor sediments along the coast in the areas which were
formerly valleys and drainageways
Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have brought
the Waikiki area to its present form Many of the resulting fills are of poor quality in terms
of supporting heavy structural loads

Microtunneling
Microtunneling pipe jacking is a trenchless process for installing underground
pipelines ducts culverts and utilities The technique involves the use of powerful
hydraulic jacks that push or jack successive pipe sections through the ground
behind a remotely controlled excavating unit or cutting disk head at the same time
as the excavation is taking place within the excavating unit
In order to install a pipeline using this technique jacking and receiving pits will
need to be constructed A concrete thrust block constructed behind the jacking
frame within the jacking pit provides the reaction to the hydraulic jacks
In weak poor ground conditions piling or other arrangements may need to be
employed to increase the reaction capability of the thrust block The hydraulic
jacks pressing against the back of the pipeline advance the disk cutting head into
the soil formation To ensure that the jacking forces are distributed around the
circumference of a pipe being jacked a thrust ring is normally provided with the
design depending on the number of jacks being used The jacks are
interconnected hydraulically to ensure that the thrust from each jack is the same
W O 3912 00 B Page 11
The number of jacks used may vary depending on the pipe size the strength of
the jacking pipes the length to be installed and the anticipated frictional
resistance
The disk cutting head of the tunnel boring machine is controlled via cables from a
cabin on the surface where the operator monitors speed pressure and tunneling
accuracy At the face of the tunnel a rotating cutter head digs into the soil and
rock and mixes it with groundwater and water pumped from the surface Rocky
material is reduced to a maximum of inch size in a crushing chamber located
directly behind the cutter disk The excavated material is pumped out of the tunnel
as slurry The water introduced into the machine is matched in pressure and
volume to the material being displaced This prevents over excavation of the
tunnel face thereby reducing the potential for cave in of the soil surrounding the
new casing The cutting disk speed and direction of rotation the hydraulic jacking
system as well as the slurry system operations can be controlled from one control
panel located remotely at the launch pit The hydraulic steering cylinders located
on the disk cutting head can be extended to steer the head back on course should
any deviation be detected A laser guidance system provides information such as
length of drive vertical and horizontal heading vertical horizontal and_ roll
deviation from the programmed course and steering cylinder status data on a
near continuous basis so that adjustments can be made continuously to keep the
disk cutting head on course

Thrust Block Resistance
The thrust block provides the reaction for the main jacks when pushing the
pipe forward The allowable passive resistance of the soil which is a
function of the strength of the soil will control the thrust resistance that can
be developed It may seem reasonable to allow the thrust block to develop
the full passive resistance because this is a temporary construction loading
condition However it must be recognized that relatively large strains are
required to develop the full maximum passive resistance When jacking
loads approach the maximum passive resistance there will be large
movements of the thrust block in response to the strains required to
develop full passive resistance These movements may result in damage to
buried utilities or other facilities behind the thrust block
In addition large movements of the thrust block are not desirable for pipe
jacking because the movements are usually not uniform and it becomes
difficult to maintain the thrust block perpendicular to the pipe alignment If
the thrust block does not remain square the force applied to the pipe may
not be uniform resulting in eccentric loading which could damage the pipe
Design of the thrust block for this project should take into consideration the
potential presence of loose sands adjacent to the shaft excavations These
loose sand deposits may limit the jacking forces that can be mobilized
unless a rigid reinforced concrete thrust block is constructed to distribute
the jacking forces over a large enough area to avoid overstressing these
soils

W O 3912 00 B Page 17
Movements of the thrust block can also be controlled by limiting the
allowable passive resistance to about one half of the maximum passive
resistance This corresponds to a much lower level of strain and smaller soil
movements If the project requires higher jacking forces than can be safely
transferred to the soil mass behind the thrust block the soil should be
improved or stabilized

Horizontal Directional Drilling HDD
Horizontal directional drilling is a trenchless steerable installation method using
a multi axis drilling machine to bore a small diameter pilot hole The pilot hole is
bored using either controlled fluid jetting or fluid assisted mechanical cutting or
combinations of both The pilot hole is reamed as necessary to accommodate
the pipe The pipe is pulled back into the reamed hole by the drilling machine
The advantage of a HDD installation on this project would be the elimination of
the deep jacking and receiving pits needed for microtunneling installation This
would greatly reduce or eliminate the shoring and dewatering requirements
However due to limitations on the drilling angles at the entry and exit points at
the ground surface HDD would require a much longer installed length of pipe
This would require relocation of some of the lateral lines resulting in longer
lengths of lateral pipes Additionally the accuracy of HDD is less than that of
microtunneling therefore care must be taken when crossing under the existing
box drain to provide the proper clearance between the new pipe and existing
box Nevertheless HDD appears to be a viable and possibly cost effective
alternative for this project
Generally the HDD hole would be drilled nearly horizontal under the box culvert
section with entry and exit angles limited to a maximum of about 14 degrees
from horizontal Shallow working pits may be required at each end of the bore to
allow placement of the drill and allow transition to the conventional pipe trench
installation
W O 3912 00 B Page 19
The vertical and horizontal alignment of the drill hole is monitored from the
surface by a locator which tracks signals from the transmitter contained inside
the drill head housing The contractor should carefully monitor the drilling
operation when approaching the box culvert to assure proper clearance
Waterline Installation by Trench Method
Trench Excavation
Based on the information provided for the proposed water main alignment the
majority of the pipeline invert will be about 4 to 5 feet below the existing ground
surface and be constructed by trench excavation Besides the Ala Naio Stream
crossing some localized deeper and shallower inverts are planned to cross the
existing utilities Our field exploration disclosed that the site is generally underlain
by loose and or soft alluvial soils at shallow depths along the proposed water main
alignment The depth of the unconsolidated soils may extend to about 4 to 11 feet
below the ground surface Below the unconsolidated materials generally
competent and permeable coral formation may be encountered As a result some
of the deeper trench excavations such as the utility crossings may encounter
medium dense to dense permeable coral formation within the trench bottoms
The contractor should be made aware of this potential condition Shallow
groundwater conditions may be expected on the order of about 3 5 to 5 feet below
the ground surface
We anticipate that the trench excavations will encounter shallow groundwater
conditions and potentially unstable subsoil conditions associated with the
loose soft silty alluvial sands It is also possible that some of the excavation
bottoms will encounter the consolidated coral formation In either case dewatering
of the excavations may be necessary due to the relatively permeable condition of
the subsoils
Excavation Method
It is anticipated that conventional earthmoving equipment may be used to excavate
the trenches based on the subsurface conditions encountered However it should
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3912 00 B Page 20
be noted that existing abandoned underground utilities and other below grade
concrete structures may exist at the site and would require heavy equipment
excavation Furthermore some of the deeper excavations may encounter hard
coral formation that may necessitate the use of heavy equipment to excavate In
general the method and equipment to be used for excavation should be
determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations
The excavated soils from the trench excavation should be stockpiled no closer
than a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the excavation from the edge of
the excavation to reduce the potential for ground movement
Excavation Support
We envision that the bottom of the trenches will typically extend to a depth of
about 5 to 7 feet below the existing ground surface Because the proposed new
water line will be along the roadway sloping of the sides of the trenches may not
be possible due to the limited space Based on the excavations anticipated
shoring of the sides of the trenches will be necessary to facilitate the proposed
trench excavations and to protect personnel working in the trench All trench
excavation dewatering and construction works should be conducted in
conformance with applicable Federal State and other safety requirements
Temporary shoring such as interlocking sheet piles may be used to shore the
trench excavation especially where dewatering of the trenches will be necessary
However it should be noted that the proposed water main will traverse other utility
lines therefore care should be exercised to avoid damaging the existing utility
lines if sheet piles are to be used as shoring If sheet pile shoring is used it would
also serve as a cut off wall for the trench dewatering operations where
groundwater is encountered within the depths of the excavations To use the
sheet piles as a cut off wall the sheet piles should extend to a sufficient depth to
reduce the amount of water to be dewatered within the trench
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with all applicable
safety requirements It is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be

retained by the contractor to design and evaluate the shoring system used
The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor
Even if good construction procedures are followed some movement of the shoring
system and the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses
during excavation Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to
estimate accurately the magnitude of ground movement The magnitude of ground
movements also depends greatly upon workmanship such as how quickly and

Dewatering
We anticipate that the trenches will generally be about 5 to 7 feet deep Shallow
groundwater conditions were encountered in the borings at depths of about 3 5 to
5 feet below the existing ground level Therefore dewatering of the excavations
may be necessary to facilitate the proposed construction in these areas
For trenches that extend below the high groundwater levels dewatering with a
cut off wall is recommended We recommend that dewatering provisions for
the trenches be included in the contract documents for the proposed construction
The dewatering operation should be conducted in such a manner that the
dewatering will not cause areal ground subsidence which may cause potential
damage to the existing structures
Since trench dewatering may involve the discharge of groundwater from the
dewatering operation into the adjacent drainage system a National Pollutant
Discharge Elimination System NPDES permit will be necessary Boring No 1
was converted to a groundwater monitoring well during our field exploration for
sampling of groundwater as required by the NPDES permit The groundwater
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3912 00 B Page 22
sampling has been completed and the results are provided in a separate report
The contractor should consult their independent consultant or the
State of Hawaii Department of Health for the latest regulations and
information pertaining to the NPDES permit application
Dewatering for construction is the sole responsibility of the contractor
The selection of equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the
contractor and he she should be aware that modifications to the dewatering
system may be required during construction depending on the conditions
encountered The dewatering method selected should have minimal impact on the
existing groundwater levels surrounding the proposed excavation Dewatering
operations should be performed such that only clear water is pumped Excessive
pumping which removes soil fines may result in a blowing or heaving of the
excavation bottom or sides considering the soft loose soils encountered near the
streams
In addition special caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering utility
trenches connected to the excavations If this occurs the granular bedding and or
backfill in the utility trenches could act as subdrains and cause significant areal
groundwater drawdown resulting in settlements and potential damage to utility
lines and or other existing adjacent structures
It should be noted that the subsoil conditions encountered below the groundwater
table at the site consist of highly permeable sand and coral formation Because
the proposed water main is located in a developed area the dewatering operation
should be conducted in such a manner that the dewatering will not cause areal
ground subsidence which may cause potential damage to the existing structures
Therefore consideration should be given to a dewatering system that includes a
cut off wall
We believe that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents should be
written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural groundwater

may include but are not limited to well pumping grouting cut off walls

We suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in the selection of
a suitable method of dewatering
1 The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or damage to
existing buildings roads and the environment
2 The dewatering method should maintain stability of and also provide safe
and dry working conditions in the excavation
3 The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow modifications
to accommodate various ground conditions
Thrust Blocks and Earth Pressure on Pipes
We envision that thrust blocks will be required at some locations such as the
Ala Naio Stream crossing to provide resistance to the thrust forces due to the pressurized
pipes The thrust force acting on the pipes may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the face of the thrust block Resistance due to passive pressure may be
calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of
depth pcf above the groundwater table and 150 pcf below the groundwater for thrust
blocks embedded in the in situ soil To estimate the frictional resistance between the
thrust block and surrounding soils a coefficient of friction of 0 3 may be used These
values assume that the soil around the block is well compacted and the top of the thrust
block is embedded a minimum of 3 feet below the ground surface
Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the
load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the pipe bedding is an important design
consideration For pipes bearing on the medium dense to very dense coral a minimum
of 6 inches of compacted granular bedding material should be provided below the pipe
For pipes with inverts near the soft loose soils an additional 18 inch of No 3B Fine gravel
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3912 00 B Page 24
wrapped in a filter fabric Mirafi 140N or equivalent should be provided below the
bedding layer for more uniform support A typical section of the trench is presented on
Plate 3
The granular bedding should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width of
one fourth the outside pipe diameter and should extend half way up the sides of the pipe
The bedding material should consist of clean granular material such as No 3B Fine
gravel Before the placement of bedding material the excavated trench bottom should be
observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii to confirm that firm materials are
exposed at the bottom of the trench or if the bedding should be extended deeper as
recommended above
Backfill
In general the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the
Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction September 1986 The first zone
of backfill extending from the bedding material to at least 12 inches above the top of the
pipes should consist of select free draining well graded granular material to reduce the
compaction effort required and resulting stresses on the pipe The granular material
should be well graded less than 1 inch in maximum size and should have less than
10 percent passing the No 200 sieve Compaction by water ponding or jetting may be
allowed provided the granular material is self draining such as No 3B Fine gravel
The trench backfill from 12 inches above the top of the pipes to within 2 feet below
the finish subgrade may consist of the excavated on site sandy soils The backfill should
be placed in thin lifts normally 8 inches in loose lift thickness with each lift mechanically
compacted to a minimum 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to
the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of
the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
The trench backfill from 2 feet below the finished subgrade elevation up to the subgrade
elevation should consist of select borrow compacted to not less than 95 percent relative
compaction A typical section of the trench is presented on Plate 3

Precautions should be taken against the occurrence of pipeline floatation before
and during trench backfilling due to possible influx of large quantities of groundwater into
the trenches It should be noted that the uplift buoyant forces due to groundwater influx
may lift empty or even partially filled pipes
Earth Pressure Loads on Pipe
Loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the trench the size of the
pipes the unit weight of the backfill material and the frictional resistance between the
backfill material and the trench walls For a preliminary estimate of the vertical loads on
the buried water main pipes an average unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic foot pcf for
the backfill material and a coefficient of friction of 0 3 between the backfill and trench
sidewalls may be used Earth pressure forces upon the pipe increase rapidly with
increased width of the trench Therefore the width of the water line trench should be kept
to a minimum Traffic loads on the buried pipes should also be considered where the
proposed water line alignment is located within traveled ways
Settlements
Primary settlement of the new force main construction is normally caused by the
difference in the unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier
compacted backfill material placed over the pipes The net increase in loading will cause
settlement of the underlying subsoils below the trench invert Based on our calculations
primary settlement on the order of less than 1 inch is anticipated for the project
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good
workmanship will be engaged during construction Additional settlement could occur if
improper trench support or dewatering procedures are used Therefore we believe that
the proposed force main should be designed with sufficient flexibility to accommodate
possible settlements
