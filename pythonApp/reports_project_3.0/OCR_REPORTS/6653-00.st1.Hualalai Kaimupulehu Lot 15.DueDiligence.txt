Regional Geology 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covering an area of 
approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of the following 
five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea (activity during recent geologic 
time), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801), and Mauna Loa and Kilauea (both still active). 
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of Hualalai Mountain, which 
makes up the west-central portion of the island. The lava formation observed at the project 
site (prior to site grading activities) appears to consist of both a'a and pahoehoe type lava 
flows, which spread and ponded as they approached the ocean. A’a lava typically is 
characterized by a porous, rough, and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged 
accumulation of rock fragments, cobbles, and boulders. Pahoehoe lava is characterized by 
a smooth, rope-like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular (porous) rock. 
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas, especially the pahoehoe type 
lava. Cavities form when the lava is in a molten state, and they represent both lava tubes 
(intra-flow cavities) and blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities). Lava tubes form when 
molten lava drains from the cooling flow, leaving a hollow tube-like structure, which may 
extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow. Blisters and pockets are generally 
smaller in horizontal extent. 
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite 
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions. In general, water is transmitted 
through the porous rock matrix, along joints, fractures and inter-flow contacts, cavities, and 
along clinker layers. Clinker and cavities serve as primary water transmission features 
because of their high permeability. 
Site Description 
As mentioned previously, the project site is at Lot No. 15 of the Kaimupulehu 
Estates (formerly Parcel 13) of Hualalai Resort. The Kaimupulehu Estates generally is 
bounded by golf course Hole No. 12 to the north, Hole No. 11 to the east, Hole No. 10 to 
the south, and Hole No. 18 to the west. Lot No. 15 encompasses an area of about 
47,480 square feet. 
Based on the grading plan, the original ground surface elevations within the 
building envelope boundary ranged from about +148 to +155 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) 
at the majority of Lot No. 15. A Site Plan of the lot is provided on Plate 2. Lot No. 15 
generally was graded to a finished pad elevation of about +152.5 feet MSL. The areas 
within the building envelope generally was over-excavated down to about +150.5 feet MSL 
during the mass grading operations to provide at least 2 feet of structural fill materials 
below the finished pad elevation. Therefore, mass grading at Lot No. 15 generally 
consisted of excavations of up to about 5 feet deep and placement of up to about 5 feet of 
new fills to achieve the design grades.

Subsurface Conditions 
In general, the original site topography (prior to site grading activities) consisted of 
irregular (or hummocky) lava flow terrain with frequent topographic depressions separated 
by adjacent higher relief mounds and low ridge features. 
Based on our observations during the mass grading operations, the project site 
generally was underlain by medium hard to hard basalt formation and clinker materials. It 
should be noted that lava tubes, cavities, and blisters are commonly encountered in 
basaltic lava flows in the project vicinity. In addition, several lava tube features were 
observed at the project site during the mass grading operations. It should be noted that 
near-surface lava tubes detected within the limits of grading for the mass grading 
operations generally were collapsed, and the loose materials from within the collapsed 
lava tube were removed. The resulting excavation was backfilled with compacted fill in 
general accordance with the recommendations outlined in the earthwork specifications. 
Fill materials consisting of some cobbles and boulders and structural fills were 
placed in the fill areas. As mentioned previously, areas within the building envelope of the 
lot generally was over-excavated to about +150.5 feet MSL to provide at least 2 feet of 
structural fill materials below the finished pad elevation. Structural fills generally consisted 
of well-graded granular materials generally smaller than 6 inches in dimension. It should 
be noted that general fills consisting of cobbles and boulders of up to about 2 feet in 
dimension were placed at least about 2 feet below the finished grades within the pad 
areas (in fill areas) and choked off with finer-sized materials.

Lava Tubes and Cavities 
As mentioned previously, lava tubes and cavities commonly are encountered in the 
pahoehoe basalt formations in and around the project site area. Lava tubes and cavities 
may vary significantly in size from small blisters to large lava tubes of 20 feet in diameter 
or more. In general, the majority of small cavities are limited in lateral extent. However, it 
should be noted that the potential for long lava tubes that may traverse across the entire 
project site exists. The general orientation of the long lava tubes is generally in a 
mauka-makai direction. 
At the commencement of the mass grading operations at the site, extensive 
subgrade preparation procedures were employed within the limits of grading to reduce the 
potential for collapse of near-surface cavities and/or voids within the lots. The intent of the 
subgrade preparation procedures is to assist in detecting and collapsing near-surface 
cavities and/or voids contained in the porous near-surface materials. It should be noted 
that the subgrade preparation procedures employed for this project are consistent with the 
standard of practice for similar type projects and similar subsurface conditions along the 
Kohala Coast on the Island of Hawaii. 
In general, the subgrade preparation in fill areas consisted of ripping the original 
ground to a depth of about 3 feet or until refusal using a ripper tooth of a large bulldozer to 
assist in detecting and collapsing near-surface cavities and/or voids contained in the 
porous near-surface materials. After the fill surfaces have been ripped, the ground 
generally was leveled with the use of a large bulldozer working the surface for about four 
passes to provide a relatively level surface. After leveling of the rugged surfaces with the 
bulldozer, the fill subgrades were generally proof-rolled with a sheepsfoot roller compactor 
(minimum 20 tons) for about eight passes to help detect and collapse near-surface cavities 
and/or voids. 
Subgrades in cut areas generally were proof-rolled with a sheepsfoot roller 
compactor (minimum 20 tons) for about eight passes to help detect and collapse 
near-surface cavities and/or voids. 
Based on the extensive subgrade preparation procedures conducted during the 
mass grading of the site, we believe that potential for encountering lava tubes and cavities 
generally have been significantly reduced. Special attention should be given to the site 
preparation requirements where additional site grading is planned. In general, where lava

Foundations 
We envision the buildings for the proposed residential dwelling generally will consist 
of single-story structures. Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the project 
site, we believe the proposed structures may be supported on a shallow foundation 
system consisting of spread and/or continuous strip footings. As mentioned previously, 
lava tubes and cavities may be present in the basaltic lava flows at the project site. To 
reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of the cavities 
directly below building foundations, a program of cavity probing and grouting of the 
foundation locations for buildings located over cut areas and fill areas may be necessary 
during construction. 
Additional Services 
The need for probing and grouting under the structure foundations in the cut and fill 
areas should be further evaluated based on the actual subsurface conditions at the 
structure locations and the extent of the structures proposed. Therefore, we recommend 
evaluating the subsurface conditions by conducting a design-level field exploration 
program consisting of drilling and sampling borings and/or test pits during the design 
phase of this project. 
Limitations 
It should be noted that it is not possible to observe all the site conditions based on a 
review of literature only. Variations of subsurface conditions from those described in this 
letter report may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become 
evident until further geotechnical engineering exploration is conducted at the site or 
construction is underway. A design-level geotechnical engineering exploration should be 
conducted to confirm and/or modify the preliminary recommendations provided herein. Our 
services on this project were performed in accordance with generally accepted standards 
of geotechnical engineering practice; no warranty is expressed or implied. 
This letter report has been prepared solely for the purpose of assisting the owner in 
the evaluation of potential risks of the project only. Therefore, this report may not contain 
sufficient data, or the proper information, to serve as the basis for detailed design and 
preparation of construction drawings. A design-level geotechnical exploration should be 
conducted for detailed design of the project.
