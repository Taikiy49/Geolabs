INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed New Ala Moana Chevron Station project located at the
intersection of Ala Moana Boulevard and Queen Street in the Ala Moana area in the
District of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of
the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommenda
tions resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses
These recommendations are intended for the design of foundation site grading and
excavation only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to
the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated
to be of the middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be of the late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age When the building of the Waianae and Koolau
volcanoes came to an end only a slight embayment existed on the southern coast of
Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of
the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available
such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3541 00 Page 6
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits
at correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in
the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials Land development and reclamation projects
within the last century have brought the project site to its present form

Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our exploration we believe
that a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings may
be used to support the proposed one story building food mart and pump island
canopy An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 p s f may be used for the design
of footings bearing on the medium dense or dense near surface soils and or fill
material Footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade The allowable bearing pressure recommended is for dead plus
live loads and may be increased by one third 14 for transient loads such as temporary
wind and or seismic forces We estimate that the total settlements under the
anticipated design loads to be on the order of less than 1 inch with differential
settlements of approximately inch
Because the existing underground tank structures are located within the footprint
of the proposed pump islands some of the pump island canopy footings will be located
above the excavation and backfill of the existing tanks Therefore the resulting
excavation for the removal of the underground tanks should be properly backfilled to
provide good foundation support for the canopy footings Typically the canopy footings
for pump islands are embedded at least 5 feet below the finish grade to achieve the
required uplift resistance However because groundwater was encountered within
5 feet below the ground surface in the borings we believe that it would be difficult to
prepare the subgrade for the canopy footings where it is near the groundwater level
Therefore we recommend that the embedment of the canopy footing be limited to
about 3 feet below the existing ground surface to reduce the potential for difficulty in
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3541 00 Page 10
footing subgrade preparation and to reduce the potential for encountering the
groundwater level during construction
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the spread footing and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings
A coefficient of friction of 0 30 may be used between the base of the footings and the
supporting materials Resistance due to passive pressure may be calculated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f above
the groundwater table and 150 p c f below the groundwater for footings embedded in
the in situ soil These values assume that the concrete for footings is placed directly
against the footing excavations Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive
resistance in the upper 12 inches below finished grade should be neglected
We anticipate that the proposed structures such as the canopy for the pump
islands will be subjected to uplift forces Uplift loads may be resisted by a combination
of the following 1 dead weight of the structures and 2 weight of backfill material
above the footing where applicable Contribution of dead weight from the backfill may
be estimated using a unit weight of 110 p c f For evaluating uplift forces due to
hydrostatic pressures a groundwater level elevation of 2 feet MSL or higher may be
used for design to take into consideration tidal fluctuations
Where footings are to be located adjacent to below grade structures or utilities
the footings should extend to a depth below an imaginary 45 degree plane projected
upward from the bottom of the below grade structure or utility This requirement is
necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional
structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement

Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed
structures such as the one story building and pump islands We recommend that the
subgrade be properly prepared and kept moist prior to placement of cushion fill and
slab concrete We envision that the slabs on grade will be supported by the medium
dense or dense sandy and gravelly soils near the ground surface
In general the subgrades for slabs on grades should be scarified to a depth of
at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted
to not less than 90 percent relative compaction The compaction requirement should
be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction for areas subjected to
vehicular traffic such as the pump islands Relative compaction refers to the in place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
For more uniform support of interior slabs such as for the one story building
food mart we recommend that the concrete slab on grade be supported on a
minimum 4 inch cushion fill The cushion fill layer should consist of open graded
gravels such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation which will also
serve as a capillary moisture break The slab cushion fill should be placed as soon as
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3541 00 Page 12
possible after the scarification and recompaction of the subgrade soils as recommended
above For slabs on grade with moisture sensitive surfacing we recommend that an
impermeable membrane be placed over the cushion fill to reduce potential migration
of moisture vapor through the concrete slab In order to promote more uniform curing
of the slab concrete and to provide protection of the vapor membrane we recommend
that a 2 inch layer of moist fine sand be placed above the membrane prior to placing
the slab concrete The sand should be moistened slightly prior to placing concrete
Where concrete slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as at the pump
islands we recommend that the slab be supported on a 6 inch layer of base course
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction in lieu of the 4 inch cushion
fill layer and 2 inch sand The base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate
The subgrade should also be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture
conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to not less than
95 percent relative compaction For design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade
reaction of 250 pounds per cubic inch p c i may be used for the compacted base
course The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to any unpaved area should be
embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade

Dewatering
Since the proposed excavations will be about 15 feet deep and groundwater was
encountered at about 4 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface dewatering of the
proposed excavations will be necessary for the proposed construction The excava
tions for the underground fuel storage tanks will need to be in a relative dry condition
to facilitate the installation of the tanks and subsequent backfill operations
Because of the granular nature of the subsoil conditions encountered it is
anticipated that dewatering of these excavations would involve large volumes of water
In addition because the proposed project site is located in a developed area the
dewatering operation should be conducted in such a manner that the dewatering will
not cause areal ground subsidence which may cause potential damage to the existing
structures Therefore consideration should be given to a dewatering system that
includes a cut off wall to reduce the volume of water to be removed within the
excavation and to reduce the areal extent of groundwater draw down outside of the
excavation Dewatering by means of a well point system along the outside of the
excavations is generally not recommended
Since the excavation dewatering will involve discharge of groundwater from the
dewatering operation into the adjacent drainage system a National Pollutant Discharge
GEOLABS HAWAII

their independent consultant or the State of Hawaii Department of Health for the latest
regulations and information pertaining to the NPDES permit application
Dewatering for construction is the sole responsibility of the contractor The
dewatering operations should be coordinated with the shoring support so that the
stability of the excavations is not jeopardized and the operations should be carried out
without softening the bottom of the excavations The selection of equipment and

Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all
surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed anywhere on
the site during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate
landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs

reduce migration of landscape water into the pavement section Alternatively a
subdrain system could be constructed to collect excessive water from landscaping
irrigation For long term performance we recommend a subdrain system be
constructed adjacent to paved landscape areas
Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines would be required for the proposed project It
should be noted that groundwater was encountered within 5 feet of the existing ground
surface Therefore any trench excavation deeper than 5 feet will likely encounter
groundwater In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular
bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
be used under the pipes The backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes or 1 foot
above the groundwater level should consist of free draining backfills such as No 3B
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3541 00 Page 22
Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from compaction of the
backfill
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes or
1 foot above the groundwater level to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should
consist of select granular material or the on site granular soils The backfill should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in maximum
8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are
below pavement areas the upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement
subgrade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Also it is advised that gutter
systems be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the founda
tions and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be
avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the
subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent loss of strength of the near surface soils The foundation
excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting
of the concrete to reduce water infiltration
