GEOTECHNICAL ENGINEERING EXPLORATION 
ROAD RECONSTRUCTION AT BISHOP DRIVE AND KE ALA MANO ROAD 
KAMEHAMEHA SCHOOLS – KAPALAMA CAMPUS 
HONOLULU, OAHU, HAWAII 
Dear Mr. Ring: 
This geotechnical engineering report presents the results of our geotechnical 
engineering exploration performed for the Reconstruction at Bishop Drive and Ke Ala 
Mano Road project at the Kamehameha Schools – Kapalama Campus in Honolulu on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our 
geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the design of pavements. 
The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at 
the end of this letter report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
 The project involves the pavement reconstruction at Bishop Drive and Ke Ala 
Mano Road intersection at the Kamehameha Schools – Kapalama Campus. Ke Ala Mano 
Drive and Bishop Drive are generally the main roadways on the school campus; therefore, 
these roadways experience or are subjected to a high volume of vehicular traffic and 
heavy buses.  The pavement at this location is deteriorated and requires reconstruction.  
Surface water runoff from the higher elevation athletic field located to the east of the 
intersection generally flows down the hill and onto the intersection. We understand that 
some drainage improvements to address the surface water runoff issue are also planned 
for the proposed project.   
REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late

Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island.  The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  The Koolau Volcano reached the 
end of its main shield-building phase about 2 million years ago. 
The project area is generally composed of basaltic rock built by extrusion of the 
lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series. These rocks are generally characterized by flows of 
jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers. In-situ chemical 
weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years. The 
weathering process has formed a mantle of residual and saprolitic soils. In general, 
saprolite is mainly composed of silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of 
volcanic rocks. The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with increased depth. Fills 
placed during the initial development of the campus may underlie the ground surface at 
portions of the project site. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling three borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, at selected locations within the project limits. The 
borings generally extended to depths of about 5.5 to 6.5 feet below the existing pavement 
surface. The approximate boring locations drilled for this project are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In addition, Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) testing was conducted in the 
borings to estimate the CBR value of the subgrade soils.  This instrument is typically used 
to assess material properties of the pavement subgrade soils. The 17.6-pound DCP can 
be used to estimate the strength characteristics of fine and coarse-grained soils, granular 
construction materials and weak stabilized or modified materials. The 17.6-lb DCP cannot 
be used in highly stabilized or cemented materials or for granular materials containing a 
large percentage of aggregates greater than 2 inches.   
In general, our borings encountered 3 to 4 inches of asphaltic concrete underlain by 
13 to 14 inches of medium dense to dense sandy gravel fill.  Below the fill, residual soils 
were encountered to depths of about 2 to 5.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  The 
residual soils were underlain by weathered basalt extending to the maximum depths 
explored of about 5.5 to 6.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  The relative hardness 
of the basalt was generally soft in Boring Nos. 1 and 2 and medium hard in Boring No. 3. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the shallow borings at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are expected to fluctuate 
depending on seasonal rainfall, time of year, surface water runoff, and other factors.  
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered from our field exploration are 
presented on the Logs of Borings in Appendix A. Results of the laboratory tests performed 
on selected soil samples are presented in Appendix B. The results of the DCP testing are 
shown in Appendix C.

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS AND VISUAL PAVEMENT SURVEY 
The project site is at the intersection of Bishop Drive and Ke Ala Mano Road within 
the Kamehameha Schools – Kapalama Campus in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii.  The location of the site is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Bishop Drive and Ke Ala Mano Road are two lane roadways consisting of asphaltic 
concrete pavements.  The center portion of the intersection is generally level. Ke Ala Mano 
Road, which heads toward the main entrance, slopes downward to the north. Bishop 
Drive, which heads toward the elementary school campus, slopes downward to the 
southwest.  Bishop Drive, which heads toward the middle school campus, slopes upward 
in the east direction. 
A pavement condition survey of the project area was performed on March 7, 2014. 
Based on our field observations, the general condition of the pavements is at medium 
severity distress level.  
The visual classification was performed in general accordance with the definitions 
described in the “Distress Identification Manual for the Long-Term Pavement Performance 
Program” dated June 2003 prepared by the Federal Highway Administration (FHWA).

PAVEMENT DESIGN 
The design procedures used in determining the new flexible pavement section is 
based on the “Guide for Design of Pavement Structures (1993)” developed by the 
American Association of State Highway and Transportation Officials (AASHTO). In 
general, we recommend removing the existing asphaltic concrete layer and base/subbase 
material and replacing it with new asphaltic concrete of about 2 inches in thickness 
underlain by 4 inches of asphalt concrete base over about 6 inches of base course.

at the school campus; therefore, these roadways are subjected to a high volume of

Design Subgrade Conditions 
Based on our field exploration, we anticipate that the new pavement subgrade will 
consist of a thin layer of granular fill over silty clay (CH) soils.  Laboratory CBR testing 
suggest that the subgrade soils consisting of silty clay with a mixture of some gravel and 
sand exhibit a CBR value of about 12 and a swell of about 1.8 percent remolded at near 
the in-situ moisture content in a laboratory compacted condition. 
Based on our DCP testing, the average in-situ CBR values range between 17 and 
42.  The broad range of the CBR values determined from the DCP testing is likely due to 
the presence of gravel material encountered in the subgrade soils 
Based on our field exploration, laboratory test results, and engineering analyses, 
we believe that a design CBR value of 12 may be adopted for the design of the new 
asphaltic concrete pavement for the Ke Ala Mano Road and Bishop Drive intersection. 
Pavement Structural Sections 
Due to the deterioration of the existing asphaltic concrete pavements, we 
recommend removing the existing asphaltic concrete pavement section and replacing with 
a new pavement section consisting of asphaltic concrete, asphalt concrete base, and 
aggregate base course material. Based on the above anticipated traffic and the design 
CBR value, we recommend utilizing the following flexible pavement section for design.

After removal of the existing AC layer and the existing soils to accommodate the 
new pavement structural section, the exposed subgrade soils should be proof-rolled with a 
minimum 10-ton (static weight) drum roller for a minimum of six passes to compact the 
exposed subgrade soils. The subgrade should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
If soft soils are exposed at the pavement subgrade or the soils are pumping during 
the proof-rolling operations, we recommend providing a stabilization layer consisting of 
8 inches of aggregate base course over a layer of triaxial geogrid (such as Tensar TX7 or 
equivalent) underlain by a woven geotextile fabric (such as Mirafi HP370 or equivalent) as 
shown on Plate 3.

A Geolabs representative should monitor the pavement subgrade preparation to 
observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and to 
confirm whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar to those encountered in our 
field exploration. 
The aggregate base course should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. 
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density. 
Asphalt concrete base (ACB) material should consist of asphalt-treated basalt 
aggregate, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and 
compacted to no less than 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity 
determined in accordance with ASTM D2041. Asphalt concrete (AC) material should be 
constructed in accordance with Section 401 Asphalt Concrete Pavement of the Hawaii 
Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction, and its amendments. 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to 
saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be maintained 
to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. Surface water 
ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development of good shoulder 
drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage structures is 
strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration or premature 
failure of the pavements. 
CONCRETE SWALE 
We understand that a concrete swale will be constructed along the east side of the 
Bishop Drive and Ke Ala Mano Road intersection.  The concrete swale will be about 4 
inches thick and about 3.5 to 5.5 feet wide.  We anticipate that the subgrade soils would 
consist of clayey soils.  To provide a more uniform bearing surface for the concrete swale, 
we recommend placing a minimum 6-inch thick layer of non-expansive select granular fill 
below the concrete swale.  The select granular fill should be compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction.  Prior to placing the select granular fill, the clayey 
subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at 
least 2 percent above the optimum moisture, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction.

SITE GRADING 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, asphaltic 
concrete, and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly 
off-site. Soft and/or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill should be over-excavated to expose firm materials, and the 
resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fills. The excavated soft 
and/or organic soils should be properly disposed off-site and/or used in landscape areas, 
where appropriate. Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices 
for over-excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the 
over-excavation quantities. 
After clearing and grubbing, finished subgrades and areas designated to receive 
fills should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 
2 percent above the optimum moisture, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction.  Finished subgrades in areas subjected to vehicular traffic should be 
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.   
Where shrinkage cracks develop in the subgrade soil after compaction, we 
recommend preparing the subgrade soil again as recommended above. Saturation and 
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage 
may require over-excavation and replacement of the softened areas with well-compacted 
fill. The need for over-excavation due to soft subgrade soil conditions should be evaluated 
in the field by our representative. 
The excavated on-site soils may be re-used as a source of general fill materials 
provided that the materials are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, clay lumps and 
rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension. Imported general fill 
materials should consist of soil materials with a maximum particle size of 3 inches or less 
with sufficient fines (between 10 and 60 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve) to 
prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass. In addition, general fill materials 
should have a CBR value of 12 or greater and a swell of 1 percent or less when tested in 
accordance with ASTM D1883. It should be noted that the general fill requirements 
presented herein are intended as guidelines only and may be modified based on additional 
laboratory testing and field observations on the available fill materials conducted by 
Geolabs. 
The non-expansive, select granular fill required directly below the swale should 
consist of crushed coral or basalt. The material should be well graded from coarse to fine 
with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension. The material also should 
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. In addition, the 
material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum 
swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883.

General fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the

granular fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture,

FILL SLOPES 
Fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to one 
vertical (2H:1V) or flatter.  The filling operations should start at the lowest point and 
continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill 
placement recommendations. 
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope 
ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. Water should be diverted away from the tops 
of slopes and slope planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the 
potential for erosion of the finished slopes.

LIMITATIONS 
The findings and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from our site reconnaissance, field exploration, laboratory testing, 
and past project experience in the vicinity. Variations of subsurface conditions from those 
described in this letter report may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may

elevations should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used.

boundaries between the soil and/or rock types and, as such, may denote a gradual

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling three borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, to depths of approximately 5.5 to 6.5 feet below 
the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with solid-stem auger and 
rotary coring equipment. 
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural 
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis.  These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing 
in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil 
Classification System, as shown on Plate A-0.1. Rock was described in general 
accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on Plate A-0.2. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings, Plates 
A-1 through A-3.  
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches.  In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop.  The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths.

Dynamic Cone Penetration Tests 
Dynamic Cone Penetration (DCP) testing was performed at the three boring 
locations (Boring Nos. 1 through 3) to obtain pavement support characteristics of the 
in-situ soils. The tests were performed using a Salem Dual Mass Dynamic Cone 
Penetration to a depth of about 1.8 to 5.0 feet below the existing pavement surface. 
The DCP device consists of two 16 mm-diameter stainless steel rods with the lower 
rod containing an anvil, a replaceable 60o pointed tip and depth markings. The upper rod 
holds a 17.6 lb drop hammer with a 22.6 inch drop distance. At the beginning of a test, the 
cone is “seated” by dropping the hammer until the widest part of the cone is just below the 
testing surface at which point the initial penetration is recorded as 0. The operator then lifts 
and drops the hammer one or more times depending on the strength of the soil at the test 
location.  The number of blows for a minimum of 1-inch penetration is recorded.  The 
process is continued until the desired depth of testing is reached or the full length of the 
lower rod is buried. 
Data from our DCP testing indicates the penetration index (PI) which is simply the 
penetration distance of the cone for each drop of the hammer expressed as millimeters 
per blow. The penetration indices obtained from our tests were correlated with common 
pavement design parameters such as California Bearing Ratio (CBR). Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the DCP Records, 
Plates C-1.1 through C-3.2.
