GEOTECHNICAL ENGINEERING SERVICES
DUE DILIGENCE STUDY
CHILD AND FAMILY SERVICES EXPANSION
Dear Mr Uemura
This letter report presents the findings from our site reconnaissance and engineering
analyses performed in support of the diligence study for the proposed Child and Family
Services Expansion project The proposed Child and Family Services Expansion project is
located along Fort Weaver Road in the District of Ewa on the Island of Oahu Hawaii
This letter report summarizes our findings and discussions based on our site
reconnaissance and review of available information The findings and discussions are
intended to advise the owner about potential risks involved and the geotechnical
considerations that may need to be addressed for the proposed project The findings and
discussions presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
Based on the information provided we understand that the Child and Family Services
CFS is proposing to expand its existing facility along Fort Weaver Road in the District of Ewa
on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The proposed 2 acre expansion site is roughly triangular in
shape bordering the west and south boundaries of the existing CFS site
Anew Community Center covering approximately 7 344 square feet in area is planned
that will provide assembly space for conducting workshops seminars education and training
that are related to the services and programs of CFS A full service kitchen will be included
to accommodate banquets for up to about 320 people In addition a new Counseling Facility
covering approximately 3 444 square feet in area with a residential design and an interior
home environment is planned for the expansion project As part of the development about
112 parking stalls with a second entrance and exit along Fort Weaver Road will be included
for the project

facility along Fort Weaver Road in the District of Ewa on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The
proposed 2 acre expansion site is roughly triangular in shape bordering the west and south
boundaries of the existing CFS site
Based on our field reconnaissance the project site presently consists of vacant land
with a sparse cover of dry vegetation and some deleterious materials and debris scattered

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
As mentioned previously field exploration consisting of drilling and sampling borings
was not conducted for this due diligence study Instead research and review of available soil
and boring data in the vicinity of the proposed project site was conducted to evaluate the
subsurface conditions at the proposed project site
Based on our field reconnaissance and the available information it appears that the
majority of the project site is covered by a deposit of light brown silty clay soils near the
existing ground surface Our field observations indicate that the near surface silty clay soils
generally exist in a very dry and loose state commonly encountered in the area In general
we envision that the silty clay soils will increase in stiffness and moistness with increasing
depths In addition our experience in the Ewa area suggest that the near surface silty clay
soils may exhibit moderate to high shrink swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations
in the soil moisture contents
Groundwater is not anticipated at shallow depths below the existing ground surface
In general the site is at an elevation several tens of feet above the highest basal water table
in the area However groundwater levels can vary significantly depending on seasonal
rainfall groundwater withdrawal and or injection and other factors

Slope Erosion and Slope Failure
Based on our field reconnaissance a significant number of erosion gullies was
observed along the edges and over the face of the existing slope at the southern boundary of
the project site Some of these erosion gullies measured over 10 feet wide and 5 feet deep
with the surrounding soils existing in a very dry and loose state We believe that the erosion
gullies present at the site is a result of surface water runoff over the sparely vegetated ground
surface underlain by the highly erosive silty clay soils present across the project site We
believe that these erosion gullies if left unattended may increase further in size depth and
number and could eventually progress to a state causing failure of the existing slope
Because the proposed expansion will encroach along the edge of the existing slope
we believe that the design and construction of the expansion project should address the
following items which are presented below
e Removal of Erosion Gullies
e Cut and Fill Slope Design
Erosion Protection and Long Term Slope Stability
Removal of Erosion Gullies
In general the area exposing the erosion gullies should be over excavated down to the
bottom of the existing erosion gullies Based on our field observations we believe that
an over excavation depth on the order of about 4 to 5 feet deep may be anticipated for
the gully removal work Due to the deteriorated condition of the existing slope face we
believe that substantial excavation of the existing slope may be required to remove the
eroded portion of the existing slope The actual extent of the removal work should be
evaluated by a representative from Geolabs in the field during the earthwork
operations
Prior to the placement of new fill materials the over excavated subgrade resulting from
the gully removal work should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and
recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density as determined by ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the
water content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Cut and Fill Slope Design
As mentioned previously erosion gullies were also observed over the existing slope
face at the project site Depending on the final grading concept the entire existing
slope may need to be removed and reconstructed to provide a stable embankment for
the construction of improvements on top of the embankment In general we believe
that the planned cut and fill slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of

fill slope inclination fill slopes may be designed with a slope inclination as steep as

New fills should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the

placement recommendations Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and

Excessive surface water runoff over the slope face may cause erosion of the exposed
soils thus potentially jeopardizing the long term stability and performance of the cut
and fill slopes Therefore the slope face should be protected by appropriate slope
planting or other means as soon as possible to reduce the potential for significant
erosion of the finished slopes In addition V ditches may be considered at the top and
bottom of the planned slopes to provide proper drainage of surface water runoff
Erosion Protection and Long Term Slope Stability
Implementation of an adequate erosion control program would be crucial to the
satisfactory performance of the planned cut slopes and fill embankments against
potential failures In general erosion of the existing slopes planned cut slopes or new
fill embankments are caused by surface water runoff General guidelines such as
establishment of vegetation and provision of interceptor drainage ditches and earth
berms have been discussed above and should be incorporated into the design and
construction of the proposed expansion project It should be noted that provision of
permanent ground cover vegetation will likely require that a permanent irrigation be
installed to irrigate the ground cover considering the dry climatic conditions of the area
Other erosion control methods or applications such as Grouted Rubble Paving GRP
and concrete lining may need to be considered as part of the erosion control program
of the existing slope along the southern boundary of the project In addition to these
conventional rigid lining systems the use of flexible geosynthetic matting as a viable
solution for slope erosion control has become increasingly more acceptable and may
be considered for this project
Deep Shrinkage Cracks
Our field observations indicated that abundant shrinkage cracks are present at the
existing ground surface across the project site In general shrinkage cracks will develop in
the ground due to the lack of maintenance of the soil moisture content as the surface soils
start to dry out under the sun and dry weather conditions The development of shrinkage
cracks in the ground was apparently intensified by the prolonged dry climatic conditions
characteristic of the Ewa area resulting in large fluctuations of the soil moisture content

If the existing ground cracks are left unattended for an extended period of time and are
allowed to penetrate deeper below the surface the ground cracks could adversely affect the
performance of the future building foundations The ground cracks could serve as conduits
for water to percolate into the foundation soils and cause loss of strength of the foundation
soils resulting in potential settlement of the building structure In addition migration of the
foundation soils into the openings of the shrinkage cracks could undermine the foundation
system and result in potential settlement of the building structure Therefore remedial work
to correct or mitigate the observed ground cracks should be implemented prior to fill placement
and construction of the proposed structures
After clearing and grubbing we recommend that the proposed building and pavement
footprints be over excavated about 2 to 3 feet below the existing ground surface to remove the _
zone with shrinkage cracks The over excavation should also extend laterally about 2 to 3 feet
beyond the building and driveway limits If walkways are planned around the building
structures the over excavation should extend laterally to the outside edge of the walkways
The over excavated subgrades and other on grade areas outside building and pavement
footprints should then be proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton vibratory drum roller about
6 to 8 passes to assist in detecting and collapsing near surface voids and loose zones created
by the shrinkage cracks The exposed subgrades should be proof rolled without
moisture conditioning of the subgrade soils at the pre existing moisture contents Loose
areas disclosed during the proof rolling operation should be backfilled with general fill material
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The proof rolling operations
should be conducted under the near continuous observation of a representative from Geolabs
Subsequent to the proof rolling operations the subgrades should then be scarified to
a minimum depth of 12 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test
procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding
to the maximum dry density
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend
that the subgrade soil be prepared again as recommended above Saturation and subsequent
yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted fill The need for
over excavation due to soft subgrade soil conditions should be evaluated by a representative
from Geolabs in the field Since the development of shrinkage cracks is largely due to
fluctuations in the moisture content of the on site expansive soils we believe that the efforts
should be directed at maintaining the soil moisture content at a relatively constant level These
long term solutions may include landscaping installation of concrete walkways around the
building structures and or placement of non expansive fills in the yard areas

Expansive Soil Conditions
Our experience in the Ewa area suggests that the near surface silty clay soils exhibit
moderate to high shrink swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture
contents As a result we recommend that the expansive clayey soils be capped with a
minimum of 2 feet of non expansive select granular capping fill material below all building slab
areas prior to constructing the lightly loaded building structures and improvements The
expansive clayey soils below the pavement areas should also be capped with a minimum of
2 feet of non expansive select granular fill material In addition we recommend that the
footing embedment around the perimeter of the building structures be increased to a minimum
of 3 feet below the lowest adjacent finished grades to reduce the potential for significant water
infiltration into the underlying foundation soils Deep foundation support system drilled shafts
may be required for the new structure encroaching into the edge of the existing slope within
15 feet of the top of the slope Special attention should be given to the recommended
procedures for subgrade soil preparation below slabs and driveways in order to reduce the
potential for shrink swell effects of the near surface clayey soils
Additional Exploration
Additional field exploration should be conducted for the final design of the site grading
and structure foundations As mentioned previously the critical geotechnical considerations
include the following 1 presence of shrinkage cracks and erosion gullies 2 erosion control
and long term stability of the existing slope along the southern boundary and 3 design of the
building foundations with respect to the expansive soil conditions
