Project Considerations
The Parker Wells Nos 3 and 4 project site is in the Waikoloa area in the District of
South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii The project consisted of construction of two
water tanks designated as 610 and 1103 reservoirs In addition installation of
waterlines and appurtenances access roads and other related water system
improvements were made for the project
lsemoto Contractor Company Ltd was the contractor for the earthwork operations
at the project site Based on our records the earthwork operations for the Parker Wells
Nos 3 and 4 project were conducted between September 2006 and September 2007
Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii is the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers an area
of approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of the
following five shield volcanoes Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea activity during recent
geologic time Hualalai erupted in 1801 1803 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both still
active
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of Mauna Kea which has
formed the west central portion of the island The project site appears to be underlain by
basaltic lava flows of the Hamakua Volcanic Series of Mauna Kea which were deposited
during the Pleistocene Epoch Stearns and Macdonald 1946

The Hamakua Volcanic Series is typically composed of basaltic lava flows which
are mantled by a relatively thin layer of palagonitized ash locally referred to as Pahala
Ash The ash soil at the project site differs from Pahala Ash found in other portions of the
Island of Hawaii in that the in situ moisture content is generally lower and the soil exists in
a dry friable state The soils at the site have been subjected to wind erosion which has
resulted in the formation of a desert pavement on the surface of the site This desert
pavement is a surficial layer of cobbles and gravel which were left behind as the finer ash
was blown away
The lava formation encountered at the site appears to be of both a a and pahoehoe
type flows which spread and ponded as they approached the ocean A a lava is typically
characterized by a porous rough and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged
accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders Pahoehoe is
characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of
vesicular porous rock
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas especially pahoehoe type
lava Cavities form when the lava is still in a molten state and they represent both lava
tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes form
when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which
may extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow Blisters and pockets
inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted
through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities
and along clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore
they serve as the primary water transmission features
Subsurface Conditions
As previously mentioned the project site is in the Waikoloa area in the District of
South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii In general the original topography of the site prior
to site grading activities consisted of irregular or hummocky lava flow terrain with
frequent topographic depressions separated by adjacent higher relief mounds and low
ridge features
Based on our observations the project site is generally underlain by cobbles and
boulders with varying amounts of volcanic ash materials at the ground surface The
surface materials generally were underlain by interbedded layers of dense pahoehoe
basalt formation and clinker materials It should be noted that lava tubes cavities and
blisters are commonly encountered in basaltic lava flows Near surface lava tubes
cavities and blisters detected within the limits of grading generally were collapsed and
the loose materials from within the collapsed lava tube were removed The resulting

Discussion and Recommendations
Site grading at the 610 and 1 103 reservoir sites generally consisted of excavation
of about 3 to 15 feet in height to achieve the design grades The majority of the other
areas of the project site were graded with less than about 3 feet of cuts and fills
During the grading operations extensive subgrade preparation procedures were
employed within the limits of grading at the commencement of the grading operations for
the project to reduce the potential for collapse of near surface cavities and or voids within
the grading limits The intent of the subgrade preparation procedures is to assist in
detecting and collapsing near surface cavities and or voids contained in the porous
near surface materials It should be noted that the subgrade preparation procedures
employed for this project are consistent with the standard of practice for similar type
projects and similar subsurface conditions on the Island of Hawaii
In general the subgrade preparation in fill areas consisted of ripping the original
ground to a depth of about 3 feet or until refusal using a ripper tooth of a large bulldozer to
assist in detecting and collapsing near surface cavities and or voids contained in the
porous near surface materials After the fill surfaces have been ripped the ground
generally was leveled with the use of a Komatsu 275 bulldozer working the surface for
about four passes to provide a relatively level surface After leveling of the rugged surfaces
with the bulldozer the fill subgrades generally were proof rolled with a sheepsfoot roller
compactor minimum 20 tons for about eight passes to help detect and collapse
near surface cavities and or voids
In addition a program of probing and grouting was conducted at subgrades within
the reservoir footprints The contractor Isemoto Contracting Co Ltd surveyed and staked
the 610 and 1103 water tank locations and probe hole locations The probe holes were
marked along the perimeter footings spaced at a spacing of about 10 feet or less on
center In addition probe holes were drilled within the tank floor area at about 10 foot on
center starting at the center of the tank and radiating out at about 10 foot radial
increments The probe holes were drilled with an air track drill rig The probe holes were
about 3 inches in diameter and extended to depths of about 10 to 12 feet below the
ground surface The depths of the probe holes corresponded to about 10 feet below the
bottom of the slabs on grade elevation After completion of the probe drilling operations
the probe holes were filled by injecting grout into the probe holes at low to moderate
pumping pressures at the pumping pressures from a grout pump
In general fill materials consisting of structural fills were placed in the fill areas of
the project site Structural fills consisted of well graded granular materials generally
smaller than 6 inches in dimension Subgrades in cut areas generally were proof rolled

Base course materials were used as fill and backfill materials under reservoirs
within utility trench excavations and other areas Samples of the on site crushed
materials utilized as fill and backfill materials were obtained and transported to our office
for testing where appropriate The maximum dry densities and optimum moisture
contents were established in our laboratory in general accordance with ASTM D 1557
Modified Proctor and the test results are summarized on Plate 1
The fill and backfill materials generally were moisture conditioned to above
optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent of the maximum
dry density as determined in accordance with ASTM D 1557
During the earthwork operations periodic field density tests were performed in
general accordance with ASTM D 2922 and D 3017 Nuclear Gauge Method where
appropriate Results of the compaction tests performed are summarized on Plates 2 1
through 2 9 of this report These tests were performed on the prepared ground surfaces
and on constructed fills and backfills at the site Test locations and elevations were
estimated by pacing and interpolating from on site survey stakes These locations and
elevations should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods
used Areas assessed to be unsatisfactory were generally reworked until the specified
minimum compaction was obtained and or accepted by our field representative based on
visual observation and hand probing
Based on the observations of our field representatives it is our professional
opinion that the earthwork operations for the above mentioned project observed by
Geolabs in the period covered in this report were performed in general conformance with
the intent of the project plans and specifications

Limitations
It should be noted that it is not possible to continuously observe all activities
occurring simultaneously on a construction site Therefore some of the earthwork may
have been performed without our observation However the level of earthwork
observation and testing services provided appears to be reasonable in reducing some of
the geotechnical risks to the project It should be recognized that there are variations in the
accuracy and statistical repeatability of the tests and methods used to monitor the
earthwork construction and materials Our services were performed in accordance with
generally accepted standards of geotechnical engineering practice no warranty is
