INTRODUCTION
This report summarizes the findings of the subsurface investigation
at the subject site and presents recommendations for the design and
construction of the foundations for the proposed structures
PROJECT E ONSIDERATIONS
It is presently planned to construct a six story Agricultural Science
Facility with a connecting two story office structure A one story
equipment building is to be located immediately west of the main
structure
W Page 1974
The site grading to obtain the proposed finish floor level of elevation
89 feet will entail maximum cuts of approximately 7 feet at the north
western corner of the Mechanical Equipment Building and maximum fills
approaching 2 feet at the southeastern corner of the main structure
It is our understanding that this structures will be of reinforced
concrete with combined dead and live loads approaching 1 000 kips per
cotumn
SITE CONDITIONS
Surface
The project area is located on the mauka side of the University of
Hawaii s Manoa Campus on Oahu The proposed building site is west
of the existing Agricultural Engineering Facilities on Maile Way near
its intersection with East West Center Road
The site is presently occupied by several wood frame structures A
drainage ditch is located along the northern side of the structures
and apparently several water and sewer utility lines traverse the
Site The ground surface has a general 3 downward slope from west
to east
Geology
Manoa Valley is situated between the Waahila Ridge to the east Round
Top and Tantalus Ridge to the west and the Koolau Mountains to the
north It is a typical volcanic eroded valley resulting from the
weathering of the basalt
W O 547 00 Page 1974
Manoa Stream is presently located only a few blocks east of the project
area and has probably changed its course many times since the Pleisto
cene Period and the formation of the Koolau Volcanic Shield The
Honolulu Volcanic Series eruptions could also have effected the stream
course
The site is underlain by decomposed volcanics and reworked alluvium
The alluvium is generally compacted and quite weathered At depths
it is difficult to distinguish from the original decomposed basaltic
rock from the Honolulu Volcanic Series Local irregularities can be
expected in the elevation of the on site lava flows which apparently
resulted from ponding of lava while still in a liquid stage Hard
dense basalts can be expected at depths below 30 feet Isolated
pockets of red cinders resulting from the eruption of the nearby
Sugar Loaf Crater can be expected
Subsurface Conditions
Subsurface conditions at the site were explored utilizing eight test
borings drilled at the locations shown on the Site Plan and Boring
Locations Plate 1 A detailed description of the field exploration
methods and the accompanying laboratory testing is given in the appen
dix along with the boring logs and the laboratory test results
The subsurface explorations disclosed that the site is blanketed by
4 to 30 feet of soil underlain by hard basalt or lava The uppermost
soil consist of 2 to 9 feet of clayey silt or silty clay adobe
which varied from stiff to extremely stiff in consistency
W O 547 00 Page 1974
Below the upper adobe soil the subsurface conditions became more vari
able 3 andi4 encountered decomposed rock or weathered
lava boulders The decomposed rock extended until a hard basalt or
lava was encountered or to the extent of the borings In Boring 8
the adobe was separated from the decomposed rock by approximately
three feet of silty sandy gravel alluviun
In Borings 5 and 6 a loose gravelly cinder layer was encountered
immediately below the adobe In Boring 6 the cinders extended to
a depth of 15 feet where hard decomposed basalt rock was encountered
In Boring 7 the adobe layer was thin with a thickness of only two
feet It is underlain by approximately 4 feet of decomposed rock
which in turn was underlain by a thick layer of silty gravel with
some sand alluvium extending to a depth of approximately 20 feet
This appears to be stream deposited materials with densities varying
from medium dense to dense It is underlain by a thin lava flow or
boulder
Ground water was not encountered throughout the subsurface explorations
DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
At the proposed finish floor level of élevation 89 foundations at a
nominal depth below the floor would bear on both decomposed rock and
compressible adobe soils The differences in bearing capacities of
these two materials and the variable nature of the subsurface condi
tions would produce large differential settlements It is therefore
recommended that all foundations be extended through the adobe to bear
upon the underlying decomposed rock or cinders
W O 547 00 Page 1974
The cinders are located within a limited area near Borings 5 and 6 and
should provide adequate support since the cinders are lightly cemented
Except near Boring 8 the foundations will generally require excavations
of 2 to 5 feet to penetrate the adobe soil At Boring 8 the adobe
extends to approximately elevation 77 and will require an approximately
12 foot excavation Due to the irregular nature of the surface of the
decomposed rock it is recommended that the contract documents provide
unit quantities or an allowance to permit the foundation bottom ele
vations to be adjusted during construction to conform with the sub
surface conditions
Foundations penetrating the adobe and bearing upon the underlying
materials can be designed for maximum net contact bearing pressures
of This pressure may be increased by 1 3 when considering
short term wind and seismic loadings Based upon a bearing
pressure the estimated settlements are 0 5 inch total and 0 3 inch
differential
Floor Slab
Slab on grade construction can be utilized if the expansive subgrade
soils are undercut a minimum of 18 inches and backfilled with com
pacted non expansive materials such as S4C coral mudrock or cinders
This material should be compacted to a minimum of 90 of the maximum
dry density established by American Society for Testing and Materials
ASTM Test D 1557 72T The floor slabs should also be divorced from
the load bearing members to prevent distresses due to small differen
tial movements
W O 547 00 Page 1974
Slabs on grade to be provided with floor coverings should be protected
by a plastic vapor barrier It is anticipated that the granular non
expansive backfill will prevent the build up of capillary moisture be
neath the floor slabs
The foundation backfill will provide support for the floor slabs and
should also be compacted to 90 of the maximum density
The fill placement should be monitored on a full time basis with perio
dic in place density determinations to assure that the recommended de
gree of compaction is consistently achieved The borrow materials to
be utilized for the backfill also should be tested and approved prior
to placement
The on site excavated adobe material can be utilized for fill except
within 18 inches of the floor slab sugrade The material is highly
plastic and may be difficult to compact in confined areas The con
tractor should be given the option of utilizing the on site soils or
an approved off site borrow which could be more easily compacted
Construction Monitoring
The foundation excavations should be inspected and approved prior to
the placement of the concrete reinforcing to assure that the founda
tions are bearing upon competent materials and no expansive soils re
mains The undercutting and backfilling operations should also be
W O 547 00 Page 1974
monitored to assure compliance with the specifications The recom
mendations given in this report are contingent upon such inspections

please do not hesitate to contact us

Field Explorations
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling eight
borings utilizing truck mounted equipment The depths of the borings
varied from 16 5 feet to 40 0 feet and entailed both soil sampling
and rock coring The locations of the boring are shown on the
Site Plan and Boring Locations Plate 1 included in the text of this
report
The soils were classified by visual and textural examinations in the
field by our geologist who continuously supervised the drilling opera
tions The classifications were verified by visual inspection and
testing in the laboratory All soils were classified in accordance
with the Unified Soil Classification System A graphic representation
of the soils and rocks encountered is presented on the boring logs
Plates A 1 thru A 8
Soil samples were obtained by driving either a 2 4 inch I D or a stan
dard penetration sampler with a 140 pound hammer free falling a dis
tance of 30 inches The blow counts to drive the sampler the last 12
inches are shown on the boring logs at the appropriate sample depth

selected samples to aid in the classification of the soils The re
sults of these tests are presented on the boring logs at the appropriate
sample location
