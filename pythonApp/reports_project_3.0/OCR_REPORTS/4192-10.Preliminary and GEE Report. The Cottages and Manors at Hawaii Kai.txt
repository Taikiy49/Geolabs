Subsurface Conditions
Our current field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling seven
additional borings designated as Boring Nos 4 through 10 extending to depths of about
16 5 to 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface
In general our field explorations disclosed that the northern boundary of the project
site is generally underlain by weathered basalt rock formation at shallow depths Towards
the lower and southern part of the project site our field exploration generally encountered
5 to 6 feet of fill materials ranging from stiff clays to medium dense sands with a mixture of
cobbles and boulders The near surface fill materials were generally underlain by in situ soils
consisting of dense to very dense cemented silty sands and volcanic tuff deposits extending
to the maximum depth explored of approximately 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface
Due to the nature of the undocumented fills encountered at the site it should be noted that
localized zones of loose and or soft materials may exist across the soil horizon that are not
readily identified in the drilled borings during our field exploration
Groundwater was encountered in some of the drilled borings at depths ranging from
about 8 6 to 9 6 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of the field exploration It
should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall time of year
seasonal precipitation run off storm surge conditions and other factors
Foundations
Based on the field exploration conducted for the proposed development we anticipate
that the majority of the project site is generally underlain by surficial fill materials overlying
cemented silty sands and volcanic tuff deposits at greater depths In general we believe that
shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings bearing on the
recompacted in situ soils or new engineered fills may be used for support of the proposed
buildings planned across the project site An allowable bearing pressure on the order of
about 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of foundations bearing
on the recompacted in situ soils or new compacted fills This bearing value is for
those caused by wind or seismic forces
As mentioned previously due to the nature of the undocumented fills encountered
at the site we believe that there exists the potential for localized zones of soft and or loose
materials in the upper fill layer Therefore we recommend that the footing foundations be
structurally connected with tie beams to improve the rigidity of the foundation thus reducing
the effects of potential differential settlements
The bottom of the footing over excavations should be scarified to a minimum depth
of 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and recompacted
to not less than 95 percent relative compaction If soft and or loose soils are encountered
at the over excavated subgrade levels deeper over excavation may be required to expose
the underlying firm material The over excavation should be backfilled with non expansive
select granular fill compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction or the bottom
of footing may be extended down to the underlying competent material

Slabs On Grade
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by near surface
fill soils with low to moderate expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations
To reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from
shrinking and swelling of the expansive soils due to changes in the moisture content we
recommend that the subgrade soils be properly prepared and capped with at least 12 inches
of non expansive select granular fill capping fill material below the slab cushion The
capping fill should also extend beyond the new slab on grade footprint a minimum of
24 inches
Based on our field exploration and the design finished floor elevations for the project
we anticipate that the proposed buildings may straddle across the in situ soils new
engineered fills and weathered basalt rock formation due to the sloping ground condition at
the project site Therefore the 12 inch capping fill recommended above will also serve to
provide more uniform support for the building slabs on grade that may otherwise bear directly
on differing subgrade materials
The non expansive select granular fill should be placed as soon as possible after the
subgrade soils are properly prepared The prepared capping fill and the underlying subgrade
soils should be wetted and kept moist prior to the final placement of concrete Where

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
General fill materials should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The non expansive
select granular fill materials should be placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to
at least 95 percent relative compaction The aggregate subbase and base course
materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density Compaction
should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers multiple wheel
pneumatic tired rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment Water
tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed for compaction of the fills

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the
western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern
two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active and its eastern
flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in central Oahu
The project site is located at the southeastern end of Koolau and its geomorphology
and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of sea level
during the Pleistocene Epoch The base of the stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau
Basalt
The Koolau volcanic shield was built during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene
Epoch by thin bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities ceased
approximately 2 5 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicates that the island of
Oahu has subsided as much as 1 200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic activity
Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to fill
the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels rose
because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained essentially stable
during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower relative to the
present sea level of Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of terrigenous sediments in the
heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at corresponding higher elevations and
lagoon marine sediments in the quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea
stands caused streams to carve valleys in the volcanic dome of Koolau

W O 4192 10 Page 6
During the late Pleistocene Epoch volcanic activity was renewed on Koolau with
eruptions of the Honolulu Volcanic Series In the vicinity of the site eruptions along the Koko
Fissure deposited pyroclastic materials consisting of tuff and cinder in portions of the valleys
cut into the southeastern end of Koolau
The site is situated at the mouth of Hahaione Valley and was formerly part of Kuapa
Pond a shallow estuary formed at the confluence of Hahaione Kamilonui and Kamiloiki
Valleys Land reclamation efforts filled portions of this estuary to create new land for
development

Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry the surface
water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after
construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the
pavement areas we recommend that the curbs be extended a minimum of 2 inches into the
subgrade soil below the base and subbase course layers to reduce the potential for migration
of landscape water into the pavement section Alternatively a subdrain system could be
constructed to collect excessive water from landscaping irrigation For long term
GEOLABS INC
W O 4192 10 Page 23
performance we recommend that a subdrain system be constructed adjacent to the
paved landscaped areas
Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines will be required to serve the proposed development
In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding consisting
of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under the pipes
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
should also be used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide
adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus
reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material
to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide
adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in
settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of select fill material The backfill should
be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in maximum 8 inch level
loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to
reduce the potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement
areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems
be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from the foundation
and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should
also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms
and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils Drainage swales
should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain surface run off away from
the slabs and foundations

foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent loss of strength and or heaving of the near surface clayey soils The foundation
excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration

examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered in the borings are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 7

Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 2 0 inch OD standard penetration sampler or a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with
a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected soil layers in the field The
