REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central
Oahu
After a long period of cessation of the shield building volcanic activity returned
with the formation of a series of lava flows cinder cones and tuff cones which are known
as the Honolulu Volcanic Series Many of the eruptions were rather explosive in nature
due to the interaction of groundwater with the rising magma resulting in steam explosions
which expelled large quantities of pyroclastic material predominantly ash and cinder
Some explosive eruptions produced ash that cemented together to form a firm rock
known as tuff locally known as mudrock
The project site is located on the southwestern flank of the Koko Crater of the
Honolulu Volcanic Series The subsoil conditions at the project site are generally formed
by the deposition of recent pyroclastic materials and lava flows from eruptions of the
vents of members of the Honolulu Volcanic Series

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the proposed project site is underlain by medium
dense to dense sandy and gravelly soils over hard tuff mudrock extending to the
maximum depths drilled of about 7 5 and 16 feet below the existing ground surface
Based on our field exploration and laboratory testing we believe that a shallow
foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings bearing on the
compacted near surface soils may be used to support the proposed new residence We
anticipate that site preparation will involve minor cuts and fills on the order of less than
2 feet to provide level areas for the proposed new structure Detailed discussion and
recommendations for design of foundation site preparation and other geotechnical
aspects of the project are presented in the following sections of this report
Foundations
Based on the subsoil conditions encountered at the site we recommend that a
shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings bearing on the
compacted near surface soils be used for support of the proposed new residence An
allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be used for
the design of foundations bearing on the compacted subgrade This bearing value is for
dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third for transient loads such as those
caused by wind or seismic forces
At the request of the project structural engineer we also evaluated the foundation
supporting strength of the subgrade soils below the portion of the existing structure with
the Polynesian roof Based on the local geologic information and subsurface soil
conditions encountered at the site we estimate that the subgrade soil at this location may
provide an allowable bearing pressure of about 2 000 pounds per square foot p s f for
support of the existing structure

Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed new
structure In general the subgrades for slabs on grades should be scarified to a depth of
at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction If soft and or loose materials
are encountered at the slab subgrade level they should be over excavated to expose the
underlying firm materials The over excavation should be backfilled with select granular
material compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
For more uniform support of interior slabs we recommend that the concrete slab
on grade be supported on a minimum 4 inch cushion fill The cushion fill layer should
consist of open graded gravels such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C33
No 67 gradation or basalt termite barrier BTB which will also serve as a capillary
moisture break The slab cushion fill should be placed as soon as possible after the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3618 00 Page 9
scarification and recompaction of the existing subgrade soils as recommended above
For slabs on grade with moisture sensitive surfacing we recommend that an
impermeable membrane be placed over the cushion fill to reduce potential migration of
moisture vapor through the concrete slab In order to promote more uniform curing of the
slab concrete and to provide protection of the vapor membrane we recomimend that a
2 inch layer of moist fine sand or BTB be placed above the membrane prior to placing the
slab concrete The sand should be moistened slightly prior to placing concrete
Where concrete slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as the garage
slab we recommend that the slab be supported on a 6 inch layer of base course
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction in lieu of the 4 inch cushion
fill layer and 2 inch sand The base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate
The subgrade should also be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture
conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and compacted to
not less than 95 percent relative compaction For design of structural slabs a modulus of
subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per cubic inch p c i may be used for the compacted
base course The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to any unpaved area should be
embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade

Site preparation should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii It is

following recommendations are intended to provide guidelines for the design of site
preparation work

Following demolition of the existing house the area within the contract grading
limits should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and
other unsuitable materials generated from demolition should be removed and disposed of
off site After clearing and grubbing the exposed subgrade should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 8 inches properly moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction For subgrades
of improvements subjected to vehicular traffic such as garage slab and pavements the
compaction requirement should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage
by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Any soft weak or yielding areas disclosed during clearing operations should be
over excavated to expose firm ground and the resulting excavation should be backfilled
with engineered fill compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Prior to
placement of the backfill the over excavated subgrade should be scarified to a depth of
at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction The excavated soft and or organic
soils should be properly disposed of off site
The on site granular soils generated from the excavation may be reused as a
source of fill and backfill material Where used as fill and backfill material the on site soils
should be properly moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed
in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction Permanent fill slopes may be designed with a slope
inclination of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter Fills placed on slopes
steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched

Utility Trench
We envision that new utility connections may be needed for the proposed project
In general a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials
such as No 3B Fine gravel should also be used for the trench backfill up to about
12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce
the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of on site material or select
granular material The backfill should be placed in about 8 inch foose lifts and

potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the
upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to
not less than 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the residence should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations
and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent loss of strength of the near surface soils The foundation
excavations should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately
after setting of the concrete to reduce waiter infiltration Drainage swales should be
provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away
from the slab and footing foundations

borings to depths of approximately 7 5 and 16 feet below the existing ground surface at

appropriate sample depths
