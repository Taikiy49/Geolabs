INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the existing bulkhead at the proposed drain outlet at Kewalo Basin for the
proposed Ward Avenue Extension project The project site is located south of the Ward
Avenue and Ala Moana Boulevard intersection in the District of Honolulu on the Island of
Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration previous explorations laboratory
testing and engineering analyses These recommendations are intended to assist in the
design of the new sheetpile bulkhead only The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central
Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of Koolau and its geomorphology
and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea
level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain
The subsurface conditions in the Ala Moana area are dominated by fossil
Pleistocene Age coral algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of
the sea which are intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the
stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt
During the late Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and
lower relative to the present sea level of Oahu About 15 000 years ago a relatively
rapid rise in sea level occurred During that rise the valleys in the project area were
drowned In the last 10 000 years or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand
Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy
estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in thick deposits of soft loose sediments
along the coast in the areas that were formerly valleys and drainageways
GEOLABS INC
W O 3847 20 Page 6
In the early part of this century the Ala Moana area consisted of low marshy
wetlands As the city of Honolulu grew and the Ala Moana area was urbanized
man made fills were placed to reclaim the marshy areas and lagoons The fill was placed
upon lagoonal deposits consisting of silty sands with coral fragments The lower clayey
material was deposited during periodic influxes of non marine alluvial debris that
apparently smothered the coral reef development Land development and reclamation
projects within the last 100 years have brought the Ala Moana area to its present form
Many of the resulting fills are of poor quality in terms of supporting heavy structural loads
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The proposed drain outlet and new bulkhead sheetpile at Kewalo Basin are
located south of the Ward Avenue and Ala Moana Boulevard intersection in the District
of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project site is located on the western
side of Kewalo Basin adjacent to a boat slip and a docking pier
The project site is bounded by concrete masonry unit CMU buildings to the
north and west The southern building was demolished recently to make room for the
proposed drain outlet and asphalt paved driveway The project site also contains a
perimeter chain link fence which generally resides over the alignment of the existing
bulkhead
The deck south of the mauka bulkhead and within the boat slip consists of a
concrete topped wharf deck supported by wooden beams and steel piers The deck
east of the eastern bulkhead and mauka of the boat slip consists of a concrete topped
wharf deck supported by concrete beams and piers
The existing ground surface generally consists of a thin asphalt pavement over
the existing fill material or a concrete deck The existing ground surface is generally at
an elevation of about 7 25 feet MSL

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the results of our exploration we believe that a cantilever sheetpile wall
should be installed behind the existing bulkhead to a minimum depth of 40 feet below the
existing ground surface The new sheetpiles should extend along the existing bulkhead
where the lateral distance between the sheetpiles and the proposed driveway is 20 feet or
less
To reduce the potential for differential settlement within the pavement area the
backfill beneath the proposed driveway alignment and within a 10 foot lateral distance
behind the bulkhead should consist of CLSM controlled low strength concrete material or
flowable fill The CLSM should be placed within Elevation 2 feet MSL and the bottom
of the recommended pavement section
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering design
recommendations are presented in the following sections of this report

the sheetpile bulkheads expressed as equivalent fluid earth pressures are listed
in the following table Note that passive resistance from the wedge of soil created
by the sloping portion of the dredged excavation should be discounted

Due to the relatively porous nature of the on site soils relatively small unbalanced

likely result in an unbalanced hydraulic pressure
Overall Slope Stability
We have evaluated the overall slope stability of the sheetpile bulkhead using
Bishop s Simplified Method of limit equilibrium analysis Based on our analyses
the factors of safety exceed 1 5 which is the minimum factor of safety normally
recommended
Corrosion Protection
Based on pH and resistivity values obtained from nearby locations for the Ward
Avenue Extension project the soil is highly corrosive to steel and metals in contact
with the soils The seawater may also contribute to the highly corrosive
environment We recommend that proper protection against potential corrosion be
included in the design A corrosion engineer should be consulted for detailed
recommendations for corrosion protection

Installation Considerations
We believe that installation of the sheetpiles should be possible without the aid of
predrilling However we recommend that sufficient contingency fund be kept to
accommodate extra costs due to difficult penetration or the need to predrill through
hard coral layers
Dewatering
Dewatering of the seawater or groundwater will likely be necessary for the
installation of the sheetpile and construction of its concrete cap Therefore we
recommend that dewatering provisions such as a National Pollutant Discharge
Elimination System NPDES permit be included in the contract documents for
construction The contractor should consult his her independent consultant of the State
of Hawaii Department of Health for the latest regulations and information pertaining to
the permit application
Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the contractor The selection of
equipment and methods of dewatering should be determined by the contractor who
should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during
construction depending on conditions encountered The dewatering method selected
should have minimal impact on the water level surrounding the proposed excavation In
addition dewatering operations should be performed such that only clear water is
pumped Excessive pumping which removes soil fines may result in blowing or
heaving of the excavation bottom or sides
It should be noted that the subsoils in the zone where dewatering will take place
consist of sandy materials which may be considered to be relatively permeable It is our
opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents should be written to
include all works or systems required to lower the natural water table and or to exclude
water from the excavations to allow for construction of the proposed structures under safe
and dry conditions

Dewatering Considerations
We suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in the selection of
a suitable method of dewatering
a The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or
damage to the existing structures
b The dewatering method should maintain stability of and also provide
safe and dry working conditions in the excavation
C The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow for
modifications to accommodate various ground conditions
Dewatering Precaution and Monitoring
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural
water level must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to dewatering It is
recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be retained by the contractor
to design and evaluate the dewatering system used
The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His her qualified representative who should be
required to be continuously present on site during dewatering activities will have
the best opportunity to promptly observe the effects of dewatering during
construction and to implement as soon as possible necessary precautionary or
remedial measures including but not limited to stopping or slowing down the
dewatering operations
Where encountered at the bottom of excavations permeable granular subsoils
may be susceptible to piping and quick conditions The dewatering operations
should be carried out without creating a quick condition or softening at the
excavation bottoms Therefore the project dewatering operations should be
performed without pumping out soil fines pumping clear water only and should be
coordinated with shoring installation such that the excavation stability is not
adversely affected
GEOLABS INC
W O 3847 20 Page 13
Water drawdown outside of the excavation will cause additional settlement
resulting from consolidation of the loose soft compressible soils Therefore the
use of a deep well system outside the excavations to draw down the groundwater
level should not be allowed Special caution should also be taken to avoid
dewatering utility trenches connected to excavations If this occurs the granular
bedding and or backfill in the utility trenches could act as subdrains and cause
significant areal groundwater drawdown resulting in settlements and potential
damage to utility lines and or other existing adjacent structures
Excavation and Fill Placement
Excavation and fill placement operations should be observed by a representative
of Geolabs Inc It is important that a representative from our office observe whether
the exposed soil conditions encountered during excavation are similar to those
encountered in our exploration The following recommendations are intended to
provide guidelines for the design of excavation and fill placement work
Excavation and shoring work should conform to the requirements of local state
and federal regulations Surcharge loads such as stored material and equipment and
operating equipment and traffic should not be allowed within a 1H 1V plane projected
upward from the bottom of the trench unless accounted for properly in the shoring
design Excavation and backfill work should be performed in accordance with applicable
sections of the Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road Bridge and Public Works
Construction 1994 The excavation support and shoring system used is the
responsibility of the contractor and must comply with all applicable safety requirements
The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor Therefore it is recommended that a qualified
geotechnical engineer be retained by the contractor to design and evaluate the shoring
system used His her representative who should be required to be continuously present
on site during excavation and construction work will have the best opportunity to promptly
observe changing conditions during construction such as unforeseen subsoils

unexpectedly high groundwater table inappropriate construction sequence or techniques
etc which may affect shoring stability
However it must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and
the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation
Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to accurately estimate the
magnitude of movement The magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship such
as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are installed on the subsoil
conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of excavation
Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed
properly and as early as practical and that the adjacent ground should be continuously
monitored for cracks dips and or other indications of movements with instruments

Fills and backfills from 2 feet above the water table up to the finished grade should
be moisture conditioned to a minimum of 2 percent above the optimum moisture content
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Fill placement should commence at the
lowest point and continue upward in level compacted lifts
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
Structural fill and or backfill material should consist of well graded granular
materials less than 3 inches in maximum dimension The material should have a
laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 20 or more and should have a
maximum swell of one percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883 In
addition it should contain between 10 and 30 percent passing the No 200 sieve In
general most of the on site soils meet the above specifications Imported fill materials
should be tested by Geolabs Inc for conformance with our recommendations prior to
delivery to the project site for its intended use
CLSM should have a 28 day compressive strength of 50 to 250 psi and a
maximum aggregate size of sand 4 sieve size if it is desired to allow possible future
excavation with normal construction equipment CLSM should have a maximum setting
time of 24 hours where the setting time is defined as the amount of time required to
achieve 12 psi CLSM should not contain corrosive materials and all materials within the
mix should meet ACI standards for corrosivity

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 to depths of about 49 to 78 feet
below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with auger
rotary wash and rock coring equipment
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by an engineer or geologist who monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 and D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
standard penetration sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 to 24 inch
drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations ASTM D 2937
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Fourteen 14 sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 were performed on selected
samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soil and to aid in soil
classification The results of these tests are presented on Plates B 1 through B 3
One 1 Atterberg Limits Test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected
sample of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification
Results of this test are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depth Graphic representations of the test results are provided on Plate B 4
