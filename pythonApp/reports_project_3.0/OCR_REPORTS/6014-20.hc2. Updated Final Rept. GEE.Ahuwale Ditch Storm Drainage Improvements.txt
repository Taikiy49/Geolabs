new rectangular concrete ditch with a 2.5-foot tall eastern wall and a 1.5-foot tall western

proposed rectangular concrete ditches on a shallow spread footing foundation system.

swelling potential. Although the laboratory test results indicate that the clayey colluvial soils

dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads such as 
those caused by wind or seismic forces.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene 
to early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield, volcanic activity returned with a 
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are 
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. The project site is located on the 
southwestern flank of the Koolau Mountain Range. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of 
the sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available 
such that the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii and in the heads of valleys, the erosional 
processes are dominated by detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls 
and are transported downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as 
colluvium.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration and laboratory test results, clayey colluvial soils 
were encountered at the project site. Therefore, we recommend providing a 6-inch thick 
compacted aggregate base course layer below the concrete ditches and the footing 
foundations. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,500 psf may be used for the 
design of the concrete wall footing bearing on the compacted aggregate base course 
materials.  
A shotcrete lining should be placed along the upslope side of Ditches A and B, 
where colluvium is exposed on the slope face to reduce the potential for erosion of the 
colluvium. If basalt rock is encountered, the shotcrete lining may be omitted since the 
potential for erosion of the basalt rock is low. Detailed discussions and 
recommendations for the design of the drainage ditch improvement project are 
presented in the following sections.

3.3 
Drainage Ditch Lining 
Based on our site reconnaissance, the existing slope face along Ditches A and B 
exposed medium hard basalt rock and colluvium. In general, the colluvium consisting of 
grayish brown silty clays with varying amounts of sands, gravel, cobbles, and boulders 
was observed during our field exploration. 
A 6-inch thick shotcrete lining should be placed along the upslope side of the 
restored interceptor ditch where colluvium is exposed on the slope face to reduce the 
potential for erosion of the exposed colluvium. We believe that the shotcrete lining may 
be constructed on slopes no steeper than 1H:1V inclination. 
The shotcrete lining should be well-drained to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic 
pressures. The drainage system would consist of a prefabricated drainage product, 
such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, placed near the top of the lining and extending to near 
the base of the lining. The drainage product should discharge to weepholes spaced at 
about 10 feet center-to-center.  
If basalt rock is encountered, the concrete lining may be omitted since the 
potential for erosion of the basalt rock is low.

3.4 
Site Grading 
We anticipate that the site grading work for the project will generally consist of 
minor cuts and fills along the interceptor ditch alignment. At the on-set of earthwork, the 
area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared. Vegetation, 
construction debris, deleterious materials, and other unsuitable materials should be 
removed and disposed of properly off-site or in a designated area to reduce the 
potential for contamination of the excavated materials with the spoils.  
Based on the field exploration, massive boulders and hard basalt rock were 
observed. Therefore, we anticipate that some very difficult excavations may arise in 
localized areas of ditch and footing excavations encountering the massive boulders and 
hard basalt rock. Contractors are encouraged to examine the site conditions and 
subsurface data to make their own reasonable and prudent interpretation. 
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing should be over-excavated to 
expose firm natural ground, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
well-compacted fill. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be properly disposed 
of off-site.  
Imported fill and backfill materials, if required, should consist of non-expansive 
select granular material, such as crushed coral or basalt. The material should be 
well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest 
dimension. In addition, the material should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles 
passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have a laboratory California Bearing 
Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less 
when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. 
Fill and backfill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers 
to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry 
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum

3.5 
Cut and Fill Slopes 
The cut slopes exposing colluvium consisting of the grayish brown silty clays with 
varying amounts of sands, gravel, cobbles, and boulders may be designed with a slope 
inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. The cut slopes may be steepened to an inclination of 
0.5H:1V where medium hard basalt rock is exposed. 
Permanent fill slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. 
Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the 
existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding. The filling operations 
should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in 
accordance with the above fill placement recommendations. Fill slopes should be 
constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a 
well-compacted slope face. In addition, slope planting should be provided as soon as 
possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by performing 
15 Dynamic Cone Penetration (DCP) testing, designated as DCP Nos. 1 through 15, 
extending to depths of about 5.4 to 17.9 inches below the existing ground surface. In 
addition, three bulk soil samples, designated as Bulk-1 to Bulk-3, were obtained for 
laboratory testing. The approximate DCP and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site 
Plan, Plate 2. 
The procedures used in our DCP tests are in general accordance with 
ASTM D6951, Standard Test Method for Use of the Dynamic Cone Penetrometer in 
Shallow Pavement Applications. The DCP apparatus consists of a ⅝-inch diameter steel 
rod with a 60-degree conical tip. The rod is topped with an anvil that is connected to a 
second steel rod. This rod is used as a guide to allow a 17.6-lb hammer to be repeatedly 
raised and dropped from a height of 22.6 inches. The connection between the two rods 
consists of an anvil to allow for quick connections between the rods and for efficient 
energy transfer from the falling weight to the penetrating rod. 
Once the test apparatus is assembled, the DCP is placed at the test location and 
the initial penetration of the rod is recorded to provide a zeroing scale. While holding the 
rod vertically, the weight is raised to the top of the rod (22.6 inches above the anvil) and 
dropped. The penetration of the rod is measured after each drop. 
In the DCP tests conducted by Geolabs, the same equipment described above was 
used to record the depth of penetration per hammer drop, as shown in the field logs 
provided in Plates A-1.1 through A-15.2.

Laboratory Tests 
One Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D4318) was performed on a selected soil 
sample to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. A graphic presentation of the test results 
is provided on Plate B-1. 
Two Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. Graphic 
presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on Plate B-2. 
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support and swelling 
characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-3 through B-5.
