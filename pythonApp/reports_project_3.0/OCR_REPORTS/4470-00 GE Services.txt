PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located at the existing Waimanalo Gulch Sanitary Landfill in the Ewa
District on the Island of Oahu Hawaii as shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 The
Waimanalo Gulch is a V shaped nearly symmetrical natural gulch feature that was developed
as a Sanitary landfill The gulch is composed of layered basalt rock that was eroded by stream
activity over the passage of geologic time
We understand it is desired to expand the existing landfill footprint from approximately
64 acres to about 123 4 acres In order to achieve the desired landfill expansion it is
proposed to construct some temporary cut slopes and inter slope benches in the existing
layered basalt rock to support the installation of the landfill base liner Based on the available
conceptual plans the proposed temporary cut slopes will be constructed predominantly along
the eastern gulch slope with some relatively minor excavation of the western slope located
toward the northerly end of the gulch
Based on the available conceptual plans dated February 2000 we understand that the
overall proposed cut slope inclination is designed at two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or
flatter In addition we understand that the proposed cuts associated with the planned
inter slope benches and stabilized walls may be designed as vertical cuts Furthermore we
understand that the cuts proposed for the top of the slope outside the limits of the lined landfill
along the eastern slope may be designed at an inclination of 72H 1V

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu encompasses approximately 604 square miles of land area and
may be divided into 4 general geographic regions The regions include the
Mountains Mountains 3 Leilehua Plateau and 4 Coastal Plain The Island
of Oahu was formed by the merging of basalt lavas erupted from two prominent shield
volcanos the Wai anae and Ko olau volcanos Of the two the Wai anae volcano is the
oldest having breached the ocean surface nearly 4 million years ago The Ko olau volcano
rose above sea level approximately 2 7 million years ago making it the younger of the two
volcanos
In general the two shield volcanos were built by the long term eruption of thinly
bedded a a and pahoehoe lava flows and pyroclastic materials which were erupted from
elongate and linear trending volcanic vents associated with the prominent rift zones of each
of the volcanos The younger Ko olau lavas banked against the older lava flows of the
Wai anae volcano to form the Leilehua Plateau which comprises the central interior portion
of the Island of Oahu The Wai anae Mountains which form the western half of the Island
of Oahu contain the older basalt rock formations on the Island of Oahu The Ko olau
Mountains form the eastern half of the island and include the rock layers exposed along the
extending from Waimanalo to Kahuku
During the final stages of eruption and continuing well after the cessation of
Wai anae and Ko olau eruptions the island mass is believed to have subsided thousands
of feet under the weight of the rock In addition the force of stream erosion combined with
the effect of early large scale fracture and slumping of portions of the volcanic landmass
contributed to the evolution of the island landform Other factors that had a significant
impact on the erosion of the Island of Oahu include the glacioeustatic sea level fluctuations
that occurred during the Pleistocene Age As the continental glaciers advanced and
retreated over geologic time so too did the sea levels in response to large changes of the
ocean volume These sea level fluctuations effected the erosional baselevel which in turn
altered the terrestrial and offshore erosional and depositional processes and environments
As a result coral may be encountered at higher elevations on land and terrestrial deposits
may be encountered embedded in offshore marine deposits
Between 850 000 to 70 000 years ago Recent Age volcanic activity returned to the
Island of Oahu with the eruption of ash cinder and some basalt lavas from vents located
on the eastern and southeastern portions of the Island of Oahu These most recent
eruptions are collectively known as the Honolulu Volcanic Series Subsequently continued
erosion by stream and ocean wave activity has worked on the exposed land surfaces to
form the island topography of the present day

Plate 1 The project site is located at Waimanalo Gulch which was carved into the
southwestern portion of the Wai anae Mountain range on the western side of the Island of
Oahu The Waimanalo Gulch is one of a series of parallel trending gulches that drain from
the upper reaches of the Wai anae Mountains down toward the southwest facing coastline
Waimanalo Gulch is located on the arid portion of Leeward Oahu and contains an
ephemeral stream which remains dry except during rainfall events
The project site is bound to the east and west by relatively steep valley walls
composed of layered basalt rock that dip gradually down toward the southwestern
coastline The layers of basalt rock consist of relatively dense lava flow separated by
seams of less dense basalt lava flow termed clinker The alternating rock layers represent
individual flow unit cores dense rock separated on the top and bottom by more fractured
and porous clinker seams The exposures visible along the valley walls show the layered
character with the more dense layers exposed as prominent rock outcroppings that can be
traced laterally along the hillslope
Very little alluvial colluvial soil development is exposed on the hillslopes Most of the
surficial soils are located near the top of the hillslopes on the exposed ground surfaces
The soil deposits appear to consist of dark brown clayey soils with sand and weathered
gravel The soil deposits observed at the ridgeline elevations of the site are generally on
the order of about 3 to 5 feet in thickness
Based on our observation of the site some preliminary slope excavation work has
commenced in the vicinity of the eastern hillslope The exposed rock materials observed
in the fresh excavations appear to consist of moderate to highly weathered layered basalt
rock As anticipated the more highly weathered rock consists of the bedded clinker seams
with thickness generally on the order of about 4 to 8 feet The clinker seams are of
medium hardness and may be picked by a hammer with some difficulty to produce sandy
and gravelly materials The more dense lava flow cores appear to be moderately
weathered and hard to very hard with thickness generally on the order of about 10 to
20 feet Some massive outcroppings that were very difficult to rip and excavate with the
bulldozer were observed
Based on our observation of the excavation of the eastern hillslope the exposed
rock materials appear to be uniformly weathered throughout the height of the hillslope
Seepage of groundwater was not observed in the exposed cuts The regional groundwater
level for this portion of the island is located at elevations that are significantly lower than
the elevations of the project site However some minor groundwater seepage may be
anticipated following rainfall due to percolating rainwater
Based on our observation of the site and vicinity significantly sized lava tubes or
cave features were not exposed However some potential lava tube features may be
buried in the basalt rock comprising the hillside It is anticipated that the potential lava tube
trend would be aligned in the northeast to southwest direction which is roughly parallel to

Seismic Refraction Survey
A seismic refraction survey was performed at three locations selected within the
project site The survey was performed using a Geometrics S 12 12 channel seismograph
and a 12 pound sledgehammer as the shot energy source The locations selected for the
survey were located within the proposed cut slope area of the eastern hillslope at the
project site The seismic refraction survey locations were positioned on the existing terrace
cuts that provided a generally flat ground surface located on freshly cut exposures of the
basalt rock The approximate location where the seismic refraction surveys were
performed is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Seismic refraction technology is a non intrusive geophysical means of evaluating the
relative density thickness and depth of subsurface materials such as soil and rock layers
Seismic refraction is used to record the compressional wave primary or P wave response
first arrival of the wave as it passes through or is refracted at different velocities in various
types of soil and rock mediums The technology is a useful exploration tool where geologic
layers exist with increasing material density and with increasing depth A condition that
normally holds true for about 80 percent of surficial naturally occurring geologic deposits
Based on the results of the seismic refraction survey the velocity of the rock
materials was found to range between about 2 700 to 15 300 feet per second The lower
velocities are believed to be representative of the highly weathered less dense clinker
seams and the higher velocities are believed to be representative of the less weathered
denser cores of the lava flows Based on our interpretation of the seismic refraction survey
data the recorded compressional wave velocities are representative of the weathered
basalt rock materials observed at the site
It should be noted that a limitation of the seismic refraction survey is that lower
velocity earth layers cannot be detected underlying higher velocity layers due to the faster
first arrival times of the higher velocity layer which mask the slower first arrival time of the
deeper lower velocity layer As a result for the layered condition that exists at the project
site essentially only the first two layers can be analyzed as other lower velocity layers
existing at deeper depths will not be detected

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our observation of the exposed basalt rock comprising the existing temporary
construction cut slope and the rock outcroppings of undisturbed hillslope located above we
believe that there are no obvious geotechnical constraints for long term rock slope stability that
would preclude the overall flattening of the existing slope to 2H 1V or flatter inclinations at the
project site
We believe that the proposed temporary vertical cuts of about 5 to 20 feet in height
associated with the proposed benches and stabilized walls are feasible for the short term
stability of the rock slope Details pertaining to the stabilized walls were not available at the
time this letter report was prepared
We believe that the planned cuts of 2H 1V inclination located at the top of the cut slope
adjacent to the property boundary should be feasible for the long term stability of the rock
slope However it should be noted that some alluvial colluvial soil and boulder deposits may
be encountered at the ground surface of the upper portion of the cut slopes located near the
top of the ridgeline Where these unconsolidated soil deposits are encountered we
recommend that the cut slope be flattened to a maximum inclination of 2H 1V
Excavated Slope Faces
It is anticipated that the finished slope faces produced by the excavation may be
irregular due to the inter layered character of the hard and medium hard rock thus a
roughened surface exposure should be anticipated especially on the steeper cut slope faces
The irregular slope face mentioned may consist of overhanging harder rock layers sandwiched
between recessed medium hard layers of rock We recommend that the excavated slope
faces be observed during and after excavation to observe the condition of the exposed rock
materials
Excavation
Based on our observation of the exposed weathered rock materials and the progress
of the recent slope excavation work using large bulldozers we believe the rock materials may
be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment such as backhoes and ripping with
large bulldozers However excavations encountering the dense basalt formations embedded
in the hillslope may require the use of hoerams chipping or possibly blasting if permitted
