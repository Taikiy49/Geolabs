Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be
late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age As volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano
ceased lava flows from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope
forming a broad plateau known as the Schofield Plateau
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of this plateau generated
sediments which were transported to the coast In the project vicinity and to the south
these sediments accumulated and interbedded with marine sediments and coral algal
reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge The thickness of the sedimentary wedge
ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1 Highway to over 1 000 feet at
Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation or
caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu Deposition of sediments
has continued from earlier geologic time through the present Agricultural development
within the last 100 years has brought the project area to its present form

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic
plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic
activity therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during
volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most are so
small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments However some of
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to moderate
damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii Because majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii
over 90 percent are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic activity and
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of
Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than
Magnitude 5 M5 however earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of
Hawaii
To a lesser degree the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5 Therefore moderate to strong earthquakes have
occurred in the County of Maui The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Islands
of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu For example small
landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of
Some houses on the Island of Oahu were reportedly damaged as a
result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 M7

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the Kapolei Lofts Apartments site is generally
underlain by about 7 5 to 26 5 feet of medium stiff to very hard clay overlying medium
dense to very dense and medium stiff to hard coralline deposits Our laboratory tests
indicate that the near surface clayey soils exhibit high shrink swell characteristics when
subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents Therefore special attention
should be given to the site preparation recommendations for the site grading and
foundation design to account for the highly expansive soil conditions
Based on the generally stiff subsurface soil conditions encountered we believe
that a shallow foundation system slabs on grade with thickened edge footings and
spread footings may be used to support the proposed building structures Due to the
highly expansive soil conditions we recommend capping the expansive clayey soils
with a minimum of 36 inches of non expansive select granular fill material below
building slab areas We also recommend providing 36 inches of non expansive select
granular fill material below the driveways walkways and lanai slabs In addition special
attention should be given to the recommended procedures for subgrade soil preparation
below slabs and pavements to reduce the potential shrink swell effects of the near
surface clayey soils
Field observations during our exploration indicated that shrinkage cracks have
developed on the existing ground surface across the project site The existing shrinkage
cracks if left unattended may increase further in size under prolonged dry weather
conditions Shrinkage cracks located adjacent to the building foundations are potentially
detrimental since water can readily infiltrate into the subsurface through the crack
openings and soften the subsoils around the building foundations thus affecting their
performance Therefore we recommend scarifying the existing ground surface
moisture conditioning and recompacting during the site preparation operations In
addition appropriate maintenance measures should be implemented to reduce the
potential for recurrence of shrinkage cracks around the building structures

Slab On Grade Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we believe that slabs on grade
with thickened edge footings may be used to support the one to three story wooden
residential and commercial structures planned Our exploration indicates that most of
the site is generally underlain by moderately to highly expansive clayey soils at or near
the existing ground surface Unless slab on grade foundations supported by these
expansive soils are properly designed there is a potential for future distress to the
residential and commercial structures resulting from wetting and subsequent swelling of
the expansive clayey soils
To reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from the swelling of the
on site expansive soils we recommend providing non expansive select granular fill
material capping fill for a minimum depth of 36 inches below the building slab cushion
The capping fill should also extend beyond the perimeter of the house slab a minimum
of 2 feet The perimeter thickened edges of the slabs should be embedded a minimum
of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent exterior grade The embedment of the interior
thickened edge footings may be reduced to a minimum depth of 12 inches below the
adjacent finished grade The base of the thickened edge should have a minimum width
of 12 inches
The slab edges should incorporate a sufficient amount of top and bottom
longitudinal steel reinforcement In addition the concrete slab should be reinforced with
reinforcing steel bars The project structural engineer should be consulted for design
details of this thickened edge which is intended to function both as a moisture barrier
and as a perimeter bearing wall footing

Shrinkage Cracks
Our field observations indicated that substantial shrinkage cracks within the
surface clay soils are present at the existing ground surface of the project site In
general shrinkage cracks will start to develop in the ground due to the lack of
maintenance of the soil moisture content as the soils start to dry out under the sun and
dry weather conditions The development of shrinkage cracks in the ground was
apparently intensified by the prolonged dry climatic conditions in the Kapolei area
resulting in large fluctuations of the soil moisture content Detailed discussions of the
shrink swell behavior of the on site soils and the mechanism of shrinkage crack
development are presented below
Based on the laboratory test results performed the on site clayey soils generally
have a moderate to high shrink swell potential when subjected to fluctuations in the
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
W O 5567 50
GEOLABS INC
Page 17
Hawaii California
moisture content and may be classified as expansive soils In addition it will likely
lose substantial strength when saturated and will shrink appreciably when
subjected to drying resulting in shrinkage cracks In general the mechanism of
shrink swell in expansive soils is a complex phenomenon and is influenced by a
number of factors including soil characteristics and environmental factors
Expansive soil characteristics can generally be measured by engineering properties
such as soil plasticity and density to evaluate the shrink swell potential of soils In
general the soil plasticity reflects the capacity of the clay minerals in the soil to
retain large amounts of water while maintaining a coherent structure Therefore a
highly plastic soil such as those encountered at the project site will have a greater
potential for volume change shrink and swell due to the loss or absorption of
moisture under cycles of drying and wetting In addition increased soil densities
through compaction or natural depositional history such as desiccation generally
have greater shrink swell potential
Environmental factors include the initial moisture content of the soil and moisture
variations over time The initial moisture content generally impact the potential of
the soil to absorb or expel water from the soil system In general a dry soil will have
a higher capacity to absorb water and swell while a wet soil will lose water more
readily upon exposure to dry conditions and will shrink more Moisture variations
can generally be caused by climate surface drainage vegetation permeability etc
Shrinkage Crack Development Mechanism
Due to the high plasticity of the on site clayey soils shrinking of the near surface
soils starts to develop as soil moisture evaporates from the soil system under the
prolonged dry climatic conditions characteristic of the Kapolei area These
shrinkage cracks may close up to a certain degree upon wetting as the soils
expand during periods of wet weather or heavy rains However shrinkage of the
on site soils and subsequent cracking of the ground surface will recur when the dry
weather returns Therefore the development of shrinkage cracks will recur when
the on site clayey soils are subjected to prolonged dry climatic conditions which

causes large fluctuations in the moisture content unless some measures are taken
to maintain the soil moisture content at a relatively constant level
In areas where ponding water occurs the wet ground will have a higher potential
for shrinkage cracks to develop since the wet soils will lose water more readily upon
exposure to dry conditions and will shrink more Therefore it should be recognized
that this phenomenon will recur and continue unless measures are taken to
maintain the soil moisture content relatively constant in order to reduce the
shrink swell behavior of the on site clayey soils due to moisture fluctuations

Recommendations for Site Preparation
If the existing ground cracks are left unattended for an extended period of time
and are allowed to penetrate deeper below the surface the ground cracks could
adversely affect the performance of the future building foundations The ground cracks
could serve as conduits for water to percolate into the foundation soils and cause loss of
strength of the foundation soils resulting in potential settlement of the building structure
In addition migration of the foundation soils into the openings of the shrinkage cracks
could undermine the foundation system and result in potential settlement of the building
structure Therefore remedial work to correct or mitigate the observed ground cracks
should be implemented prior to the proposed building construction
Based on our field observations shrinkage cracks up to about 1 inch wide and
12 to 18 inches deep were observed Therefore we recommend scarifying the existing
ground surface to a minimum depth of 12 inches moisture conditioning to at least
2 percent above the optimum moisture content and compacting to at least 90 percent
relative compaction prior to commencement of the site work grading operations to
remove the zone with shrinkage cracks
It should be noted that a proper site drainage system is also critical to the
performance of the foundation system and reduction in the development of shrinkage
cracks Therefore drainage swales should be properly established and that the building
pads should be graded to provide positive drainage away from the building foundations

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Five one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The test
results are summarized on Plate B 1
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test ASTM D 1883 was performed on a
bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The test results are presented on Plate B 3
