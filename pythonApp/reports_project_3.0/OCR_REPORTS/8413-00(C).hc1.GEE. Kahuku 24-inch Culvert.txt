SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, our field exploration encountered about 8 and 12 inches of asphaltic 
concrete pavement underlain by a surface fill layer overlying shallow marine deposit, older 
alluvium, and coralline detritus.  The surface fill layer encountered was about 9 feet thick 
and generally consisted of medium dense to very loose silty sand and sandy gravel and stiff 
silty clay.  Shallow marine deposit consisting of medium dense to loose silty sand and sand 
was encountered below the surface fill layer, extending to a depth of about 16.5 feet below 
the existing ground surface. Below the shallow marine deposit, older alluvium consisting of 
very stiff silty clay was encountered to depths of about 24 to 26 feet. Coralline detritus 
consisting of loose to medium dense silty gravel and silty sand was encountered below the 
older alluvium layer and extended to the maximum depth explored of about 32 feet below 
the existing pavement surface.   
Groundwater levels were encountered in the drilled borings at depths varying from 
about 7.0 to 8.3 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration.  
The groundwater levels measured generally correspond to about Elevations +1.2 to 
+2.5 feet MSL. Due to the proximity of the project site to the Pacific Ocean, it is likely that 
the groundwater level measured in the boring during our field exploration will be affected by 
tidal fluctuation.  In addition, the water levels at the project site may be influenced by 
seasonal precipitation, runoff, storm surge conditions, and other factors.  
We anticipate that headwall structures will be bearing on the medium dense to very 
loose fills and/or marine deposits encountered below the existing pavement surface.  We 
recommend providing a bedding layer consisting of 6 inches of open-graded gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) on top of a subgrade stabilization layer consisting of 24 
inches of No. 2 Rock (ASTM C33, No. 4 gradation) wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric 
(Mirafi 180N or equivalent) below the bottom of the headwall structure for uniform support. 
For footing foundations bearing on the recommended bedding and subgrade stabilization 
layers, bearing pressures of up to 6,000, 2,700 and 2,000 psf may be used for the extreme 
event limit, strength limit, and service limit states, respectively.  The foundations should be 
embedded a minimum of about 2 feet below the adjacent finish grade.  
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
recommendations for the design of the proposed 24-inch culvert project.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from 
two shield volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to 
be of middle to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be 
of late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age.  As volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano 
ceased, lava flows from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope 
forming a broad plateau known as Schofield Plateau. 
The project is located in the Kahuku area within the District of Koolauloa on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The coastal area of Kahuku lies within the Coastal Plain province 
of Oahu and is located to the northeast of the Ko’olau Mountain province.  As a result, 
much of the generally flat land area is underlain by unconsolidated coastal sediments 
(coralline sands and silts) with pockets of hard, cemented sand dunes (sandstone) and 
coral/limestone rock formation. 
Progressing from the coastal area toward the hills of the Ko’olau Mountains, the 
subsurface conditions gradually change to reflect an increase in thickness of terrestrial 
sediments.  The terrestrial sediments include the alluvial soils derived from the hills and 
valleys located southwest of the coastline.  These alluvial soils may overlie buried coral 
formations and sand deposits in the subsurface.  The deposition of sediments has 
continued from earlier geologic times through the present.  Subsequently, development 
within the last 100 years has brought the project area to its present form.

3.1.3 Dynamic Lateral Earth Pressures 
Dynamic lateral earth forces due to seismic loading may need to be considered in 
the design of headwalls, if appropriate, that are free to deflect by as much as 
0.5 percent of the structure height. For design in accordance with the AASHTO 
LRFD methods, the force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures associated with 
seismic loading (PGA = 0.318g) may be estimated using 5.9H2 pounds per linear 
foot of wall length for level backfill conditions. It should be noted that the dynamic 
lateral earth forces provided assume that the wall will be allowed to move laterally 
by up to about 1 to 2 inches in the event of an earthquake.  
If the estimated amount of lateral movement is not attainable or the retaining 
structure is restrained, the retaining structure should be designed with higher 
dynamic lateral forces for a restrained condition. For a restrained condition (less 
than 0.5 inches of lateral movement), dynamic lateral forces due to seismic loading 
may be estimated using 10.4H2 pounds per linear foot of wall (H measured in feet) 
for level backfill conditions. 
The resultant force should be assumed to act through the mid-height of the wall. 
The above dynamic lateral earth forces are in addition to the static lateral earth 
pressures provided above. An appropriately reduced factor of safety may be used 
when dynamic lateral earth pressures are accounted for in the design of the 
retaining structure. 
3.1.4 Drainage 
Retaining walls should be well-drained to reduce the potential for the build-up of 
hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system should consist of permeable 
material, such as ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation material, placed directly around a 
perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at the base of the wall discharging to an 
appropriate outlet or weepholes. As an alternative, a prefabricated drainage 
product, such as MiraDrain, may be used instead of the drainage material.  The 
prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a 
perforated pipe at the base of the wall.

3.2 
Pipe Cushion 
The stress distribution against the bottom of the pipe has a significant effect on the 
load supporting capacity of the pipe. Therefore, the pipe cushion is an important design 
consideration. For pipes bearing on the medium dense silty sand and sandy gravel fill 
soils and/or marine deposit, a minimum of 6 inches of compacted granular cushion 
material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) should be provided 
below the pipe. Where soft and/or loose soils are encountered at or near the invert of the 
pipes, a stabilization layer consisting of an additional 24 inches of open-graded gravel, 
such as No. 3B Fine gravel, wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or 
equivalent) should be provided below the cushion layer for uniform support.  
The granular cushion layer should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width 
of one-fourth (¼) of the outside pipe diameter, or trench width, whichever is greater and 
should extend 12 inches above the pipe. Before the placement of cushion material, a 
Geolabs representative should observe the excavated trench bottom to confirm that firm 
materials are exposed at the bottom of the trench or whether a stabilization layer should 
be provided below the cushion layer.

gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) to reduce the compaction effort required and 
resulting stresses on the pipe. In addition, it is critical to use this free-draining material to 
reduce the potential for the formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide 
adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill could result in backfill 
settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes (or 
12 inches above the groundwater level, whichever is higher) to the pavement subgrade 
should consist of the excavated on-site soils or imported fill materials that are free of 
vegetation, deleterious materials, organic material, and clay lumps and rock fragments 
greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension.  It should be noted that the fill requirements 
presented herein are intended as guidelines only and may be modified based on 
additional laboratory testing and field observations on the available fill materials 
conducted by Geolabs during the project construction. 
Imported fill materials used for trench backfill, if required, should consist of select 
granular fill materials.  Select granular fill materials should be well-graded from coarse to 
fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and contain between 
10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have a 
laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of 
1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. Geolabs should test the 
imported fill materials for conformance with these recommendations. 
As an alternative, to facilitate the backfilling and reduce the time required for 
applying compaction effort, Controlled Low Strength Material (CLSM) may be used to 
backfill the trenches. The CLSM should be proportioned to produce a backfill material that 
is self-compacting and able to be excavated with conventional excavation equipment. The 
28-day compressive strength of CLSM should be between 50 and 150 pounds per square

3.4 
Trench Backfill Compaction and Placement 
Backfill materials consisting of the on-site surface fill soils should be 
moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture, placed in level 
lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction. Imported fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. The upper 3 feet of backfill 
materials below the finished pavement grade should be compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction. 
Aggregate subbase materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, 
and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction 
refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry 
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture 
is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry 
density. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or 
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or ponding 
should not be allowed to compact the excavated surface on-site fill soils or imported fill 
materials. Precautions should be taken against the occurrence of pipeline flotation before 
and during trench backfilling due to the possible influx of large quantities of groundwater 
into the trenches. The buoyant uplift forces due to groundwater influx may lift empty or 
even partially water-filled pipes. 
Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during backfilling to 
assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper 
moisture content.

3.5 
Pavement Restoration 
It is anticipated that AC pavement will be used to repave the trench along the 
24-inch diameter RCP culvert alignment within the roadways. We have assumed that the

on the near-surface soils obtained from our field exploration.  Based on the laboratory 
test results obtained, we have adopted a design Resistance (R) value of 28 for the 
pavement subgrade material in our pavement design analyses. Based on the traffic 
loading information provided and pavement design analyses performed, the following 
flexible pavement section is recommended for the proposed pavement restoration:  
Flexible Pavement   
  4.0-Inch Asphaltic Concrete 
  8.0-Inch Asphaltic Concrete Base (92 Percent Max Theoretical Specific Gravity) 
  6.0-Inch Aggregate Subbase (95 Percent Relative Compaction)

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
two exploratory borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to a depth of about 
32 feet below the existing pavement surface. The borings were drilled with a truck-mounted 
drill rig equipped with 6-inch OD hollow-stem augers. In addition, one bulk sample of the 
near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1, was obtained to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the near-surface soils. The approximate boring and bulk sample locations 
are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550 Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the Standard 
Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the 
field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive 
strength of the sample. The pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on the Logs 
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths, and a 
graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Two Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. 
Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on Plate B-2. 
Two one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples 
to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils. The test results are 
summarized on Plate B-3. 
Two Unconfined Compression Strength tests (ASTM D2166) were performed on 
selected soil samples to evaluate the compressive strength of the soil.  The test results are 
presented on Plates B-4 and B-5. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D 3080) were performed on selected soil samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the in-situ soils. The test results are presented 
on Plates B-6 and B-7. 
One laboratory Resistance (R) Value test (ASTM D2844) was performed on a bulk 
sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the 
soils. The test results are presented on Plate B-8.
