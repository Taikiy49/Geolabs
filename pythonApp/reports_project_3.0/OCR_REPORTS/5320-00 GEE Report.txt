1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Reconstruction of Playcourts at the Halawa District Park in Halawa
on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for
the proposed project These recommendations are intended for the design of the
reconstruction of playcourts only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of
this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The Halawa District Park project is located in the Halawa area on the Island of
Oahu Hawaii The project involves the reconstruction of tennis volleyball and
basketball playcourts The reconstruction will also involve construction of walkways
light poles for the lighting system fence gates tennis practice walls landscaping
irrigation system and miscellaneous playcourt appurtenances The sizes of the existing
tennis court and volleyball court will be increased

to accomplish this we conducted an exploration program consisting of the following

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The
younger shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in
age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became
extinct while Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below
Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed
a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau Both Waianae and Koolau
Ranges have lost most of their original shield outlines and are now long narrow ridges
shaped largely by erosion
The site is located in the lower portion of Halawa Valley which has been brought
to its present form by a complex series of geologic events The original Halawa Valley
was a youthful valley cut into the Koolau Dome during a lower stand of the sea Several
subsequent stages of alluvial depositional processes have filled the valley floor with
fluvial deposits
At an intermediate stage between two series of alluvial deposits pyroclastic
materials such as cinder were laid across the mouth of the valley by members of the
Honolulu Volcanic Series These deposits of volcanic material most likely originating
from Aliamanu and Salt Lake Craters caused a diversion of Halawa Stream to the west
Stream erosion and diversion of the course of Halawa Stream have shaped the area to
its present topography During the last few decades man has been an important force
in shaping Halawa Valley to its present form with the placement of extensive fills over
much of the older alluvial deposits

2 2 Site Description
The project site is located within Halawa District Park along the western side of
Iwaiwa Street and can be accessed from Apela Street in the Halawa area on the Island
of Oahu Hawaii
The existing playcourts were covered with 2 inches of asphaltic concrete
underlain by about 6 inches of base course The asphalt surface was in relatively poor
condition with many cracks The playcourt surface was level and in an L shape
Based on the schematic site plan the existing ground surface elevations at the site
range from about 139 to 142 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
2 3 Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling five borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 5 extending to depths of
about 11 to 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of
the borings drilled for the project are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Boring Nos 1 and 5 drilled in the parking lot areas generally encountered 6 to
9 inches of asphaltic concrete underlain by several inches of sandy gravel fill material
base course The borings drilled in the landscape area generally encountered topsoil
In general our field exploration encountered very stiff silty and clayey soils
underlain by dense to medium hard volcanic tuff extending to the maximum depths
explored
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings drilled at the time of our field
exploration However it should be noted that groundwater levels could fluctuate
depending on factors such as seasonal rainfall storm surge conditions and other
factors Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered during our field exploration
are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 5 of Appendix A

Construction Considerations
Drilled shafts are desirable for the light pole foundations because of the significant
increase in lateral and uplift load capacities when compared to shallow foundations
However the performance of the drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the
contractor s method of construction and construction procedures In our opinion
the following conditions would have an impact on the effectiveness and cost of the
drilled shaft foundations
Workmanship
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in
drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes
Since the project site is generally underlain by very stiff soil and tuff with some sand
layers temporary casing may be required during the foundation construction work
to reduce the potential for caving in of the drilled holes Drilling by methods utilizing
drilling fluids is not recommended
Free fall concrete placement will be allowed in dry excavation only If groundwater
more than 3 inches from the bottom of the hole is encountered placement of
concrete by wet construction methods using a tremie pipe will be required The
concrete should be placed in a suitable manner by displacing the water in an
upward fashion from the bottom of the drilled hole

3 2 Site Preparation
Based on the existing grades we believe that minor site grading work may be
required to achieve the finished grades at the site In general areas within the contract
grading limits should be thoroughly cleared at the on set of earthwork Vegetation
debris deleterious materials and other unsuitable materials including the asphaltic
concrete pavement should be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft loose
and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to receive fill or
future improvements should be over excavated to expose firm or dense material and
the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill The excavated
soft loose and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site In addition
contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for
over excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variation in the
over excavation quantities
Existing structures and pavements to be demolished should be completely
removed The excavations resulting from the demolition work should be backfilled with
select granular fill material compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the resulting excavation
should be properly backfilled with select granular fill material moisture conditioned to

compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Utilities to be abandoned
in place under the proposed structure should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete or

3 3 Building Foundations Including Tennis Practice Wall
Based on the generally very stiff subsoil conditions encountered at the site we
believe that shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the
proposed buildings An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 000 pounds per square
foot psf may be used for the design of shallow foundations bearing on the in situ stiff
to very hard silty clays This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing
over excavations the material should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm
materials The resulting over excavations should be backfilled with on site material to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and by passive earth pressure acting
against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of friction of 0 35
may be used for footings bearing on the stiff to very hard silty clays Resistance due to
passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of
200 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soil around
footings is well compacted The passive pressure should be reduced for foundations
located on slopes Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the
upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected

3 5 Slabs On Grade
It is our understanding that new concrete walkways will be constructed around
the playcourt Our laboratory tests indicate that the silty clay soils have a low to
moderate potential for shrinking and swelling due to moisture fluctuation To reduce the
potential for future distress to the slabs on grade resulting from shrinking and swelling
of the silty clay soils we recommend that the subgrade soils underneath the slab areas
be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The underlying subgrade
soils should be kept moist until the final placement of concrete Where shrinkage
cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soils be
thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or prepared again Saturation and
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor
drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted fill To reduce the potential for substantial shrinkage cracks in the
slabs crack control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the
walkways with expansion joints provided at right angle intersections
Areas adjacent to the slab should be backfilled tightly against the slab edges with
the on site soils and graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential
for water ponding around the slabs

APPENDIX A

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling five borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 5 extending to depths of
about 11 to 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using
a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers and coring
tools The approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer or geologist who monitored the drilling
operations on a near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 5
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals

value would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous methods of
excavation

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The
results of the test are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depth Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM D 1883 was performed
on a selected sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils Results of the test are presented on Plate B 2
Five one inch ring swell tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate
the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results of the tests are summarized on
Plate B 3
