SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, the borings drilled between Mud Lane and Hohola Drive encountered 
about 7 to 11 inches of asphaltic concrete over 12 to 34 inches of dense to very dense 
sand and gravel fill material underlain by medium stiff to stiff volcanic ash consisting of 
clayey silt extending to the maximum depths explored.  Several borings encountered 
medium dense to dense clinker at depths of about 2.5 to 5 feet below the existing 
pavement surface.  
The borings drilled between Puu Nani Drive and Mana Road generally 
encountered asphaltic concrete ranging in thickness from about 9 to 16 inches over fill 
material consisting of dense to very dense sandy gravel about 3 to 18 inches thick.  The 
fill material was underlain by volcanic ash consisting of medium stiff to stiff clayey silt 
extending to the maximum depths explored.  We did not encounter groundwater in the 
shallow drilled borings at the time of our field exploration.  
Based on our field exploration, we believe that the drainage and retaining 
structures would likely bear on the stiff volcanic ash consisting of clayey silt.  We 
recommend bearing values of at least 3,000 and 6,000 psf to evaluate the foundation 
bearing conditions under the strength limit and extreme event limit states, respectively, 
using Load Resistance Factor Design (LRFD) method. For the service limit state 
condition, a bearing value up to 2,000 psf may be used.   
Based on our field exploration, volcanic ash soils will likely be exposed where cut 
slopes are planned.  However, basalt rock may be exposed near Mud Lane. In general, 
we believe the cut slopes exposing basalt rock (near Mud Lane) may be designed with 
a slope inclination of 1H:1V or flatter. In areas where volcanic ash or highly fractured 
basalt are exposed at the cut slope faces, the cut slopes need to be flattened to 
inclinations of 2H:1V or flatter.  
Permanent fill slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. 
This assumes that the fill slopes will be constructed using well-graded granular 
materials. In addition, fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and 
benched. The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and 
specific design recommendations.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago with an 
area of approximately 4,000 square miles.  This island was formed by the activity of five 
shield volcanoes:  Kohala, which has long been extinct; Mauna Kea, which has had 
activity during recent geologic time; Hualalai, which last erupted in 1801; and Mauna 
Loa and Kilauea, both of which are still active. 
The project site is located on the northern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield 
Volcano, which has been built up by successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and 
pyroclastic materials.  These lava flows are believed to be late Pleistocene in age and 
belong to the Hamakua Volcanic Series.  Cavities are commonly encountered in these 
lava flows.  Cavities form when lava is still in a molten state and represent both lava 
tubes (intra-flow cavities) and blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities).  Lava tubes are 
formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube-like 
structure, which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow.  Blisters 
and pockets (inter-flow cavities) are generally smaller in horizontal extent. 
2.2 
Site Description 
The proposed project involves improvements to Mamalahoa Highway between 
Mud Lane and Mana Road in South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii.  The project limits 
are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
Mamalahoa Highway is generally a two-lane roadway consisting of flexible 
pavement.  The terrain along the project limits varies from fairly level along the existing 
roadway to sloping along the roadway embankment slopes. The existing pavement 
generally appeared to be in relatively fair to good condition at the time of our field 
exploration. However, the existing pavement between the western project limit and 
Mokuloa Drive appeared to be in relatively poor condition.  Distresses such as rutting 
and alligator cracking were observed at the pavement surface. The severity of the 
alligator cracking appeared to be moderate to high.  Small potholes were forming in 
areas with alligator cracking of high severity.  This area is super elevated and appeared

Subsurface Conditions

sampling twenty-eight borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 28, extending to 
depths of about 4.5 to 6.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. In addition, five 
bulk samples of the anticipated subgrade soils, designated as Bulk-1 through Bulk-5,

3.1 
Drainage and Retaining Structures 
We understand that drainage (inlets/manholes) and small retaining structures 
may be required for the roadway widening project.  The following general guidelines 
may be used for the design of drainage and retaining structures planned for the 
roadway widening project. 
3.1.1 Drainage and Retaining Structure Foundations 
Based on our field exploration, we believe that the drainage and retaining structures 
would likely bear on the stiff volcanic ash consisting of clayey silt.  Based on the 
Load and Resistance Factor Design (LRFD) Method, we recommend bearing 
values of at least 3,000 and 6,000 pounds per square foot (psf) to evaluate the 
foundation bearing conditions under the strength limit and extreme event limit 
states, respectively.

In general, the bottom of the foundations should be embedded a minimum of 2 feet 
below the lowest adjacent finished grades.  The foundation subgrade should be 
compacted to a firm and unyielding base.  If soft/loose soils are encountered at the 
foundation subgrade, the soft/loose soils should be over-excavated to expose 
firm/dense soils and the over-excavation should be backfilled with compacted 
Type A Structure Backfill Material conforming to Section 703.20 of the Hawaii 
Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction, 2005 (HSS). 
Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a 
45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility 
trench, or they should extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines.  
This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade 
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable 
foundation settlement.  
Based on a service limit state bearing pressure of 2,000 psf, we estimate that 
foundation settlements for footings bearing on volcanic ash as recommended 
herein to be less than 1 inch with differential settlements on the order of ½ inch. 
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction developed 
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth 
pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. 
Coefficients of friction of 0.35 and 0.30 may be used for footings bearing on the 
volcanic ash for the extreme event and strength limit states, respectively.  
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using equivalent fluid 
pressures of 350 and 175 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf) for 
extreme event and strength limit states, respectively.  These passive pressure 
values assume that the soils around the footings are well compacted. Unless 
covered by pavements or slabs, the passive pressure resistance in the upper 
12 inches below the finished grade should be neglected.

3.1.4 Drainage 
Retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic 
pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of 1 cubic foot of permeable 
material, such as AASHTO M43, No. 67 gradation material, placed directly around 
a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at the base of the retaining structure. 
The perforated pipe should discharge into an appropriate outlet or weepholes. As 
an alternative, a prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, 
may be used instead of the gravel drainage material. The prefabricated drainage 
product should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe placed at the 
base of the retaining structure.

3.2.1 Site Preparation 
In general, the areas within the contract grading limits should be cleared and 
grubbed thoroughly at the on-set of earthwork. Vegetation, debris, deleterious 
material, and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of 
properly off-site or disposed of in a designated area to reduce the potential for 
contaminating the excavated materials. 
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over-excavated to 
expose stiff and/or dense materials. The resulting excavation should be backfilled 
with aggregate subbase materials. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should 
be properly disposed of off-site. 
After clearing and grubbing, the existing ground surface should be scarified to a 
depth of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum 
moisture, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of 
the maximum dry density of the same soil same soil established in accordance with 
AASHTO T-180 (or ASTM D1557). Optimum moisture is the water content 
(percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.  
Where volcanic ash soils are exposed at the subgrade, the volcanic ash soils 
should be compacted with a drum roller (10-ton static weight) a minimum of 6 to 8 
passes to achieve a relatively firm and unyielding condition.  Due to the potentially 
thixotropic nature of the volcanic ash soils (when wet), use of the vibratory action of 
the compactor should be carefully controlled to avoid disturbing the volcanic ash 
soils.  In addition, moisture-conditioning of the volcanic ash soils should be carefully 
controlled to avoid over-wetting of the soils.  It should be noted that the 
near-surface volcanic ash soils may be sensitive to the moisture content and 
difficult to achieve a firm and unyielding subgrade condition.  Therefore, additional 
compaction effort with proper moisture control may be necessary to achieve a firm 
and unyielding condition.

3.2.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, the excavated on-site granular soils may be re-used as a source of 
general fill materials provided that they are free of particles greater than 6 inches, 
organics and deleterious materials. Due to the high moisture content of the volcanic 
ash, the excavated volcanic ash soils should not be re-used as a source of general 
fill materials. 
Imported general fill materials should consist of well-graded granular materials 
generally less than 6 inches in maximum dimension with sufficient fines to prevent 
the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass. Imported general fill materials 
should be free of organics and deleterious materials and should be suitable for the 
intended use.  The maximum particle size of the general fill should be limited to a 
maximum of 3 inches for fills and backfills to be placed in confined locations. 
Confined locations are defined as areas where the compaction equipment is limited 
in size to less than 2 tons in static weight.  
Aggregate subbase materials should conform to the requirements stipulated in 
Subsection 703.17 of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for Road and 
Bridge Construction, 2005 (HSS). 
3.2.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with AASHTO T-180 (ASTM D1557). Optimum moisture is the water content 
(percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. Compaction

acceptable compaction equipment. 
3.2.4 Cut and Fill Slopes 
We envision cut slopes would expose the volcanic ash soils.  Basalt rock may be 
exposed at the cut slopes near Mud Lane. In general, we believe the cut slopes 
exposing basalt rock (near Mud Lane) may be designed with a slope inclination of 
one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V) or flatter. In areas where volcanic ash or 
highly fractured basalt are exposed at the cut slope faces, the cut slopes need to be 
flattened to inclinations of 2H:1V or flatter. A Geolabs representative should 
observe and map the cut slopes during construction to evaluate whether the 
exposed cut slope materials are consistent with our assumptions. 
Permanent fill slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. 
This assumes that the fill slopes will be constructed using well-graded granular 
materials. In addition, fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed 
and benched.  
In general, the filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in 
level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement 
recommendations. Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back 
to the design slope ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. The fill slope face 
should be finished to a relatively smooth and well-compacted surface. Surface 
water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes, and slope planting should 
be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for significant erosion of the 
finished slopes.  
3.2.5 Excavation 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by granular fill 
and volcanic ash consisting of clayey silt. Some areas along the roadway may 
encounter clinker at depths of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface.  We 
anticipate that the near-surface materials may be excavated with normal heavy

3.3 
Utility Lines 
We anticipate that most of the trenches for utilities will be excavated in the 
granular fill and the volcanic ash soils encountered in the borings drilled at the site. In 
general, granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of open-graded gravel (AASHTO M43, 
No. 67 gradation materials) is recommended below the pipes for uniform support. 
Free-draining granular materials, such as open-graded gravel (AASHTO M43, No. 67 
gradation materials), should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 
12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce 
the compaction effort of the backfill. It is critical to use free-draining materials around the 
pipes to reduce the potential for the formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and 
to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes, which could result in backfill 
settlement. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes 
to the top of the subgrades or finished grade may consist of the on-site granular soils 
generally less than 6 inches in maximum particle size or aggregate subbase. Volcanic 
ash excavated from the trenches should not be used for trench backfill.  The backfill 
material should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum water content, placed in 
maximum 8-inch level loose lifts, and mechanically compacted to no less than 
90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground 
subsidence. Where trenches are below pavement areas, the compaction requirement 
for the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be increased 
to at least 95 percent relative compaction.

A P P E N D I X   A 
Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway alignment by drilling 
and sampling twenty-eight borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 28. The 
borings extended to depths of about 4.5 to 6.5 feet below the existing pavement 
surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 
through 2.11. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.   Graphic representations of 
the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through 
A-28. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts 
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample.  Results of the pocket penetrometer test results 
are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation 
of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the 
appropriate sample depths. 
Four Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are presented on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of the test 
results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Three Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136), including two Hydrometer 
tests (ASTM D422), were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation 
characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification.  Graphic presentations of the 
grain size distribution are provided on Plate B-2. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the shear strength parameters. The test results are presented on Plates B-3 
and B-4. 
Five laboratory Resistance (R) Value tests by Ninyo & Moore (ASTM D2844) 
were performed on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement 
support characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-5 through 
B-9.
