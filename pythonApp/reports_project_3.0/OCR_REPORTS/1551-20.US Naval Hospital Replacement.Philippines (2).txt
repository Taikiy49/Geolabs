INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of an additional geotechnical
engineering investigation performed for the proposed Project P 223
Hospital Replacement U S Naval Hospital at Subic Bay in Luzon
Republic of the Philippines
A geotechnical engineering investigation was performed previously
by our firm for the general project area Preliminary evaluation
disclosed extreme differences in topography and subsurface conditions in
the area Therefore the previous investigation was designed to
delineate the general conditions in the area to assist in the siting of
the proposed structures
Based on the information from the geotechnical study and the final
hospital complex layout the improvements were sited in an area with
potentially better soil conditions
The final hospital complex location has been situated partly over
the soft lowlying swamp level and partly over the high stiff ground
area This additional investigation was conducted to determine more
detailed subsurface information for final design of the structures and
improvements
The general location of the project site is shown on the Area Map
Plate 1 and Project Location Map Plate 2

The purpose of our investigation was to evaluate the subsurface
conditions at the site to provide pertinent soil data such as shear

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The project site is located within the Western Volcanic Intrusive
Province of Luzon and comprises Recent to Pliocene Quaternary Volcanic
deposits The E W Trending San Antonio Fracture Zone is probably the
closest identified major fault zone about 8 to 10 miles northerly of
the site
At the project site the volcanic deposits are primarily of
pyroclastic origin and consist of lithic tuffs volcanic breccia
agglomerate and volcanic mudflows lahar believed to have been
generated by explosive eruptions from Mount Natib Mount Santa Rita and
Mount Balakibok three of the major volcanic centers which comprise the
Zambales Bataan Volcanic Highlands
Examination of the geomorphology of the area leads to the
conjecture that the site is located on a marine deltaic flat located at
the toe of a rugged mountain terrain The lowlying area was cut during
low stands of the sea by streams originating in the mountainous areas to
the east of the site
Numerous glacial eustatic sea level variations caused the emergence
and submergence of the land mass that produced soil deposits at various
levels both above and below the present sea level

The site investigation carried out revealed that the surface
materials in the lowlying portions of the study area are mainly of a
low energy depositional estuarine or lagoonal environment which would be
encountered in a sheltered bay area such as Subic Bay
Owing to the low energy estuarine or deltaic environment of

Soil Improvement Program
In order to accelerate the settlements reduce the waiting time
and to compensate for some of the long term secondary
settlement a soil improvement program consisting of the
following is recommended
1 Vertical Drain The time required for the settlement to
occur is governed primarily by the distance that the water
in the soil pores must travel to drain out Normally the
water travels up or down in the profile until a permeable
layer is encountered The settlement time varies with the
square of the distance the water must travel drainage
path The presence of the vertical drain reduces the
drainage path the water must travel as it is squeezed out
of the soft soil Hence it greatly accelerates the rate
of settlement To be effective the drain has to extend
from the ground surface to the bottom of the soft layer A
schematic diagram of a typical vertical drain installation
is shown on Plate E 3

Different types of drain e g prefabricated and sand
drains are available However because of its
comparatively lower cost and relative ease of installation
we believe that prefabricated drains are preferrable for
this project These drains usually consist of a core of
plastic and a fiiter sleeve of paper fibrous material or
porous plastic Size of the drains is usually on the order
of 100 mm wide by 1 to 7 mm thick
The rate of improvement depends to a large extent on the
drain spacing Our estimated drain spacings required to
achieve 90 consolidation within a 12 month period is about
5 feet The drains are normally placed in a triangular
pattern
Since the use of vertical drains on granular soils is not
effective we recommend that the vertical drain

Liquefaction Evaluation
When a loose saturated sand deposit is subject to vibration such
as during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in
volume causing an increase in pore water pressure If drainage is
unable to occur rapidly enough due to the large extent of the sand
deposit to dissipate the build up in pore pressure the effective
stress internal strength of the soil is reduced Under sustained
vibration the pore water pressure build up could equal the overburden
pressure essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and
causing it to act as a viscous fluid This phenomenon is called
liquefaction The major factors affecting the liquefaction
characteristics of a soil are
1 Grain size distribution Fine and uniform sands are more prone
to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2 Initial relative density Loose sands are most susceptible to
liquefaction Liquefaction potential decreases with increased
relative density
3 Magnitude and duration of vibration Liquefaction potential
increases with increases in the magnitude and duration of the
earthquake
Our borings disclosed that the project site jis underlain at
shallow depth by a layer of very loose silty fine sand The thickness
of this layer varies between 10 to 30 feet As discussed in our seismic
site response study the hospital structure will be designed to resist a
design earthquake with a 10 probability of exceedence in 100 years
This design earthquake has been determined to have a magnitude of 7 6 on
the Richter scale Because of the very shallow water table almost at
the ground surface and the extremely loose consistency of the sand
layer we believe that it is very likely that during a strong earthquake
M 7 6 occurring at close distance to the site 10 to 20 miles this
layer of loose sand will liquefy

For the current investigation the subsurface conditions at the

Soil samples were obtained by driving either a 2 0 inch 0 0

depths

sampler was used The average hydraulic pressures in P S I required to
advance the tube into the soft sediments are shown at the appropriate
depths on the boring logs

soil and then rotated The change of the measured torque with angular
rotation was monitored Maximum torques of both the undisturbed and
remolded soil condition were recorded The test results are summarized

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
The laboratory testing for this project were partly performed by
our laboratory in Honolulu and partly by a laboratory in Manila
The moisture content unit weight and Atterberg Limit
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the
classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of the
moisture content and unit weight tests are presented on the Boring Logs
at the appropriate sample depths The results of the laboratory testing
program performed for the previous investigation are summarized on
Plates B 1 through 8 32
The Atterberg Limits are summarized on Plate B 33
In light of the fact that substantial amount of granular materials
were encountered during our soil investigation an extensive sieve
analysis program was performed Altogether 34 tests were performed on
samples from the swamp the high ground and the area at the toe of the
high ground The test results are summarized on Plates B 34 through
B 42
One moisture density test modified Proctor was performed on bulk
samples obtained from the high ground area The purpose of the test was
to determine the compaction characteristics of the material The test
result is summarized on Plate B 43
Three consolidation tests were performed on samples obtained from
the clayey layer The purpose of the test was to evaluate the
settlement characteristics The results of the tests are presented on
Plates B 44 through B 46
One direct shear test was performed on a remolded sample of the cut
material from the hillside The purpose of the test was to determine
the shear strength parameter of the soil The test result is presented
on Plate B 47
Three triaxial shear tests were performed on the soil samples from
the soft clayey layer These tests included the conditions of
unconsolidated undrained and consolidated undrained with pore pressure
measurements The purpose of the tests was to evaluate the shear
strength parameters of the soils The test results are presented on
Plates B 48 through 8 50

Settlement Analysis
The swamp area at the project site is underlain by layers of
compressible loose sandy and soft clayey materials A settlement
analysis was performed to evaluate the effect of filling this area
The analysis for the loose sandy layer was carried out by Janbu s
procedure which utilized the relationship between the porosity and the
compressibility of the soil Reference 3 The initial porosity of the
loose sandy soil for this project was estimated by its consistency and
initial stress condition The results of the analysis are summarized on
Plate C 1 which shows the total settlement of the sandy layer under
different thicknesses of fill For the rate of settlement of the sandy
layer we believe that a large majority of the total settlement would
occur within the period of construction
The analysis for the clayey layer was carried out by the use of the
computer program CONSOL developed by the Department of Civil
Engineering at the University of California at Berkeley The computer
program calculates the total and time rate of settlement for
one dimensional layered soil masses using an implicit finite difference
method
Two of the most important soil properties that the program requires
as input are the compression index and the coefficient of
consolidation For the current analysis these latter data were
obtained from twelve 12 one dimensional consolidation tests and
fifteen 15 time settlement plots
The results of the analysis can be divided into the following
catagories
1 Effect of the fill thickness on total primary settlement of
various thickness of soft deposit the results are summarized
on Plate C 2
2 Effect of thickness of soft tayer on the time required to
achieve 90 total primary settlement the results are
summarized on Plate C 3
3 Effect of thickness of soft layer on the percentage of total
primary settlement after one year the results are summarized
on Plate C 4

For the sandy layer the amount of secondary settlement will be

APPENDIX OD
Slope Stability Analysis
Slope stability analyses for the perimeter of the fill at the swamp
area and the high ground cut slope were performed
The analyses were carried out by using the computer program STABR
developed by the Department of Civil Engineering at the University of
California at Berkeley The program calculates the factor of safety by
using both the Ordinary Method of Slices and the Bishop s Modified
Method both of which involve circular failure surface STABR can also
perform pseudostatic analysis of the slope under seismic conditions
For the analysis of the perimeter fill slope the most critical
condition lowest factor of safety is at the end of the fill operation
when the underlying weak soil is under the load of the new fill As
time passes the underlying soil consolidates and gains strength
Therefore the factor of safety increases
For the current project stability analyses were performed for both
analysis an earthquake force represented by a seismic coefficient of
0 15g was applied to the sliding mass The results of the analyses are
presented on Plate D 1
Plate D 1 shows the variation of the factor of safety due to the
change in slope ratio It indicates that the critical slope ratio
which corresponds to a factor of safety of 1 5 during the end of filling
condition is about 5 1 With the use of this slope ratio the
perimeter slope is found to have adequate long term stability even under
seismic condition
For the analysis of the high ground slope the most important
factors that control the slope stability are those from the groundwater
and earthquake conditions
High groundwater condition would cause a decrease in slope
stability due to saturation of the soils which could reduce the strength
parameters of the soils and reduction in the effective stress in the
soil mass Additionally seepage forces would increase the driving
force resulting in a decrease in the slope stability On the other
hand earthquakes produce an additional driving force which increases
slope instability
