Project Considerations 
Koa Ridge is a master-planned development project located in Central Oahu 
between Waipio and Mililani Town on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. We understand the 
development will encompass roughly 770 acres and is planned for residential, 
commercial, educational, and recreational uses. Road “C” generally traverses the 
development in an east-west direction. 
Movements at the tops of Drain Manholes DMH-C4 and DMH-C5 were observed 
after installation. DMH-C4 has an invert elevation of +409.21 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) 
and connects with two 48-inch Reinforced Concrete Pipes (RCPs). DMH-C5 connects on 
top of a 10-foot by 8-foot box culvert with a culvert invert of about elevation +409.83 feet 
MSL at the manhole location. We understand it is desired to investigate the backfill and 
subgrade conditions at the two manhole structures to assist in determining the cause of 
the movements.

Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 and 102, extending to a depth of about 
40 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our borings generally encountered a surface pavement section about 1-foot thick 
consisting of 5 and 6 inches of asphaltic concrete underlain by dense sandy gravel.

Previous Investigation and Observations 
The elevation of the tops and invert elevations of the manhole structures were 
surveyed on May 12, 2021.  For DMH-C4, the top of the manhole structure elevation was 
+439.23 feet MSL and with an invert elevation of +408.66 feet MSL. The top of the 
manhole structure was close to the finished grade of the roadway, and the plan invert 
elevation for DMH-C4 is +409.2 feet MSL. Therefore, the as-built invert elevation was 
about 0.54 feet lower than the plan invert elevation. 
For DMH-C5, the top of the manhole structure elevation was +440.2 feet MSL and 
with the box culvert invert elevation of 409.59 feet MSL. The top of the manhole structure 
was close to the finished grade of the roadway.  The plan box culvert invert elevation for 
DMH-C5 was 409.71 feet MSL. Therefore, the as-built invert elevation was about 
0.12 feet lower than the plan invert elevation. 
Additional ground survey readings were taken in the area on July 27, 2021.  These 
additional survey readings were taken after the final paving of the roadway in this area.  
The additional survey readings indicated that the sidewalks and concrete curbs have 
settled up to 4.32 inches.  The largest settlement was measured on the sidewalk near 
Catch Basin CB-C8.  The settlement measured on the top of curbs at the northwest, 
northeast, and southwest portions of the intersection of Road “C” and Road “CC” ranged 
from 0.84 to 1.2 inches. 
Previously, we had noticed wet and soft ground conditions near the Modular 
Wetland Unit (MW-05) which connects to a catch basin (CB-C8). In addition, distress to 
the sidewalk area was observed. We understand that when the contractor installed the 
piping for the Modular Wetland Unit, the piping was backfilled with No. 3B Fine gravel up 
to 1 foot above the top of the pipes and leaving about less than 6 inches for the 
landscaping soil.

Findings From the Field Exploration 
Boring No. 101 was drilled adjacent to DMH-C4 and extended to a depth of about 
40 feet below the existing pavement surface. There was a zone of very soft and medium 
stiff fill material between 10 and 26.5 feet depths. The water contents ranged from 34 to 
40 percent which were much higher than the optimum water content of about 28 percent. 
The fill material at the bottom of the drain manhole structure was very dense. 
Boring No. 102 was drilled adjacent to DMH-C5 and extended to a depth of 40 feet 
below the existing pavement surface. A zone of medium stiff fill material was encountered 
between 6 and 30 feet depths. The water contents ranged from 31 to 44 percent, which 
were much higher than the optimum water content of about 28 percent. The fill material 
below the bottom of the box culvert structure was stiff and very dense. 
Potential Cause of the Drain Manhole Structure Movement and 
Wet Soil Conditions 
Based on the May 12, 2021 survey of the tops of the manhole structures, the as-
built invert of the manhole (DMH-C4) and box culvert (DMH-C5) were about 0.54 and 
0.12 feet lower than the plan invert elevations, respectively. Our borings drilled adjacent 
to the manhole structures encountered very dense materials at the bottoms of the drain 
manhole structure and the box culvert. Therefore, we do not believe that these structures 
settled and may have been constructed at these invert elevations. 
During construction, the manhole structures were backfilled with on-site clayey 
soils above the cushion layer to the bottom of the pavement section. Typically, it is difficult 
to thoroughly compact the on-site soils immediately adjacent to the manhole structures 
due to avoidance of damage to these structures. Therefore, the on-site soils against the 
sides of the manhole structures are not as tightly compacted compared to areas away 
from the manhole structures and may form a path where subsurface water can travel. 
As indicated previously, wet and soft ground conditions were encountered near the 
Modular Wetland Unit (MW-05). In addition, a large amount of accumulated water was 
encountered in the No. 3B Fine gravel backfill for the MW-05 system. The results of the 
laboratory testing performed on the recovered soil samples from the borings indicated 
that the water contents of the fill soil were much higher than the optimum water content 
from depths between 10 to 26.5 feet and 6 to 30 feet for DMH-C4 and DMH-C5,

Recommended Remedial Measures 
We understand that the roadway at these drain manhole structures was recently 
paved to finished grade. Since the loading on the roadway typically extends to a relatively 
shallow depth of 5 feet or less, we believe that remediation of the soft subgrade is not 
needed at this time. Instead, we recommend that the roadway be monitored for 
settlement. Visual observation or settlement survey readings should be performed or 
taken. Should excessive settlement occur, remedial measures for the roadway pavement 
and subgrade would need to be taken. 
It is important that additional subsurface water not be introduced into the subgrade, 
which may further soften the subgrade soils and cause distress to the roadway and 
existing surface and subsurface structures. The drain holes drilled into the CB-C8 catch 
basin structure would need to be maintained so subsurface water in the MW-05 system 
can flow out into the catch basin instead of backing up in the No. 3B Fine gravel backfill 
and surrounding soils. To reduce the potential for excess water in the catch basin backing 
up into the MW-05 system through the drain holes, we recommend that a one-way valve 
or flap be installed at the existing drain holes. 
Consideration should be given to removing the No. 3B Fine gravel used to backfill 
the gap between the Modular Wetland Unit and the curb and gutter, and backfilling with 
sand-cement grout. This will reduce the potential for excess water from softening the 
bottom of the curb and gutter, and from water entering into the aggregate base course 
layer should the drain holes be clogged and subsurface water accumulates in the No. 3B 
Fine gravel backfill of the MW-05 system. 
If the drain holes in the existing catch basin cannot be maintained, reconstruction 
of the MW-05 system should be performed. The piping for the MW-05 system should be 
backfilled with Controlled Low Strength Material (CLSM) or an imperious membrane 
(30 mil) and a filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equal) should be used around the piping and 
No. 3B Fine gravel. The imperious membrane should be sealed and made watertight at 
the pipe connections.
