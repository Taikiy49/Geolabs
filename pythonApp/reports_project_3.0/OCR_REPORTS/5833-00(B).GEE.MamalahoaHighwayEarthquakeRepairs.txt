generally encountered a surface layer consisting of fill ash and clinker clayey silt sand

support the new retaining wall structures For the new wall at Mile Post 8 9 we
recommend using bearing pressures of up to 30 000 and to evaluate the
extreme event limit state and strength limit state of the footings respectively These
bearing values are based on the footings bearing on or embedded into the hard basalt
formation For the new wall at Mile Post 4 4 we recommend using bearing pressures of
up to 20 000 and to evaluate the extreme event limit state and the strength limit
state respectively

wall and slope should be backfilled with CLSM The seismic lateral earth pressure may be
neglected in the wall design However the concrete wall should be designed for the
seismic inertia forces of the concrete wall and CLSM backfill
We understand that rock anchors will be utilized in the new wall foundation to resist
the seismic loading An average bond stress of 2 000 and may be used for the
clinker and hard basalt rock formation respectively Anchor testing should be performed
during construction to confirm the bond stress used in the design
Due to the potential for encountering cavities in the basalt formation we
recommend implementing a program of probing and grouting at the foundation locations of
the new wall structures during construction We recommend probing and grouting of the
new wall foundations to reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from
the collapse of potential cavities below the foundations

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers
an area of approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the eruptive
activity of five major shield volcanoes Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea some activity
during recent geologic time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 1803 and Mauna Loa
and Kilauea both still active
The project site is on the southern flank of Hualalai Mountain which makes up
the west central portion of the island Most of the near surface basaltic lava formation
at the site appears to be composed of pahoehoe type lava flow which spread and
ponded as it approached the ocean Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like
or billowy surface with an internal structure of vesicular rock In addition to the
pahoehoe type lava some land area of the project site appears to be locally covered by
a a lava flows The a a lavas are typified by the appearance of loose rubbly clinker
layers interspersed with seams of very dense massive basalt rock The dense basalt
rock layers represent the hard interior core of the individual lava flow
Cavities or voids are encountered commonly in pahoehoe lava especially the
more fluid pahoehoe lava Cavities form when the lava is still in a molten state and
represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and _ pockets
inter flow cavities Lava tubes are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling
flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance
longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities generally are closed cavity features that
are usually smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water
quite readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is
transmitted through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and _inter flow
contacts cavities and along clinker layers Clinker and cavities serve as the primary
water transmission features because of their high permeability

3 3 Alternate Retaining Wall System
Because of the high seismic loading 0 7g conventional retaining walls may not
be feasible at the new wall sites An alternate wall system consisting of a reinforced
concrete wall with Controlled Low Strength Material CLSM backfill may be considered
The embankment behind the wall should be sloped back at a one horizontal to
one vertical 1H 1V inclination or flatter and the area between the new wall and slope
should be backfilled with CLSM To reduce the lateral pressure to the new wall the
CLSM backfill should be placed in incremental layers behind the wall The embankment
slope should be keyed and benched into the natural ground In addition a subdrain
should be installed near the bottom of the slope as shown on the Alternate Retaining
Wall System Plate 3
We believe that the 1H 1V slope into the granular rocky embankment material is
a stable unit independent of the new concrete wall In addition we believe that the
static lateral pressure from the hardened CLSM backfill would be minimal Based on
the above we believe that the static lateral earth pressure against the concrete wall will
be minimal A minimal lateral earth pressure of 10 pcf may be used Also we believe
that the seismic lateral earth pressure may be neglected However the concrete wall
should be designed for the seismic inertia forces of the concrete wall and CLSM backfill
A horizontal coefficient of seismic inertia force of 0 5amax may be used provided the wall
will be allowed to move laterally by up to about 7 inches in the event of an earthquake
Lateral loads may be resisted by friction between the bottom of the foundation
and bearing soil and between the CLSM backfill and the embankment For the wall at
Mile Post 8 9 site a coefficient of friction of 0 70 and 0 56 may be used for the Extreme
Event and Strength Limit States respectively for footings bearing on the basalt rock
formation For the wall at Mile Post 4 4 site a coefficient of friction of 0 55 and
0 44 may be used for the Extreme Event and Strength Limit States respectively for
footings bearing on clinker For CLSM backfill against the granular embankment
material a coefficient of friction of 0 55 and 0 44 may be used for the Extreme Event

3 4 Rock Anchors
We understand that rock anchors may be utilized as part of the retaining wall
foundation to resist the overturning moment during a seismic event The rock anchors
will be installed at the heels of the walls
A rock anchor is constructed by drilling a borehole installing a steel tendon or
reinforcing bar into the borehole and grouting the borehole The basic components of a
grouted rock anchor consist of the anchorage and the bonded length of the steel tendon
or reinforcing bar The bonded length of the rock anchor is the portion of the steel
tendon or reinforcing bar that is directly bonded to the grout allowing it to transfer the
applied tensile load into the ground
We recommend locating the top of the lower bonded length about 2 to 3 feet
below the bottom of the footing level Subsequent to anchor testing the upper bonded
length should be grouted up to the bottom of footing level
In general the rock anchors would derive support principally from the bond
between the grout and the hard basalt rock with interbedded clinker layers encountered
at the project site The bond stress which is the pullout resistance per unit area of the
grout soil interface contact is variable depending on the type of soil and grout the
overburden stress and the construction procedures used in installing the rock anchors
For a rough estimate an average bond stress of 2 000 and 10 000 pounds per square
foot psf may be used for the clinker and hard basalt rock formation respectively The
bond stress used in the design will need to be confirmed in the field during construction

Rock Anchor Testing
In general we recommend performing creep testing on one rock anchor for the wall
at Mile Post 8 9 and on one rock anchor for the wall at Mile Post 4 4 before
construction of the remaining rock anchors The creep tests should consist of
subjecting the rock anchor to at least 100 percent of the design loads and the load
should be held for at least 8 hours
Pullout tests proof tests should be performed on all the remaining rock anchors
during construction to confirm the bond stresses used in the design We
recommend testing all of the constructed rock anchors for pullout The pullout tests
should consist of subjecting the rock anchors to at least 100 percent of the design
loads and the load should be held for at least 10 minutes
Creep and pullout tests on the rock anchors are integral parts of the design
Therefore we recommend conducting the creep and pullout tests under the
observation of a Geolabs representative
3 5 Foundation Probing
Based on our experience in the area voids and or cavities commonly exist in the
basalt rock formation at the project site To reduce the potential for loss of foundation
support resulting from the collapse of cavities below a footing we recommend
implementing a program of cavity probing and grouting at the foundation locations of the
retaining walls during construction
For estimating purposes we recommend drilling a probe hole for every
100 square feet of foundation area or at 10 feet on center along the wall foundations
The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to a depth of
at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of footing elevation If cavities and or voids
are encountered or suspected during the probing operation additional probe holes
should be drilled at closer spacing to aid in delineating the vertical and lateral extent of
the cavity and or void The probe holes and cavities discovered should be backfilled
with sand cement grout injected at low to moderate pressures We recommend utilizing

3 6 Site Grading
We anticipate that the grading work will generally consist of fills on the order of
about 18 feet thick or less relative to the existing ground surface for the construction of
the roadway realignment and widening A Geolabs representative should monitor site
grading operations to observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during

At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly Vegetation debris deleterious material and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off site or stockpiled in
a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with well compacted engineered fill In general the excavated soft and wet
soils may not be re used as a source of fill and backfill materials

After clearing and grubbing the existing ground surface should be scarified to a
depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction For pavement subgrades the
compaction requirement should be a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil same soil established in accordance with
AASHTO T 180 or ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
In general the on site silty gravel and sand and clayey silt encountered during
our field exploration should be suitable for use as general fill materials provided that the
maximum particle size is less than 3 inches in largest dimension The excavated
on site materials generated from excavations into the near surface materials may be
used as general fill or backfill materials provided that they are screened of the
over sized materials and or processed to meet the gradation requirements less than
3 inches in largest dimension In addition fill materials should be free of vegetation
and deleterious materials However the excavated soft and wet soils may not be
reused as a source of fill and backfill materials
Imported materials to be used as select granular fill should consist of
non expansive granular material such as crushed coral basalt or cinder sand The
select granular fill should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than
3 inches in largest dimension The material should also contain less than 15 percent
particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value
of 25 or more and should have a maximum swell value of 1 percent or less Imported fill
materials should be tested for conformance with these recommendations prior to
delivery to the project site for the intended use
Fills and backfills should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted
to at least 90 percent relative compaction Aggregate base course and aggregate
subbase materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the highway realignment and widening
sites by drilling and sampling eight borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 8
extending to depths ranging from about 20 to 25 feet below the existing ground surface
We drilled the borings using truck mounted and skid mounted drill rigs equipped with
continuous flight augers and coring equipment The approximate boring locations are
shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 1 and 2 2
Our engineer geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by
visual and textural examination in the field He monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1 Graphic
representations of the materials encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs
of Borings Plates A 1 through A 8
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of the basalt rock materials encountered at the site were obtained
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that
uses a hollow bit to cut into the basalt rock formation The basalt rock materials left in
the hollow core of the bit are mechanically recovered for examination and description
Rock cores were described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as
shown on the Rock Log Legend Plate A 0 2
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
One Direct Shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected soil sample
to evaluate the shear strength parameters The test results are presented on Plate B 1
Six Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on rock core
samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the basalt rock formation
The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D 1883 were performed
on bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils The samples were remolded to near the optimum moisture
content The test results are presented on Plates B 2 through B 4
