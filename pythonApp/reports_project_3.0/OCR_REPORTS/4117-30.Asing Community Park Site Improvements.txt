In accordance with our proposal dated we have performed a
geotechnical engineering exploration for the Asing Community Park Site Improvements project
in the District of Ewa on the Island of Oahu Hawaii This letter report summarizes the findings

for the proposed project These findings and discussions are intended for design input only
and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed project site is located within the existing Asing Community Park in the
District of Ewa on the Island of Oahu Hawaii It is proposed to construct playcourts which
include basketball volleyball and tennis courts Concrete walkways are planned which will
lead from the existing parking area to the proposed play courts as shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 In addition we understand that it is planned to install a lighting system
Structural details for the proposed light poles at the site were not available at the time
this report was prepared Based on our experience and the typical standard size light poles
we anticipate that the proposed light pole will be of galvanized steel and or aluminum
construction of approximately 25 feet in height Based on these typical conditions we
anticipate that the structural loading at the base of the light pole may be as follows
e Vertical load 500 pounds
Lateral load 1 000 pounds
Overturning moment 25 000 foot pounds
Special attention was given to the lateral load supporting characteristics of the
subsurface materials for the light pole foundation at the project site

PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the surface and
subsurface conditions at the project site The subsurface information obtained was used to
develop an idealized soil rock data set for the formulation of geotechnical engineering
recommendations pertaining to the design of the proposed Asing Community Park Site
Improvements project Our work was performed in general accordance with our fee proposal
dated Our scope of work included the following tasks and work efforts
1 Mobilization and demobilization of a truck mounted drill rig and two operators
to the project site and back
2 Drilling and sampling of three borings to depths of approximately 15 8 to 16 feet
below the existing ground surface for a total of 47 8 lineal feet below the existing
ground surface
3 Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the borings by a field
engineer from our firm
4 Laboratory testing of selected soil samples obtained during the field exploration
as an aid in classifying the materials encountered and evaluating the pertinent
engineering properties
5 Engineering analyses of the field and laboratory data for the purpose of
developing geotechnical recommendations pertaining to the design of
foundations concrete walkways site preparation and pavements for the
proposed project
6 Preparation of this report summarizing our work on the project and presenting
our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
7 Coordination of our overall work on the project by an engineer from our firm
8 Quality assurance of our work and client design team consultation by a principal
engineer from our firm
9 Miscellaneous work efforts such as drafting word processing and clerical
support

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The proposed project is located east of the existing West Loch Elderly Housing
Complex which is located at the northeastern corner of Renton Road and Fort Weaver Road
The proposed project is located in the Ewa District on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The site
is bounded by a parking lot to the south an extension of Renton Road to the west and a
drainage channel to the east and north as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The site is slightly sloping downward from west to the east with existing ground surface
elevations ranging from about 37 to 40 feet Mean Sea Level MSL Majority of the project
site was covered with lawn grass At the time of our investigation ground surface shrinkage
cracks were noted at various locations of the project site
FIELD EXPLORATION
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of about 15 8 to
16 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings drilled are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on Plate 3 Graphic representations of the materials encountered are
presented on the Logs of Borings Plates 4 through 6
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the
field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive
strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the Logs
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

LABORATORY TESTING
Selected soil samples from the field exploration were tested in the laboratory with an
emphasis on evaluating the moisture density and pavement support characteristics The
following laboratory tests were performed on selected soil samples obtained from the field
exploration
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed in accordance with
ASTM D 2216 and D 2937 respectively on selected soil samples as an aid in the
classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
One Atterberg Limits test was performed in accordance with ASTM D 4318 ona
selected sample of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil
Classification The test results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate 7
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR test was performed in accordance with
ASTM D 1883 on a bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils The results of the test are presented on Plate 8
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling three borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of about 15 8 to 16 feet below the
existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan

In general our field exploration disclosed that the project site is underlain by stiff to very
stiff silty clays with varying amounts of basaltic and coralline gravel near the ground surface
The silty clays grade from very stiff to hard with depth extending to the maximum depths
explored of about 15 8 to 16 feet below the existing ground surface
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings drilled during our field exploration

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The project site is underlain by stiff to very stiff silty clays with varying amounts of
basaltic and coralline gravel near the existing ground surface which grades to hard with depth
In consideration of the lateral load and moment demands acting on the proposed light pole
foundations we recommend that drilled shafts embedded in the very stiff to hard silty clays be
used to support the proposed lighting system We recommend that the drilled shafts consist
of 30 inch diameter shafts embedded a minimum of 5 feet below the lowest adjacent finished
grades for the proposed light pole foundations located on level ground conditions Detailed
discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering recommendations for design are
presented in the following sections of this report
Light Pole Foundations
In order to develop the required resistance to the lateral load and moment demands
the proposed light pole structures should be supported by a foundation system consisting of
cast in place concrete drilled shafts As previously indicated we envision that the proposed
light poles will be of galvanized steel and or aluminum construction of approximately 25 feet
in height Based on these typical conditions we anticipate that the structural loading at the
base of the light pole may be as follows
e Vertical load 500 pounds
e Lateral load 1 000 pounds
Overturning moment 25 000 foot pounds
Based on the anticipated structural loads for the new light poles we recommend that
the light pole foundations consist of cast in place concrete drilled shafts with a minimum
diameter of 30 inches Based on the anticipated structural loads and the subsurface
conditions encountered at the site we recommend that drilled shaft foundations with an
embedment length of not less than 5 feet below the design finished grades be used for the
new light pole structures located on level ground conditions If the actual loading of the light
pole is higher than the assumed structural loads the drilled shaft foundation should be
re evaluated for the new foundation demands
The cast in place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally from
skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding soils An allowable compressive load
capacity of up to 10 kips per shaft may be used for dead plus live loads and may be increased
by 50 percent for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces The anticipated settlements
of the light pole foundations due to compression loads are estimated to be on the order of less
than 0 25 inches We believe that the settlements are elastic and should occur as the loads
are applied

Lateral loads imposed on the light pole foundations may be resisted by resistance of
the shaft soil system to deflection Lateral load resistance of the drilled shaft is a function of
the stiffness of the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable deflection at the top
of shaft and induced moment in the shaft As indicated above each drilled shaft may be
subjected to a lateral load of 1 000 pounds with an associated overturning moment of up to
about 25 000 foot pounds at the ground surface Based on our foundation analyses the
maximum moment induced in the drilled shaft is about 25 000 foot pounds acting at the top
of the shaft Our lateral load analyses indicated that the estimated deflection at the top of the
drilled shaft should be less than 0 25 inches
A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 6 to 9 inch range should be used to provide
close contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Concrete should be placed
in a suitable manner to reduce the potential for segregation of the aggregates from the
concrete mix If precast concrete pole bases are used the gap between the pole base and
the surrounding soils should be backfilled with concrete or lean concrete A representative
from Geolabs should be present to observe the installation of drilled shafts during construction
to confirm the subsurface conditions and embedment depths
Concrete Walkways
We understand that it is planned to construct concrete walkways leading from the
existing parking lot to the proposed playcourts We recommend that a minimum 12 inch thick
layer of non expansive select granular fill be placed below the concrete walkways The
subgrade soils below the walkway should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above
the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
The select granular fill should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Crack control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways and at
right angle intersections Areas adjacent to slabs should be backfilled tightly against the slab
and graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding next
to the slab
Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and the proposed finished grades we anticipate that
minimal site grading work will be required for construction of the new improvements at the
subject site In general the area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly
cleared and grubbed at the on set of earthwork Vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of off site Soft and yielding areas
encountered should be over excavated to expose firm material and the resulting excavation
should be backfilled with well compacted fill The excavated soft soils should not be used as
fill material
Finished subgrades and areas designated to receive fill should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum

moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test
procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding
to the maximum dry density
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend
that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close cracks or prepared again as recommended
above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement
weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement
with well compacted fill
In general we envision that the excavated on site materials may be re used as fill or
backfill materials provided that deleterious materials are removed and that the excavated
on site materials are processed to a maximum particle size of about 3 inches Fill and backfill
materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in
level lifts of 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction
We understand that imported fill will be required for the project In general imported
fill should consist of non expansive select granular fill material such as crushed coral basalt
or cinder sand The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger
than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles
passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more
and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM
D 1883 Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs for conformance with these
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use
Cut and fill slopes planned at the site may be designed based on a slope inclination of
two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter Finished slopes should be protected as soon
as they are graded to reduce the potential for significant erosion of the finished slopes Slope
planting or other means of slope protection should be provided to reduce the potential for
significant erosion of the finished slopes

The subgrade soils should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to

Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry
surface water off the pavement Surface water ponding should not be allowed on or near the
pavement areas during or after construction
Utility Trenches
New utility lines and utility connections will likely be needed for the proposed project
In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials such as
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the initial trench
backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes
It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below
the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes mproper
trench backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils The backfill
materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in
about 8 inch loose level lifts and mechanically compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground subsidence Where trenches
are located below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement
grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the proposed playcourts and concrete walkways should
be sloped to shed water away from the slabs and foundations and to reduce the potential for
ponding Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should be avoided
Planters next to foundations and slabs should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains
to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils

These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation and slab recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be
properly backfilled against the slabs immediately after setting the concrete to reduce water
infiltration In addition drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should
be maintained to drain surface run off away from slabs and foundations

Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that Geolabs be retained to provide geotechnical engineering
services during construction of the proposed project The items of construction monitoring that
are critical requiring Special Inspection include observation of the light pole foundation
construction subgrade preparation and fill and backfill placement Other aspects of earthwork
construction should also be observed by a representative from Geolabs This is to observe
compliance with the design concepts specifications or recommendations and to expedite
suggestions for design changes that may be required in the event that subsurface conditions
differ from those anticipated at the time this report was prepared The recommendations
provided in this report are contingent upon such observations If the actual exposed
subsurface conditions encountered during construction are different from those assumed or
considered in this report then appropriate modifications to the design should be made
