INTRODUCTION
This pavement justification report presents the results of our geotechnical
engineering analyses for the design of new pavements for the proposed Mohouli Street
Extension project in the Punahoa area in the District of South Hilo on the Island of
Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the design of pavements only The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
We understand that a new roadway connecting Ainako Avenue to Mohouli Street
Mohouli Street Extension is planned in the Punahoa area of South Hilo on the Island of
Hawaii as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The proposed alignment commences near
the intersection of Ainako Avenue with Kaumana Drive proceeds east traverses Alenaio
Stream and Kukuau Street and terminates at the intersection of Mohouli and Komohana
Streets for a total length of about 6 650 feet 1 26 miles The new roadway will have two
lanes with an 80 foot wide right of way Based on the plan and profile drawings provided
we anticipate that the proposed roadway alignment will approximately follow the existing
terrain with the majority of the site grading consisting of fills of up to about 9 feet thick
Minor cuts of up to about 5 feet deep may be required in localized areas to achieve the
design finished grades for the proposed roadway construction Based on our experience
in the vicinity of the site it is anticipated that the majority of the proposed roadway
alignment may be underlain by a thin layer of surficial alluvial soils overlying basalt
formation at shallow depths
REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the activity of five shield
GEOLABS INC
W O 3553 00 Pavement Justification Page 3
volcanoes which include the following Kohala which has long been extinct Mauna Kea
which has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801
and Mauna Loa and Kilauea which are still active
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield
Volcano which has been built up by successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and
pyroclastic materials The near surface lava flows are from the Mauna Loa Volcano
overlying the Mauna Kea Shield The lava flows of the Mauna Kea Shield are believed to
be Pliocene to Pleistocene in age and belong to the upper member of the Hamakua
Volcanic Series The near surface lava flows of the Mauna Loa Shield may be on the
order of only 750 to 1 500 years old
The lava formation at the site appears to be pahoehoe flow which is characterized
by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous
rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lava These cavities were formed
when the lava was still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities
and inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava tubes are formed when molten
lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend
for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller
in horizontal extent
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The proposed Mohouli Street Extension project site is located in the District of
South Hilo on the Island of Hawaii The proposed alignment will extend the existing
Mohouli Street in a northwesterly direction traversing undeveloped land to connect with
the existing Ainako Avenue at Kaumana Drive as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general the project site slopes down gently to the northeast and eastward to the
ocean Existing ground surface elevations at the site range from about 420 feet Mean
Sea Level MSL at the planned intersection with Kaumana Drive to about 280 feet MSL
at the planned intersection with Komohana Street

The existing ground surface was generally observed to be composed of pahoehoe
lava flows that originated from the Mauna Loa Volcano located at a distance to the

broad basins where storm water runoff has deposited the generally thin alluvial
accumulations above the basalt rock formation
At the time of our field exploration the site was observed to be densely vegetated
with tall grass and small to medium size trees such as Haole Koa and Guava trees

dense basalt rock formation and some rocky fills consisting of cobbles and boulders
Based on our observation of the relatively level ground surface it appears that the area

6 inches in thickness Slow seepage of surface water emanating from the area upslope

Based on our observation of the topography and vegetation the broad relatively low area
located between about Station 4 50 and Station 25 00 appears to be subjected to some
surface water flow during rainfall events

Design Subgrade Conditions
Our field exploration program along the proposed roadway extension alignment
consisted of drilling and sampling a total of 13 borings designated as
Boring Nos 1 through 13 extending to depths ranging from about 15 to 40 5 feet below
the existing ground surface Two bulk samples designated as CBR 1 and 2 were
obtained from the near surface materials to evaluate their pavement support
characteristics The approximate locations of the drilled borings and near surface bulk
samples are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our field exploration along the proposed roadway extension generally
encountered relatively competent basalt rock formation from near the surface extending
to the maximum depth explored of about 40 5 feet below the existing ground surface
Detailed descriptions and graphic representations of the materials encountered from our
field exploration are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 13 of
Appendix A Laboratory tests were performed on selected soil samples and the results
are presented in Appendix B
Based on our field exploration and the current design concept we anticipate the
pavement subgrade soils will likely consist of compacted 6 inch minus granular fill
materials imported and or in situ basalt rock formation along the proposed roadway
extension alignment Based on our experience with similar granular materials we believe
that the 6 inch minus granular fill materials and the in situ basalt rock formation wouid
exhibit a Kentucky California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 20 or more Therefore a
design Resistance R value of 50 based on correlations by the State of Kentucky was
used in our pavement analyses for the subgrade materials

After clearing and grubbing the future pavement areas should be excavated

refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil determined in accordance with AASHTO Test Method T 180
ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
In areas where the pavement subgrades expose dense clinker materials or basalt
rock formation the subgrades should be proof rolled with a minimum 15 ton vibratory
roller for a minimum of six passes to help detect and collapse near surface cavities in lieu
of the scarification and compaction The vibratory drum roller should travel at a speed not
faster than 250 feef per minute If needed a leveling course consisting of aggregate
base course 1 inch minus may be provided on the prepared pavement subgrade prior to
the placement of the ACB The leveling course should be proof rolled to a dense and
unyielding condition prior to placement of the ACB The ACB material should consist of

Fill Materials
In general fills placed within the upper 3 feet of embankment fills below the
finished pavement grade should consist of well graded granular materials less than
6 inches in largest dimension structural fill The structural fill material should have
sufficient fine materials sands and gravel to prevent the occurrence of voids in the
compacted mass
Embankment fill materials from the existing ground up to 3 feet below the finished
pavement grade should consist of granular materials less than 12 inches in largest
dimension general fill Boulders i e rock fragments greater than 12 inches in largest
dimension up to 3 feet in maximum dimension may be placed in the embankment fill up
to 3 feet below the finished pavement grade Boulders greater than 18 inches in largest
dimension should not be placed within 6 feet of the slope face A typical fill section is
shown on Plate 3
Rock or boulder fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum
dimension of the majority of the rocks or 3 feet whichever is less Placement should be
by haul trucks traversing previously placed lifts and dumping at the edge of the currently
placed lift Spreading of the rock fill should be performed by a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or
similar heavy equipment to facilitate seating of the rock The rock fill should be watered
heavily during placement by water trucks traversing in front of the current rock lift face and
spraying water continuously during placement
The soft and or wet soils encountered should not be used as fill materials and
should be properly disposed of off site Excavated clinker materials if encountered may
be used as a source of fill material or blended with basalt rock fragments to provide a
relatively well graded fill material Cut materials generated from excavations into the
underlying basalt formation may be used as general or structural fill materials provided

Compaction Requirements
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of
rocks boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number of
passes of a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be
conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs On the basis of this testing program the number of passes
may then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction
At the start of the grading phase of the project a test section should be
constructed to evaluate the number of passes of a compactor needed to achieve the
desired level of compaction for the embankment fill The test section should be
40 by 20 feet at the top at least 3 feet high and should have a side slope of
1 5 horizontal to 1 vertical or flatter The test section should be constructed of
well graded granular materials less than 6 inches in largest dimension structural fill The
test section should be constructed in lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose lift thickness
Construction of the test section should be conducted under the full time observation by a
Hawaii Licensed Civil Engineer with a minimum of five years of experience in
Geotechnical Engineering

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling 13 borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 13 extending to depths of
about 15 to 40 5 feet below the existing ground surface using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools The approximate locations of
the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates
A 1 through A 13
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler or a 3 inch OD split barrel sampler with a 140 pound
hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last
12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
