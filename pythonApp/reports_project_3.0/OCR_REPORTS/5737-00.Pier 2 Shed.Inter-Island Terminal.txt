SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration, the harbor improvements site was generally
underlain by dense fill and medium dense beach deposit overlying coral reef materials
consisting of lagoonal/coralline detritus and coral formation. The top elevation of the coral
reef varied from approximately +0 to -9 feet MSL and extended to the maximum depth
drilled of about -23 feet MSL. At the time of our field exploration, we encountered
groundwater in the borings at depths of about 3 to 8 feet below the existing ground
surface. The measured groundwater levels generally correspond to about +3 to
+0 feet MSL.
Based on subsurface conditions encountered in the borings and based on the
structural load demands, we recommend supporting the new 45-foot floodlight pole on a
deep foundation system consisting of 18-inch diameter cast-in-place concrete drilled shaft.
In general, the drilled shafts for the new light pole should extend to about 15 feet below the
bottom of footing elevation.
Due to the specialty foundation work and potential variation of subsurface soil
conditions, special provisions should be given to the qualifications of the drilled shaft
sub-contractor and quality assurance during the drilled shaft foundation installation.
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific
design recommendations.

SECTION 1.0 - GENERAL
1.1. Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration and
engineering analyses performed for the proposed Demolition of the Pier 2 Shed and
Miscellaneous Site Work, Inter-Island Barge Terminal project within the Kahului Harbor
on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the
Project Location Map, Plate 1.
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations based
on our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses. The
recommendations presented herein are intended for the design of light pole foundations
and site works only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to
the limitations noted at the end of this report.
1.2 Project Considerations
The project consists of two packages including improvements of Pier 2 and site
works along Ala Luina Street at Inter-Island Barge Terminal within the Kahului Harbor
on the Island of Maui, Hawaii, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
The improvements of Pier 2 include the demolition of the existing shed to create
more space for Young Brothers and installation of a new 45-foot high floodlight mounted
on a 5-foot high concrete pedestal. The improvements will also require installing an
8-inch waterline and electrical conduits. The site work along Ala Luina Street consist of
installing new 45-foot high light poles mounted on 5-foot concrete pedestal, and
improving the existing pavements with new fencing and a new walkway for pedestrian
access.

SECTION 2.0 - SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2.1 Regional Geology
The Island of Maui is the second largest of the Hawaiian Islands and
encompasses approximately 728 square miles of land area. The Island of Maui was
formed by two volcanic mountains; the older Puu Kukui of West Maui (Tertiary Epoch)
and more recent Haleakala of East Maui (Pleistocene Epoch). A broad low elevation
plain, the Maui Isthmus, was located between these two volcanoes and formed by the
coalescence of the lava flows emitted from Haleakala. Volcanic activity began on the
two mountains in the Pleistocene Era and generally ended by the Holocene Era. During
the evolution of the Island of Maui during the Pleistocene, the island experienced
episodes of partial submergence and emergence related to the last significant Ice Age
sea level fluctuations. During the Pleistocene time of significantly lower sea levels, the
Island of Maui was once connected as a single landmass with the Islands of Molokai,
Lanai, and Kahoolawe.
Haleakala was generally built in three distinguishable series of lava flows. The
lower unit, the Honomanu Volcanic Series (late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Epoch)
consists of thinly-bedded, tholeiitic basaltic pahoehoe and a’a lavas. These lavas tend
to be very permeable and carry basal water. Overlying this unit is the Kula Volcanic
Series (early Pleistocene Epoch) that is primarily composed of thicker alkalic a’a flows,
which may contain many inter-stratified, thin ash-soil layers. A long quiescent period
followed, during which time canyons were carved in the volcano, although a few
eruptions may have occurred during this erosion interval in addition to erosion due to
eustatic changes (higher and lower stands of the sea level). At the end of the rest
period, copious flows erupted along the southwest and east rifts. This is the third and
upper rock unit, known as the Hana Volcanic Series (Pleistocene Epoch). The lavas
range in composition from ultra-basic olivine porphyries to non-porphyritic, alkalic
basalts.
The project site is near the northern end of the island or on the lower northern
slope of the East Maui Volcano (Haleakala).

2.2 Site Description
The harbor improvements project is located at Pier 2 at the Inter-lsland Barge
Terminal within the Kahului Harbor, on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The Demolition of the
Pier 2 Shed is situated in the middle of the Pier 2 extending to the southeastern end of
the Pier 2. The Site Work site begins from the western end of Ala Luina Street
traversing the north near Pier 3, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
Based on the information shown on the provided as-built drawings, we
understand that the existing shed structure is partially supported on the fill and on the
existing pile supported concrete pier deck. Based on the current design concept, the
new light pole foundation will be installed in the fill area. The entire Pier 2 site surface

The Miscellaneous Site Work site is situated along Ala Luina Street. The existing
Ala Luina Street is a two-lane roadway. The barge terminal is on the western side of the
street while the private properties are on the southern side.
The project site is relatively flat with ground elevations varying from +8 to
+10 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).

SECTION 3.0 - DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration, the project sites were generally underlain by
surface fill and beach sand overlying coral reef materials. The coral reef materials
consisted of lagoonal deposit, coralline detritus and coral formation. Although highly
weathered coral formation was only encountered at Boring No. 4 at depth of about
14 feet below the ground, the hard coral formation may occur in the other areas beyond
our borings at shallow depths. Groundwater was encountered at depths of about 4 to
8 feet below the ground surface, equivalent to the about Elevations +3 and +0 feet MSL.
Based on subsurface conditions encountered at the site and relatively high
loading demands, a deep foundation system consisting of 18-inch diameter
cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts is recommended to support the new 45-foot light
poles. The drilled shaft foundation may be designed to extend to a depth of about
15 feet below the bottom of the footing elevation to provide sufficient vertical and lateral
resistance.
Detailed discussions and recommendations for design of foundations and site
work are presented in the following sections of this report.
3.1 Light Pole Foundations
The following loading information was provided by the structural engineer for
45-foot high floodlight pole.

Based on our engineering analyses and the above structural loads, we
recommend supporting the proposed 45-foot light poles on a deep foundation system
consisting of 18-inch diameter cast-in-place drilled shafts. The drilled shafts should
extend a minimum of 15 feet below the bottom of footing to develop a sufficient lateral
resistance and for an allowable compressive load capacity of about 30 kips with factor
of safety of 2.0.

In general, the cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support
primarily from skin friction between the drilled shaft and the surrounding soil/coral
materials. The end-bearing component of the drilled shafts has been discounted in our
analysis due to difficulties associated with obtaining a clean bottom during construction
in the drilled shafts.
Lateral loads imposed on the light pole foundation may generally be resisted by
deflection of the shaft-soil system. Lateral load resistance of the drilled shaft is a
function of the stiffness of the surrounding soil, the stiffness of the pile, allowable
deflection at the top of the pile, and the induced moment in the pile. The lateral load
analyses were performed using the program LPILE-plus for Windows, which is a
microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference, laterally loaded deep foundation
program originally developed at the University of Texas at Austin. The program solves
for deflection and bending moment along the deep foundation as a function of depth
under vertical, lateral loads and bending moment, applying on top of the shaft.
The analyses were carried out with the use of non-linear “p-y” curves to
represent soil moduli at various depths. The analyses were performed for laterally
loaded 18-inch diameter drilled shaft with associated vertical load and over-turning
moment, as previously mentioned. We envision that top of the drilled shaft foundation
will be extended to the ground surface. Therefore, the loads were applied on top of the
drilled shaft. The following table summarizes the results of lateral load analyses

SECTION 3 — DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our foundation analyses, the maximum moment induced in the drilled
shaft slightly increases to 66,185 pound-feet within a depth of about 2 feet below the top
of the shaft. Our lateral load analyses indicated that the estimated lateral deflection at
the top of the drilled shaft should be less than % inch.
Settlement of the drilled shaft foundation will result from elastic compression of
the shaft and subgrade response of the foundation embedded in the soils/coral reef
materials. Total settlements of the drilled shafts under the structural loads are estimated
to be on the order of less than 0.5 inches. We believe that a significant portion of the
settlement is elastic and should occur as the loads are applied.
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend, to a significant extent, on
the frictional resistance between the shaft and the coral reef materials and/or
surrounding soils. Therefore, proper construction techniques especially during the
drilling operations are important. The contractor should exercise care in drilling the shaft
holes and in placing concrete into the drilled holes.
Boulders and hard coral formation may be encountered during shaft excavation
in localized areas within the fill and coral reef materials. The contractor should expect
hard drilling if the boulders and coral are encountered. The drilled shaft contractor will
need to have the appropriate equipment and tools to drill through these types of natural
obstructions, where encountered. A sufficient contingency fund should be included in
their bids.
Encountering the localized soft/loose zones should be anticipated within the
beach deposits and coral reef materials. Due to the soft and/or loose consistency of
these materials, caving-in and/or sloughing of these materials will likely occur during the
drilling operations. To reduce the potential for caving-in, temporary casing of the drilled
holes may be required during the drilled shaft installation or the use of drilling fluids
(mineral and/or polymer slurry) may be necessary to maintain the integrity of the drilled
hole during drilled shaft installation.

Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids may have a significant effect on the
supporting capacity of the drilled shaft; therefore, use of drilling fluids should be
specifically approved by the engineer upon evaluation of the type of drilling fluids
proposed.
Care should be exercised during removal of the temporary casing to reduce the
potential for "necking" of the drilled shaft. Therefore, a minimum 5-foot head of concrete
above the bottom of the casing or adequate concrete head to counter the hydrostatic
pressures should be maintained during removal of the casing. The anticipated
groundwater level at the project site may also pose construction difficulties since proper
observation of the sides and bottoms of the drilled shaft may not be possible.
Because of the anticipated groundwater table within the depths of the drilled
shaft excavations, concrete placement by tremie methods will be required during drilled
shaft construction under such conditions. The concrete should be placed in a suitable
manner by displacing the water in an upward fashion from the bottom of the drilled hole.
Fresh/sound concrete should be visible at the top of the temporary casing or at the
ground elevation prior to termination of the concrete pour. A low-shrink concrete mix
with high slump (8 to 9-inch slump range) should be used to provide close contact
between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils. The concrete should be placed in a
suitable manner to reduce the potential for segregation of the aggregates from the
concrete mix.
In addition, the concrete should be promptly placed after drilling (within 24 hours
after drilling of the holes) to reduce the potential for softening of the sides of the holes.
Furthermore, drilling adjacent to a recently constructed shaft (within three shaft
diameters of the recently constructed drilled shaft) should not commence until the
concrete for the recently constructed drilled shaft has cured for a minimum of 24 hours.
We anticipate that the drilled shaft installation will require a highly experienced
drilled shaft subcontractor to install the drilled shaft foundations. Therefore,

3.2 Utility Trench
We understand that new electrical duct lines and a new 8-inch waterline will be
required to service the proposed new lighting system and as part of the site
improvements. In general, granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of clean sand or S4C
is recommended under the duct lines. Free-draining granular materials, such as clean
sand or S4C, should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 6 inches
above the duct lines to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the
compaction effort of the backfill, thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes. It is
critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes.
Improper backfill material and placement procedures could result in backfill settlement
and pipe damage.
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level of 6 inches above the duct
lines to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on-site granular soils or
imported select granular fill material. The backfill should be placed in maximum 8-inch
level loose lifts, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and
mechanically compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the
potential for future ground subsidence. Where trenches are below pavement areas, the
upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water
content corresponding to the maximum dry density.

The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon
information obtained from field borings. Variations of conditions between and beyond
the field borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not
become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will

gradual transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown

noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in rainfall, perched groundwater
conditions, stream water level, and other factors.

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling four borings,
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, extending to depths of about 16 to 31 feet below
the existing ground surface. The approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown
on the Site Plan, Plate 2 and borings were drilled using truck-mounted drilling
equipment equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools.
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist, who observed the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed by visual
observation and testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic
representations of the materials encountered in the borings drilled are presented on the
Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-4.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving
a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In
addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 or 24-inch
drive are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths.
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample. Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937)
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties. The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
One Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D 4318) was performed on a selected soil
sample to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations of the test
results are provided on Plate B-1.
Five Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 and C 136) were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution of the soils are
provided on Plate B-2.
