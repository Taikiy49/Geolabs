REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas
banking against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad plateau
referred to as the Schofield Plateau
GEOLABS INC
W O Page 5
The lava flows on the plateau have undergone in situ chemical weathering to depths
of 50 to 150 feet and are characterized by red highlights in the soil This in situ chemical
weathering of the lava has formed a mantle of residual soils which grade to saprolite over
basalt rock formation with depth Based on radiometric measurements the decomposition
of these soils has taken place over an approximate 1 2 million years In general saprolite
is composed mainly of silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks
The deep narrow gulches such as Waikakalua Kipapa and Kaukonahua are the result of
this in situ weathering and stream erosion Spheroidally weathered boulders are also
indicative of this type of weathering and typical in the Schofield Plateau

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the proposed subdivision site is generally underlain by
stiff clayey soils extending to the maximum depth explored of about 21 5 feet below the
existing ground surface Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site
we believe that slab on grade foundations with thickened edge footings bearing on the
recompacted clayey soils or compacted fill material may be used to support the proposed
new residential structures
Our laboratory test results suggested that the near surface clayey soils exhibit slight
to low shrink swell potential when subjected to normal fluctuations in the soil moisture
contents To reduce the potential for distress to the future lightly loaded slabs on grade
resulting from the swelling of the near surface soils we recommend that the subgrade soils
be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above
the optimum moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Use of the vibratory action of a compactor should be carefully controlled to
avoid inducing a pumping subgrade condition The slab subgrade should be kept moist and
free of shrinkage cracks prior to placement of cushion fill and or slab concrete
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered along the proposed off site drain
and sewer alignments we anticipate that conventional earthmoving equipment may be

Building Foundations
In general we envision that slab on grade foundations with thickened edge footings
will be used to support the proposed new residential structures anticipated for the subject
project Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by
near surface clayey soils with slight to low expansion potential when subjected to normal
moisture fluctuations To reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded
slabs on grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of the clayey soils due to changes in
the moisture content we recommend that the subgrade soils be properly prepared in
accordance with the recommendations presented in the Site Grading section of this report
The prepared subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist prior to final placement of
concrete Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend that the soils be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks Saturation and
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage
may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted
engineered fill
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be
used for the design of shallow foundations bearing on the recompacted clayey soils or
compacted fill material This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
GEOLABS INC
W O Page 9
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 38 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted clayey soils or
compacted fill materials Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated
using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf
This assumes that the soils around the footings are well compacted or undisturbed
Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of
soil should be neglected
The perimeter thickened edges of the slabs should be embedded a minimum of
12 inches below the lowest adjacent finish grade In addition the base of the thickened edge
should have a minimum width of 12 inches For footings constructed near tops of slopes or
on sloping ground the footings should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum
horizontal setback distance of 8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the
face of the slope Footings located adjacent to planned or existing retaining walls should
be embedded deep enough to avoid surcharging the retaining wall foundations or the
planned retaining walls should be designed to resist the additional structural loads
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements the foundation
should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the
bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation
settlement
The house pads for Lot and 95 are situated next to steep natural or fill
slopes more than 20 feet high House foundations located close to such slopes may be
subjected to downhill creep movements Deeper footings or pipe piles may be required for
portions of these houses Further evaluation is recommended when details for the houses
of these lots are available

In general the bottom of footings should bear on the very stiff to hard near surface
soils The bottom of the footing excavations should be recompacted to at least 90 percent
relative compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to
placement of reinforcing steel or concrete If soft and or loose materials are encountered
at the bottom of footing excavations the material should be over excavated to expose firm
materials The over excavation should be backfilled with the approved on site soils
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom should
be extended down to bear on the underlying competent material Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with procedures described in ASTM
Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density

recommendations we estimate that total settlements of footings supported on the
recompacted stiff on site soils or new compacted fills to be on the order of about 1 inch
We estimate that the differential settlements between adjacent foundations to be on the
order of about 0 5 inches

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic pressures
A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of permeable
material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation placed
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations facing down at the base of the
wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used
instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also be
hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of the on site clayey silt or
select granular fill material Unless covered by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches
of backfill should consist of low expansion relatively impervious soils to reduce water
infiltration behind the walls

of the clayey soils
Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration we believe
that the planned cut slopes will expose the very stiff to hard clayey soils In general
the cut slopes planned at the project site may be designed with a slope inclination of
2H 1V or flatter We recommend that Geolabs be consulted for planned cut slope
where a cut slope inclination steeper than 2H 1V is desired
Permanent fill slopes constructed using the on site materials or imported fill
materials may also be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fills
placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the
existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations
should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers
in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations Fill slopes should
be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a
well compacted slope face In addition slope planting should be provided as soon
as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes

Pavement Design
New asphaltic concrete pavements will be required for the proposed subdivision
development The anticipated subgrade soils for the proposed new pavements as
encountered in our borings generally consist of very stiff to hard clayey silt soils Based
on our field exploration and laboratory testing the on site soils appear to have CBR values
ranging from 9 to 38 with expansion values of less than 1 percent Considering the light to
medium traffic anticipated for the new pavement areas we believe that the following
preliminary pavement design may be used for budgeting purposes
Fiexible Pavements
2 0 inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 inch Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
6 0 inch Aggregate Subbase 95 Percent Relative Compaction
14 0 inch Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade
The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction Subgrades should be
thoroughly moistened and kept moist until covered with the pavement surface course We
recommend that CBR and density tests be performed on the actual subgrade soils
encountered during roadway construction to confirm the adequacy of the above section
The recommended section also assumes that adequate drainage will be provided for the
paved areas Weepholes should be provided in drainage catch basin structures to relieve
water which may accumulate in the subgrade and cause pumping failure of the pavement
The weepholes should be installed at the bottom elevations of both the aggregate base
course and subbase course layers A filter fabric should be placed at the weepholes to
reduce the potential for migration of the aggregate base course and subbase materials
from entering the catch basins
The aggregate base course and subbase should consist of crushed basalt
aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction In general the
paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry

Excavations

Earth Pressure Loads on Pipes
Loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the trench the size of the pipes
the unit weight of backfill material and the frictional resistance between the backfill
material and the trench walls To calculate the vertical loads on the buried pipes an
average unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic foot pcf for backfill material and a
coefficient of friction of 0 3 are recommended Earth forces upon the pipe generally
increase rapidly with the width of the trench Therefore the width of the trench should
be kept to a minimum Traffic loads on the buried pipes if applicable should also be
considered for the portions of the pipes located in roadway areas
Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a great effect upon the load
supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore pipe bedding is an important design
consideration In general a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation or similar free draining material is
recommended under the pipes and manhole structures located over stiff dense soil
conditions If soft and or loose soils are encountered at the invert elevations of the
utility lines and manhole structures we recommend that an additional 24 inches of
No 2 Rock ASTM C 33 No 4 gradation wrapped in a filter fabric Mirafi 180N or
equivalent be provided below the new pipe and manhole structures for more uniform
support under such conditions The granular bedding should extend beyond the sides
of the pipe a minimum width of one fourth 1 4 the outside pipe diameter A typical
section of the trench detail is presented on Plate 3 Before the placement of bedding
material the excavated trench bottom should be observed by a representative from
Geolabs to confirm whether firm materials are exposed at the bottom of the trench or
if the bedding should be extended deeper as recommended above

Backfill
In general trench backfill should be performed in accordance with the Hawaii
Standard Specifications for Road Bridge and Public Works Construction 1994
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation should be used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the
pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce compaction of the
backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes Compaction by water
ponding or jetting should not be allowed for this project Improper trench backfill could
result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on site soils or select fill
material The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content placed in maximum 8 inch horizontal loose lifts and mechanically compacted
to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future
ground subsidence Where trenches are located below pavement areas the upper
3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to at
least 95 percent relative compaction
Pipe Settlement
Primary settlement of the new pipe installation is generally caused by the difference
in the unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted
backfill material placed over the pipeline The net increase in loading may cause
settlement of the underlying subsoils below the trench invert Based on our
exploration and analyses we believe that small primary settlements less than about
1 inch may be anticipated The above estimate assumes that proper construction
procedures and good workmanship will be engaged during construction Additional
settlements could occur if improper trench support procedures are used

Drainage
The finished grades outside the houses should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems
be installed around the houses and that the discharge be diverted away from the foundation
and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should
also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms
and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils Drainage swales
should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain surface run off away from
the slabs and foundations
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent loss of strength and or heaving of the near surface clayey soils The foundation
excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Three one inch ring swell tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate
the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results of the tests are summarized on
Plate B 1
Two sets of corrosion tests including pH ASTM G 51 and minimum resistivity
ASTM G 57 were performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration
The results of these tests are summarized on Plate B 2
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentations of the test results are
provided on Plate B 3
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 4 and B 5
