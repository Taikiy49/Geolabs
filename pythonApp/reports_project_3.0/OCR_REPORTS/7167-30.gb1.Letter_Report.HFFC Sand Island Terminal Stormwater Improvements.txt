REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of the western 
third of the island, while the younger Koolau shield forms the majority of the eastern 
two-thirds of the island. It is believed that Waianae Volcano became extinct while Koolau 
Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in 
Central Oahu. 
The Koolau Volcanic Shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by 
thinly-bedded basaltic lava flows. The main shield-building activities ceased 
approximately 2.5 million years ago. Evidence from drilled wells indicates that the Island 
of Oahu has subsided by as much as 1,200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic 
activity (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970).

During that period of subsidence, coral-algal reefs began to grow on the southern 
coast of the Island of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth. The 
growth of the reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence. A series of lagoons 
formed behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated 
in the lagoons (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970). 
During the later Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred 
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great 
continental glaciers advanced, the level of the ocean fell since there was less water 
available to fill the oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded or melted, global 
sea levels rose because more water was available. The land mass of Oahu remained 
essentially stable during these changes, and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. 
These glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and 
lower relative to the present sea level of Oahu. 
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of 
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations, and deposition of lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal 
deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials. In addition, renewed subaerial 
erosion of the upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under 
relatively high-energy conditions. 
During periods of no significant sea level changes, continued stream action 
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal 
sediments. During these stable periods, the growth of fringing coral/algal reefs formed 
distinct ledges of reef limestone at elevations roughly conforming to the level of the sea 
during that period. About 15,000 years ago, a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred. 
During that rise, the valleys in the project area were submerged. In the last 10,000 years 
or so, the sea level has adjusted to its present stand. Terrigenous and marine sediments 
have continued to accumulate in low-energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting 
in thick deposits of soft/loose sediments along the coast in areas that were formerly 
valleys and drainageways. 
The area north of the project site is geologically situated on the southwestern flank 
of the Koolau Volcanics. These volcanics are composed of basaltic lava flows of early 
Pleistocene Age. The basalt is overlain by a complex inter-layered system of terrestrial 
and marine sedimentary deposits. During the later Pleistocene Age, the sea level rose 
and fell several times as a result of glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulting in local swamp 
conditions, which were later superimposed by more recent deposits of terrestrial detritus 
of sand, gravel, silt and clay eroded from the older rock of the area. In recent time, the 
efforts of man have shaped the low-lying land to its present form by the placement of fill 
in the low-lying area.

The Honolulu Coastal Plain is composed of interbedded alluvial, marine, and 
post-erosional volcanic deposits. There is evidence that a basaltic flow of the Honolulu 
Volcanic Series, possibly originating from the Makuku vent in Nuuanu or from Punchbowl, 
reached the eastern portion of what is now Sand Island. 
During the Waipio stand of the sea at Elevation –60 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL), 
this layer of basalt would have provided a resistant surface, which could have diverted 
the flows of Nuuanu and Kapalama Streams to the west. The diversion of these 
two streams would have resulted in the deposition of extensive subaerial sediments 
consisting of alluvial clays and silts. These exposed alluvial deposits would have become 
consolidated due to seasonal moisture changes. 
Subsequently, as the sea level rose to the Waimanalo stand of the sea 
(at Elevation +25 feet MSL) and then fell to its present level, the stream-laid alluvium was 
buried beneath backreef deposits of sands as a fringing coral reef grew and emerged on 
the seaward margin of the island. Land reclamation projects in the past century have built 
Sand Island and the surrounding areas to its current topography. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
five borings, designated as Boring Nos. 4 through 8, extending to depths of about 6.5 to 
11.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Boring Nos. 4 and 5 encountered 3 to 4 inches of asphaltic concrete underlain by 
2 to 5 inches of sandy gravel fill.  The sandy gravel fill was underlain by fill consisting of 
medium dense silty/gravelly sands and medium stiff sandy silt extending to depths of 
about 3.5 to 5.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  Below the fills, lagoonal deposits 
consisting of very soft silty clay and very loose silty sand were encountered extending to 
a depth of about 6.5 feet below the existing ground surface.   
Boring Nos. 6 through 8 encountered fill consisting of medium dense silty/gravelly 
sand extending to depths of about 2.5 to 3.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Below 
the silty/gravelly sand fill, we encountered very loose to loose fill consisting of silty/gravelly 
sand extending to depths of about 5.5 to 6.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  The 
fills were underlain by lagoonal deposits consisting of very soft to soft silty clay extending 
to a depth of about 11.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  
We encountered groundwater in the borings at depths of about 3.7 to 4.3 feet 
below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration. It should be noted 
that groundwater levels are subject to change due to tidal fluctuations, rainfall, time of 
year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors.

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered medium dense fills that graded to very 
loose to loose with depth.  The fills extended to depths of about 3.5 to 6.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface.  Below the fills, very soft to soft lagoonal deposits were 
encountered in the borings extending to the maximum depth explored of 11.5 feet. 
Groundwater was encountered in the drilled borings at depths of about 3.7 to 4.3 feet 
below the existing ground surface. 
We understand that a new paved perimeter road is planned as part of the 
stormwater improvements project. Detailed discussions and recommendations for the 
design of the new paved perimeter road are presented in the following sections.

planned for the project.  Based on the existing topography, we believe that the new

The subgrade soils under the pavement areas should be scarified to a depth of 
about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Soft and/or loose soils 
encountered at the pavement subgrade should be over-excavated to the underlying firm 
or dense materials. The resulting over-excavation should be backfilled with 
well-compacted fill. 
The aggregate base course should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates and 
should conform to the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge 
Construction (2005). An accredited testing laboratory should test the imported fill 
materials for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site 
for the intended use. 
The aggregate base course should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage 
of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D1557.  Optimum moisture is the 
water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.   
Paved areas should be sloped, and drainage gradients should be maintained to 
carry surface water off-site. Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site 
during or after construction. A subdrain system is recommended to collect the excess 
water from landscaping irrigation. 
Site Preparation 
We anticipate that the grading work for the project will consist of minimal cuts and 
fills.  At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be cleared 
and grubbed thoroughly. Surface vegetation and other unsuitable materials should be 
removed and disposed of properly off-site. Soft and yielding areas encountered during 
clearing below areas designated to receive fill and/or future improvements should be 
over-excavated to expose firm material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled 
with well-compacted fill. The excavated soft soils should be disposed of properly off-site 
and/or used in landscape areas where appropriate. 
After clearing and grubbing, cut subgrades and areas designated to receive fills 
should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture, and recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.

Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage 
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. 
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density.  
In general, the on-site materials generated from the excavations may be used as 
a source of fill or backfill materials, provided that they are screened of the over-sized 
materials and/or processed to less than 3 inches in the largest dimension.  The excavated 
on-site materials consisting of the soft/loose lagoonal deposits should not be used as fill 
or backfill materials. 
Imported materials required for site filling should consist of select granular fill 
material, such as crushed coralline and/or basaltic materials. The materials should be 
well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no greater than 3 inches in the largest 
dimension. In addition, the materials also should contain between 10 and 30 percent 
particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The materials should have a CBR value of 30 or 
higher, and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with 
ASTM D1883.  
Fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, 
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction, as appropriate. Aggregate base course 
should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in level 
lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or 
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or ponding 
should not be allowed to compact the fills.  Where compaction is less than required, 
additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture content, as 
necessary, to obtain the specified compaction.

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling five borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 4 through 8, extending to depths of about 6.5 to 11.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. Five bulk samples of the near-surface soils, 
designated as Bulk-4 through Bulk-8, were obtained to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the near-surface soils. The approximate boring and bulk sample 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.  Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-5. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the boring drilled in general 
accordance with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the boring drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required 
for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample 
depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the Logs of 
Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of the test results is 
provided on Plate B-1. 
Four Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distributions is provided on Plate B-2. 
Five Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557 B) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships. The test results are presented on Plates B-3 through B-7. 
Five laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
selected bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-8 through B-12.
