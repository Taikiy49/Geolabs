SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration at the proposed new finger pier and bulkhead alignment 
generally encountered loose lagoonal deposits extending to about Elevation –20 feet MSL. 
Below the loose compressible deposits, a hard coral ledge underlain by medium dense to 
dense coralline detritus materials were generally encountered and extended to the 
maximum depth explored of about 150.5 feet below the water surface. 
Based on our field exploration and engineering analyses, we recommend using 
20-inch octagonal, precast prestressed concrete piles for foundation support of the new 
finger pier.  An allowable compressive load capacity of up to 65 tons (130 kips) may be 
used for foundation design of the above-recommended piles.  We believe that the piles 
would derive support from skin friction and end-bearing on the medium dense to dense 
coralline detritus materials starting at about Elevation -30 to -60 feet MSL. 
A steel sheet pile bulkhead with tie-rods and deadman anchors is planned along 
the perimeter of the laydown area.  The area between the new bulkhead and deadman 
anchors should be over-excavated to -5 feet MSL (along the south end) and -4 feet MSL 
(along the east and west ends) prior to backfilling with surge rocks and select granular fill 
materials.  The bottom of the over-excavation should be lined on all sides with a layer of 
woven filter fabric, such as Mirafi 600X or equivalent, and backfilled with surge rocks up to 
about Elevation +0 feet MSL.  The remaining backfill up to the finished subgrade should 
consist of select granular fill material underlain by a layer of non-woven filter fabric 
(Mirafi 180N or equivalent).  Due to the presence of loose sandy soils anticipated at the 
bottom elevation of the deadman anchors, we recommend providing a stabilization layer 
below the deadman anchors for more uniform support.  
In order to reduce the potential for pre-mature refusal during the pile driving 
operation, we recommend predrilling through the upper coral ledge and potential boulders 
and obstructions to facilitate the driven pile foundation and steel sheet pile bulkhead 
installation. 
Based on our laboratory test results, the subsoils at the site may be considered 
highly corrosive.  Because of the high corrosion potential of the subsoils, special attention 
should be given to corrosion protection of the steel sheet piles, tie-rods, and other metallic 
substructures (such as ductile iron water lines) at the project site. As a minimum, the 
exposed portions of the steel sheet piles should be surfaced with an epoxy coating to 
reduce the potential for corrosion of the exposed portions of the steel sheet piles.

The loose lagoonal deposits encountered at the site are potentially compressible

performed for the Keehi Lagoon Shipyard project in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu,

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae volcano forms the bulk of 
the western third of the island while the younger Koolau shield forms the majority of the 
eastern two-thirds of the island. It is believed that Waianae Volcano became extinct 
while Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank is partially buried below 
Koolau lavas in Central Oahu. 
The Koolau Volcanic Shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by 
thinly-bedded basaltic lava flows. The main shield-building activities ceased 
approximately 2.5 million years ago. Evidence from drilled wells indicate that the Island 
of Oahu has subsided by as much as 1,200 feet since the cessation of this early 
volcanic activity (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970). 
During that period of subsidence, coral-algal reefs began to grow on the southern 
coast of the Island of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth. The 
growth of the reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence. A series of 
lagoons formed behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments 
accumulated in the lagoons (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970). 
During the later Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred 
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great 
continental glaciers advanced, the level of the ocean fell since there was less water 
available to fill the oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded or melted, global 
sea levels rose because more water was available. The land mass of Oahu remained 
essentially stable during these changes, and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. 
These glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher 
and lower relative to the present sea level of Oahu. 
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of 
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at

During periods of no significant sea level changes, continued stream action

sediments. During these stable periods, the growth of fringing coral/algal reefs formed

10,000 years or so, the sea level has adjusted to its present stand. Terrigenous and 
marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low-energy estuarine or lagoonal 
environments resulting in thick deposits of soft/loose sediments along the coast in areas 
that were formerly valleys and drainageways. 
The area north of the project site is geologically situated on the southwestern 
flank of the Koolau Volcanics. These volcanics are composed of basaltic lava flows of 
early Pleistocene Age. The basalt is overlain by a complex inter-layered system of 
terrestrial and marine sedimentary deposits. During the later Pleistocene Age, the sea 
level rose and fell several times as a result of glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulting in 
local swamp conditions, which were later superimposed by more recent deposits of 
terrestrial detritus of sand, gravel, silt and clay eroded from the older rock of the area. In 
recent time, the efforts of man have shaped the low-lying land to its present form by the 
placement of fill in the low-lying area.

post-erosional volcanic deposits. There is evidence that a basaltic flow of the Honolulu

2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic 
plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic 
activity; therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground 
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The 
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so 
small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of 
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate 
damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on 
the Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii 
(over 90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and 
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of 
Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than 
Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of 
Hawaii. 
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes 
greater than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have 
occurred in the County of Maui. The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Islands 
of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu. For example, small 
landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of 
1938 (M6.8). Some houses on the Island of Oahu were reportedly damaged as a 
result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 (M7+). 
In the last 150 years of recorded history, we are not aware of earthquakes greater 
than Magnitude 6 that have occurred on the Island of Oahu. An earthquake of 
Magnitude 4.8 to 5.0 occurred along the Diamond Head Fault in 1948 on the Island

2.4.3 Liquefaction Potential 
Based on the International Building Code (2003 Edition), the project site should be 
evaluated for the potential for soil liquefaction. Based on the subsurface conditions 
encountered and a design peak ground acceleration of 0.241g, liquefaction may 
occur resulting in appreciable settlements at the ground surface. Therefore, the 
effects of potential liquefaction should be taken into consideration in the design of 
the proposed development. 
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the 
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build-up of excess 
pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by 
earthquakes. In this process, when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to 
vibration (such as during an earthquake), the soil tends to densify and decrease in 
volume causing an increase in pore water pressure. If drainage is unable to occur 
rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up of pore water pressure, the effective stress 
(internal strength) of the soil is reduced. Under sustained vibrations, the pore water 
pressure build-up could equal the overburden pressure, essentially reducing the 
soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid. During 
liquefaction, the soil acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal and 
vertical movements, and if not confined, will result in significant deformations. 
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained 
sands and loose silts with little cohesion. The major factors affecting the liquefaction 
characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows:

3.1.1 Predrilling 
Based on our field exploration, a hard coral ledge was encountered in the shallow 
subsurface in the majority of the borings drilled across the project site.  In addition, 
a shallow limestone shelf could be present at the project site based on the diving 
survey and water jet probing conducted by Sea Engineering, Inc.  To facilitate pile 
installation, we recommend predrilling the proposed pile locations prior to the pile 
driving operation.  Based on our field exploration results, predrilling depths on the 
order of about 30 to 60 feet below the ground or water surface would be required to 
facilitate the pile driving operations through the upper coral ledge encountered at 
the project site.  The recommended predrilling depths should be confirmed and/or 
modified by Geolabs during the test pile program depending on the subsurface 
conditions encountered and the pile driving equipment proposed by the contractor. 
We wish to emphasize that the diameter of the predrilled holes should be limited to 
approximately 22 to 24 inches to provide the driven piles with sufficient soil contact 
for lateral load resistance.  If an over-sized predrilled hole is encountered during 
construction, the annular space between piles and predrilled holes should be 
backfilled with pea gravel or grouted with tremie concrete to provide the 
recommended lateral load capacity. 
3.1.2 Downdrag Loads 
As previously mentioned, the loose lagoonal deposits may be susceptible to 
liquefaction in case of a seismic event producing a peak ground acceleration of 
about 0.241g at the site.  In the event of seismically induced liquefaction, settlement 
of the loose lagoonal deposits will occur at the site.  When the loose lagoonal 
deposits move downward relative to the pile, the downward movement will result in 
negative skin friction forces, generally known as downdrag forces, acting on the pile 
shaft.  The magnitude of the downdrag force depends on the thickness of the 
liquefiable layer, relative density of the potentially liquefiable lagoonal deposits, and 
the pile diameter.

3.1.3 Uplift Load Resistance 
Uplift loads imposed on the pile foundation may be resisted by a combination of the 
dead weight of the driven pile and by shear resistance acting along the pile surface 
and the adjacent soils. Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions, we believe 
that an ultimate uplift load capacity of 100 kips may be used for the driven pile 
foundations.  The ultimate uplift load capacity should be used for transient loads 
only. For sustained uplift loads acting on the foundations, the ultimate uplift load 
capacity should be reduced by a factor of safety of 2.0.  The uplift load capacity 
provided includes the weight of the pile.  The recommended uplift load capacity 
also assumes that the pile will be cast in one piece and will be installed without the 
use of a non-mechanical splice.  The structural capacity of the pile member in 
tension should be checked by the project structural engineer. 
3.1.4 Lateral Load Resistance 
Lateral loads imposed on the finger pier structure may be resisted by the lateral 
load capacity of the driven piles.  Lateral load resistance of driven piles is a function 
of the stiffness of the surrounding soil, the stiffness of the pile, allowable deflection 
at the top of the pile, and the induced moment in the pile.  In general, we 
recommend spacing the driven piles a minimum of 2.5 times the diameter of the 
pile from center-to-center to reduce the effect of group action.

3.1.5 Pile Foundation Settlements 
Settlement of the pile foundations will result from elastic compression of the pile 
member and subgrade response of the foundation embedded in the medium dense 
to dense coralline detritus materials anticipated at the project site. Total settlements 
of the pile-supported foundation under the design loads are estimated to be on the 
order of less than 0.5 inches. Differential settlements between adjacent piles should 
not exceed about one-half of the total foundation settlement. We believe that a 
significant portion of the settlement is elastic and should occur as the loads are 
applied. 
3.1.6 Pile Hammer Selection 
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a rated energy of 
about 60,000 foot-pounds of energy per blow.  The hammer should be equipped 
with an energy control level such that driving within the loose/soft soils may be 
controlled at a lower energy to reduce the potential for tensile stress development 
within the piles, which could damage or cause cracking of the piles. 
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into 
consideration the “matching” of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be 
driven.  This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy 
to the top of the pile and also reducing potential damage to the piles.  Geolabs

3.1.7 Pile Driving Criteria 
For preliminary design of the 20-inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles, 
pile driving may be terminated when the aggregate blow counts are at least 
10 blows per inch for the last 3 inches, or when the blow counts steadily increase 
with depth and reach an aggregated blow count of 30 blows for the last foot.  The 
preliminary driving criteria should be confirmed and/or modified after selection of 
the pile driving equipment and performance of a wave equation analysis. 
Each pile should be driven continuously without interruption.  A pile may be rejected 
or its design capacity may be reduced when the driving resistance is interrupted for 
more than 4 hours and the pile cannot be driven to the required depth.  During soft 
driving, such as when the pile tip is within soft/loose soils, the hammer stroke 
(or energy) should be carefully controlled such that significant tensile stresses do 
not develop within the pile. 
Because variations in soil strength characteristics are likely to exist in the bearing 
soils, the supporting capacity of each pile should be confirmed in the field by a 
Geolabs representative during driving.  It should be noted that the pile design was 
developed from our pile analysis using the field exploration data.  A test pile 
program should be carried out to confirm our preliminary pile design and establish 
the pile driving criteria.  The production pile driving criteria may be revised based on 
the test pile program.

Pavement Joints 
Due to the rigidity of the Portland cement concrete pavements, significant 
stresses may develop in the concrete from variations in temperature and moisture 
SECTION 3. DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
W.O. 5857-10

Page 40

content.  In order to relieve the high level of stress and to reduce the potential for 
significant cracking, adequate joints should be provided in the Portland cement concrete 
pavement.  The following describes the various types of joints and general guidelines 
that should be implemented in the design of concrete pavements. 
3.8.1 Crack Control Joints (Contraction Joints) 
In general, decreases in temperature or moisture content, such as that during 
concrete curing, will cause the concrete to contract and possibly crack. Crack 
control joints are used to provide controlled cracking due to volume changes and 
relieve the stresses caused by curling or warping.  Crack control joints, such as saw 
cut joints or formed grooves, are provided to a depth of generally one-fourth (¼) the 
slab thickness.  These saw cut joints or formed grooves (crack control joints) 
provide a weakened plane, which will likely crack through the full depth as the 
concrete shrinks during the curing process. 
In general, the spacing of crack control joints should be limited to a maximum of 
20 feet.  Smaller joint spacing should be used if the concrete pavement will be 
subjected to extreme temperature gradients during the placement and/or life span 
of the pavement.  The crack control joints should be provided in both longitudinal 
and transverse directions.  The transverse joint spacing should not vary from the 
longitudinal joint spacing by more than 25 percent. 
3.8.2 Expansion Joints (Isolation Joints) 
Expansion joints are typically used to relieve compressive stresses developed at 
critical locations due to fluctuations in temperature.  Expansion joints are cut to 
the full depth of the concrete slab and compressible filler materials are placed in 
the cut.  These joints allow for isolated lateral movement of the slabs and reduce 
the potential for damage to the adjoining concrete (spalling of the edges of the 
concrete slabs). 
Expansion joints should be used at pavement intersections and at intersections of 
pavements with fixed structures, such as the light poles and mooring bollard.

Because expansion joints generally do not provide for load transfer across the joint, 
placement of dowels with lubrication on one end of the dowel should be considered 
for load transfer across the expansion joint, where required. 
3.8.3 Construction Joints 
Construction joints are generally required when two abutting slabs are placed at 
different times.  Therefore, construction joints usually form the edges of each day’s 
work.  For the relatively thick concrete slab (8 inches) planned for this project, the 
construction joints should consist of a keyed or butted joint.  In general, construction 
joints should be aligned with the crack control or expansion joints to reduce the 
number of pavement joints required.

6 inches of free-draining granular materials (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) below the 
pipes for uniform support. Free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel

(ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), also should be used for the initial trench backfill up to

It is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation 
of voids below the haunches of the pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides

We envision that loose and/or soft lagoonal deposits will be encountered at or 
near the invert elevations along some of the on-site utility lines planned. Therefore, we 
recommend providing a subgrade stabilization layer consisting of 18 to 24 inches of 
No. 2 Rock (ASTM C 33, No. 4 gradation) wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric 
(Mirafi 180N or equivalent) below the bedding layer for uniform support. The 
stabilization layer should extend beyond the sides of the pipe a minimum width of 
one-fourth (1/4) the outside diameter of the pipe or 12 inches, whichever is greater.  In 
general, we anticipate that utility lines with an invert elevation deeper than about 
+2 feet MSL would encounter the soft and/or loose lagoonal deposits and will likely 
require the use of the stabilization layer. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes 
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated granular 
materials with a maximum particle size of 6 inches or select granular fill materials. The 
backfill material should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, 
placed in maximum 8-inch level loose lifts, and mechanically compacted to at least 
90 percent relative compaction. In areas where trenches will be in paved areas, the 
upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement finish grade should be compacted 
to no less than 95 percent relative compaction. 
3.11 Drainage 
The finished grades outside the new building should be sloped to divert water 
away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding. In

discharged away from the foundation and slab areas. Excessive landscape watering 
near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided. Planters next to foundations

drains to reduce the potential for excessive water infiltration into the subsurface.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
three on-land borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, extending to depths of 
about 48 to 53 feet below the existing ground surface. The on-land borings were drilled 
using truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools.  
In addition, the subsurface conditions at the sheet pile bulkhead and finger pier were 
explored by drilling two offshore borings, designated as Boring Nos. 4 and 5, extending to 
depths of about 149 and 150.5 feet below the water surface.  The offshore borings were 
drilled with a skid-mounted drill rig placed on a fabricated floating platform (raft) equipped 
with continuous flight augers and coring tools.  The approximate locations of the on-land 
and offshore borings are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of 
Borings, Plates A-1 through A-5. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample.  Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of

Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the

length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. 
For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any 
drilling, mechanical, and handling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material 
is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be 
shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe 
the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of 
Rocks and Minerals” by Robert S. Carmichael (1989).

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Two one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples to 
evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface clayey soils. The test results are 
summarized on Plate B-1. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations of the test results 
are provided on Plate B-2. 
Six Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 & C 136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the test results are provided on Plates B-3 and B-4. 
Three Unconfined Compression tests (ASTM D 7012 Method C) were performed 
on selected core runs to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the coral 
formation encountered. The test results are presented on Plate B-5. 
One Consolidation test with time rates (ASTM D 2435) was performed on a sample 
of the potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the 
materials encountered.  Results of the consolidation test are presented on Plate B-6. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D 1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils.  The test results are presented on Plates B-7 and B-8. 
Corrosion tests, including three pH (ASTM G 51), two Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G 57), one Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and one Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0) 
tests, were performed by our office and TestAmerica Analytical Testing Corporation on 
selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration. The test results are summarized 
on Plate B-9.
