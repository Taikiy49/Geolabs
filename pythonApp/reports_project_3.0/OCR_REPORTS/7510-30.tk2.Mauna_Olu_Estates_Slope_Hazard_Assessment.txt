Dear Mr. Robinson: 
As requested, we have conducted a slope hazard assessment for the Mauna Olu 
Estates – Aistar Properties residential development in Makaha Valley on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii.  This letter report summarizes our findings and discussions based on our 
field reconnaissance and engineering analyses of the existing hillside conditions and 
proposed cut slopes planned for the project. The findings and discussions presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located in Makaha Valley on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii and 
consists of approximately 26 acres of developed land to the northwest of the existing 
Makaha West Golf Course. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
The site includes 18 subdivided estate lots fully accessible with private streets and 
serviced by utilities. It is proposed to develop the site into a single-family home 
community with approximately 120 units and a clubhouse with a swimming pool, as 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Based on the existing topography and the grading plan provided, we understand 
that cuts of up to 20 feet in vertical height with a slope inclination of two horizontal to one 
vertical (2H:1V) are planned to achieve the design grades.  In accordance with The 
Revised Ordinances of Honolulu (ROH), Chapter 14, Section 14-14.2(d)(2), a slope 
hazard assessment will need to be conducted to evaluate whether potential slope and 
rockfall hazards may exist that will require mitigation and/or treatment measures.

SITE RECONNAISSANCE 
A site reconnaissance was conducted on August 22, 2018, to obtain a visual 
overview of the existing site conditions with respect to the occurrence of potential slope 
hazards such as existing slope instability and potential rockfall conditions. Our 
reconnaissance included visual observations and mapping of rock exposures, if 
applicable, during a foot-traverse of the existing ground surface within the proposed 
development area. Our geologist performed the site reconnaissance under dry weather 
and ground conditions using the available grading plan for location reference.  
The primary purpose for the site reconnaissance was to obtain an overview of the 
existing ground surface conditions within the project site. We recorded photographs of the 
existing ground conditions including typical surface rock exposures encountered at the 
project site. Selected site condition photographs are presented in Appendix A.  The 
approximate locations of the photographs taken are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
The project site is located within the existing Mauna Olu Estates, which is a 
24-hour guard gated community in Makaha Valley on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The 
proposed residential project consists of approximately 26 acres of developed land located 
to the west of Mauna`olu Street. 
Based on our field observations, the project area consists of open land generally 
covered with grasses with some shrubs and trees in localized areas.  A single-story 
recreation building with an asphalt paved parking lot was observed toward the 
northwestern side of the project site.  Based on our field observations and the grading 
plan provided, the topography at the site is generally flat to slightly sloping down from the 
northeast toward the southwest with existing ground surface elevations ranging from 
about +470 to +360 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). 
Based on our site reconnaissance and the available project boring logs, the site 
generally is situated on a heterogenous talus deposit consisting of very stiff to hard silty 
clays and clayey silts with varying amounts of gravel, cobbles, and boulders. 
The majority of the project site appears to have been previously cleared during the 
mass grading work of the existing gated community.  Based on our site reconnaissance, 
very limited surface boulders were observed at the existing ground surface throughout the 
project site.  A few boulders are visible that are completely embedded in the ground 
surface (see Photo No. 856 as an example).  The only existing slopes encountered are 
along the western boundary of the site where the ground slopes down towards a ravine 
bordering the western side of the project site. 
Based on our site reconnaissance, no visible signs of earth movement and/or 
slope instability were observed at the existing ground surface.  In addition, we did not 
observe any potential rockfall source materials on the existing hillside surrounding the 
proposed subdivision site. Therefore, we believe the risk for potential rockfall hazard 
emanating from off-site with loose boulders rolling down the existing hillside and into the 
subject development is very low to non-existent. 
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our site reconnaissance, visible evidence of active surface slope 
instability or visible evidence of past landslide/debris flow deposits was not observed at the 
project site. The existing graded ground appears generally stable and obvious visible signs 
of previous deep-seated landslide earth movement or surface debris flow activity were not 
encountered.  
Based on our site reconnaissance, very limited surface boulders were observed at 
the existing ground surface throughout the project site.   It is anticipated that future ground 
disturbances at the project site, such as grubbing of existing trees and shrubbery and 
other excavation activity, have potential to generate additional loose rocks that could

Potential Rockfall Hazards 
Based on our observations, it is our professional opinion that the very limited 
occurrence of loose (un-rooted) smaller dimension surface rocks at the adjacent hillside 
and within the development site should not present a dangerous falling rock hazard for the 
subdivision development. The typical small rock size and angular to sub-rounded rock 
shape combined with the maximum slope height of about 20 to 30 feet standing at a 
2H:1V inclination and composed predominantly of stiff soils should limit the production and 
fall of potentially dangerous falling rock hazards. 
However, the existing surface rocks and additional rocks that may be exposed by 
construction development of the site do have the potential to ravel from exposed and 
unvegetated slopes to create nuisance-type accumulations of rocky debris on the lower 
slope. Therefore, to reduce the potential for potential rockfall and nuisance-type rock 
raveling and lower slope accumulation, we recommend the following: 
New cut slope should be graded no steeper than 2H:1V  inclination. 
Non-embedded (unrooted) surface rocks including loose cobbles and 
boulders greater than 6 inches in dimension should be removed from the 
slope surface prior to placing erosion control materials and permanent 
vegetation. The rocks should be properly disposed of offsite. 
All finished slope surfaces should be promptly covered with an appropriate 
erosion control material until permanent ground cover vegetation is fully 
established. 
We believe that the potential for rockfall hazard occurring from the faces of the 
new cut slopes during construction when these potentially embedded cobbles and 
boulders are being exposed would be greatly reduced if the above recommendations are 
implemented for the project. The Contractor should exercise care during construction of 
the cut slopes and any potential rocks or boulders exposed during the excavation process 
should be removed properly. It is our opinion that the boulder hazard would be negligible 
once the exposed rocks and boulders are properly removed from the slope faces. 
Global Stability of New Cut Slopes 
We understand that new cut slopes up to 20 feet in vertical height at an inclination 
of 2H:1V are planned for the project construction. Based on the borings drilled for the 
project, we envision that the new cut slopes planned for the project would be underlain by 
very stiff to hard silty clays and clayey silts.  A slope stability analysis was performed 
based on this anticipated subsurface condition and the proposed cut slope design

LIMITATIONS 
The findings and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from visual site observations and the borings drilled at the site. 
Variation in the surface and subsurface conditions between our observation points may 
occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until 
additional field exploration or construction is underway.  If variations then appear evident, it 
will be necessary to re-evaluate the findings and recommendations provided herein. 
It should be noted that slopes composed of rock materials deteriorate with the 
passage of time due to natural weathering processes, wet-dry and hot-cold cycles, and 
erosion conditions.  Due to the inherent deterioration of rock slopes resulting from natural 
processes (weathering, wet-dry and hot-cold cycles, and erosion conditions), the potential 
for rockfall hazard at any site changes with the passage of time.  Therefore, the findings 
and recommendations contained in this letter report may be used only within a reasonable 
time from the date of issuance of this letter report.  Land use, site conditions, and/or other 
factors may change with the passage of time.  Therefore, additional work to evaluate the 
applicability of the findings and preliminary recommendations contained in this letter report 
due to changes with the passage of time will be required.  Finally, there are no guarantees 
in the professional engineering and architectural design fields with respect to potential 
rockfall hazards due in large part to the unpredictable nature of rockfall activity, which is 
affected by many external variables including natural and man-induced causes.
