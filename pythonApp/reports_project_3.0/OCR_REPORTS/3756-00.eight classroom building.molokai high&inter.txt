INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Eight Classroom Building project within the existing Molokai
High Intermediate School in the Hoolehua area on the island of Molokai Hawaii The
general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses of the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input
only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located within the existing Molokai High Intermediate School
in the Hoolehua area on the island of Molokai Hawaii It is proposed to construct a new
eight classroom building consisting of two single story structures as part of the
development plan for the Molokai High Intermediate School campus
Based on the information provided the new eight classroom building project will be
located to the south of Lihi Pali Avenue and north of the existing Cafetorium The project
will consist of two detached single story structures with CMU wall and metal roof
construction According to the project structural engineer maximum column loads would
be on the order of about 20 kips with wall loads of about 2 5 kips per lineal foot
In addition we understand that on site sewage disposal system consisting of
shallow leaching field is planned at the southwest corner of the project site Therefore
field percolation tests were conducted at the proposed sewage treatment area to obtain
data for preliminary design of the sewage disposal system Subsequent to the

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Molokai was built during the early to middle Pleistocene Epoch by
basaltic lava flows from two shield volcanoes East Molokai Mountain and West Molokai
Mountain The project site is located on the Hoolehua Plain between the East and West
Molokai Domes The Hoolehua Plain or isthmus of Molokai is composed of lava flows
from the East Molokai Volcano which banked against the older West Molokai Volcano
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global
sea levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Molokai remained
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature
These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher
and lower relative to the present sea level on Molokai
Subaerial erosion of the emerging East and West Molokai Volcanoes and
subsequent deposition of the clastic debris as coalescing alluvial fans formed the present
configuration of the Hoolehua Plain Late Pleistocene to Recent Epoch weathering has
produced a residual lateritic soil over saprolite and extremely weathered basalt bedrock
underlying the site

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered stiff to hard silty clays and clayey silts
throughout the depths of the borings drilled at the subject site Based on the existing
topography at the project site minor site grading work is anticipated to attain the design
finished grades for the proposed building construction The majority of the site grading
efforts will consist of cuts up to about 10 feet high to create a relatively level area for the
new sewage disposal field located to the north of the new classroom buildings Based on
the relatively competent subsoil conditions encountered in our borings and the anticipated
structural loads it is our opinion that shallow foundations bearing on the undisturbed in
situ stiff soils may be used for support of the proposed eight classroom building project
Our laboratory swell test results indicate that the near surface soils exhibit slight to
low swelling and shrinkage potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations Therefore
it is our opinion that the building slab may be constructed on grade provided that the
subgrade is moisture conditioned and prepared in accordance with the recommendations
presented in the Slabs On Grade section of this report Detailed discussion of these
items and our geotechnical engineering design recommendations are presented in the
following sections of this report
Building Foundations
Based on the current design concept and the generally competent subsoil
conditions encountered at the site we believe that shallow spread and or continuous
footings may be used for support of the proposed eight classroom building An allowable
bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be used for the
design of footing foundations bearing on the undisturbed in situ stiff to hard silty clay and
clayey silt soils This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3756 00 Page 8
For footings bearing on the undisturbed in situ stiff soils the bottom of the footing
excavations should be lightly compacted to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing
surface prior to the placement of reinforcing steel and concrete If soft and or loose
materials are encountered at the bottom of footing excavations they should be over
excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation should be
backfilled with select granular material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction or the footing bottom may be extended down to the underlying competent
material
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grades If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements
they should be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane
extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of
the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for
footings bearing on the undisturbed in situ stiff soils as recommended herein to be less
than 1 inch total with differential settlements on the order of inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil Resistance due to
passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of
300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f This assumes that the soil around
footings is relatively undisturbed or well compacted Unless covered by pavements or
slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected A

coefficient of friction of 0 3 may be used for footings bearing on the lightly compacted in
situ stiff soils
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii prior to the placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the
foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depths
Slabs On Grade
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by near
surface silty clay and clayey silt soils with swelling potential ranging from slight to low
when subjected to moisture fluctuations To reduce the potential for structural distress of
the slabs on grade resulting from the swelling of the near surface soils we recommend
that the subgrade soils beneath the slab areas be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to between 2 and 5 percent above the optimum moisture and
recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The slab subgrades
should be kept moist prior to placement of concrete Where shrinkage cracks are noted
after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soils be thoroughly moistened
to close all cracks Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to
inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and
replacement with well compacted engineered fill
In accordance with the requirements of D A G S Technical Memorandum No 61
dated a basaltic termite barrier BTB should be incorporated into the slab
on grade design of new facilities Therefore we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick
layer of cushion fill consisting of BTB be placed below the slabs to serve as a termite
barrier and to provide more uniform support of the slabs To reduce future moisture
infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture vapor
barrier such as a plastic membrane is recommended on top of the BTB cushion layer
Two inches of BTB should be placed above the plastic membrane to provide protection of
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3756 00 Page 10
the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab concrete Flexible floor coverings such as
carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered since they can better mask minor slab
cracking
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project We
recommend that concrete walkways be supported on at least 6 inches of select borrow or
non expansive select granular fill material The select borrow or non expansive select
granular fill material should be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction The subgrade soils underneath the sidewalk slabs should be prepared in
accordance with the recommendations presented above Contraction joints should be
provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at right
angle intersections The area adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly against the
slab with non expansive impervious soil and graded to divert water away from the slab to
reduce the potential of water ponding around the foundation
Site Preparation
We envision that minimal site grading work is anticipated for the project
construction In general the following guidelines should be used for site grading At the
on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly
cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials
should be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing below any areas designated to receive fill
should be over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation
should be backfilled with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft and or
organic soils should be properly disposed of off site
Footings left behind from the relocation of the existing portable buildings should be
completely removed Any over excavation resulting from the demolition should be
backfilled with compacted granular fill material Existing utilities that are to be abandoned
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3756 00 Page 11
should be removed and the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with select
granular fill material placed in 6 to 8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction Utilities that are to be abandoned in place under the
proposed structure should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete under low pressure
After clearing and grubbing all areas at grade or to receive fill should be scarified
to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to between 2 and 5 percent above the
optimum moisture and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that
the soil be thoroughly moistened to close all cracks Saturation and subsequent yielding
of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over
excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted engineered fill
Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation
and engineered fill placement to account for variations in the over excavation quantities
The excavated on site residual soils may be used as a source of fill and backfill
material provided that they are prepared in accordance with our recommendations On
site residual soils to be used for fills and backfills should be moisture conditioned to
between 2 and 5 percent above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding
8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction Where needed imported fill materials should consist of non expansive
granular material such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material
should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest
dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200
sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a
maximum swell of less than 1 percent Imported fill material should be tested by
Geolabs Hawaii for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the
project site for its intended use

Import fill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 10 inches in loose thickness and compacted
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the
in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the
same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum
dry density

tility Trench
We envision that new utility connections may be required for the project A
granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials such as
No 3B Fine gravel should also be used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches
above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the
compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is
critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential of formation of voids below
the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Improper backfill material and placement could result in settlement of the backfill and
damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of on site soils or select fill
material The backfill should be placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and
mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the
GEOLABS HAWAII

potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the
upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to
a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the new classroom buildings should be sloped to shed
water away from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised
that gutter systems be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted
away from the foundation areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and
slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have
concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as
possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away from the slab and
footing foundations

The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling five
borings to depths ranging from about 5 to 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface at
the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled
using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The material encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 5
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer test was performed on selected cohesive soil sample
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer test
are summarized on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample depth

The field percolation rates of the on site soils were estimated by performing two
falling head percolation tests in Boring Nos 1 and 2 Field percolation testing were
performed in general accordance with the State of Hawaii Department of Health s
Administrative Rules Chapter 2 Wastewater Systems and Chapter 10 of the
Ten States Standards to evaluate the permeability of the subsurface soils for wastewater
disposal The test results are presented on Plates C 1 and C 2
The percolation tests were performed by drilling 4 inch diameter boreholes to a
depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface Six to nine inches of gravel were
then placed at the bottom of the boreholes to protect the bottom of the holes from
scouring and sediments
During testing the holes were carefully filled with water to a minimum depth of
12 inches over the gravel pack The time intervals and water drops were then recorded
to provide data upon which to base calculation of the percolation rates
