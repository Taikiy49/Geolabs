INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Lower Reservoir 327A located in the Red Hill area on the
island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and _ geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses for the Lower Reservoir 327A These recommendations are intended for the
design of foundations site grading and pavements only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located in the vicinity of the underground fuel storage tanks in
the Red Hill area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii Based on the information
provided we understand that it is desired to demolish the existing 0 5 Million Gallon MG
concrete reservoir and replace it with two fresh water reservoirs designated as
Reservoir 327A lower reservoir and Reservoir 327B upper reservoir as shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2
Based on the plans provided the new reservoirs will be of steel construction with a
concrete floor slab The lower reservoir measuring approximately 36 feet in diameter
and 33 feet high will be located in the lower grounds to the west of the existing reservoir
The finish floor elevation for the lower reservoir will be at approximately 78 75 feet with a
minimum 10 inch thick reinforced concrete slab All elevations presented herein unless
specified otherwise are referenced to an assumed bench mark elevation of 88 52 feet
and is a PK Nail in asphalt concrete designated as Station B as shown on Sheet C1
of the project plans The upper reservoir measuring approximately 48 feet in diameter
and 19 feet high will be situated at approximately the same location of the existing

92 75 feet with a minimum 8 inch thick reinforced concrete slab
The project will commence with the construction of the lower reservoir followed by

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in central Oahu
The Koolau volcanic shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by
thinly bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities ceased
approximately 2 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicate that the island of
Oahu has subsided as much as 1 200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic
activity Macdonald and Abbott 1970
The bedrock of the Red Hill area is composed of Koolau volcanic series basalt
This group of rocks makes up the bulk of the Koolau Range of Oahu and has an exposed
thickness of approximately 3 000 feet These lavas were erupted from three rift zones in
the sea floor during the Pliocene time approximately 2 to 3 million years ago
Subsequent weathering extending into recent times has produced a residual soil crust
on the top of these flows The weathering oxidation hydrolyzation of the
ferro magnesian iron and manganese bearing minerals of the basalt has produced a
characteristic red iron oxide rust stain in the residual soils hence the name Red Hill

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by very stiff to
hard silty clay and clayey silt soils extending to the maximum depth explored of about
26 5 feet below the existing ground surface Based on the subsoil conditions
encountered at the project site we believe that shallow foundations consisting of a
perimeter ring footing bearing on the recompacted in situ stiff soils may be used for
support of the proposed new lower reservoir
Based on our laboratory tests the near surface clayey soils exhibit a slight
shrink swell potential when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents
To reduce the potential for distress to the future slab on grade resulting from swelling of
the near surface soils and to provide more uniform support we recommend that the
on site clayey soils be capped with a minimum of 6 inches of compacted base course
below the reservoir slab areas In general the subgrade soils should be scarified to a
depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
The slab subgrade should be kept moist and free of shrinkage cracks prior to placement
of base course and slab concrete Our geotechnical engineering recommendations
pertaining to the design of foundations site grading and pavements are further discussed
in the following sections of this report
Reservoir Foundations
Based on the current design concept and the generally stiff subsoil conditions
encountered at the site we believe that shallow foundations may be used for support of
the proposed new lower reservoir construction An allowable bearing pressure of up to
3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of a perimeter ring footing
bearing on the recompacted stiff clayey soils anticipated at the project site This bearing
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4005 00 A Page 8
value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient
loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
For footings bearing on the in situ stiff soils the bottom of the footing excavations
should be recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction to provide a relatively
firm and smooth bearing surface prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete If soft
and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations they
should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation
may be backfilled with on site soils compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction or the footing bottom may be extended down to the underlying competent
material
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive
earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system
A coefficient of friction of 0 3 may be used for foundations bearing on the in situ stiff soils
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the
soil around footings is well compacted The passive pressure should be reduced for
foundations located on slopes Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive
resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grades For footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping ground
the footings should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal set back
distance of 8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the slope face
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the
utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4005 00 A Page 9
avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for foundation settlement
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for
footings bearing on the recompacted in situ stiff soils as recommended herein to be less
than 1 inch total with differential settlements on the order of 0 5 inches
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the
foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depths
Slab On Grade
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by
near surface clayey soils with a slight expansion potential when subjected to moisture
fluctuations To reduce the potential for future distress to the slab on grade resulting from
shrinking and swelling of these clayey soils due to changes in the moisture content we
recommend that the subgrade soils below the slab concrete be properly prepared and
capped with at least 6 inches of base course compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction The 6 inch compacted base course will also serve to provide more
uniform support for the reservoir slab The area adjacent to the reservoir slab should be
backfilled tightly against the slab with compacted on site soils and graded to divert water
away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the slab and
foundation
Site Grading
Based on the existing topography and the design finish floor elevation of the lower
reservoir we anticipate that the majority of the site grading work for the proposed
construction will consist of minor cuts up to about 5 feet deep
At the on set of earthwork the areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4005 00 A Page 10
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill should
be over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft and or organic soils
should be properly disposed of off site or used in landscaping areas if appropriate
Due to the slightly expansive nature of the on site clayey soils and proximity of the
lower reservoir site to the existing underground fuel storage tank it is our opinion that the
on site soils generated from cut areas should not be used as fill and backfill for the project
construction If the excavated soils are contaminated with hydrocarbon or other
substances an environmental consultant should be consulted for proper handling and
disposal of the contaminated soils Addressing the environmental considerations for this
project is beyond the scope of our work
After clearing and grubbing areas beneath future slabs should be over excavated
where necessary to provide the minimum 6 inch thick compacted base course below the
slab concrete Prior to placement of the base course layer the subgrade soils should be
scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above
the optimum moisture and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after preparation of the subgrade we recommend that
the soil be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks and recompacted Saturation and
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor
drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted engineered fill
The on site clayey soils generated from the cut areas should not be used as fill
and backfill materials below the reservoir footprint and pavement areas as discussed
earlier They should be disposed of properly off site or used in landscaping areas if
appropriate Aggregate base course material should meet the material requirements for
Aggregate for Untreated Base Section 703 06 as specified in the Hawaii Standard
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4005 00 A Page 11
Specifications for Road Bridge and Public Works Construction 1994 Imported fill
material if required should consist of non expansive imported granular material such as
crushed coral basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded from coarse to
fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain
between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should
have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of less
than 1 percent Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its
intended use
Base course and imported granular fill materials should be moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91
test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density Compaction should be accomplished by
sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment
Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed to compact the fill materials
Cut and fill slopes planned for the subject project should be constructed at an
inclination of 2H 1V or flatter All fills should be keyed and benched into the existing slope
to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations should start at the
lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the
fill placement recommendations presented above Excessive water should be diverted
away from the tops of slopes and slope planting should be provided as soon as the fill
slopes are completed to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes
Site grading operations should be observed by a _ representative of
Geolabs Hawaii It is important that a representative from our office observe the site

Service Road Pavements
Considering the light vehicular traffic anticipated for the service roadway around
the perimeter of the water reservoir the following pavement section may be used based
on the results of the borings and visual observations
2 0 Inch Asphalt Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
8 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness over Compacted Moist Subgrade
The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction The base course should consist of
crushed basalt aggregate compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
CBR and field density tests should be performed on the actual subgrade soils
encountered during construction to confirm the adequacy of the above section
The recommended section considers only light traffic conditions and assumes that good
drainage will be provided adjacent to paved areas
Water Lines
We envision that new water lines will be required for this project In general for
support of the water lines we recommend that a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches
of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under the pipes The initial
backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes should consist of free draining backfills
such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from
compaction of the backfill It is critical that a free draining granular material be used to
reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide
adequate support for the sides of the pipes The use of on site clayey soils as backfill
immediately around water line pipes is not recommended
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4005 00 A Page 13
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finish grade may consist of the on site soils or select granular
material The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
placed in about 8 inch loose lifts and mechanically compacted to at least 90 percent
relative compaction Where trenches are located in paved areas the upper 3 feet of the
trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to not less than
95 percent relative compaction
For utility lines located below the footprint of the reservoir tank the utility trenches
should be backfilled with concrete or lean concrete up to the bottom of the base course
layer The base course layer recommended below the tank floor should be placed above
the concrete to provide more uniform support for the tank slab The concrete around the
utility lines below the reservoir tank structure should be placed neat against the sides of
the excavation
Drainage
The finished grades outside the reservoir structure should be sloped to shed water
away from foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive
landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be
properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce water infiltration In addition drainage swales should be provided as
soon as possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away from slabs
and foundations

The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings to a depth of about 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Engineer who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the
materials encountered is presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Three one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples
to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results of these tests are
summarized on Plate B 1
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected soil
sample to evaluate its liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are presented on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depth Graphic
representation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
