Our exploratory borings drilled at the replacement bridge site generally encountered
a surface fill layer of stiff to very stiff sandy silt and dense silty sand and gravel extending
to depths of about 0 3 to 5 5 feet below the existing ground surface The fill layer was
underlain by hard to very hard basalt rock formation about 10 to 19 5 feet thick generally
consisting of slightly weathered to relatively unweathered basalt Below the hard to very
hard basalt formation medium hard to hard basalt formation with clinker layers was
encountered extending to the maximum depths drilled of about 43 1 feet below the existing
ground surface Occasional voids were encountered within the basalt with clinker
formation Along the relocated Kamani Street our borings encountered approximately
5 feet of surface fill above medium hard to hard basalt rock formation with
occasional clinker layers Our borings drilled within the upstream area generally
encountered a surface fill and alluvial layer ranging from about 4 to 4 5 feet in thickness
The surface fill layer was underlain by a volcanic ash deposit followed by hard to very hard
basalt rock formation with occasional clinker layers Groundwater was not encountered in
the borings at the time of our field exploration
Based on our field exploration we recommend that a shallow foundation system
consisting of continuous strip footings bearing directly on or embedded in the hard to very
hard basalt formation be used to support the replacement bridge structure We
recommend that bearing pressures of up to 60 000 and be used to evaluate the
extreme even limit state and strength limit state of the footings respectively These
bearing values are based on the footings bearing on or embedded into the hard to very
hard basalt formation upper basalt formation For foundations bearing on the underlying
medium hard to hard basalt with clinker formation we recommend that lower bearing
pressures be used to design the bridge foundations Therefore we recommend that lower
bearing pressures of up to 20 000 and be used to evaluate the extreme event
limit state and the strength limit state respectively
Because of the relatively high axial and lateral bearing values recommended for
this project concrete should be used to backfill the inadvertent over excavations below
and to the sides of the foundations In addition concrete will be required as backfill below
the foundations for inadvertent over excavations to reduce the potential for scour and

1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Mamalahoa Highway Emergency Replacement of Paauau Stream
Bridge project located along Route 11 in the District of Kau on the Island of Hawaii The
general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations retaining
structures and site grading only The findings and recommendations presented herein
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes including the following Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea some activity during
recent geologic time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 and is considered to be dormant and
Mauna Loa and Kilauea both are siill active The project site is situated on the southeast
flank of the Mauna Loa Shield Volcano
The volume of the Mauna Loa Shield Volcano extending from the ocean floor is
estimated to be on the order of about 10 000 cubic miles making it possibly the largest
volcanic mountain on earth Furthermore it is estimated from current lava production that
Mauna Loa may have been forming for the past 1 to 2 million years Mauna Loa is
actually composed of two separate shield volcanoes Mauna Loa and Ninole The latter
having been covered by Mauna Loa lavas leaving only a few localized surface
expressions visible at present The Mauna Loa shield has been built by volcanic eruptions
along two principal rift zones that extend southwestward and east northeastward from the
summit caldera
The majority of the surface rock exposures at Mauna Loa consist of lavas and
volcanic deposits belonging to the Kau Volcanic Series Pleistocene and Holocene age
and the older Kahuku Volcanic Series Pleistocene age In addition a regional ash
deposit identified as Pahala Ash Pleistocene age forms a soil mantle overlying and
interbedded within the rocks of the Kau Volcanic Series The ash deposits typically occur
as kipukas isolated deposits with some significant accumulations ranging up to about
80 feet in thickness
Pahala Ash is a low plasticity silt derived from the weathering of ash cinder and
Pele s Hair Pahala Ash generally has low shear strength and when the moisture content
is high enough it loses significant strength when it is remolded becomes thixotropic
Therefore the typical Pahala Ash may be potentially liquefiable during seismic events

Based on our site reconnaissance the Paauau Stream channel is incised through
Kau Volcanic Series basalt rock The near surface basalt rock at the project site is
believed to represent a series of a a lava flows The a a lava flows are formed by generally
more viscous slower moving lava and are usually represented by a solid rock core
sandwiched between upper and lower less dense clinker layers The surface
appearance of some a a lava flows may mislead the casual observer to believe that the
entire lava flow is plainly a mass of loose pile of rocks when in fact just below the rubbly
surface lies a dense and continuous core of very hard rock Individual a a lava flows are
typically thicker than the more fluid pahoehoe type of lava flow The slower rate of
advancement and the generally greater thickness of a a lava flows allow the interior core to
flow and cool more slowly than the upper and lower boundary surfaces and therefore
creating the characteristic dense lava flow core and bounding clinker layers
Based on our observation of the exposed stream banks and road cuts in the vicinity
of the existing Paauau Stream Bridge the near surface lava flows generally consist of a

2 2 Faulting and Seismicity
Based on our review of available geologic information the Paauau Stream Bridge
project site is not located in the immediate proximity of mapped geologic fault structures
The terminus of the nearest mapped faults is located approximately 3 8 miles toward the
north of the project site as shown on the Geologic Map Plate 3 Others have mapped
these faults previously as concealed normal faults Concealed faults are faults where the
surface expression of the fault structure has not been identified
Furthermore the project site is located easterly of multiple northeast to southwest
oriented normal faults associated with the regional Kaoiki Honuapo Fault System which is
located toward the west and southwest of the site In addition some possible ground
instability earthquake potential may be associated with the east rift zone of Kilauea
Volcano which is located approximately 4 miles toward the east of the site Therefore the

3 1 Seismic Design Considerations
Based on the design criteria provided by the State of Hawaii Department of
Transportation Highways Division the Emergency Replacement of Paauau Stream
Bridge project will need to be designed based on a seismic acceleration coefficient of
0 80g Based on the subsurface materials encountered in the borings drilled and the
geologic setting of the area the project site may be classified from a seismic analysis
standpoint as a rock site corresponding to a Type Soil Profile A seismic site coefficient
of 1 0 may be used for the project site for seismic analysis of the replacement bridge
structure
As an alternative to the standard response spectrum curves a site specific
response spectrum analysis has been conducted for this project Discussion and detailed
information with respect to the site specific response spectrum analyses performed by our
office for this project should be referred to the Seismic Response Evaluation Report
transmitted under separate cover
To assist in the modeling of the behavior of the bridge structure during an
earthquake we believe that the abutment structure backfill provides a significant amount
of lateral load resistance in the form of passive pressure resistance The abutment
structure may be considered as a large footing bearing on the abutment backfill soils for
resistance of lateral loads generated during a seismic event in the longitudinal direction
Considering the type of structure backfill materials recommended behind the abutment
structures Type A Structure Backfill the stiffness of the abutment fill soils will generally
be mobilized prior to the lateral load resistance of the foundations
Provided that intimate contact exists between the backfill soils and the abutment

abutment wall per inch of deflection may be used in resisting lateral loads To reduce the

3 2 Scour Potential
Scour is the result of the erosive action of flowing water excavating and carrying
away material from the bed and banks of the stream Total scour over a period of time
generally consists of three components 1 Aggregation and Degradation 2 Contraction
Scour and 3 Local Scour The rates of scour depend on a number of factors such as the
shape and dimensions of a pier or abutment depth of flow velocity of approach flow size
and gradation of stream bed material and bed configuration
One of the factors affecting the scour depth is the grain size characteristic of the
stream bed material The median diameter of the stream bed material Dso in conjunction
with the depth of flow and flow velocity is used to calculate the flow velocity of the stream
bed materials in scour depth analysis Based on the materials encountered in the borings
drilled at the bridge site we anticipate that moderately fractured basalt formation with
relatively high RQD values greater than 50 percent will be encountered at the stream bed
and foundation level Based on the guidelines on scour potential by the Federal Highway
Administration the basalt formation at the bridge site may be considered to be a
non erodible material and would not be subject to stream scour

3 4 Foundation Probing
Based on our experience in the area and observation of the core samples retrieved
voids and or cavities commonly exist in the basalt rock formation at the project site In
addition small voids were encountered in the basalt rock formation encountered in several
of the borings drilled To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from
the collapse of cavities below a footing we recommend that a program of cavity probing
and grouting at the foundation locations abutments and piers be implemented during
construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that a probe hole be drilled for every
100 square feet of foundation area or at 10 feet on center along the abutment and pier
foundations The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend
to a depth of at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of footing elevation If cavities
and or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operation additional probe
holes should be drilled at closer spacing to aid in delineating the vertical and lateral extent

of the cavity and or void The probe holes and cavities discovered should be backfilled
with sand cement grout injected at low to moderate pressures We recommend that a low
strength sand cement grout mixture with a slump range of 6 to 9 inches be utilized for the
grouting operations As an alternative to sand cement grout Controlled Low Strength
Material CLSM may also be used for the grouting operations
Because of the potential for encountering large cavities and or voids at the site we

operations are complete In addition it should be noted that a longer lag time between the

The approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer or geologist who monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 15
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6
inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
A sieve analysis test ASTM C 136 and D 1140 was performed to determine the
grain size distribution of the soil The grain size distribution is used to classify the soil in
accordance with the Unified Soil Classification System The test results are summarized
on Plate B 1
Ten unconfined compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on rock core
samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the underlying basalt rock
formation The test results are summarized on Plate B 2
