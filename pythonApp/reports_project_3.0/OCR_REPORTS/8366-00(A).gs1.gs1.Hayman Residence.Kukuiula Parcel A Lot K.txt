Regional Geology 
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single dissected basaltic shield volcano built 
by the extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series beginning about 5 to 
6 million years ago. The eruption of the Waimea Volcanic Series ended about 2½ million 
years ago and was followed by a long period of erosion. Following the cessation of this 
main volcano shield building phase, about 1½ million years ago, there was renewed 
volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of the post-erosional Koloa Volcanic 
Series and the concurrent deposition of the thick alluvial sediments of the Palikea 
Formation. 
Rock formations of the Koloa Volcanic Series are generally characterized as thick 
lava flows composed of dense basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in north-south 
trends at various locales. Associated with the Koloa Volcanic Series lava flows are some 
deposits of pyroclastic materials (volcanic ash and cinders), which usually form cinder 
cones surrounding the vent. Rock formations of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of 
the eastern half of the Island of Kauai, including the project site.

widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great continental 
glaciers accumulated, the level of the ocean fell since less water was available to fill the

The project area is generally composed of basaltic rock built by extrusion of the 
lavas of the Koloa Volcanic Series. The basalt rock formation observed in the Koloa area 
appears to be representative of the pahoehoe lava flow type, which spread and ponded 
as it approached the ocean from inland areas. Pahoehoe lavas are typically characterized 
by a smoother, billowy surface and internal structure of vesicular (porous) rock with limited 
clinker materials.  
In some places, in-situ chemical weathering of the Koloa lava flows has formed a 
mantle of residual and saprolitic soils. In general, saprolite is composed mainly of silty 
material, while residual soils are more clayey. Both residual and saprolitic soils are typical 
of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks. The residual and saprolitic soils grade to a 
basaltic rock formation with increased depth. Agricultural development within the last 100 
years and recent mass grading work have brought the area to its present form. 
The project site is located at the foot of the southern flank of Manuhonuhonu 
hillside. The geologic maps indicated that the site is underlain mainly by basalt rock 
formation of the Koloa Volcanic Series.  We anticipate that the basalt rock formation is 
overlain by residual and saprolitic soils. 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is a vacant lot, designated as Lot K of the Parcel A Subdivision, within 
the Kukui’ula Development in Koloa, on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. The project site is 
located on the downslope side of Noho Kai Place. The lot is bordered to the north by Noho 
Kai Place, and to the west and east by Lot L and Lot J, respectively. The lot overlooks, to

In general, the topography of the lot slopes down from south at an average gradient 
of about 8 percent with existing ground surface elevations ranging from about +104 to +82 
feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) at the northeast and southwest portions of the site, respectively.

Site Reconnaissance 
Our engineer conducted a general ground surface reconnaissance of the proposed 
project site on August 23, 2021.  We observed the following existing site conditions at the 
time of our site visit: 
Access to the project site is via Noho Place. 
In general, the project site is in a well-maintained condition with weed grass

We did not observe visual evidence of mass slope instability. 
Anticipated Subsurface Conditions 
Subsurface conditions at the project site were evaluated based on our site 
reconnaissance; and literature research of in-house reports, plans, maps and other 
information. Based on the geologic setting of the project site and our experience in the 
vicinity, we anticipate that the subsurface materials generally would consist of fill and 
residual soils overlaying saprolitic soils or basalt rock formation.  
Based on the mass grading plans, the lower one-quarter of the lot was fill and the 
upper three-quarter of the lot was in cut.  
We anticipate that the fill and residual soils will generally consist of silty clay or clayey 
silt, and the saprolitic soils will generally consist of clayey silt and silty sand and gravel. We 
anticipate that the underlying basalt rock formation may likely be relatively shallow at about 
1 to 2 feet below the existing ground surface. Based on our previous borings performed in 
the development area, we do not anticipate that groundwater levels will be encountered 
during the site grading for the project. However, seepage water from the underlying basalt 
rock formation may be encountered after the heavy rainfall.

Excavations & Potential Cavities 
The anticipated residual and saprolitic soils may be excavated with normal heavy 
excavation equipment, although some of the excavations may encounter cobbles and 
boulders. Deep excavations and excavations encountering cobbles, boulders, and basalt 
formations may require the use of hoerams. 
We anticipate that the underlying basalt rock formation may be encountered at 
relatively shallow depths. Hard rock excavation using hoerams may be required in this area 
for site grading and utility installation. Where basalt rock formation is encountered near the 
finished grades, proof-rolling the rocky subgrade should be performed to collapse 
near-surface cavities. 
Expansive Clayey Soils Beneath Slabs-on-Grade 
The on-site clayey soils anticipated within the project site may exhibit moderate to 
high expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations. Therefore, a layer of 
non-expansive select granular fill (typically 12 inches to 24 inches thick) may be required 
beneath slabs-on-grade to reduce moisture changes in the slab subgrade soils and reduce 
the potential for slab distress from shrinkage and heaving. 
Building Foundations 
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions, we believe that the new 
lightly-loaded residential structures (one to two stories) may be supported on shallow spread 
and/or continuous footings. 
Additional Services 
The presence and extent of the underlying basalt rock formation should be further 
evaluated. Therefore, we recommend evaluating the subsurface conditions by conducting a 
design-level field exploration program consisting of drilling and sampling borings during the 
design phase when details of the proposed project are established.
