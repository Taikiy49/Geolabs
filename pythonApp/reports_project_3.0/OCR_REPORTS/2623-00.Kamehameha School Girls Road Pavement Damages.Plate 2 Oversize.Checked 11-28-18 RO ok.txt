INTRODUCTION
In accordance with your request we have performed a geotechnical engineering
exploration of the area of damaged pavement along Girls Road at the Kamehameha
Schools in Honolulu Oahu Hawaii Our work was performed in general accordance with
our revised proposal dated This report summarizes our efforts on the
project and presents our findings and recommendations pertinent to the repair of the
damaged pavement and rehabilitation of Girls Road
The general location of the site and its vicinity is shown on the Project Location
Plan Plate 1
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the subsurface
conditions at the site to develop a generalized soils data set for the formulation of
preliminary geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the repair of the
damaged pavement and rehabilitation of Girls Road at the Kamehameha Schools in
Honolulu Oahu Hawaii

its width rests on cut and about half rests on embankment placed on the hillside There

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Oahu was built up during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene by
the activities of two basaltic shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau Koolau is the
younger of the two and reached the end of its main shield building phase about 2 million
years ago The project site is located on the southern flank of Koolau
Koolau was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas fromm northwest to southeast
trending rift zones These lava flows are interbedded pahoehoe and aa flows with
pahoehoe being the dominant form The project site is situated on Kamehameha Heights
which is an erosional remnant ridge above Kalihi and Puunui
Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth ropy or billowy surface and an internal
structure of generally vesicular rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe
lavas These cavities were formed when the rock was still molten during its deposition
and represent both lava tubes and interflow cavities such as blisters and pockets
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2623 00 Page 6
Aa is characterized by a rough spiny surface of clinker and has a core of dense
rock Generally there is another layer of clinker at the base of the flow Cavities are
occasionally found in aa flows
Lava tubes are formed when liquid lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a
hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the
flow Interflow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalts are considered to be highly permeable rock and transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions Water is transmitted through the
porous rock matrix along joints fractures and interflow contacts through cavities and
along clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is very high therefore
these serve as the major water transmission features
In situ weathering of the basaltic lava flows at the project site has generated a
mantle of residual soils and saprolites over the underlying basaltic lava rock Saprolite
is a form of residual soil that retains the structure of the original rock mass from which it
was derived
During the development of the school campus man made earthwork consisting
of cuts and fills were used to adjust grades and level the area

Soil Conditions
In general the drilled borings encountered very stiff silty clays and clayey silts
overlying weathered in situ basalt formation These stiff soils are generally residual and
saprolite soils derived from the in place chemical weathering of the parent basalt rock
formation Some of the stiff soils represent man made fills placed during the development
of the campus area which were derived from on site residual soils and saprolites
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings during our field
exploration Seepage was noted in our subsequent monitoring of the piezometer installed
in Boring No 1 creating a static water level of about 3 2 feet below the existing ground
surface in the piezometer
Seepage is frequently encountered in hillside areas and is dependent upon weather
conditions and site specific geological factors Therefore there is a potential that other
areas of seepage or transient perched groundwater could be encountered during future
construction

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Our field exploration and visual observations at the site indicate that the existing
pavement of Girls Road has become damaged and that its serviceability is currently poor
to unsatisfactory
Generally our borings encountered competent soils below the existing pavement
section although a zone of soft moist soil was encountered in Boring No 2 at depths
between about 2 5 to 5 0 feet below the existing surface
Our laboratory testing indicates that the subgrade soils have generally good
bearing strength They are however moderately expansive
Pavement Damages
Defining the cause of pavement failures can be a difficult task Damages to
pavements can be the result of one or more of several mechanisms such as pavement
fatigue subgrade failure pumping failure and overloading of the pavement
Generally failures can be considered to occur in one of two modes structural
failures and functional failures Structural failures occur when one or more of the
components of the pavements breaks downs resulting in damage to the pavement
Functional failures occur when the pavement can no longer carry out its intended
function e g the surface of the pavement becomes too rough for trafficability or too
weak to support traffic and are usually the result of advanced age or wearing out of the
pavement
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2623 00 Page 9
Based upon our field exploration and observations at the site it appears that the
damages at the site are predominately structural failures with a few functional failures It
also appears that there are several mechanisms involved in the damages to Girls Road
Fatigue failures are characterized by cracking due to repeated stress applied to the
pavement at levels below the ultimate strength of the pavement material
Pavements are usually designed for a finite life span of repeated cyclical loading
at a certain stress level i e for a life of a certain number of years with a quantified
amount of traffic at a defined axle load
In general flexible pavements constructed of asphaltic concrete underlain by base
materials are designed for a life of about ten 10 years without the necessity for
overlayment with additional asphaltic concrete However the common design of
two 2 inches of asphaltic concrete and six 6 inches of base course assumes
light to medium passenger vehicles and does not account for heavy bus traffic
Continued life of the pavement is also dependent upon resurfacing before the
surface of the pavement becomes severely cracked due to fatigue
Heavy Traffic
Although we do not know when Girls Road was first constructed and paved it
appears that the road is relatively old as evidenced by the width and condition of
the road and the original use of a stone lined ditch along side the road Based on
our observations at the site it also appears that the road is subjected to a relatively
high amount of bus traffic It is our opinion that a portion of the damage is the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2623 00 Page 10
result of fatigue which has been accelerated by overloading of the pavement with
heavy bus traffic
Subgrade Failure
Pumping of the pavement and subgrade is significant cause of pavement fatigue
and failure Pumping is caused by water trapped in the pavement section When
water is entrapped and then subjected to external forces such as wheel loads
there is a hydraulic response causing an elastic distribution and subsequent
rebound of the forces similar to the action of a diaphragm pump This pumping
action can cause the pavement to crack The cracking allows additional water to
enter and further aggravate the situation Pumping of water trapped between
overlays can cause spalling of the pavement surface Spalling of the surface
course and cracking patterns generally associated with pumping were noted at the
project site
Drainage
Water in the subgrade of a pavement can cause softening of the subgrade soils
particularly expansive soils which can result in the pavement section losing
strength and becoming damaged or failing Water can enter the subgrade from
cracks in the pavement surface from seepage or high groundwater conditions in
the subgrade soils and from poor drainage adjacent to paved areas Once water
enters it can move through the relatively permeable base course to different areas
of the pavement Also since the subgrade is covered by the pavement surfacing
once water enters the subgrade it cannot evaporate and may remain there for
long periods of time unless drained from the subgrade area
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2623 00 Page 11
Our exploration and observations at the site indicate that the subgrade soils are
poorly drained Our observations also indicate that there is a large potential for
water to enter the subgrade from cracked pavement areas upslope of the project
site and from the sides of the road In particular it was noted that there are no
gutters on most of Haleakala Annex and that the runoff from the roof of the
building drains towards Girls Road There also may be some seepage in the slope
adjacent to Boring No 2
Utility Lines
Another significant problem at the site is the utility lines and other site
improvements which were installed through the original road section Our
observations at the site indicate that there is a lot of cracking and pavement
distortion in the areas where the newer water line and the concrete bus stop pad
were installed
When a trench is excavated through an existing pavement it is very difficult to
compact the trench backfill the new base course and the asphalt surface course
to match the surrounding subgrade and pavement section The new subgrade
and pavement section will continue to consolidate under traffic loads causing
settlement of the pavement over the trench Even after the consolidation stops
shear stresses can cause cracking through the pavement and through
subsequent overlays along the edges of the trench
Asphalt Deformation
Another consideration in damages to flexible pavements i e asphaltic concrete
pavements is that the asphaltic concrete is elastic and is subject to plastic
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2623 00 Page 12
deformation This plastic deformation tends to cause cracking and or distortion
of the pavement surface This is common when there is a slope to the pavement
or where the edges of the pavement are not confined It is particularly severe at
locations requiring frequent stops such as bus loading unloading areas
On slopes generally at grades of 10 per cent or steeper the asphaltic concrete
can plastically creep down the slope under traffic loads particularly at acceleration
and deceleration areas This plastic creep will cause the pavement to develop a
wavy surface In areas where traffic passes close to an unconfined edge of a
pavement the asphaltic concrete will creep towards the outer edge of the
pavement causing longitudinal cracks This is particularly true in curves where
centrifugal forces on the vehicles generate a thrust load on the pavement
To summarize it is our opinion that the most significant factors in the damages to
Girls Road are the age of the original pavement section the apparent infiltration of water
into the pavement section and the previous installation of utility lines and other
improvements through the original pavement It is our recommendation that the
pavement section be reconstructed from subgrade level up to a new surface course and
that adequate subgrade drainage be provided as part of the reconstruction effort The
reconstruction effort if performed as recommended below would include measures to
provide uniform subgrade support for the new pavement section thereby reducing the
potential of future cracking along existing utility trenches
Pavement Reconstruction
As discussed above it is our recommendation that the entire pavement section of
Girls Road be reconstructed This reconstruction should include removal of the existing
asphaltic surface and base course followed by replacement with a new pavement section
In our opinion the use of either flexible asphaltic concrete pavement or rigid portland
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2623 00 Page 13
cement concrete pavement would be suitable for the new pavement section Generally
the selection of pavement type would be dependent upon economic and planning factors
In general asphaltic concrete pavements have a lower initial cost but have shorter
service lives and require more frequent rehabilitation i e overlays of new asphaltic
concrete
Conversely portland cement concrete pavements have a higher initial cost but
longer service lives and require less maintenance In addition portland cement concrete
pavements are rigid and are not subject to creep or deformation failure The primary
disadvantage to portland cement concrete pavements is loss of subgrade support and
premature failure through pumping if the selection and construction of subgrade
materials are not very carefully controlled They also lose their structural integrity if it is
necessary to cut through them for utility installation
The reconstruction should consist of stripping the old asphaltic concrete pavement
and removing the existing coral base course material and subgrade material to the
appropriate depth for the subgrade of the new pavement section If the base course
material can be segregated and stockpiled without becoming contaminated by the
underlying clay soils it may be recycled as subbase course material Reuse of the
existing coral base course as base course material is not recommended since in our
experience coral base course has a tendency to deteriorate over time and it is most likely
that the existing base course has degraded
Any soft weak or yielding areas encountered during the stripping operations
should be over excavated to firm ground and the resulting excavation should be backfilled
with well compacted engineered fill
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2623 00 Page 14
After the existing pavement section has been removed the subgrade should be
scarified to a depth of approximately twelve 12 inches moisture conditioned to slightly
above optimum moisture content and then compacted to a minimum of 95 of its
maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 Any pumping or
yielding areas encountered during the compaction operations should be over excavated
to firm ground and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with material conforming
to Section 30 of the Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction Select Borrow
for Subbase Course dated should be placed in level lifts not
exceeding eight 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 of
their maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78

Subdrainage
It has been our experience with projects having similar soil conditions to find that
when a granular select material is used as a capping over clay soils there is a tendency
for water to accumulate at the interface between the select material and the underlying
Clay soils It appears that the granular select material is relatively permeable and that the
Clay soils are effectively impermeable Precipitation and landscape irrigation water infiltrate
through the topsoil into the select material and form a perched water table on the clay
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2623 00 Page 16
Problems have occurred when this water reaches the clay subgrade soils
supporting pavement sections When the water seeps into road subgrades it can soften
the subgrade soils and weaken the pavement structure
To minimize the potential of accumulation of water along the select material and
Clay soil interface we recommend that subdrains be installed to intercept water and divert
it to appropriate drainage locations such as drain inlets or to daylight on adjacent slopes
We recommend that subdrains be installed along the sides of and underneath the road
prisms
The subdrains under the road prism should generally be at spacings of about 200
feet A typical subdrain arrangement is shown on Plate 2 Subdrains should also be
installed at intersections with other roads and parking areas where seepage may enter
from these other paved areas The subdrains could either be of the conventional type
i a perforated pipe embedded in free draining gravel or one of the newer synthetic
types e g MiraDrain or ConTech 100
Off Site Drainage
It was noted during the observations made as part of our field exploration that the
slope uphill of Girls Road between the road and Haleakala Annex is being eroded by
runoff from the roof of the building The runoff water from the roof could potentially
infiltrate the subgrade of the reconstructed road and cause pavement deterioration
The runoff water could also infiltrate into the backfill of the future ditch walls which
will function as retaining walls This could result in excess pressures on the walls
Continued erosion of the slopes could also potentially cause their destabilization

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was developed to schedule appropriate tests
to aid in determining pertinent engineering properties of the soil and or rock materials
sampled
Classification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Moisture Content Determinations are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid
in determining certain properties such as relative saturation The moisture content
expressed as a percentage of the dry weight of the tested sample is shown at the
appropriate location on plates summarizing test results and at the appropriate sample
depths on the Boring Logs
Atterberg Limits tests are used to determine the plasticity of the fine grained portion
of soils The Plasticity Index of the material is used to classify the soil under the Unified
Soil Classification System The results of these tests are shown at the appropriate sample
depths on the Boring Logs
Physical Property Tests
Proctor ASTM D 1557 78 tests and California Bearing Ratio ASTM D 1883 tests
were performed on selected samples as an aid to Evaluating their embankment fill and
pavement support characteristics The results of the proctor and California Bearing Ratio
tests are presented on Plates B 1 through B 4 in the following Appendix
Expansion tests were performed on selected samples to provide data pertaining
to the swelling potential of the soils encountered at the site The results of these tests are
presented on Plate B 5 of the following Appendix
