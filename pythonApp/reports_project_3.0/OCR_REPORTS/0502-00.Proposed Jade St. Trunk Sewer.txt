INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our soils and geologic in
vestigation for the proposed trunk sewer line along Jade Street
in Makaha Valley Oahu Hawaii as shown on the Site Plan
Plate l
The purposes of this investigation were to evaluate the geologic
and soil conditions along the alignment and provide recommenda
tions for the design and construction of the proposed trunk
sewer line
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed sewer line begins at the makai edge of Farrington
Highway and continues mauka along Jade Street for a total length

of 4 150 feet The sewer line will be a gravity flow 24 inch
reinforced concrete pipe with its invert from 7 to 14 feet below
the existing grade The alignment of the line is shown on the
Site Plan Plate l
SITE CONDITIONS
Surface
The proposed alignment runs parallel to and on the northside
of the existing paved Jade Street roadway which traverses
through built up residential lots The alignment surface is
generally level and overgrown with sparse weeds and grass
Overhead telephone and electric wires cross the alignment to
the individual residences
The existing alignment ground elevation varies from 12 5 feet
at its makai end up to an elevation of about 64 feet at its
mauka terminal
Geology
The Makaha Valley between Puu Keaau and Puu Kamaileunu was
formed by erosion of Waianae Volcanic Mountain Range basalts
during the late Tertiary Period of Oahu s development The
plain between the mountains was formed by the combined construc
tional sedimentary processes of coral reef and alluvial plain
formation Clastic non marine alluvial debris was washed into
Page 3
W O 502 00
the Makaha embayment from the Waianae Volcanic Range as the
region sank The embayment may never have been very deep and
probably filled with sediment as it sank This is evidenced
by the presence of shallow water coralline algae reefs These
reefs apparently grew during the warmer inter glacial stages
of the Ice Age
A late stage lowering of sea level island emergence has left
the reef exposed above the sea level
Recent sub tropical weathering and erosional and depositional
processes have covered the reef to varying depths with allu
vial clay
To explore the subsurface conditions at the site eight borings
were drilled at the locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 1
A more detailed description of the field exploration and labor
atory testing is contained in the Appendix which also includes
the log of each boring and the results of our laboratory tests

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Relatively good subsurface conditions were encountered in our
borings along the alignment Very hard coral and fractured
rock layers exist in the lower makai and upper mauka end por
tions of the proposed alignment respectively These layers
may be difficult to excavate The sewer trench can be excava
ted with vertical sides Granular bedding material will be
required beneath the conduit
Loads
Loads on buried conduits are influenced by the width of exca
vation unit weight of backfill material and frictional
resistance between backfill material and trench walls If
the trench is made too wide the pipe may be subjected to
excessive vertical load
We therefore recommend that the trench width be kept to a
minimum The distance between the pipe and trench walls gener
ally should not exceed one fourth of the pipe diameter The
Trench and Bedding Diagram is illustrated on Plate 2

Bearing Capacities
The relatively hard coral dense silty sand and weathered fractured
rock which were encountered at the site will provide good support
ing capacities For design purposes a value of 4000 pounds per
square foot can be used for bearing capacities
Based on the above value total and differential settlements will
be negligible

Bedding
Bedding conditions will effect the distribution of stresses
beneath the pipe and its load carrying capacity The contact
between the pipe and foundation material is therefore an
important design consideration
We recommend that a Class B bedding be used to support the
pipe This can be achieved by two methods
1 Shaped Bottom with Tamped Backfill The bottom of the
trench excavation should be shaped to a cylindrical surface
with a radius of 2 inches greater than the radius to the
outside of the pipe and sufficient to allow 60 percent of
the width of the pipe to be bedded in a sand fill placed
in the shaped excavation Carefully compacted backfill
shall be placed at the sides of the pipe to a thickness
of at least 12 inches above the top of the pipe It may
be difficult to shape thesbottom of the trench where hard
coral and basalt rock is encountered
2 Compacted Granular Bedding with Tamped Backfill The
pipe should be bedded in compacted fine granular material
placed on a flat trench bottom The bedding should have
a minimum thickness of 6 inches under the conduit The
granular bedding should have a width of one fourth the out
Side pipe diameter on each side and should extend halfway
Page 8

up the pipe at the sides see Plate 2 The remainder of
the side fills should be filled with compacted material
CONSTRUCTION
Excavation
The maximum depth of excavation for this project will be approx
imately 15 feet and no groundwater is anticipated except near
Farrington Highway
Construction methods and equipment used for trench excavation
should be left up to the Contractor within practical limits
but it is anticipated that normal excavating equipment may be
used for most of the trenches
The coralline algae reef and basalt rock encountered in our
borings will contain local hard areas that may require hard
ripping blasting or jack hammering

used should be extended to a minimum of two feet below the
bottom of the pipeline

Sheeting and Bracing
It is our opinion that the trench walls will require shoring
mainly in the middle portion of the pipeline where sandy silts
were encountered and possible in the lower makai portion
where some sandy silts and coral sands were encountered The
clayey silts and decomposed basalts should stand vertically
for short periods provided no surcharge loads are placed with
in 10 feet of the excavation The excavation should be pro
vided with bracing in accordance with all applicable safety
requirements The design and safety of the sheeting should
be the responsibility of the Contractor
As an alternate the Contractor may elect to cut back the
Slope where silty sands and sands are encountered It is our
recommendation that the trench walls be cut back to a 1
horizontal to 1 vertical slope It should be taken into
account that greater vertical pressures will be transmitted
to the pipe by the widening of the trench Local sloughing
may occur at sand seams or at decomposed basalt rock areas
These conditions may require alternate trench stability
recommendations
The Contractor at the site should bring to the Soil Engineer s
attention any changed conditions affecting trench excavation
Stability Our firm will be available for consultation re
garding trench stability

The design of the shoring system should be based on the Lateral
Pressure Distribution Diagram shown on Plate 3
Backfill
Backfill material around the conduit should consist of selected
free draining well graded material and should be placed with
care by hand or suitable equipment so that the pipe will not be
displaced or damaged Backfill should be compacted by jetting
or mechanical equipment to a density consistent with design
parameters We recommend a minimum of 90 percent of maximum
density
The onsite materials especially the coral sands and silty
sands are generally suitable as backfill material provided
organic materials debris and other objectional material is
not included in the backfill Rock greater than 6 inches in
diameter should not be allowed in the backfill

APPENDIX
FIELD EXPLORATION
Subsurface soil conditions along the alignment were explored by
drilling eight test borings utilizing truck mounted rotary auger
equipment The depth of the borings ranged from 14 to 21 feet
below the existing ground surface Locations of the borings are
shown on Plate 1 in the text of this report The Boring Logs
are presented in Plates A 1A to A 1H in this appendix
In all inch diameter undisturbed or Standard Pene
tration Test samples were taken at selected depths The sampler
was driven with a 140 pound hammer free falling a distance of
30 inches The number of blows required to drive the sampler
the last 12 inches were recorded and are plotted at the appro
priate depths on the boring logs Samples obtained were
visually classified in the field The classifications were
verified by inspection and testing in the laboratory All
soils were classified in accordance with the Unified Soil
Classification All rocks and corals were classified by local
geologic terminology
LABORATORY TESTING
In place densities and field moisture contents were made on all
undisturbed samples These results are presented on the Log of
