2.1. Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers
an area of approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of
the following five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea (activity during
recent geologic time), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801-1803), and Mauna Loa and Kilauea
(both still active).
The project site is situated on the western flank of Hualalai Mountain, which
makes up the west-central portion of the island. The lava formation encountered at the
site appears to be of both a'a and pahoehoe type flows, which spread and ponded as
they approached the ocean. A’a lava typically is characterized by a porous, rough, and
irregular flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including
cobbles and boulders. Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth, rope-like or billowy
surface and an internal structure of vesicular (porous) rock.
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas, especially pahoehoe type
lava. Cavities form when the lava is in a molten state and represent both lava tubes
(intra-flow cavities) and blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities). Lava tubes form when
molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube-like structure that may
extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow. Blisters and pockets are
generally smaller in horizontal extent.
Basalt is considered to be relatively permeable and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions. In general, water is transmitted
through the porous rock matrix, along joints, fractures and inter-flow contacts, cavities,
and along clinker layers. Because of the high permeability, clinker and cavities serve as
the primary water transmission features.

2.2 Site Description
The project site is at the County of Hawaii baseyard (Tax Map Key: 8-1-16: 038)
along Mamalahoa Highway in the Kealakekua area of South Kona on the Island of
Hawaii.
Based on our observations, the site is about 15 feet higher in elevation than
Mamalahoa Highway. The west side of the site (closer to Mamalahoa Highway) appears
to have been filled. A rock wall without any grout between the void spaces is used to
retain the fills. The rock wall is along the northwest half, the west, and southwest half
property lines. The rock wall has collapsed along a portion of the western property line
(near the northwest corner of the site) exposing the retained fills. We understand that
the rock wall may have collapsed during the earthquakes that occurred on October 15,
2006. A crack was observed along the west side property line behind the retaining wall
indicating some lateral movement of the fill mass and rock wall occurred during the
earthquakes.
The site is relatively level except for the driveway, which has a steep incline.
Two one-story buildings were observed. The larger building is along the north side and
the smaller building is at the southeast corner of the site. The buildings appear to be
wood and steel-framed structures with the steel frame portion of the structures having
corrugated steel sidings.
The asphaltic concrete pavements for the driveways and parking areas appear to
be in relatively poor condition. Several potholes and patches were observed throughout
the parking areas and driveways.

2.4 Seismic Design Considerations
Based on the 1997 UBC, the Island of Hawaii is located within Seismic Zone 4
with an associated effective peak ground acceleration of 0.40g. Therefore, the project
site may be subjected to seismic activity, and seismic design considerations will need to
be addressed for the project. The following sections provide discussions on the
seismicity of the Island of Hawaii and the soil profile for seismic design at the site.
2.4.1 Earthquake and Seismicity
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the
tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to
volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or
during volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not
erupt. The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but
most are so small that they only can be detected by sensitive instruments.
However, some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause
minor to moderate damage.

SECTION 3 — DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Detailed discussions and specific recommendations for design of foundations,

3.1 Building Foundations
Based on our field exploration, the west side of the site consisted of fills ranging
from about 9.5 to 14.5 feet thick. The fills on the east side ranged from about 0.4 to
2.5 feet thick. The relative density of the fills varies from loose to dense. Based on the
subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend supporting the new
buildings on a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and/or continuous
footings. Because the relative density of the in-situ fills varies at the site, we believe that
the in-situ fills should be excavated a minimum of 24 inches below the bottom of footing
elevations or until basalt rock is encountered, whichever occurs first. The resulting
over-excavation should be backfilled with structural fill materials (6-inch minus)
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.
We recommend using an allowable bearing pressure of up to 4,000 pounds per
square foot (psf) for design of footings bearing on well-compacted structural fill
materials or basalt rock. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be
increased by 50 percent for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces.
Footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent grade. Foundations next to other foundations, utility trenches, easements or
other retaining walls should be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane
extending upward from the bottom edge of the structure or utility trench, or the footings
should extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This requirement is
necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with additional
structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement.

If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our

Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by friction between the

3.2 Foundation Probing
Based on our exploration and experience in the area, voids and/or cavities may
be present in the basalt formation. Due to the anticipated light structural loads, we
believe that the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of the
cavities below the foundations bearing on basalt rock is relatively small. If it is desired to
further reduce the potential for loss of foundation support, then a program of cavity
probing and grouting at the foundations bearing on basalt rock may be conducted
during construction.
For estimating purposes, we recommend drilling probe holes at 10-foot on center
for the continuous strip footings. In addition, probe holes should be drilled at each
isolated spread footing location. The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter
and should extend to a depth of at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of foundation.

If cavities and/or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing

Field Exploration
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling six borings, designated as
Boring Nos. 1 through 6, extending to depths ranging from about 16 to 26 feet below the
existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan,
Plate 2. The borings were drilled using truck-mounted drill equipment equipped with
rotary coring tools.
Materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist, who observed the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed by visual
observation and testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings,
Plates A-1 through A-6.
Samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch
OD standard penetration sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths.
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113, Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left
in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description.
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core
run, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals."

APPENDIX C
Percolation Testing
APPENDIX C
Field Percolation Testing
One field percolation test was performed in Boring No. 4A in general accordance
with the State of Hawaii, Department of Health's Administrative Rules,
Chapter 11-62-31.2, "Wastewater Systems" and Chapter 10 of the Ten State’s
Standards to evaluate the permeability of the subsurface soils for wastewater disposal.
The test was performed by drilling 4-inch diameter borehole extending to depths of
about 5.0 feet below the existing ground surface. Approximately 1 to 2 inches of gravel
was then placed at the bottom of the borehole to protect the bottom of the hole from
scouring and sediments. During the test, the hole was carefully filled with water to a
minimum depth of 6 inches above the gravel. The time intervals and water drops were
then recorded to provide data upon which the calculation of the percolation test was
based. The percolation test results are presented on Plate C-1.
In addition, one percolation test was conducted in Boring No. 3 using the
constant head method. In general, the drilled boring had a diameter of about 3.5 inches
and extended to a depth of about 20.0 feet below the existing ground surface. Water
was pumped into the borehole for the constant head test method. The flow rate was
recorded by timing and calculating the volume as a relatively constant flow rate was
achieved. The constant head test results are presented on Plate C-2.
