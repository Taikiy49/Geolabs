In accordance with your request, we conducted a geotechnical engineering 
evaluation in support of the mass grading design for the proposed Mid-Level Well 
Development, Phase 1 project in the Holualoa area on the Island of Hawaii. This letter 
report presents our earthwork recommendations for the site development of the project 
only. It should be noted that the earthwork recommendations provided are subject to the 
limitations noted at the end of this letter report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project is located at the end of Hienalolii Road in the Holualoa area in the District 
of North Kona on the Island of Hawaii. The project generally consists of constructing a new 
well pad for the County of Hawaii, Department of Water Supply. We understand that new 
structures are not planned for the current phase of work. 
Based on the preliminary plans provided, the new well pad will have a finish pad 
elevation of about +762 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). Mass grading consisting of cuts of 
less than about 1 to 2 feet in depth and fills of up to about 8 feet in height may be required 
to achieve design grades. 
EXISTING CONDITIONS 
The project site is located at TMK:  7-5-003: 001 in the Holualoa area in the District 
of North Kona on the Island of Hawaii. Access to the project site is through an unpaved dirt 
road from the end of Hienalolii Road. There are multiple locked cattle gates and a 12-foot 
long wooden bridge along the dirt road before reaching the project site. 
The project site was generally moderately vegetated with wild grasses at the time of 
our site reconnaissance. There are also several large trees of up to about 6 feet in diameter

ANTICIPATED SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Based on our site reconnaissance and experience in the vicinity of the project site, 
we anticipate that the subsurface conditions may generally consist of surface soils 
underlain by dense basalt formation. The surface soils generally consist of stiff sandy 
silts. Rock outcrops were observed in many areas of the project site. Therefore, we 
envision that the thickness of the surface soils, where present, is relatively thin. Basalt 
formation is anticipated at or near the existing ground surface. The basalt formation at the 
site is generally anticipated to be slightly to moderately weathered and hard to very hard. 
In general, cavities and/or voids are commonly present in the basaltic lava flows in 
the vicinity. Therefore, the presence of cavities and/or voids should be expected during 
construction. 
Groundwater is not anticipated to be encountered during construction. However, 
water levels are expected to vary with seasonal precipitation, stormwater runoff, and other 
factors.

thoroughly cleared and grubbed. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and other

unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site to reduce the 
potential for contamination of the excavated materials. 
Loose and/or soft soils, where encountered, should be over-excavated to expose 
firm and/or dense materials, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
well-compacted fills. The contractor should exercise caution while clearing and grading 
near collapsed lava tube features and/or cavities, which may represent potential lava 
tubes or cavities that may further collapse under the load of heavy construction 
equipment. 
After clearing and grubbing, areas to be filled should be ripped using a tooth of a 
large bulldozer to a depth of about 3 feet below the existing ground surface at the 
commencement of grading. The purpose of ripping the subgrades is to assist in detecting 
and collapsing cavities and/or voids contained in the near-surface materials encountered 
at the project site. 
After the fill surfaces have been ripped to a depth of about 3 feet below the surface, 
the ground should be leveled with the use of a Caterpillar D-9 bulldozer or similar size 
bulldozer working the surface a minimum of four passes to provide a relatively level 
platform. After leveling of the rugged surfaces with a bulldozer, we recommend 
proof-rolling the fill subgrades with a large vibratory drum roller (minimum 15 tons static 
weight) for a minimum of eight passes to help detect and collapse near-surface cavities 
and/or voids. The vibratory drum roller should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet 
per minute (3.5 miles per hour). 
Finished subgrades in cut areas should be proof-rolled with a minimum 15-ton 
(static weight) vibratory drum roller for a minimum of eight passes. The vibratory drum 
roller also should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute. The requirement 
for ripping the ground surface by a depth of about 3 feet (in fill areas) may be omitted for 
cut subgrades. In addition, proof-rolling of the subgrade with a Caterpillar D-9 bulldozer 
for a minimum of four passes may be omitted in the cut subgrades, provided that the 
subgrades are relatively level to operate the vibratory drum roller. 
The ripping and proof-rolling operations should be performed in the presence of a 
Geolabs representative. Yielding areas, loose areas, or cavities disclosed during clearing 
and proof-rolling operations should be over-excavated and backfilled with compacted fill 
materials. Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for 
over-excavation of the collapsed lava tube features (and other features) and backfilled 
with compacted fill to account for variations in the over-excavation and backfill quantities. 
Fills and Backfills 
In general, we recommend capping the fill areas of the project site with a minimum 
2-foot thick layer of structural fill materials. Structural fill materials should consist of 
well-graded granular materials generally less than 6 inches in maximum dimension with 
sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass. In addition,

structural fill materials should contain less than 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 
sieve by weight. In general, the excavated on-site materials may be reused as a source 
of structural fill materials, provided that they are processed to meet the maximum particle 
size and gradation requirements presented above. 
Fill and backfill materials below the minimum 2-foot thick structural fill layer may 
consist of general fill materials. General fill and backfill materials should consist of 
well-graded granular materials, the majority of which are less than 12 inches with an 
absolute maximum dimension of 18 inches. Materials between 12 and 18 inches in 
particle size should be limited to about 15 percent or less of the total volume of general 
fill materials. The excavated on-site materials, if less than 18 inches in maximum 
dimension, may be used as a source of general fill or backfill materials, provided that they 
are processed to meet the above gradation requirements for general fill. If the excavated 
materials do not contain sufficient fines to produce a well-graded material, off-site borrow 
or on-site rock crushing of larger rock fragments or boulders should be considered to 
provide the required gradation and particle sizes to develop a well-graded material. 
Where overcutting of the finished cut subgrades occur during the grading 
operations, backfilling of the overcut area should be treated as fill areas. Therefore, the 
above fill material recommendations should apply to the overcut areas, and the fill 
materials required for backfilling will depend on the depth of over-cut below the finished 
subgrade. 
Imported fill materials (where required) should be free of organics and deleterious 
materials and should be suitable for the intended use. In addition, imported fill materials 
should be observed and/or tested by Geolabs prior to being transported to the site for the 
intended use. 
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in 
loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction. General fills and backfills should 
be placed in level lifts of about 18 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with 
ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practical in fills composed of 
rocks, boulders, and/or cobbles. Instead, a testing program should be conducted at the 
start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a Geolabs representative 
to evaluate the number of passes needed to achieve the desired level of compaction. 
Based on this testing program, the number of passes may then be used as the field 
criterion for adequate compaction.

Excavation 
Based on our site reconnaissance, we envision the project site generally is 
underlain by basalt formation at or near the ground surface. It is anticipated that the 
fractured basalt formation may be excavated readily with normal heavy excavation 
equipment, such as excavators, and ripped with large bulldozers. However, some of the 
excavations in native ground will encounter dense basalt formation and will likely require 
the use of hoerams or chipping. 
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the subsurface materials are 
based on our visual observation of the site conditions and our experience in the vicinity 
of the project site. Contractors should be encouraged to examine the site conditions to 
make their own reasonable and prudent interpretation. 
Cut and Fill Slopes 
In general, we believe the cut slopes planned at the site exposing the basalt 
formation may be designed with a slope inclination of one horizontal to one vertical 
(1H:1V) or flatter. In areas where surface soils, clinker layers or highly fractured basalt 
are exposed at the cut slope faces, the cut slopes may need to be flattened to inclinations 
of 2H:1V or flatter. Therefore, provisions to flatten the cut slopes and/or construct 
retaining walls should be considered in the project design where 1H:1V cut slopes are 
planned. 
Permanent fill slopes constructed of the general fill and structural fill materials 
recommended herein may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. In 
addition, fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be benched.

LIMITATIONS 
The findings and recommendations submitted in this letter report are based, 
in part, from our site reconnaissance and experience in the vicinity of the project site. 
Variations of subsurface conditions from those assumed herein may occur, and the nature 
and extent of these variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If 
variations then appear evident, Geolabs should be contacted to re-evaluate the 
recommendations presented in this letter report.

Phase 1 project in accordance with generally accepted geotechnical engineering 
principles and practices. No warranty is expressed or implied. This letter report has been 
prepared solely for the purpose of assisting the design engineers in support of the 
earthwork considerations for the project only. 
The owner/client should be aware that unanticipated subsurface conditions are 
commonly encountered. Unforeseen subsurface conditions, such as perched

may require additional probing or corrections in the field (which may result in construction 
delays) to attain a properly constructed project. Therefore, a sufficient contingency fund 
is recommended to accommodate these possible extra costs.
