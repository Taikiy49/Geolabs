INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering consultation
performed for the proposed traffic signal light poles and utilities as part of the proposed
Ward Avenue Extension project in the District of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii
The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and _ geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our engineering analyses based on nearby previous field
explorations These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations for the
proposed traffic signal lights manholes and utility structures only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

intersection of Ward Avenue and Ala Moana Boulevard The proposed project also
includes construction of new manholes utility structures and utilities The proposed
Ward Avenue Extension project site extends from Auahi Street to the intersection of Ilalo
Street and Ahui Street as shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central
Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of Koolau and its geomorphology
and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea
level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain
The subsurface conditions in the Ala Moana area are dominated by fossil
Pleistocene Age coral algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of
the sea which are intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic
section in this area is Koolau Basalt
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower
relative to the present sea level of Oahu About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise
in sea level occurred During that rise the valleys in the project area were drowned In
the last 10 000 years or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous
and marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal
environments resulting in thick deposits of soft loose sediments along the coast in the
areas that were formerly valleys and drainageways
In the early part of this century the Ala Moana area consisted of low marshy
wetlands As the city of Honolulu grew and the Ala Moana area was urbanized
man made fills were placed to reclaim the marshy areas and lagoons The fill was placed
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3847 10 Page 5
upon lagoonal deposits consisting of silty sands with coral fragments The lower clayey
material was deposited during periodic influxes of non marine alluvial debris that
apparently smothered the coral reef development Land development and reclamation
projects within the last 100 years have brought the Ala Moana area to its present form
Many of the resulting fills are of poor quality in terms of supporting heavy structural loads

poles may be supported on drilled shaft type foundations to resist the generally high
ground line and torsional moments induced by loading of the poles

each traffic signal light pole structure The recommended lengths of the drilled shaft
range from 8 to 12 feet below the ground surface

loads in addition to either torsional or ground line moments using the computer software
program LPILE Plus for Windows The analyses were based on a free head condition
and a maximum lateral deflection of not more than inch at the top of the shaft

Drilled Shaft Foundations
Based on the loading conditions provided and the subsurface conditions
encountered we recommend that the poles for the traffic signal lights be supported by
drilled shaft foundations Based on the bottom diameter of the steel poles 24 inches at
the base the available auger core barrel sizes from the local drilling companies and the
shaft lengths anticipated drilled shaft diameters of 30 inches were considered in our
GEOLABS HAWAII

foundation analyses The major considerations in our foundation analyses included the
lateral loads and governing moments induced in the drilled shaft foundations
In general lateral load resistance for drilled shafts is a function of the stiffness of
the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable deflection at the top of the shaft
and induced moment in the shaft Based on a maximum horizontal deflection of inch at
the top of the drilled shaft subjected to torsional moments or ground line overturning
moments and lateral forces we have evaluated the required drilled shaft lengths using
the computer software program LPILE Plus for Windows This program is a
microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference laterally loaded pile program originally
developed at the University of Texas at Austin The program solves for deflection and
bending moment along a drilled shaft under lateral loads as a function of depth The
analysis was carried out with the use of internally generated non linear p y curves to
represent soil moduli The lateral deflection was then computed using the appropriate soil
moduli at various depths The analyses were performed for a laterally loaded 30 inch
diameter cast in place concrete drilled shaft The results of our engineering analyses are

Construction Considerations
The performance of drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the Contractor s
method of construction construction procedures and workmanship As a result we
recommend that a representative from Geolabs Hawaii be present to observe the
installation of drilled shafts during construction In our opinion the following may have a
significant impact on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we anticipate that drilling
through loose soils will be required during construction of the majority of the drilled shaft
foundations To reduce the potential for caving in of the drilled holes temporary casing of
the drilled holes will be needed during construction Installation of the casing may be by
vibration driving or twisting to advance the casing The contractor should be made aware
that temporary casing of the drilled holes will be needed In addition care should be
exercised during removal of the temporary casing to reduce the potential for necking of
the drilled shaft Therefore a minimum 5 foot head of concrete should be maintained
above the bottom of the casing and or groundwater level during removal of the casing
The load carrying capacity of drilled shafts depends to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in drilling the
shaft holes and in placing concrete in the holes The bottom of all drilled shafts should be
cleaned of disturbed material prior to constructing the foundation The bottom should
also be fairly level Backfill against the shaft foundations should not be allowed
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids slurry is not recommended
Groundwater conditions are anticipated therefore placement of concrete by wet
construction methods using a tremie pipe will be required A low shrink concrete mix with
high slump 6 to 9 inch slump range should be used to provide close contact between
the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils The concrete should be placed in a suitable
manner to reduce segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix and to displace
disturbed material from the bottom of the drilled shaft

Dewatering
Dewatering of the groundwater may be necessary for the construction of
manholes and utility structures Therefore we recommend that dewatering provisions
such as a National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System NPDES permit be included
in the contract documents for construction The Contractor should consult his her
independent consultant or the State of Hawaii Department of Health for the latest
regulations and information pertaining to the permit application
Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the Contractor The selection of
equipment and methods of dewatering should be determined by the Contractor who
should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during
construction depending on conditions encountered The dewatering method selected
should have minimal impact on the water level surrounding the proposed excavation In
addition dewatering operations should be performed such that only clear water is
pumped Excessive pumping which removes soil fines may result in a blowing or
heaving of the excavation bottom or sides

Dewatering Considerations
We suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in selection of a
suitable method of dewatering
a The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or damage to
existing structures
b The dewatering method should maintain stability of and also provide safe
and dry working conditions in the excavation
Cc The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow for
modifications to accommodate various ground conditions
Dewatering Precaution and Monitoring
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural
water level must be carefully evaluated by the Contractor prior to dewatering It is
recommended that a qualified Geotechnical Engineer be retained by the
Contractor to design and evaluate the dewatering system used
The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be made the sole
responsibility of the Contractor His her qualified representative who should be
required to be continuously present on site during dewatering activities will have
the best opportunity to promptly observe the effects of dewatering during
construction and to implement as soon as possible necessary precautionary or
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3847 10 Page 17
remedial measures including but not limited to stopping or slowing down the
dewatering operations
Where encountered at the bottom of excavations permeable granular subsoils
may be susceptible to piping and quick conditions The dewatering operations
should be carried out without creating a quick condition or softening at the
excavation bottoms Therefore the project dewatering operations should be done
without pumping out soil fines pumping clear water only and should be
coordinated with shoring installation such that the excavation stability is not
adversely affected
Water drawdown outside the excavation will cause additional settlement resulting
from consolidation of the loose soft compressible soils Therefore the use of a
deep well system outside the excavations to draw down the groundwater level
should not be allowed Special caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering
utility trenches connected to excavations If this occurs the granular bedding
and or backfill in the utility trenches could act as subdrains and cause significant
areal groundwater drawdown resulting in settlements and potential damage to
utility lines and or other existing adjacent structures
