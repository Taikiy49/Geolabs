2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in
age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The project site is
located on the southern flank of the Koolau Volcano
The Koolau Volcano was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the
northwest to southeast trending rift zones These lavas consist of interbedded
pahoehoe and a a type flows with pahoehoe lava being the dominant form A a is the
Hawaiian term for basaltic lava flow typified by a rough jagged spinose clinkery
surface Pahoehoe is the Hawaiian term for basaltic lava flows typified by smooth
billowy or ropy surfaces After the cessation of the main shield building phase of Koolau
Volcano there was a long period of erosion
During the Pleistocene Epoch there was a renewal of volcanic activity with the
eruptions of vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series Along the crest of the ridge along
the westerly side of Manoa Valley there are the Tantalus Roundtop and Sugarloaf
vents When these vents were active one or more lava flows from these vents spilled
down the westerly wall of the valley and moved across the valley floor These lava
flows ponded and filled the lower portion of Manoa Valley The ponding allowed the
lava to densify as it cooled forming dense massive rock The new lava flows from these
vents blocked Manoa Stream and forced the stream to the east between the East West
Center and Waahila Rise These flows generally terminated in the Moiliili District where
the basalt rocks were quarried in the past Reef limestone related to the Waimanalo
stand of the sea underlies the basalt at the former Moiliili Quarry

After clearing and grubbing areas at grade or areas designated to receive fills
should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test Designation
D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight corresponding to
the maximum dry density

Field Exploration
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling seven borings designated
as Boring Nos 1 through 7 extending to depths ranging from about 22 5 to 60 feet below
the existing ground pavement surface The approximate locations of the borings drilled at
the project site are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using both
truck mounted and track mounted drill rigs equipped with continuous flight augers and
rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered in the test borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through
A 7
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the coral rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of soil rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of coral rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Two one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples of
the near surface fill soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the subsurface soils The
results of these tests are summarized on Plate B 1
Three sieve analysis tests ASTM C 117 and C 136 were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution of the soils are provided
on Plate B 2
Eight unconfined compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on selected
core samples to evaluate their unconfined compressive strength of the basalt rock
formation encountered at the existing quarry wall Results of the unconfined compression
tests are presented on Plate B 3
