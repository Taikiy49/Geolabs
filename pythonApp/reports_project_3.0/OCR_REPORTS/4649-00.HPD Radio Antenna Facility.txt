INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the Honolulu Police Department Radio Antenna Facility project located on Puu Papaa
Ridge in the immediate vicinity of the main gate of Kaneohe Marine Corps Base on the
Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1

This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses These
recommendations are intended for the design of foundations and site preparation only The
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end
of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project involves the construction of a radio antenna facility on a ridge in the
immediate vicinity of the main gate of Kaneohe Marine Corps Base on the Island of Oahu
Hawaii Based on the information provided the radio antenna facility will consist of a
legs spreading out 8 foot center to center distance on a 12 foot by 12 foot footing The
facility will also include a control building and generator pad

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age
After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion incised deep valleys into
the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and
tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The site is located on the eastern side of Oahu just south of Mokapu Peninsula near
the top of the ridgeline These volcanic rocks are part of the Caldera Complex of the Koolau
Volcanic Series The present interpretation is that the lavas were accumulated as flows in the
caldera of the Koolau volcano

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by dense closely
fractured basaltic rock Based on the subsoil conditions encountered at the project site we
believe that shallow foundations consisting of spread footings or mat foundation bearing on
the compacted structural fill material or dense basaltic rock may be used to support the
proposed antenna control building and generator pad
The basalt formation encountered near the existing ground was hard but closely
fractured with Rock Quality Designations RQD of less than 50 percent The footings for the
new antenna structure should be embedded into the basalt formation Detailed discussions

Antenna Foundations
Based on the generally competent subsoil conditions encountered at the site we
believe that a spread footing or mat foundation bearing on the in situ dense rock formation
may be used to support the proposed antenna tower structure An allowable bearing pressure
of up to 5 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for design of shallow footing
foundations bearing on the in situ dense rock formation This bearing value is for
caused by wind or seismic forces
The bottom of the footing foundation should be embedded a minimum of 2 feet below
the lowest adjacent finish grade Deeper embedment may be required to provide the required
capacity against uplift and moment reactions at the base of the tower Resistance to uplift
loads may be provided by the dead weight of the structure and the footing foundation
Additional resistance to uplift loads may be provided by the weight of the backfill material
placed above the footings if applicable A unit weight of 110 pounds per cubic foot pcf may
be used for the compacted on site materials
The bottom of the footing excavation should be firm and unyielding prior to placement
of reinforcing steel or concrete Soft and or loose materials encountered at the bottom of the
footing excavation should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The
resulting over excavation should be backfilled concrete
Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below
a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom
edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines
We estimate the total foundation settlements under the anticipated light structural loads
to be less than 1 inch with differential settlements between the footings to be on the order of

Building Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we recommend
that shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings be used to support
the proposed control room and generator pad at the site An allowable bearing pressure of
up to may be used to design the new footings bearing on compacted structural
fill or medium hard to hard basalt formation This bearing value is for dead plus live loads
and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade Footings constructed near the tops of slopes or on slopes should be
embedded deep enough such that the horizontal distance measured from the outside edge
at the base of the footing to the slope face is not less than 6 feet Foundations located next
to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane
extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the
utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable
foundation settlement

Tiedown Anchor System for Spread Footings
Additional uplift resistance for the footings may be provided by post tensioned tiedown
anchors Tiedown anchors could be embedded into the spread footing for the supporting legs
Alternatively the tiedown anchor may be connected to the supporting leg directly embedded
in the footing The tiedown anchors should consist of steel reinforcing bars or tendons
installed in a drilled hole which is grouted with structural concrete The tiedown anchors
fasten to competent rock at a location well away from the structure itself This approach is
accomplished by creating two zones along the steel anchor A minimum bonded length of
10 feet should be provided below the unbonded length over which the tension load is
transferred to the surrounding rock mass A minimum unbonded length of 10 feet should be
provided between the top of the anchor and the structure over which no tension load is
transferred to the surrounding rock mass The unbonded length should be grouted with lean
concrete or CLSM Clean sand should be placed at the interface between the structural
concrete and the CLSM portion of the tiedown anchors
Loads acting on the tiedown anchors are resisted by bond stress between the structural
concrete and the surrounding rock in the bonded length For preliminary estimating purposes
GEOLABS INC
W O 4649 00 Page 8
we believe that a bond stress of 5 ksf may be used to size the tiedown anchors It should be
noted that the bond stress is dependent on the type of rock and grout the overburden stress
and the construction procedures used in installing the tiedown anchors
Each of the permanent tiedown anchors should be load tested to confirm the capacity
of the tiedown anchors The proof ioad tests should consist of subjecting the permanent
tiedown anchors to at least 150 percent of the design loads of the tiedown anchor The full test
load of the tiedown should be held for at least 15 minutes
The tiedown anchor system should be installed by a specialty contractor experienced
in such work and has completed construction of at least five tieback or tiedown systems Due
to the specialized nature of the tiedown anchor construction observation and testing of the
tiedown anchors should be designated as a Special Inspection item Therefore we
recommend that a representative from Geolabs Special Inspector be present to observe the
geotechnical aspects of the tiedown anchor construction including observation and testing of
the tiedown anchors
Slabs On Grade
For concrete slabs on grade not subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that a
minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation be placed below the slabs to serve as a capillary break and to provide more uniform
support of the slabs To reduce moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor
coverings an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the No 3B Fine gravel
Two inches of moist fine sand may be considered above the moisture barrier to provide
protection of the moisture barrier and to aid in curing of the slab concrete Provisions of the
2 inches of moist fine sand above the moisture barrier will be left up to the discretion of the
project structural engineer Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be
considered since they can better mask minor slab cracking
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project We recommend
that concrete walkways be supported on 4 inches of non expansive select granular fill The
GEOLABS INC

select granular fill should be compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Crack
control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion
joints at right angle intersections Areas adjacent to the slab should be backfilled tightly
against the slab with on site soil and graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce the
potential for water ponding around the foundations
Excavation
Based on our field exploration at the site the project site is underlain by fractured
basalt formations ranging from medium hard to hard at shallow depths We anticipate that
the basalt formation may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment such as
ripping with large bulldozers where the rock is closely fractured or highly weathered
However excavations in localized areas will likely require the use of hoerams chipping or
blasting
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based
on our visual observation of the existing rock formation and field data from the boring
performed at the site We recommend that contractors be encouraged to examine the site
conditions and the boring data to make their own interpretations
Grading
Based on the existing topography we envision that the site grading work for the
proposed project will generally consist of minor cuts and fills
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared Debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed and
disposed of off site Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas
designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over excavated to expose firm
material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted engineered
fill The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site
Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation
and engineered fill placement to account for variations in the over excavation quantities
GEOLABS INC
W O 4649 00 Page 10
The smaller on site rock materials generated from the cut areas may be reused as a
source of general fill material provided that they are free of vegetation deleterious materials
and rock fragments greater than 6 inches in maximum dimension If required import fill should
consist of non expansive select granular material such as crushed basalt or coral The
material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in
largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No
200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have
a maximum swell of less than 1 percent Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs
for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its
intended use
Fill materials should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the
in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is
the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other
types of acceptable compaction equipment Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be
allowed to compact the on site soils
Site grading operations should be observed by a representative from Geolabs It is
important that a representative from our office observe the site grading operation to evaluate
whether undesirable materials are encountered and whether the exposed soil rock conditions
are similar to those encountered in our field exploration
Utility Trench
We envision that new utility connections may be required for the proposed project In
general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding consisting of
6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under the pipes Free
GEOLABS INC
W O 4649 00 Page 11
draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
should also be used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide
adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus
reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material
to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide
adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in settlement
of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of select fill material The backfill should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in maximum 8 inch level loose
lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce
the potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the
upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement finish grades should be compacted to
at least 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the structures should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems be
installed around the structures and that the discharge be diverted away from the foundation
and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should
also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms
and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils Drainage swales
should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain surface run off away from the
slabs and foundations
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent loss of strength and or heaving of the near surface soils The foundation
excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
one boring designated as Boring No 1 extending to a depth of about 29 feet below the
existing ground surface The boring was drilled using portable drilling equipment equipped
with continuous flight augers and coring tools The approximate location of the boring
drilled is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the boring were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered in the boring are presented on the Log of Boring Plate A 1
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
