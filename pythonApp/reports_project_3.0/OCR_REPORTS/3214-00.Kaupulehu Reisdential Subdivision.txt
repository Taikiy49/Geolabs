INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering reconnaissance
and exploration performed for the proposed Kaupulehu Residential Subdivision
Development of Parcels A A 1 and K 1 through K 4 in the area of Kaupulehu North
Kona on the island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field reconnaissance and
exploration and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from
our analyses for the proposed mass grading project These recommendations are
intended for design input only and may not be appropriate to form the basis for
construction cost estimates nor for construction bidding purposes

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is located on the northwestern flank of Hualalai Mountain which
has formed the west central portion of the island and consists of the prehistoric basaltic
lava flows of the Hualalai Volcanic Series The prehistoric flows comprising Mount
Hualalai were most likely erupted during the late Pleistocene Epoch Stearns and
MacDonald 1946 in contrast the single historic eruption of Huaialai took piace in
about 1800 or 1801 The lava flows associated with this eruptive event are located to
the north Kaupulehu Lava Flows and south unnamed flows of 1801 of the proposed
residential development However some portions of the proposed residential
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3214 00 Page 6
development may be located on or within erupted volcanics associated with the 1801
eruptions
Hawaiian volcanic activity is typified by an abundance of flank eruptions along
rift Zones or zones of weakness within the earth s crust The project site is situated just
to the north of the northwest rift zone of Hualalai
Lava Flows
The lava flows observed at the site generally consist of pahoehoe and a a type
lava extrusions The pahoehoe type flow is characterized by a smooth billowy or ropy
surface in contrast to the a a which is typified by a very rough spiny or rubbly surface
The two types intergrade and occasionally is difficult to classify a particular flow or part
of a flow as one or the other
Most flows emerge from the vent as pahoehoe changing to a a as they advance
downslope The reverse change from a a to pahoehoe usually does not occur
although on occasion pahoehoe will burrow beneath an a a flow and emerge at a lower
margin giving the false appearance of a flow changing from a a to pahoehoe In
general the more viscous the lava the greater its tendency to change to a a Flows
often divide to pass on both sides of a hill or slight elevation reuniting on its downhill
side but leaving it uncovered These islands left in the flows are known as kipukas in
Hawaii Although the original island was once a high spot on the pre flow surface
increase in thickness of the flow may ultimately result in the kipuka being lower than
the surrounding lava surface
Basalts are considered to be relatively permeable rock and can transmit water
quite readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions Water is transmitted through
the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3214 00 Page 7
along clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they
serve as the major water transmission features
A a Flow
A a flows are fed by open rivers of lava located near the center line of the flow
From the central river some lava spreads laterally to moving margins of the flow but
a larger quantity moves downhill to feed the actively advancing flow front At the end
of an eruption the fluid lava may partially drain out of the central river of an a a flow
leaving a distinct depressed channel below the adjacent flow surfaces Such channels
are visible within the a a flows of the project site These channels may in places
reflect the potential occurrence of subsurface lava tubes and or collapse features
The clinkery surface expression of an a a flow usually covers a massive dense
central core of the flow The clinkery surface feature may extend to depths of 5 to 10
feet before changing into the solid core of the flow It is the core of the flow which is
actually the active flowing part while the clinkery flow top is merely carried along at the
surface At the front of the flow clinker fragments from the top crust tumble down and
are buried under the advancing lava This results in a layered flow of a solid core
separated by clinker material at the top and bottom of the flow unit
Pahoehoe Flow
The feeding rivers of pahoehoe flows quickly crust over and develop more or
less continuous roofs Therefore the lava stream flows within a tunnel of its own
making This tunnel is known as a lava tube and is found more frequently in pahoehoe
than a a The ropy surface of pahoehoe is the result of dragging and wrinkling of the
solidifying but still plastic crust by the moving liquid beneath Tumuli and pressure
ridges are surface features of pahoehoe caused by the binding and coalescence of
portions of the flow
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3214 00 Page 8
The project site is situated on the western flank of Hualalai Mountain which
makes up the west central part of the island The basalt formation encountered at
portions of the site appears to be of pahoehoe flow which spread and ponded as it
approached the ocean Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy
surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly
encountered in pahoehoe lavas These cavities were formed when the lava was still
in a molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and inter flow
cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava tubes are formed when molten lava drains
from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large
distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in
horizontal extent

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our subsurface exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling three
borings to depths of about 20 feet below the existing ground surface The purpose of
this exploration was to confirm the presence or absence of potential lava tubes at three
selected sites where surface features were mapped in the field reflecting potential lava
tube hazards The boring locations were located in the field by our field engineer at
points which were thought to intersect the general trend of the collapsed surface
features The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Geologic Map
and Site Plan Plate 2
In general our limited subsurface exploration encountered surficial zones of
loose clinker material with small voids of less than a few inches in thickness extending
to depths of about 1 to 6 feet below the existing ground surface Dense basalt rock
with interbedded clinker seams were generally encountered below this loose zone to
the maximum depths explored of about 20 feet below ground level Boring No 3
encountered a buried lava tube at a depth of about 6 feet below the surface
extending to a depth of about 9 feet below the ground surface It should be
emphasized that the vertical and lateral extent of buried cavities can be highly variable

surface and subsurface conditions at the project site consist generally of basalt rock
formation with frequent cavities and some clinker layers Based on our evaluation of
the subsurface conditions with respect to the proposed development the principal
geotechnical engineering considerations for the proposed residential development
include the following
1 presence of the uncontrolled placement of the stockpiled materials
2 placement and compaction of the rock fill and choking filter materials for
the proposed development

and
4 construction of foundations and utilities which may also include dry wells
within the boulder rock fills
Based on the proposed grading plan for the project it appears that the proposed
grading for the parcels will require cuts and fills generally on the order of about 14 feet
or less in thickness In addition the existing stockpiles of earth material should be
removed to expose firm natural ground and be replaced with compacted engineered
fill As previously indicated some of the stockpiles may be as high as 40 feet above
the surrounding natural ground elevation

excavation of the cuts planned at the site Based on the materials encountered in our
borings we anticipate that the excavated clinker rock materials may experience some
shrinkage during handling and compaction due to the porous and friable nature of the
material Based on the relatively close fracturing observed in the rock core from our
borings the excavated rock material would likely break down to boulders cobbles and
gravels with few fines However it should be noted that some large boulders may be
generated and should be expected Large boulders in excess of 4 feet but less than

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to
reduce potential contamination of the excavated materials The site is relatively
barren and devoid of vegetative growth however kiawe tree growth and
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3214 00 Page 17
associated deeper roots particularly within Parcel K 3 should be excavated and
removed As previously indicated the existing stockpiles of earth materials
should be removed to expose firm natural ground
In areas to receive fill the subgrade should be scarified to a depth of 12 inches
where possible and compacted to 90 percent relative compaction Otherwise
the subgrade should be proof rolled with a D 9 bulldozer or similar heavy
equipment minimum 6 passes to help detect and collapse near surface cavities
Any loose areas or cavities disclosed during clearing operations should be
backfilled with compacted engineered fill Caution should be exercised while
grading near any collapse features which may represent potential lava tubes or
cavities which may further collapse under the load of heavy equipment
In the areas denoted as potential subsurface lava tubes or collapse features on
Plate 2 of this report the subgrade should be ripped to a minimum depth of 3
feet to collapse or open any shallow voids Such voids should be filled with
structural fill in accordance with the procedures discussed below The ripped
materials should then be recompacted utilizing the equivalent equipment and
procedures for structural fill Therefore unit prices for additional ripping and
engineered fill construction should be obtained during construction bidding

Any imported material if necessary should be observed and or tested by

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
In general fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12
inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the
same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3214 00 Page 20
multiple wheel pneumatic tired rollers or other types of acceptable compaction
equipment
Rock fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding 2 times the maximum
dimension of the rocks or 4 feet whichever is less Placement should be by
rock trucks traversing previously placed lifts and dumping at the edge of the
currently placed lift Spreading of the rock fill should be by dozer to facilitate
seating of the rock The rock fill should be watered heavily during placement
by water trucks traversing in front of the current rock lift face and spraying water
continuously during placement Compaction equipment such as a 20 ton
compactor or other compaction equipment providing suitable energy to achieve
the required compaction should be utilized The number of passes to be made
to achieve proper compaction should be determined at the onset of site grading
by the performance of a trial compaction test or by plate bearing tests
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of
rock boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the
number of passes of a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of
compaction should be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project
under the observation of a representative from Geolabs Hawaii On the basis
of this testing program the number of passes may then be used as the field
criterion for adequate compaction

The field exploration disclosed that the site is underlain by very closely to widely
fractured volcanic rock ranging from medium hard to very hard It is anticipated
that the volcanic rock may be excavated with normal heavy excavation

Based on our field reconnaissance and previous experience in the area the
surface of the a a lava flows may be relatively easy to excavate with

Utilities
We envision that new utility lines will be required for development of this project

For utility lines that extend below the recommended 4 foot choker fill layer near the

pipes to reduce the potential for migration of the trench backfill through the rock fill
voids This choker fill layer should be compacted to not less than 90 percent relative

adequate support around the pipes and to reduce compaction of the backfill thus
reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill should consist of granular material
generally less than 6 inches in maximum size and compacted to at least 90 percent
relative compaction Where trenches will be located in paved areas the upper 2 feet
of the trench backfill below the road subgrade should be compacted to not less than
95 percent relative compaction

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 at the approximate locations
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The depths of our borings were about 20 feet below
the existing ground surface They were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped
with rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic presentation of the materials
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left
in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals
