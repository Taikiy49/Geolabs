INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Kapolei Parkway Bridge project located within the Villages of
Kapolei in the District of Ewa on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical engineering recommen
dations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses
for the proposed bridge project These recommendations are intended for design of
foundations and site grading only The findings and recommendations presented herein
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed Kapolei Parkway Bridge project is located within the Villages of
Kapolei in the District of Ewa on the island of Oahu Hawaii Based on the information
provided it is proposed to construct a bridge along Kapolei Parkway across the existing
Kapolei Lower Channel which is located near the eastern end of the existing Kapolei
Parkway as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on the plans provided the proposed bridge will be approximately 120 feet
long from abutment to abutment about 125 feet wide and about 18 feet above the
bottom of the Kapolei Lower Channel floor The proposed bridge will be a two span
bridge supported at each end by abutments and by a center pier located within the open

drainage channel The west abutment of the bridge structure will straddle an existing twin
box culvert of about 36 feet wide discharging into Kapolei Lower Channel as shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2 The structural loading of the bridge at the east and west
abutments is approximately 25 kips per lineal foot of abutment and the structural loading
at the center pier is about 36 kips per lineal foot of width In addition the total lateral
loads acting on the bridge foundations in the longitudinal and transverse directions are
estimated to be approximately 2 800 and 3 200 kips respectively
Based on the plans provided we anticipate that site grading will involve minor fills

design finish grades Based on a bottom footing elevation of approximately 52 feet

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age As volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from Koolau
banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau known as Schofield
Plateau
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3602 00 Page 5
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of this plateau generated
sediments which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and to
the south these sediments accumulated and interbedded with marine sediments and
coral and algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge The thickness of the
sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1 Highway to
over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This sedimentary wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves
as the confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of southern
Oahu Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the
present Agricultural development within the last 100 years and the recent mass grading
work for the Villages of Kapolei have brought the area to its present form

The subsurface conditions at the proposed project site were explored by drilling
and sampling six borings to depths of about 46 5 to 83 5 feet below the existing ground
surface Boring Nos 1 and 4 were drilled near the west abutment and Boring Nos 3 and
6 were drilled near the east abutment of the proposed bridge Boring Nos 2 and 5 were
drilled at the center pier location from the channel floor of the Kapolei Lower Channel
The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on our exploration the project site is underlain primarily by medium dense
silty sands and silty clayey coral gravels below near surface clayey materials In Boring

drilled of approximately 59 5 and 79 5 feet below the channel floor Two zones of very
stiff silty clays with thicknesses of about 2 5 and 9 feet were encountered in Boring No 5

Nos 3 and 4 at depths ranging from about 40 to 69 feet below the existing ground

corresponded to elevations of about 9 to 9 feet MSL Due to the proximity of the project

are also subject to change due to seasonal precipitation storm surge conditions and

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our exploration and the heavy
structural loads of the bridge structure we recommend that a deep foundation system
used to support the bridge structure The drilled shafts would derive vertical support
primarily from adhesion between the shaft and the surrounding soils The portion of the
west abutment above the existing twin box culvert will straddle the box culvert and will be
supported on a series of four 48 inch diameter drilled shafts on either side of the box
culvert By straddling the existing box culvert the existing box culvert and the walls at the
open channel do not need to be demolished in order to construct the bridge
We envision that site grading would consist of excavations at the abutment
locations to construct the abutment walls and substructure and subsequent backfilling In
addition some minor fills on the order of about 3 feet relative to the existing ground
surface will be needed to raise the grades for the approaches to the bridge structure We
also anticipate that a trench excavation on the order of about 24 feet below the surface
and subsequent backfilling will be required for installation of the new 8 inch sewer line
and 30 inch sewer line relocation and construction of the two new manholes
Due to the proximity of the new 8 inch sewer line and the 30 inch sewer line
relocation to the bridge foundations the trench excavation should be kept to a minimum
to reduce the potential for disturbing the foundation soils around the drilled shafts for the
bridge foundation As a result an open cut trench excavation with sloping sidewalls is not
recommended The trench excavation for the sewer lines should be shored and the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3602 00 Page 8
trench excavation and backfilling operation should be completed prior to installation of the
drilled shafts for the west abutment In addition in order to reduce the impact of typical
pipe cushion backfill gravels from potentially running into the drilled shaft excavations
we recommend that controlled low strength materials lean concrete be used as the pipe
cushion backfill around the sewer pipe up to about 2 feet above the top of the pipe

Foundation Settlements
Settlement of the drilled shaft foundation will occur as a result of compression of
the drilled shaft and subgrade response of the foundation embedded in the subsoil
conditions described in this report Total drilled shaft settlements are estimated to be on
the order of less than 1 inch with differential settlements between the abutments
estimated to be less than 0 5 inches We believe a significant portion of the settlement is
elastic and should occur as the loads are applied Post construction foundation
settlements should be approximately 0 5 inches or less
Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations
In general the performance of drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the
contractor s method of installation and construction procedures The following conditions
would have a significant effect on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft
foundations
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the
contact between the shaft and the surrounding soils Therefore proper construction
techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in drilling the shaft holes
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3602 00 Page 12
and in placing concrete into the drilled holes The subsurface materials at the bridge site
consist predominantly of sands and sandy gravels coral detritus with stiff clays near the
ground surface Hard coral material may be encountered in localized areas and may
need special tools during drilling operations Due to the granular nature of the material
encountered in the borings there is potential for cave in of the material during the drilling
operations Therefore to reduce the potential for caving in of the drilled holes temporary
casing of the drilled holes during shaft drilling should be required In addition drilling for
the shafts adjacent to the hole being drilled may not commence until the drilled shaft has
been completed by placement of concrete to the top of shaft elevation
Based on the estimated tip elevations of the drilled shafts groundwater should be
expected in the drilled holes during shaft installation Therefore placement of concrete
by tremie concrete methods is recommended A low shrink high slump 7 to 9 inch
slump range concrete mix should be used to provide close contact between the drilled
shafts and the surrounding soils The concrete should be placed promptly after drilling
within 24 hours to reduce the potential for softening of the sides of the drilled holes The
concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce segregation of the aggregates
from the concrete mix During concrete placement a minimum of 5 feet of concrete head
or adequate concrete to resist the water pressure in the drilled hole should be maintained
above the bottom of the tremie pipe or bottom of the temporary casing prior to extraction
of the temporary casing It should be noted that substantial concrete over pour should be
expected considering the generally porous nature of the coral detritus subsurface
materials at the site
Due to the presence of groundwater proper observation of the sides and bottom of
the drilled shaft may not be possible Therefore it is imperative that a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii be present at the site to observe the drilling and installation of drilled
shafts during construction Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids is not recommended
because use of drilling fluids may reduce the frictional resistance between the drilled shaft
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3602 00 Page 13
and surrounding soils The bottom of the drilled hole should be relatively free of loose
materials prior to tremie concrete placement
Trial Shaft Program
In general a trial shaft program is normally required for a bridge project
Considering the diameters and structural loading of the drilled shafts we recommend that
a trial shaft program including the performance of a load test be undertaken to fulfill the
following objectives
To examine the adequacy of the methods and equipment proposed by the
contractor to install the drilled shafts
To confirm or modify the estimated tip elevations of the drill shafts
e To assess the contractor s method of placing and extracting the temporary
casing required for the drilled shaft
To assess the contractor s method of tremie concrete placement
To achieve these objectives we recommend that the trial shaft program consist of
drilling one 18 inch diameter trial shaft to a depth of about 70 feet below the ground
surface near the west abutment location outside of the west abutment foundation The
trial shaft should be structurally reinforced and instrumented with six embedment strain
gages for load testing purposes The embedment strain gages should be placed starting
from the bottom at an elevation of about 5 feet above the tip of the trial shaft and
subsequently at 10 foot intervals After installation of the trial shaft a load test should be
conducted on the reinforced trial shaft The pile load test should be performed in general
accordance with the Quick Load Test Method of ASTM Test Designation D 1143 The
trial shaft should be loaded to an ultimate load of about 600 kips and should be held for a
minimum of 12 hours
We recommend that a representative from Geolabs Hawaii be present during the
trial shaft program to evaluate the contractor s method of drilled shaft installation and to
evaluate the subsurface materials encountered In addition Geolabs Hawaii should
perform the instrumented load test on the reinforced trial shaft It should be noted that the

Any imported soil should be select fill such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or
cinder sand Select fill material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no
particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension Select fill should have a laboratory
CBR value of 20 or more with a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 AASHTO T 193 In addition select fill
GEOLABS HAWAII

material should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve
Imported soils should be brought to above the optimum moisture content and compacted
to not less than 95 percent relative compaction All surfaces should be finished to create
smooth unyielding subgrades and should be kept moist until covered by concrete slabs
or pavements Import materials should be tested and approved by Geolabs Hawaii prior
to delivery to the project site for its intended use
For backfill behind the abutment structure we recommend that the backfill material
consist of well graded granular fill material conforming to Type A Structural Backfill
Material of Section 703 20 of the Hawaii Standard Specifications 1994 The material
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in level
loose lifts not exceeding 8 inches and compacted to not less than 95 percent of relative

Utility Li
We understand that a new 8 inch sewer line of about 15 lineal feet connecting to
the relocated 30 inch sewer line is planned for the proposed project The invert of the
new sewer line is anticipated to be on the order of about 22 feet below the existing
ground surface Therefore we anticipate that excavations up to about 24 feet below the
existing ground surface will be needed to install the sewer line and associated manholes
In addition it is also planned to extend the existing 12 inch water line at the site across
the channel by attaching the water line to the proposed bridge The invert of the
underground portion of the water line will be about 4 feet below the ground surface
Trench Excavation and Support
Based on the pian and profile provided the inverts of sewer and water lines will be
about 22 and 4 feet below the existing ground surface respectively Based on the
borings drilled very stiff clays and medium dense sands and gravels may be
encountered during trench excavation In general the method and equipment to
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3602 00 Page 18
be used for excavation should be determined by the contractor subject to practical
limits and safety considerations The excavated soils from the trench excavation
should be stockpiled no closer than a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the
excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential for ground
movement
As previously mentioned due to the proximity of the new 8 inch sewer line and the
30 inch sewer line relocation to the west abutment foundations the trench
excavation should be kept to a minimum to reduce the potential for disturbing the
foundation soils around the drilled shafts for the bridge foundation As a result an
open cut trench excavation with sloping sidewalls is not recommended The
trench excavation for the sewer lines should be shored and the trench excavation
and backfilling operation should be completed prior to installation of the drilled
shafts for the west abutment Based on the relatively deep excavations
anticipated shoring of the sides of the trenches will be necessary to facilitate the
proposed trench excavation and backfill and to protect personnel working in the
trench All trench excavation works should be conducted in conformance with all
applicable Federal State and other safety requirements
Pipe Bedding
Pipe bedding is an important design consideration since distribution of bearing
stresses against the pipe bottom has a great impact upon the load supporting
capacity of the pipe In general we recommend that a minimum of 6 inches of
clean granular bedding material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gravel be placed below the proposed water and sewer pipes Prior to placement
of the granular bedding material the bottom of the trench should be firm and
unyielding For pipes with inverts in soft loose soils an additional stabilization
layer consisting of at least 18 inches of No 3B Fine gravel wrapped in a filter
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3602 00 Page 19
fabric such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent should be provided below the 6 inch
bedding layer for support of the pipes
Backfill
In order to reduce the impact of typical pipe cushion backfill clean gravels from
potentially running into the drilled shaft excavations we recommend that controlled
low strength materials lean concrete be used in the first zone of backfill material
extending from the bedding material to at least 2 feet above the top of the pipes
The trench backfill from 2 feet above the top of the pipes above the controlled low
strength material to the finish subgrade may consist of the excavated on site soils
with the exception of any soft loose wet soils The general backfill should be
placed in thin lifts with each lift moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above
the optimum moisture content and mechanically compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction The trench backfill within 3 feet of the pavement
surface elevation should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction
Manhole Structures
We understand that two new sewer manholes will be constructed as part of the
proposed project In general we believe that the bottom of the manhole structures will
bear on the very stiff clayey and silty soils or medium dense sands and gravels
encountered in the borings To provide more uniform support for the manhole structures
we recommend that the manhole structures be supported on a 6 inch layer of cushion fill
consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation An allowable bearing
pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be used for design of the
manhole structures bearing on the stiff clayey soils or medium dense sands and gravel
Where soft soils are encountered at the foundation level of the manhole structures we
recommend that the soft soils be removed to expose firm materials and the resulting
excavation should be backfilled with engineered fill compacted to at least 90 percent
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3602 00 Page 20
relative compaction Settlements on the order of 1 inch may be anticipated for the
manhole structures
The lateral earth pressures acting on the manhole structures will depend on the
type of backfill used the extent of backfill and the compactive effort on the backfill
material around the structures We recommend that the new manhole structures be
designed for an equivalent fluid pressure of 55 pounds per cubic foot p c f for a dry
condition This recommended lateral earth pressure value assumes that the backfill
around the manhole structures is properly placed and compacted between 90 and
95 percent relative compaction For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the
structure a rectangular distribution with uniform pressure equal to 55 percent of the
vertical surcharge pressure acting over the entire depth of the structure may be used in
design Additional analyses during design may be needed to evaluate the surcharge
effects of point loads and line loads
Pavements
We envision that new flexible pavements will be required for the roadway
extension leading to the proposed bridge In general the new pavements for the
roadway extensions should match the existing pavement section of the existing Kapolei
Parkway which is 4 inches of asphalt concrete over 7 inches of aggregate base course
over 18 inches of select borrow subbase Prior to placement of the select borrow
subbase the pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction The select borrow subbase and
aggregate base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate They should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in level loose lifts
not exceeding 8 inches and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all
surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed anywhere on the

system could be constructed to collect excessive water from landscaping irrigation For
long term performance we recommend that a subdrain system be constructed adjacent
to paved and landscaped areas

FieldExploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling six
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 to depths of about 46 5 to 83 5 feet
below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer or geologist who monitored the drilling
operations on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed
visually and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation
of the materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 1 through
A 6 2
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing _
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results
of the tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths and
presented graphically on Plate B 1
Three sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Two direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples of the
soils to evaluate their strength characteristics Results of the tests are presented on
Plates B 3 and B 4
Three sets of corrosivity tests including pH ASTM D 2976 and minimum
resistivity ASTM G 57 were performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration The test results are presented on Plate B 5
Three sets of chemical analyses tests including Chloride and Sulfate tests were
performed on selected samples of the soils to evaluate their corrosion characteristics
The chemical analyses were performed by Environmental Laboratory of the Pacific
Results of the tests are summarized on Plate B 6
