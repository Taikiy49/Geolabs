SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The proposed waterline alignment is generally underlain by residual/saprolite soils, 
consisting of stiff to very stiff silty clays and clayey silts extending to the maximum depth 
explored of approximately 26.5 feet below the existing ground surface. We did not 
encounter groundwater in the drilled boring at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on the relatively competent subsurface soil conditions encountered at the 
site, we believe that an allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for the 
design of shallow foundations bearing on the recompacted in-situ soils and/or new 
compacted fills. Due to sloping ground terrain, we recommend installing Grouted Rubble 
Paving (GRP) on the finish grade adjacent to the new post-pier footings to minimize future 
surface soil erosion. 
Based on the laboratory test results, we anticipate the in-situ clayey soils may 
exhibit a slight to low potential for shrinking and swelling when subjected to moisture 
fluctuations. To reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slab-on-grade 
resulting from shrinking and swelling of the near-surface clayey soils, we recommend 
properly preparing the slab subgrades by scarifying to a minimum depth of 12 inches, 
moisture-conditioning to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content, and 
recompacting to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
geotechnical recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Hi’i Waterline Replacement project on the Island of Lanai, Hawaii. The 
project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project. 
These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of the new 
waterline installation using open trench method, foundations, slabs-on-grade, retaining 
structures, earthwork, and pavements only. The findings and recommendations 
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located to the south of Lanai City on the ridge between the 
Palawai Basin and Hi’i Flat. The waterline traverses along the ridge hillside next to a 
narrow unpaved road up to a water reservoir. The existing Hi’i Waterline provides potable 
water to the Manele and Palawai areas on the Island of Lanai. The main transmission line 
from the Hi’i Tank to the Pressure Reducing Valve (PRV) station has experienced 
frequent/catastrophic breaks. 
The proposed waterline replacement is approximately 1,500 linear feet long 
between the existing Hi’i tank and PRV station. It should be noted that majority of the 
existing waterline was constructed along the steep slopes, consisting of an above-grade 
single pipeline system supported on concrete pedestal piers. It was proposed to replace 
the existing Hi’i Waterline. We understand the new waterline will be dual pipes following 
the same alignment as the existing waterline supported on a new post-pier foundation 
system. Detailed waterline design was not available at the time this report was prepared.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Lanai is a shield volcano built by eruptions at the summit and along 
three rift zones more than 1.20 to 1.46 million years ago. The principal rift zone trends 
northwestward as a broad ridge and is responsible for the conspicuous elongation of the 
island in that direction. A less conspicuous bulge on the southern side of the island is the 
result of building on the southwest rift zone. The summit of the shield collapsed to form a 
caldera from which a shallow graben, bordered by an echelon of step faults, extends 
south southeastward toward Manele Bay. Numerous dikes exposed in the sea cliff 
indicate that this Manele Graben lies along another rift zone. 
The caldera was largely, but not completely, filled by lava flows and the present 
Palawai Basin is a remnant of the caldera. Just to the west of it, Miki Basin (with an 
average diameter of about 0.9 miles) is a nearly filled pit crater. The top of the ridge 
between these basins is about 140 feet above the floor of the Palawai Basin. On the 
south, the floor of Palawai Basin merges with the floor of the Manele Graben, where the 
most recent lava flows in the caldera overflowed onto the outer slope of the volcano. The 
project site is located within the Palawai Basin.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, our field exploration indicates that the proposed waterline alignment is 
underlain by residual/saprolite soils, consisting of stiff to very stiff clayey/silty soil 
extending to the maximum depth drilled of 26.5 feet below the ground surface. We did not 
encounter groundwater in the drilled boring at the time of our field exploration. Severe soil 
erosion features were observed along the existing waterline alignment, consisting of 
undermined footing, settlement, and slope raveling. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be 
used for the design of shallow foundations bearing on the recompacted in-situ soils and/or 
new compacted fills. Based on the laboratory test results, we anticipate the in-situ clayey 
soils may exhibit a slight to low potential for shrinking and swelling when subjected to 
moisture fluctuations. To reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slab-
on-grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of the near-surface clayey soils, we 
recommend properly preparing the slab subgrades by scarifying to a minimum depth of 
12 inches, moisture-conditioning to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture 
content, and recompacting to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
3.1 
Foundations 
Based on the information provided, we envision the new waterline will be 
supported on a post-pier foundation system. Based on the subsurface conditions at the 
project site, we recommend using shallow spread footings to support the new waterline. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be 
used for the design of the shallow foundations bearing on the recompacted on-site soils. 
This allowable bearing pressure is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-
third (⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. In general, 
we recommend embedding the bottom of the footing a minimum of 18 inches below the 
lowest adjacent grades. 
Footings constructed near the tops of slopes or on sloping ground conditions should 
be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal setback distance of 6 feet

measured from the outside edge of the footings to the face of the slope. If foundations are 
next to utility trenches or easements, the footings should be embedded below a 45-degree

be extended down to bear on the underlying competent materials. 
If the foundations are designed and constructed as recommended herein, we 
estimate total settlements of footings supported on the recompacted on-site soils to be 
on the order of about 1 inch or less. Differential settlements between adjacent footings 
supported on similar materials should be on the order of about 0.5 inches or less. 
As mentioned previously, the existing ground adjacent to the existing concrete 
pedestal piers experienced severe soil erosion resulting in footing undermining, 
settlement, and/or slope raveling. Consideration may be given to placing Grouted Rubble 
Paving (GRP) around the new post-pier footing, to reduce risk of the soil erosion. 
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction developed 
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure 
acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A coefficient of friction of 
0.35 may be used for footings bearing on the compacted on-site soils. Resistance due to 
passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 
350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf) assuming that the soils around the 
footings are well compacted. The passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of the soil 
should be neglected unless covered by pavements or slabs.

3.2 
Slabs-On-Grade 
Based on the laboratory test results, we anticipate that the in-situ clayey and/or 
silty soils may exhibit a slight to low potential for shrinking and swelling when subjected 
to moisture fluctuations. To reduce the potential for future distress to lightly loaded slabs-
on-grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of the near-surface clayey and/or silty 
soils, we recommend properly preparing the slab subgrades by scarifying to a minimum 
depth of 12 inches, moisture-conditioning to at least 2 percent above the optimum 
moisture content, and recompacting to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
For any interior building slab (not subjected to vehicular traffic or machinery 
vibration), we recommend placing a minimum 4-inch-thick layer of cushion fill consisting 
of open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) below the slabs. The open-graded 
gravel cushion fill would provide uniform support of the slabs and would serve as a 
capillary moisture break. To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration through 
the slab and subsequent damage to floor coverings, we recommend placing an 
impervious moisture barrier on top of the open-graded gravel cushion fill layer. Flexible 
floor coverings, such as carpet or sheet vinyl, should be considered because they can 
better mask minor slab cracking. 
Where the slabs-on-grade will be subjected to equipment vibration and/or vehicular 
traffic, we recommend placing a 6-inch layer of aggregate subbase below the slabs in lieu 
of the 4-inch thick gravel cushion fill layer. The aggregate subbase should consist of 
crushed basaltic aggregates compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
Where slabs are intended to function as rigid pavements, a minimum slab thickness of 6 
inches may be used for preliminary design purposes. Provisions should be made for 
proper load transfer across the slab joints that will be subject to vehicular traffic. The 
thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at least 12 
inches below the lowest adjacent grade.

3.3 
Retaining Structures 
We envision that retaining structures may be required for the proposed project. The 
following guidelines may be used for the design of the retaining structures. 
3.3.1 Retaining Structure Foundations 
In general, we believe that retaining wall foundations may be designed in 
accordance with the recommendations and parameters presented in the 
“Foundations” section. In addition, retaining wall foundations should be at least 
18 inches wide, and the bottom should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches 
below the lowest adjacent finished grades. 
For sloping ground conditions, the bottom of the footing should extend deeper to 
obtain a minimum 6-foot setback distance measured horizontally from the outside 
edge of the footing to the face of the slope. Wall footings oriented parallel to the 
direction of the slope should be constructed in stepped footings.

3.3.3 Drainage 
Retaining walls should be well-drained to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic 
pressures. A typical drainage system should consist of a 12-inch-wide zone of 
permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), 
placed directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at the base of the 
wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes. As an alternative, a 
prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used 
instead of the drainage material. The prefabricated drainage product also should be 
hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall. 
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of compacted on-site 
materials or free-draining compacted fills, where specified by the designer. Unless 
covered by concrete slabs, the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of 
low-expansion, relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential for excessive 
water infiltration behind the walls. 
3.4 
Site Grading 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
thoroughly cleared and grubbed. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site to reduce the 
potential for contamination. 
Soft and/or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill should be over-excavated to expose firm materials, and the 
resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fills. The excavated soft 
and/or organic soils should be properly disposed of off-site and/or used in landscape 
areas, where appropriate. Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit 
prices for over-excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the 
over-excavation quantities. 
In general, the excavated on-site soils should be suitable for use as general fill 
materials, provided that it is free of vegetation debris and the maximum particle size is 
less than 6 inches in the largest dimension. The on-site materials generated from the

excavation may be used as a source of general fill or backfill materials, provided that they

Fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose

Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with 
ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or 
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or ponding 
should not be allowed to compact the fills. Where compaction is less than required, 
additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment moisture content, as 
necessary, to obtain the specified compaction. 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by stiff to hard 
clayey/silty soils. It is anticipated that the clayey/silty soil may be readily excavated with 
normal heavy excavation equipment, such as excavators, and ripped with large 
bulldozers.

3.6 
Corrosion Potential 
Laboratory corrosion tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity (ASTM 
G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), were performed 
on a selected sample obtained during our field exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of 
the near-surface soils at the project site. Results of the corrosivity tests are presented in 
Appendix B. 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the 
resistivity the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. Conversely, 
the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of metallic objects. 
Results of the resistivity testing indicate that the on-site clayey/silty soils have a 
resistivity value of 6,000 ohm-cm. Therefore, the on-site clayey/silty soils may be 
considered moderately corrosive (Corrosion Rating of 4) for buried metallic structures 
based on the Board of Water Supply, City & County of Honolulu, Water System External 
Corrosion Control Standards, dated 1991. A professional corrosion engineer should be 
consulted to review the test results and provide detailed recommendations for corrosion 
protection, if deemed necessary. 
The chloride content level of the on-site soils encountered near the existing ground 
surface is relatively low, on the order of 26 parts per million (ppm) or less, while the sulfate 
content is not detected (very low). 
Based on the chloride and sulfate content tests performed on the on-site soils, the 
test values are generally relatively low. Therefore, we believe that either Type I or Type 
II cement may be used for the concrete in contact with the ground planned for the project. 
It may be appropriate to consult with a professional corrosion engineer to review the test 
results and provide detailed recommendations for corrosion protection.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
one boring, designated as Boring No. 1, extending to a depth of about 26.5 feet below 
the existing ground surface. The approximate boring location is shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. The boring was drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig with continuous flight 
augers.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representation of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Log of Boring, Plate A-1. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the log of boring indicates the number of blows required 
for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized 
on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depths.

Dynamic Cone Penetrometer Tests 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored using Dynamic Cone 
Penetrometer (DCP) testing at four locations, designated as DCP-1 through DCP-4, 
extending to depths of about 10.2 to 11.4 feet below the existing ground surface. The 
approximate DCP locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Portable Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) was used for probing by driving an 
8-mm diameter 60° steel cone tip with an 8-kg hammer falling 575-mm in vertical height. 
The penetration depth was recorded per blow and converted to standard penetration 
resistance (SPT, ASTM D1586) using a correlation between Penetration Index (PI) and 
SPT, developed by Sowers and Hedges and later Livneh and Ishai. Both PI and SPT blow 
counts are shown on the Logs of DCP at the appropriate depths, presented on Plates C -1.1 
through C-4.2.
