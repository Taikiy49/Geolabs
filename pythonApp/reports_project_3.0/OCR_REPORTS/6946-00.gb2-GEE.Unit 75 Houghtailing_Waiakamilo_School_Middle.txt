In general, our field exploration concluded that the pavement structure at 
Houghtailing Street, on average, consists of about 6 inches of asphaltic concrete over 
12 inches of base/subbase material.  The pavement structure at Waiakamilo Road 
consists of asphaltic concrete with an average thickness of about 7 inches and 
base/subbase material with an average thickness of about 9 inches.   
The pavement structure at School Street between Liliha Street and Notley Street, 
on average, consists of about 10 inches of asphaltic concrete over 8 inches of 
base/subbase material.  The pavement structure at Middle Street between Notley Street 
and H-1 Freeway consists of asphaltic concrete with an average thickness of about 
8 inches over base/subbase material with an average thickness of about 12.5 inches.

1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering analyses 
performed for the Rehabilitation of City Streets, Unit 75 Houghtailing Street, Waiakamilo 
Road, School Street and Middle Street project in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location 
Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our 
analyses for the roadway rehabilitation project. The findings and recommendations 
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The Rehabilitation of City Streets project is part of an overall Pavement Survey 
Project covering various locations across the City and County of Honolulu. Generally, 
the Unit 75 project will involve rehabilitation of approximately 1.6 miles of 
Houghtailing Street and Waiakamilo Road and approximately 2.4 miles of School Street 
and Middle Street in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The roadway alignments 
of the pavement rehabilitation project are shown on the General Site Plan, Plate 2.   
The roadways are generally two-way roads with two travel lanes in each 
direction.  Houghtailing Street between Hillcrest Street and School Street has a single 
lane in each direction.  The roadways consist of asphaltic concrete pavements.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age.  After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Volcano, volcanic activity returned with a 
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations.  These series are 
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series.  The project site is located at the south 
western flank of the Koolau Mountain Range and also extends to the coastal plain of 
southern Oahu. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii and in the heads of valleys, the erosional 
processes are dominated by detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls 
and are transported downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as 
colluvium.  Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley, 
alluvial processes become dominant, and the sediments are transported and deposited 
as alluvium. 
The coastal plain was built on the eroded flanks of the Koolau Volcano, which 
forms the eastern two-thirds of the Island of Oahu.  The coastal plain was built by 
extensive accumulation of alluvium derived from erosion of the volcano, interbedded 
with coral reefs and associated deposits.  During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea 
levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of continental glaciation.  As the glaciers 
grew and advanced, less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea 
levels fell below the present stands of the sea.  When the glaciers melted and receded, 
an excess of water became available such that the sea levels rose to elevations above 
the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations.  When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level.

2.2 
Visual Pavement Condition Survey 
A pavement condition survey of the project area was performed on November 7, 
10, and 11, 2014. During the field survey, our engineer walked on the sides of the street 
and determined pavement distress types through visual classification. 
The visual classification was performed in general accordance with the 
definitions described in the “Distress Identification Manual for the Long-Term Pavement 
Performance Program” dated June 2003 prepared by the Federal Highway 
Administration (FHWA). Some of the pavement distress definitions are presented in the 
table below. 
ASPHALT PAVEMENT DISTRESSES  
Alligator Cracking 
(Fatigue) 
Alligator cracks are a series of interconnecting cracks caused by 
fatigue failure of the asphaltic concrete surface under repeated 
traffic loading. 
Block Cracking 
Block cracks are inter-connected cracks that divide the pavement 
into approximately rectangular pieces. 
Longitudinal 
Cracking 
Longitudinal cracks are cracks parallel to the pavement’s 
centerline or laydown direction. 
Transverse 
Cracking 
Transverse cracks are cracks extending across the pavement at 
approximately right angles to the pavement centerline or direction 
of laydown. 
Patch 
Patch is an area of pavement that has been replaced with new 
material to repair the existing pavement. 
Pothole 
Potholes are small bowl-shaped (usually less than 30 inches in 
diameter) depressions in the pavement surface. 
Rutting 
A rut is a surface depression in the wheel paths. 
Shoving 
Shoving is a longitudinal displacement of a localized area of the 
pavement surface generally caused by braking or accelerating of 
vehicles.  Shoving is usually located on hills or curves, or at 
intersections.  It also may have associated vertical displacement. 
Bleeding 
Excess bituminous binder occurring on the pavement surface, 
usually found in the wheel paths. 
Polished 
Aggregate 
Polished Aggregate is the area of pavement where the portion of 
aggregate extending above the asphalt binder is either very small, 
or there are no rough or angular aggregate particles to provide 
good skid resistance.

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling 28 borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 28, at selected locations along the roadways. The 
borings generally extended to depths of about 2.0 to 7.0 feet below the existing 
pavement surface. In addition, the boring exploration was supplemented by coring the 
existing pavement section to depths of about 1.3 to 4.0 feet below the existing 
pavement surface at 28 selected locations, identified as C-101 through C-128.  The 
approximate boring and coring locations for this project are shown on the Site Plans, 
Plates 3.1 through 3.13. 
In addition, Dynamic Cone Penetrometer testing was conducted in Boring Nos. 1 
through 28 to estimate the CBR value of the subgrade soils.  This instrument is typically 
used to assess material properties of the pavement subgrade soils. The 17.6-pound 
DCP can be used to estimate the strength characteristics of fine and coarse-grained 
soils, granular construction materials and weak stabilized or modified materials. The 
17.6-lb DCP cannot be used in highly stabilized or cemented materials or for granular 
materials containing a large percentage of aggregates greater than 2 inches.  The 
results of the DCP testing are shown on the DCP logs in Appendix C.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our pavement analyses indicate that existing pavement structure at several of 
the roadways may be insufficient to support the medium to high volume traffic condition.  
In addition, we believe that the poor bonding of the AC overlays/lifts may have attributed 
to the distresses that developed in several of the roadways.  Therefore, we recommend 
reconstruction of these roadways, which consist of removing the existing AC and 
portions of the existing base/subbase, if required, and replacing with a new pavement 
section consisting of asphaltic concrete and asphalt concrete base.  The recommended 
pavement sections are provided in the “New Pavement Design” section of this report. 
If soft soils are exposed after the milling process, we recommend providing a 
stabilization layer in lieu of proof-rolling the soft subgrade.  The stabilization layer should 
consist of 8 inches of aggregate base course over a layer of geogrid (such as Tensar 
Triax TX 7 or equivalent) underlain by a woven geotextile fabric (such as Mirafi HP370 
or equivalent).    
Ideally, we recommend reconstructing the entire roadways with the new

3.6 
Site Preparation 
After removal of the existing AC and base/subbase layers to accommodate the 
new pavement structural sections, the exposed subgrade soils should be proof-rolled 
with a minimum 10-ton (static weight) drum roller for a minimum of eight passes to 
compact the exposed subgrade soils. The drum roller should be operated at a speed of 
about 300 feet per minute.  
If subgrade soils with relatively high in-situ moisture are exposed, we recommend 
proof-rolling the pavement subgrade without the use of vibration. Use of vibration during 
SECTION 3. DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS  
W.O. 6946-00 
GEOLABS, INC. 
Page 20 
Hawaii • California 
proof-rolling will induce a pumping condition of the subgrade soils with high in-situ 
moisture. 
If soft soils are exposed after the milling process, we recommend providing a 
stabilization layer in lieu of proof-rolling the soft subgrade, which may induce pumping 
condition.  The stabilization layer should consist of 8 inches of aggregate base course 
over a layer of geogrid (such as Tensar Triax TX 7 or equivalent) underlain by a woven 
geotextile fabric (such as Mirafi HP370 or equivalent) as shown on Plate 5. The 
aggregate base course should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture 
content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with AASHTO T-180 
(ASTM D1557). Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor the pavement subgrade preparation to 
observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and 
scarification process and to confirm whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar 
to those encountered in our field exploration. 
Asphalt concrete base (ACB) material should consist of asphalt-treated basalt 
aggregate, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and 
compacted to no less than 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity 
determined in accordance with AASHTO T-209 or ASTM D2041. Asphalt concrete (AC) 
material should be constructed in accordance with Section 401 Asphalt Concrete 
Pavement of the Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction, and 
its amendments. 
3.7 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures.

Field Exploration – Borings and Corings 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway rehabilitation project by 
drilling and sampling eighteen borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 18, extending 
to depths ranging from about 2.0 to 7.0 feet below the existing pavement surface. In 
addition, the boring exploration was supplemented by coring the existing pavement section 
to depths of about 1.3 to 4.0 feet below the existing pavement surface at twenty-eight 
selected locations, identified as C-101 through C-128.  The approximate boring and coring 
locations for this project are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.13. The borings 
were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and 
coring tools. 
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Rock 
were described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered are 
presented on the Logs of Borings and Corings, Plates A-1 through A-46. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Dynamic Cone Penetrometer Tests 
Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) testing was performed at the 18 boring 
locations (Boring Nos. 1 through 18) to obtain pavement support characteristics of the 
in-situ soils. The tests were performed using a Salem Dual Mass Dynamic Cone 
Penetrometer to a depth of about 2.5 to 5 feet below the existing pavement surface. 
The DCP device consists of two 16mm-diameter stainless steel rods with the 
lower rod containing an anvil, a replaceable 60o pointed tip and depth markings. The 
upper rod holds a 17.6 lb drop hammer with a 22.6 inch drop distance. At the beginning 
of a test, the cone is “seated” by dropping the hammer until the widest part of the cone 
is just below the testing surface at which point the initial penetration is recorded as 0. 
The operator then lifts and drops the hammer one or more times depending on the 
strength of the soil at the test location.  The number of blows for a minimum of 1-inch 
penetration is recorded.  The process is continued until the desired depth of testing is 
reached or the full length of the lower rod is buried. 
Data from our DCP testing indicates the penetration index (PI) which is simply the 
penetration distance of the cone for each drop of the hammer expressed as millimeters 
per blow. The penetration indices obtained from our tests were correlated with common 
pavement design parameters such as California Bearing Ratio (CBR). Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the DCP Logs, 
Plates C-1 through C-18.2.
