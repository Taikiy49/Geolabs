REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is situated on the eastern flank of Mauna Loa The lava
formation encountered at the site appears to be of pahoehoe flow which spread and
ponded as it approached the ocean Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like
or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities are
commonly encountered in pahoehoe lavas These cavities were formed when the lava
was still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and inter
flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava tubes intra flow cavities are formed
when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure
which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities
are generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalts are generally considered to be relatively permeable rock and can
transmit water quite readily in the horizontal and vertical directions Water is normally
transmitted through the porous rock matrix along fractures and cavities and along
clinker layers Therefore the permeability of the basalt will be highly dependent on the
presence of fractures cavities and clinker layers

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling 20 borings to depths
ranging from about 6 to 21 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate
locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our field exploration encountered near surface basalt fragments
consisting of medium hard basalt gravels and cobbles extending to depths of about 1
to 11 feet below the existing ground surface The near surface basalt fragments were
underlain by volcanic rock formation consisting of hard to very hard basalt with
occasional voids extending to the maximum depths explored of approximately 21 feet
below the existing ground surface The basalt formation encountered ranged from
slightly to severely fractured and slightly to moderately weathered The basalt

Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe
that the on site basalt formation and or the proposed compacted structural fills would
provide suitable foundation support for the proposed buildings Shallow isolated spread
and or continuous footings may be used to support the proposed buildings Specific
recommendations for design of foundations of the proposed structures are discussed
below
Based on a proposed finish floor elevation of 70 feet MSL for the Wal Mart
Store the building will straddle cut and fill areas In general the southwestern half of
the building will be in cuts of about 1 to 2 feet with the northeastern half of the building
in fills of about 1 to 5 feet To provide more uniform support for foundations bearing
across basalt cut and fill transitions and to reduce the potential for differential
settlements of the foundations bearing on rock cut and fill ground we recommend that
all footings bear on at least 12 inches of compacted structural fill To provide the
compacted fill beneath footings in cut areas the foundation subgrade should be over
excavated by at least 12 inches below the bottom of footing subgrade elevation and be
replaced with 12 inches of structural fill compacted to at least 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91

As previously indicated our field exploration encountered voids in 5 of the
20 borings drilled at this site The voids encountered in the borings ranged from about

probing and grouting be conducted during construction We recommend that the

footings located in fill areas where the fill thickness is less than 5 feet below the bottom
of footing
For estimating purposes we recommend that probes be drilled at each spread
footing location and on 10 foot centers for continuous footings The probe holes should
be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below the planned

bottom of footing If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during the

Percolation Testing
Percolation tests were conducted in four drilled borings Boring 16
and 19 to provide data for design of drywells used for disposal of stormwater runoff
The results of our percolation tests are presented in Appendix B of this report
In general drywells on the island of Hawaii are typically 5 feet in diameter and
20 feet in depth These typical drywells may generally be designed for disposal
capacities of up to 6 cubic feet per second cfs Based on the percolation test results
the subsurface conditions at Boring and 19 have low hydraulic
conductivities ranging from 1 5x10 to 6 8x10 feet per minute These values
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3383 00 Page 24
correspond to a disposal capacity of less than for a typical drywell 5 feet
diameter 20 feet deep At Boring No 13 a computed hydraulic conductivity of
3 8 feet per minute was obtained from the percolation tests This corresponds to a
disposal capacity of up to 6 cfs for a typical drywell
Based on our research of the drywells located along East Makaala Street
Ohuohu Street and Prince Kuhio Plaza the drywells on adjacent sites are generally
installed to depths ranging from 40 to 80 feet below the ground surface to obtain a
disposal capacity of up to of water has been noted during heavy storms
in several drywells installed only to 20 feet deep at a new project site in the area The
drywells were later extended to a depth of up to 80 feet below the ground surface to
improve their disposal capacities
Due to the anticipated low disposal capacity for a typical 20 foot deep drywell
at the project site we believe that it may be more economical to extend the drywell to
greater depths than to install additional drywells for disposal of stormwater runoff The
Owner Client should be made aware that the drywell extension may have a
considerable cost impact to the project as well as construction delays if unfavorable
subsurface percolation rates are encountered during construction Considering the
highly variable disposal percolation rates of the subsurface conditions and the poor
performance of drywells in the vicinity of the project site we strongly recommend that
further drilling and percolation tests be performed at each of the proposed drywell
locations prior to construction
Because of the likely need for extension of the drywell from the planned 20 foot

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
20 borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 20 at the approximate locations shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2 The approximate depths of our borings range from 6 to 21 feet
below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using truck mounted drill rigs
equipped with rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 20
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material
in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
Very Poor 0 25
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100

APPENDIX B
Percolation Testing
eS
Five percolation tests were performed in four drilled borings Boring
16 and 19 during our field exploration In Boring No 6 both constant and falling head
test methods were utilized In Boring No 13 the constant head method was used In
Boring and 19 the falling head test method was used
The constant head test was performed by pumping water into the borehole until
the water surface reached equilibrium for a sufficient period of time The depth to the
water level and the rate of pumping were then measured
The falling head test was performed by measuring the rate of water level drop
after filling up the borehole with water
The test data and the corresponding calculated percolation rates of the subsoils
are presented on Plates B 1 through B 5
Graphic representation of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs
of Borings in Appendix A
