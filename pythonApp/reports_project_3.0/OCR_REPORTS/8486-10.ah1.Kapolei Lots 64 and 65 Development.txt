SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered fill materials overlying 
stiff alluvium and medium dense coralline detritus extending to depths of up to about 
41.5 feet below the ground surface. Groundwater was not encountered at the time of our 
field exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change 
due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
Our laboratory tests indicate that the near-surface clayey soils exhibit moderate to 
high shrink-swell characteristics as well as potential collapsing when subjected to 
fluctuations in the soil moisture contents. Special foundation subgrade preparations should 
be considered to reduce the risk of structural distresses due to expansive soil conditions. 
Generally, we anticipate the earthwork would likely involve two primary types of 
materials: select granular fill and general fill. Select granular fill should consist of 
non-expansive granular material, such as crushed coralline and/or basaltic materials. 
General fills may consist of the on-site soils with a maximum particle size of 6 inches and 
should be free of deleterious materials. In general, the excavated on-site materials may be 
reused as a source of general fill material, provided they are free of vegetation, debris, 
deleterious materials, and other unsuitable materials. 
General fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to 2 to 4 percent above the optimum moisture content and 
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The non-expansive select granular fill 
materials should be placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted to at least 90 percent 
relative compaction (or 95 percent relative compaction for finished subgrades subjected to 
vehicular traffic). 
The on-site clayey soils should not be allowed to dry out and should be protected to 
retain the moisture by keeping it moist or immediately capping it with new fill. If the on-site 
clayey soils dry out and shrinkage cracks develop, the soils should be re-scarified, 
moisture-conditioned, and recompacted again. 
Based on the existing grades and conceptual plan provided, we anticipate that the 
grading work consisting of fills of about 3 to 5 feet thick will be required to achieve the design 
finished grades for the project. We recommend imported fills consisting of non-expansive, 
select granular fill materials.

supported on a shallow foundation system bearing on the non-expansive, select granular fill 
materials. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for the design of

SECTION 2. SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene 
to early Pleistocene in age. As the volcanic activity in Waianae Volcano ceased, lava 
flows from Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad 
plateau now known as the Schofield Plateau. The project site is on the southeastern flank 
of the Waianae Volcano. 
Physical and chemical weathering, followed by erosion of the Schofield Plateau, 
generated sediments that were transported to the coast. In the project vicinity and to the 
south, these sediments have accumulated and are interbedded with marine sediments 
and coral/algal reef formations, forming a sedimentary wedge. The thickness of the 
sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H-1 Highway to 
over 1,000 feet at Kapolei Beach. This wedge forms the Kapolei Plain and serves as the 
confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu. 
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic times through the present.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill 
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When 
the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the 
calcareous reef materials.

Existing Site Conditions

SECTION 3. DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the Kapolei Lots 64 and 65 Development project 
site is generally underlain by fills overlying alluvium and coralline detritus extending to 
depths of up to about 41.5 feet below the ground surface. Groundwater was not 
encountered at the time of our field exploration. However, it should be noted that 
groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal 
precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
Our laboratory tests indicate that the near-surface clayey soils exhibit moderate to 
high shrink-swell characteristics as well as potential collapsing when subjected to 
fluctuations in the soil moisture contents. Based on the provided information, we 
anticipate the site grading consisting primarily of fills less than 5 feet and minor cuts to 
achieve the design finished grades. To reduce the risk of structural distresses due to the 
moderate to high shrink/swell potential of the clayey soils, we recommend implementing 
the following special subgrade preparation procedures during construction. 
Clearing and grubbing the existing ground surface. 
Scarifying 12 inches of soils at the ground surface after the clearing and 
grubbing operation, moisture-conditioning to about 2 to 4 percent above the 
optimum moisture content, and recompacting to 90 percent relative 
compaction. 
Maintaining moisture conditions of the subgrade until covered by the 
imported non-expansive select granular fills. 
The intent of the above site preparation recommendations is to create a uniform 
reworked clayey soil layer to close-up the existing shrinkage cracks throughout the site 
and to reduce the future soil swelling/heaving potential by preparing the clayey soils with 
elevated moisture contents.

fill materials. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for the design

3.1.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site to reduce 
the potential for contamination of the excavated materials.  
After clearing and grubbing, the subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of at 
least 12 inches, moisture conditioned to about 2 to 4 percent above the optimum 
moisture content, and recompacted to 90 percent relative compaction. The intent of 
the above-recommended site preparation procedures would serve to accomplish the 
following: 
1. 
Close up the existing shrinkage cracks observed and anticipated 
throughout the site. 
2. 
Reduce the future soil swelling/heaving potential by preparing the 
clayey soils with elevated moisture contents. 
3. 
Decrease the affinity of the dry clayey soil for water. 
4. 
Create a barrier to reduce water infiltration into the underlying dry 
clayey soil. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

Soft and/or yielding areas exposed below areas designated to receive fill or future

Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement

3.1.2 Fills and Backfills

materials: select granular fill and general fill. Select granular fill should consist of 
non-expansive granular material, such as crushed coralline and/or basaltic materials.

imported fill materials for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery

3.6 
Corrosion Potential 
Two sets of laboratory corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum 
Resistivity (ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), 
were performed (by our office and Eurofins TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc.) on selected 
soil and bulk samples obtained from our field exploration. Results of the corrosivity tests 
are presented in Appendix B. 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower 
the resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to the corrosion 
of metallic objects. 
On the basis of the laboratory resistivity, the subsurface soil at the project site has 
minimum resistivity values of approximately 900 and 2,000 ohm-cm corresponding to 
corrosion ratings of 1 (Extremely Corrosive) and 2 (Very Corrosive), respectively, based 
on the guidelines provided by the City & County of Honolulu – Board of Water Supply. 
Design of metallic substructures, such as waterline pipes or other metallic piping, should 
consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure. 
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent on the pH and the chloride and sulfate contents found in the soil. 
In general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm), sulfate 
content of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH of greater than 5.0 may be considered 
“non-corrosive” to underground concrete pipelines and structures. Based on the 
laboratory-tested values of pH, chloride content, and sulfate content of the in-situ soils, 
we believe the near-surface soils at the project site may be considered “non-corrosive”; 
therefore, either Type I or Type II (Type I/II) cement may be used for the concrete in 
contact with the ground. It may be appropriate to consult with a professional corrosion

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
eleven borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1, 2, 101 through 107, I-1 and I-2, extending to 
depths between about 5 and 51.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, four 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1 and Bulk-101 through 
Bulk-103, were obtained to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the 
near-surface soils. The approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site 
Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers. 
Our geologists classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-11.  
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Five Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. A graphic presentation of the test 
results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Six Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136), including three hydrometer tests 
(ASTM D422), were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation 
characteristics of the soil and to aid in soil classification. Graphic Presentations of the 
grain size distributions are provided on Plates B-2 and B-3. 
Four One-Dimensional Swell or Settlement Potential tests (ASTM D4546 
Method A) were performed to evaluate the swell and/or compressibility characteristics of 
relatively undisturbed soil samples under different surcharge pressures. The soil sample 
is allowed to settle until it stabilized under a surcharge pressure. Water is then added to 
the soil sample to evaluate its collapse or swell potential in a saturated condition. The test 
results are presented on Plates B-4 through B-7 
Six one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the on-site soils. The test results 
are summarized on Plate B-8. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-9 and B-10. 
To evaluate the undrained shear strength of the soils, an Unconsolidated 
Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXUU) test was performed on a selected in-situ soil 
sample in accordance with ASTM D2850. The approximate in-situ effective overburden 
pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the “undisturbed” sample. The 
test results and the stress-strain curve are presented on Plate B-11. 
Three Unconfined Compression (UC) tests (ASTM D2166) were performed on 
selected in-situ samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the on-site 
cohesive soils. The test results are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. Stress-strain curves of the unconfined compression tests are presented 
on Plates B-12 through B-14.

Field Infiltration Test 
Field infiltration tests were conducted at the site to evaluate the infiltration 
characteristics of the subsurface materials encountered. The infiltration tests were 
performed at selected locations, Borings I-1 and I-2, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
These tests were performed in general accordance with the procedures in Appendix D of 
the State of Maryland, Department of the Environment “Stormwater Design Manual, 
Volumes I and II” (rev. 2009). These procedures are consistent with other states’ 
procedures and may generally be considered an industry standard. 
The field infiltration tests were performed by augering a borehole to a selected test 
depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface. Upon reaching the test depth, a 
solid casing was set to the bottom of the drilled hole to allow infiltration only through the 
soil exposed on the bottom of the boring. Falling head infiltration tests were performed to 
determine the infiltration rate of the underlying subsurface materials. The test consisted 
of four trials of filling the casing with 24 inches of water and taking periodic readings over 
a one-hour trial period. The infiltration rate is then calculated based on the result of the 
fourth and last trial for the test location. The test results are presented on Plates C-1 and 
C-2.
