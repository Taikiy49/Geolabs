2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Lanai is a shield volcano built by eruptions at the summit and along
three rift zones more than 1 20 to 1 46 million years ago The principal rift zone trends
northwestward as a broad ridge and is responsible for the conspicuous elongation of
the island in that direction A less conspicuous bulge on the southern side of the island
is a result of building on the southwest rift zone The summit of the shield collapsed to
form a caldera from which a shallow graben bordered by an echelon of step faults
extends south southeastward toward Manele Bay Numerous dikes exposed in the sea
cliff indicate that this Manele graben lies along another rift zone
The caldera was largely but not completely filled by lava flows and the present
Palawai Basin is a remnant of the caldera Just to the west of it Miki Basin with an
average diameter of about 0 9 miles is a nearly filled pit crater The top of the ridge
between them is about 140 feet above the floor of the Palawai Basin On the south side
the floor of Palawai Basin merges with that of the Manele graben through which the last
lava flows in the caldera overflowed onto the outer slope of the volcano On the
northeastern side Palawai Basin is bordered by a steep slope about 492 feet high
beyond which lies a nearly level bench of about 0 9 miles wide The thick massive
character of the lava flows in this bench indicates that it is a part of the floor of the filled
caldera Beyond the bench another steep slope rises to Lanaihale Summit 3 316 feet
altitude Southwest of the Lanaihale Summit at an elevation of 1 000 to 2 000 feet is
the Central Plateau of Lanai The soils consist of well drained fine textured and
moderately fine textured soils developed in volcanic ash and in material weathered from
basic igneous rocks
At the northern side of Lanaihale Summit is Maunalei Gulch one of several pit
craters filled by talus breccias and later lava flows So far as is known all of the lavas of
Lanai are theolitic basalts The lava flows range from about 1 foot to 100 feet in
thickness but average about 20 feet and seldom exceed 49 feet except where they
have been ponded in depressions

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the design guidelines presented in the Advisory Circular
No Airport Pavement Design and Evaluation prepared by the Federal
Aviation Administration FAA we understand that a stabilized pavement section would
be required to accommodate heavy aircraft weighing 100 000 pounds or more Based
on the soils encountered and the assumed forecast traffic mix the stabilized flexible
pavement section would consist of 4 inches of hot mix asphalt surface over 7 inches of
asphalt treated base course The available as built information for the existing aviation
apron indicates that the pavement section consists of 4 inches of asphaltic concrete
over 6 inches of stabilized base course over 7 inches of aggregate base course
Due to the uncertainty of the future aircraft traffic at Lanai Airport and the
potential for encountering areas consisting of moderately expansive soils at the
pavement subgrade we believe that it would be beneficial to use a new pavement
section similar to the existing apron pavement section Therefore we recommend using
a stabilized flexible pavement section of 4 inches of hot mix asphalt surface underlain
by 6 inches of asphalt treated base course over 6 inches of aggregate subbase course
It should be noted that the inclusion of a subbase course layer would also provide a more
firm bearing surface to facilitate placement of the asphalt treated base course and
improve the drainage below the pavement
For a_ stabilized rigid pavement section we recommend 10 inches of

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly Vegetation debris deleterious and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off site to reduce
the potential for contamination of the excavated materials Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill
should be over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting
excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill The excavated soft soils
should be disposed properly off site Contract documents should include additive
and deductive unit prices for over excavation and engineered fill placement to
account for variations in the over excavation quantities
After clearing and grubbing the areas to receive fills and the finished subgrades in
cut areas should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to
at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to no less than
90 percent relative compaction The compaction requirement should be increased
to no less than 95 percent relative compaction in areas where aircraft traffic is
planned Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as
a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density

Fill and Backfill Materials
The fill and backfill materials up to the pavement section may consist of the
excavated on site soils provided that they are screened of vegetation deleterious
materials and particles greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension Imported
general fill materials should consist of materials with particle sizes of 3 inches or
less in maximum dimension The material should have a laboratory California
Bearing Ratio CBR value of 12 or higher and a swell potential of no more than
1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills up to the bottom of the pavement section should be
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to no
less than 90 percent relative compaction The compaction requirement within the
upper 12 inches below the pavement section should be increased to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of the soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the
same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the
water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory
rollers multiple wheel pneumatic tired rollers or other types of acceptable
compaction equipment Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed
when compacting the fill material

Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings it appears that cut
slopes would expose soils consisting of stiff silts and clays In general we believe
that cut slopes into the soil materials may be cut at an inclination of two horizontal
to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter
Fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fills
placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the
existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations
should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers
in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope
ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face Track rolling of slopes should not be
accepted In the event that over cutting of a slope occurs keying and benching
requirements should be implemented instead of backfilling the slope to the design
grade with sliver fills Water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and
slope planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for
erosion of the finished slopes

3 8 Base and Subbase Preparation
The new pavement sections recommended for this project will consist of hot mix
asphalt or portland cement concrete surfaces overlying stabilized base stabilized
subbase P 403 and or subbase course over compacted subgrade The primary
functions of the stabilized base subbase and subbase courses consist of the following
1 To provide uniform support for the pavement surface course throughout the
design life
2 To reduce potential pumping by providing drainage through the use of a
material with little fines
3 To provide a construction platform to expedite construction
The required subbase materials should conform to the project specification
requirements The aggregate subbase courses should be moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture content placed in thin lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness
and compacted to no less than 100 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
The stabilized base and subbase consisting of asphalt treated base and subbase
materials respectively should consist of asphalt treated basalt aggregate placed in a
layer not to exceed 7 inches in compacted thickness and compacted to at least
91 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity determined in accordance with
ASTM D 2041

Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a

3 10 Aircraft Hangar Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we recommend supporting the
aircraft hangar on a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous
footings An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may
be used for the design of footings bearing on the stiff to hard silts and clays This
transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the
lowest adjacent finished grade Foundations next to utility trenches and or easements

should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the
bottom edge of the utility trench or embedded as deep as the inverts of the utility lines
This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures
with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation
settlement
We recommend recompacting the soils encountered at the footing subgrade level
to no less than 90 percent relative compaction Soft and or loose materials encountered
at the footing subgrade level should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm or
dense materials The over excavation should be backfilled with select granular fill
material compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction or the footings may
be extended down to the firm or dense materials
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate that total settlements of footings bearing on the
recompacted soils may be on the order of about 1 inch We estimate differential
settlements between adjacent footings to be on the order of about 0 5 inches
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by friction developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 35 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted residual soils
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the
soils around the footings are well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs
the passive pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected It
should be emphasized that surface water should be diverted away from the foundations
We recommend that a Geolabs representative observe footing excavations prior

3 11 Underground Utilities
We anticipate that the underground utilities will primarily consist of water
drainage and sewer lines In general we recommend using granular bedding consisting
of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation under the pipes to
support the utility lines The initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes
should consist of free draining backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the
potential for pipe damage from compaction of the backfill It is critical to use a free
draining granular material as the initial trench backfill to reduce the potential for
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the
sides of the pipes Use of clayey soils as initial trench backfill directly around utility pipes
is not recommended
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level of 12 inches above the pipes
to the finished subgrade should consist of the approved on site soils or general fill
material The backfill material should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
future ground subsidence The upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement
grade should be compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction Mechanical
compaction equipment should be used to compact the backfill materials Compaction
efforts by water tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed
3 12 Drainage
The finished grades outside the aircraft hangar structure and apron area should
be sloped to shed water away from the foundations pavement section and to reduce the
potential for ponding Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and
pavement should also be avoided These drainage requirements are essential for the
proper performance of the above foundation and pavement recommendations since
ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of
strength In addition areas around the foundation and pavement should be properly
backfilled to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration into the subsurface

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling 15 borings
extending to depths about 11 5 to 31 5 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 We drilled the borings
using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field He monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 15
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests performed on selected samples are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Two one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples
of the silty and clayey soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the subsurface soils
The test results are summarized on Plate B 1
Three Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Test results
are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Two Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics Direct shear test results are presented on
Plates B 3 and B 4
Five laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the on site soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the
soils The test results are presented on Plates B 5 through B 9
