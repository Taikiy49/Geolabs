This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Aua Road Route 6 Drainage and Surface Rehabilitation project near
the Village of Aua on the island of Tutuila American Samoa The general location of
the project site is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical engineering recommen

analyses The recommendations presented herein are intended for design input and
may not be appropriate to form the basis for preparation of final construction cost
estimates nor for construction bidding purposes

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Tutuila was built during the Pliocene and early Pleistocene Age by
the extrusion of basaltic lavas and pyroclastic materials from five volcanoes Stearns
1944 Following the cessation of the main shield building activity there was a long
period of erosion accompanied by the growth of fringing coral reefs During the late
Quaternary volcanism was renewed by eruptions of lava and pyroclastic materials at
several vents on the south side of the island forming the Tafuna Leone Plain
The main mountain mass of Tutuila was divided into seven formations Stearns
1944 The rocks are thought to be of Pliocene Age consisting of thinly bedded to
massive flows of basalt andesitic basalt and andesite interbedded with cinder cones
and tuff beds with thrachytic dikes plugs and crater fills The rock units were erupted
from as many as five eruptive centers but the principal volcano was near the middle
of the island Pago Pago Harbor is believed to occupy part of the collapsed caldera of
that principal volcano Erosion along a major fault along the caldera wall formed the
near vertical cliff on the northeast side of the harbor
Prior to the eruption of the Leone Volcanics beach and lagoonal deposits
accumulated on and behind a submerged barrier reef at the present site of the Leone
Plain These deposits were later buried under several hundred feet of volcanic
materials These older beach and lagoonal deposits consist partially of sand and
coralline gravels
Three tuff cones and a thick sequence of lava flow form the Plain between
Tafuna and Leone The rock is thought to be of Holocene Age possibly only a few
thousand years old as evidenced by geologic relationships in the area The lava flows
are mainly thinly bedded basalts with interbeds of tuff and breccia

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
On the basis of our field exploration fill materials consisting of clayey and
gravelly soils were encountered at the proposed project site below the existing
pavement to depths of approximately 2 to 8 feet below the surface The near surface
fills are underlain by clayey silts and sandy gravels to the maximum depth explored of
about 20 feet below the existing ground surface At some boring locations cobbles
boulders and weathered basalt formation were encountered below the near surface
soils
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3212 00 Page 13
Based on our field exploration and site reconnaissance portions of the existing
Aua Road pavement have deteriorated significantly such that its serviceability is
currently poor to unsatisfactory We believe that one of the most significant causes of
the observed pavement deterioration is a result of poor surface and subsurface
drainage conditions at the site In addition the lack of a proper base course layer
below the asphalt concrete pavement has also contributed to the failures of the
pavement Poor subgrade soil conditions such as saturated clays and non uniform
subgrade soils encountered in the borings have also contributed to the pavement
deterioration observed
Based on observations at the site the surface and subsurface drainage
conditions of the roadway are poor as evidenced by ponding of rain water in depressed
areas where the pavement had settled Drainage structures such as catch basins or
storm drains were not observed along portions of the road alignment Where drainage
structures were observed they were generaily obstructed by vegetation and debris
Therefore we recommend that the existing drainage ditch along road alignment be
reconstructed to intercept the runoff water from slopes
As a result of the poor surface drainage water may be introduced into the
pavement section and subgrade from cracks in the pavement surface from seepage
into the subgrade soils and from poor drainage adjacent to paved areas Water in the
subgrade soils may cause softening of the clayey subgrade soils which can result in
the pavement distress and subsequent failure Therefore we recommend that the
existing roadway pavements be removed and be replaced with new pavements
designed in accordance with the recommendations presented herein A small section
of the roadway near the top of the mountain pass may be rehabilitated by placement
of overlays however the long term performance of this section may be questionable
if subsurface drainage is not provided below the pavement

Pavement Drainage System
One of the primary distress mechanisms observed in pavement structures is
pumping due to saturation of the base course and or subgrade soils In addition free
water voids in the pavement section repeated heavy wheel loads and an erodible
base can also cause pumping
in general the primary source of free water in the pavement section is surface
water infiltration through cracks and joints in the pavement A major source of
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3212 00 Page 17
infiltrated moisture is the longitudinal pavement shoulder joint Water also enters the
pavement section from shallow ditches To reduce moisture infiltration into the
pavement structure all pavement joints and cracks should be sealed to reduce
infiltration and pavement drainage systems should be installed to collect and divert free
water from the pavement section Therefore considering the high rainfall in this area
we recommend a subsurface drainage system be provided on both sides of the
roadway alignment to divert subsurface water The pavement drainage system
recommended consists of three elements
1 A permeable base to provide rapid drainage of free water that may enter
the pavement structure
2 A longitudinal edge drain collector system to convey accumulated water
from the permeable base to appropriate outlet structures
3 A filter separator layer to reduce the potential for migration of fines into
the permeable base from the subgrade subbase or shoulder base
material
Permeable Base
A properly designed and constructed permeable base can rapidly drain water
that infiltrates the pavement structure and will not allow high water pressures to
build up beneath the pavement Because of the relatively steep terrain of the
roadway alignment surface water flows with high velocities are anticipated
Therefore we recommend the use of a permeable base for support of the
pavements in lieu of the normal aggregate base course The permeable base
material would be more free draining for the anticipated wet conditions As

recommended in the Pavement Design section a 6 inch thick permeable base
layer is recommended for the flexible and rigid pavement sections Where the
pavements will be subjected to flooding conditions such as at stream crossings
we recommend that the thickness of the permeable base material be increased
to a minimum of 12 inches
Due to the different thickness of the surface course for the new flexible and rigid
pavements where both new flexible and rigid pavements are used we
recommend that a transition section be provided between the flexible and rigid
pavement sections to allow better water flow in the pavement drainage system
A typical pavement transition section is illustrated on Plate 6
Two types of permeable base treated or untreated may be used for the new
pavements planned for this project depending on the site conditions Treated

or cement treated permeable base CTPB In general treated permeable base
is recommended when there is lack of confinement for the pavement structure
Where there is confinement for the pavement section untreated permeable base

be confined on both sides of the roadway by a drainage ditch therefore an
untreated permeable base may be used in the pavement section
Permeable base material should consist of a hard durable crushed angular
basalt aggregate with essentially no fines A permeable base consisting of

filtration The major criteria considered for a geotextile drainage filtration
application include 1 soil retention piping resistance 2 permeability

For a geotextile to perform effectively as a filter in a geotextile drainage system
it must remain free draining by having opening characteristics compatible with
the surrounding soils The choice of a correct geotextile is critical to the
formation of a stable and effective soil filter Unless proper fabric piping
resistance clogging resistance and constructability strength requirements are
obtained it is doubtful that the desired results will be obtained Construction
installation and monitoring must also be provided to ensure that the materials
have been installed properly
Therefore we recommend that a layer of geotextile fabric such as Mirafi 140N
or equivalent be provided between the upper permeable base and the
underlying subgrade material for the rigid pavement section to reduce the
potential for migration of fines into the permeable base from the underlying
subgrade or shoulder base material Generally non woven needle punched
geotextiles are better for pavement drainage applications than heat bonded
geotextiles Woven or slit film geotextiles should not be used The geotextile
fabric used should conform to the specifications as presented in Appendix C
For the flexible pavement section the aggregate subbase course layer see
Appendix C for gradation requirements would serve as a filter for the permeable
base therefore a geotextile fabric would not be required for the flexible
pavement section
Pavement Joints
Where rigid pavements will be used we recommend that pavement joints and
joint sealants be provided Joint sealants should be used to provide a seal to reduce
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3212 00 Page 22
potential infiltration of water from surface runoff Joint sealants also provide a
compressible joint to help control cracking due to volume changes in the concrete as
a result of fluctuations in the temperature In general joints should be provided in rigid
pavements for the following
1 To reduce potential curling and warping stresses in the pavement caused
by temperature and moisture gradients across the pavement slabs
2 To reduce and control cracking due to volume changes in the concrete
3 To reduce damage to immovable structures
4 To facilitate construction

Based on the information provided to us minor cuts and fills on the order of
about 1 foot are planned along the centerline of the proposed roadway reconstruction
project Therefore site grading will involve removal of the existing pavements and
construction of new pavements Items of site preparation that are addressed in the
following subsections include the following 1 Site Preparation and 2 Requirements

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed The existing pavement sections should be
removed and the asphalt concrete should be disposed of off site All vegetation
GEOLABS HAWAIl
W O 3212 00 Page 23
debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed
of properly off site to reduce potential contamination of the excavated materials
After clearing and grubbing the exposed subgrade should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 8 inches properly moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95
percent relative compaction For the clayey subgrades the compaction
requirement may be reduced to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the
water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
During the scarification process of the subgrade if over sized materials greater
than 3 inches in largest dimension are exposed the over sized materials should
be completely removed from the subgrade to provide a more uniform subgrade
soil condition for construction of the pavement structure The cobbles and
boulders removed should not be reused in the project as fill material
Any soft weak or yielding areas disclosed during clearing operations should be
over excavated to expose firm ground and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with engineered fill compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction Prior to placement of the backfill the over excavated subgrade
should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to at
least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and compacted to at least
90 percent relative compaction The excavated soft and or organic soils should
be properly disposed of off site Contract documents should include additive and

account for variations in the over excavation quantities

Fills and Backfills
In general the on site granular soils may be used as fill and backfill material if
the over sized particles greater than 3 inches in largest dimension are
screened The on site silty and clayey soils may be used as fill material below
the pavement structures provided that they are screened of over sized materials
properly moisture conditioned and compacted as recommended herein
Where needed select fill material should be well graded from coarse to fine with
no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension The material should have
a laboratory CBR value of 15 or more with a maximum swell of less than
1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 In
addition the fill material should contain a maximum of 20 percent particles
passing the No 200 sieve
Granular fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches
in loose thickness moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
GEOLABS HAWAII

compaction For clayey fills the compaction requirement may be reduced to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
All permanent fill slopes should have inclinations of two horizontal to one vertical
2H 1V or flatter All fills placed on slopes with a five horizontal to one vertical
5H 1V slope inclination or steeper should be keyed and benched Fill slopes
should be constructed by over filling past the design limits of the slope and
cutting back to the design slope to provide adequate compaction at the edges
of the slope
Pavement Overlay
Pavement overlays may be considered for the portion of the roadway near the
mountain pass Only minor pavement surface deterioration was observed along this
portion of the roadway However based on our borings the base layer directly below
the asphalt concrete pavement does not appear to meet the requirements of a typical
base course layer therefore the long term performance of this section may be
questionable especially if subsurface drainage is not provided below the pavement
However if pavement overlay is desired for this section of the roadway we
believe that asphalt concrete overlays would be more practical when compared to
concrete overlays since the existing pavement is asphalt concrete If concrete overlays
are to be used all cracks greater than inch in width should be filled prior to placing
the concrete overlay The smaller cracks should be filled with hot liquid crack filler
while the larger cracks should be filled with asphalt hot mix or cold patch Due to the
minimum concrete overlay thickness and the cost of repair and patching work it is our
opinion that concrete overlays would not be cost effective nor practical
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3212 00 Page 26
For asphalt concrete overlay we recommend a minimum overlay thickness of
at least 2 inches Prior to asphalt concrete overlay the existing asphalt concrete
surface should be thoroughly swept to remove all loose materials A tack coat should
be applied to the existing asphalt concrete followed by a pavement reinforcing fabric
to bond the new asphalt concrete overlay to the existing pavements Pavement
reinforcing fabric should be aligned and placed with no wrinkles that lap If manual
laydown methods are used the fabric should be unrolled aligned and placed in
increments of approximately 30 feet Adjacent borders of the fabric should be lapped
between 2 and 4 inches Pavement reinforcing fabric should be manufactured from
polyester polypropylene or polypropylene nylon material The fabric should be non
woven and heat treated on at least one side Guideline specifications for the pavement
reinforcing fabric are presented in Appendix C of this report
The minimum pavement section recommended is 2 inches of asphalt concrete
over 4 inches of aggregate base course where the existing pavement will serve as the
subbase Placement of a base course layer less than 4 inches in thickness is not
recommended due to practical considerations As a result where the elevation
differential between the existing pavement surface to the finished pavement elevation
is less than about 6 inches we recommend that full depth asphalt concrete overlays
be used Asphalt concrete should be placed in lifts not exceeding 3 inches in
thickness
To provide a smooth transition from the full depth pavement overlay to the typical
roadway section we recommend that a five horizontal to one vertical 5H 1V slope be
provided between the full depth asphalt concrete to the 3 inches of asphalt concrete in
lieu of an abrupt change The pavement reinforcing fabric recommended should be
placed over this slope The relatively smooth transition and the pavement reinforcing

fabric should help reduce the potential for reflection cracking due to an abrupt change
in the asphalt concrete thickness
Underground Utilities
For support of utility pipes we recommend that a granular bedding consisting of
6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under the
pipes We recommend that a minimum cover of 3 feet be provided between the top of
utility pipes and the finished grade to facilitate construction of the pavement structure
and compaction of the trench backfill The backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes
should consist of free draining backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the
potential for damaging the pipes from compaction of the backfill
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to
the finish grade may consist of the on site excavated materials provided that the on site
materials are screened of any oversized materials less than 3 inches in maximum
dimension Trench backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture placed in 8 inch maximum horizontal loose lifts and mechanically compacted
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction for granular type backfill For clayey
backfill material the compaction requirement may be reduced to 90 percent relative
compaction

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on selected soil
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Three 1 inch ring swell tests were performed on selected samples to evaluate
the swelling potential of the subsoils The test results are summarized on Plate B 1
Four Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of the
tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Eight sieve analyses tests were performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is presented on Plates B 3 through
B 10
One compaction ASTM D 1557 91 test was performed on a bulk sample of the
soils retrieved to evaluate the dry density and moisture content relationship The test
results are summarized on Plate B 11
Two California Bearing Ratio tests were performed on the bulk samples retrieved
to evaluate the pavement support characteristics The test results are presented on
Plate B 11
