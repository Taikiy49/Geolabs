Dear Mr. Fong: 
Geolabs observed the helical pile installation and testing for the Port Allen Small Boat 
Harbor, Floating Dock Improvements project. This letter report summarizes our geotechnical 
engineering observations and testing during the helical pier construction installation for 
Floating Dock Improvements project on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. 
Geolabs provided geotechnical engineering services for the Port Allen Small Boat 
Harbor, Floating Dock Improvements project in general accordance with the requirements 
outlined in the project technical specifications section 35 51 13– Floating Dock System. The 
purpose of our geotechnical observations was to monitor the geotechnical engineering 
aspects of the helical pier installation and testing for conformance with the plans and 
specifications. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located at Port Allen Small Boat Harbor in the Ele’ele area on the 
Island of Kauai, Hawaii. The project involves the construction of a new floating dock system 
and improvements to the wharf access. The new floating dock is anchored by helical piers, 
designed for an allowable tensile load capacity of 4.4 kips and 6.6 kips (2.2 tons to 3.3 tons). 
The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
The general contractor, Hawaiian Dredging Construction Company, Inc. installed and 
tested the helical piers.

HELICAL PIER INSTALLATION AND TESTING 
As part of the project requirements, a helical pier system was designed, consisting 
of 76 individual piers driven into the mudline. The helical piers consisted of galvanized steel 
pipe segments with helical steel plates with diameters of 10, 12, and 14 inches, on the first 
pile segment only. The helical pier segments were driven into the ground utilizing Komatsu 
138US excavator equipped with a Digga 9ADS driver.  
The helical pier installations, verification and proof tests were performed by Hawaiian 
Dredging Construction Company, Inc. The approximate locations of the helical piers are 
shown on the Helical Pier Location Plans, Plates 2.1 and 2.2. Installation and testing of the 
helical pier were conducted from April 3, 2018 to April 25, 2018. A summary of the helical 
pier installations and testing are presented on Plates 3.1 through 3.4. 
Helical Pier Installation 
A Geolabs representative observed the installation of the helical piers. The helical 
pier segments were driven into the ground utilizing Komatsu 138US excavator equipped 
with a Digga 9ADS driver.  
The helical piers were installed to depths between 27 and 37 feet below the existing 
measured mudline, corresponding to Elevations of -33.2 to -46.2 feet Mean Sea 
Level (MSL). Helical piers were driven to a minimum depth and resistance measured by a 
torque meter. The tops of the piers were driven such that they were leveled with the existing 
mudline. Installation logs of the helical piers recorded by our representative are presented 
on Plates 4.1 through 4.81, Helical Pier Installation Records. 
Helical Pier Testing 
Five pre-production helical piers (TP-1 through TP-5) were installed for verification 
testing and evaluation of the production helical pier installation criteria. The locations of 
verification test nails are also shown on Plates 2.1 and 2.2. The pre-production helical piers 
were installed to depths of 27 to 35 feet below the existing mudlines, to evaluate the bearing 
stratum. Verification testing was conducted on each test piers to a maximum test load of 
19 kips (ultimate load capacity) at 1 to 4 days after initial installation, to evaluate the gain of 
the pier capacity over time. The maximum test load was held for at least 15 minutes to verify 
the creeping of the pier system. Results of the verification testing are presented on Plates 
5.1.1 through 5.5.2. 
Based on the evaluation of the verification testing conducted on the pre-production 
helical piers, it appeared that the helical pier system is capable of providing the ultimate load 
resistance of 19 kips at three-day age after initial driving with a torque resistance of about 
2,100 foot-pounds and lengths of the piers about 27 and 34 feet below the mudline at Pier 
A and Pier B, respectively.

In accordance to the project specifications, twelve production helical piers (SF-09, 
SF-14, SF-16, SF-19, SF-31, SF-40, SF-44, SF-46, SF-51, SF-60, SF-63, and SF-74) were 
selected for proof testing. Proof test helical piers were loaded to at least 125 percent of the 
design loads (either 5.5 kips or 8.3 kips). The maximum test load was held for about 
15 minutes. Results of the proof tests are presented on Plates 6.1 through 6.12. 
It should be noted that Helical Pier SF-51 was installed with a torque resistance of 
1,900 foot-pounds at end of installation, less than 2,100 foot-pounds of installation criteria. 
The proof testing was performed on SF-51 to a maximum test load of 16 kips, to verify the 
pull-out capacity. Pull out failure was not observed at the maximum test load. 
Based on our observations, we believe that the helical piers observed by Geolabs 
have been installed in general conformance with the project plans and specifications and 
within the limits of generally accepted construction practices. Based on our observations of 
the helical pier verification and proof testing performed by the contractor, it is our 
professional opinion that the helical piers have been installed to provide sufficient load 
capacity without reaching pull-out failure at the design load capacities with a factor of safety 
of greater than 1.25. 
LIMITATIONS 
It should be noted that it is not possible to continuously observe all activities occurring 
simultaneously on a construction site. Our observation services were performed in 
accordance with generally accepted standards of geotechnical engineering practice; no 
warranty is expressed or implied. Provision of construction monitoring by an engineer is not 
insurance, nor does it guarantee construction of any type. The contractor shall retain primary 
responsibility for the quality of the work, for adhering to plans and specifications, and for 
repairing defects regardless of when they are found.
