installation of new utility lines such as water sewer storm drain electrical and
communications

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas the Waianae and
Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger Koolau
Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age and forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was
still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking against its
eastern flank Both Waianae and Koolau Ranges have lost most of their original shield
outlines and are now long narrow ridges shaped largely by erosion
The project area is generally composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of
lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows
of jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers In situ chemical
weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred during the last 1 to 2 million years
The weathering process has formed a mantle of residual soils and saprolite In general
saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of
volcanic rocks The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with depth
2 2 Site Description
The Manana Navy project site is near the Pearl Highlands Shopping Center
Sam s Club in the Manana area of Pearl City on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The
entrance into the project site is through Acacia Road off Kamehameha Highway as
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The project site is currently occupied by existing housing units that will be
demolished as part of the project The project site generally slopes down gently from
northeast to southwest Based on the topographic survey map provided the elevations
at the project site range from about 160 feet Mean Sea Level MSL along the
northeastern portion of the project site to about 127 feet MSL along the southwestern
portion

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly if applicable Deleterious and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed properly off site to reduce the potential
for contaminating the excavated materials
After clearing and demolishing the project area finished subgrades in cut areas and
areas designated to receive fills should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum
dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to
the maximum dry density
Where yielding areas or soft and or loose areas are disclosed during clearing and
grubbing operations or during the scarification process the soft and or loose
materials should be over excavated to expose firm dense conditions and the
resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill materials
Therefore contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for

Fills and Backfills
In general the excavated on site clayey soils may be re used as a source of
general fill materials provided that they are free of vegetation deleterious materials
and clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension
The on site clayey soils if accepted for use as general fills may be used as part of
the trench backfill or in landscape areas
Imported general fill materials should consist of soil materials with a maximum
particle size of 3 inches or less with sufficient fines between 10 and 60 percent
particles passing the No 200 sieve to prevent the occurrence of voids in the
compacted mass In addition general fill materials should have a CBR value of
12 or greater and a swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with
ASTM D 1883
Select granular fills if needed should consist of select granular materials such as
crushed coralline or basaltic materials The materials should be well graded from
coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The
materials should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a
maximum swell of 1 percent or less Imported fill materials should be tested for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the
intended use
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements

thickness moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Select granular fill
materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to at least

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
3 2 Underground Utility Lines
We envision that new utility lines i e water sewer storm drain electrical and
communications will likely be required for project development In general granular
bedding consisting of 6 inches of open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is
recommended below the pipes for uniform support Free draining granular materials
such as open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be used for the
initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support
around the pipes Itis critical to use the free draining materials around the pipes to
reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide
adequate support around the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill around the
pipe could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level of 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade may consist of the excavated on site soils provided that they
are free of deleterious materials The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
appreciable future ground subsidence Where trenches will be below areas subjected to
vehicular traffic paved areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the
pavement grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
3 3 Thrust Block
We envision that thrust blocks will be required at the bends of the new water
lines The following recommendations may be used for the preliminary design and
sizing of the thrust blocks Lateral loads acting on the thrust blocks may be resisted by
the friction developed between the bottom of the thrust blocks and the bearing materials
and by passive earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the thrust block
A coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used for the concrete thrust blocks bearing on the
stiff on site clayey soils
In general we envision that the bottom of the thrust blocks will be embedded at
least 3 feet below the existing ground surface We envision stiff clayey soils and basalt

3 4 Corrosion Potential
A total of 20 soil resistivity surveys were conducted at three selected locations
Method and a Nilsson Model 400 resistance meter manufactured by Nilsson Electrical
Laboratory Inc The method utilizes four surface probes or pins electrodes to evaluate
the average resistivity of the underlying soil mass The average resistivity values of the
soil mass extending from the existing ground surface to the test depth was assessed
by using this method at the approximate survey locations shown on the Site Plan Plate
2 Results of the field soil resistivity surveys are presented on Plate C 1 of Appendix C
In addition we conducted laboratory tests including pH minimum resistivity
chloride contents and sulfate contents to evaluate the corrosivity of the underlying
subsurface soils encountered at the project site Based on the test results presented on
Plate B 6 the subsurface soils at the project site generally exhibit a minimum resistivity
value of about 1 270 to 6 580 ohm cm indicating that the subsurface soils are very to
moderately corrosive to buried metallic structures Therefore we recommend properly
designing substructures especially metallic substructures to be protected against
potential corrosion A corrosion engineer should be consulted for detailed
recommendations on corrosion protection

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the housing project site were explored by drilling
and sampling three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths
of about 21 to 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring
locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on the Log Legend Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests performed on selected samples are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX C
Field Resistivity Testing
APPENDIX C
Field Resistivity Testing
Resistivity generally is recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects In general the lower the
resistivity the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure
Conversely the higher the resistivity the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of
metallic objects
Geophysical survey equipment i e resistance meter was mobilized to the project
site on for the field survey Twenty soil resistivity surveys were
conducted at five locations designated as R 1 through R 5 on the Site Plan Plate 2
using the in Pin Method and a Nilsson Model 400 resistance meter
manufactured by Nilsson Electrical Laboratory Inc Our field resistivity work was
performed under sunny conditions with generally dry to damp surface conditions
The method utilizes four surface probes or pins electrodes to evaluate the
average resistivity an indication of corrosivity of the underlying soil mass The average
resistivity values of the soil mass extending from the existing ground surface to the test
depth was assessed by using this method at the approximate survey locations
The average soil resistance was evaluated at each survey location with the
electrodes aligned at equal spacings of various distances based on the anticipated utility
depths The depth of penetration of a resistivity sounding is a function of the electrode
spacing
The resistance meter Nilsson Model 400 provides a voltage between the outer
electrodes causing an electrical current to flow between them The voltage drop between
the inner electrodes is then measured by a sensitive built in voltmeter As a result the soil
resistance in ohms is obtained from the voltmeter dial reading The average soil
resistivity in ohms cm which is directly related to both resistance and spacing can then
be computed Results of the field resistivity surveys are presented on Plate C 1 It should
be noted that the soil resistivity may be influenced by temperature weather conditions soil
moisture existing underground utilities and other factors
