INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the design of the proposed addition to the existing Chinen residence at 813
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3023 00 Page 2
21st Avenue in the Kaimuki area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general
location of the project site and its vicinity is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings of our exploration and presents our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from analyses of the data obtained
during the exploration These recommendations are intended for use in project planning
and to provide engineering design input The recommendations and findings presented
herein are not intended for other uses nor are they intended for final construction
estimation or bidding purposes
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the subsurface soils
and rock conditions at the site of the proposed residence in order to provide data for the
formulation of geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the design of site
grading retaining walls and building foundations for the proposed addition to the existing
single family residence
The scope of our exploration consisted of the following tasks and work efforts
1 Mobilization of a portable drill rig and operators to and from the project site
2 Drilling and sampling of three 3 borings to depths ranging between about
9 5 and 10 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the project site
3 Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the borings by a Field
Engineer Geologist from our firm

4 Laboratory testing of the selected samples obtained during the field
exploration as an aid to classifying the materials encountered and
determining their engineering properties
5 Analyses of the field and laboratory data for the formulation of geotechnical
engineering considerations and recommendations pertinent to the site
grading retaining walls and foundation design of the proposed addition to
the existing residence
6 Preparation of six 6 copies of this report summarizing our work on the
project and presenting our findings and recommendations
7 Coordination of our overall work on the project and client design team

8 Miscellaneous work efforts such as drafting word processing and clerical

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is situated in the southeasterly portion of the Kaimuki area of
Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location of the site and its vicinity
is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1

The site slopes gently down in a southeasterly direction with ground surface
elevations ranging from about 105 feet Mean Sea Level MSL along 21st Avenue to
about 87 feet MSL along the rear of the lot
The proposed addition will be constructed at the rear or southeasterly portion of
the existing residence
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to construct a two story addition to the existing residence Detailed
plans of the addition were not available during the preparation of this report however it
is anticipated that the lower story of the addition will match the grades of the existing half
basement of the home and that the upper story will be of wood framed construction
Details of the proposed earthwork were not available for the preparation of this
report One possible scenario would be for the site to be left at the existing grades and
the lower story of the addition could be built using post and beam construction
An alternate scenario could be to place fill to raise the grades in the area of the
addition and to construct the lower story with a slab on grade floor It is anticipated that
most of the project earthwork will be to create a level pad for the new addition This
grading scenario would involve fills of less than about five feet 5 in thickness
Access to the area of the proposed addition is relatively restricted It is our feeling
that it may be difficult to mobilize construction equipment to the back of the existing home
for placement of fill material Therefore it is our opinion that the use of post and beam
construction would probably be preferable in terms of constructability and possibly in
terms of economics

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The eastern half of the island of Oahu is composed of a single basalt shield
volcano built by the extrusion of the lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series during the late
Pliocene and early Pleistocene After the cessation of this main shield building phase
ended there is a long period of erosion followed by a renewal of volcanic activity with the
extrusion of the basaltic lavas of the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The project site is situated on the southeasterly flank of Kaimuki which is a small
shield volcano of the Honolulu Volcanic Series
Subsurface Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
three 3 borings to depths ranging between 9 5 and 10 5 feet below the existing ground
surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our borings encountered a relatively thin surface layer of fill or residual
soil consisting of medium stiff silty clay Underlying this surface layer our exploration
encountered medium dense gravel and cobbles A a clinker and basalt formation to the
maximum depths explored
Boring No 3 encountered what is believed to be an abandoned cesspool at a
depth of about two feet 2 below the existing ground surface This suspected cesspool
has an open void area of about 7 5 feet in height The material at the bottom of the void
consists of soft wet reddish brown clayey silt
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings during our exploration

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our findings the proposed addition to the existing residence is feasible
provided that our recommendations are followed Our exploration encountered relatively
competent residual soils and volcanic rock formation underlying the site
Our exploration also encountered what appears to be an abandoned cesspool
This cesspool should be opened cleaned of soft or loose material and backfilled in
accordance with the recommendations given herein
Our laboratory testing indicates that the surface clay soils are moderately
expansive These expansive soils should not be used for structural fill or backfill
It is our opinion that shallow spread or strip footing foundations may be used to
support the proposed addition Due to the topography of the site and space constraints
in the area of the proposed addition it is our opinion that the use of post and beam
construction would be very appropriate for this project in lieu of constructing retaining
walls or using embankment fill to raise the grade of the sloping site to match the elevation
of the existing residence
Foundation Support
Our field exploration encountered relatively competent residual soils and basaltic
rock formation at relatively shallow depths below the existing ground surface It is our
opinion that shallow spread or strip footing foundations may be used to support the
proposed addition An allowable bearing capacity of 2 000 pounds per square foot may
be used for foundations designed in accordance with the recommendations presented
herein
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3023 00 Page 7
Foundations should consist of individual post footings embedded a minimum of
eighteen inches 18 below the lowest adjacent grade For foundations located adjacent
to slopes or on sloping ground the foundations should be further embedded i e deeper
to provide a minimum setback of six feet 6 from the toe of the foundation measured
horizontally out to the nearest adjacent slope face
As previously discussed it is our opinion that post and beam construction with
wood framing for the proposed addition would be very appropriate for the proposed
residential addition project Access to the site by construction equipment is limited and
it would be difficult to mobilize heavy equipment and material for the placement and
compaction of fill
Generally we envision that the individual excavated spread footing foundations
could be installed by the use of manual labor or small equipment to excavate for the
foundations
Our laboratory testing indicates that the upper clayey soils are moderately to highly
expansive when wetted after drying To reduce the potential of structural distress from
swelling of these clayey soils the foundation excavations should be cut neatly with vertical
side walls The foundations should be cast neat against the excavation to reduce the
need for formwork and backfilling The soils exposed in the excavation should be moist
but not saturated before the placement of concrete
If any soft or weak areas are encountered at the bottom of any footing excavation
they should be remedied by over excavation of the soft and or weak materials to
competent clinker or rock formation

Existing Cesspool
The existing cesspool found on the site should be opened cleaned of loose soft
materials and backfilled with select granular material such as select borrow The
backfill should be placed in level lifts not exceeding eight inches 8 in loose thickness
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent of its maximum dry density as established
by ASTM Test Method D 1557 91
Site Preparation
Prior to the commencement of any earthwork all areas within the grading limits
should be cleared of all vegetation and deleterious materials The resulting stripped
material should be disposed of properly off site Any soft weak or yielding areas
disclosed during the clearing operations should be overexcavated to firm ground and
the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted engineered fill
Our laboratory testing indicates that the on site clayey soils have a moderate to
high swelling potential if allowed to become saturated after drying It is our opinion
that use of this material for fill and backfill should be avoided for this project
Any imported material required for fill or backfill should conform to the require
ments stipulated in Section 30 of the Standard Specifications for Public Works
Construction Select Borrow for Subbase Course dated September 1986
Prior to the placement of fill the existing surface soils should be scarified to a
depth of about six inches 6 moistened to above optimum moisture content and
recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent of its maximum dry density as established
by ASTM Test Method D 1557 91

W O 3023 00 Page 9
Generally any fills required should be placed in level lifts not exceeding ten
inches 10 in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent maximum
dry density as established by ASTM D 1557 91 Trench and structural backfills should
be placed in level lifts not exceeding eight inches 8 in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent maximum dry density per ASTM Test Method
D 1557 91 The upper two feet 2 of fills and backfills below structure subgrade level
should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent maximum dry density
Any fills to be placed on slopes having ratios steeper than five horizontal to one
vertical 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the underlying soil or basalt
formation to reduce potential instability of the new fill See Plate 3
Slope ratios of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter may be used for
the design of cut and fill slopes
Excavation
Generally if the addition is built using post and beam construction only limited
excavation for the individual footings is anticipated Generally it is anticipated that
excavation for these individual footings will be in the upper residual soils
Our field exploration indicates that the site is most likely underlain by clinker and
dense basalt at relatively shallow depths If other site excavation into the rock
formation is required it is anticipated that hard excavation and or ripping will be
encountered Generally hard excavation is considered to be using techniques such
as heavy ripping hoerams or splitting Generally the excavation method should be
left to the contractor

Retaining Walls
Retaining walls may be required for portions of the proposed addition The
guidelines for design of retaining walls are as follows
1 For lateral earth pressures assuming that granular backfill material is
used the following values may be used
Walls unrestrained at the top 35 p s f equivalent
active condition level fluid pressure
backfill per foot of depth
Walls restrained at the top 50 p s f equivalent
at rest condition fluid pressure
level backfill per foot of depth
Traffic and other surcharge loads are not included in the above recom
mended pressures
2 Allowable bearing capacities of 3 000 p s f for retaining wall foundations
bearing on stiff residual soils or clinker rock formation
3 A passive earth resistance of 300 p s f per foot of depth may be used for
the wall footing on level areas This value assumes that the foundation is
cast neat against the dug excavation or that dense granular material is
used to backfill against the foundation
The passive pressure must be reduced for walls located on slopes
4 A base friction factor of 0 4 may also be used to determine the resistance
against sliding of the wall

5 The wall foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded a
minimum of eighteen inches 18 below the lowest adjacent grade For
sloping ground the footing should extend deeper to obtain a minimum
six foot 6 set back from the outside edge of the footing measured
horizontally to the slope face
6 A subsurface drainage system subdrains and or weephole should be
utilized behind the walls to reduce the build up of hydrostatic pressure
behind the walls Subdrains should be constructed properly to drain
water away from the wall
7 Select granular backfill material such as 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation is recommended behind the retaining wall to reduce
excessive compaction required and to aid in the drainage of the area
behind the wall The granular backfill snould have a minimum horizontal
thickness of 18 inches from the back face of the wall
For the backfill of exterior retaining walls the granular backfill should
extend to a height of two feet 2 below finish grade The remaining
backfill should consist of an impervious capping of select non expansive
soil
For the backfill of interior walls the granular backfill should extend to the
slab subgrade

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by the drilling and sampling
of three 8 borings to depths ranging between 9 5 and 10 5 feet below the existing
ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 Portable
drilling equipment was utilized to drill the borings
Drilling in the upper soils was accomplished by using continuous flight augers to
advance the borings Soil samples were obtained by using drive penetration samples
driven by a 140 pound hammer free falling 30 inches The number of blows required to
drive the sampler one foot 1 or a fraction thereof are presented at the appropriate
sample depths on the Boring Logs Plates A 1 through A 3
The drive samplers used were the two inch 2 O D Standard Penetration Sampler
sampler to obtain and preserve relatively undisturbed samples for laboratory testing
Samples of the rock material encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which uses a hollow bit
to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the annulus of the bit is
mechanically recovered for examination classification and testing The recovered core
were logged by an Engineer or Geologist from our firm Descriptions of the recovered
rock cores are presented on the Boring Logs Plates A 1 through A 3
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material that is in excess of four inches 4 in length without any discontinuities
discounting any drilling induced fractures or breaks if 2 5 feet of sound material is
recovered from a five foot 5 core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 50 Generally the following is used to describe the
relative quality based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was developed to schedule appropriate tests
to aid in determining pertinent engineering properties of the soil and or rock materials
sampled
Classification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Moisture Content Determinations are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid
in determining certain properties such as relative saturation The moisture content
expressed as a percentage of the dry weight of the tested sample is shown at the
appropriate location on plates summarizing test results and at the appropriate sample
depths on the Boring Logs
Physical Property Tests
Tests are performed to develop a correlative index of certain physical parameters
of a soil These physical parameters may be used in engineering analyses pertaining to
the performance or suitability of a soil
Expansion tests are performed to evaluate the swelling potential of soils under
structural loads These tests aid in evaluating the materials for use as foundation support
and embankment The results of this are summarized on Plate B 1
