INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Kulana Hale Il Assisted Living Makai Tower project in
Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project
site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations slab on
grade and site grading only The findings and recommendations presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion
incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of Punchbowl Hill The
Punchbowl Hill Puowaina is a tuff cone near the center of Honolulu built against the end
of a spur of the Koolau Range The tuff consists mostly of brown palagonitized vitric ash
and lapilli with scattered fragments of coral limestone and Koolau basalt
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3834 00 Page 6
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations

encountered stiff to very stiff silty soils overlying medium dense to dense sandy soils at
greater depths Based on the generally dense subsurface conditions encountered at the
site we believe that the proposed 15 story residential tower structure may be supported
on a rigid mat foundation bearing on the near surface stiff to very stiff silty soils and
medium dense to dense sandy soils encountered in our borings Because of the lighter

Based on discussions with the project structural engineer we understand that the
mat foundation for the tower structure will be on the order of about 7 feet thick In

Mat Foundation for Tower Structure
Based on the generally dense subsurface conditions encountered at the project
site we recommend that the proposed 15 story residential tower structure be supported
on a rigid mat foundation bearing on the near surface stiff to very stiff sandy silts and
medium dense to dense silty sands encountered in our field exploration Because the
foundation soils may consist of cohesive soils silts and cohesionless soils sands
proper subgrade preparation of the foundation soils will be required to provide more
uniform bearing conditions for the mat foundation Subgrade preparation for the mat
foundation is discussed in the Site Grading section of this report
On the basis of our field exploration and engineering analyses an allowable
bearing pressure of up to 5 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be used for design of
the mat foundation bearing on the stiff silty and dense sandy soils This bearing value is
for dead plus live loads and may be increased by as much as 100 percent when
considering short term transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
The allowable bearing pressure provided above is a net value therefore the weight of the
mat foundation may be discounted Based on the pressuremeter test results and our
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3834 00 Page 10
analyses a modulus of subgrade reaction of 200 pounds per square inch per inch of
deflection p c i may be used in the design of the mat foundation
Lateral loads imposed on the building foundation may be resisted by frictional
resistance developed between the bottom of the mat foundation and the bearing soil and
by a combination of passive earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the
foundation system A coefficient of friction of 0 4 may be used to evaluate the sliding
resistance between the bottom of the mat foundation and the supporting subgrade
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f This assumes that the
backfill around the mat foundation is well compacted Unless covered by pavements or
slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
Resistance to uplift loads may be mobilized by the dead weight of the structure
and the mat foundation In addition the weight of the soil below the ground floor slab on
grade and above the mat foundation may be used to resist uplift loads Contribution of
dead weight from the soil above the mat foundation may be estimated using a unit weight
of 110 pounds per cubic foot p c f

Shallow Foundations for Parking Structure
We understand that lighter structural loads are anticipated for the portion of
building outside the residential tower footprint of the proposed development Therefore it _
is our opinion that a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous
wall footings bearing on the recompacted near surface soils or new engineered fills may
be used for support of the portion of building outside the tower footprint An allowable
bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be used for the
design of footings bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or new engineered fills This
bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by 50 percent for transient
loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces Construction of the structural
connection between the parking structure and the heavily loaded residential tower
building should be delayed as much as practical to accommodate the potential for
differential settlements between the two structures
Spread and or continuous footings for the parking structure base structure
outside the tower footprint should be embedded a minimum of 2 feet below the lowest
adjacent finish grades New footings to be located adjacent to the existing structures
should be lowered to the same depths as the existing building footings or deeper to avoid
surcharging the existing foundations The actual depths of the existing footings should be
confirmed in the field during construction If foundations are located next to below grade
structures utility trenches or easements the footings should extend to a depth below an
imaginary 45 degree plane projected upward from the bottom of the below grade
structure or utility This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below
grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settiement
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for
footings bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or new engineered fills as
recommended herein to be less than 1 inch total with differential settlements on the order
of inch

Slabs On Grade
The ground floor slab on grade will be supported on compacted fill needed to
backfill the space between the top of the mat foundation and the bottom of the ground
floor slab We envision that the compacted fill material will consist of imported select
granular material or the on site granular soils both of which are considered
non expansive The on site silty and clayey soils which are slightly to moderately
expansive should not be used as the backfill material Slab subgrades should be
scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3834 00 Page 13
maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
For the portion of slab on grade with moisture sensitive surfacing we
recommend that the concrete slab be supported on a minimum 4 inch layer of cushion
fill consisting of open graded gravels such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation for more uniform support of the slab and to serve as a capillary
break The cushion fill layer should be placed as soon as possible after scarification
and recompaction of the existing subgrade soils as recommended above To reduce
moisture infiltration through the slab concrete a plastic vapor membrane should be
provided below the slab A 2 inch thick moist fine sand should be placed on the plastic
vapor membrane to aid in the curing of concrete
Where the concrete slab on grade will be subjected to vehicular traffic we
recommend that the slabs be supported on a 6 inch layer of base course in lieu of the
4 inch gravel cushion layer In addition the 2 inch moist fine sand and vapor barrier
may be omitted for these slabs The base course should consist of crushed basalt
aggregate compacted to 95 percent relative compaction We recommend that the
concrete slab be designed by a structural engineer for the intended loads and should
be reinforced as a structural slab to accommodate some amount of differential
movement
For the design of structural ground floor slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of
250 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection p c i may be used for the
compacted base course A minimum slab thickness of 6 inches may be used for
preliminary design purposes Provisions should be made for proper load transfer
across the slab joints which are subjected to vehicular traffic The thickened edges of
slabs adjacent to any unpaved area should be embedded at least 12 inches below the
lowest adjacent finished grade

Drainage
A subsurface drainage system should be utilized behind the walls to reduce
potential build up of hydrostatic pressures A typical drainage system may consist

prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used
instead of the drainage material with filter fabric The prefabricated drainage
product should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of
the wall

expose the underlying firm material The resulting over excavation should be

Slabs and foundations of existing structures that are to be demolished should be
completely removed Any over excavation resulting from the demolition should be
backfilled with lean concrete or compacted granular fill material Existing utilities to
be abandoned should be removed and the resulting excavation should be
properly backfilled with select granular fill material placed in 8 inch loose level lifts

After demolition clearing and grubbing the areas within the project limits should
be over excavated where necessary to the bottom of the mat foundation level
The exposed subgrade should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture

non yielding condition
Soft and or loose areas encountered at the foundation subgrade level should be

Fills and Backfills
Based on our field exploration the excavated on site sandy soils excluding the
slightly to moderately expansive silty soils and organic materials may be re used
as a source of fill material between the top of the mat foundation and the bottom of
the ground floor slab If imported fill materials are required the imported fill and or
backfill materials should consist of non expansive materials such as crushed
coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded from
coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension The
material should have a California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 20 or higher and a
swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1883 It should also contain between 10 and 30 percent particles
passing the No 200 sieve
Excavated on site soils and or imported materials intended for fills should conform
to the above mentioned requirement and should be tested and approved by
Geolabs Hawaii prior to its intended use or delivery to the project site
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3834 00 Page 19
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by
dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Excavation
We anticipate excavations on the order of up to about 10 feet below the existing
ground surface will be required for the construction of the mat foundation for the tower
structure Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by stiff to
very stiff silty soils over medium dense to dense sands within the anticipated depths of
the excavation Therefore we believe that conventional excavation equipment may be
used for the proposed excavation Due to the limited construction space and the
relatively deep excavation anticipated we believe that a temporary shoring system may
be necessary to support the proposed excavation especially alongside the existing
buildings on the mauka and makai sides of the project site
Excavation Method
In general the method and equipment used for excavation should be determined
by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations Based on
our field exploration the in situ stiff to very stiff sandy silts and medium dense to
dense silty sands encountered in our borings should be rippable by a bulldozer or
other heavy equipment
Excavation Support
Excavation for construction of the mat foundation will need to be adequately
shored to reduce the potential for adjacent ground movement especially along the
mauka and makai sides of the subject property The excavated materials should
not be stockpiled adjacent to the planned excavation within a horizontal distance
equal to the depth of the excavation to reduce the potential for ground movement
The excavation support and shoring system used are the responsibility of the
contractor and must comply with all applicable safety requirements

For the excavation along Kalakaua Avenue ewa side Mauka Tower and along
the Diamond Head side we believe that the excavation face may be stabilized with
shotcrete or gunite to reduce the potential for sloughing or unraveling of the
exposed soils For protection of the excavation alongside the gas station building
and the low rise structures along the mauka boundary we recommend that a

Utility Trench
We envision that new utility connections such as sewer drain and water lines
may be required for the project In general a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation should be used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to
provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce compaction of the backfill thus
reducing the potential for damaging the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on site soils The backfill
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in
maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent
relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches
are below pavement areas the upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement
surface should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction

Drainage
Due to the proximity of the proposed excavation to the existing building footings
ponding of water near the existing footing level and within the mat foundation excavation
may cause saturation and subsequent loss of strength of the foundation soils below the
existing footings Therefore we recommend that appropriate measures be taken during
construction to reduce the potential for water ponding at the bottom of the mat foundation
excavation
The finished grades outside the building structure should be sloped to shed water
away from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that
gutter systems be installed around the building structure and that the discharge be
diverted away from the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering
near the foundations slabs and pavements should also be avoided Planters next to
foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water
infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls and slab footings immediately after setting
of the concrete to reduce water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon
as possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away from the slab and
foundations

APPENDIX C
_ Pressuremeter Testing
The pressuremeter test basically consists of placing an inflatable cylindrical probe
in a predrilled hole and expanding the probe while measuring the changes in volume and
pressure in the probe The purpose of pressuremeter tests is to characterize the stress
strain relationship of the in situ soil
The pressuremeter tests were performed in general conformance with Procedure
A of ASTM Test Designation D 4719 87 An NX size single cell pressuremeter
manufactured by Roctest was used in the tests The pressuremeter probe has a length to
diameter ratio of above 6 and the volume of the uninflated probe V is 75 cubic
inches in The borehole was prepared by driving a 3 inch O D split barrel sampler with
a 140 pound hammer free falling 24 inches Approximately equal pressure steps were
used to inflate the pressuremeter probe in such a way that approximately 9 to
27 increments were obtained for the test For every pressure step the probe volume
readings were taken at 30 and 60 seconds after the pressure increment was applied
The volume difference between the 30 second and 60 second readings are referred to as
the creep volume V
The test results were corrected for pressure loss due to the stiffness of the probe
membrane and hydraulic head Figure 1 depicts a typical pressuremeter test curve The
test curve is based on the 60 second volume readings The creep curve is a plot of V
versus the pressure applied to the borehole wall P The P reading is believed to be
associated with the total in situ lateral stress The creep pressure P is related to the
in situ yield stress Above P plastic deformations become prevalent The test curve
typically shows an almost straight line section within the range of low volume increase In
this range a constant soil deformation modulus called pressuremeter modulus E can
be evaluated as follows
