Reinforced Fill Slope 
The fill slopes planned at the chiller units access road between Stations 3+00 
and 4+00 may be designed with a slope inclination of up to 1H:1V provided that the fill 
materials are reinforced with adequate layers of geotextile to strengthen the fill 
materials. 
Geotextiles consist of woven fabrics that generally provides a cost-effective 
solution to slope stability problems, resolving issues such as insufficient right-of-way, 
high surcharge loads, poor quality fills, high seismic forces, steep slopes, and difficult 
landslide repairs.  The construction methodology is briefly described as follows. 
As the slope is constructed, near-horizontal layers of geotextiles are placed in the 
compacted fill at predetermined levels.  The lengths of the geotextile layers are 
designed to anchor potential failure zones into stable interior sections of the 
embankment or hillside.  As forces develop within the fill mass, the high-modulus 
geotextiles are immediately pulled into tension.  The geotextiles transfer this tensile 
force from the unstable soil back into less-stressed portions of the slope, and stability is 
thus established. 
Based on our slope stability analyses, we recommend the vertical spacing of the 
high-strength geotextile fabric (such as Mirafi HP270 or equivalent) be provided at a 
maximum of 1.5 feet with a minimum horizontal distance of 6 feet extending into the 
embankment.  A wrapped face should be provided for each geotextile layer to reduce 
sloughing of the fill materials at the slope face.  The wrapped face is typically constructed 
by turning up the geotextile at the slope face and returning the geotextile a minimum 
horizontal distance of 5 feet into the embankment below the next geotextile layer.  A 
reinforced fill slope detail is presented on Plate 3. 
To provide additional stability, we recommend constructing a keyway into the 
compacted subgrade soils at the toe of the reinforced fill slope.  The keyway should be 
seated firmly into the compacted subgrade soils.  This keyway should be a minimum of 
about 1 foot deep and a minimum of about 6 feet wide at the base of the keyway.   
In general, we recommend that the reinforced fill of the reinforced soil slope should 
consist of select granular fill material compacted to at least 90 percent of relative 
compaction with moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content.  Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557 test

Facility Access Road 
Based on the information provided, the Facility Access Road between Aliamanu 
Drive intersection and the Bivouac entrance will be reconstructed.  We understand that 
the road will be reconstructed with asphaltic concrete pavement.  Based on the 
information provided by the project civil engineer, the road will be subjected to mainly 
buses.  The average frequency of buses per day was not provided; therefore, we 
assumed about 50 buses per day in our pavement analysis.  We also assumed that the 
pavement subgrade soils would consist of new granular fills, or the existing granular fills 
encountered in our borings drilled along the Facility Access Road. 
The pavement analyses were performed in general accordance with the Unified 
Facilities Criteria (UFC) 3-250-01 (November 2016) and the Department of Defense’s 
pavement design software – Pavement-Transportation Computer Assisted Structural 
Engineering (PCASE). Based on the above discussion, we recommend the following 
flexible pavement section should be used for preliminary planning and design purposes: 
Flexible Pavement Section (Facility Access Road) 
  4.0-Inch Asphaltic Concrete  
  6.0-Inch Aggregate Base Course (CBR=80) 
10.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Subgrade 
The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction.

Temporary Construction Access Road for Water Tank 
Due to cost considerations, a hardened surface, such as asphaltic concrete or 
Portland cement concrete, is not planned for the relatively steep temporary access road.  
We understand that the contractor is considering gravel surfacing for the temporary 
access road.  We believe that a gravel-surfaced road on a relatively steep incline could 
result in rapid deterioration of the road due to heavy truck usage and concentrated 
surface water runoff.  Therefore, periodic maintenance of the temporary access road will 
be required during the life span of the temporary access road.   Maintenance could 
consist of regrading to remove potholes, ruts and/or areas eroded from surface water 
runoff.   
To reduce the frequency of road maintenance, we believe that larger sized 
aggregates, such as surge rocks (6-inch minus), should be used for the gravel-surfaced 
access road.  Based on the above discussion, we recommend the following 
gravel-surfaced pavement section should be used for preliminary planning and design 
purposes: 
Temporary Gravel-Surfaced Pavement Section 
12.0-Inch Surge Rocks (6-inch minus) 
12.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Subgrade 
The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction.  A non-woven geotextile fabric (Mirafi 180N or 
equivalent) should be provided between the pavement subgrade and the surge rocks to 
reduce migration of fines into the void spaces of the surge rock layer.  
If volcanic tuff is exposed at the pavement subgrade, the requirement of 
scarification may be omitted. Instead, the subgrade consisting of volcanic tuff should be 
proof-rolled with a minimum 10-ton vibratory drum roller or similar heavy equipment for 
a minimum of eight passes to detect the presence of near-surface soft and/or loose,
