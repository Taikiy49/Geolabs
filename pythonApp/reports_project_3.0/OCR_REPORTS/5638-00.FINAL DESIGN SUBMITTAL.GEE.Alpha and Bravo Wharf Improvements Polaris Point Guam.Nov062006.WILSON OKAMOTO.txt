SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration at the proposed new wharf extension and bulkhead alignment 
generally encountered coralline detritus deposits, consisting of loose to medium silty sands 
and gravel grading to loose clayey sands with gravel, overlying limestone formation.  The 
limestone formation was encountered between about Elevation –60 feet to -70 feet MLLW 
datum. Due to the presence of some fines content and the normally erratic nature of the 
coralline detritus deposits, the relative density of the coralline detritus deposits varies 
considerably from loose to medium dense, and up to refusal conditions in some cases. 
The new generator and fire pump building sites generally are underlain by competent 
near-surface soils consisting of medium dense to dense silty sands and gravel.  
Based on the information provided, we understand that steel sheet pile bulkheads 
augmented with king piles (single or double) with tie-rods and deadman anchors will be 
used for the wharf extension design. The area behind the new bulkhead should be 
backfilled with free-draining granular fill materials up to about 12 inches or more above the 
water level. In general, relatively clean, free-draining granular fill materials, such as surge 
rock fill or drain rock (2-inch maximum size granular materials), should be used to backfill 
the space behind the sheet pile bulkhead to about the water level or up to about 
Elevation +2 feet MLLW. The free-draining granular fill may extend to the bottom of the 
deadman anchor to provide for uniform bearing support.  In addition, a layer of non-woven 
geotextile fabric (Mirafi 180N, Pro Pex 4553, or equivalent), should be provided between 
the underlying free-draining granular fill materials and the well-graded granular backfill 
material above and also between Elevation +3 feet and -3 feet MLLW (6 feet vertical 
height) for the full length along the shoreline between the new fills and the existing on-site 
soils. 
The loose coralline detritus deposits encountered at the project site are potentially 
compressible and will consolidate under the new fill loads. Therefore, pavement 
construction and other improvements in the fill (such as underground utilities) should be 
delayed as much as practical to allow the existing ground to settle and adjust to the new fill 
loads. Based on our evaluation, we believe that a settlement waiting period on the order of 
about 6 to 8 weeks after completion of the filling operations would be sufficient to allow the 
filled ground to achieve equilibrium.

Introduction

performed for the proposed Bravo Wharf Extension located adjacent to the Alpha &

SECTION 2.0 - SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Guam is the largest and southernmost of the Mariana Island 
archipelago, which is an island arc parallel to the trend of the subduction zone forming 
the Mariana Trench. The Mariana Trench is part of a belt of trenches, which extend from 
Japan to the Palau Islands and include the Japan Trench, Mariana Trench, Yap Trench, 
and Palau Trench. 
The Island of Guam can be broadly divided into three geologic provinces:  
the southern mountains, the central mountains, and the northern plateau.  The island 
formation started during the Eocene Epoch by the eruption of basaltic rhyolite lavas.  The 
mountainous central portion of the island is composed of these Eocene volcanic rocks and 
conglomerates, tuffs, and limestones of the Eocene and Oligocene Epochs.   
Lava flows and limestone formations of Miocene Epoch make up the majority of the 
southern one-third of the island.  The plateaus of the northern portion of the island were 
formed while this part of the island was still beneath the sea level.  During the Oligocene 
and Miocene Epochs, a complex series of coral and algal reefs formed an elongated 
platform of limestones and related deposits.  Tectonic activity has lifted these coral-algal 
limestones above sea level to form the northern Guam Plateau. 
The central portion of the island consists of the transition from the volcanic 
materials of the southern mountains to the limestone formations of the northern plateau.  
The project site at the Polaris Point area is located in the south-central portion of the 
island.  The local geology is dominated by the volcanic materials and associated materials 
of the southern mountains.  Due to surface drainage courses of the southern mountain 
terrain, the near surface soils in the area consist of Holocene age alluvium and associated 
deposits, such as coralline detritus and lagoonal deposits.  These younger unconsolidated 
soil deposits are generally more susceptible to liquefaction and slope stability issues, 
especially around the shoreline areas.

Earthquakes and Seismicity

Point Fault, Santa Rita - Talofofo River Valley Fault Zone, and Tamuning - Barrigada 
Fault.  In addition to the larger faults, several faults and fault zones within proximity to

2.5 
Seismic Design Considerations 
The project site will be subjected to seismic activity and should be evaluated for 
the potential for soil liquefaction.  We understand that the design peak ground 
acceleration associated with the Level 1 (50% probability of exceedance in 50 years) 
and Level 2 (10% probability of exceedance in 50 years) design earthquakes are 0.21g 
and 0.38g, respectively.  We also understand that the probable magnitudes of the 
earthquakes associated with the Level 1 and Level 2 design earthquakes are 
Magnitude 6.0 and Magnitude 6.5 seismic events.  Based on the design peak ground 
accelerations and the design magnitude earthquakes, we conducted a liquefaction 
potential assessment of the saturated granular soils at the Bravo Wharf Extension site. 
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near 
the ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build-up of excess 
pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes.  
In this process, when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration (such as 
during an earthquake), the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an 
increase in pore water pressure.  If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to 
dissipate the build-up of pore water pressure, the effective stress (internal strength) of 
the soil is reduced.  Under sustained vibrations, the pore water pressure build-up could 
equal the overburden pressure, essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and 
causing it to behave as a viscous fluid.  During liquefaction, the soil acquires sufficient 
mobility to permit both horizontal and vertical movements, and if not confined, will result 
in significant deformations.

Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained 
sands and loose silts with little cohesion.  The major factors affecting the liquefaction 
characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows: 
1. 
Grain Size Distribution - Fine and uniform sands and silts are more 
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well-graded sands. 
2. 
Initial Relative Density - Loose sands and silts are most susceptible to 
liquefaction.  Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to relative 
density. 
3. 
Magnitude and Duration of Vibration - Liquefaction potential is directly 
proportional to the magnitude and duration of the earthquake. 
We evaluated the liquefaction potential of the saturated granular soils at the 
Bravo Wharf Extension site based on the procedures outlined by Seed and Idriss (1982) 
and Seed, Idriss and Arango (1983) and updated by the National Center for Earthquake 
Engineering Research (NCEER) in 1997.  In general, the field SPT N-values obtained 
from the five new borings (Boring Nos. 101 through 105) was normalized for the 
standard corrections, such as overburden pressure, fines content, and design 
earthquake magnitude, in the liquefaction potential assessment.  The five new borings 
were drilled utilizing approximately 4-inch diameter rotary wash methods. 
In general, the subsurface information obtained from the borings indicates that 
coralline detritus deposits and limestone formation underlie the project site.  It should be 
noted that the relative density of the coralline detritus deposits encountered at the Bravo 
Wharf Extension site is highly variable ranging from loose silty sands with gravel and 
cobbles to medium dense silty sands and gravel.  In some cases, refusal conditions 
(based on SPT sampler penetration resistance) were encountered indicating highly 
variable and erratic conditions at the project site. 
Some of the coralline detritus deposits consist of loose silty sands with some 
cohesion, which may be considered as potentially liquefiable during seismic events.

some fines content and the normally erratic nature of the coralline detritus deposits, the 
relative density of the coralline detritus deposits varies considerably from loose to 
medium dense, and even up to refusal conditions in some cases. The new generator

above. In addition, a layer of non-woven geotextile fabric also should be provided

tested and approved prior to delivery to the site to confirm that it is suitable for the 
intended purpose. 
3.4.4 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
As indicated previously, fills or backfills below the water level should consist of 
free-draining granular materials, such as the surge rock or drain rock fill, as 
recommended above. Mechanical compaction of these materials will not be 
required during placement. However, densification by pounding the material with 
the bucket of a backhoe or by other acceptable means should be done in a 
consistent manner during placement. The last lift of the material above the water 
level should be proof-rolled a minimum of 6 to 8 passes with the use of a minimum 
15-ton vibratory drum roller or similar equipment prior to the placement of the 
non-woven geotextile fabric, such as Mirafi 180N, Pro Pex 4553, or equivalent, and 
capping with the compacted granular fill to the finished subgrade. 
Granular fills and backfills above the water level should be placed in level lifts not 
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.

3.5 
Settlement Monitoring 
To allow the underlying soils to reach equilibrium under the new fill loads and to 
reduce the potential for adverse effects of post-construction settlements, the actual 
settlement rate resulting from the new fill placement should be monitored. Based on our 
experience and engineering judgment, we believe that a settlement waiting period on 
the order of about 6 to 8 weeks after completion of the filling operations to the design 
finished subgrades would be sufficient to achieve equilibrium. Construction of the 
pavements and improvements above and in the fills should not commence until the 
survey readings from the settlement waiting period indicate that the fills have achieved 
equilibrium or are near equilibrium. A proper contingency plan should be devised to 
accommodate the potential delay in construction schedule and the possible extra costs 
that may be involved, if a longer settlement waiting period is required. 
To monitor the actual settlement rate, we recommend installing a minimum of 
12 settlement gauges across the site. A typical settlement gauge detail is provided on 
Plate 9 for reference. The gauges should be read optically by a professional surveyor, 
and the readings should be transmitted to Geolabs for review in a timely manner. The 
settlement gauges should be established on the partially filled ground surface (at about 
+3 feet MLLW) prior to subsequent filling operations. A baseline elevation of the gauges 
should be established by two surveyed readings taken a minimum of 24 hours apart. 
Subsequently, readings should be taken once a week until the settlement rate has 
stabilized.

Field percolation tests were performed to evaluate the infiltration characteristics of

Pavement Joints 
Due to the rigidity of the Portland Cement Concrete (PCC) pavements, significant 
stresses may develop in the concrete from variations in temperature and moisture 
content. In order to relieve the high level of stress and to reduce the potential for 
significant cracking, adequate joints should be provided in the PCC pavement. In 
addition to the jointing requirements, the perimeter joint around the edge of the rigid 
pavements should be doweled to reduce the potential for slab separation along the joint

due to stop and go traffic. The following describes the various types of joints and 
general guidelines that should be implemented in the design of concrete pavements. 
3.9.1 Crack Control Joints (Contraction Joints) 
In general, decreases in temperature or moisture content, such as that during 
concrete curing, will cause the concrete to contract and possibly crack. Crack 
control joints are used to provide controlled cracking due to volume changes and 
relieve the stresses caused by curling or warping. Crack control joints, such as saw 
cut joints or formed grooves, are provided to a depth of generally one-fourth (¼) the 
slab thickness. These saw cut joints or formed grooves (crack control joints) 
provide a weakened plane, which likely will crack through the full depth as the 
concrete shrinks during the curing process. 
In general, the spacing of crack control joints should be limited to a maximum of 
20 feet. Smaller joint spacing should be used if the concrete pavement will be 
subjected to extreme temperature gradients during the placement and/or life span 
of the pavement. The crack control joints should be provided in both longitudinal 
and transverse directions. The transverse joint spacing should not vary from the 
longitudinal joint spacing by more than 25 percent. 
3.9.2 Expansion Joints (Isolation Joints) 
Expansion joints typically are used to relieve compressive stresses developed at 
critical locations due to fluctuations in temperature. Expansion joints are cut to the 
full depth of the concrete slab and compressible filler materials are placed in the 
cut. These joints allow for isolated lateral movement of the slabs and reduce the 
potential for damage to the adjoining concrete (spalling of the edges of the concrete 
slabs). 
Expansion joints should be used at pavement intersections and at intersections of 
pavements with fixed structures, such as the light poles, mooring bollards, etc. 
Because expansion joints generally do not provide for load transfer across the joint, 
placement of dowels with lubrication on one end of the dowel should be considered 
for load transfer across the expansion joint, where required.

3.9.3 Construction Joints 
Construction joints generally are required when two abutting slabs are placed at 
different times. Therefore, construction joints usually form the edges of each day’s 
work. For the relatively thick concrete slab (16 inches) planned for this project, the 
construction joints should consist of a keyed or butted joint. In general, construction 
joints should be aligned with the crack control or expansion joints to reduce the 
number of pavement joints required. 
3.10 
Utility Trenches 
We envision that new utility lines, such as water lines, will be required for the 
project. In general, we recommend providing granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
free-draining materials, such as bedding sand or open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, 
No. 67 gradation), below the pipes for uniform support. If soft and/or loose soils are 
encountered at the proposed pipe inverts, an additional 18 inches of open-graded 
gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) wrapped in a non-woven geotextile fabric 
(Mirafi 180N, Pro Pex 4553, or equivalent) should be provided below the bedding layer 
for uniform support. Free-draining granular materials, such as bedding sand or 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), should be used for the initial trench 
backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes or groundwater level to provide adequate 
support around the pipes and to reduce the need for substantial effort in compaction of 
the backfill, thus reducing the potential for damage to the pipes. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes 
or groundwater to the finished subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated 
on-site soils or select granular fill material. The backfill should be moisture-conditioned 
to above the optimum moisture content, placed in maximum 8-inch level loose lifts, and 
mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the 
potential for appreciable future ground subsidence. Where trenches are below 
pavement areas, the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade 
should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction.

A P P E N D I X   A 
Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
seven borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 107, extending to depths ranging 
from about 26.5 to 130 feet below the mudline or existing ground surface. The offshore 
borings (B-101 through B-103) were drilled with a skid-mounted drill rig placed on a raised 
platform equipped with rotary wash drilling equipment. The on-land borings (B-104 through 
B-107) were drilled with a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with rotary wash drilling method 
or hollow-stem augers. The seven borings were drilled at the approximate locations shown 
on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.3. 
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural 
examination in the field by a technician, who monitored the drilling operations. These field 
classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were 
classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System, as shown on 
Plate A. Graphic representations of the materials encountered are provided on the Logs of 
Borings, Plates A-1 through A-7.  Please note that the Logs of Borings are presented in 
metric. 
The majority of the soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general 
accordance with ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by 
driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound (63.5-kg) hammer 
falling 30 inches (450 mm). In addition, some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were 
obtained in general accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, 
by driving a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with the same hammer and drop. The 
blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths.

Laboratory Testing 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Three Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths, and 
graphic presentations of the test results are provided on Plate B-1. 
Twenty-three Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 & C 136) were performed on 
selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in 
soil classification. The fines content of the sample tested (percent particles passing the 
No. 200 sieve) is indicated on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.  
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plates B-2 through B-6. 
One Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression test (ASTM D 2850) was 
performed on a selected in-situ soil sample to evaluate the undrained shear strength of 
the soils.  The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressure was used as the 
applied confining pressures for the relatively “undisturbed” soil sample.  The test results 
and the stress-strain curve are presented on Plate B-7.

Field Percolation Testing 
The field percolation rates of the on-site soils at the proposed leach field site were 
estimated by performing falling-head percolation tests in excavated pits, designated as P-1 
and P-2. Field percolation tests were performed to evaluate the permeability of the 
subsurface soils for wastewater disposal. The test results are presented on Plates C-1 and 
C-2. 
The percolation tests were performed by excavating a pit of approximately 4 to 
5 feet wide to a depth of approximately 4 feet below the existing ground surface.  During 
testing, the pit was filled with water to approximately 12 inches above the bottom of the 
pit. The time intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which 
calculation of the percolation tests were based.
