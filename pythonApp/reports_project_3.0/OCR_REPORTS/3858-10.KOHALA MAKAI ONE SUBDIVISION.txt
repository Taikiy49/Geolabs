INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed 0 5 Million Gallon MG Reservoir for the Kohala Makai One Subdivision
located in the District of North Kohala on the Island of Hawaii The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical recornmendations derived from our analyses for the proposed
reservoir project These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations
retaining structures site grading and pavements only The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed reservoir project site is located mauka of the Akoni Pule Highway within
the Kohala Ranch Subdivision in North Kohala on the Island of Hawaii The general project
area is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 It is proposed to construct a 0 5 million
gallon MG reservoir tank in support of the development of the Kohala Makai One
Subdivision to be constructed on the makai side of the Akoni Pule Highway Based on the
information provided we understand that the new reservoir will be a circular tank with a
diameter of about 70 feet We understand that the proposed reservoir will be a reinforced
concrete structure with a height of approximately 20 feet The finished floor elevation for the
reservoir will be at approximately 252 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
Based on the topographic map provided the existing ground surface elevations at the
project site vary from about 246 to 255 feet MSL In general we anticipate that
construction for the proposed reservoir structure will involve site grading consisting of cuts
on the order of about 7 feet deep or less and fills up to about 6 feet thick In addition
construction will include an access road with a retaining wall of approximately 6 feet in height
An asphaltic concrete paved access road will be provided from the cul de sac to the reservoir
tank site and around the tank structure

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
GEOLABS INC
W O 3858 10 Page 4
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is situated on the southwestern flank of Kohala Mountain which is the
oldest of the five shield volcanoes forming the Island of Hawaii Kohala Mountain was built
during the Pleistocene Epoch by basaltic lava flows from the Polou and Hawi Volcanic
Series Typically the lava flows of the Polou Volcanic Series are composed of tholeitic
olivine basaltic lava flows grading to oceanite In general such flows are characterized as
thinly bedded with a high degree of porosity
The basaltic lavas in this area generally mantled by a relatively thin layer of
palagonitized ashy soils locally referred to as Pahala Ash The soil encountered at the
project site may also be a product of in situ weathering of the lavas rather than an air laid
ash In general the majority of the lava flows underlying the ashy soils at the project site are
in a a form which is characterized by a rough spiny or rubbly surface of fragmental clinkers
Typically these a a flows are underlain by a dense core of massive basalt which is
frequently interbedded with thinner less continuous clinker layers
Basalt is considered to be relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted through
the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and along
clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they serve as
the primary water transmission features

Based on our field exploration the subsurface conditions at the project site consist
generally of a surface soil layer varying from about 3 5 to 15 feet thick overlying basalt
formation extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 25 feet below the
existing ground surface Based on our evaluation of the subsurface conditions with respect
to the proposed reservoir construction the principal geotechnical engineering considerations
for the proposed reservoir project include the following
e foundation support for the reservoir structure straddling a cut and fill transition
e probing and grouting for potential cavities beneath the foundations and
e site preparation and grading
We understand that the reservoir tank structure will straddle a cut and fill transition
Based on the existing grades and the finished floor elevation of the reservoir tank structure
we envision that the reservoir foundation will be supported on about 4 feet of fill on the fill
side of the reservoir In general reservoir tank structures are located entirely in a cut
condition to avoid the cut and fill condition below the structure To provide more uniform
support of the reservoir structure straddling a cut and fill condition we recommend that the
tank structure bear entirely on structural fill of uniform thickness Based on the finished
floor elevation and the existing topography at the site we recommend that the foundations
for the reservoir tank structure bear on at least 4 feet of structural fill In addition we
recommend that the final 12 inches of fill below the tank structure consist of aggregate
base course material 1 inch minus
Based on the conditions described above we believe that a shallow foundation
system bearing on structural fill may be used to support the proposed reservoir structure

Based on the proposed finished floor elevation of the proposed reservoir we envision
that the reservoir tank structure will straddle a cut and fill transition In general reservoir tank
structures are located entirely in a cut condition to avoid the cut and fill condition below the

Foundation Probing
Based on our field exploration voids and or cavities may be present in the basaltic
lava flows encountered at the project site To reduce the potential for loss of foundation
support resulting from collapse of the cavities below foundations we recommend that a
program of cavity probing and grouting be conducted during construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that probes be drilled at each interior
foundation location and 10 foot on centers for the perimeter foundation The probe holes
should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below the
planned bottom of foundation If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during
the probing operation additional probes should be drilled at closer spacing to help delineate
the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes and cavities
discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected at low to moderate
pressures A typical sand cement grout should have a slump of about 6 to 9 inches As an
alternative controlled low strength material or flowable fill may be used in lieu of sand
cement grout for the grouting operations
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during bidding
In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and grouting
operations are completed The contractor should also be made aware that a longer lag time
between probing grouting operations and foundation construction might be required in the
construction schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs Inc This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of
cavities and to allow additional recommendations to be made if excess grout take and or
changed conditions are observed

Dynamic Lateral Earth Pressures
Since the project site is located on the seismically active Island of Hawaii dynamic
lateral earth pressures will need to be considered in the design of retaining structures
Dynamic lateral earth pressures due to seismic loading a 0 40g may be
estimated by using 10H psf rectangular pressure distribution for level backfill

appropriately reduced factor of safety may be used when dynamic lateral earth
pressures are accounted for in the design of the retaining structure
Drainage
Retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 foot wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the retaining
structures discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used
instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also be
hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the retaining structure
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone should consist of granular fill material
less than 3 inches in maximum dimension Unless covered by concrete slabs the
upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively impervious material to reduce
the potential for water infiltration behind the retaining structure

Fill and Backfill Materials
As previously indicated we recommend that the reservoir tank structure be supported
on a minimum of 4 feet of structural fill below the bottom of footing elevation to
account for the cut and fill transition In addition we recommend that the tank floor
and foundations for the proposed reservoir be supported by a minimum of 12 inches
of aggregate base course for more uniform support Fills required outside the tank
structure or below the aggregate base course layer may consist of structural fill
materials Structural fill materials should consist of well graded granular material of
6 inches in largest dimension with sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids
in the compacted mass The excavated on site materials may be re used as a source
of structural fill materials provided that they are processed to meet the above
gradation requirements
If the excavated on site materials do not contain sufficient fines to produce a well
graded material off site borrow or on site rock crushing of large sized rock fragments
or boulders should be considered to provide the required gradation and particle sizes
to develop a well graded material Imported material if necessary should be
observed and or tested by Geolabs Inc for its suitability prior to being transported to
the site

The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of well graded granular materials less
than 6 inches in maximum size The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture placed in about 8 inch loose lifts and mechanically compacted to at least
90 percent relative compaction Where trenches are located in paved areas the upper 3 feet
of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to not less than
95 percent relative compaction
For utility lines located below the footprint of the reservoir tank the utility trenches
should be backfilled with concrete or lean concrete up to the bottom of the base course
layer The base course layer recommended below the tank floor and foundations should be
placed above the concrete to provide more uniform support for the tank foundations The
concrete around the utility lines below the reservoir tank structure should be placed neat
against the excavation
Since basaltic rock formation was encountered at the surface the trench work for
utility installation will likely involve excavation in the basaltic rock formation and will require
hard ripping or hoerams Care must be exercised by the contractor to avoid over ripping
especially within the reservoir tank footprint which would disrupt the structure of the rock
formation resulting in a potential loss of bearing strength for foundations in its vicinity

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths of about 23 and 25 feet
below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The
blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained by using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
