INTRODUCTION
This report presents the findings of our injection well performance evaluation for the
Kuilima Resort Wastewater Treatment Plant in Kahuku on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The
findings and recommendations presented herein are based on observations recorded
during our site visit the performance of short term injection testing of the wells and
communication with the operators and manager of the treatment plant facility The
approximate location of the treatment plant facility and the injection well field are shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1
Our work was performed in general accordance with our fee proposal dated
May 2 2008 and the State of Hawaii Department of Health Underground Injection
Control UIC Injection Well Status Report Guidelines dated October 1999 This injection
well performance evaluation report covers the current monitoring period which extends
from June 2007 through May 2008

SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Project Considerations
The Kuilima Resort Wastewater Treatment Plant injection wells are utilized for
the disposal of secondary treated effluent generated within the Turtle Bay Resort
development The majority of the effluent treated at the plant may be used for irrigation
of the resort golf courses within the Turtle Bay Resort complex Therefore the wells are
used for the injection of treated effluent on a stand by basis during periods of time when
the effluent is not being utilized for irrigation purposes
As a condition of the Underground Injection Control UIC permit UIC Permit
No UO 1532 issued for the facility it is required that routine well monitoring be
performed by a professional geologist or engineer that is familiar with injection well
operations Geolabs Inc has prepared this injection well evaluation report to provide
the owner the operator and the regulatory agency with information regarding the
performance of the injection wells to aid in the prevention of adverse public health
effects from an injection well system malfunction or failure This evaluation is intended
to assist in ascertaining that the well system remains operational
1 2 Purpose and Scope

2 2 Well Field Description
The wells are on the easterly side of the tee boxes for the 10th Hole of Golf
Course G 2 The wells are situated in an open grass area between the tees and the
moat around Punahoolapa Marsh The topography of the well field area is generally
level with some topographic mounds and depressions associated with the adjacent golf
course terrain Based on a topographic map of the well field and vicinity ground surface
elevations range from about 5 to 6 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
The well heads are enclosed in below ground sump vaults of approximately
4 feet by 4 feet in plan dimension and about 5 feet in depth The concrete vaults are
covered with hinged steel plate doors and are approximately flush with the adjacent
grass covered ground surface
Inside the vaults the well heads consist of steel tees with flange plates bolted to
their tops for security and to allow the build up of additional head if needed These
plates were removed by plant personnel for the well inspection and performance of the
injection tests In 1994 two inch diameter inspection ports were installed in the well
covers to eliminate the need for removal of the entire top plate during future
inspection testing However to allow complete access to the well interior for the
performance of the short term injection tests and visual inspection of the exposed well
casing the top plate of each well was removed

3 1 Well Field Usage
In general only two of the three wells are placed in use for effluent disposal
when the well field system is activated The third well is off line on a rotating basis so
that at any given time only two wells are in operation Flow to an individual well is
controlled by a valve adjacent to each well head Each valve is housed in a steel cased
below ground vault of about 12 inches in diameter An upstream main control valve
diverts the effluent flow from the treatment plant to either the well field or the irrigation
pond which serves as a storage reservoir for golf course irrigation water
The following information pertaining to the effluent flow to the well field is based
on our review of the available WWTP Daily Operating Log records for the period of
May 2007 through May 2008 and personal communication with the plant manager
According to the plant operator most of the treatment plant effluent had been used for
irrigation of the resort golf courses during the past year except during periods of
elevated rainfall when the golf course irrigation needs were typically reduced According
to the WWTP personnel during the last twelve months when the well system was
activated the wells were typically operated for about eight hours per day or less
Based on our review of the Daily Operating Logs the greatest effluent flows to
the well field occurred during the months of November 2007 through February 2008
when high well field flows averaged about 4 to 15 million gallons per month The lowest
effluent flows to the well field occurred between June 2007 and October 2007 and
between March 2008 and May 2008 when well field flows ranged from zero to about
200 000 gallons per month

3 2 Injection Well Re Development
Based on information provided by the treatment plant operator the three injection
wells were aerated and pumped out re developed twice during the past 12 month
operational period The wells were aerated between November 27 and
and again between May 13 and 19 2008 in a routine effort to
maintain the disposal capacity of the system
A 130 cubic foot per minute cfm air compressor was utilized to blow out the
injection wells by lowering the air line into the bottom of the well The discharge of well
water produced by the airlift cleaning was contained within the well vault and pumped to
a tanker truck for proper disposal During the May 2008 well aeration approximately
12 000 gallons of water was pumped and hauled from the well field for proper disposal

3 5 Short Term Injection Tests
The short term injection tests were performed for each of the three wells by
diverting the available effluent into each well separately Each well was isolated from
the others for the duration of each test by adjusting the valves controlling the effluent
flow to individual wells The following is a generalized summary of the injection testing
procedure
W O INC Page 14
Hawaii California
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND FINDINGS
Prior to the injection testing all three wells were isolated off line The individual
tests were performed by opening only the control valve for the well being tested The
valve was opened slowly while observing the head level in the well The control valve
was slowly opened until the head level rose to near the top of the well at which time
opening of the valve was discontinued to avert a well overflow The injection well control
valve was not adjusted for the duration of the injection testing The short term injection
testing of each well averaged about 40 minutes
During injection testing the head level response was continually measured and
recorded using an electronic piezometer pressure transducer and data logging
recorder linked to a portable computer Head level readings were also recorded at
selected intervals by manual measurement and recording for verification Thus the
head level response could be monitored continuously to record the fluctuation of head
level within the wells Manual readings collected from the totalizing flow meter were
recorded during the short term injection test to obtain the total quantity of injected
effluent and to estimate the average rate of flow into each well The duration of the
short term injection tests ranged between about 40 and 42 minutes The average
incoming flow rate to the well field from the WWTP was calculated to range between
about 560 gallons per minute and 722 gallons per minute during the injection testing
According to the plant personnel the incoming flow rate to the well field is mainly
controlled by the volume of effluent flow passing through the WWTP sand filtration
system The flow rate normally fluctuates as the various sand filters operate in a
switching cycle

Based on our observation of the injection well system operation it is believed that
as the individual injection well is opened or closed some delay and surge of flow is
encountered which affects the short term head level response In addition the
effluent flow from the WWTP typically fluctuates as the sand filters operate on a
cyclic basis Based on our observations the average incoming flow rate from the
WWTP during the current injection testing was about 500 to 800 gallons per minute
which was greater than the available flow from the plant during last years injection
testing In addition the short term injection testing procedure was changed from the
previous testing procedure to permit a more direct estimation of the individual well
disposal capacity The procedure change involved opening the individual well
control valve enough to increase and maintain the head level to near the top of the
well for the test duration As a result a better estimation of the well disposal
capacity was achieved
Based on our observations of the static water level in the wells measured with the
well closed and the results of our short term injection tests we conclude that the
disposal capacity of Well and 3 has increased relative to the prior well
performance evaluation
We believe that well redevelopment and cleaning efforts have a significant positive
impact on maintaining the performance of the injection wells Therefore we
recommend conducting well re development cleaning more than once per year in
an effort to maintain satisfactory disposal capacity Based on the current injection
testing results Well No 2 appears to have the greatest disposal capacity followed
by Well No 1 and finally Well No 3

3 6 Recommendations
Based on the short term injection test results and the available well usage data
we believe the disposal capacity of the three injection wells was maintained and may
have increased slightly relative to the previous well performance evaluation conducted
in May 2007 To maintain the disposal capacity of the well system we recommend well
re development and cleaning twice per year continued well operation rotation and well
field usage at least once per week to reduce possible bio fouling It is our opinion that
the wells should be activated at least weekly with a minimum average monthly well field
flow of about 200 000 gallons or greater to maintain good operational capacity of the
well field system
Injection wells sustain losses in capacity over time due to natural biological and
chemical fouling processes or other introduced factors such as suspended solids or debris
contained in the effluent In addition extended periods of well inactivity and the
introduction of poor quality effluent containing excessive suspended solids may cause
acceleration of the normal losses in capacity It should be noted that some loss in capacity
may be permanent and irreversible however it has also been proven that redevelopment
W O INC Page 21
Hawaii California
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND FINDINGS
of injection wells can aid in the recovery of lost capacity provided that the proper methods
are utilized on a regular basis
Based on our injection well evaluation we recommend that the owner operator
continue to operate the injection wells on a routine rotating basis in an attempt to maintain
the disposal capacity of the system In addition we recommend that the owner operator
closely monitor and document the operation of each of the wells to assure that overflows
or back ups related to a well failure are detected prior to their occurrence In the event an
overflow of a well or other failure occurs and the situation cannot be remedied the
owner operator should promptly notify the Department of Health Safe Drinking Water
Branch
We believe that by maintaining the use of the wells on a rotating basis especially
during periods of low effluent availability the current operational performance of the wells
could be maintained Keeping the wells operational is not normally a problem however
we believe it is crucial to maintain rotating operation of all the wells during the periods of
time when low effluent flow are experienced We suggest that the actual well usage
rotation be recorded in the Daily Operating Logs to show the pattern of individual well
usage
We recommend that the owner operator continue recording field observations such
as the following 1 notes regarding the overall system operation 2 documentation of well
maintenance performed including well cleaning and replacement of faulty parts and
instruments 3 observation of the head levels within the wells prior to and during injection
events 4 daily flow volumes 5 a log of individual well usage and 6 notes pertaining to
the increase or decrease of the total flow to the plant for treatment This information will be
used to evaluate the future performance of the injection wells In addition the results of our
short term injection testing of the wells may be used to evaluate the operation of the wells
with respect to prior well inspection data
Generally redevelopment should be considered if head levels continue to increase
over time or when well failures such as overflow or system back ups may be anticipated
However when the wells begin to fail it may often be too late to restore the capacity

Therefore based on the effectiveness of the current well redevelopment schedule we
believe it may be prudent to continue well cleaning at biannual intervals
Well redevelopment should be performed by a professional experienced with
coastal injection well construction and rehabilitation The selected redevelopment method
should be carefully evaluated based on the existing well design proper containment and
disposal of wastewater generated by the activity and other factors specific to the site and
operation of the wells
Based on our observations the valves that control the flow to each injection well
appear to be working satisfactorily however the valves are not visible due to their
submergence below water Therefore the valve cannot be visually inspected In
addition the long term and continual submergence of the valve may accelerate
corrosion of the hardware We do not believe that this is a critical concern however
consideration may be given to reconstructing the valve vault so that it is sealed from the
infiltration of groundwater to facilitate inspection for signs of wear
It should be noted that a possible leak was detected at the control valve for
Well No 3 We observed strong bubbling activity indicative of the introduction of some
effluent and or air within Well No 3 despite the well valve being fully shut We also
observed air bubbling activity within the Well No 3 control valve vault We recommend
that the Well No 3 control valve be checked for possible leakage and repaired if
necessary
In consideration of other possible long term improvements that could facilitate
monitoring of the injection well performance it is suggested that individual flow metering
devices be installed between each well control valve and well head The installation of
additional flow meters would provide direct readout of flow data for each individual well
which would improve the direct correlation between the flow rate and the head level
response in the well As a result changes in the well flow rate could be monitored and
adjustments to the control valve could be made to achieve the desired flow rates that
can be compared from year to year Currently only the total flow to the well field is
known therefore when more than one well is operating simultaneously it is not possible
