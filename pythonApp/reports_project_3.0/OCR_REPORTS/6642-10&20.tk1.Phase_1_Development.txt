Subsurface Conditions 
Our field exploration program conducted to date consisted of drilling and 
sampling 18 test borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1, 3, 4, 6, 7, 9, 11, 13, 17, 19 
through 26, and 28, extending to depths ranging from about 9 to 21.4 feet below the 
existing ground surface.  In general, our field exploration indicates that the project site is 
generally underlain by residual soils and saprolite, consisting of very stiff to hard silty 
clays and clayey silts, extending to depths of about 10 to 17 feet below the existing 
ground surface.  Soft to hard basalt rock formation was encountered in some of the 
drilled borings at depths ranging between about 10 and 21.4 feet below the existing 
ground surface, the maximum depth explored. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field 
exploration.  It should be noted that groundwater levels can significantly vary depending 
on rainfall, temperature, surface runoff, groundwater withdrawal, and other factors. 
Seismic Design Considerations 
In general, earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic 
plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic activity; 
therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during volcanic 
eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground movement of magma 
that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The Island of Hawaii experiences 
thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so small that they can only be detected 
by sensitive instruments. However, some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, 
and a few cause minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of magma 
are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the Island of 
Hawaii. Because majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over 90 percent) are related to 
volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes with 
increased distance from the Island of Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has experienced 
numerous earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not 
confined only to the Island of Hawaii. 
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes 
greater than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have occurred in 
the County of Maui. The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Islands of Hawaii and 
Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu. For example, small landslides occurred on the

Preliminary Geotechnical Engineering Recommendations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site and the 
anticipated finished grades for the proposed development, we believe that shallow 
foundations consisting of spread and/or continuous footings bearing on the 
recompacted on-site soils and/or new compacted fills needed to achieve the design 
finished grades may be used for support of the proposed new buildings.  Geotechnical 
engineering recommendations for the new housing development will be provided after 
completion of the remaining field exploration work.  
Based on our laboratory test results, the surface clayey soils exhibit low to 
moderate shrink/swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture 
contents.  Therefore, we recommend providing a minimum 12 inches of non-expansive, 
select granular fill materials below the building slabs due to the expansive nature of the 
on-site clayey soils. 
Observations during our field exploration indicate that some shrinkage cracks 
exist at the existing ground surface across the project site. If left unattended, the 
existing shrinkage cracks may increase further in size under prolonged dry weather 
conditions.  Shrinkage cracks adjacent to the building foundations are potentially 
detrimental because water can readily infiltrate through the crack openings and soften 
the subsurface soils around the building foundations, thus affecting their performance. 
As a result, special attention should be given to the recommended site 
preparation procedures prior to the fill placement operation.  In general, we recommend 
proof-rolling the exposed subgrades (after clearing and grubbing) with a minimum 
10-ton vibratory drum roller a minimum of six passes to assist in detecting and 
collapsing near-surface voids and loose zones created by the shrinkage cracks.  After 
the proof-rolling operations, the proof-rolled subgrades should be scarified,

below the lowest adjacent finished grades.  Footings constructed near tops of slopes or on 
sloping ground conditions should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum 
horizontal setback distance of 6 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the 
face of the slope.  Footings located adjacent to planned (or existing) retaining walls should 
be embedded deep enough to avoid surcharging the retaining wall foundations, or the 
planned retaining walls should be designed to resist the additional structural loads. 
If foundations are next to utility trenches or easements, the footings should be 
embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of 
the utility trench or the footing should be embedded to a depth as deep as the inverts of 
the utility lines.  This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade 
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable 
foundation settlement. 
If structure foundations are designed and constructed in strict accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate total settlements of footings supported on the 
recompacted on-site soils and/or new compacted fills should be on the order of about 
1 inch or less.  We estimate that differential settlements between adjacent footings 
supported on similar materials to be on the order of about 0.5 inches. 
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction developed

Slabs-On-Grade 
A layer of low to moderately expansive clayey soils blankets the project site at the 
existing ground surface.  Unless slabs-on-grade constructed above these expansive soils 
are properly designed, there is a potential for future distress to the lightly loaded 
slabs-on-grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of the clayey soils due to changes in 
the moisture content.  To reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from swelling 
of the subgrade soils, we recommend properly preparing the subgrade soils prior to fill 
placement.  In addition, we recommend providing a minimum 12 inches of non-expansive, 
select granular fill materials below the building slab areas.  The non-expansive, select 
granular fill also should extend beyond the perimeter of the building structures a minimum 
of 24 inches (or extend to the edge of the concrete sidewalk surrounding the building, if 
applicable). 
For interior building slabs (not subjected to vehicular traffic or machinery vibration), 
we recommend placing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No. 3 Fine 
gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) below the slabs.  The open-graded gravel cushion 
fill would provide uniform support of the slabs and would serve as a capillary moisture 
break.  To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration through the slab and 
subsequent damage to floor coverings, an impervious moisture barrier, such as a plastic 
membrane, is recommended on top of the No. 3 Fine gravel cushion fill layer. Flexible floor 
coverings, such as carpet or sheet vinyl, should be considered because they can better 
mask minor slab cracking.  In addition, we recommend designing interior walls to 
incorporate some flexibility in accommodating a small amount of possible ground 
movements. 
Where the slabs will be subject to vehicular traffic or machinery vibration, we 
recommend providing a 6-inch layer of aggregate subbase below the slabs in lieu of the 
4-inch thick gravel cushion fill layer. The aggregate subbase should consist of crushed 
basaltic aggregates compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
For the design of slabs supported on aggregate subbase, a modulus of subgrade 
reaction of about 250 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection (pci) may be used for 
the compacted aggregate subbase.  Where slabs are intended to function as rigid 
pavements for trucks, a minimum slab thickness of 6 inches may be used for preliminary 
design purposes.  Provisions should be made for proper load transfer across the slab 
joints that will be subject to vehicular traffic.  The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to 
unpaved areas should be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade.

Our field observations indicate that some shrinkage cracks are present at the 
existing ground surface across the project site.  In general, shrinkage cracks will 
develop in the ground due to the lack of maintenance of the soil moisture content 
as the surface soils start to dry out under the sun and dry weather conditions.  If 
the existing ground cracks are left unattended for an extended period of time and 
are allowed to penetrate deeper below the surface, the ground cracks could 
adversely affect the performance of the future building foundations.  The ground 
cracks could serve as conduits for water to percolate into the foundation soils 
and cause loss of strength of the foundation soils resulting in potential settlement 
of the building structure.  In addition, migration of the foundation soils into the 
openings of the shrinkage cracks could undermine the foundation system and 
result in potential settlement of the building structure.  Therefore, remedial work 
to correct or mitigate the observed ground cracks should be implemented prior to 
fill placement and construction of the proposed structures. 
After clearing and grubbing, the exposed subgrades should be proof-rolled with a 
minimum 10-ton vibratory drum roller a minimum of six passes to assist in 
detecting and collapsing near-surface voids and loose zones created by the 
shrinkage cracks.  The exposed subgrades should be proof-rolled without 
moisture-conditioning the subgrade soils (at the pre-existing moisture contents).  
Loose areas disclosed during the proof-rolling operation should be backfilled with 
general fill material compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  
The proof-rolling operations should be conducted under the near-continuous 
observation of a Geolabs representative. 
Subsequent to the proof-rolling operations, the subgrades should then be 
scarified to a minimum depth of 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 
2 percent above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction.  Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry 
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the 
same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the 
water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry 
density. 
Where shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade, we 
recommend that the subgrade soil be prepared again as recommended above.  
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement 
weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and 
replacement with well-compacted fill.  The need for over-excavation due to soft 
subgrade soil conditions should be evaluated in the field by a Geolabs 
representative.

degree of compaction.  We recommend that these earth berms be removed to 
expose the underlying firm ground. 
The bottom of the drainage ditches and earth berms should be scarified to a 
minimum depth of 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above 
the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction. Backfill materials for the drainage ditches should be placed 
in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to 
about 2 percent above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to at least 
90 percent relative compaction. 
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
General fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above 
the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content 
(percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
The non-expansive, select granular fill materials recommended below the 
building and sidewalk slabs should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture, placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to at least 95 and 90 percent relative compaction, respectively.  
Aggregate base course and subbase materials should be moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches 
in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction. 
Our field observations indicate that the terrain at the project site is gently sloping 
from the north towards the south. New fills placed against the sloping ground 
should be benched into the existing slope to provide a well-compacted and stable 
mass.  The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in 
level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement 
requirements.  Where new fill slopes will be required for this project, the fill 
slopes may be designed using a slope inclination of two horizontal to one vertical 
(2H:1V) or flatter.

Excavation 
It is anticipated that the on-site soils may be excavated with normal heavy 
excavation equipment. However, areas of hard excavation may be encountered 
where hard basalt rock formation occurs at shallow depths or in deep utility 
trenches and may require chipping and/or the use of hoerams. If excavation into 
the underlying rock formation is required for site grading, the work will generate 
boulders and over-sized material. The boulders and over-sized material may be 
incorporated into deeper fill areas as discussed in the “Boulder Disposal” 
subsection herein. Otherwise, crushing the boulders for general fill and roadway 
subbase is recommended, or hauling boulders off-site may be necessary.  
Boulder Disposal 
Our field exploration indicated that some boulders and rock outcrops could be 
observed at or near the existing ground surface in localized areas across the 
project site.  In addition, over-sized materials, such as cobbles greater than 
6 inches in dimension and boulders, i.e., rock fragments larger than 12 inches in 
largest dimension, are anticipated from cuts into the dense basalt rock formation.  
Over-sized materials, such as cobbles greater than 6 inches in dimension and 
boulders less than 5 feet in largest dimension, may be used in general fills below 
3 feet from the finished subgrades provided that the following recommendations 
are followed: 
Boulders less than 5 feet in largest dimension may be utilized as general 
fills, provided that the top of the boulder is placed at least 3 feet below the 
finish subgrades.  Boulders or portions of boulders should not encroach 
within 3 feet of the finish subgrade elevation.  Boulders larger than 5 feet in 
maximum dimension should not be used as general fill unless reduced in 
size. 
Boulders should be spread out at the bottom of deeper fills and must not be

Off-Site Drain Line 
The following guidelines and recommendations may be used for the installation 
of the off-site drain line extending from the new on-site detention basin to the new outlet 
headwall planned on the south side of Halewili Road. In general, good construction 
practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling of the trenches for the new 
utilities. Based on the results of our field exploration, it appears that conventional 
excavation methods may generally be utilized for utility line excavation. The contractor 
should determine the method and equipment for trench excavation subject to practical 
limits and safety considerations. In addition, the excavations should comply with the 
applicable federal, state, and local safety requirements. The contractor should be 
responsible for trench shoring design and installation. 
In general, we recommend providing granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) under the pipes for uniform support. 
Free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation), should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above 
the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes. It is critical to use this 
free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches 
of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench 
backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes 
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of compacted on-site soils (with 
a maximum particle size of 6 inches) or select granular fill material. The backfill should 
be placed in maximum 8-inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to no less 
than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future 
ground subsidence. Where trenches are below pavement areas, the upper 3 feet of the 
trench backfill below the pavement finished grade should be compacted to a minimum 
of 95 percent relative compaction. 
Because basaltic rock formation was encountered at relatively shallow depths 
during our field exploration, it appears that some of the trench work for the drain line 
installation may involve excavation in the basaltic rock formation and will require hard 
ripping or the use of hoerams. The contractor must exercise care to avoid over-ripping, 
which would disrupt the structure of the rock formation and result in a potential loss of 
bearing strength for footings in its vicinity.
