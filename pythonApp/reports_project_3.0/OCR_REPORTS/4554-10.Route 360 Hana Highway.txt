2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes the older West Maui
Volcano Tertiary Epoch and the more recent East Maui Volcano also known as
Haleakala Volcano Pleistocene Epoch The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow gently
sloping plain located between these two volcanoes The Isthmus of Maui was created
by lava flows from Haleakala Volcano ponding against the West Maui Volcano and is
comprised of alluvium washed from the slopes of West Maui and East Maui Haleakala
Volcanoes The erosional processes are dominated by the detachment of soil and rock
masses from the mountain walls and the soil materials are transported downslope
toward the isthmus primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these materials reach the
stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become dominant and the
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that large
volumes of water are transmitted for very short durations Because of this the transport
of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load consists of a
poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When the
erosional base levels change these sediment loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes
begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the
materials Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the
coarse grained portion of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or
conglomerate Simultaneously erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors
and upstream in headwaters This continued erosion generates material which is
transported downslope covering the older alluvial soil deposits Depending on the local
base level and rate of transport these newer sediments are generally transient in terms
of geologic time In addition their consistency and density are generally less than those
of the older partially consolidated deposits

Underlying the alluvial soil deposits are overlapping lavas from the West Maui
and East Maui Volcanoes The project site is located on the northern flank of the East

the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows of

the lava changed to very hard thickly bedded flows of andesitic composition These
lavas have been grouped as the Kula Volcanic Series Typically the basalt rock

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by shifts
in the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before
or during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the

underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not
erupt The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but
most of the earthquakes are so small that they may only be detected by sensitive
instruments However some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and
a few cause minor to moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii
over 90 percent of earthquakes are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic
activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the
Island of Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5 M5 however earthquakes are not confined only to the
Island of Hawaii
To a lesser degree the Island of Maui has experienced numerous earthquakes
greater than M5 Therefore moderate to strong earthquakes also have occurred
in the County of Maui For example two substantial earthquakes have been
recorded in the County of Maui including the Maui Earthquake of and
the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 M7

Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations
In general the performance of drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the
contractor s method of installation and construction procedures The load bearing
capacities of drilled shafts depend on the frictional resistance between the shaft
and the surrounding soil Therefore the contractor should exercise care in drilling
the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes
The subsurface conditions at the project site generally consisted of saprolite and
weathered basalt formation The degree of weathering in the basalt formation is
likely to vary significantly by nature with localized hard layers and relatively
unweathered zones Therefore some difficult drilling conditions may be
encountered in these subsurface conditions Caving in of the materials may occur
during drilling operations due to seepage water which has been observed along the
faces of the mauka cut slopes To reduce the potential for significant caving in of
the holes temporary casing of the drilled holes may be necessary

As mentioned previously groundwater was not encountered in the borings drilled in
the areas requiring drilled shaft foundation construction However it should be
noted that the groundwater levels likely will change due to seasonal precipitation
surface water runoff and other factors Due to the generally wet conditions
observed at the site seepage water should be expected and should be anticipated
at the site during the drilled shaft installation The presence of groundwater in the
drilled shaft holes will require placement of concrete by tremie methods Therefore
a representative from Geolabs should be present at the site to observe the drilling
and installation of drilled shafts during construction
A low shrink high slump concrete mix 7 to 9 inch slump in wet holes and 6 to
8 inch slump in dry holes should be used to provide close contact between the
drilled shafts and surrounding materials The concrete should be placed promptly
after the completion of drilling within 48 hours to reduce the potential for caving in
of the drilled holes The concrete also should be placed in a suitable manner to
reduce the potential for segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix As
mentioned previously placement of concrete by tremie methods should be
considered because groundwater seepage water may be encountered in the
drilled holes During concrete placement a minimum of 5 feet of concrete head
should be maintained above the bottom of the tremie pipe at all times

3 3 Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography we anticipate that the earthwork for the
proposed project may consist of excavation and backfilling of about 6 feet or less
relative to the existing ground surface in order to construct the drilled shaft caps Based
on the generally limited earthwork anticipated for this project the following general
guidelines may be used for specifying the earthwork requirements for the project
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared Vegetation debris rubbish concrete slabs asphaltic concrete
pavements and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly
off site or in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the
excavated materials The resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with
general fill materials The general fill materials should be moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture content placed in 8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
In general the excavated on site soils may be re used as a source of general fill
materials provided that the materials are free of vegetation deleterious materials and
clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension However
it should be noted that the on site materials may be wet due to the high rainfall
environment of the project site In addition the excavated materials likely will require
aeration to achieve the proper moisture contents in order to allow the materials to be
compacted to the specified compaction requirements

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths of about 60 5 and
70 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted
drill rig equipped with rotary coring tools The approximate locations of the borings drilled
are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the site were obtained by
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material
left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and
description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD also is a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core
run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
