In accordance with your request we have performed a preliminary review of
available in house soils information pertaining to the Kapolei Makai Alternative Site for the
proposed University of Hawaii UH West Oahu Campus project located in the District of
Ewa on the Island of Oahu Hawaii This letter report summarizes our general assessment
of the geotechnical aspects for the proposed project site The findings and preliminary
recommendations provided are intended for planning and site selection input only and are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
We understand that the Site Selection Study for the proposed UH West Oahu
Campus is presently underway Furthermore we understand that it is desired to obtain
some general information pertaining to the anticipated soil conditions and possible
geotechnical engineering issues that may impact the development of the Kapolei Makai
site We understand that the information may be used in the site selection process and for
preliminary cost estimation for development of the site
The Kapolei Makai project site encompasses approximately 500 acres of
undeveloped land located in Kapolei in the District of Ewa on the Island of Oahu Hawaii
The 500 acre site is bounded by the existing Kapolei Golf Course toward the west
Farrington Highway toward the north and the proposed Nerin Scuth Road alignment
located toward the east through south of the project site We understand that initially only
320 acres located at the northern half of the 500 acre site may be developed for the future

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The Kapolei Makai property exists as undeveloped land that is vegetated with
grasses and scattered shrubs which cover the ground surfaces from exposure The
Kapolei Makai property consists of approximately 500 acres of former agricultural land
located on the Ewa Plain Trees are generally absent except where located along the
banks of the existing drainage gulches that traverse the site The existing ground surface
elevations range from about 150 feet Mean Sea Level MSL at the northeast corner of
the site located adjacent to Farrington Highway to about 60 feet MSL at the southern
portion of the site bordering the proposed North South Road alignment The natural
terrain slopes very gently down toward the south and southwest between approximately
0 7 percent to 1 9 percent slope gradient The steeper terrain is located at the northern
portion of the site

The site was utilized for extensive cultivation of sugar cane therefore some
remnants of agricultural use such as dirt roads ditches flumes and pipelines remain

gulches which are identified as the Kaloi and Hunehune Gulches The gulches range
between about 5 to 15 feet in depth and dissect the brownish colored silty clay soils that
predominantly compose the near surface alluvial soils of the site
ANTICIPATED SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Based on a review of available soils information and our experience in the area the
project site is underlain by extensive deposits of alluvial soils generally consisting of brown
and reddish brown colored stiff to very stiff silty clays The alluvial soils are derived from
the erosion of the adjacent highlands and deposition of the materials on the low
topographic gradient of the Ewa Plain region The clayey alluvial soils have been tested
previously and found to be moderately to highly expansive when subjected to changes in
the soil moisture content
Within the southern portion of the Kapolei Makai property coralline deposits have
been encountered underlying the near surface alluvial soils The underlying coralline
materials are not exposed at the ground surface within the project area however the
deposits may be encountered at depths of about 20 feet below the ground surface at the
southern portion of the property The regional groundwater surface is anticipated to exist
at depths corresponding to approximately mean sea level
DISCUSSION
Based on our preliminary assessment of the Kapolei Makai property we do not
believe that there are significant geologic and or geotechnical constraints present which
would preclude the development of the proposed project Based on experience in the
area we believe that the primary geotechnical considerations for the development of this
site appear to be site drainage the occurrence of abandoned agricultural infrastructure
and possible backfilled undocumented ditches or reservoirs associated with past use of
the land and expansive soil conditions
Based on our review of the regional site drainage and surface soil conditions
occasional large volumes of surface water runoff are believed to pass through the site
during storm events via the Kaloi and Hunehune Gulches The primary stream flow
originates from the mountainous area located on the mauka side of the Interstate Route
H 1 Highway The flow is then transported via the gulches through the project site to
discharge in off site retention basins or at the shoreline The surface water runoff is
believed to increase rapidly and in substantial volumes as evidenced by the large basalt
rock boulders deposited in the upper reaches of the gulches and the scour observed at the
existing bridge and gulch banks In between higher rainfall events the gulches are
believed to be dry

The site had been used for the extensive cultivation of sugar cane As a result the
site drainage may have been artificially altered by the diversion of naturally occurring
surface water runoff thus inhibiting the natural development of surface drainage courses
at the site In addition the runoff entering the property may have been diverted to
reservoirs for on site storage thus reducing the total flow which may have otherwise
traversed the project site Furthermore the clayey soils of the site have generally low
permeability which may result in lower surface infiltration rates For these reasons we
believe that site drainage should be planned to allow for possible increase in surface water
runoff derived from both on site and off site localities Therefore the project civil engineer
should be consulted for planning and design of these site drainage issues
As previously noted the site was utilized for large scale sugar cane cultivation
Therefore abandoned agricultural equipment and structures such as buried pipes surface
flumes earthen reservoirs and ditches pumps and other structures may be encountered
at the site In addition soft soil conditions related to the possible historic use of earthen
reservoirs and irrigation ditches may be encountered at the site These features may be
observed at the ground surface or may be buried as undocumented fills
It should be assumed that the soils within the upper 3 feet of the ground surface
have been disturbed from past agricultural use of the site and will need to be properly
engineered for support of new structures planned at the site Therefore special attention
should be given to the site preparation requirements based on the grading of the site cuts
and fills to reduce the impact of these features on the future development of the campus
Depending on the cuts and fills at the site site preparation consisting of over excavation
and replacement of the excavated material with compacted fill may be required to reduce
the impact of these features on the future building structures
Based on a review of available soils data from the project area vicinity and our
observations of the site we anticipate that the site to be underlain by clayey soils that
exhibit moderate to high shrink swell potential upon wetting and drying of the soil
Therefore it is important that a geotechnical field exploration be conducted to aid in
evaluating the extent and degree of the expansive soil conditions at the site In addition
the on site clayey soils are believed to possess low CBR values Therefore the building
foundations and pavement subgrades will need to be over excavated to remove the
potentially unsuitable soils and be replaced with imported select granular fills It should be
noted that the select granular fills below the future building foundations and pavements will
need to be imported from off site sources
Our field observations indicated that substantial shrinkage cracks are present at the
existing ground surface across the project site In general shrinkage cracks develop in
the ground due to the lack of maintenance of the soil moisture content as the soils start to
dry out under the sun and dry weather conditions The development of shrinkage cracks
in the ground is intensified by the prolonged dry climatic conditions in the Kapolei area
resulting in large fluctuations of the soil moisture content

It should be noted that if shrinkage cracks are left unattended for an extended
period of time and are allowed to penetrate deeper below the surface after
development of the campus the ground cracks could adversely affect the performance
of the future building foundations The ground cracks could serve as conduits for water
to percolate into the foundation soils and cause loss of strength of the foundation soils
resulting in potential settlement of the structures In addition migration of the
foundation soils into the openings of the shrinkage cracks could undermine the
foundation system and result in potential settlement of the structures Therefore
remedial work to correct or mitigate the shrinkage cracks should be implemented as
part of the campus development
Because the development of shrinkage cracks is largely due to fluctuations in the
moisture content of the on site expansive soils we believe that efforts should be directed
towards maintaining the soil moisture content at a relatively constant level The
phenomenon of shrinkage crack development will recur depending on climatic conditions
unless measures are taken to maintain the soil moisture content relatively constant
thereby reducing the shrink swell behavior of the on site clayey soils due to moisture
content fluctuations Some of the long term solutions may include installation of concrete
walkways or aprons around the structures placement of non expansive fills in the yard
areas and or provision of landscaping along with an irrigation system to maintain the
moisture contents of the near surface soils in a relatively narrow range
In summary we believe that the primary geotechnical considerations for the
development of this site appear to be site drainage the occurrence of abandoned
agricultural infrastructure and possible backfilled undocumented ditches or reservoirs
associated with past use of the land and expansive soil conditions Due to the relatively
dry climatic conditions of the Ewa Plain we wish to emphasize the importance of proper
site drainage and landscaping along with a permanent irrigation system to assist in
controlling the expansive soil conditions and to reduce the potential for significant erosion
of the soil materials at the site
LIMITATIONS
The preliminary findings and recommendations submitted in this letter report are
based in part upon information obtained from literature review and site observations only
A subsurface exploration program consisting of drilling borings and sampling of soils was
not conducted for this preliminary assessment Therefore variations in subsurface
conditions will likely occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not become
evident until additional geotechnical exploration is underway If variations then appear
evident it will be necessary to re evaluate the preliminary findings and recommendations
provided in this letter report
