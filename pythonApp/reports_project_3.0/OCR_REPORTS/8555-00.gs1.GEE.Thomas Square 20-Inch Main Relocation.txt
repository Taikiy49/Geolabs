SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

tuff or fill consisting of medium stiff to stiff sandy silt and asphaltic concrete (AC) and

depths of about 10 to 11 feet. Coral limestone formation was encountered in three of the 
borings at depths of about 10 to 11 feet, extending the maximum depth explored of about 
11.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Our boring performed on South King Street 
encountered a pavement structure consisting of about 8 inches of AC pavement underlain 
by about 13 inches of aggregate base course. Our boring performed on Victoria Street 
encountered a pavement structure consisting of about 5 inches of AC underlain by about 
6 inches of aggregate base course. We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled 
borings at the time of our field exploration.  
A utility plan was not available at the time this report was prepared. Based on the 
information provided, we anticipate that the new waterline pipe inverts will be about 4 feet 
below the existing ground surface. Installation of the proposed water mains will involve 
excavation into the underlying fill, weathered volcanic tuff, and cinder. In general, we 
anticipate that the in-situ soils along the proposed water main alignments may be 
excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment. As mentioned above, our field

project alignment. Therefore, some trench excavations may extend into the coral 
limestone formation and may require chipping and/or the use of hoerams to remove the 
materials and achieve the design invert elevations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Thomas Square: 20-Inch Main Relocation project in Honolulu on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering 
analyses for the project. These recommendations are intended for the design of trench 
excavation, backfilling, drainage structures, thrust block/vault design, site grading, 
pavement restoration, and corrosion potential only. The findings and recommendations 
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located near Thomas Square in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii. The Thomas Square: 20-Inch Main Relocation is part of the Metro 180’ System 
and consists of installing 1,500 linear feet of waterline replacement with pipe diameters 
of 6 and 20 inches along the following roadways: South Hotel Street, Ward Avenue, South 
King Street, Victoria Street, and within Thomas Square Park.  
Site grading information was not available at the time this report was prepared. 
Based on the information provided, we anticipate that the new waterline pipe inverts will 
be about 4 feet below the existing ground surface. Therefore, we anticipate that site 
grading may consist of trench excavations about 5 feet deep and restoring the trench to 
the existing ground surface elevations.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. 
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age 
and forms majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. As volcanic activity in the 
Waianae Volcano ceased, lava flows from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded 
eastern slope forming a broad plateau known as the Schofield Plateau.  
The project site is located on the southern flank of the Koolau Volcano and on the 
coastal plain of southern Oahu. Therefore, the geomorphology and subsurface conditions 
in the area are directly related to the glacio-eustatic fluctuations of the sea level during 
the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain. The coastal plain 
was built on the eroded flanks of the Koolau Volcano. The coastal plain was built by 
extensive accumulation of alluvium derived from erosion of the volcano, interbedded with 
coral reefs and associated deposits. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. Most of the coastal plains were developed during the Pleistocene 
Epoch when the sea levels fluctuated significantly. As the glaciers grew and advanced, 
less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the 
present stands of the sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water 
became available such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations. 
The subsurface conditions at the site are dominated by fossil Pleistocene Age 
coral/algal reef deposit and related deposits, which are intercalated with terrigenous

2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
Thomas Square is bounded by South Beretania Street, Ward Avenue, South King 
Street, and Victoria Street in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The planned 20-Inch 
diameter water main starts along South Hotel Street, travels south along Ward Avenue, 
east along South King Street, north along Victoria Street and ends near the intersection 
with Young Street. The 6-inch diameter waterline branches off the 20-inch diameter water 
main along Ward Avenue, running east, take a 90-degree bend to the north, and 
terminates at an existing water meter within Thomas Square. The project site is shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Based on our field observations and the topographic survey map provided, the 
project site is relatively flat with a gentle slope from mauka to makai. The existing ground 
surface elevation ranges from about +17.3 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) near the center of 
Thomas Square to about +12.6 feet MSL along South King Street.

3.2 
Trench Excavation Support 
We anticipate that excavations up to about 5 feet below the existing ground surface 
will be required for the installation of the new water mains. Deeper excavations may be 
required where the water mains traverse existing underground utilities. Where deeper 
excavations (greater than 5 feet in depth) are planned, temporary shoring or sloping and

The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable 
safety requirements. The contractor should be solely responsible for the adequacy and 
safety of the shoring installation. The contractor’s representative should be on-site at all

changing or unforeseen conditions, such as high groundwater, inappropriate construction 
sequence or techniques, etc., which may affect the shoring stability.

Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal 
to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential for 
excessive ground movement. 
It is important to install adequate shoring and to maintain it tight against the 
excavation walls with proper bracing during construction. The properly braced shoring is 
essential to reduce the potential for appreciable lateral movements of the adjacent ground 
into the excavation, which may result in potential settlement or distress to adjacent 
structures or other improvements. It should be noted that relatively thick layers of very 
loose to medium dense cinder sand were encountered in several of our drilled borings. 
The cinder sand may tend to slough and “run” during excavation, causing a loss of support 
to the surrounding pavement. Special attention should be paid to properly bracing the 
trench excavations into the underlying cinder deposits. 
It must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and the 
adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation. Due 
to the complexity of the stress changes, it is difficult to accurately estimate the magnitude 
of movement. The magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship, such as how 
quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are installed, the subsurface 
conditions, the size of the excavation, and the rate of excavation. 
Therefore, it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed 
properly and as early as practical. The adjacent ground should be continuously monitored 
for cracks, dips, and/or other indications of movements with instruments until the 
subsurface water system pipes are finally backfilled. 
3.3 
Pipe Cushion 
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the 
load supporting capacity of the pipe. Therefore, the pipe cushion is an important design 
consideration. For pipes bearing on the underlying fills, weathered volcanic tuff, and 
cinder, a minimum of 6 inches of granular cushion material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation), should be provided below the pipe. Where soft and/or 
loose soils are encountered at or near the invert of the pipes, a stabilization layer 
consisting of an additional 18 inches of open-graded gravel, such as No. 3B Fine gravel,

3.4 
Trench Backfill 
In general, the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the 
Water System Standards, State of Hawaii 2002. The first zone of backfill extending from 
the cushion material to at least 12 inches above the top of the pipes (or 12 inches above 
the groundwater level, whichever is greater) should consist of select free-draining 
granular material (ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation) to reduce the compaction effort required 
and resulting stresses on the pipe. In addition, it is critical to use this free-draining material 
to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide 
adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill could result in backfill 
settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the pavement subgrade should consist of the excavated on-site soils or imported fill 
materials that are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, organic material, and clay 
lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension. It should be 
noted that the fill requirements presented herein are intended as guidelines only and may 
be modified based on Geolabs’ additional laboratory testing and field observations on the 
available fill materials. 
Imported fill materials used for trench backfill should be well graded from coarse 
to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in the largest dimension. The material should 
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material 
should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher and a swell

3.5 
Trench Backfill Compaction and Placement Requirements 
Backfill materials consisting of the on-site soils should be moisture-conditioned to 
at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches 
in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. The 
upper 3 feet below the pavement grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction. 
Imported fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture, placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Aggregate base course should be 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not 
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with AASHTO T180 (ASTM D1557). Optimum moisture is the water content 
(percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or 
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or ponding 
should not be allowed. 
Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during backfilling to 
assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper 
moisture content. Where compaction is less than required, additional compactive effort

Pavement Restoration 
It is anticipated that AC pavement will be used to repave the top of the trenches 
along the water main alignments to match the existing roadway pavements along Ward 
Avenue, South King Street, and Victoria Street. Traffic volume information was not 
available at the time this report was prepared. Therefore, Geolabs requested traffic 
volume data from the State of Hawaii Department of Transportation (HDOT) – Highways 
Division and the City and County of Honolulu – Department of Transportation Services. 
Two traffic counts were conducted within the project vicinity. State of Hawaii 
Department of Transportation (HDOT) conducted a traffic count along Ward Avenue 
between South Beretania Street and Kinau Street on July 25, 2019. The City and County 
of Honolulu conducted a traffic count along South King Street near 1054 South King Street 
on April 25, 2023. Copies of the traffic counts are provided on Plates D-1.1 and D-1.2 in 
Appendix D. 
A traffic count was not available for Victoria Street. Therefore, we have assumed 
that the average daily traffic for Victoria Street is one-half of Ward Avenue.

Thrust Blocks 
We envision that thrust blocks will be required at some locations to provide 
resistance to the thrust forces due to the pressurized pipes. The thrust force acting on the 
pipes may be resisted by passive earth pressure acting against the face of the thrust 
block. We anticipate that most of the planned inverts will be about 4 feet below the existing 
ground surface. However, some of the thrust blocks may need to be located at deeper 
depths in areas where the new water main traverses existing utilities. 
Based on our field exploration, medium stiff fill, medium stiff weathered volcanic 
tuff, and loose to medium dense volcanic cinder were encountered within the depths 
explored. Therefore, we recommend using a lateral bearing pressure of 500 pounds per 
square foot (psf) for the design of thrust blocks bearing against the in-situ materials 
underlying the site. 
3.9 
Settlements 
Primary settlement of the water mains is normally caused by the difference in the 
unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted backfill 
material placed over the pipes. The net increase in loading will cause the settlement of 
the underlying subsoils below the trench invert. Based on our engineering analyses, 
settlement on the order of less than ½ inch is anticipated for the project. 
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good 
workmanship will be engaged during construction. Additional settlement could occur if 
improper trench support is used.

3.10 Corrosion Potential 
Laboratory corrosion tests, including pH, minimum resistivity, chloride content, and 
sulfate content testing, were performed on selected samples obtained during our field 
exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of the in-situ soils near the planned invert elevations 
of the new waterlines. The test results are summarized and presented in Appendix B. 
Detailed results of the Chloride Content (EPA 300.0) and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0) 
performed by Eurofins TestAmerica are presented in Appendix C.  
Based on the updated version of the Board of Water Supply, City and County of 
Honolulu Water System External Corrosion Control Standards dated July 28, 2021, there 
are two soil corrosivity categories. Category A (Moderately to Severely Corrosive) applies 
to soil meeting any one of the following conditions. Corrosion Category B (Negligibly to 
Mildly Corrosive) is applicable when none of the following conditions are met. The 
updated version of the standards is effective as of the publishing date for Oahu only. 
 Resistivity ≤ 5,000 ohm-cm 
 pH ≤ 6.5 
 Water-soluble chloride concentration ≥ 500 ppm 
 Water-soluble sulfate concentration ≥ 1,000 ppm 
 Within fluctuating saltwater table 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
regarding the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the 
resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to the corrosion 
of metallic objects. Results of the laboratory testing indicate that the on-site soils in the 
vicinity of Ward Avenue and South King Street have resistivity values of about 7,000 to 
26,000 ohm-cm with pH values of about 8.3 to 8.7. The on-site soils in the vicinity of 
Victoria Street have a resistivity value of about 2,100 with a pH value of about 7.9. 
Therefore, the on-site soils near the planned invert elevations of the new waterlines in the 
vicinity of Ward Avenue and South King Street may be considered as Corrosion Category 
B (Negligibly to Mildly Corrosive) based on the updated Board of Water Supply External 
Corrosion Control Standards dated July 28, 2021. However, the on-site soils near the

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Four sieve analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification.  
Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on Plate B-1. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested. Results of the direct 
shear test are presented on Plates B-2 and B-3. 
Four sets of corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51) and Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57) performed by our office and Chloride (EPA 300) and Sulfate Content 
(EPA 300) performed by Eurofins TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc., were performed on 
selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration. An additional Minimum 
Resistivity test was performed on a fourth sample. The test results are presented on 
Plate B-4. 
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-5 through B-7.
