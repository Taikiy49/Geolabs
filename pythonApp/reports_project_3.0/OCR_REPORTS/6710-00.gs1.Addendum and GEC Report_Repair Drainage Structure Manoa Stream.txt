Soil Nail Testing
Since boring information is not available at the project site we recommend
performing a minimum of one pre production verification test on sacrificial soil nail In
addition proof tests should be performed on at least 10 percent of the production soil nails
during construction to confirm the bond stresses used in the design
The verification test should be loaded gradually to 150 percent of the design load
for the creep test and subsequently to the maximum test load of about 200 percent of the
design load In addition the load at every loading increment should be held for at least 10
minutes and creep tests should be held for at least 60 minutes The proof tests should
consist of subjecting the soil nail to at least 150 percent of the design loads and the load
should be held for at least 10 minutes until stable The proof test nails may be
incorporated into the permanent soil nailed slope provided the nail satisfies the test criteria
Pullout tests and creep tests on the soil nails are integral parts of the design of the soil
nailed slope system Therefore a Geolabs representative should observe the pullout tests
and creep tests

SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our site observations at the location of the existing drainage structures at
Manoa Stream behind 2748 Poelua Street in Manoa Valley indicated severe erosion that
caused the undermining of the existing drainage spillway concrete apron The stream bed
at the downstream side of the spillway eroded by as much as 5 to 6 feet deep causing
portions of the concrete spillway to be undermined and break off The erosive force of the
currents downstream of the outfall structure also eroded the backfill materials behind the
wing wall and undermined the adjacent slope of the adjoining property
Based on our observations and research on the prevailing geological conditions at
the site the banks of the stream are vulnerable to erosion and need to be protected from
further damage The restricted access and environmentally sensitive nature of the job site
require construction methods using portable equipment and materials We anticipate the
need to divert stream flow to provide a relatively dry working area during construction We
believe this can be achieved with the use of temporary cofferdams made of sand bags
Considering the limitations of access to the site the use of gabion rock mattresses to
stabilize the eroded streambed across the drainage outflow structure and gabion units to
protect the eroded banks may be considered as these can be assembled in place and
makes use of smaller size rocks that are easier to transport with smaller equipment The
base layer of the gabions should be placed below the expected maximum scour depth or
the toe may be protected with flexible gabion mattresses that will conform to the scoured
area without compromising the stability of the bank or bed protection The foundation of
the new gabion wall may be designed using an allowable bearing capacity of
bearing on firm alluvial colluvial soils at the toe of the stream bank Unbalanced lateral
loads acting on the gabion wall may be resisted by friction developed between the bottom
of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure acting against the
near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used
for footings bearing on firm on site soils Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be
estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 150 pcf The passive pressure should be
reduced for foundations on slopes
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific
design recommendations

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to
early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone eruptions These later eruptions
are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of
the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available
such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosion processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these
materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become
dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
The project site is located within the central valley floor Basalt rock formation of
the Koolau Volcanic Series forms the stratigraphic base of the region and is believed to
underlie the near surface deposits of mixed alluvial and colluvial materials

Existing Site Conditions
The project site is along the Manoa Stream behind 2748 Poelua Street in Manoa
Valley in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii This portion of the stream is flanked
by residential properties on both the east and west banks The nearest access is via a
narrow easement with an effective width of about 5 feet on the north side of 2748
Poelua Street The rear side of this property is enclosed with a chain link fence mounted
on a CRM wall that sits along the crest of the slope about 15 feet above the stream The
opposite bank of the stream is overgrown with tropical vegetation and trees
The stream bed at the drainage outfall is lined with a 10 foot wide concrete
spillway that connects the drainage apron from the culvert to the opposite east bank of
the stream Water running over the spillway apparently eroded the stream bed creating
a 5 to 6 foot deep depression on the downstream side of the spillway The scouring
migrated under the spillway slab and portions of the wingwall and apron of the drainage
outfall structure including the toe of the slope of the adjacent downstream property

Subsurface Conditions
Based on our previous explorations within the Central Manoa Valley area and the
USDA Soil Conservation Service maps the soil formation in the locality developed in
old gravelly colluvium and alluvium also known as the Lolekaa silty clay series with a
substratum of strongly weathered gravel underlain by basalt formation

Erosion Hazards
Erosion hazard within the silty clay soil material ranges from slight to severe
depending on the percentage of the slope where it is located relative to runoff velocities
Runoff in the range of medium to rapid 25 to 40 slope would correspond to an
erosion hazard of moderate to severe On the other hand when runoff is slow to medium
such as in slopes of 8 to 15 percent the erosion hazard would be slight to moderate
Because of the rapid nature of the runoff from the drainage outfall the erosion hazard at
the project site is severe

Drainage Structure Rehabilitation
During strong rain events we envision that the combined effect of the rapid
discharge from the drainage outfall and the swollen southward current from the stream
creates erosive turbulence at their convergence This is manifested by the extent of
erosion at the downstream side of the spillway apron that created a 5 to 6 feet
depression in the stream bed The sudden drop in flow elevation contributes to the
erosive energy of the water The lateral migration of the void resulted in the undermining
of the concrete spillway apron including the slope behind the lower wing wall area of the
outlet structure
To reduce the erosive energy of the water at the stream and drainage discharge
junction we recommend elevating the eroded portion of the stream bed and providing a
stepped grade downstream transition to lessen the turbulence To achieve this we
recommend two schematic solutions as follows
Scheme 1 Elevate the scoured depression at the downstream side of the apron
with layers of stone filled gabion mattresses Prior to placement of the bottom layer of
gabion mattress we recommend placing of a filter fabric such as Mirafi 180 at the

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
placed directly around a perforated pipe perforations facing down at the base of
the wall above the ordinary high water level of the stream discharging to an
appropriate outlet or weep holes As an alternative a prefabricated drainage
product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used instead of the drainage
material The prefabricated drainage product also should be hydraulically
connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of compacted on site
materials or free draining compacted fills Unless covered by concrete slabs the
upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of low expansion relatively impervious
materials to reduce the potential for excessive water infiltration behind the walls

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly Vegetation debris deleterious and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site Due to the environmentally
sensitive nature of the site minimum ground disturbance should be observed It is
anticipated that the clearing and grubbing work may need to be performed incrementally
Extra care should be exercised during the clearing and grubbing operations to prevent
contamination of the stream
Soft and yielding areas below gabion wall subgrade should be over excavated to
expose firm natural material The resulting excavation should be lined with filter fabric such
as Mirafi 180 prior to placement of gabions or before backfilling with select granular
materials or well graded clean gravel The excavated soft and or organic soils should be
disposed of off site Special attention should be given to the potential presence of soft
and or loose sedimentary soils along the stream bed where gabion walls are planned

Gabion Construction
The area to be protected by the gabion wall and gabion mattresses must first be
smoothened out to the desired final grade or slope prior to installing the filter fabric or
gravel filter in accordance with the design plans
The gabions are then assembled tied together and bent into the desired form
and all ends and diaphragms are laced into place The assembled gabions are then
placed in their proper location and tied to all surrounding gabions It is important that all
adjacent gabions are tied together This prevents movement and failure due to the loss
of a basket gabion element out of a protected area Tying or lacing should be done
according to the gabion manufacturer s recommendations
After a sufficient number of gabions are assembled in place filling can start Care
should be exercised in the placement of the stone fill in the gabions to prevent damage
