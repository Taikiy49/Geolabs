2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of the lava of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene
Epoch more than 2 3 million years ago Following the cessation of this main shield
building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of the basaltic lavas
of the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the alluvial
sediments of the Palikea Formation Our exploration of the site and its vicinity indicates
that the area is underlain by a thin surface layer of residual soil derived from the in situ
weathering of Koloa Basalts Under the surface soil and exposed in areas of the project
site is a basaltic rock formation of the Koloa Volcanic Series
Agricultural development within the last 100 years and recent mass grading work
has brought the area to its present form

2 3 Subsurface Exploration
The subsurface conditions at selected locations of the project site were
explored by the performance of 17 seismic refraction surveys from December 26 through
The approximate locations of the seismic refraction surveys are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The objective of the seismic refraction survey was to evaluate the velocity of the
compressional waves transmitted through the subsurface materials at the survey
locations From the calculated wave velocities the approximate depth of soil cover
overlying rock formation was estimated and the relative hardness of the buried earth
materials was evaluated
Refraction seismography is a geophysical survey method based on the
transmission of elastic energy compressional waves through geologic materials The
refraction method consists of measuring the travel times of compressional waves along
preset distances of a monitoring array placed at the ground surface The compressional
waves are generated by an impulse energy source such as a sledge hammer impact on
the ground surface The waves are refracted at buried interfaces between geologic
materials with contrasting densities The refracted wave rays are received by geophones
placed at the ground surface and recorded by the seismograph instrument The raw field
data collected consists of wave form travel time and distance information which is then
manipulated to convert it into a format of velocity variation with depth
W O INC Page 5
SECTION 2 SITE CHARACTERIZATION
The recorded field data is input into a desktop computer for processing which
involves the interpretive selection of the first arrival times of the refracted wave rays The
data processing also involves mathematical calculations that yield approximate depths and
wave velocities for the various geologic materials located below the survey line The
calculated velocities may be empirically related to the excavation characteristics of the
materials
The conduct of seismic refraction surveys and the interpretation of the data are well
established and reasonably straight forward although they are not invariant The user can
change the field layout of equipment and apply experienced judgment and interpretation in
the handling of the raw data obtained from the field survey As a result the evaluations
derived from a seismic refraction survey are best coupled with intrusive subsurface
exploration such as test pits and borings which serve to validate the interpretation of the
seismic field data
The primary limitation of the seismic refraction survey is that the geologic materials
must possess increasing density with increasing depth for the data computation and
geologic model construction to work effectively In other words the P wave velocity must
increase with increasing depth Thus soft soil or weak rock layers underlying dense rock
or other high velocity layers may not be detected or modeled correctly due to a condition
known as wave velocity inversion In addition the interpretation of seismic refraction data
assumes that the geologic layers being modeled extend continuously across the survey
location
The seismic refraction surveys were conducted by Geolabs using a Geometrics
sledge hammer energy source The raw field data was analyzed and geologic models
were produced using the Seismic Refraction Interpretation Program SIP software by
Rimrock Geophysics

Based on the results of the seismic refraction survey it appears that hard basalt
rock formation underlies the reddish colored clayey surface soils at depths ranging from
about 2 feet to about 12 feet below the existing ground surface The higher P wave
velocity of the basalt rock contrasts significantly with the lower P wave velocity of the near
surface clay soils therefore the geologic models produced by the interpretation of the
seismic data are in fairly good agreement Of the 17 locations surveyed the estimated
average depth to basalt rock appears to be about 5 to 8 feet below the existing ground
surface
The anticipated depth to basalt rock varies with location across the project site In
general some thicker saprolitic soils composing a more gradual transition to weathered
basalt rock may be encountered in the regions of greater topographic relief such as the
hills located at northern portions of the project site Elsewhere at the site the reddish
colored surficial residual soils appear to grade rather abruptly to hard basalt rock

materials appear to have seismic velocities on the order of about 1 000 to 1 800 feet per

There is potential for objectionable impacts or hazards resulting from blasting
operations These generally originate from one or more of the following factors
1 Flyrock
2 Ground Vibration
3 Air Blast
These factors are discussed in the following paragraphs
a Flyrock
Flyrock is the blasters term for flying rock or other debris which is
launched into the air during open blasting by the force of the detonating
explosives These projectiles often have tremendous kinetic energy and
can crush or destroy objects in their flight path or at their point of impact
The occurrence of flyrock may be reduced and possibly eliminated by
increasing the confinement or the blast depth of the explosive charges
and or by using safety mats

5 Ground Vibration
With respect to the subject project ground vibration is probably the most
critical potential hazardous factor to be anticipated during blasting
operations When an explosive detonates below ground it generates
intense stress waves in the surrounding material These waves crush the
rock immediately adjacent to the explosion and permanently distorts and
cracks the rock for some distance
Beyond the area in which the intensity of the stress waves attenuate to
such a degree that there is no longer any permanent deformation of the
rock the stress is further propagated through the rock as elastic waves
These elastic waves create ground vibration
The potential of the ground vibration to damage a structure is a function of
the intensity of the vibration and the natural frequency of the structure
According to the DuPont Blaster s Handbook a formula has been
developed to relate the intensity of ground vibration at a given point in
terms of particle displacement velocity and acceleration to the weight of
the explosive charge and the distance between the explosion and the
point of interest
Vibration would be a consideration in the vicinity of the developed areas
such as the residential development situated along the southern and
eastern borders of the proposed site
c Aijr Blast
The air blast from an explosion is a compression wave in the air Noise is
the portion of the air blast with frequencies between 20 and
concussion is the portion with frequencies below 20 Hz The DuPont

3 2 Light Building Foundations
Details of the proposed structures were not available at the time this report was
prepared However we anticipate that the building structures will consist of light one and
two story wood framed structures For preliminary design purposes preliminary
foundation guidelines are presented below The guidelines should be reviewed and
updated prior to final structure design
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot psf may be
utilized for the design of shallow foundations bearing on the recompacted on site soils
and or the compacted fills needed to raise the existing grades to the design finished
grades This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third
1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces Foundations
should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the adjacent ground surface
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 4 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted on site soils and or
new compacted fills Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using
an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This
assumes that the soils around footings are well compacted or undisturbed Unless
covered by driveways or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should
be neglected
Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded
below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility
trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid
surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce
the potential for foundation settlement If foundations are designed and constructed in
accordance with our recommendations we estimate that total settlements would be on the
W O INC Page 21

order of 1 inch We believe that the differential settlements between adjacent foundations
to be on the order of about 0 5 inches
The slab subgrades including the select granular fill should be kept moist until the
placement of cushion fill and concrete Six to 24 inches of depending on the actual
subgrade conditions select granular fill material should be provided above the underlying
potentially expansive subgrade soil thereby reducing the loss of moisture from the clayey
subgrade soils In our opinion the reduction in potential drying and wetting of the
subgrade soils should reduce the impact of shrink swell effects on the slab due to potential
heaving of the underlying expansive clayey soils when wetted
We recommend that a minimum 4 inch layer of cushion fill be incorporated below
the concrete slab The cushion fill should consist of free draining materials such as
No 3 Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation which would also serve as a capillary
moisture break The slab subgrade should be thoroughly wetted prior to concrete
placement to allow the select granular fill layer to remain in a moist condition To reduce
the potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an
impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the cushion layer Flexible floor
coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered on the slab since they can
better mask minor slab cracking It is also recommended that interior walls be designed to
incorporate some flexibility to accommodate small amounts of possible ground
movements
Areas adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly against the slab edges with

APPENDIX C
Field Exploration Borings
APPENDIX C
Field Exploration Borings
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
8 borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 8 to depths of about 20 0 to 25 6 feet
below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with auger and
coring equipment
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 8
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and
drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX D
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX D
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations ASTM D 2937
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Five sets of resistivity ASTM G 57 and pH tests ASTM G 51 were performed on
selected samples to evaluate the corrosion potential of the soil The results of these tests
are presented on Plate D 1
Two one inch ring swell tests were performed on remolded samples to evaluate the
swelling potential of the soils under surcharge loads These tests were run on air dried
samples Results of these swell tests are presented on Plate D 2
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected soil sample
to evaluate its liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test results are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate D 3
Four California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on bulk
samples of near surface soils to evaluate the strength characteristics for pavement
subgrade support Results of these CBR tests are presented on Plates D 4 through D 7
Four Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on the
bulk samples obtained to evaluate the relationship between the moisture content and
the dry unit weight of the near surface soils The test results are presented on
Plates D 8 through D 11
