INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our preliminary geotechnical engineering
exploration for the design of the mass grading of the proposed Kapolei golf course
development The project site is located near the junction of Farrington Highway and Fort
Barrette Road adjacent to Puu Kapolei on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general
location of the project site and its vicinity is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
Our work on the project was performed in general accordance with our proposal
to Park Engineering dated This report summarizes our preliminary
findings and recommendations
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed Villages of Kapolei development consists of a planned residential
community with related facilities The project will be developed in several phases
The proposed golf course site encompasses approximately 185 acres of land area
and was until recently used for the cultivation of sugar cane
It is proposed to clear and grade the site for the development of an eighteen 18
hole golf course with appurtenant facilities such as a clubhouse and maintenance area
The grading work will provide borrow material for use in other developments on the
Villages of Kapolei The preliminary grading plans provided to us indicate cuts of up to
about twenty 20 feet below the existing ground surface
The preliminary plans also indicate the extensive use of water features in the golf
course

formulation of preliminary geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the
design of the mass grading for the proposed golf course development
Primary areas of emphasis in our exploration were
1 Obtaining an overview of the general soil condition which will be
encountered during mass grading

formulate recommendations pertaining to the design of the proposed water
features

to and from the project site

GENERAL SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is situated to the southeast of the junction of Farrington Highway
and Fort Barrette Road near Puu Kapolei in the Ewa District on the island of Oahu
Hawaii The general location of the site and its vicinity is shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 1

The project site has previously been used for the cultivation of sugar cane up
through the present Most of the site is densely vegetated by sugar cane
In general the topography is nearly level with drainageways and irrigation ditches
crossing the site The overall slope and drainage is in a southerly direction
Elevations at the site vary from about 58 to 100 feet above Mean Sea Level
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active and
its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of Waianae and its geomorphology
and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of sea level
during the Pleistocene and the genesis of Pearl Harbor and the Ewa Coastal Plain The
subsurface conditions in the majority of the coastal plain are dominated by fossil
Pleistocene age coral algal reef deposits laid during high stands of the sea which
intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic section in this area
is Waianae Basalt
The Waianae and Koolau volcanic shields were built during the late Pliocene and
early Pleistocene by thin bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities
ceased approximately 5 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicates
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2459 00 Page 6
that the island of Oahu has subsided as much as 1200 feet since the cessation of this
early volcanic activity Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of the
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed
behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the
lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the Pleistocene Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a result
of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to
fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower
relative to present sea level on Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
corresponding higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs
The concurrent growth of reefs and lagoonal sediments may have also resulted in
deposits of coral algal limestone and marls within the predominantly lagoonal sedimentary
unit
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2459 00 Page 7
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef
deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of upper areas of the
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal
sediments
The geologic history of the project area has been further complicated by the
deposition of recent pyroclastic materials and lava flows from eruptions of the vents of the
Upper Member of the Waianae Volcanic Series including Puu Makakilo Puu Palailai and
Puu Kapolei These post erosional i e following the cessation of shield building events
were contemporaneous with the Pleistocene sea level fluctuations
Many of the eruptions were rather explosive in nature owing to the interaction of
groundwater with the rising magma resulting in steam explosions which expelled large
quantities of pyroclastic material predominantly ash and cinder These deposits of
pyroclastics have consolidated to form volcanic tuff locally referred to as mudrock
Agricultural land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years
have brought the Ewa Honolulu area to its present form
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by the drilling and sampling
of twelve 12 borings at the project site to depths of about 21 5 feet below the existing
ground surface

W O 2459 00 Page 8
In general our exploration encountered a relatively thin surface layer of stiff brown
silty clay Alluvium ranging in thickness between about six 6 to twelve 12 feet
Below the silty clay strata our borings encountered variable soil conditions ranging
from dense sandy soils Coral Detritus to stiff sandy silts representing the weathering
products of pyroclastic materials ash and clinker from nearby eruptive centers of the
Upper Member of the Waianae Volcanic Series
Groundwater was encountered at depths of 15 0 and 13 0 feet below the existing
ground surface in Boring No 9 and 10 respectively It is believed that these water levels
represent perched water tables and that they may be localized Some isolated moist to
wet zones were noted in other borings These zones may indicate possible zones of
groundwater seepage
Falling head percolation tests were conducted in Boring and 10
These tests indicate that the subsoils below the surface alluvial silty clay generally have
rapid soil absorption rates
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration along with the boring logs are

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our exploration and analyses it is our opinion that development of the
proposed golf course project is feasible from a geotechnical engineering point of view
The soils encountered at the site are generally competent and should provide adequate
bearing for the site improvements of the proposed development The excavated materials
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2459 00 Page 9
should provide a suitable source of fill material Some hard excavation and or ripping
may be encountered in localized cemented areas or when bouldery horizons are
encountered
Site Grading
In general based on the results of our preliminary exploration it appears that
conventional grading methods may be used for the mass grading portion of the project
The following recommendations are general guidelines for the design of the mass
grading and for the development of general specifications
Prior to commencement of grading all areas within the proposed grading limits
should be cleared of all vegetation and deleterious materials The resulting grub and spoil
material should either be disposed of properly off site or else the material should be
shredded chipped and stockpiled for use in landscaping
Due to the dry and sometimes windy site conditions moisture conditioning of fill
material will be necessary It is also advisable to institute a dust control program during
grading operations
Soft or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below any areas
designated to receive fill should be over excavated to firm natural material and the
resulting depression backfilled with well compacted engineered fill
In general the excavated soil materials should be suitable as fill material Any
boulders or cobbles larger than twelve 12 inches in maximum dimension should be
removed from the excavated material and either disposed of off site or be placed in the
deeper fills as discussed below
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2459 00 Page 10
The material should be adequate as a source of trench and structural backfill if
needed Trench and structural backfill should not contain any rock or lumps larger than
six 6 inches in maximum dimension
Imported material if required should be non expansive with no particles greater
than six 6 inches in size and no more than fifteen 15 percent passing the 200 sieve
It is recommended that if additional off site borrow is required it should be tested by the
geotechnical engineer to determine if it is suitable for its intended use prior to its delivery
to the jobsite
Large boulders or rock fragments should be buried in the deepest portions of the
fills provided that they are not nested and an adequate amount of compacted finer
grained soil fills the space between the boulders to reduce the potential of loss of fines
from the upper portion of the fill Care must be exercised to avoid placement of any
boulders in locations which would conflict with future utility lines In general boulder or
rock fragments larger than twelve 12 inches in size should be placed at depths greater
than four 4 feet below finish subgrade level
Generally fills required should be placed in level lifts not exceeding twelve 12
inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 85 maximum dry density as
established by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 Trench and structural backfills should be
placed in level lifts not exceeding eight 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to
a minimum of 90 maximum dry density per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
Fills to be placed on slopes having ratios steeper than five horizontal to one vertical
5H 1V should be keyed and benched into firm soil or in situ coral formation to lessen
potential instability of the new fill see Plate 3 The filling operations should start at the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2459 00 Page 11
lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the
above recommendations for fill placement
Excavation
Based on the results of our preliminary exploration it appears that most of the
materials encountered in the golf course development area may be conventionally
excavated Some hard excavation and or ripping may be encountered in localized
cemented areas or when bouldery horizons are encountered Generally the excavation
method should be left to the contractor However care should be exercised to avoid
overcutting which would necessitate backfilling of the overcut area This is particularly
critical in slope areas to avoid the placement of sliver fills on the slope faces in an attempt
to repair the overcut area
Dewatering
During our field exploration groundwater was observed only in Boring Nos 9 and
10 at respective depths of 15 0 and 13 0 feet below the existing ground surface As
previously discussed it is believed that this is perched water and may be localized and or
transient
Based on the preliminary grading concepts shown on the Illustrative Site Plan it
appears that the site grading at these locations may not encroach on the groundwater
levels The final site grading concepts should be carefully reviewed with respect to these
groundwater levels
If it is necessary to excavate below the groundwater levels additional exploration
and permeability testing is recommended to provide data for the development of
dewatering plans
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2459 00 Page 12
Slopes
Provided that on site materials are utilized design cut and fill slopes of two
horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter are recommended Fill slopes should be
constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio Care should be
exercised to avoid overcutting on cut slopes in order to avoid the placement of sliver fills
on the slope faces in an attempt to repair the overcut area
Sandy and silty soils such as those encountered below the surface clayey soils
at the project site are susceptible to erosion when exposed particularly in slope areas
Water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and slope planting utilized to
reduce potential erosion and or instability of the finished slopes
In general midpoint slope benches should be provided for slopes steeper than
three horizontal to one vertical 8H 1V and having vertical heights greater than 20 feet
Infrastructure Installation
It is anticipated that underground utilities such as water sewer drain and irrigation
lines will be installed as part of the development work Based on the results of our field
exploration it appears that relatively conventional excavation methods may be utilized for
utility line trenching However bouldery horizons may be encountered in the deeper
excavations
In general the subsurface materials encountered at the site are relatively
competent and would require minimal shoring However areas of loose sand or gravel
may be encountered Trench shoring design and installation should be left to the
contractor However trench shoring and bracing should conform to the appropriate
health and safety requirements
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2459 00 Page 13
Trench backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding eight 8 inches in
loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 maximum dry density per ASTM
Test Method D 1557 78
Mechanical compaction equipment is highly suited to the materials encountered
at the project site Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be used as a means to
attempt to compact the on site soils
Ponds and Water Features
It is our understanding that the proposed golf course development will involve
ponds and water features Due to the dry site conditions and the anticipated permeable
nature of the materials at the site we recommend that the ponds be lined with synthetic
flexible membranes to reduce the possibility of seepage To reduce the potential of
punctures in the flexible membranes a cushion layer of sand is recommended for the
bottoms and sides of the ponds and water features and should be installed prior to the
placement of the flexible membrane The cushion should be a minimum of six 6 inches
thick
Although the flexible membranes are considered to be impervious there is a
possibility of seepage due to punctures in the liner or breaks in the seams Therefore
we recommend that a subdrain system be installed below the sand cushion This
subdrain should be in a herringbone pattern and be daylighted to drain outside the pond
embankment
Foundation Support
Our laboratory testing for this preliminary exploration indicates that the on site soils
generally have low to moderate expansion potential It is our opinion that relatively

Additional Exploration
This report is based upon a preliminary exploration for the subject project The
majority of the project site is densely vegetated and not accessible to truck mounted
exploration equipment without extensive trail clearing Therefore we recommend that
additional exploration work be performed in critical areas which were not accessible such
as the lake sites and other deep cut areas to confirm the assumptions upon which our
preliminary recommendations are based

Design Review
Plans and specifications for the proposed construction should be forwarded to the
geotechnical engineer for review and written comment prior to construction This review
is needed to determine adherence to the earthwork and foundation recommendations
given herein If this review is not made the geotechnical engineer cannot assume

Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the geotechnical engineer be retained to provide
geotechnical engineering services during construction of the sitework excavation and
foundation phases of the work This is to observe compliance with the design concepts
specifications or recommendations and to allow design changes in the event that
subsurface conditions differ from that anticipated prior to start of construction The
recommendations given in this report are contingent upon such observations
Any imported material required should be non expansive tested and approved by
the geotechnical engineer prior to hauling to the site
If actual exposed rock soil conditions encountered during construction are different
from those assumed or considered in this report then appropriate modifications to the
design should be made

V Placement and Compaction of Fill
A _ The fill shall be placed in horizontal layers not exceeding eight inches 8 in
loose thickness Each layer shall be spread uniformly and blade mixed to
attain uniformity of material and water content within each layer
Additional fill material shall not be placed on any fill layer which has not been
properly compacted Subsequent removal of any unacceptable layer shall
be done at the Contractor s expense

C All fill shall be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent of the maximum dry
density established by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 unless otherwise
specified in the Geotechnical Engineer s Report
D Boulders encountered in excavations may be used in the deeper fills
provided that the following are followed
1 Boulders shall be spread out and must not be nested together They
should be placed so that compaction equipment will be able to compact
around them
2 No part of the boulder should enroach any closer to finished subgrade
than the boulder s largest dimension and in no case be closer than 3
feet to finished subgrade
3 No boulder larger than 5 feet in its largest dimension should be placed
in the fill It may be necessary to reduce the size of larger boulders or
dispose of them off site
4 Care must be exercised to avoid placement of boulders in the proposed
utility alignments to prevent difficulty in the later excavation of the utility
trenches
