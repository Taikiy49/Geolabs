INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed New Parking Lot for the Maunalani Nursing and Rehabilitation Center in the
Maunalani Heights area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
derived from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses for the
proposed project The recommendations presented herein are intended for the design of
pavements storm water runoff disposal systems and retaining structures only The findings
and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located within the existing Maunalani Nursing and Rehabilitation
Center located in the Maunalani Heights area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii
Based on the information provided we understand that it is desired to construct a new parking
lot to provide about 33 additional parking spaces at the site We understand that a drywell to
be located at the center of the new parking lot is planned for disposal of storm water runoff
for the new parking lot Based on our field observations we envision that a new retaining wall
will be required for grade separation at the eastern portion of the site In general we envision
only minor grading consisting of cuts and fills on the order of approximately 4 feet would be
required to achieve the design finished grades at the site
Based on our experience we anticipate that the near surface clayey soils at the site
would exhibit a moderate expansion potential when subjected to changes in the moisture
content Therefore special attention was given to the subgrade soil conditions for the design
of the parking lot pavements and retaining wall foundations

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger shield
Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age and forms the majority
of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active
and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lava banking against its eastern flank
The project site is composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lava of the Koolau
Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows of jointed dense vesicular
basalt with thin interbedded clinker layers In situ chemical weathering of the Koolau lava
flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years The weathering process has formed a
mantle of residual soils which grade to saprolite with depth In general saprolite is composed
mainly of silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite
material grades with depth to basaltic rock formation

The project site is located within the existing Maunalani Nursing and Rehabilitation
Center in the Maunalani Heights area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The
proposed project site is located to the south of the existing lower parking lot and to the north
of Halehoola Place The site is currently vacant and is sloping down toward the south as

western half of the site is located on a relatively steep slope An existing retaining wall is
located at the eastern portion of the site providing grade separation between the existing lower
parking lot and the proposed new parking lot A few trees were noted at the site at the time
of our field exploration The project site is separated from Halehoola Place by chain link
fences which span the southern boundary of the site The site is covered with lawn grass with
an associated sprinkler system Weathered rock outcrops were exposed on the ground at the
southern portion of the site

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the new parking lot site were explored by drilling and
sampling two test borings extending to depths of about 15 and 21 5 feet below the existing
ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our field exploration encountered stiff to very stiff silty clays of approximately
5 to 8 feet thick starting at the existing ground surface The silty clay layer was underlain by
saprolite materials consisting of medium dense to dense silty sands and sandy clays extending
to the maximum depth explored of about 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface In
addition highly weathered rock was encountered at a depth of about 11 feet in Boring No 2
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings at the time of our field exploration
However it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to seasonal
precipitation tidal fluctuations storm surge conditions and other factors
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered in the borings are presented on the
Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2 of Appendix A Results of the laboratory tests performed
on selected soil samples are presented in Appendix B
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
As previously indicated our field exploration generally encountered stiff to very stiff silty
clays of approximately 5 to 8 feet thick starting at the existing ground surface The silty clay
layer was underlain by saprolite materials consisting of medium dense to dense silty sands
and sandy clays extending to the maximum depth explored of about 21 5 feet below the
existing ground surface In addition highly weathered rock was encountered in Boring No 2
at about 11 feet deep Groundwater was not encountered in the borings at the time of our
exploration
Based on our laboratory tests the near surface silty clay layer may be classified as
moderately expansive when subjected to moisture fluctuations Due to the expansive nature
of the near surface materials special attention should be given to the subgrade preparation

be considered relatively impermeable for the disposal of storm water runoff Therefore the

near surface silty clay layer for better disposal capacities Detailed discussions and specific
design recommendations pertaining to these items and other geotechnical aspects of the
project are presented in the following sections of this report
Pavement Design
Based on the information provided we understand that a new parking lot will be
constructed at the project site We anticipate that the vehicle loading for the proposed new
parking lot will consist primarily of passenger vehicles pick up trucks and occasional
moderately loaded trucks We have made our pavement designs assuming the pavement
subgrade soil will generally consist of the near surface clayey soils as encountered during our
field exploration If site grading exposes soils other than those assumed additional tests
should be performed to confirm or revise the recommended pavement sections for actual field
conditions Based on the above assumptions the pavements for the new parking lot may be
designed using either of the following two pavement sections
Flexible Pavement Section
2 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
12 0 Inch Aggregate Subbase 95 Percent Relative Compaction
20 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Moist Subgrade
Rigid Pavement Section
6 0 Inch Portland Cement Concrete
8 0 Inch Aggregate Subbase 95 Percent Relative Compaction
14 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Moist Subgrade
Based on the results of our laboratory tests the near surface clayey soils exhibit a
moderate expansion potential when subjected to changes in the moisture content Therefore
we recommend that the subgrade be properly prepared and kept moist until the placement of
subbase and base course materials The pavement subgrade should be scarified to a depth

Retaining Structures
Based on the information provided we understand that a retaining wall is planned for
grade separation at the eastern portion of the project site Based on the subsurface conditions
encountered in our exploration we recommend that a shallow foundation system consisting
of continuous footings be used to support the retaining wall
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot psf may be
used for the design of the retaining wall footings bearing on the recompacted on site soils
This allowable bearing pressure is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third
1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by temporary wind and or seismic forces The
bottom of the footing excavations should be moisture conditioned and recompacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior to placement of reinforcing steel and or
concrete
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent grade If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should
be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of
the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to

Site Preparation
We envision that site grading work consisting of minor cuts and fills on the order of
approximately 4 feet will be required for the proposed project construction In general we
recommend that the following guidelines be used for the minor site grading anticipated at the
site At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish asphaltic concrete pavements
and other unsuitable materials within the grading limits should be removed and disposed of
properly off site
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas
designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over excavated to expose firm or
dense material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill The
excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site Contract
GEOLABS INC
W O 4432 00 Page 11
documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and
compacted fill placement to account for variation in the over excavation quantities
Existing underground utilities to be abandoned at the site should be removed The
resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with on site fill materials
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in 6 to 8 inch loose lifts
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of
the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
After clearing and grubbing areas beneath the future pavement areas should be
scarified to a depth of 12 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction In addition the subgrade
soils should be wetted and kept moist until placement of the pavement structural section or
aggregate subbase for concrete pavement
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend
that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or scarified and prepared again as
recommended above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to
inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and
replacement with well compacted fill at no additional cost to the owner
Imported fill materials if required should consist of non expansive granular material
such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded
from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension In addition it
should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material
should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of
1 percent or less Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs for conformance with
these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use

Utility Trenches
We envision that some new utility lines and utility connections will likely be needed for
the proposed project In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular
materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used
for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support
around the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides
of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to
the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils The backfill
materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in
about 8 inch loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground subsidence Where trenches
are located below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement
grade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades of the new parking lot should be sloped to shed water away from
the existing and new retaining structure foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding
Excessive landscape watering near the wall foundations and pavement areas should also be
avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains
to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils
GEOLABS INC
W O 4432 00 Page 13
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
retaining structure foundations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be properly
backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce
the potential for excessive water infiltration In addition drainage swales should be provided
as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the
foundations and pavement areas

on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped
with continuous flight solid stem augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials encountered are
presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples retrieved
in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
One set of sieve analysis ASTM C 136 D 1140 was performed on a selected
sample of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soil and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate B 1
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on a bulk sample
of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content relationships The
test results are presented on Plate B 2
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR Test ASTM D 1883 was performed on
a bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the
soils The test results are presented on Plate B 3
