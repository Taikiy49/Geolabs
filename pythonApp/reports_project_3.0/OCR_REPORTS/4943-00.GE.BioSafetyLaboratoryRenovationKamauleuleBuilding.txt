PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located within the existing Department of Health State
Laboratory Facility at 2725 Waimano Home Road in the upper ridges of Pearl City on the
Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
Based on the information provided it is proposed to construct a new Bio Safety
Laboratory as an extension to the existing Kamauleule Building as shown on the Site
Plan Plate 2 The new extension will be a single story structure measuring approximately
46 feet long and 23 to 28 feet wide in plan dimensions The new laboratory extension will
be of CMU wall construction with heavy floor loads and machinery on the rooftop Based
on the information provided by the project structural engineer wall loads are anticipated
to be on the order of about 5 kips per lineal foot with floor loads in excess of 100 pounds
per square foot psf The finish floor elevation of the new Bio Safety Laboratory is set at
about 656 feet Mean Sea Level MSL Based on the existing topography and the
anticipated finish floor elevation minimal site grading work will be required to attain the
design finish grade for the proposed construction

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Based on our review of the subsurface soil rock data provided in the
afore mentioned reports prepared by our office in 1989 and 1992 it appears that the
project site is generally underlain by very stiff to hard brown clayey silts extending to the
maximum depth explored of about 46 5 feet below the original grades at the site
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of the field
explorations However it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change
with time seasonal precipitation and other factors The subsurface information that may
be relevant to the project site is provided on Plates 3 through 7 of this report for
reference
Our review of the construction drawings indicated that up to about 5 feet of fills
were placed during the earthwork construction of the existing laboratory to achieve the
design finished grades at the new laboratory extension location In addition up to about
15 feet of trench backfills were placed during the installation of the 12 inch drain line
traversing the project site We understand that the excavated on site clayey silt soils
were used as fill and backfill materials during the earthwork construction for the existing
laboratory It should be noted that the fills and backfills might have been placed and
compacted properly at the time of construction However due to the passage of time
and water infiltration from the landscaping irrigation the quality and existing conditions
of the fills and backfills at the new laboratory extension may have deteriorated

Previous field explorations conducted at the subject site indicated that the new
Bio Safety Laboratory extension location may be underlain by very stiff to hard clayey
silts extending to depths of over 40 feet deep Due to the proximity of the new

laboratory extension to the existing building basement wall and site retaining wall we
believe that the use of shallow footing foundations may impose a surcharge load onto
the existing walls which may result in horizontal deflections of the walls and lead to

laboratory extension Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical
engineering design recommendations are presented in the following sections of this

Pipe Pile Foundations
As previously mentioned a deep foundation support system consisting of pipe
piles is recommended for the support of the new building extension to reduce the
potential adverse effects on the existing building basement wall and site retaining wall
In addition the pipe pile foundation will also serve to transfer the axial load from the
new building extension through the thick trench backfill into the underlying in situ stiff
clayey soils
The pipe pile foundation is typically constructed by drilling a borehole placing a
pipe pile in the hole and backfilling the borehole with concrete Pipe piles are desirable
because they can be readily installed in access restrictive environments and in
numerous soil types and ground conditions In addition installation of the pipe piles
generally causes minimal disturbance to adjacent structures the adjacent soils and the
environment
An allowable compressive load capacity of 35 kips per pile may be utilized for the
design of pipe pile foundations embedding a minimum of 40 feet below the footing
bottom elevation Frictional contribution from the upper 20 feet of subsoils has been
reduced in our engineering analyses due to the uncertainty of the existing soil
conditions resulting from landscaping irrigation and trench backfill Contribution from
the end bearing component has also been ignored due to the difficulty in obtaining a
clean bottom for end bearing support The allowable compressive load capacity for the
loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces

The pipe piles should consist of galvanized steel with an outside diameter of
about 3 inches and a minimum thickness of 0 16 inches Rebar reinforcement should
be utilized to connect the pipe piles and the footing The pipe pile should be installed in
a minimum 8 inch diameter hole The annulus between the hole and the pipe pile
should be filled with concrete
The spacing of the pipe piles should be based on the allowable compressive
load capacity building loads and the ability of the wall footing to span unsupported
between pipe piles The pipe piles should be spaced no closer than 2 feet from center
to center to avoid reduction in vertical capacity due to group action
Settlements of the pipe pile foundations will result primarily from the elastic
compression of the pile member and subgrade response We estimate the total
settlement of the pipe pile supported foundation to be less than about 0 5 inch with
differential settlements to be less than about 0 25 inch We believe that these pipe pile
settlements are primarily elastic and should occur as the loads are applied
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by a combination of the
lateral load capacity of the pipe piles and the passive pressure against the vertical faces
of the grade beams Passive earth pressure against the vertical faces of the grade
beam may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 250 pounds per square
feet per foot of depth pcf
The load carrying capacity of the pipe piles depends to a large extent on the
contact between the pipe piles and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in drilling
the holes installing the pipe piles and placing concrete in the holes The bottom of
holes should be cleaned of soft and or disturbed material prior to installing the pipe pile
The performance of the pipe pile foundations will depend significantly upon the
contractors method of construction and construction procedures As a result we
recommend that a representative from Geolabs Inc be present to observe the drilling
and installation of the pipe piles during construction
Floor Slab
We understand that significant loading is anticipated for the floor slab of the new
laboratory extension Due to the proximity of the existing building basement wall we
believe that the heavily loaded floor slab should also be structurally supported on the pipe
pile foundations to avoid surcharging the existing building basement wall Because of the
uncertainty of the existing soil conditions resulting from landscaping irrigation and trench
backfill the structurally supported floor slab on pipe pile foundations will also serve to
reduce the potential for floor slab settlement resulting from the heavy floor loads over the
potentially compressible subsoils
In general a base course layer beneath the structurally supported floor slab will
not be necessary However we understand that the clayey soils encountered at the site

exhibit low to moderate expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations To
reduce the potential for future distress to the floor slab we recommend that the subgrade
soils be properly prepared and capped with 6 inches of non expansive select granular fill
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The subgrade soils should
be scarified to a depth of about 10 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture and compacted to about 90 percent relative compaction
The non expansive select granular fill should be placed as soon as possible after
the subgrade soils are properly prepared The prepared select granular fill and the
underlying subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist until the final placement of
concrete Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend that the soils be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or prepared again
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather
and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted fill
Exterior concrete walkways will likely be required for the proposed project We
also recommend that concrete walkways be supported on 6 inches of non expansive
select granular fill The select granular fill should be compacted to at least 90 percent
relative compaction In addition crack control joints should be provided at intervals equal
to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at right angle intersections
Thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at least
12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade Areas adjacent to the slab should be
backfilled tightly against the slab edges with the on site soil and graded to divert water
away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the foundations and
slabs
Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and the anticipated finish floor elevation minimal
site grading work will be required for the proposed construction At the on set of
earthwork the areas within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and
grubbed Vegetation debris deleterious materials and other unsuitable materials should
be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas encountered during
clearing and grubbing below any areas designated to receive fill should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft and or organic soils
should be properly disposed of off site or used in landscaping areas if appropriate
Slabs and foundations of the existing structures that are to be demolished should
be completely removed Any over excavation resulting from the demolition should be
backfilled with compacted granular fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned should
be removed and the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with select
granular fill material placed in 6 to 8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of

95 percent relative compaction Utilities that are to be abandoned in place under the
proposed structure should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete under low pressure
After demolition clearing and grubbing areas beneath the future building slab
should be over excavated where necessary to provide the minimum 6 inch select
granular capping fill below the concrete slab The clayey subgrade at the over excavated
level should be scarified to a depth of about 10 inches moisture conditioned to at least
2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to about 90 percent relative
compaction Where shrinkage cracks are noted after preparation of the subgrade we
recommend that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close all cracks Saturation and
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor
drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well
compacted engineered fill at no additional cost to the owner Contract documents should
include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and engineered fill
placement to account for variations in the over excavation quantities
Select granular fill materials needed for the non expansive capping layer should
consist of non expansive imported granular material such as crushed coral or basalt
The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3
inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles
passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or
more and should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent Imported fill materials
should be tested by Geolabs for conformance with these recommendations prior to
delivery to the project site for its intended use
Fill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
placed in level lifts not exceeding 10 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum moisture is
the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Site grading operations should be observed by a representative from Geolabs It
is important that a representative from our office observe the site grading operation to
evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation process
and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those encountered in our field
exploration
Utility Trench
New utility connections may be required for the proposed new addition A granular
bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is
recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine
gravel should also be used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes

to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort of the
backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is critical to use this
free draining material to reduce the potential of formation of voids below the haunches of
pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper backfill
material and placement could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select fill material The backfill
should be placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not
less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground
subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 2 feet of the trench
backfill below the pavement finish grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95
percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the new building extension should be sloped to shed
water away from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised
that gutter systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted
away from the foundation areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and
slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have
concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as
possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away from the slab and
footing foundations
