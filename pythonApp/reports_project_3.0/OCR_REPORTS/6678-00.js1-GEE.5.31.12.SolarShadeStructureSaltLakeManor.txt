PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project involves the construction of two carport solar shade structures and a
small concrete equipment pad in the parking lot at Salt Lake Manor in the Salt Lake area
on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The two carport solar shade structures measure
approximately between 8 and 11 feet in height and will cantilever out about 19 feet over
the existing parking lot We envision that the solar structures will be supported on
cast in place concrete drilled shafts

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae and
Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late
Pliocene in age 4 million years ago and forms the bulk of the western one third of
the island The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene Ice Age in age 5 million years ago and forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island The end of volcanic activity associated with the

Koolau Volcano was followed by a long period of landmass erosion whereby deep stream
valleys were carved into the dome shaped Koolau Shield Volcano
The project site is located near the southeastern flank of the Aliamanu vent and
Salt Lake vent complex of the Honolulu Volcanic Series part of the Koolau Volcano As
the molten magma ascended through these vents it reacted with the groundwater
generating steam This steam caused explosive eruptions which deposited low cones of
ash material that have consolidated to form volcanic tuff locally referred to as mudrock
The Aliamanu tuff is an older water laid ash deposit that was laid down when the
sea level was higher than the present level The Aliamanu eruptive event deposited its
material over alluvium originating from Halawa Stream After this phase of volcanic activity
ceased sedimentary deposits accumulated over the Aliamanu tuff before the next phase
of volcanic activity began Then the Salt Lake eruptions began when the sea level was at
about 40 to 60 feet below its present level The Salt Lake eruptive event deposited its
material over earlier tuffs generated from the older Aliamanu events

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Structural loading information for the solar shade structures were not available at
the time this report was prepared Based on the limited available space and assumptions
of the foundation load demand we envision that the solar shade structures will be
supported on cast in place concrete drilled shafts A small reinforced concrete slab with
thickened edge footings may also be required to support solar equipment and
accessories Detailed discussions and recommendations for foundation design are
presented in the following sections
Drilled Shaft Foundations
In order to develop the required bearing and lateral load resistances the proposed
new solar shade structures may be supported by a foundation system consisting of
vertical support principally from skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding soils
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we recommend using an ultimate skin
friction of 2 0 kips per square foot ksf for the weathered volcanic tuff encountered at the
site to determine the ultimate compressive load capacity A factor of safety of 2 0 should
be used to determine the allowable compressive load capacity of the cast in place drilled
shafts The allowable compressive load capacity is for supporting dead plus live loads
and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind
or seismic forces
Uplift loads imposed on the drilled shafts may be resisted by a combination of the
dead weight of the drilled shaft and shear along the shaft surface and the adjacent soils
An ultimate uplift skin friction of for the weathered volcanic tuff may be used to
achieve the uplift load capacity for transient loading The uplift load capacities should not
be further increased for transient load applications For sustained uplift loads we
recommend reducing the uplift load capacity by 50 percent to resist sustained uplift loads
The project structural engineer should check the capacity of the drilled shaft in tension
Lateral loads imposed on the solar shade structures may be resisted by the lateral
load capacity of the drilled shaft The lateral load resistance of the drilled shaft is a
function of the stiffness of the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable
deflection at the top of the shaft and the induced moment in the shaft
For rigid shafts the lateral resistance due to passive earth pressure may be
estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of
depth pcf For flexible shafts a coefficient of lateral subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per
square inch per inch pci of deflection may be used In general the drilled shafts should
have a minimum center to center spacing of 8 0 times the shaft pile diameter to avoid
reduction in the lateral load capacity of the shaft pile
The finish grades at the project site should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding This drainage requirement is essential

Construction Considerations
Drilled shafts are desirable for the solar shade structure foundations because of the
significant increase in lateral and uplift load capacities when compared to shallow
foundations However the performance of the drilled shafts will depend significantly upon
the contractor s method of construction and construction procedures In our opinion the
following conditions would have an impact on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft
foundation
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in drilling the
shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes
A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 6 to 9 inch range should be used to
provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soil The concrete
should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce the potential for segregation of the
aggregates from the concrete mix
A Geolabs representative should be present at the project site to monitor the drilling
and installation of drilled shafts during construction to confirm the assumed subsurface
conditions and should be designated as a Special Inspection item in accordance with
Section 1704 of the International Building Code 2003 Edition
Equipment Pad
We understand that a small reinforced concrete slab with thickened edge footings
may be required to support solar equipment and accessories In general we believe that
an allowable bearing pressure of up to about may be used for the design of the
concrete slab The bottom of the concrete slab should be embedded a minimum of
24 inches below the lowest adjacent finish grade bearing on the weathered volcanic tuff
formation or compacted fill The weathered volcanic tuff formation is generally anticipated
at depths of about 2 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface This bearing value is for
those caused by wind or seismic forces
Lateral loads acting on the reinforced concrete pad may be resisted by friction
developed between the bottom of the pad and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 35 may be used for footings bearing on the weathered volcanic tuff formation
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of about 300 pcf This assumes that the soils around the footings are well

Utility Trench
We envision that new electrical duct lines will be required to service the proposed
new solar electric system In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of clean
sand or No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the
duct lines If soft loose soils are encountered at or near the invert of the pipes a
stabilization layer consisting of an additional 18 inches of open graded gravel wrapped in a
non woven filter fabric Mirafi 180N or equivalent should be provided below the bedding
layer for uniform support
Free draining granular materials such as clean sand or No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to
about 6 inches above the duct lines to provide adequate support around the pipes and to
reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of pipe damage It
is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Improper backfill material and placement procedures could result in backfill settlement and
pipe damage
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 6 inches above the duct lines
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on site soils or imported select
granular fill material The backfill should be placed in maximum 6 inch level loose lifts
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and mechanically
compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future
ground subsidence Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content corresponding to
the maximum dry density

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to an average depth of
about 21 0 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring locations are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight augers and or rotary coring tools
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous full time basis These classifications were further reviewed visually
and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with
ASTM D 2487 Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1 Rock
materials were described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as
shown on the Rock Log Legend Plate A 0 2 Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some samples
were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D 1586
Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths The
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows
required for the specific sampler type used The blow counts may need to be factored to
