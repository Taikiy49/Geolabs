exploration performed for the proposed reservoir site construction at the West Hawaii
Sanitary Landfill site in North Kona on the island of Hawaii The general location and
vicinity of the site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is situated in the Puuanahulu area of the District of North Kona on
_ the island of Hawaii It is proposed to develop the site as a municipal sanitary landfill for
the disposal of solid waste As part of the project it is proposed to construct a reservoir
for potable water and supply for fire protection

sanitary landfill project site At the time of that exploration the site of the proposed
reservoir had not been established Therefore this portion of the project was not

the ground surface of the reservoir site

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling three
3 borings to a depth of about 15 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate locations of the three 3 borings designated as Boring and 11
are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 Boring Nos 1 through 8 were drilled previously and
were presented in our report dated
In general a surface layer of loose to medium dense basalt gravel and cobble
clinker ranging from one 1 to three feet 3 in thickness was encountered during our
field exploration Below this surface layer massive basalt rock was encountered to the
maximum depth explored
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings during our field exploration

Foundation
We understand that the planned water tank is about 32 feet in diameter and
perimeter ring foundations will be utilized for support of the structure In consideration
of the generally competent subsurface conditions encountered at the site we believe that
shallow footings may be used to support the proposed water tank
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 4 000 pounds per square foot p s f may
be used for the design of footing foundation bearing on the in situ basalt rock formation
dense rockfill or compacted clinker in level areas This bearing value is for dead plus live
loads and may be increased by up to one third for loads of short duration such as
those caused by wind or seismic forces
in order to provide uniform support for the foundation and to reduce the potential
for foundation distress resulting from differential subgrade conditions between the massive
basalt rock and rock fill the portion of the footings in massive basalt rock should be over
excavated 12 inches below the footing subgrade level backfilled with similar rock fill
material in 8 inch loose horizontal lifts and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent of the
maximum dry density determined in accordance with procedures described in ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91
Footings should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent
finished grade
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
developed against the embedded near vertical faces of the foundation system and by
frictional resistance developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing

Utility Pipe Installation
It is anticipated that underground utility pipes will be installed as part of the water
reservoir site development Based on the results of our field exploration we anticipate
relatively hard excavation conditions will be encountered for trenching where the utility line
is located in massive basalt rock
In general the loose to medium dense clinker materials encountered at shallow
depths below the surface of the site would tend to unravel or slough into open trench
excavations and may require minimal shoring or sloping back the trench walls It is our
opinion that sloping of the trenches in clinker formations to temporary slopes of about
one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V would provide adequate stability for utility
installation The side slopes of temporary utility trenches in massive basalt rock may be
steepened

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling 3
borings to a depth of about 15 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were
drilled by a truck mounted rotary drill rig advancing continuous flight augers or rotary core
drilling tools as appropriate The logs of the borings are shown on Plates A 1 through A 3
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination description and
evaluation
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks such as if 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0
foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring Logs as
the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
Very Poor 0 25
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100
The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its
relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense basalt with a high RQD
would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous methods of
excavation
