ANTICIPATED SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Based on available information and our experience in the area, we anticipate that
the subsurface conditions at the project site are quite different from the typical conditions
found elsewhere in the Waikiki area. It should be noted that the coral ledge that is typically
encountered at depths of about 35 to 50 feet below the ground level in the Waikiki area is
likely absent at the northern portion (along Ala Wai Boulevard) of the project site due to
erosion by an ancient alluvial stream channel (known as Old Ala Wai Drainage) as shown
on Plates 2 and 3 of this report.
The buried ancient alluvial stream channel generally is oriented (longitudinally) in a
northeast-southwest direction. We understand that the buried alluvial stream channel may
be on the order of about 200 to 300 feet wide. The buried ancient alluvial stream channel
appears to be flanked by a relatively shallower coralline shelf at about 35 to 50 feet
depths. The coral ledge within the channel has been eroded away and has been replaced
with thick sequences of alluvial soils extending to depths of about 110 to 180 feet below
the existing ground surface, or more. Surface fills blanket the site at the ground surface
and are underlain by highly compressible lagoonal soils extending to the coral shelf or the
alluvial sediments of the ancient alluvial stream channel. Groundwater should be
anticipated at depths of about 3 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface.
Due to the low ground elevation, urban setting, and proximity to Ala Wai Canal and
Waikiki Beach, we anticipate that the subsurface conditions at the site may pose
significant challenges for the design and construction of the proposed development. At
this time, we anticipate that the presence of potentially liquefiable soils and its impact on
the proposed development may need significant geotechnical engineering considerations.
Dewatering during excavation and construction of the elevator pits and other below-grade
structures should also be considered to meet the pristine water quality required for
disposal into State waters. Depending on the final design concept, groundwater control
measures, such as jet grouting, soil mixing or other methods, may need to be taken into
consideration in the project development.

Liquefaction Potential
Based on the 1997 Uniform Building Code (UBC) (current code adopted by the City
and County of Honolulu), the project site may be subjected to seismic activity and the
potential for soil liquefaction at the project site will need to be evaluated. Based on the
anticipated subsurface conditions and a design peak ground acceleration of 0.15g,
liquefaction of the loose lagoonal deposits at the site may occur resulting in appreciable
settlements at the ground surface. Therefore, the effects of potential liquefaction at the
site will need to be taken into consideration in the design of the development.
In general, the site is anticipated to be underlain by soft and/or loose lagoonal
deposits overlying stiff alluvial soils and medium dense coralline detritus deposits at
greater depths. The underlying alluvial soil deposits and medium dense coralline detritus
at the project site generally are not highly susceptible to liquefaction. However, the
lagoonal deposits consisting of loose silty coralline sands and gravel with little cohesion
are potentially liquefiable during a seismic event of Magnitude 6 with an associated peak
ground acceleration of 0.15g.
Therefore, the project site could be subjected to appreciable ground settlements in
the event of liquefaction during a moderate earthquake (M6+) with a peak ground
acceleration of 0.15g. Based on the relatively flat site topography, we believe that the
potential for lateral spreading associated with liquefaction at the site would not be a
significant design consideration for the project. These seismic related issues should be
further evaluated and potential mitigation measures should be presented for
implementation during the design and construction of the project.
Soil Profile Type for Seismic Design
As previously indicated, the subsurface conditions at the project site will likely
consist of surface fills (up to about 5 feet thick) over loose lagoonal deposits (25 to 35 feet
thick) overlying alluvial deposits and medium dense to dense coralline detritus extending
to over 100 feet below the ground surface. Based on the geology of the area and the
subsurface materials anticipated within the upper 100 feet of depth, the project site will
likely be classified as a “Soft Soil Profile.” Therefore, the seismic design of the building
structures at the project site likely would need to be designed based on a Soil Profile Type
Se in accordance with Table 16-J of the Uniform Building Code (1997).
Based on the subsurface conditions at the site and the type of development
proposed, we believe that a site-specific seismic response evaluation should be conducted
for the project to assist the structural engineer in the seismic design of the structure. The
seismic response evaluation should include (1) seismic shear wave velocity profiling using
Cone Penetrometer Tests (CPT) and (2) performance of a site-specific response spectrum
analysis.

FINDINGS AND DISCUSSIONS
Based on our past project experience in the area and review of the available
information, we believe that the proposed project is generally feasible from a geotechnical
engineering point-of-view. However, we anticipate that the subsurface conditions at the
site may pose significant challenges for the design and construction of the proposed
development. At this time, we anticipate that the significant geotechnical engineering
considerations at this site may include, but are not limited to, the presence of potentially
liquefiable soils and its impact on the proposed development and the dewatering
considerations and their impacts on the surrounding properties. To a lesser degree, the
loose and highly compressible materials commonly associated with lagoonal environments
also may have an impact on the design and construction of this project. The potential
impacts of these significant geotechnical considerations, as they relate to the design and
construction of the proposed development, are discussed in the following subsections:
Seismic Design Considerations
Foundation Considerations
Ground Settlement
Dewatering of Below-Grade Excavations
Seismic Design Considerations
As mentioned previously, the project site is likely underlain by potentially liquefiable
soils. Therefore, the effects of a potential seismic event will need to be taken into
consideration in the design of the new condominium project planned at the project site.
We recommend that a site-specific seismic response evaluation, including (1) seismic
shear wave velocity profiling using CPT and (2) a site-specific response spectrum
analysis, be conducted for the seismic design of the high-rise structure.
Foundation Considerations
Based on the highly compressible lagoonal deposits anticipated at the project site
and the significant structural demands on the high-rise condominium foundations, we
believe that a deep foundation support system, such as driven pile foundations or drilled
shaft foundations, would be required for support of the proposed structure. In general, we
understand that driven pile foundations are not as desirable in the Waikiki area due to
concerns (by the adjacent residents) of the noise and vibrations generated during the pile
installation process. In addition, due to the presence of the buried alluvial stream channel
and the high structural loads of the building structure, we believe that drilled shaft
foundations may be the preferred foundation support system for the development.
Therefore, evaluation of the feasibility of both foundation alternatives to support the
proposed building structure should be conducted for the development.

In general, we believe that each column location for the building structure may be |
supported by one drilled shaft to reduce the size of the footing at each column location.
Based on the anticipated structural loads (on the order of about 2,000 to 3,000 kips per
column), the proposed building structure likely would be supported by a combination of 36,
42, and 48-inch diameter cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts, depending on the vertical
and lateral load demands on the foundations. The drilled shafts would extend through the
loose and/or soft lagoonal deposits and would embed into the stiff alluvial soils and
medium dense coralline detritus deposits. The drilled shafts would derive vertical support
primarily from adhesion between the shaft and surrounding soils. Based on preliminary
analyses, drilled shafts on the order of about 100 to 150 feet deep would be required to
develop allowable compressive loads of about 2,000 to 3,000 kips per drilled shaft to
support the structural loads anticipated at the column locations of the proposed building
structure.
It should be noted that we envision potentially difficult drilling conditions would be
encountered during installation of the drilled shafts due to the presence of the loose
granular soils and shallow groundwater levels anticipated at the project site. Therefore,
temporary casing of the drilled shaft holes will be required (especially through the upper
loose and/or soft lagoonal deposits) to reduce the potential for caving-in of the holes
during the drilling operation.
In addition, the potential for presence of the coral ledge (outside the buried alluvial
stream channel) at portions of the site also poses some construction difficulties for the
drilled shaft installation. Therefore, to reduce the impact of these construction difficulties,
we anticipate that the temporary casing should be embedded through lagoonal deposits
(and the coral ledge, if present). Furthermore, placement of concrete by tremie methods
will be required for the drilled shafts because groundwater will be encountered within the
depths of the drilled shafts.
For the driven pile foundation alternative, we anticipate that 16.5-inch octagonal,
precast prestressed concrete piles with a preliminary pile length on the order of about
130 to 150 feet long would be required to achieve an allowable compressive load capacity
of about 100 to 120 tons per pile. Due to the potential presence of an upper coral ledge in
localized area of the project site, we believe that predrilling extending to depths on the
order of about 50 feet below the ground surface in selected areas may be required to
facilitate the pile driving operations through the upper coral ledge. A detailed field
exploration program will be required to delineate the lateral extent of the buried alluvial
stream channel.

Ground Settlement
We anticipate that new fills may be required to raise the existing ground surface to
achieve the design finished grades at the site. Due to the presence of loose and/or soft
lagoonal deposits underlying the site at shallow depths, we believe that the new fills will
need to be placed as soon as practical to induce the anticipated ground settlements to
occur prior to construction of structures and pavements. Settlement monitoring should be
established to evaluate the magnitude and rate of the estimated settlements during the
settlement waiting period prior to construction of the foundations over the fills. If the
settlement monitoring period is observed and implemented for the project, it is possible to
support the ground floor slab of the building on-grade.
Dewatering of Below-Grade Excavations
Due to the shallow groundwater levels anticipated at the site, dewatering of the
planned excavations, such as the elevator pits and other below-grade structures, will be
required for the project construction. Because of the problems associated with dewatering
excavations in the Waikiki area, special attention should be given to the effects of
dewatering on the adjacent properties and beyond. In addition, disposal of the dewatered
effluent during construction of the below-grade structures also would be problematic
because the dewatered effluent will need to meet the pristine water quality required for
disposal into State waters.
Based on our initial evaluation of the dewatering requirements of projects in the
Waikiki area, we believe that the use of soil stabilization by the jet-grouting method or
similar methods may need to be implemented for groundwater control for the deeper
excavations, if planned. In addition, the excavation support system for the below-grade
construction will have a significant effect on the dewatering considerations of the project.
The selected shoring system for the below-grade excavations will need to be carefully
evaluated to reduce the potential impacts of the shoring on the adjacent properties and
travelways.
FIELD EXPLORATION PROGRAM
The geotechnical engineering exploration should be directed at evaluating the
presence of potentially liquefiable soils anticipated at the project site, which will affect the
design and construction of the proposed development. In addition, relatively “undisturbed”
samples should be obtained for laboratory consolidation tests to evaluate the consolidation
characteristics of the lagoonal deposits for settlement analyses.
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions and the significant foundation
demands of the high-rise building, we believe that boreholes extending to depths of
approximately 180 feet below the existing ground surface would be required to evaluate
the subsurface materials for foundation design.

Due to the shallow groundwater levels anticipated at the site, dewatering of the
planned excavations, such as the elevator pits and other below-grade structures, will be
required for the project construction. Therefore, we recommend that groundwater
monitoring wells be installed during the field exploration program for the performance of
field permeability tests, collection of groundwater samples for National Pollutant
Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) permit application, and groundwater level
monitoring purposes.
In order to conduct a site-specific seismic response evaluation, we recommend that
the field exploration include the use of Cone Penetrometer Tests (CPT) with seismic shear
wave velocity profiling. The CPT method involves inserting a sensitive probe into the soil
at a constant speed. Various sensors embedded in the probe continuously measure the
tip resistance, side friction, and induced pore pressures as the probe is systematically
pushed through the soil. In general, subsurface explorations consisting of CPT probes
yield more accurate results for highly sensitive materials, such as the loose granular soils,
because the data is obtained directly from the in-situ soils. Therefore, CPT with seismic
shear wave velocity profiling is considered to be the industry standard for higher order
analysis, such as the seismic response evaluation, especially in the anticipated subsurface
conditions at the project site.

to over 100 feet below the ground surface. Based on the geology of the area and the
subsurface materials anticipated within the upper 100 feet of depth, the project site will
likely be classified as a “Soft Soil Profile.” Therefore, the seismic design of the building
structures at the project site likely would need to be designed based on a Soil Profile Type
Se in accordance with Table 16-J of the Uniform Building Code (1997).
Based on the subsurface conditions at the site and the type of development
proposed, we believe that a site-specific seismic response evaluation should be conducted
for the project to assist the structural engineer in the seismic design of the structure. The
seismic response evaluation should include (1) seismic shear wave velocity profiling using
Cone Penetrometer Tests (CPT) and (2) performance of a site-specific response spectrum
analysis.

information, we believe that the proposed project is feasible from a geotechnical
engineering point-of-view. We envision that the geotechnical engineering aspects of this

Waikiki/Ala Moana areas, such as the Ko’olani, the Lanikea, and Moana Pacific projects

Seismic Design Considerations
As mentioned previously, the project site is likely underlain by potentially liquefiable
soils. Therefore, the effects of a potential seismic event will need to be taken into
consideration in the design of the new condominium project planned at the site. We
recommend that a site-specific seismic response evaluation, including (1) seismic shear
wave velocity profiling using CPT and (2) a site-specific response spectrum analysis, be
conducted for the seismic design of the high-rise structure. We anticipate that deep
foundations may be utilized to mitigate the liquefaction concerns.
Foundation Considerations
Based on the compressible lagoonal deposits anticipated at the project site and the
significant structural demands on the high-rise condominium foundations, we believe that
a deep foundation support system, such as driven pile foundations or drilled shaft
foundations, would be required for support of the proposed structure. We envision that
these deep foundation support systems, on the order of about 100 to 150 feet deep, would
be similar to the designs for high-rise buildings in the Waikiki/Ala Moana areas. Evaluation
of the feasibility of driven pile and drilled shaft foundation alternatives to support the
proposed building structure should be conducted for the development.
In general, we believe that each column location for the building structure may be
supported by one drilled shaft to reduce the size of the footing at each column location.
Based on the anticipated structural loads (on the order of about 2,000 to 3,000 kips per
column), the proposed building structure likely would be supported by a combination of 36,
42, and 48-inch diameter cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts, depending on the vertical
and lateral load demands on the foundations. The drilled shafts would extend through the
loose and/or soft lagoonal deposits and would embed inio the stiff alluvial soils and
medium dense coralline detritus deposits. The drilled shafts would derive vertical support
primarily from adhesion between the shaft and surrounding soils. Based on preliminary
analyses, drilled shafts on the order of about 100 to 150 feet deep would be required to
develop allowable compressive loads of about 2,000 to 3,000 kips per drilled shaft to
support the structural loads anticipated at the column locations of the proposed building
structure.
It should be noted that difficult drilling conditions may be encountered during
installation of the drilled shafts due to the presence of the loose granular soils and shallow
groundwater levels anticipated at the site. To reduce the potential for caving-in of the
drilled holes, temporary casing of the drilled holes or the use of drilling fluids may be

Ground Settlement
Based on the existing topography at the site, we anticipate that minimal site grading
work will be required to achieve the design finished grades at the site. Due to the
presence of loose and/or soft lagoonal deposits underlying the site at shallow depths, we
believe that new fills, if required, should be placed as soon as practical to induce the
anticipated ground settlements to occur prior to construction of structures and pavements.
Settlement monitoring should be established to evaluate the magnitude and rate of the
estimated settlements during the settlement waiting period prior to construction of the
foundations over the fills.
Dewatering of Below-Grade Excavations
Dewatering is not anticipated since a basement level is not planned. However,
some dewatering may be required during excavation and construction of the elevator pits
and other below-grade structures. Special attention should be given to the effects of
dewatering on the adjacent properties and beyond.
Based on our initial evaluation of the dewatering requirements of projects in the
Waikiki area, we believe that the use of soil stabilization by the jet-grouting method or
similar methods may need to be implemented for groundwater control for the deeper
excavations, if planned. In addition, the excavation support system for the below-grade
construction will have a significant effect on the dewatering considerations of the project.
The selected shoring system for the below-grade excavations will need to be carefully
evaluated to reduce the potential impacts of the shoring on the adjacent properties and
travelways.

FIELD EXPLORATION PROGRAM
The geotechnical engineering exploration should be directed at evaluating the
presence of potentially liquefiable soils anticipated at the project site, which will affect the
design and construction of the proposed development. In addition, relatively “undisturbed”
samples should be obtained for laboratory consolidation tests to evaluate the consolidation
characteristics of the lagoonal deposits for settlement analyses.
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions and the significant foundation
demands of the high-rise building, we believe that boreholes extending to depths of
approximately 180 feet below the existing ground surface would be required to evaluate
the subsurface materials for foundation design.
Due to the shallow groundwater levels anticipated at the site, dewatering of the
planned excavations, such as the elevator pits and other below-grade structures, will be
required for the project construction. Therefore, we recommend that groundwater
monitoring wells be installed during the field exploration program for the performance of
field permeability tests, collection of groundwater samples for National Pollutant
Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) permit application, and groundwater level
monitoring purposes.
In order to conduct a site-specific seismic response evaluation, we recommend that
the field exploration include the use of Cone Penetrometer Tests (CPT) with seismic shear
wave velocity profiling. The CPT method involves inserting a sensitive probe into the soil
at a constant speed. Various sensors embedded in the probe continuously measure the
tip resistance, side friction, and induced pore pressures as the probe is systematically
pushed through the soil. In general, subsurface explorations consisting of CPT probes
yield more accurate results for highly sensitive materials, such as the loose granular soils,
because the data is obtained directly from the in-situ soils. Therefore, CPT with seismic
shear wave velocity profiling is considered to be the industry standard for higher order
analysis, such as the seismic response evaluation, especially in the anticipated subsurface
conditions at the project site.
