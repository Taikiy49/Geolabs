This letter report summarizes our geotechnical engineering recommendations

this letter report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located at a recently constructed parking lot in the 
southeastern end of the Leeward Community College campus in the Pearl City area on 
the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. 
Based on the information provided, the asphalt concrete paved parking lot has 
experienced substantial settlement up to about 1.7 feet as well as horizontal separation 
along the concrete curb and gutter. The distressed asphalt pavements were observed 
generally along the south and southwest side of the parking lot. Based on our field 
observations and the existing topography, it appears that the distressed pavements may 
be supported on about 5 to 10 feet of fill materials placed during the parking lot 
construction. 
We understand that it is desired to evaluate the probable cause of the pavement 
distresses and formulate geotechnical engineering recommendations for the remedial 
design.

SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age. As the volcanic activity in Waianae Volcano ceased, lava flows 
from Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau, 
now known as the Schofield Plateau. The project site is on the southwestern flank of the 
Waianae Volcano. 
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of Schofield Plateau 
generated sediments, which were transported to the coast. The lava flows on the plateau 
have weathered to depths of about 50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the red colors 
of the soil. The deep narrow canyons, such as Waikakalua, Kipapa, and Kaukonahua, are 
a result of this weathering. The brown and reddish brown soils generally consist of ancient 
alluvial deposits overlying the weathered rock. The project site is located on the southern 
side of the Schofield Plateau, north of the Pearl City Peninsula, which is located between 
Middle and East Lochs. 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling five borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, extending to depths of about 10.5 to 16.5 feet 
below the existing pavement surface. In addition, one bulk sample of the near-surface 
soils, designated as Bulk-1, was obtained to evaluate the swelling potential and 
pavement support characteristics of the near-surface soils. The approximate boring and 
bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our borings generally encountered a pavement structure consisting of about 
4 inches of asphaltic concrete and about 5 inches of base course material. Three 
borings (B-1 through B-3) drilled along the southern edge of the parking lot 
(where pavement distresses were observed) encountered about 7 to 12 feet of loose to 
medium dense fill materials. Two borings (B-4 and B-5) located about 30 to 35 feet 
north of the southern edge of the parking lot encountered about 7 feet of medium stiff 
clayey fill materials. Underlying the fill materials, alluvial deposit consisting of medium 
stiff clayey soils were encountered and extended to the maximum depth explored of 
about 16.5 feet below the existing pavement surface.  
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings drilled at the time of our field 
exploration. However, groundwater levels may change due to seasonal precipitation, 
surface water runoff, and other factors.

Based on the pattern of pavement distresses and the results of our field 
exploration program, we speculate that the pavement settlement and lateral movement 
may be caused primarily by improper fill placement and compaction, resulting in

discussions and geotechnical engineering recommendations for the remedial design of 
the parking lot stabilization are presented in the following subsections. 
Parking Lot Stabilization 
Based on the observed pavement distresses, settlement, and slope instability, 
we recommend removal of the existing loose fill materials and replacement with a 
stabilization layer along the entire southern edge of the parking lot. The stabilization 
layer should consist of 3 feet of select granular fills sandwiched with layers of reinforcing 
geogrid, such as Tensar TX160 or equivalent, placed at every 12-inch vertical spacing. 
In order to reduce the potential for slope instability, it is our opinion that the limits 
of the stabilization layer should extend into the adjacent competent grounds.  Based on 
our evaluation, we recommend that the stabilization layer be extended a minimum 
horizontal distance of approximately 30 feet from the top of slope along the southern 
edge of the existing parking lot.  In addition, the stabilization layer should extend to a 
minimum depth of 3 feet below the bottom of the pavement section and laterally out to 
the slope face. 
The over-excavated subgrade should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and recompacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place 
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as 
determined by ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
One layer of reinforcing geogrid should be placed on top of the recompacted 
subgrade overlain by approximately 12 inches of select granular fill material.  This 
should be followed by the 2nd and 3rd installments of the reinforcing geogrid and 12-inch 
compacted select granular fill.  Lastly, the pavement structure may be constructed on 
top of the 3rd layer of select granular fill, matching the adjacent existing parking lot 
pavement section.  It should be noted that adjacent rolls of reinforcing geogrid should 
be overlapped a minimum length of 12 inches.

Site Preparation 
We anticipate that the majority of the site grading work will involve excavations 
and backfilling of the upper 3 feet of existing fill materials below the pavement structural 
section.  In general, a Geolabs representative should monitor the site preparation work 
to confirm whether the exposed soil conditions during excavation are similar to those 
anticipated and/or encountered in our field exploration. The following recommendations 
are intended to provide guidelines for the site preparation work. 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
thoroughly cleared and grubbed. Vegetation, debris, existing pavements, deleterious 
materials, and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly 
off-site. 
In general, the on-site granular materials generated from the excavations may be 
reused as a source of non-expansive select granular material provided they meet the 
material requirements presented below. The on-site clayey soils should not be reused 
as a source of fill material for the stabilization layer and should be disposed of properly 
off-site.  Care should be exercised during the excavation process to reduce the potential 
for contamination of the granular materials from the adjacent and underlying clayey 
soils. 
Select granular fill required for the stabilization layer should consist of 
non-expansive select granular materials, such as crushed coral or basalt. The material 
should be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in 
largest dimension. The material also should contain between 10 and 30 percent 
particles passing the No. 200 sieve. In addition, the material should have a laboratory 
California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell 
of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. Imported fill 
materials should be tested for conformance with these recommendations prior to 
delivery to the project site for the intended use. 
The non-expansive, select granular fill material should be moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction. Compaction should be

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling five borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, extending to depths of about 10.5 to 16.5 feet 
below the existing pavement surface. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted 
drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.  Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-5. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in 
general accordance with ASTM  D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample.  Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits.  The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.  Graphic presentation of the test 
results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Three Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distributions is provided on Plate B-2. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested.  The test results are 
presented on Plates B-3 and B-4. 
Two consolidation tests with time rates (ASTM D 2435) were performed on 
selected samples of the existing fill materials to evaluate the compressibility 
characteristics of the materials encountered.  Results of the consolidation tests are 
presented on Plates B-5 and B-6. 
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test (ASTM D1883) was performed on a 
bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plate B-7. 
One Modified Proctor compaction test (ASTM D1557C) was performed on a bulk 
sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships. The test results are presented on Plate B-8.
