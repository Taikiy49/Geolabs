INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Upgrade of Underground Fuel Storage Tank project at the Waimanalo
Wastewater Treatment Plant located in Waimanalo on the island of Oahu Hawaii The
general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommenda
tions resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses
These recommendations are intended for design input of foundation site grading and
excavation only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to
the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
We understand that it is proposed to install a new underground fuel storage tank
at the Waimanalo Wastewater Treatment Plant in the district of Koolaupoko on the
island of Oahu Hawaii Based on the information provided to us we understand that
the proposed new underground fuel storage tank will be a fiberglass tank The new
tank will have plan dimensions of approximately 20 feet long by 6 feet wide and 5 to
6 feet high At the time this report was prepared there are two proposed tank locations
as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Detailed information for the project was not available at the time this report was
prepared Based on the available information we understand that the new under
ground fuel storage tank will be embedded about 3 to 4 feet below the existing ground
surface Therefore to install the new underground fuel storage tank an excavation on
the order of about 8 to 10 feet below the existing ground surface will be required
GEOLABS HAWAI

In addition based on our previous experience in the area the project site may
be underlain by stiff clayey fill soils over soft loose sandy deposits at relatively shallow
depths Therefore we anticipate that shoring and dewatering of the excavation for the
new underground fuel storage tank may be needed for the proposed construction

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated
to be of the middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be of the late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age When the building of the Waianae and Koolau
volcanoes came to an end only a slight embayment existed on the southern coast of
Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
GEOLABS HAWAIE
W O 3364 00 A Page 5
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of
the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available
such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition caused the top of the basaltic lava
flows to be highly weathered and mantled by residual soils During the Kaena stand
of the sea level at approximately 90 feet MSL of the Pleistocene Epoch a delta of
silt sand and gravel built out into the embayment near the project site Agricultural
land development and reclamation projects within the last century have brought the
project site to its present form
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The proposed underground fuel storage tank project is located within the
Waimanalo Wastewater Treatment Plant in the District of Koolaupoko on the island of
Oahu The location of the proposed tank had not been finalized at the time this report
was prepared The two possible locations provided are located to the north and west
of the existing generator building as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The two possible tank locations are presently vacant Based on our field
observations and a review of the topographic map prepared by ControlPoint Surveying
and Engineering Inc the topography of the site is relatively level with existing ground
surface elevations of approximately 12 to 13 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsoil conditions at the subject site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings to depths of about 36 and 41 feet below the existing ground surface
The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 At the
GEOLABS HAWAI

Therefore two borings were drilled around the possible tank locations to accommodate

In general our field exploration encountered near surface clayey fills consisting
of very stiff silty clays to depths of about 7 to 10 feet below the existing ground surface
Underlying the near surface fills loose to medium dense silty sands were generally
encountered to depths of about 18 to 18 feet below the existing ground surface
Below the sands our borings encountered alluvial soils consisting of very stiff clayey
silts with sands and gravels extending to the maximum depths drilled of approximately
36 and 41 feet below the existing ground surface
Groundwater was encountered in the two borings at depths of about 10 and
12 feet below the ground surface at the time of our field exploration These
groundwater levels correspond to elevations of about 0 and 1 8 feet MSL It should
be noted that groundwater levels measured in the borings during our field exploration
will likely be affected by tidal fluctuations In addition the groundwater levels at the
project site may also be influenced by seasonal precipitation storm surge conditions
and other factors

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
In general our field exploration indicates that the proposed project site is
underlain by near surface clayey fills over loose sandy soils overlying very stiff clayey
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3364 00 A Page 7
silts to the maximum depths drilled of about 36 to 41 feet below the existing ground
surface Groundwater was encountered at about 10 and 12 feet below the ground
surface at the time of our field exploration
We understand that the top of the proposed tank structure will be embedded
about 3 to 4 feet below the existing ground surface Therefore construction of the
underground fuel storage tank will require an excavation on the order of about 10 feet
below the ground surface Because groundwater was encountered at about 10 feet
below the surface it may be prudent to design the underground fuel storage tank for
hydrostatic uplift pressures due to groundwater Therefore if the tank will be designed
to resist hydrostatic uplift forces the tank should be supported on a mat foundation
If the tank will not be designed for hydrostatic uplift forces the tank may be supported
on a 2 foot thick gravel or sand bedding wrapped in a filter fabric
Because some loose subsoils were encountered some movement of soils should
be anticipated due to changes in the earth stresses during and after construction
Therefore we recommend that some flexibility in the connection of the piping network
to the underground fuel storage tank be incorporated in the design to accommodate
possible earth movements
Due to the relatively deep excavation the presence of loose soil layer and the
relatively confined area we believe that shoring of the excavation during construction
may be needed Dewatering of the excavation may or may not be necessary
depending on the fluctuations in the groundwater level and the depth of the excavation
Detailed discussions and recommendations for design of foundation site
grading excavation and shoring and other geotechnical aspects of the project are
presented in the following sections of this report

Foundations
As previously mentioned the underground fuel storage tank may be supported
on a minimum 2 foot thick gravel or sand bedding wrapped in a filter fabric such as
Mirafi 140N or equivalent The gravel layer should consist of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation or similar material Ifa sand bedding is used the sand
should be compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91
If the underground fuel storage tank will be designed for hydrostatic uplift forces
we recommend that the tank be supported on a mat foundation An allowable bearing
pressure of up to 1 500 p s f may be used for the design of the concrete mat bearing
on the medium dense sands This allowable bearing pressure is for dead plus live
loads and may be increased by one third 14 for transient loads such as temporary
wind and or seismic forces
If soft loose subsoils are encountered near the foundation level it may be
necessary to provide a 2 foot gravel layer wrapped with a layer of filter fabric such as
Mirafi 140N or equivalent below the concrete mat for more uniform bearing support and
as a working platform for construction The fabric should be lapped a minimum of
24 inches at the end of each roll and along the sides The gravel layer should consist
of clean granular material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
Lateral loads acting on the tank structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the mat footing and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the foundation A coefficient of
friction of 0 35 may be used between the base of the footings and the supporting
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3364 00 A Page 9
materials Resistance due to passive pressure may be calculated using an equivalent
fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f above the
groundwater table and 150 p c f below the groundwater for footings embedded in the
in situ soils or compacted backfill These values assume that the concrete for footings
is placed directly against the footing excavations Unless covered by pavements or
slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below finished grade should be
neglected
Resistance to uplift loads may be mobilized by the dead weight of the tank
structure the concrete mat and the backfill material above the concrete mat
Contribution of dead weight from the backfill may be estimated using a unit weight of
evaluating uplift forces due to hydrostatic pressures a high groundwater level elevation
of 3 feet MSL may be used for design to take tidal fluctuations into consideration
Settlements of the new tank structure will result from the compression of the
soft loose subgrade soil layer due to the placement of the heavier backfill material We
estimate the total settlement of the tank structure to be on the order of 1 inch We
believe that most of the settlements should occur during the construction period as the
excavation is backfilled

We understand that additional fill placement is not planned for this project If fill
placement are planned at the site Geolabs Hawaii should be consulted to review
and or revise the recommendations presented herein The following recommendations
are intended to provide guidelines for the design of site grading work
1 At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits
should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris
rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed
of off site
2 The on site soils generated from the excavation may be reused as a
source of fill and backfill material However the on site soft and wet
clayey soils should not be reused as fill or backfill material
3 Imported fill and or backfill material should consist of well graded granular
materials of less than 3 inches in maximum dimension The material
should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 15 or
more and should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent when
tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 In addition
it should contain between 10 and 30 percent passing the No 200 sieve
Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for conformance
with our recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its
intended use In addition open graded material such as No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should not be used as backfill
material from the top of the concrete mat up to ground surface to reduce
the potential for migration of the surrounding fine soils into the backfill
material
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3364 00 A Page 11
4 All fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches
in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as
a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density Fill placement should commence at the lowest
point and continue upward in level compacted lifts
Excavation
In order to install the underground fuel storage tank we anticipate that an
excavation on the order of about 10 feet below the existing ground surface will be
required for the project construction In general the method and equipment to be used
for excavation should be determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and
safety considerations In general our field exploration encountered a near surface
clayey fill underlain by loose to medium dense sandy soils over stiff silty soils
Therefore we anticipate that conventional excavation techniques may be used for the
excavation with occasional hard horizons due to cobbles and boulders
Excavation Support
Because of the relatively deep excavation and the loose subsoil conditions
encountered in our exploration it is our opinion that shoring support such as
steel sheetpiles may be required for the project construction The temporary
shoring support should conform to the requirements of local state and federal
regulations

The selected excavation shoring system must comply with all applicable safety
requirements It is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be
retained by the contractor to design and evaluate the shoring system
The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be the sole
responsibility of the contractor His representative who should be required to
be continuously present on site during excavation and construction work will
have the best opportunity to promptly observe any changed conditions during
construction such as unforeseen subsoils unexpectedly high groundwater table
inappropriate construction sequences and or techniques all of which may affect
shoring stability
However it should be noted that some movement of the shoring system and the
adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during
excavation The magnitude of movement is difficult to accurately estimate due
to the complexity of the stress changes The magnitude depends greatly upon
GECLABS HAWAI
W O 3364 00 A Page 13
workmanship such as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports
are installed and also on the subsoil conditions the size of the excavation and
the rate of excavation
Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be
installed properly and as early as practical and that the adjacent ground be
continuously monitored for cracks dips and or other indications of movements
with instruments such as settlement markers and inclinometer Such monitoring
is particularly critical in areas adjacent to existing structures and utilities
We strongly recommend that the contractor retain a competent engineering
consultant to design and to coordinate the implementation of the excavation
support system where necessary
Utility Trench
We envision that a new piping network will be required for the project In
general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used
under the pipes Where soft and or loose soil deposits are encountered near the pipe
invert elevations we recommend that the utility pipe be supported on an additional
18 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation wrapped ina geotextile
fabric Mirafi 140N or equivalent The backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes
should consist of free draining backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the
potential for damaging the pipes from compaction of the backfill A typical section of
the trench detail is presented on Plate 4
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select granular material or

the approved on site soils The soft and or wet excavated soils should not be used as
trench backfill The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 2 feet
of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to not less
