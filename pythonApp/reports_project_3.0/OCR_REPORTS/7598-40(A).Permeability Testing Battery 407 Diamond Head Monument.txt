Dear Ms. Miyasato: 
This letter report presents the results of our permeability testing conducted for the 
Hawaii Department of Defense Battery 407, Underground Injection Control (UIC) project 
in the Diamond Head Crater area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and 
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes our field observations and presents the results of our 
permeability testing and engineering analyses for the project. These findings are intended 
to support the preparation of an Annual UIC Injection Well Status Report (2021) for 
Injection Well No. 1 at the project site. The findings and recommendations presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
Based on the information provided, the Hawaii Department of Defense Battery 407 
facility at the Diamond Head Monument operates a single injection well, identified as Well 
No. 1, for the on-site disposal of septic effluent. The Battery 407 facility is identified by 
UIC Facility I.D. No. 3-1548.01. The Injection Well No. 1 at the Battery 407 facility is 
regulated by UIC Permit No. UO-2883.  
We understand that observation and permeability testing services are desired in 
support of the preparation of an Annual UIC Injection Well Status Report (2021) for 
Well No. 1 at the Hawaii Army National Guard Battery 407 facility at the Diamond Head 
Monument.

EXISTING WELL AND SITE CONDITIONS 
The location of Well No. 1 is within the Diamond Head State Monument Park as 
approximately indicated on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. The well is located at the 
end of an existing access roadway and parking area designated for the Hawaii Army 
National Guard Battery 407 facility. The well is on generally level unpaved ground near 
the base of a hill slope. The ground surface adjacent to the well consists of soil mixed 
with some aggregate and is generally devoid of vegetation growth. Photograph 
Nos. 1 and 2 illustrate the general conditions surrounding the vicinity of the well head. 
The well head is generally constructed flush with the existing ground surface and 
consists of an approximately 30-inch square opening covered by a steel plate. The steel 
plate covering the well head opening is recessed about 2.5 inches below the surrounding 
concrete which supports the steel plate cover. The steel plate is rusted but remains in 
generally fair condition and appears to be structurally sound. It seems that the paint on 
the steel plate has deteriorated since the previous year. Photograph Nos. 3 and 4 present 
the existing condition of the well head.

We observed no existing aboveground appurtenances associated with the septic 
tank system and piping leading to the well. Based on the information provided by the 
owner/operator, there is no existing flow meter or valve control system associated with 
the well operation. 
We observed an existing steel access ladder mounted within the well. The ladder 
is severely corroded and appears to be weakened and unstable for use. We are unable 
to distinctively determine if the ladder is more corroded than the previous year. Upon 
opening the well cover, no noticeable odor was emanating from the well. 
We observed that the well interior was lined with Concrete Masonry Units (CMU) 
blocks with gap openings of about 2 inches wide in between the individual CMU blocks.  
The well floor was observed to be firm and unyielding, and appeared to consist of broken 
rock material and concrete debris. Sediment or other significant debris was not observed 
within the well. The well floor elevation is slightly uneven and ranges in measured depths 
between about 17.8 and 18.3 feet relative to the top of the concrete well head and the 
existing ground surface. Some broken slabs of concrete were present on the well floor 
below the effluent discharge line feeding the well. Photograph Nos. 5 and 6 show the 
existing well interior conditions. 
The integrity and uniformity of the existing well lining appear to be in good 
condition. However, we observed a localized crack or gap expressed as a vertically 
oriented break in the CMU lining that extends approximately 3 to 4 feet up from the well 
floor. The gap appears to be older in age and generally stable. No other structure 
deformation or indication of raveling loss of earth material from behind the lining was 
observed. 
A PVC effluent discharge pipe (estimated at 3 to 4 inches in diameter) was 
observed penetrating the well sidewall at a depth of about 4 feet below the existing ground 
surface. No liquid effluent was observed discharging from the pipe during our observation 
and testing efforts. No other fluid accumulation was observed within the well. An 
approximate 6-inch diameter clay pipe, believed to represent an older abandoned effluent 
discharge pipe, was also observed penetrating the well sidewall at a depth of about 6 feet 
below the existing ground surface. There was no fluid discharge observed from the clay 
pipe.

PERMEABILITY TESTING 
Our evaluation of the well permeability characteristics was conducted on 
September 7, 2021, between the hours of about 1000 and 1100 under dry weather and 
ground surface conditions. 
Permeability testing for Well No. 1 generally involved the short-term injection of 
clear water from a water tanker truck, followed by measurement of the falling head test in 
the well until the well drained to the pre-testing condition (dry). The permeability testing 
was conducted in an effort to evaluate the drainage (infiltration) functionality of the 
existing well. 
Prior to the start of the permeability test, no effluent was observed discharging into 
the well. We observed no fluid accumulation on the well floor. 
The test was initiated by discharging the contents of a 2,000-gallon capacity water 
tank under gravity feed through a 1.5-inch diameter hose placed in the lower portion of 
the well. Based on our measurements, approximately 1,100 gallons of clear water was 
discharged to the well from the water tanker over a period of about 30 minutes, resulting 
in an average discharge flow rate of about 36.7 gpm, with a total accumulated water head 
of about 1 foot above the bottom of the well. 
When the short-term injection discharge ended, a falling head test was performed 
to record the fall of the accumulated water level within the well until a dry well condition 
was achieved (pre-test condition). Based on the results of the falling head test, a time 
elapse of about 24 minutes was needed to completely drain the well to a dry (no head) 
condition. 
Based on the results of the short-term injection and falling head permeability test, 
the operation and capacity of the well appear to be very good, exceeding the required 
capacity for the existing facility needs.

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the results of our visual observations and the short-term permeability 
testing of Well No. 1 at the Battery 407 Diamond Head Monument facility, the well appears 
to be operating satisfactorily with no overt sign of potential failure. 
Based on the falling head permeability testing conducted, Well No. 1 has an 
effluent capacity of at least 36.7 gpm, equivalent to about 52,800 gallons per day (gpd), 
which exceeds the estimated design flow capacity of 3,400 gpd. A graph showing head 
versus time was recorded during the injection and percolation, as illustrated on Plate A of 
the Appendix.

Based on our observation of the existing well site and vicinity conditions, we 
observed the following conditions: 
The well cover was originally painted yellow to reduce the rate of corrosion; 
however, the paint has begun to deteriorate. Repainting the steel plate may 
be considered in the near future for maintenance. 
Warning signage is posted to avoid using the deteriorated well access 
ladder. 
A sediment containment sock had been placed at the ground surface 
around the upslope side of the well cover to reduce the potential for debris 
to wash onto the well head cover from the adjacent slope.  
Several painted steel bollards were installed to protect the well head from 
vehicle damage.
