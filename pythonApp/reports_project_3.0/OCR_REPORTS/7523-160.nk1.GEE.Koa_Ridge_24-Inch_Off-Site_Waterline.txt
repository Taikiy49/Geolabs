SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered residual soil consisting of stiff to hard 
silty clay extending to the maximum depth explored of about 21.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. Core stones were not observed during our drilling and sampling. 
However, sampler refusal conditions were encountered at depths between about 5 to 
17 feet below the existing ground surface. However, it should be noted that groundwater 
levels are subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface 
water runoff, and other factors. 
Excavations for this project will generally consist of trench excavations for 
underground waterline installation. We anticipate these excavations may encounter stiff 
to hard clayey residual soils in our borings. However, some of the excavations may 
encounter boulders and clusters of cobbles within the residual soils. It is anticipated that 
most of the material may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment. 
However, deep excavations and boulder excavations may require the use of hoerams. 
In general, the subsurface soils encountered during our field exploration may be 
used in the upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the pavement subgrade, provided that the materials are free of vegetation, deleterious 
materials, organic materials, and clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in 
maximum dimension.  
Based on the laboratory testing, the corrosion potential of the subsurface soils at 
the project site meets the Corrosion Category A criteria (moderately to severely 
corrosive). Therefore, we recommend designing the new waterline using Corrosion 
Category A based on the updated version of the Board of Water Supply, City and County 
of Honolulu Water System External Corrosion Control Standards. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
recommendations for the installation of the new waterlines.

Introduction

performed for the 24-Inch Off-Site Waterline project at the Koa Ridge Development in 
Waipio on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown 
on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering 
analyses for the project. These recommendations are intended for the design of trench 
excavation, backfilling, drainage structures, thrust block/vault design, site grading, and 
corrosion potential only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject 
to the limitations noted at the end of this report.

between Waipio and Mililani Town on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. We understand the

future roadways. Therefore, site grading activities for the waterline project will primarily

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. The Waianae Volcano became 
extinct while the Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried 
below the Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank. These banked or ponded lavas 
formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau. 
The project site is located within the Schofield Plateau. The Schofield Plateau was 
formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded against the already eroded slopes 
of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene Epoch. The dips of the lava beds are near 
horizontal (between 3 to 5 degrees from horizontal). The lava flows on the plateau have 
undergone in-situ weathering to depths of 50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the 
reddish color of the soil. 
This in-situ weathering of the lava has formed a mantle of residual soils that grade 
to saprolite with increasing depth. In general, saprolite is composed mainly of silty 
material, while residual soils are more clayey. Both residual and saprolitic soils are typical 
of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks. The residual and saprolitic soils grade to a 
basaltic rock formation with increased depth. Spheroidally weathered boulders within the 
residual and saprolitic soils are typical in the Schofield Plateau.

Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas

 Fills and Backfills 
In general, the on-site soils may be reused as a source of general fill material, 
provided they are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, and rock fragments 
greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension. Imported fill materials should consist 
of crushed basalt or coral.  
Non-expansive, select granular fill material should be well-graded from coarse to 
fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in the largest dimension and should 
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The 
material should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio value of 20 or more and 
should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested in accordance 
with ASTM D1883.

3.2 
Underground Waterline 
We understand that a 24-inch diameter waterline will be required for the project. In 
general, good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling 
of the trenches for the new utilities. The contractor should determine the method and 
equipment to be used for trench excavation, subject to practical limits and safety 
considerations. In addition, the excavations should comply with the applicable federal, 
state, and local safety requirements. The contractor should be responsible for trench 
shoring design and installation. 
In general, we recommend providing granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) under the pipes for uniform support. 
The granular bedding layer should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width of 
one-fourth (¼) of the outside pipe diameter or trench width, whichever is greater. 
Free-draining granular materials, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation), also should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above 
the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes. It is critical to use this 
free-draining material to reduce the potential for the formation of voids below the 
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper 
trench backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of excavating on-site soils or select 
granular fill material. The backfill should be placed in maximum 8-inch level loose lifts and 
mechanically compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the 
potential for appreciable future ground subsidence. The upper 3 feet below the finished 
grade in planned roadway areas should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in 
accordance with ASTM D1557.

3.3 
Underground Utility Structures 
Based on the information provided, we anticipate new underground utility structures, 
such as valve vault structures, will be required for the project. Based on our field exploration, 
we anticipate the new underground utility structures will be constructed in the stiff to hard 
residual soils and/or compacted general fills at the project site. Based on the subsurface 
conditions encountered, the following general guidelines may be used for the design of the 
drainage structures at the project site. 
 Underground Utility Structure Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, an allowable bearing pressure 
of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be utilized for design of the 
underground utility structures bearing on the stiff to hard residual soils and/or 
compacted general fills at the project site. To provide uniform bearing support for 
the new structures, we recommend providing a minimum 6-inch thick layer of No. 3 
Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) below the bottom of the drainage 
structures.  
Foundation subgrades should be recompacted to a firm and unyielding surface 
prior to the placement of the No. 3 Fine gravel. Soft and/or loose materials 
encountered at the bottom of the excavations should be over-excavated to expose 
the underlying firm materials. The over-excavation should be backfilled with select 
granular fill materials compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  
Based on our engineering analyses, we believe that the net increase in bearing 
pressure on the underlying stiff to hard residual soils and compacted general fills 
resulting from the underground utility structure construction would be minimal. 
Settlements on the order of less than 1 inch are anticipated for the drainage 
structures.

 Backfilling Underground Utility Structures 
The underground utility structure excavations will need to be properly backfilled to 
reduce the potential for subsidence at the ground surface. The excavated on-site 
soils or imported fill materials that are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, and 
clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension may

(percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
3.4 
Thrust Blocks 
We envision that thrust blocks will be required at some locations to provide 
resistance to the thrust forces due to the pressurized pipes. The thrust force acting on the 
pipes may be resisted by passive earth pressure acting against the face of the thrust 
block. We understand that most of the planned inverts will be about 5 feet below the 
design finished grades.  
Based on our field exploration, we anticipate that most of the trench excavations 
will likely encounter residual soils consisting of stiff to hard silty clay. Therefore, we 
recommend using lateral bearing pressures of up to 1,500 psf for the design of thrust 
blocks bearing against the stiff residual soils underlying the site.

3.6 
Corrosion Potential 
Design of metallic substructures, such as waterline pipes or other metallic piping, 
should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure. Two sets of 
laboratory corrosion tests, including pH and minimum resistivity, were performed by our 
office to evaluate the corrosivity of the on-site soils encountered. The test results are 
summarized and presented in Appendix B. In addition, corrosivity tests were performed 
on samples retrieved from our previous field explorations (Boring Nos. B-2 and 101 
through 104). These test results are presented in Appendix C, and the locations of the 
borings from our previous field explorations are presented on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Detailed results of the Chloride Content (EPA 300.0) and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0) 
performed by Eurofins TestAmerica are presented in Appendix D. 
Based on the updated version of the Board of Water Supply, City and County of 
Honolulu Water System External Corrosion Control Standards dated July 28, 2021, there 
are two soil corrosivity categories. Category A (Moderately to Severely Corrosive) applies 
to soil meeting any one of the following conditions. Corrosion Category B (Negligibly to 
Mildly Corrosive) is applicable when none of the following conditions are met. The 
updated version of the standards is effective as of the publishing date for Oahu only. 
• Resistivity ≤ 5,000 ohm-cm 
• pH ≤ 6.5 
• Water-soluble chloride concentration ≥ 500 ppm 
• Water-soluble sulfate concentration ≥ 1,000 ppm 
• Within fluctuating saltwater table 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
regarding the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the 
resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling three borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 201 through 203, each extending to a depth of about 21.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous 
flight augers.  In addition, one bulk sample of the near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1, 
was obtained to evaluate the support characteristics of the near-surface soils. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, Penetration 
Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration 
sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler 
the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
