GENERAL SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located in the Kawaihae 2 section of the District
of South Kohala on the island of National Historic
Site is a historic cultural and archeological site managed and operated
by the National Parks Service The Historic Site is bounded to the west
by Samuel Spencer Beach Park to the north and south by raw undeveloped
land and to east by Queen Kaahumanu Highway
The terrain is generally rolling with sam steep slopes locally
approaching a ratio of one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V The
terrain typically reflects the original surface of thick basaltic lava
flows in the A a form The ground surface elevations range from about 30
to 150 feet above sea level with drainage trending towards the west
The site is vegetated with a thin cover of dry grasses with scattered
kiawe algaroba trees
Most of the ground surface is covered with basaltic cobbles ard
gravel mantling an ashy soil There are localized exposures of basalt
clinker and rock formation

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The project site is underlain by basaltic lava flows of the Hamakua
Volcanic Series of Mauna Kea which were deposited during the Pleistocene
Stearns and Macdonald 1946
The Hamakua series is typically composed of basaltic lava flows which
are mantled by a relatively thin layer of palagonitized ash locally
referred to as Pahala Ash The ash soil at the project site differs from
Pahala Ash found in other portions of the island of Hawaii in that the
in situ moisture content is generally lower and the soil exists in a dry
friable state
The soils at the site have been subjected to wind erosion which has
resulted in the formation of a desert pavement on the surface of the
site This desert pavement is a surficial layer of cobbles and gravel
which were left behind as the finer ash was blown away
At and in the area of the project site the lava flows are in the Ata
form which is characterized by a rough spiny or rubbly surface of
fragmental clinker underlain by a dense core of massive basalt which
frequently has a thinner less continuous layer of clinker below the
massive core The clinker surface of the lavas at the site have been
mantled by the Pahala Ash as discussed above
Visual observations of exposures of existing road cuts indicate that
the clinker layers may be as much as ten 10 feet thick with
discontinuous ledges of hard basalt interbedded within the clinker

Blasting Considerations
It is our understanding that there are concerns regarding the use of
explosives at the site for excavation at the site due to the potentially
sensitive nature of the historic and cultural features at the National
Historic Site It is our opinion that most of the rock at the site could
be excavated without the use of explosives However the progress of work
would be slow and inefficient Further the vibrations caused by the
heavy ripping and or chipping with hoerams may be as detrimental if not
more so than those caused by carefully controlled blasting The following
discussions are presented to aid in the seléction of an excavation method
Hazards or objectionable impacts from blasting operations generally
originate from one or more of the following
1 Flyrock
2 Ground Vibration
3 Air Blast
Flyrock
Flyrock is the blaster s term for flying rock or other debris which
is launched into the air during open blasting by the force of the
detonating explosives These projectiles often have tremendous kinetic
energy and can crush or destroy things in their flight path or at their
point of impact i
Flyrock generated during the earthwork for the proposed road grading
could potentially strike nearby features If the flyrock has sufficient
kinetic energy it could create significant damage
The occurrence of flyrock may be reduced amd possibly eliminated by
increasing the confinement or depth of the explosive charges and or by
using safety mats

Ground Vibration
With respect to the subject project ground vibration is probably the
most critical potential impact When an explosive detonates below ground
it generates intense stress waves in the surrounding material These
waves crushes the rock immediately adjacent to the explosion and
permanently distorts and cracks the rock for some distance
When the intensity of the stress waves attenuates to the point that
there is no longer any permanent deformation of the rock the stress is
propagated through the rock as elastic waves These elastic waves create
ground vibration
The potential of the ground vibration to damage a structure is a
function of the intensity of the vibration amd the natural frequency of
the structure According to the Du Pont Blaster s Handbook a formla
has been developed to relate the intensity of ground vibration at a given
point in tems of particle displacement velocity amd acceleration to
the weight of the explosive charge and the distance between the explosion
and the point of interest

Refraction seismography is based on the transmission velocities of
compression waves through various geologic materials Generally the
denser or more consolidated a material is the faster the transmission
velocity The compression waves are refracted at the interfaces between
materials and then received by surface geophones and recorded by the
seismograph The recorded data is down loaded to a portable computer for
processing either in the field or stored for later processing
The techniques will yield approximate depths or thickness of
materials below the survey area and give their approximate compression
wave velocities These velocities may be empirically related to the
excavation characteristics of the materials The following legend

A
Cut slopes shall be excavated to the lines and grades indicated
on the contract drawings and where necessary adjusted in
accordance with the recommendations of the Geotechnical Engineer
during construction

Fill Materials
Material for fill shall consist of on site soils or approved import
material Generally fill material shall contain less than 5 percent
organic matter and other deleterious material and shall not contain
rocks or lumps in excess of twelve inches 12 in diameter Rock
fragments between twelve inches 12 and twenty four inches 24 in
diameter may be used as recommended in the Geotechnical Engineering
Exploration report
Representative samples of the materials to be used as compacted fill
shall be tested by the Geotechnical Engineer Samples of the
proposed materials shall be submitted to the Geotechnical Engineer at
least seven working days prior to its intended jobsite delivery
Placement and Compaction of Fill
A The fill shall be placed in horizontal layers which when
compacted will not exceed eighteen inches 18 in thickness
Each layer shall be spread uniformly and blade mixed to attain
uniformity of material and water content within each layer

Additional fill material shall not be placed on any fill layer
which has not been properly compacted Subsequent removal of

Rock fragments or boulders up to than twelve inches 12 in
diameter may be utilized provided they are not concentrated in
any one area and that there is sufficient fine grained material
to fill the voids between the rocks
Rocks greater than twelve inches 12 in diameter shall he
removed from the fill unless specifically approved for placement
_in the fill by the Geotechnical Engineer
All fill shall be compacted to a minimm of 90 percent of the

unless otherwise specified in the Geotechnical Engineer s
Report
Boulders encountered in excavations may be used in the deeper
fills provided that the following are followed
1 Boulders shall be spread out and must not be nested
together They should be placed so that compaction
equipment will be able to compact around them
2 No part of the boulder should encroach any closer to
finished subgrade than the boulder s largest dimension and
in no case be closer than 3 feet to finished subgrade
3 No boulder larger than 2 feet in its largest dimension
should be placed in the fill It may be necessary to reduce
the size of larger boulders or dispose of them off site

Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in
excavated volcanic rock material due to the large particle
size Instead a testing program consisting of visual
observations and test strips to determine the lift thickness and
number of passes of a compactor required to achieve the desired
compaction should be conducted at the start of the project
The number of passes can then be used as the criteria for
compaction
The compaction equipment may be sheepsfoot vibratory or
pneumatic tire rollers suited to the soil type and capable of
producing the specified results in an expedient and efficient
manner The equipment shall be in good working condition fully
ballasted and self cleaning
The compaction shall be accomplished in a systematic and uniform
manner over the entire fill area to produce a wniformly
compacted fill
The Contractor shall be required to obtain the above specified
levels of compaction out to the finish slope of fill slopes
This may be achieved by either overbuilding the slope and
cutting back to the compacted core or by direct compaction of
the slope face with suitable equipment
Fill placed against previously graded cut or fill slopes shall

firm material and the transition stripped of loose material
prior to fill placement Fill shall be placed and compacted
from the low side up
