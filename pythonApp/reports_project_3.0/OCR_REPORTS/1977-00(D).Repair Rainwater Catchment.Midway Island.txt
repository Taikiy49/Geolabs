INTRODUCTION
In accordance with your request we have performed a geotechnical
exploration of the Primary Rainwater Catchment Area on Midway Island Our
exploration was performed in general accordance with our revised proposal
dated This report summarizes our work on the project and
presents our findings and recommendations
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the
surface and near surface conditions at the project site to develop a
generalized data set for the formulation of geotechnical engineering
recommendations pertinent to repair remedial action to the Primary
Rainwater Catchment Area of Midway Island
The scope of our work consisted of the following tasks and work
efforts
1 Mobilization and demobilization of our personnel and equipment to
and from Midway Island via Military Airlift Command MAC
flights
2 Field observations of the surface conditions in the Catchment
Area
W O 1977 00 D Page 2
3 The drilling of 43 soil cement cores from the surface of the
Primary Rainwater Catchment Area and patching of the drilled
holes with soil cement
4 The drilling of three 3 short borings in selected areas of the
catchment
5 On site interviews with naval and civilian personnel on Midway
Island to obtain data about water collection consumption and
rainfall data
6 Laboratory testing of selected samples obtained during our
exploration
7 Engineering analysis of the field and laboratory data and report
preparation
8 Miscellaneous work efforts such as drafting word processing and
clerical support
GENERAL SITE DESCRIPTION
The Primary Rainwater Catchment Area is located in the western half
of Midway Island see Plate 1 The site is a triangular area of
approximately 30 acres bounded by Runway 6 24 to the south the taxiway to
the east and the abandoned Runway 3 21 to the northwest
Also included in the exploration area were two ditches one fronting
the R 2 sluice gate and one at Catch Basin No 3
Most of the area is nearly level and paved Within the project
limits there are three 3 large steel water reservoirs tanks two 2
small CMU concrete buildings and a series of loop antennae The area has
been paved to prevent the infiltration of rainfall into the ground There
is a barely perceptible slope towards the northeast where there is a
ponding basin which serves as the intake area of the catchment system

During our field work it was noted that there are several shallow
depressions birdbaths in the paved surface and that these depressions
appear to have been created during the paving of the area see Photo 1
It was also observed that many of the cold joints between panels of
pavement were lapped rather than butted thereby creating ridges which
impede the drainage of runoff water see Photo 2 The surface of the
soil cement pavement was for the most part relatively rough with some of
the soil aggregate being exposed through the cement skin see Photo 3
In addition a few areas where grasses and weeds have become established
were observed In these areas the soil cement pavement is friable
sometimes to the point where it may be dug out by hand see Photo 4
In the R 1 reservoir intake area several large cracks were noted in
the soil cement along with additional areas where the paving material
could be dug by hand Visual observations also disclosed areas where air
bubbles could be seen coming from the floor of the pond which indicate
that water is leaking through the floor of the pond see Photo 5
The R 2 ditch leads into the sluice gate of the R 2 Primary Reservoir
and has been lined with one half to three fourths inch sized basalt gravel
and a synthetic membrane The synthetic membrane has become brittle and
is deteriorating
The ditch at Catch Basin 3 is a shallow swale and is unlined except
for a small patch of gunite near the middle of the length of the swale
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The existing Primary Rainwater Catchment area was constructed in the
late 1950 s It was repaired and resurfaced in about 1979 by forces of
the Naval Mobile Construction Battalions Seabees The original repair
design called for a six inch 6 thick layer of soil cement graded to
drain to the R 1 reservoir intake area of the eastern end of the catchment
area The as built grading plans by the Russ Smith Corporation
indicate an average slope of about 0 7 percent or roughly 140H 1V
W O 1977 00 D Page 4
The rainwater catchment system which includes the primary area and
secondary areas such as taxiway and runway shoulders is the only source
of fresh water available on Midway Island According to on base
personnel there is a brackish water lens underlying portions of the
island However this lens is not considered to be developable as a
domestic water source except by blending with fresh catchment water during
periods of critically low precipitation
Based on discussions with personnel stationed on Midway Island the
efficiency of the catchment system has diminished to the point where fresh
water supplies are becoming critical
Therefore it is necessary to develop plans for repair remedial
action to the catchment system to improve the efficiency of the system
FIELD EXPLORATION
The surface and near surface conditions at the site were explored by
the drilling and sampling of forty three 43 soil cement cores from the
surface of the Primary Catchment area and the drilling of three 3
borings to depths of 3 feet below the existing ground surface at selected
points within the Catchment area see Plate 2 These cores and borings
were drilled utilizing a portable drilling machine which had been
mobilized to Midway Island by a Military Airlift Command flight A
summary of these cores is presented in Appendix A Field Exploration
The results of laboratory tests to determine the compressive strength of
selected cores are presented in Appendix B Laboratory Testing
Neither cores nor borings were performed for the ditches at the R 2
sluice gate and at Catch Basin No 3 since it was indicated by the PACDIV
engineer during his site visit that these areas would be gunited by the
Seabees

In addition to the drilling of the cores and borings observations of
the existing site conditions were made and interviews were conducted with
personnel stationed on Midway Island regarding the history of the
catchment average fresh water usage on the island and rainfall records
The water usage and rainfall records are presented in Appendix C Water
Data
In order to obtain an estimate to the infiltration rate of water into
the pavement five 5 infiltration tests were performed at selected sites
on the pavement The results of these tests are also presented in

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based upon our observations and field exploration it is our opinion
that the problems experienced with the Primary Rainwater Catchment at
Midway Island are generally more of a civil engineering nature rather than
a geotechnical engineering problem The available rainfall records
indicate that precipitation on Midway Island tends to be a series of light
rains during the course of a given month rather than isolated heavy
rainfalls
Given the very flat grade of the catchment pavement along with the
shallow depressions and rough surface light rainfalls cannot
realistically be expected to drain across the full width of the catchment
to the ponding intake area
Catchment Lining
Given the constraints of recommending a material which will
efficiently catch rainwater is installable by the Seabees and will have a
design life of twenty years or more it is our opinion that a new
installation of soil cement is not appropriate It appears that the
initially smooth surface of the soil cement pavement deteriorates with
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1977 00 D Page 6
time resulting in a rough surface which tends to hold the small amounts of
water which result from a light precipitation event rather than drain it
It also appears that it may be difficult for the Seabees to maintain the
extremely fine grading tolerances required to install such a flat slope in
a pavement without creating birdbaths or poorly butted joints Also the
quality control of the in place soil cement mixture seems to be
inadequate
Therefore we recommend that the use of a synthetic geomembrane be
investigated as the new surface of the catchment Discussions with
manufacturers representatives indicate that there are synthetics such as
Hypalon or high density polyethylene HDPE now available which are
capable of surviving up to twenty years of exposure The manufacturers
are willing to provide warranties to the purchaser if the materials are
properly installed Generally the manufacturer s definition of properly
installed includes installation under the supervision of the
manufacturer These discussions with the manufacturer s representatives
generally indicate that the bulk of the installation labor could be
provided by the Seabees with a minimal amount of supervisory labor from
the manufacturer
We do recommend that the catchment area be stripped of the existing
soil cement pavement and regraded to provide better drainage and to
provide a suitable subgrade for the installation of the membrane In
general the on site soils will provide a good subgrade however the
manufacturer of the product which is finally selected should be consulted
for the particular subgrade requirements of their product Serious
consideration could be given to subdividing the area into a series of
smaller catchments which collect water in individual drain inlets with Pvc
Use of the trade name Hypalon does not necessarily constitute an
endorsement of this particular product
W O 1977 00 D Page 7
or HDPE pipes draining to the R 1 area see Plate 3 The smoothness of
the plastic pipes would allow drainage at very flat grades This
subdivision would allow the use of steeper grades in each subdivided area
to facilitate drainage
During our field work it was noted that some maintenance of the
catchment area is required such as the occasional removal of sand and
other foreign objects which accumulate in the area and the almost daily
removal of deceased birds Most of these activities require the use of
some sort of vehicles Many of the available membrane materials are not
compatible with vehicular traffic Therefore we recommend that the civil
engineering design for the repair of the catchment area incorporate some
form of maintenance roads to allow for the necessary maintenance These
maintenance roads could be utilized as part of the system of holding down
the membrane See Plate 4 The joint between the membrane and the
pavement should be as tight and as flush as possible It is our opinion
that a six inch 6 thick concrete pavement section would be adequate
Alternately a membrane of sufficient strength and thickness such as
a heavy HDPE membrane to withstand light vehicular traffic could be used
Another possible problem which was noted during our field work was
the potentially high wind conditions which could occur in the catchment
area due to storms propwash and jetblast Discussions with the
manufacturers representatives indicate that they have anchoring methods
and systems available for such applications The anchoring methods vary
from material to material and manufacturer to manufacturer
Interim Repairs
Based upon our observations of the catchment it is our opinion that
some actions may be implemented immediately to improve the existing
catchment until such time as the final design of the membrane liner is
developed and installed One of these would be the sealing of the
W O 1977 00 D Page 8
ponding intake area floor with either a temporary membrane or shotcrete to
reduce leakage through the existing soil cement Another would be to
clean up the areas of friable soil cement as evidenced by the growth of
vegetation and seal them with shotcrete or some other means
It is our opinion that the sealing of the pond floor would be the
most beneficial short term effort
Additional Improvements
Our field observations at Midway Island also lead us to the opinion
that other additional improvements may be made to the catchment system to
further enhance its efficiency even with the installation of a membrane
It is our understanding that the runways taxiway and their shoulders
serve as a secondary catchment system which feed into the ponding area by
means of drainlines It was noted that the surfaces of the abandoned
runway the taxiway shoulders and the runway shoulders have also
deteriorated and have become rough and cracked It is our opinion that
resurfacing of these areas will increase the amount of water caught by the
system
Alternate Solutions
It also appears that there are alternate solutions to the present
system of catching surface runoff water as a fresh water source Although
these solutions are not within our current scope we feel that they should
be mentioned as being worth consideration
Desalinization
NR i i i Ss in i ie i ne
During our field work it was learned that there is a brackish
groundwater lens below most of the island Neither the quality nor
the sustainable yield of this lens were made available to us
however we feel that it may be possible to desalinate this water to
provide at a minimum a supplemental source of fresh water
W O 1977 00 D Page 9
Discussions with engineers in the environmental and water resources
field indicate that there are now reverse osmosis desalinization
systems available which can demineralize water of sea water salinity
to potable levels These systems are energy intensive and require a
considerable amount of electricity and maintenance
It was noted that there was almost no cloud cover over the island
during our field work Solar data was not recorded at the Midway
Island weather station however discussions with the on station
personnel indicated that the weather is usually sunny It was also
noted that there was a relatively large amount of unused land area in
the form of abandoned facilities In Middle Eastern nations solar
still systems for the desalinization of sea water have become highly
sophisticated and produce sufficient fresh water to be feasible in
water poor locations The sunny conditions and available land area
point to solar desalinization as a possibility
Civil Engineering Solutions
Other more conventional civil engineering solutions could be
collection of water from roof catchments as is commonly practiced in
remote areas of the island of Hawaii or the installation and use for
collection of a storm drain system along the base roads and the
aircraft operations apron
LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based
in part upon information obtained from visual observations field
discussions and field data points such as pavement cores and shallow
borings Variations of conditions may occur and the nature and extent
of these variations may not become evident until construction If
variations then appear evident it will be necessary to re evaluate the
recommendations given in this report
