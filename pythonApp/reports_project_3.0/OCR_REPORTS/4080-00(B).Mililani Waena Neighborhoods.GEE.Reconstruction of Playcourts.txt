INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Reconstruction of Playcourts project at the Mililani Waena
Neighborhood Park located in Mililani area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general
location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of the light pole
foundations site grading and pavements only The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below the Koolau lavas
banking against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad plateau
referred to as the Schofield Plateau on which the project site is located
The project area is composed of basalt rock built by the extrusion of lavas of the
Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows of jointed
dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers For the last 1 to 2 million
years weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred forming a mantle of residual
soils and saprolite that grade to basalt rock formation with depth In general saprolite is
composed mainly of silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic
rocks

to the maximum depth explored of about 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface In
order to develop the required bearing and lateral load resistances for the new light pole
structures we recommend that drilled shaft foundations embedded in the very stiff to hard

Light Pole Foundations
In order to develop the required bearing and lateral load resistances the new light
pole structures may be supported by a foundation system consisting of cast in place
concrete drilled shafts Based on the anticipated structural loads and the subsoil
conditions encountered at the project site we recommend that drilled shafts with a
minimum diameter of 24 inches be used to support the proposed 25 foot high light pole
structures Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site a drilled
shaft embedment length of not less than 5 feet below the design finished grades should
be used for the light pole foundations at the subject site
The cast in place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally
from skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding soils An allowable
compressive load capacity of up to 8 kips per shaft may be used for the 25 foot high light
poles These compressive load capacities are for dead plus live loads and may be
GEOLABS HAWAIl
W O 4080 00 B Page 7
increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces The
end bearing component of the drilled shafts has been discounted in our analyses
Lateral loads imposed on the light pole foundations may be resisted by the
stiffness of the shaft soil system to deflect Lateral load resistance of the drilled shaft is a
function of the stiffness of the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable
deflection at the top of shaft and induced moment in the shaft Our lateral load analyses
based on the structural loads provided indicated that the estimated deflection at the top of
the drilled shaft should be less than one fourth inch In addition the maximum
moments induced in the 24 inch drilled shafts are approximately 6 861 foot pounds
The anticipated settlements of the light pole foundations are estimated to be on the
order of less than one fourth inch We believe that a significant portion of the
settlements is elastic and should occur as the loads are applied
Construction Considerations
Drilled shafts are desirable for the light pole foundations because of the significant
increase in lateral and uplift load capacities when compared to shallow foundations
However the performance of the drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the
contractor s method of construction and construction procedures In our opinion the
following conditions would have a substantial effect on the effectiveness and cost of the
drilled shaft foundations
Difficult Drilling Conditions
Although our field exploration did not encounter basalt formations and or boulders
difficult drilling conditions may be encountered during the drilled shaft installation
considering the geologic environment at the site Appropriate drilling equipment
should be utilized by the drilled shaft contractor to install the drilled shafts to the
depths and dimensions recommended herein

Workmanship
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in
drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes
A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 6 to 9 inch range should be used to
provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soil The
concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce the potential for
segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix If pre cast concrete pole
bases are used the gap between the pole base and the surrounding soils should
be backfilled with concrete or lean concrete A representative of Geolabs Hawaii
should be present to observe the installation of drilled shafts during construction to
confirm the subsoil conditions and embedment depths

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to reduce the
potential for contamination of the excavated materials Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill
should be over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting
excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill The excavated soft
and or wet soils should be properly disposed of off site or at designated areas
on site
After clearing and grubbing the exposed subgrade should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 8 inches properly moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
Fills and Backfills
In general the on site soils generated from excavations may be re used as a
source of general fill and backfill materials provided that the recommendations
contained herein are followed On site soils to be used as fill should contain no
organic materials debris or particles greater than 3 inches in largest dimension
Over sized on site materials larger than 3 inches in maximum dimension should
be screened prior to being re used as fill materials
If imported fill is required the materials should be well graded from coarse to fine
with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension In addition the fill

material should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the
No 200 sieve The fill material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or

ASTM Test Designation D 1883 The materials should be tested and approved
by Geolabs Hawaii prior to delivery to the site to confirm that the materials are
suitable for the intended purpose
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills for the project should be placed in level lifts not exceeding
8 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optinum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
Cut and Fill Slopes
We anticipate that cut slopes would expose the very stiff to hard clayey silts
encountered in the borings In general we believe that cut slopes exposing the
very stiff to hard clayey silts may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or
flatter Cut slopes should be protected by appropriate slope planting or other
means to reduce the potential for erosion of the exposed soils as soon as possible
after the slope faces are exposed
Permanent fill slopes constructed using the excavated on site soils or imported fill
materials may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fills placed
on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the existing
slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations should
start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in

Utility Trench
We envision that new electrical duct lines will be required to service the proposed
new lighting system In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of clean sand or
S4C is recommended under the pipes Select granular 1 inch minus materials such as
clean sand or S4C should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 6 inches
above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the
compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is
critical to use this select granular 1 inch minus material to reduce the potential for
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the
sides of the pipes The bedding and initial backfill material should be compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Improper backfill material and placement
procedures could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 6 inches above the duct lines
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on site soils or imported
select granular fill material The backfill should be placed in maximum 6 inch level loose
lifts moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
future ground subsidence

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings to depths of about 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer tests provide an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil samples Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of
the test are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
One California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM D 1883 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the strength characteristics for pavement
subgrade support Results of the CBR test are presented on Plate B 2
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on the
near surface soils Results of the test are presented on Plate B 3
