REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which
time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred
to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The geologic conditions underlying the site are the result of a complex series of
depositional and erosional events due to glacio eustatic sea level changes and
post erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch The Koolau Volcanic
Shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows
Following the cessation of activity of the Koolau Volcano alluvial soil materials were
deposited in an estuarine and deltaic environment in the area of Pearl Harbor The sea
level fluctuated several times receding perhaps 300 feet below and rising about 100 feet
above the present sea level
During the Recent Epoch as the sea level rose to its current stand the area was
covered with additional sediments consisting primarily of soft and loose lagoonal marine
deposits Land development and reclamation projects in the past 40 to 60 years have
added man made fills bringing the site to its present configuration
Based on our research the anticipated surface soils underlying the project site are
described by the U S Soil Conservation Service in their publication Soil Survey of Islands
of Molokai and Lanai State of Hawaii 1972 A soil survey map
showing the distribution of the major soils at the site is presented on Plate 3 The major
soils at the site and some of their general properties are as follows
Fill Land mixed FL These soils are generally materials dredged from the
ocean or hauled from nearby areas and general material from other sources
These soils are not capable of classification
Jaucas sand JaC These are excessively drained calcareous soils developed
in wind and water deposited sand from coral and seashells The slopes range
from about 0 to 15 percent though in most places do not go beyond 7 percent
Permeability is rapid and runoff is very slow to slow Water erosion is slight

SOIL SAMPLING
A total of ten borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 and 5 through 11
extended to depths of about 15 8 to 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface A total
of five pavement cores designated as Coring and 4 through 6 extended to
depths of about 2 to 2 7 feet below the existing ground surface In addition four test
pits designated as Test Pit Nos 1 through 4 were excavated to depths of about 1 5 to
3 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring and pavement core
locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The borings and pavement cores were drilled using truck mounted drilling
equipment with continuous flight augers and coring tools Our geologist classified the
materials encountered in the borings and corings by visual and textural examination in
the field and observed the drilling operations on a near continuous basis These
classifications were further reviewed by visual observation and laboratory testing Soils
were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System as
shown on Plate A of Appendix A Graphic representations of the materials encountered
in the borings and corings drilled are presented on the Logs of Borings and Corings
Plates A 1 through A 15 of Appendix A
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 or 24 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths

LABORATORY TESTING
Laboratory testing was performed on selected soil samples obtained during the
field exploration as an aid in classifying the materials and evaluating their engineering
properties The tests performed are described below
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings and
Corings at the appropriate sample depths In addition the results are presented on
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples obtained from the test pits to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Test Pits at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic presentations of the test results are provided on Plate C 2
Nineteen Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 including nine Hydrometer
tests ASTM D 422 were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation
characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification Graphic presentations of the
grain size distribution are provided on Plates C 3 through C 6

INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of laboratory work performed by our firm on one concrete core
sample submitted by of Geolabs Inc of Honolulu Hawaii on December 4
2009 We understand the concrete core was obtained from concrete airfield pavement currently
under evaluation The concrete was reportedly placed within or shortly after 1994 We
understand the pavement is exhibiting little distress other than a few mid panel cracks The
scope of our work was limited to performing petrographic analysis on the core sample to
document the general overall quality and condition of the concrete
CONCLUSIONS
Based on our observations test results and past experience our conclusions are as follows
1
The concrete represented by the core appears to be in reasonably good condition The
overall quality of the concrete was judged to be good Although some coarse aggregate
particles exhibit darkened apparent reaction rims the concrete exhibits no evidence of
bulk expansion from alkali silica reaction ASR Further no ASR microcracking
patterns or deposits of ASR gel product were detected
2
In general the coarse and fine aggregate appeared hard sound and durable The coarse
basaltic and andesitic aggregates intersected by thin section analysis appear
holocrystalline and do not exhibit evidence of volcanic glass The fine aggregate was
comprised of a naturally occurring beach sand
3
Curing of the concrete appears to have been somewhat inadequate Carbonation ranges
from 2mm up to 16mm depth from the top surface proceeding along several sub vertical
drying shrinkage microcracks The carbonation is somewhat excessive for the age of the
concrete The concrete paste exhibits very fine shrinkage microcracks at various depths
and orientations in the core

TEST PROCEDURES
Laboratory testing was performed on and subsequent dates Our procedures
were as follows
Petrographic Analysis
A petrographic analysis was performed in accordance with Standard Operating Procedure 00
LAB 001 Petrographic Examination of Hardened Concrete ASTM C856 latest revision The
petrographic analysis consisted of reviewing the cement paste and aggregate qualities on a whole
basis on sawcut and lapped and fractured sections Reflected light microscopy was performed
under an binocular stereozoom microscope at magnifications up to 160x The
depth of carbonation was documented using a phenolphthalein pH indicator solution applied on
freshly sawcut and lapped surfaces of the concrete sample
The water cementitious of the concrete was estimated by viewing a thin section of the concrete
under an polarizing light microscope at magnifications of up to 1000x Thin
section analysis was performed in accordance with Standard Operating Procedure 00 LAB 013
Determining the Water Cement of Portland Cement Concrete APS Method An additional
smaller sawcut subdivision of the concrete sample is epoxy impregnated highly polished and
then attached to a glass slide using an optically clear epoxy Excess sample is sawcut from the
glass and the thin slice remaining on the slide is lapped and polished until the concrete reaches
25 microns or less in thickness Thin section analysis allows for the observation of portland
cement morphology including phase identification an estimate of the amount of residual
material and spatial relationships Also the presence and relative amounts of supplementary
cementitious materials and pozzolans may be identified and estimated
Air Content Testing
Air content testing was performed using Standard Operating Procedure 00 LAB 003
Microscopical Determination of Air Void Content and Parameters of the Air Void System in
Hardened Concrete ASTM C457 latest revision The linear traverse method was used The
concrete core was sawcut perpendicular with respect to the horizontal plane of the concrete as
placed and then lapped prior to testing
