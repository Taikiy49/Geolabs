INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the proposed 1 5 M G Storage Tank in Koloa on
the island of Kauai Hawaii The general location of the project site and
its vicinity is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 The
exploration was performed in general accordance with our proposal dated
This report summarizes the findings of our exploration and our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses based
on the data obtained during the exploration These recommendations are
intended for design input and are not intended for construction estimation
or bidding purposes

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to construct a 1 5 million gallon capacity potable
water storage tank on the northwestern slope of Puuhi in Koloa Kauai
Hawaii The storage tank will be adjacent to an existing 1 5 M G
Reservoir on the west and will be approximately 22 feet high and 120 feet
in diameter An access road and a concrete ditch are planned around the
reservoir
Site grading involving cuts ranging from 15 feet southern portion
to 3 feet northern portion in depth is anticipated to create a level
pad for the storage tank and access road The proposed finished floor
elevation of the storage tank is about 224 feet above Mean Sea Level

The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the
subsurface conditions at the project site in order to develop a
generalized soil rock data set for the formulation of geotechnical
engineering recommendations pertinent to the design of site grading
foundations and pavement of access roads

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located on the northwestern slope of Puuhi in
The site currently is a sugar cane field adjacent
to an existing 1 5 M G Reservoir on the west A chain link fence
separates the existing reservoir and the sugar cane field
The site slopes downward to the north with a gradient of about 10 1
H V The ground elevations range from about 222 feet northern portion
to 244 feet southern portion above Mean Sea Level
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano
built by the extrusion of the lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series
during the late Pliocene more than 2 3 million years ago Following
the cessation of this main shield building phase there was renewed
volcanic activity with the extrusion of the basaltic lavas of the
post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series

The project site is situated on a lava cone which was a vent of the
Koloa Volcanic Series
Subsurface Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by
drilling and sampling three 3 borings to depths of 25 0 to 35 0 feet
below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the
borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general the exploration encountered a surficial fill layer
consisting of clayey silt over medium stiff to stiff residual soils and
saprolite of about 4 feet in thickness Below the residual soils the
borings encountered highly weathered to slightly weathered basalt
formation to the maximum depths drilled
Groundwater was not encountered during our exploration Detailed
descriptions of the materials encountered are presented on the Boring
Logs Plates A 1 through A 3
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on the preliminary grading plans cuts up to 15 feet in the
southern portion and 3 feet in the northern portion are anticipated at the
site to provide the finished tank floor elevation of 224 feet Thus the
proposed storage tank foundation may bear partially on residual soils
and or saprolite and partially on basalt formation
Site Grading
The site grading will consist of cutting into the slope about 3 to 15
feet at the site for the proposed reservoir and access roadway

At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading
limits should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation
debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed and
disposed properly off site in order to prevent contamination of the
excavated material
Excavation
The excavation into the near surface residual soils and highly
weathered basalt may be made with conventional earthwork techniques
The underlying harder basalt may require special excavating
procedures such as a hoeram or other means to make the proposed
cuts However the actual rippability of the basalt formation should
be determined by any contractor proposing to bid on the project
It is anticipated that the site grading for the storage tank
structure will require cuts up to approximately 15 feet into the
highly weathered basalt in the southern portion of the site A
compound slope with slope ratios of 1 horizontal to 1 vertical
1H 1V for cuts in the weathered basalt and 2 horizontal to 1
vertical 2H 1V for cuts in the upper residual soils may be used
Alternatively an average overall slope ratio of 1 5 horizontal to 1
vertical may be used
Slope benches should be provided at maximum vertical height intervals
of about 20 feet if the final grading will require cuts in excess of
20 feet

Near the design finished grade of the slope faces cutting should be
done with extreme care Over cutting beyond the design slope ratio
should be avoided as attempts to backfill and recompact over cut
areas would most likely result in loose areas which would be subject
to later damage by erosion If boulders or small ledges of rock are
encountered near the design slope it may be desirable to carefully
cut or break these materials to match the design slope rather than
risking damage to the slope by loosening the soils adjacent to the
boulders or ledges by hard excavation
Surface run off water from areas above the slope should be diverted
away from the cut slope bank and prevented from flowing across the
slope face The slope should be planted or otherwise protected as
soon as possible to reduce damage and unravelling by erosion
Due to the relatively high rainfall occurring in the vicinity of the
project site and the nature of the materials encountered in the
borings it is possible that zones of seepage or intermittent
seepage may be encountered If so the geotechnical engineer should
be consulted for recommendations concerning the treatment of the
seepage

Tank Foundations
Based on our borings and the grading plan the proposed storage tank
foundations may bear mostly on basalt formation and partially on residual
soils and or saprolite
For footings bearing on the in situ residual soils an allowable
bearing value of 3500 p s f may be used The bearing value may be
increased up to 5000 p s f for footings bearing on basalt However
rather than using two values we recommend a uniform bearing capacity of
3500 p s f for foundation design
If a higher bearing value is desired the footing foundations may be
lowered to bear on the rock formation
Exterior wall footings should be embedded a minimum of 15 inches
below the lowest adjacent finished grade Footings located next to
utility trenches should extend down to a grade equal to or deeper than the
trench Alternatively the trench backfill which should be completed
first prior to foundation construction could be compacted to 95 maximum
dry density as established by ASTM D 1557 78 to minimize potential
footing settlement
All footing excavations in soil should be properly compacted to 95
maximum dry density as recommended above
The site grading should be designed to divert surface water away from
the foundations

Based on the boring data and anticipated tank loads the estimated
settlements are on the order of less than one half of an inch total and
one quarter of an inch differential
Resistance to lateral loads on the tank can be obtained by passive
earth pressure and frictional resistance A frictional coefficient of 0 4
may be used to calculate the friction between the bottom of the footings
and basalt residual soils In addition an equivalent fluid pressure of
350 p s f per foot of depth may be used to determine the passive earth
resistance against the buried vertical faces of the footings The soils
along the perimeters of the footings should be properly compacted in order
to provide the recommended passive resistance
To reduce the possibility of footing settlement due to possible
cavities or lava tubes in the underlying lava rock the footing excavation
should be probed at approximately 10 feet on centers The probe holes
should have a minimum diameter of 2 inches and extend below the bottom of
footing a depth of 6 feet The probe holes should be grouted from the
bottom of the hole using low pressures If a cavity is suspected below a
footing additional probe holes should be made to delineate the horizontal
extent of the cavity The probing and grouting operations should be
monitored by the soils engineer or the project inspector to see that these
recommendations are carried out properly and to locate any potential voids
near the footing excavations

Design Review
Plans and specifications for the proposed construction should be

Construction Observation
It is recommended that the geotechnical engineer be retained to
provide geotechnical engineering services during the excavation site
grading and foundation construction phases of the work This is to

recommendations and to allow design changes in the event that subsurface
conditions differ from that anticipated prior to start of construction
The recommendations given in this report are contingent upon such
observations
Any imported material required should be non expansive tested and

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based
in part upon information obtained from field data points such as
borings Variations of conditions between the field data points may
occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not become
evident until construction If variations then appear evident it will be
necessary to re evaluate the recommendations given in this report
The location of the field data points were determined by taping from
structures indicated on the Topographic Survey Map undated provided by
GMP Associates Inc Elevations of the data points were determined by
interpolation from elevation points on the map The physical location and
elevation of the field data points should be considered accurate only to
the degree implied by the method used
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the
field data points depict the approximate boundaries between soil types
and as such may denote a gradual transition The strata lines shown on
profiles or cross section are based upon interpolation between field data
points and may not represent actual subsurface conditions
Water level data from the field data points were collected at the
times given on the graphic representations and or in the text of this
report This data has been reviewed and interpretations made in the
formulation of this report However it must be noted that fluctuation
may occur due to variations in rainfall tides temperature and other
factors
