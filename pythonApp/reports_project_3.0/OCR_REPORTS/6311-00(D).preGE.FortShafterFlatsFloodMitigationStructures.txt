REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger Koolau shield forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in central
Oahu
The Koolau volcanic shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by
thinly bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities ceased
approximately 2 5 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicates that the island
of Oahu has subsided by as much as 1 200 feet since the cessation of this early
volcanic activity Macdonald and Abbot 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of the
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed
behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the
lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age many sea level changes occurred
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers advanced the ocean level fell since less water was available to fill
the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These

glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and
lower relative to the present sea level of Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine sediments in the
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and
calcareous materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal
deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial
erosion of the upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils
under relatively high energy conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal
sediments During these stable periods the growth of fringing coral algal reefs formed
distinct ledges of reef limestone at elevations roughly conforming to the level of the sea
during that period About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred
During that rise the valleys in the project area were submerged In the last
10 000 years or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and
marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal
environments resulting in thick deposits of soft loose sediments along the coast in areas
that were formerly valleys and drainageways In more recent time the efforts of man
have shaped the low lying land to its present form by placement of fill in low areas

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic
plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic

Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most are so

Liquefaction Potential
Based on the International Building Code 2006 Edition the project site should be
evaluated for the potential for soil liquefaction Based on the subsurface conditions
encountered and a design peak ground acceleration of 0 294g liquefaction may
occur resulting in appreciable settlements at the ground surface Therefore the
effects of potential liquefaction should be taken into consideration in the design of
the proposed development
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils near the ground
surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess pore
water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes In
this process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration such
as during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing
an increase in pore water pressure If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to
dissipate the build up of pore water pressure the effective stress internal strength
of the soil is reduced Under sustained vibrations the pore water pressure build up
could equal the overburden pressure essentially reducing the soil shear strength to
zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid During liquefaction the soil
acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal and vertical movements and
if not confined will result in significant deformations
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion The major factors affecting the liquefaction
characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows

FLOOD WALL FOUNDATIONS
A new flood wall will be installed along the makai side of Kahauiki Stream from
about the existing Government Road Bridge to near Moanalua Freeway The flood wall
will be designed with top of wall elevation at about 16 feet MSL or about 3 feet above
the flood level of 13 feet MSL
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the structural loads
provided by the project structural engineer we recommend utilizing deep foundations
consisting of 16 5 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles to support the
proposed flood wall structure The driven piles will extend through the soft loose
marsh lagoonal deposits and derive vertical support capacity from friction and end
bearing in the underlying very stiff alluvial clays starting at depths of about 40 feet below
the existing ground surface
Based on the information provided the proposed pile layout will consist of 2 pile
bents spaced at approximately 12 feet on center along the flood wall alignment Pile
No 1 closer to stream will be subjected to a maximum lateral load of about 27 kips
and a maximum axial load of up to 99 kips Pile No 2 farther from stream will be
subjected to a maximum lateral load of about 27 kips and a maximum axial load of up to
85 kips
To achieve the allowable compressive load capacity of up to 99 kips for
Pile No 1 we recommend using an estimated pile length on the order of about 96 feet
below the bottom of the pile cap for preliminary design and cost estimating purposes
An estimated pile length on the order of about 90 feet may be used to achieve the
allowable compressive load capacity of up to 85 kips for Pile No 2 The allowable
compressive load capacities for the piles are for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces
Piles should be spaced a minimum of 4 feet center to center to avoid reduction in

Consideration has been given to the use of bitumen coating to reduce the
downdrag loads on the driven piles Because of the variable thickness of the
compressible deposits ranging from about 7 to 40 feet it would be very difficult to
determine the downdrag zones for bitumen coating application along the flood wall
alignment Application of bitumen coating below the downdrag zones would
actually reduce the load carrying contribution of the underlying alluvial deposits In
addition there exist the potential for damaging the bitumen coating during handling
and pile driving and environmental concerns such as contamination of
groundwater etc Therefore we believe that increasing the pile length would be a
more prudent approach in handling the downdrag loads on the driven pile
foundations
In addition to the downdrag forces acting on the pile shaft as discussed above the

engineering analyses this downdrag load acting on the wall footing is on the order

Foundation Settlements
Settlement of the pile foundations will result from elastic compression of the pile
member and subgrade response of the foundation embedded in the
marsh lagoonal deposits and alluvium underlying the project site Total settlements
of the pile supported foundation under the design loads are estimated to be on the
order of less than 0 75 inches Differential settlements between adjacent piles
should not exceed about one half of the total foundation settlement We believe that
a significant portion of the settlement is elastic and should occur as the loads are
applied
Pile Hammer Selection
In general we recommend driving the piles with a hammer capable of delivering a
rated energy between 39 000 and 55 000 foot pounds in the field The hammer
should be equipped with an energy level control such that driving within the soft

and or loose soils may be controlled at a lower energy to reduce the potential for
tensile stress development which could damage or cause cracking of the piles
Selection of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the matching of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be
driven This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy
to the top of the pile and also reducing the potential for damage to the piles
Geolabs should review the pile driving equipment proposed to evaluate whether the
equipment is capable of installing the piles to the recommended capacity The

construction for evaluation and acceptance
Pile Driving Criteria
For preliminary design of the 16 5 and 20 inch octagonal precast concrete
prestressed concrete piles pile driving may be terminated when the driven pile has
reached its design pile tip elevation or the blow counts steadily increase with depth
and reach 5 blows per inch for the last 6 inches or when aggregated blow counts
are 60 blows for the last foot The preliminary driving criteria should be confirmed
and or modified after selection of the pile driving equipment and performance of a
wave equation analysis
Each pile should be driven continuously without interruption A pile may be rejected
or its design capacity may be reduced when the driving resistance is interrupted for
more than 4 hours and the pile cannot be driven to the required depth During soft
driving such as when the pile tip is within soft loose soils the hammer stroke
or energy should be carefully controlled such that significant tensile stresses do
not develop within the pile
Because variations in soil strength characteristics are likely to exist in the bearing
soils the supporting capacity of each pile should be confirmed in the field by a
Geolabs representative during driving It should be noted that the pile design was
developed from our pile analysis using the field exploration data A test pile
program should be carried out to confirm our preliminary pile design and establish
the pile driving criteria The production pile driving criteria may be revised based on
the test pile program

Future Filled Ground Settlement
Based on the information provided we understand that the existing ground at the
project site will be raised by about 6 to 8 feet during the future Phase III
development for long term flood mitigation purpose
As mentioned previously the majority of the project site is underlain by
compressible marsh lagoonal deposits on the order of about 7 to 40 feet thick
Therefore placement of new fills to raise the site to the future finished subgrade
elevation likely will induce some ground settlements as a result of consolidation
of the underlying compressible deposits
Based on our engineering analyses we anticipate that ground settlements on the
order of about 10 to 20 inches may be anticipated as a result of the consolidation
of the compressible deposits under the future fill loads Due to the substantial
thickness and clayey nature of the compressible marsh lagoonal deposits our
preliminary analyses suggest that 80 percent of the anticipated ground
settlement could take up to 5 to 7 years to occur To reduce the long settlement
waiting period we recommend considering a surcharge program with the
installation of wick drains during the Phase III site development
It should be recognized that it is very difficult to accurately predict the exact time
required for the filled ground to settle since the settlement rate is affected by
variations of the soil structure and the history of the soil deposition Therefore a
settlement monitoring program should be implemented as discussed in the
following section
