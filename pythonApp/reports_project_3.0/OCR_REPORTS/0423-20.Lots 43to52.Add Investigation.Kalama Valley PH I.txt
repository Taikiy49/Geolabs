As requested we have performed a soil investigation at
the above referenced 10 lots to determine the general subsoil
conditions present to evaluate the feasibility of stabilizing
the upper over steepened cut slopes
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The subject lots are part of the Kalama Valley Subdivision
Phase I The original grading design in this area assumed
rock formation at shallow depths therefore very steep design
cut slope ratios of 4 1 H V were planned
However during the mass grading operations the initial six
feet of cut at 1 encountered moderately cemented boulder
colluvium instead of the anticipated rock formation Therefore
W O 423 20
Page 2
it was decided to construct a 5 foot wide horizontal bench
and continue cutting at a slope ratio of about 1 1 H V
Subsequent field observations of the cut slope during the
slope cutting operations indicated that the cementation of
the exposed boulder colluvium deposit varied considerably
and slope flattening would be required to reduce sloughing
of the cut slope face Various alternatives for reducing
the sloughing of the cut slopes were presented at that time
Test pits were later excavated into the cut slope by another
soil engineer FGE Ltd and encountered slickensided
boulder clay deposits These slickensided deposits generally
have low shear strength and are unstable at moderate to steep
slopes Based on these findings it appeared that slope
instability could occur due to weakening of these adobe
clay deposits unless the existing slopes were stabilized
The purpose of this investigation was to explore the subsur
face soil conditions below the steep cut slopes to delineate
the extent ofithe slickensided clay deposit and to evaluate
the feasibility of stabilizing the cut slope for house con
struction

SOIL CONDITIONS
The test pits encountered quite variable soil conditions at
the subject 10 lots
Rock formation was exposed on the slope surface at Lots 43 44
and at the bottom of the slope at Lots 51 52
Previous test pits by FGE Ltd encountered slickensided
adobe clay at Lots 48 49 and 50 Additional test pits
were excavated around these areas to delineate the extent of
the adobe clay The additional test pits encountered a
thin surface layer of adobe clay over medium to dense
boulder colluvium with a silty clay matrix Additional
areas of deep adobe clay were not encountered in the dug
pits

CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the up dated field exploration it appears that
the subject lots are situated within an old drainageway
which was eroded into the underlying rock formation and sub
sequently filled with boulder colluvium deposited from erosion
of the upper hillside areas The rock formation encountered
at Lots 43 44 and 51 52 appears to indicate that these lots
could be the limits of the former drainageway
It is our observation that more recent erosion gullies were
later cut into the boulder colluvium deposit and were filled
with adobe clay deposits
Based on the test pits it appears that the deep adobe
clay deposits are concentrated in Lots 48 49 and 50 The
remaining lots except for Lots 43 44 and 51 52 appear to
have a thinner layer of adobe clay about 2 to 3 feet
over medium dense boulder colluviun
Special lot grading would still be required for Lots 45 to
52 to stabilize the existing cut slope for house construc
tion The cut slopes at Lots 43 44 consist of rocky material
and could be left as is

Stabilization of Thick adobe Clay Lots Lots 48 to 50
Due to the very weak nature of adobe soils when saturated
and the presence of pre sheared slickensided zones the
thick adobe deposits would be subject to large slope creep
or slope failure during rainy periods when saturation of
the deposit has occurred In order to minimize the possibi
lity of later slope creep or failure special lot grading
would be required to stabilize the existing cut slope
Basically the stabilization would consist of reducing the
driving forces or increasing the resisting forces Typical
alternative methods of stabilization are presented below
1 Re cut slopes to a flatter stable slope of about
6 1 H V Due to the relatively steep terrain 4
horizontal to 1 vertical beyond the existing cut
area and the proximity of the project property line
this method is not feasible for the subject lots
2 Build up lower portion of the lots to create a flatter
slope and help to buttress the adobe deposit Due
to the limited lot area in front of the cut slope
it appears that a maximum slope of about 2 horizontal

3 Remove adobe deposit and replace with select soils
Complete adobe removal appears to be unfeasible
for these lots since the adobe deposit extends be

Due to the lot and property restrictions at the site it

We recommend that the adobe clay deposit be removed up to

Foundations
As discussed above reconstruction of the existing 141
steep slopes to a 2 horizontal to 1 vertical slope ratio is
recommended to stabilize the lot slopes
The toe of the reconstructed slope would generally fall
close to the front of the lot boundary Therefore most of
the lot would be sloping at about 2 horizontal to 1 vertical
The general house layout would then consist of cutting into
the reconstructed 2 1 H V fill slope to create the semi
basement level garage with the remaining living area situ
ated over the garage
W O 423 20
Page 9
Continuous wall footings could be used for the garage peri
meter wall Individual concrete pour in place pier footings
are recommended for other column support
In the level areas the light house footings should be
embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the outside finish
grade Retaining wall footing embedment should be a minimum
of 24 inches below finish grade On sloping areas the pier
footings should be deepened an additional amount to provide
a minimum 6 foot horizontal set back distance between the
outer edge of the footing and the slope face or a minimum
of 3 foot embedment
An allowable bearing value of 2500 psf can be used for the
footings bearing on the stiff natural ground or compacted
fill
Surface water should be diverted away from the house founda
tions A concrete drainage swale and cut off wall is recom
mended behind the house units to intercept run off from the
slope face and divert it into suitable drainage outlets
around the perimeter of the units

Recommendations presented under Foundations should be

Select granular material such as 3B Fine rock should be
used to backfill the excavations behind the walls This
would improve drainage and reduce the compactive effort
required for the wall backfill which will help to reduce
the build up of excess lateral pressures behind the wall
We recommend the wall should be designed to resist lateral
earth pressures as follows
Unrestrained walls with level backfill 35 pcf equivalent
active condition fluid pressure
Unrestrained walls with 2 1 sloping 45 pcf equivalent
backfill active condition fluid pressure
Restrained walls with level backfill 50 pcf equivalent
at rest condition fluid pressure
A coefficient of friction of 0 5 between the wall foundation
and the stiff natural soil is recommended to develop the
sliding resistance of the wall Additionally a passive soil
resistance of 250 pcf can be utilized

A subsurface drainage system should be used to prevent the

Impervious soil such as the on site silty clay soil should
be used for the top 2 feet of the backfill to reduce seepage
of surface runoff behind the walls
Garage Slab on Grade
The slab subgrade should be kept moist and protected from
drying prior to concrete placement The subgrade soils
should be compacted to a minimum of 90 of its maximum dry
density
It is recommended that the slab on grade be placed over a
4 inch layer of compacted base course
