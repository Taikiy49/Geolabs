2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae Volcano
became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank was partially
buried below Koolau lava banking against its eastern flank These banked or ponded
lava formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau on which the project
site is located
The project area is composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lava of the
Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows of jointed
dense vesicular basalt rock formation with inter bedded thin clinker layers In situ
chemical weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million
years The weathering process has formed a mantle of residual soils which grade to
saprolite with depth In general saprolite is mainly composed of silty material and is
typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite grades to basaltic rock
formation with increasing depth

2 4 Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic
plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic activity

Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during volcanic
eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground movement of
magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The Island of Hawaii
experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most are so small that they can
only be detected by sensitive instruments However some of the earthquakes are
strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on
the Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the
Island of Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii over 90 percent
are related to volcanism the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground shaking
diminishes with increased distance from the Island of Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has
experienced numerous earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5 M5 however

3 2 Slabs On Grade
In general we envision that slab on grade foundations with thickened edge
footings may be used to support the proposed new structures anticipated for the project
As previously indicated the project site is generally underlain by slightly to moderately
expansive clayey soils Slabs on grade supported by these expansive soils should be
designed properly to reduce the potential for future distress to the structures resulting
from wetting and subsequent swelling of the clayey soils
To reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from swelling of the
on site expansive soils we believe that non expansive select granular fill material
should be provided for a minimum depth of 12 inches below the slab cushion
Prior to placement of the select granular fill the clayey subgrades should be
scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction The subgrade soils should be maintained in a moist condition until
placement of the select granular fill The select granular fill material should provide a
capping layer of moist soil above the underlying potentially expansive subgrade soil
thereby reducing moisture loss from the clayey subgrade soils In our opinion the
reduction of drying and subsequent wetting of the subgrade soils should reduce the
impact of shrink swell effects on the slab caused by potential heaving of the underlying
expansive clayey soils when wetted The select granular fill should be

Alternatively a subdrain system could be constructed to collect the excess water from
landscaping irrigation For long term performance consideration should be given to a
subdrain system constructed adjacent to the paved landscaped areas
3 6 Utility Trenches
We anticipate that the utilities for the industrial condominiums project may
primarily consist of water sewer and drain lines In general good construction
practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling of the trenches for the new
utilities Based on the results of our field exploration it appears that conventional
excavation methods may generally be utilized for utility line excavation at the project
site The method and equipment to be used for trench excavation should be determined
by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations In addition the
excavations should comply with the applicable federal state and local safety
requirements The contractor should be responsible for trench shoring design and
installation
In general we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation for support under the pipes
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above
the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes It is critical to use this
free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches
of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench
backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage

The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level of 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of on site soils with a
maximum particle size of 6 inches or select granular fill material The backfill should be
placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to no less than
90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground
subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench
backfill below the pavement finished grade should be compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction
3 7 Drainage
It should be noted that surface and subsurface drainage at the project site are
especially critical considering the expansive nature of the in situ clayey soils Therefore
the finished grades outside the new buildings should be sloped to shed water away from
the slabs and foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding around the structure
It is also advised to install gutter systems around the buildings and to divert the
discharge away from the slab and foundation areas Excessive landscape watering near
the slabs and foundations should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should
be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for excessive
water infiltration into the subsurface
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce the potential for excessive water infiltration into the subsurface
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to
drain surface water runoff away from the slabs and foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling three borings designated
as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of about 21 5 feet below the existing
ground surface We excavated two field test pits designated as Test Pit Nos 1 and 2
extending to depths of about 5 and 8 5 feet below the existing ground surface We
collected two surface soil samples designated as Grab 1 and Grab 2 at the project
site In addition we collected two bulk samples of the near surface soils designated as
Bulk 1 and Bulk 2 at selected locations within the new pavement areas of the project
site to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the near surface soils The
approximate boring test pit grab sample and bulk sample locations are shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2 We drilled the borings using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight augers We excavated the test pits using a Case 850 backhoe
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings and test pits by
visual and textural examination in the field He observed the drilling and excavation
operations on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by
visual observation and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general
conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 3 and Logs of Test Pits Plate A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
We performed pocket penetrometer tests on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
