SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
Of interest to our geotechnical analysis for the geotechnical aspects of the 
project are the subsurface materials encountered at the project site, the engineering 
properties of the materials encountered, and the variability of the subsurface conditions 
across the project site. Therefore, the following subsections provide a description of the

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age (2.7 to 3.4 million years ago) and forms the bulk of the western one-
third of the island. The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early 
Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age (2.2 to 2.5 million years ago) and forms majority of the 
eastern two-thirds of the island. The end of volcanic activity associated with the 
Koolau Volcano was followed by a long period of landmass erosion whereby deep 
stream valleys were carved into the dome-shaped Koolau Shield Volcano. 
Approximately 850,000 to 1,000,000 years ago, some limited volcanic activity 
resumed with the eruption of the Honolulu Volcanic Series from secondary vents 
scattered across the eastern portion of the Island of Oahu. The eruptions occurred 
along the established secondary rift zones of the Koolau Volcano. Some of the most 
notable landmarks located on the Island of Oahu, such as Punchbowl and Diamond 
Head Craters, and including Aliamanu Crater, were created by the eruption of the 
Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of 
the sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available 
such that the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level.

The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
filled pre-existing depressions and accumulated at higher elevations. 
The rock formations are mainly inclined downward towards the central volcanic 
vent. The volcanic tuff rock comprising Aliamanu Crater was created by the fall-out 
deposition of super-heated volcanic ash and cinders containing some entrained blocks 
of basaltic lava rock, which were emitted from the Aliamanu volcanic vent.

3.1 
Facility Excavation 
In general, we observed one main geologic unit within the facility. The main 
geologic unit is Quaternary Honolulu Tuffs (Qht). Tuff from vents of the Honolulu 
Volcanic Series consists of volcanic ash, which consolidated or hardened to form a 
medium hard to very hard rock that resembles siltstone or sandstone, locally called 
“mudrock”. The tuff is frequently found overlying coralline deposits or older alluvium 
units locally. 
The tuff encountered in the borings within the excavation areas was described as 
medium hard to very hard rock and massive (general fracture spacing greater than 
24 inches).  The borings encountered minimal zones of moderately (general fracture 
spacing of 6 to 12 inches) to slightly fractured (general fracture spacing of 12 to 
24 inches) tuff.  The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) values of the tuff encountered in 
the borings ranged from about 35 to 100 percent.  The RQD values of the tuff 
anticipated within the excavation areas range between 87 to 100 percent, 
corresponding to good to excellent rock quality.    
Rock Mass Rating (RMR), developed by Z.T. Bieniawski (1989), was determined 
during our field exploration.  Based on our observations, the average RMR values of the 
tuff anticipated within the excavation areas ranged between 59 and 79.  The RMR 
values generally correspond to Rating II, Good Rock and Rating III, Fair Rock.

3.2.1 Rock Anchors 
If it is not feasible to provide uplift resistance with only a shallow foundation system, 
we recommend using rock anchors in conjunction with shallow foundations to 
provide resistance to uplift loads.  In general, the rock anchor system is constructed 
by drilling a borehole, installing a steel tendon, or reinforcing bar into the borehole, 
and grouting the borehole.  The basic components of a grouted rock anchor consist 
of the anchorage and the bonded/unbonded lengths of the steel tendon or 
reinforcing bar.  In general, the unbonded length of the rock anchor is the portion of 
the steel tendon (or reinforcing bar) that does not have direct contact with the grout, 
allowing the tendon (or reinforcing bar) to elongate elastically, transferring the 
resisting force from the structure to the bonded length.  The bonded length of the 
rock anchor is the portion of the steel tendon (or reinforcing bar) that is directly 
bonded to the grout allowing it to transfer the applied load into the ground. 
We recommend rock anchors with a minimum diameter drilled of 4.5 inches.  In 
general, we envision that the rock anchors would derive uplift support principally 
from the bonded resistance between the grout and the hard volcanic tuff rock 
formation.  The bond stress, which is the pullout resistance per unit area of the 
grout/soil interface contact, is variable depending on the type of bearing material 
and grout, the overburden stress, and the construction procedures used in installing 
the rock anchors.   
The rock anchors should have a minimum unbonded length of 5 feet.  The 
unbonded length should consist of a 4.5-inch diameter permanent steel casing.  In 
general, the rock anchors should have a minimum center-to-center spacing of 
about 5 feet to avoid reduction in pull-out load capacity and to facilitate drilling of the 
rock anchors.  Our recommendations for the rock anchor uplift load capacities, 
bonded lengths, and total rock anchor lengths are presented in the following table.

3.7.4 Cut and Fill Slopes 
In general, we believe that the planned cut slopes at the new water tank site and 
the generator/transformer pad location would expose hard and massive volcanic 
tuff rock.  However, our observations indicate that the volcanic tuff could expose 
thin bedding layers at the top of the cut slope face.  These bedding layers would dip 
down adversely toward the face of the cut slopes. To provide stable cut slopes in 
volcanic tuff, these cut slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of one 
horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V) or flatter. Where a steeper inclination is required 
for the cut slopes exposing volcanic tuff, the cut slopes at these locations may be 
designed with a slope inclination of ½H:1V provided that a shotcrete facing is 
constructed on the cut slope face.  As discussed above, bedding layers dipping 
downward toward the cut slope face could be exposed at the top of the cut slope.  
These bedding layers would likely increase the potential instability of a relatively 
high and steep cut slope.  To reduce the potential cut slope instability, rock anchors 
should be installed along the top of the cut slope face prior to placing the shotcrete 
facing. Further details for the shotcrete facing and rock anchor design are provided 
in the “Shotcrete Facing” section of this report.

above the facility consists of residual and cinder deposits extending to depths of 
about 4 to 15 feet below the existing ground surface underlain by volcanic tuff rock 
formation.  The facility access road along the crater side consists of fill and colluvial 
deposits extending to depths of about 20 to 24 feet below the existing ground 
surface underlain by volcanic tuff rock formation.  The water tank site encountered

Shotcrete Facing 
We understand that relatively high and steep cut slopes are planned at the new 
water tank location.  In addition, relatively high and steep cut slopes are planned at the 
generator/transformer location, which is located at the facility crater access road.  These 
cut slopes exposing volcanic tuff rock formation may be steepened to about ½H:1V. 
The cut slopes at the new water tank and the generator/transformer locations 
would generally expose hard and massive volcanic tuff rock.  Based on our 
observations, thin bedding layers could be exposed at the top of the cut slope face.  
These bedding layers would likely dip down adversely toward the face of the cut slopes 
resulting in potential slope instability.  To provide a more stable cut slope face along the 
top portion of the cut slope, we believe that rock anchors may need to be installed along 
the top of the cut slope face.  
For estimation purposes, we recommend installing a single row of rock anchors 
about 5 feet above the top of the cut slope face.  The rock anchors should have a 
horizontal spacing of about 10 feet center-to-center.  The rock anchor should be 
installed by drilling a 4-inch diameter hole with an inclination that is normal to the slope 
face.  The grout should have a minimum 28-day compressive strength of 4,000 psi or 
greater.  The rock anchor should have an ultimate pullout capacity of 20 kips.  Based on 
our analyses, we recommend the total length of the rock anchors to be approximately 
10 feet.  The total length includes 5 feet of the unbonded length and 5 feet of the 
bonded length.  After successfully installing the rock anchors, the rock anchors should 
be post-tensioned to a lock-off load of at least 5 kips.

After placement of the rock anchors, we recommend placing a minimum 6-inch 
thick shotcrete facing along the cut slope face to reduce the rockfall potential and cover 
irregularities in the rock formation. We recommend using fiber reinforced shotcrete with 
a minimum 28-day unconfined compressive strength of 4,000 psi and follow ACI 
PRC-506.1-08, Guide to Fiber-Reinforced Shotcrete.  The fiber reinforcement should 
consist of synthetic macro fiber reinforcement in accordance with ASTM C1116-10a, 
Strux 85/50, or accepted equal. The shotcrete should also contain 128 ounces per cubic 
yard of shrinkage reducing admixture.  The water to cement ratio should be limited to 
0.45. We recommend a thorough cure of the shotcrete in accordance with ACI 308R-16.  
Any areas requiring bonding to cured areas such as joints or layered placements should 
be cleaned to remove curing which will affect bonding of the subsequent layer without 
damage to the previous layer of shotcrete. 
The cut slope protected with shotcrete facing should be well-drained to reduce 
the potential build-up of hydrostatic pressures.  A typical drainage system for the 
shotcrete facing would consist of 2-foot wide strips of a prefabricated drainage product, 
such as Miradrain or Enkadrain, placed on the face of the slope at a maximum 
horizontal spacing of 8 feet on-center.  The prefabricated drainage product should 
extend from the top to the base of the slope and should be hydraulically connected to a 
weephole near the toe of the slope.   
The shotcrete should be placed from the lower part of the cut slope area upwards 
to prevent accumulation of rebound.  The nozzleman should maintain a position to 
orient the nozzle at a distance and approximately perpendicular to the working face so 
that rebound will be minimal and compaction will be maximized. The rebound should be 
removed by blowing and raking off to avoid sand pockets in the shotcrete. 
The nozzleman should maintain the water amount at a practicable minimum so 
the mix properly adheres to the cut slope face. Water content should not become high 
enough to cause the mix to sag or fall from vertical or inclined surfaces or to separate in 
horizontal layers.  The shotcrete placement should be suspended in the event of high 
winds or heavy rain that may prevent a proper application or cause cement washing out 
or mortar sloughing.

3.12 Corrosion Potential 
Laboratory corrosion tests, including pH, minimum resistivity, chloride content, 
and sulfate content, were performed on selected samples obtained during our field 
exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of the near-surface soils at the project site. The 
Chloride Content (EPA 300.0) and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0) tests were performed 
by Eurofins TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. The test results are summarized and 
presented in Appendix C.  
The method used to control the corrosion of underground pipelines and 
structures is dependent on the resistivity, pH, chloride content, and sulfate content 
found in the soil. Based on the Board of Water Supply, City & County of Honolulu Water 
System External Corrosion Control Standards, dated 2021, soils with a resistivity of less 
than or equal to 5,000 ohm-cm, a chloride content of greater than or equal to 500 parts 
per million (ppm), a sulfate content of greater than or equal to 1,000 ppm, or a pH of 
less than or equal to 6.5 should be considered moderately to severely corrosive.  These 
moderately to severely corrosive soils should be classified as Corrosion Category A.  
Based on the results of corrosivity testing, the subsurface soils at the project site 
exhibit minimum resistivity values ranging from approximately 700 to 5,000 ohm-cm.  
The subsurface soils also exhibit pH values ranging from about 7.73 to 8.29, chloride 
content values ranging from about 5 to 310 ppm and the sulfate content values ranging 
from not being detected in some soil samples to about 180 ppm.  The minimum 
resistivity values indicate that the subsurface soils are moderately to severely corrosive; 
therefore, the subsurface soils encountered at the site should be classified as Corrosion

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at around the facility by drilling and 
sampling eleven borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101(A), 102(B), 103(C), 104(D), 
105(E), 106(F), 107 through 110, and 202 extending to depths of about 21.6 to 149 feet 
below the existing ground surface. One boring, designated as Boring No. 201, was 
drilled at the water tank location to a depth of about 81.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface.  In addition, four shallow borings were drilled to depths of about 3 to 5 feet 
below the existing ground surface at various locations across the site for infiltration 
testing.  The approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. The borings were drilled using truck-mounted drill rigs equipped with continuous 
flight augers and coring tools. 
Our geologists classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-16. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in 
general accordance with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving 
a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance 
with ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core 
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the

Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. 
For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.

would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used

Seismic Shear Wave Velocity Tests 
Seismic shear wave velocity profiling of the subsurface materials at the project 
site was performed using Seismic Cone Penetration Testing (SCPT) equipment. The 
purpose of the seismic shear wave velocity profiling of the subsurface materials was to 
more closely analyze the seismic design considerations for the project. Shear wave 
velocity testing using seismic cone penetration test equipment was performed at four 
selected boring locations, designated as B-102(B), B-103(C), B-105(E) and B-108, as 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The seismic shear wave velocity profiling was 
performed at various depths extending to depths of about 85.1 to 128.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface. 
In order to conduct the seismic shear wave velocity test in the boring, the test 
boring was advanced utilizing rotary coring methods to the maximum depth of the 
boring. Log of the materials encountered in the boring are presented on the Logs of 
Borings in Appendices A and B. After the boring was advanced to the maximum depth 
of the borehole, the bored hole was backfilled with 0.25-inch diameter coated bentonite 
pellets. The temporary casing from the coring operations was used as a tremie pipe to 
place the bentonite pellets starting from the bottom and advancing upward. When the 
bentonite pellets are in contact with the groundwater in the borehole, the pellets start to 
hydrate slowly. As the bentonite pellets hydrate, they swell and soften. The probe was 
then pushed through the softened bentonite extending to depths of about 85.1 to 128.5 
feet below the existing ground surface using seismic cone penetration testing 
equipment (SCPT). 
The seismic shear wave velocity test consists of hydraulically pushing a 10-ton 
steel electronic subtraction cone with an apex angle of 60 degrees and a projected 
surface area of 1.55 square inches (10 square centimeters) into the bored hole. The 
cone carries a uniaxial horizontal accelerator geophone to detect the arrival of a shear 
wave generated and propagated from the ground surface. The seismic measurements 
were made when the SCPT had stopped, and a shear wave was sent into the 
subsurface. A shear wave was generated at the surface by striking a loaded plank with 
a switched hammer. The propagation time of the wave from the hammer blow to the 
cone was measured at each discrete depth interval. The vector difference of these 
depths divided by the time difference for the shear wave to arrive at the various depths 
provided the average shear wave velocity over the depth interval. 
The seismic shear wave velocities measured, and the weighted average seismic 
shear wave velocity calculated for full depth of the soil profile at the boring location are 
presented on Plates B-1.1 through B-4.3 in Appendix B. The weighted average shear 
wave velocity was calculated based on the average shear wave velocity method 
described in Chapter 20 of the American Society of Civil Engineers (ASCE) 7-16, 
Minimum Design Loads for Buildings and Other Structures.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation 
of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the 
appropriate sample depths. 
One Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D4318) was performed on a selected soil 
sample to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits to aid in soil classifications. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Graphic presentations of the test results are provided on Plate C-1. 
Eleven Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plates C-2 through C-4. 
Thirty-Nine Uniaxial Compression tests (ASTM D7012, Method C) were 
performed on selected core runs to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the 
volcanic tuff rock formation encountered. Test results are presented on Plates C-5 thru 
C-7. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected sample to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested. The test results are 
presented on Plate C-9. 
One Modified Proctor compaction test (ASTM D1557) was performed on a bulk 
sample obtained to evaluate the relationship between the moisture content and the dry 
density of the near-surface soils. The test results are presented on Plate C-10. 
Corrosivity tests, including five sets of pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), were 
performed by Geolabs and Eurofins TestAmerica on selected soil samples obtained 
from our field exploration. The test results are summarized on Plate C-15.
