REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age As volcanic activity in the
Waianae Volcano ceased lava flows from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded
eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as the Schofield Plateau The project
site is located on the southwestern flank of the Waianae Volcano
Physical and chemical weathering followed by the erosion of Schofield Plateau
generated sediments which were transported to the coast In the project vicinity and to
the south these sediments accumulated and are interbedded with marine sediments and
coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge The thickness of the
sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1 Highway to
over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the
confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present

During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea
levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposited lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet
waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys
in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the calcareous
reef materials Placement of man made fills and commercial development of the area
within the last 20 years have brought the project area to its present form

Coral Outcrop CR
Coral outcrop consists of coral or cemented calcareous sand formed in shallow
ocean water during the time the ocean stand was at a higher level It is exposed
on the ocean shore on the coastal plains and at the foot of the uplands Coral
outcrop makes up about 80 to 90 percent of the acreage The remaining 10 to
20 percent consists of a thin layer of friable red soil material in cracks crevices
and depressions within the coral outcrop
Mamala Stony Silty Clay Loam 0 to 12 Percent Slopes MnC
This type of soil was formed in alluvium deposited over coral limestone and
consolidated calcareous sand It is dark reddish brown in color neutral to mildly
alkaline with moderate permeability Runoff is very slow to medium and the
erosion hazard is slight to moderate
The coral formation in the project area generally appears to be moderately
weathered and medium hard to hard in nature It should be noted that the strength
characteristics of coral formation are highly variable In addition the depositional nature of
the coral formation may create localized areas of hard crystalline coral intermixed with
soft severely fractured detritus debris It should be noted that coral formation is
frequently cavernous or contains pockets of voids Therefore the potential for the
presence of cavities and or voids within the coral formation exists across the project site
In addition cavities in the coral formation could be filled with soft and or loose coralline
detritus and alluvial deposits
Our field observations indicate that the near surface alluvial soils generally exist

addition our experience in the Ewa area suggests that the near surface soils may
exhibit moderate to high shrink swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in
the soil moisture contents These conditions are exacerbated by the generally

Cavities and Voids
As mentioned previously coral formations are generally highly variable in terms of
hardness weathering and fracturing In addition cavities and or voids are commonly
encountered in coral formations in the project vicinity In many cases cavities and voids in
the coral formation may be filled with loose coralline deposits In areas where natural
coralline deposits are anticipated near the ground surface special attention should be
given to the subgrade preparation to reduce the potential for distress to the future
improvements such as floor slabs and pavements
The excavated subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches where
possible moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and proof rolled
with a minimum 10 ton vibratory drum roller to detect the presence of potential
near surface cavities sinkholes or loose zones The vibratory drum roller should be
operated at a speed of about 200 to 300 feet per minute If the proof rolling operations
detect a near surface cavity or loose zones the area should be ripped open cleaned of
the debris and then backfilled with compacted fill materials or lean concrete

Deep Shrinkage Cracks
Our field observations and experience in the area indicate that shrinkage cracks
are commonly present at the existing ground surface underlain by the alluvial soils as
described previously In general shrinkage cracks will develop in the ground due to the
lack of maintaining the soil moisture content as the surface soils start to dry out under
the sun and dry weather conditions The development of shrinkage cracks in the
ground is apparently intensified by the prolonged dry climatic conditions that are
characteristic of the Kapolei area resulting in large fluctuations of the soil moisture
content
If the existing ground cracks are left unattended for an extended period of time
and are allowed to penetrate deeper below the surface the ground cracks could
adversely affect the performance of the future building foundations near the areas with
large shrinkage cracks The ground cracks could serve as conduits for water to
percolate into the foundation soils and cause loss of strength of the foundation soils
resulting in potential settlement of the building structure In addition migration of the
foundation soils into the openings of the shrinkage cracks could undermine the
foundation system and result in potential settlement of the building structure Therefore
remedial work to correct or mitigate the observed ground cracks should be implemented
prior to fill placement and construction of the proposed structures
In general we believe that the proposed building and pavement footprints should
be over excavated by about 3 feet below the existing ground surface to remove the
zone with shrinkage cracks after clearing and grubbing The over excavation should
also extend laterally by about 5 feet beyond the building and driveway limits If
walkways are planned around the building structures the over excavation should
extend laterally by about 5 feet beyond the outside edge of the walkways
The over excavated subgrades and other on grade areas outside building and
pavement footprints should then be proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton vibratory drum
roller for about six to eight passes to assist in detecting and collapsing near surface
voids and loose zones created by the shrinkage cracks The exposed subgrades
should be proof rolled without moisture conditioning the subgrade soils at the
pre existing moisture contents Loose areas disclosed during the proof rolling
operations should be backfilled with general fill material compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction The proof rolling operations should be conducted
under the near continuous observation of the project geotechnical engineer

Slabs On Grade
In addition to the over excavation of the expansive alluvial soils and replacement
with non expansive select granular fill materials the building slabs on grades underlain
by the expansive alluvial soils be at least 5 inches thick and should be reinforced with
reinforcing bars spaced at least 18 inches in each direction in lieu of the standard
welded wire fabric This recommendation is provided to reduce the potential for
structural distress from differential movements of the slabs due to the expansive nature
of the on site alluvial soils
For interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic or machine vibration
we recommend placing a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of
open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation below the slabs The open graded
gravel cushion fill would provide uniform support of the slabs and would serve as a
capillary moisture break To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration through
the slab and subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture barrier
such as a plastic membrane is recommended on top of the open graded gravel cushion
fill layer Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered
because they can better mask minor slab cracking We also recommend that interior
walls be designed to incorporate some flexibility to accommodate a small amount of
possible ground movements
Where the slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic or machinery vibration we
recommend providing a 6 inch layer of aggregate subbase below the slabs in lieu of the
4 inch thick gravel cushion fill layer The aggregate subbase should consist of crushed
basaltic aggregates compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Where
slabs are intended to function as rigid pavements for trucks a minimum slab thickness
of 6 inches may be used for preliminary design purposes Provisions should be made
for proper load transfer across the slab joints that will be subjected to vehicular traffic
The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at least
12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
In order to reduce the potential for distress due to differential movements
between the more heavily loaded foundations and the lightly loaded building slabs we
recommend using free floating slabs on grade with no structural connections to the wall
and column foundations Joint filler and sealant may be used to fill the openings
between the edges of the slabs and other structural elements To further reduce the
potential for distress to the building slabs on grade and foundations resulting from water
infiltration into the subsurface from areas immediately adjacent to the building
foundations we recommend considering a concrete sidewalk or pavements around
the perimeter of the building structures The non expansive select granular fill
recommended below the building slabs if applicable should extend out to the edges of
the concrete sidewalk and should be compacted to at least 90 percent relative

Lot Drainage
It should be emphasized that proper surface drainage especially within the lots is
critical to the performance of foundations and slabs constructed on expansive soil
conditions Therefore the finished grades outside the building structure should be
sloped to shed water away from the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding
In addition we recommend installing gutter systems around the building and diverting the
discharge away from the foundation and pavement areas
Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be
avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and
drains to reduce the potential for appreciable water infiltration into the subsurface soils
Drainage swales with positive drainage should be provided as soon as possible and
maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the foundations and slabs
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation systems because ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
