INTRODUCTION
In accordance with your request we have performed a geotechnical engineering
exploration of the subject project site This geotechnical engineering exploration report
does not include ground penetrating radar GPR reconnaissance because the site is not
accessible for our field crew This preliminary report is to provide pertinent grading and
foundation recommendations so that the engineering design may continue
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our preliminary geotechnical exploration was to obtain an overview
of the subsurface conditions at the project site in order to obtain data for the formulation
of preliminary geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the design of the
site grading and structure foundations for the proposed water reservoir
The project site is located downslope westerly of the Mamalahoa Highway and
upslope easterly of the HR5 well site in the Huehue Ranch Off Site Water System Project
Site on the Big Island of Hawaii

The area is characterized by topography which slopes slightly to moderately
downward toward the west The topography is formed by the original structure of the
volcanic flows which have covered the site The site is characterized by pahoehoe lava
flows with open surface blisters and collapsed surface lava tubes The area was
moderately vegetated with silver oak trees and grasses prior to clearing with a D 9
bulldozer to create access for the drilling rig
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five 5 shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is located on the northern flank of Hualalai mountain which makes
up the west central portion of the island and consists partly of the prehistoric basaltic lava
flows of the Hualalai Volcanic Series which were most likely deposited during the late
Pleistocene Epoch Sterns and MacDonald 1946
Hawaiian volcanic activity is typified by an abundance of flank eruptions along rift
zones or zones of weakness in the earth s crust The project site is situated in the
northwest rift zone of Huailalai
The lava flows at the site are of the pahoehoe type consisting of dense zones of
hard rock and loose zones of clinker with voids of varying degrees overlying and
intermixed with older pahoehoe flows The clinker zones are highly permeable and lava
tubes are not uncommon in pahoehoe flows

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
In general our field reconnaissance indicates that the proposed reservoir site is
underlain by volcanic lava flows or basaltic rock formation to the maximum depths drilled
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings during the time or our
exploration
Based on our findings construction of the proposed reservoir is feasible provided
that our recommendations are followed The underlying volcanic rock formation should
provide adequate bearing for the proposed reservoir structure Due to the existence of
cavities within the volcanic formation we recommend that a program consisting of
cavity void probing and subsequent void filling or grouting below the reservoir foundation
be implemented prior to foundation construction This program is discussed in more
detail below

Site Preparation
Prior to the commencement of any earthwork all areas within the grading limits
should be cleared of all vegetation and deleterious materials The resulting stripped
material should be removed from and disposed of properly off site Any voids or yielding
areas disclosed during the clearing operations should be over excavated to firm ground
and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted engineered
rock granular fill
Based on our exploration the majority of the materials generated from site
excavation will be various sizes of volcanic basalt rock fragments and some clinker
material If larger sizes are properly broken down or processed the on site excavated
rock materials should be suitable for use as engineered fill if it is placed and compacted
per the following recommendations If additional imported material is required the
material should conform generally to the requirements stipulated in Section 18 of the
Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction Borrow Fill dated September
1986
If the excavated volcanic materials at the site are processed and reduced to a
relatively well graded granular material with an average maximum size of about six 6
inches and absolute maximum size of twelve 12 inches the material may be utilized as
structural fill to within three 8 feet below reservoir and roadway finish subgrade
elevations Generally the upper three 3 feet of structural fills within structural areas
should consist of non expansive well graded granular materials with a maximum particle
dimension of three 3 inches
In areas to receive fills the existing surface should be proof rolled with a vibratory
drum roller with a static weight of a minimum of 20 tons to expose and collapse the near
surface voids Any exposed voids should be backfilled as described above

Generally three inch minus finer silty and gravelly fill materials should be placed
in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of
90 percent of the maximum dry density as established by ASTM D 1557 78 Trench and
structural backfills within three 3 feet from finish subgrade elevations should be placed
in level lifts not exceeding eight 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a
minimum of 90 maximum dry density per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 The upper
two 2 feet of fills and backfills below road subgrade level and the upper three 3 feet
of fills and backfills below footing or slab subgrade level should be compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent maximum dry density
Any fills to be placed on slopes having ratios steeper than five horizontal to one
vertical 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the underlying soil or basalt formation
to lessen potential instability of the new fill A schematic representation of keying and
benching is shown on the attached detail Plate 3
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in rock fills composed
of excavated basalt lava rock material Instead a testing program to determine the
number of passes of a certain compactor needed to achieve the desired level of
compaction should be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project On the
basis of this testing program the number of passes may then be used as the field
criterion for adequate compaction In general a minimum number of about five 5 to six
6 passes would be needed over each location
Excavation
The drilled borings indicate that the site is generally underlain by clinker and
weathered jointed basalt materials which may be excavated by conventional
methods such as ripping with a D 9 bulldozer In some localized instances where

the rock core recovery indicated relatively high RQD values hard excavation
utilizing a hoe ram may be required
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the subsurface materials are
based on our visual observations of the existing rock outcrops in the area and our
interpretation of the recovered boring rock core data
We recommend that all prospective contractors bidding on this site should examine
the rock outcrops and the boring data in order to make their own interpretation
In general cut slopes in dense basalt rock less than 20 feet in height may be
designed to a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V ratio Cut slopes in less
dense materials such as loose clinker cinder or soils and fill slopes should be
designed to a flatter cut slope ratio of about one and one half horizontal to one
vertical 0V
In general cut and fill slopes in excess of 20 feet in vertical height should have an
eight foot wide drainage bench constructed in the mid portion of the slope The
above general guideline may be waived by the project soils engineer on an
individual basis depending on the actual rock density condition
Soil slopes should be protected as soon as possible after construction to minimize
the erosion potential
Surface water from areas above the slope should generally be diverted away from
the slope face and to minimize run off water flowing across the slope face

Structure Foundations
Based on the data obtained during our field exploration the structure foundations
are likely to bear on in situ volcanic rock formation ranging from moderately hard basalt
rock to loose clinker Due to the possibility that lava rock conditions at the approximate
finish floor elevations will vary special consideration should be given to subgrade
preparation due to these variable conditions
Where the structures will span between very distinct and differing subgrade
conditions i e hard basalt grading to loose clinker or structural fill the slab and footing
areas should be ripped to a depth of 18 inches below the bottom of the slab or footing
excavation in the areas of hard basalt and replaced with well graded three inch minus
rock fill materials compacted to a minimum of 95 percent of their maximum dry density
to lessen the potential for differential settlement across the structure On site materials
properly processed to form a well graded gravel with a maximum size of about three 3
inches may be utilized as a source of the specified select backfill material All material
selected for use should be tested and approved by the geotechnical engineer prior to
placement
Providing that these recommendations are followed an allowable bearing pressure
up to 4 000 p s f may be used for the design of reservoir foundations This value may
be increased by one third 1 3 when temporary wind or seismic loads are considered
The bottom of footings should be embedded a minimum of twelve 12 inches
below the lowest adjacent finished grade Footings located next to utility trenches should
be extended down to an elevation equal to or deeper than that of the trench bottom The
reservoir s waterline trench backfill which should be completed prior to foundation
construction should be backfilled with lean concrete at the location near the reservoir s
perimeter footing

The site grading should also be designed to divert surface water away from the

Footing Settlement
Based upon the boring data and the anticipated reservoir loads the estimated total
spread or line footing settlements would generally be on the order of less than
one half 2 of an inch total and one fourth 4 of an inch differential settlements
between the interior and adjacent perimeter footings
Lateral Resistance
For passive earth pressure an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 p s f per foot of
depth may be used A friction factor of 0 4 may be utilized to determine the sliding
resistance of the structure
Cavity Probing and Grouting
To reduce the possibility of reservoir footing settlement due to the potential
collapse of loose pockets or cavities in clinker or lava tubes in the underlying lava
rock the footing excavations should be probed either with ground penetrating
radar GPR or with percussion drill rig at approximately ten 10 feet on centers
for continuous footing A minimum of one 1 probe should be drilled at each
column location for spread footings If a cavity is located or suspected below a
footing additional probe holes should be made to delineate the horizontal extent

of the cavity The probe holes should have a minimum diameter of three 3
inches and extend below the bottom of the footing to a depth of at least ten 10
feet The probe holes should be grouted with a sand cement slurry mixture from
the bottom of the hole upwards using low pressures The probing and grouting
operations should be monitored by the project geotechnical engineer to see that
these recommendations are carried our properly and to locate potential voids near
the footing excavations as well as to recommend modifications to the above
grouting procedures should the grout loss is excessive which may expedite the
construction program

LIMITATIONS
The analysis and recommendations submitted in this preliminary report are based
in part upon information obtained from field data points such as borings Variations of
conditions between the field data points may occur and the nature and extent of these
variations may not become evident until construction If variations then appear evident
it will be necessary to re evaluate the recommendations given in this report
The locations of the field data points were determined by measurement from a
point located in the field by the project surveyor Elevations of the data points were
determined by interpolation from elevation points on the plans prepared by Akinaka
Associates Ltd The physical location and elevation of the field data points should be
considered accurate only to the degree implied by the method used
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the field data points
depict the approximate boundaries between soil types and as such may denote a
gradual transition The strata lines shown on profiles or cross section are based upon
interpolation between field data points and may not represent actual subsurface
conditions

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the proposed reservoir site were explored by the
drilling and sampling of two 2 borings to a depth of 40 feet below the existing ground
surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were
drilled by truck mounted rotary drill rigs advancing continuous flight augers or rotary and
core drilling tools as appropriate
The materials encountered in the borings were examined and classified in general
accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2488 84 Description and Identification of
Soils Visual Manual Procedure by a field engineer from our firm
Core samples of a soil and rock materials encountered at the site were obtained
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is rotary
drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material
left in the annulus of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination classification and
testing
Recovery REC is used a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 and would be shown on
the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses This designation is only applicable to cores retrieved from in situ
rock formations RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material
that is in excess of four 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks if 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0
foot core run the RQD would be 50 and would be shown on the Boring Logs as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical of Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
Very Poor 0 25
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100
The rippability or foundation support characteristics of a rock mass is a function
of the relative hardness of the rock its relative quality brittleness and fissile
characteristics A dense basalt with a high RQD would be very difficult to rip and would
probably require blasting or splitting for excavation
