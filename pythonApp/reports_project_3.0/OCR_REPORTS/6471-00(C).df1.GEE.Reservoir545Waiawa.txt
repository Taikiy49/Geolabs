SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered competent fill soils in the existing 
reservoir embankment.  Our exploration also indicated that the reservoir may be 
decommissioned by breaching the embankments with conventional earthmoving 
equipment.  Based on our laboratory tests, the embankment soils exhibit a low to 
moderate expansion potential; and, are a potential source of general fill material. 
In general, cut and fill slopes may be designed to match the adjacent reservoir 
walls with a maximum slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. Any slope having a vertical 
height exceeding 20 feet should have midslope benches.  The benches should have a 
minimum width of 5 feet and be graded to shed water away from the slope face. 
The soil in the existing reservoir embankment may be considered as a resource for 
fill material.  The construction of stockpiles should conform to a Stockpile Plan prepared by 
the project civil engineer.   
We did not encounter groundwater in the hand auger probes at the time of our field 
exploration. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
design recommendations.

Project Considerations 
The project sites are in the Waiawa Ridge area of the south central portion of the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The sites are former plantation reservoirs that were used for 
the impoundment of irrigation water and are identified as follows: 
Oahu Reservoir 510 
Oahu Reservoir 530 
Oahu Reservoir 545A 
Use of the reservoirs was discontinued in about the mid to late 1970’s and it is 
desired to decommission the reservoirs by breaching the earth dam embankments.  It is 
anticipated that the embankments will be completely removed. 
To support the design efforts, we have provided an exploration consisting of a 
series of borings to explore existing subsurface conditions and conditions in the existing 
embankments. At the time of our field exploration, the roads to the Oahu Reservoirs 510 
and 530 sites were muddy with deep ruts; therefore, four-wheel drive equipment was 
required to access those probe locations.  Reservoir 545A is only accessible by hiking 
from the end of the dirt access road.  Those borings were drilled using hand auger 
probes.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from 
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to 
be middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the 
Island. The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early 
Pleistocene in age and forms the eastern two-thirds of the island. As volcanic activity in 
Waianae Volcano ceased, lava flows from Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded 
eastern slope forming a broad plateau, known as Schofield Plateau. 
Following the extrusion of lavas in the early Pleistocene Epoch, the island 
underwent a long cycle of erosion and weathering forming the prominent ridgelines and 
summits as we know today. During the erosion period, the interfluvial ridges and areas 
such as the Schofield Plateau were subjected to deep chemical weathering, forming a 
mantle of residual soil and saprolite which grade with depth to weathered basalt rock 
formation. 
The project site is situated on the southeast portion of the Schofield Plateau.  
The majority of the project area is underlain by residual clayey silt soils which grade with 
depth to saprolite over basalt rock formation.  Some minor surface fills are present due 
to agricultural development in the area.  The existing reservoir consists of earth berms 
that have been constructed across swales forming an open reservoir which is now 
abandoned.   
2.2 
Site Description 
The project site is situated on Waiawa Ridge to the east of Interstate Route H-2, 
as shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.  The project site is located on the north 
side of Hawaii Interstate Freeway H-1 and H-2 Interchange in the Waiawa area on the 
Island of Oahu.  The general area is bounded by Waiawa Correctional Facility to the 
north, Waiawa Stream to the west, Hawaii Interstate Freeway H-2 to the south, and 
Panaikauahi Gulch to the east.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered competent fill soils in the existing 
reservoir embankment.  Our exploration also indicated that the reservoir may be 
decommissioned by breaching the embankment with conventional earthmoving 
equipment.  Based on our laboratory tests, the embankment soils exhibit a low to 
moderate expansion potential; and, are a potential source of general fill material. 
In general, cut and fill slopes may be designed to match the adjacent reservoir 
walls with a maximum slope inclination of two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or 
flatter. Slopes having a vertical height exceeding 20 feet should have midslope 
benches.  The benches should have a minimum width of 5 feet and be graded to shed 
water away from the slope face. 
The soil in the existing reservoir embankment may be considered as a resource 
for fill material.  The construction of stockpiles should conform to a Stockpile Plan 
prepared by the project civil engineer.   
We did not encounter groundwater in the hand auger probes at the time of our 
field exploration. 
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical recommendations for 
the design of the reservoir decommissioning project are presented in the following 
sections:

3.1.1 Site Preparation 
The soil in the existing reservoir embankment is a resource of potential fill material.  
To avoid contaminating this resource with vegetation, debris, deleterious material, 
and other unsuitable materials, the areas within the contract grading limits should 
be cleared and grubbed thoroughly at the on-set of earthwork. The grubbed 
materials should be removed and disposed properly off-site or disposed in a 
designated area. 
3.1.2 Reservoir Berm Demolition 
We have assumed that the existing berm will be completely removed to 
decommission the existing reservoir.  Our field exploration indicates that the interior 
of the existing reservoir impoundment berm consists of soil materials that may be 
removed by conventional earthmoving equipment.  These materials may be 
stockpiled for use as fill in future Waiawa Ridge area developments or may be 
exported from the site for sale as general fill material. 
3.1.3 Cut and Fill Slopes 
As previously discussed, it is anticipated that the majority of grading work for the 
project will be the removal of the existing reservoir embankment.  We envision that 
cut slopes at the project site will be generally limited to the abutment areas of the 
embankment and will generally expose the stiff to very hard clays and silts 
encountered in the borings.  Some fill slopes may be required in the lower portion of 
the breach area to tie into existing ground. 
In general, cut and fill slopes may be designed to match the adjacent reservoir 
walls with a maximum slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter.  Slopes having a vertical 
height exceeding 20 feet should have midslope benches.  The benches should

3.1.4 Stockpiling 
As previously discussed, the soil in the existing reservoir embankment may be 
considered to be a resource as fill material.  The construction of stockpiles should 
conform to the Stockpile Plan prepared by the project civil engineer.  The following 
are general recommendations for the construction of stockpiles for this project: 
Site Preparation - To avoid contaminating the stockpile material with 
vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other unsuitable materials, 
the areas within the stockpile limits should be cleared and grubbed 
thoroughly at the on-set of earthwork. The grubbed materials should be 
removed and disposed properly off-site or disposed in a designated area.  
A separation layer, such as plastic sheeting, is recommended to delineate 
the bottom of the stockpile from the native soil. 
Stockpile Construction - The height of stock piles should be limited to 
about 15 feet above the existing ground surface.  Sideslopes should be 
designed with a maximum slope inclination of 3H:1V or flatter.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from in-house literature research and our field exploration. 
Variations of subsurface conditions between and beyond the field exploration points 
may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until 
construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to 
re-evaluate the recommendations provided herein. 
The boring locations indicated in this report were located in the field by using a 
hand-held Global Positioning System (GPS) receiver.  Elevations of the probes were 
estimated based on interpolation between the spot elevations and contour lines shown 
on the topographic plan provided by R.M. Towill Corporation. The physical locations and 
elevations of the probes should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by 
the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the probes depict 
the approximate boundaries between soil/rock types and, as such, may denote a 
gradual transition. We did not encounter groundwater in the probes at the time of our 
exploration.  These data have been reviewed and interpretations made in the 
formulation of this report. However, it must be noted that groundwater levels may vary 
with seasonal rainfall, temperature, and other factors.
