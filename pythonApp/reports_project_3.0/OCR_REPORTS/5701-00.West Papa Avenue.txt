SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the West Papa Avenue Injection Well project in Kahului on the Island of
Maui Hawaii The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and
engineering analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of a storm
water runoff disposal system only The findings and recommendations presented
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The project site is approximately on the southern shoulder of West Papa Avenue
near the Kahiki Street intersection in Kahului on the Island of Maui Hawaii Based on
the information provided a new drywell is proposed to dispose of storm water runoff
We understand that it is desired to install a grated drain inlet that connects to a 14 5 to
24 inch diameter perforated casing to approximately 2 feet below Mean Sea Level
MSL The approximate location is shown on the Project Location Plan Plate 2
We drilled one boring to a depth of approximately 41 5 feet below the existing
ground surface As part of the project it is desired to evaluate the feasibility of storm
water drainage through drywells therefore field percolation testing was conducted in
the boring

2 1 Regional Geology
Two major volcanoes built the Island of Maui The older volcano is West Maui
Tertiary Epoch and the more recent volcano is East Maui also known as Haleakala
Pleistocene Epoch The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow gently sloping plain between
these two volcanoes The project site is along the south and southwest flanks of West
Maui
East Maui Haleakala lava flows ponding on West Maui created the Isthmus of
Maui The isthmus is comprised of alluvium washed from the slopes of West Maui and
East Maui Haleakala The erosional processes are dominated by soil and rock
masses that detach from the mountain walls and travel downslope toward the Isthmus
primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these materials reach the stream in the central
portion of a valley alluvial processes become dominant and the sediments are
transported and deposited as alluvium
In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy and as such
transmit large volumes of water for very short duration Because of this the transport of
sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load consists of a poorly
sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When the erosional base
levels change these sediment loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes
begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the
materials Chemical processes also cause induration cementation of the
coarse grained portion of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or
conglomerate Simultaneously erosion continues in areas above the valley floors and
upstream in headwaters This continued erosion generates material that travels
downstream and covers the older alluvial soil deposits Depending on the local base
level and rate of transport these newer sediments are generally transient in terms of
geologic time In addition their consistency and density are generally less than those of
the older partially consolidated deposits

Overlapping lava flows from the West Maui and East Maui volcanoes underlie the
alluvial soil deposits During the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epoch thinly
bedded basaltic lava flows of the Honomanu Volcanic Series built the bulk of the
Haleakala Shield During the Pleistocene Epoch the characteristics of the lava changed
to very hard thickly bedded flows of andesitic composition These lavas have been
grouped as the Kula Volcanic Series Typically the basalt rock formation consists of
thinly to thickly bedded a a and pahoehoe type lava flows Development of the Kahului
area in the past decades has brought the project site to its present condition
2 2 Existing Site Conditions
The project site is on the southern shoulder of West Papa Avenue near the
Kahiki Street intersection in Kahului on the Island of Maui Hawaii The ground surface
at the proposed drywell location is fairly level and covered with grass The approximate
location is shown on the Project Location Plan Plate 2
2 3 Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling of one boring designated as Boring No 1 extending to a depth of 41 5 feet
below the existing ground surface Two percolation tests were performed at 0 to 20 feet
and 30 to 35 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring location is
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our field exploration generally encountered surface fill materials consisting of stiff
to very stiff silts with clay extending to depths of about 3 to 6 feet below the existing
ground surface The surface fill soils are generally underlain by alluvial deposits
consisting of stiff to hard clayey silts extending to depths of approximately 20 feet below
the ground surface Below the alluvial deposits the boring encountered a beach sand
deposit consisting of very dense silty sands ranging in depth from 20 to 25 feet over
alluvial deposits The underlying alluvial deposits generally consist of very stiff to hard
clayey silts and sandy silt soils and extended to the maximum depth explored of about
41 5 feet below the existing ground surface

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered surface fill materials over alluvial
deposits to the full depth of the boring The subsurface materials primarily consisted of
low permeability fine grained soils consisting of stiff to very stiff clayey silts
We understand that a new drywell system is desired to dispose of storm water
runoff Based on the results of the permeability tests conducted at the site the
subsurface conditions down to the maximum depth explored appear to be unfavorable
for the use of drainage drywells A very low disposal capacity on the order of about
0 0001 cubic feet per second cfs may be anticipated for drywells embedded in the
alluvial soils Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering
design recommendations are presented in the following sections of this report
3 1 Storm Water Runoff Disposal System
Two field permeability tests using the falling head method were performed in
Boring No 1 at selected depths to evaluate the infiltration characteristics of the
subsurface materials Results of our field permeability tests and the calculated
permeabilities are presented in Appendix C The tests indicate that the subsurface soils
have a coefficient of permeability k of about 2 x 10 feet per minute This coefficient of
permeability generally corresponds to a water disposal capacity of about for
a 2 foot diameter 32 foot deep drywell
The results of the permeability tests correspond with the low permeability clayey
silts encountered in the boring Based on the field exploration and permeability testing
the site is not suitable for drywell disposal of runoff to a depth of 35 feet Minimal
disposal capacity is anticipated unless the drywells extend deeper into more permeable
zones if present below the site

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the proposed injection well location were explored by
drilling and sampling one boring designated as Boring No 1 to a depth of 41 5 feet below
the existing ground surface The approximate location of the boring drilled is shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2 The boring was drilled using a truck mounted drilling equipment
equipped with continuous flight augers and a hand auger tool
The materials encountered in the boring were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered during drilling are provided on the Log of Boring Plate A 1
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler or a 3 inch OD split barrel sampler with a 140 pound
hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second
and third 6 inches 12 inches total of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Log of Boring
at the appropriate sample depths
