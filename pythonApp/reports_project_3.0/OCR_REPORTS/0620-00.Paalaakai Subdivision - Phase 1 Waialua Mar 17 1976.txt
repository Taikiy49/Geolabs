SUMMARY OF RECOMMENDATION
The site is suitable for the proposed residential
development
Surcharge fill would be required for the lowlying
gully below elevation 18 feet where deep organic
silts and peat formation was found Post and beam
construction is recommended in this area
The on site brown clay is expansive when it is used
as fill material Slab on grade construction can
be utilized provided it is designed for the expansive
soil conditions
Properly compacted on site clayey soil will provide
CBR values of 2 3 to 7 2 when utilized for subgrade
support
The percolation tests show that the subsurface soils
are suitable for on site sewage disposal
INTRODUCTION
The purpose of this investigation was to define subsurface con
ditions at the site and to provide recommendations for design
and construction of the proposed subdivision development The
percolation tests were used to measure the in situ soil seepage
characteristics for cesspool design This report summarizes
our findings and recommendations

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed Phase 1 development scheme is to construct a
total of 187 single family lots in two increments Incre
ment 1 locates at the eastern portion consists of 105 lots
Increment 2 occupies the western portion has 82 lots Site
grading includes fill over the existing lowlying gullies and
obtaining fill materials from their adjacent higher ground
Final site grading plans are not available at the time of this
investigation However it is anticipated that the maximum
cuts and fill will be less than 15 feet high
FIELD EXPLORATION
The subsurface soils at the site were explorated by drilling
10 test borings and 2 hand dug test pits Percolation tests
were also performed on the test pits and some of the borings
The boring and test pit locations are shown on the enclosed
Site Plan Plate 1 A detailed description of the field
exploration methods and accompanying laboratory testing is
given in the appendix along with the boring logs and laboratory
test results
SITE CONDITIONS
Surface
The proposed 33 acre subdivision site is located at the circular
junction between Kaukonahua Highway and Waialua Beach Road in
Waialua Oahu Hawaii
Page 3
W O 620 00
The property generally slopes from the Kaukonahua Highway
at the south down to Waialua Beach Road at the north at a
10 gradient
The project site was cultivated with sugar cane Shallow
earth and concrete ditches are used to irrigate the cane
field
There are two gullies on the project site The bigger gully
runs from Kaukonahua Highway for about 700 feet west toward
the lowlying area at the north cane haul road The second
gully is about 150 feet long and comes from the south inter
secting with the first gully The gullies have widths of
5 to 20 feet across the top of banks and 2 to 7 feet deep
Geology
The Waialua Haleiwa area geology is typified by alluvium at
the higher ground and massive sediments of sand and coral near
the shoreline locations These sedimentary deposits overlie
lava flows and confine the underlying artesian aquifers
The average depth of sediment can be as thick as 200 feet at
Waialua where Kiikii Stream Kaukonahua Stream and Poamaho
Stream brought in hugh amount of deposits from upper plateau
area These deposits thin out toward Anahulu River With
changes of sea level in the past this area has experienced
both swampy and coral deposits conditions

Subsurface
The exploration disclosed essentially uniform soil conditions
within the proposed project area Generally a 4 to 8 foot
crust of stiff clayey soils covers the entire site
Surface soils include reddish brown clayey silt at the upper
area adjacent to Kaukonahua Highway and brown clay at the
remaining lower area
Below the surface layers silty sand and decomposed coral were
encountered in all but one of the boring locations At boring
no 9 soft organic silts and peat formation were found 9 to
25 feet below the existing ground surface
Groundwater levels were encountered in some of the bore holes
and they varied from elevation 5 to 12 feet
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The subsurface conditions indicated the proposed subdivision
development by means of cut and fill is feasible from the soils
engineering viewpoint
Page 5
W
At the lowlying fill area where peat was encountered ground
settlement will occur over a long period of time Utility and
building constructions should be scheduled toward the end part
of the development project Surcharge time for the peat and
organic clays will be quite long
Lowlying Fill Area
In the lowlying gully area fills up to 8 feet high are anti
cipated Because of the underlying soft organic silt and peat
layers the weight of the new fill will cause the ground to
settle over a period of many years To speed up settlement
and to reduce differential settlements surcharge fill is re
commended over the lowlying area The approximate location to
be surcharged is outlined on the Site Plan Surcharge time for
the organic clays and peat will be quite lengthy about 12 months
or more from fill completion
The actual time lapse between fill completion and structural con
struction should be governed by surveyed data obtained from settle
ment gauge readings Two settlement gauges should be installed
prior to filling the lowlying area and they should be monitored
until the fill settlement has decreased to an acceptable level
Settlements
Under the proposed fill and 8 foot surcharge loadings it is esti
mated that the settlement during a 12 month surcharge period will
be up to 24 inches Additional long term settlement of about two
inches can be expected over an extended period

Site Grading
All areas to receive fill should be undercut to remove the
surface vegetations
Prior to placement of compacted fill local soft spots and
existing drainage structure encountered within the building
areas should be removed and replaced with compacted fill
The upper six inches of the existing ground surface should
be scarified brought to near optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 90 of its maximum dry density
established by
Fill over existing gullies should start at the bottom Ben
ching and keying are required to properly bond the new fill
to the slopes
Additional fills should then be placed in 9 inch horizontal
lifts and compacted to the above specified density Our
general specifications for fill placement compaction and
supervision are contained in the grading specifications in
cluded with this report as Appendix B

Other Area
The on site clayey soil is moderately expansive when it is
disturbed or remolded Therefore footing supported on this
fill material or at existing grade should be designed for
medium expansive soil condition Where footings are in cut
area low expansive soil conditions can be utilized
To minimize swelling and shrinkage of the subgrade soil deep
reinforced perimeter and interior footings are recommended
For light residential structures footing bearing on the com
pacted fill or on site stiff clayey soil allowable bearing
pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot may be used
Slab On Grade
Slab on grade is not recommended in the lowlying fill area
where peat formation was encountered
For other areas slab on grade should be designed for low to
medium expansive soil conditions For construction details
in these conditions refer to plates B 1 through B 4
The subgrade should be compacted wet of its optimum moisture
content to a minimum of 90 of its dry density as established
by designation A 6 inch layer of quarry waste
select borrow should be provided above the subgrade and compacted
to 95 of its maximum dry density

Retaining Wall
Retaining wall less than 10 feet high should be designed to
receive active lateral pressure equivalent to a fluid density
of 60 pounds per cubic foot if on site clayey CH soil are
usedgas backfill A friction factor of 0 4 can be utilized
to determine sliding resistence of the retaining wall foun
dations
If granular backfill is used the wall may be designed for
35 pounds per cubic foot equivalent fluid pressure with a
a friction factor of 0 4 The backfill should be compacted
between 90 to 95 maximum density Over compaction behind
the wall can increase the lateral earth pressure above the
design value
Weepholes or subsurface drainage system should be incorporatea
to prevent the build up of excessive hydrostatic pressure behind
the wall

FIELD EXPLORATION
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling 10
borings utilizing truck mounted equipment The depths of all the
borings terminated at 20 0 to 30 0 feet below the existing ground
surface In addition two test pits were dug down to 4 0 to 4 5
feet deep with a post digger The locations of the borings and test
pits are shown on the enclosed Site Plan and Boring Locations Plate 1
The soils were classified by visual and textural examination in the
field by our engineer who continuously supervised the drilling opera
tions The classifications were verified by visual inspection and
testing in the laboratory All soils were classified in accordance with
the Unified Soil Classification System A graphic presentation of the
soils encountered is presented on the Boring Logs Plate A 1 through A 10
Test Pit Logs are shown on Plate A 11
Soil samples were obtained by driving either a 2 4 inch I D ring sampler
or a standard penetration sampler with a 140 pound hammer free falling a
distance of 30 inches The blow counts to drive the sampler the last 12
inches are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth
The percolation tests were performed by filling the borings and test pits
with water and allowing the soils to become saturated The holes were then
refilled with water and the drop of water level or a constant intake rate
of water was recorded Plate A 12 presents the results of our percolation
tests

LABORATORY TESTING
Moisture content determinations and Atterberg Limits were made on
selected samples as an aid in the soil classification and in eva
luating soil properties The results of these tests are presented
in the Log of Borings at the appropriate sample location
Two California Bearing Ratio CBR test with modified proctor com
paction test were performed on a surface sample of the on site soil
to evaluate the suitability of the soil as fill and pavement support
The results are given on Plate A 13
A total of two direct shear test were performed on selected samples
to evaluate the strength characteristics of the encountered soils
The test results are shown on Plate A 14
To determine the consolidation characteristic of the undisturbed organic
silt and peat material a total of three consolidation tests were per
formed The consolidation curves are shown on Plate A 15 A 16 and A 17
Expansion or swell tests were conducted on ring samples 2 4 inches

below the field moisture content condition Plate A 18 summarizes all
