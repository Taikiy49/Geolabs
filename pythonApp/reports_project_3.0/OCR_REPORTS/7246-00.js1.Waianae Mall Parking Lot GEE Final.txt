PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The existing Waianae Mall is located at the northeastern corner of the 
intersection of Farrington Highway and Leihoku Street in the Waianae area on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. Based on the information provided, it is desired to repave an 
existing parking lot located at the back of the facility, east of the existing commercial 
building. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location 
Map, Plate 1. 
The existing pavement is worn and in poor condition with isolated potholes. We 
understand that three “soft areas” were noted by the paving contractor while preparing 
to install new pavement and that it is desired to evaluate the subsurface conditions to 
assist in the determination of a new suitable pavement section for this area.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from 
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to 
be middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early

Pleistocene in age. The rocks of the Waianae Volcanic Series are divided into Lower, 
Middle and Upper Members.  The lavas of the Lower Member consist largely of thinly 
bedded pahoehoe flows whereas the Middle Member consists of interbedded pahoehoe 
and a`a flows with a higher occurrence of the a`a form.  The Upper Member consists 
primarily of massive a`a lava flows.  
Erosion deeply dissected the Waianae Shield removing much of the western 
slope of the dome resulting in broad amphitheater headed valleys. During the 
Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of continental 
glaciation.  As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill the 
oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea.  When the 
glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations.  Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of 
the calcareous reef materials. 
The project site is at the foot of Puu Paheehee which is an elevational 
prominence on an erosional remnant ridge from the western flank of the Waianae 
Range.  Chemical and mechanical weathering of the volcanic rocks of the ridge has 
resulted in the deposition of an apron of colluvium and alluvium along the pediment of 
the ridge.  These deposits generally consist of remnant boulders and highly expansive 
clay.  In the lower areas where the topographic gradient decreases, the clay soils 
frequently mantle the underlying coral formation. 
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
The existing Waianae Mall is located at the northeastern corner of the intersection 
of Farrington Highway and Leihoku Street in the Waianae area on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii.  The project site is located at the existing parking lot located at the back of the 
facility, east of the existing commercial building. 
At the time of our exploration, the parking lot was paved in most areas with 
asphaltic concrete. Based on our field observation, the current asphaltic concrete 
pavement is in poor condition, weathered, and have experienced moderate to severe 
raveling.  In general, block cracking was observed on the majority of the parking lot, and 
minor to severe alligator cracking appeared at some localized areas.

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration and 
results of our pavement analyses, pavement reconstruction is recommended for the 
parking lot.  Detailed discussion of our geotechnical engineering recommendations for 
design of pavement, site preparation, and other geotechnical engineering aspects are 
presented in the following sections. 
Subgrade Strength Characterization 
Dynamic Cone Penetration (DCP) tests were performed adjacent to all 
seven boring locations in general accordance with ASTM D6951 to obtain indicative 
subsoil strength characteristics such as California Bearing Ratio (CBR). The tests were 
generally performed through the subgrade between depths of about 0.5 and 5.0 feet 
below the existing ground surface. 
The Dynamic Cone Penetrometer device consists of two 16mm-diameter 
stainless steel rods with the lower rod containing an anvil, a replaceable 60° pointed tip 
and depth markings. The upper rod holds a 17.6-pound drop hammer with a 22.6 inch 
drop distance. At the beginning of a test, the cone is “seated” by dropping the hammer 
until the widest part of the cone is just below the testing surface at which point the initial 
penetration is recorded as 0. The operator then lifts and drops the hammer one or more 
times depending on the strength of the soil at the test location. Following each 
sequence of hammer drops, a penetration reading is taken. The process is continued 
until the desired depth of testing is reached or the full length of the lower rod is buried. 
Data from our DCP testing is presented as the penetration index which is the 
penetration distance of the cone for each drop of the hammer expressed in inches per 
blow. The penetration indices obtained from our tests were correlated with a common 
pavement design parameter California Bearing Ratio (CBR). The penetration indices 
are presented on the DCP field logs shown in Appendix B.

Pavement Design 
Based on our pavement condition survey, the soft areas of the parking lot noted 
by the paving contractor have exhibited moderate to severe pavement distresses that 
are likely caused by failures in the underlying base, subbase, and/or subgrade 
materials. It appears that the root cause of the pavement failure was due to poor 
drainage which resulted in saturation of the subgrade soils. 
Design traffic information and data was not available for the preparation of this 
report.  In general, we anticipate that the vehicle loading for the parking lot under 
consideration for this project would primarily consist of passenger vehicles with some 
light pick-up trucks and occasional heavy trucks only.

pavement analysis, an average daily truck traffic of 10 trucks per day was used. The

Recompacted Existing Subgrade 
The existing subgrade soils under the new pavement structural section should be 
scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to near the optimum 
moisture, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with AASHTO T180 or 
ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor the pavement subgrade preparation to 
observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and 
scarification process and to confirm whether the exposed soil conditions are similar to 
those encountered in our field exploration.  Modification to the extent of the different 
areas may be required. CBR tests and/or field observations should be performed on the

Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development 
of good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage 
structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration 
or premature failure of the pavements.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
seven borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 7, extending to depths of about 
5.1 to 5.75 feet below the existing ground surface.  The approximate boring locations 
are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill 
rig equipped with continuous flight augers and/or rotary coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1.  
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.  Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-7. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts 
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Dynamic Cone Penetration Tests 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by performing Dynamic 
Cone Penetration (DCP) tests adjacent to each of the seven boring locations extending 
to depths of about 0.5 to 5.0 feet below the existing pavement surface.  The 
approximate locations where DCP tests were performed are shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2.  
The test locations were augered through the existing pavement section down to 
depths between about 0.5 to 1.25 feet below the existing ground surface.  DCP tests 
were then performed in general accordance with ASTM D6951, Standard Test Method 
for Use of the Dynamic Cone Penetrometer in Shallow Pavement Applications.  The 
DCP apparatus consists of a Salem Dual Mass Dynamic Cone Penetrometer and the 
tests were conducted to a maximum depth of about 5.0 feet below the existing 
pavement surface. Results are shown on the Logs of Dynamic Cone Penetration Tests, 
Plates B-1 through B-7.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties.  The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits.  The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.  Graphic presentations of the test 
results are provided on Plate C-1. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM C117 & ASTM C136) was performed on a 
selected soil sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in 
soil classification.  Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on 
Plate C-2. 
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed 
on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the soils.  The test results are presented on Plates C-3 through C-5. 
One Modified Proctor compaction test (ASTM D1557) was performed on a bulk 
sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships. The test results are presented on Plate C-6.
