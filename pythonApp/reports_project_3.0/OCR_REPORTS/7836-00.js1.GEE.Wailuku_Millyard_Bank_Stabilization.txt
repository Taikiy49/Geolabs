SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Significant erosion has occurred on the southern banks of the Wailuku River 
adjacent to existing buildings located on two lots (TMK: [2] 3-4-020: 45 and 46). High flow 
within the river and severe storm events contributed to the erosion of the existing 
riverbank. In addition, the riverbank is subject to increased velocity and scour due to a 
bend in the channel immediately upstream of the project site. To prevent further loss of 
property or potential flooding within the Wailuku business district, it is desired to restore 
the existing riverbank and provide slope protection improvements. 
Our boring located at TMK: (2) 3-4-020: 44 (Boring No. 1) generally encountered 
a surface dune sand layer consisting of 10.5 feet of medium dense sand with a little silt 
and gravel. The dune sand was underlain by alluvial deposits consisting of stiff sandy 
clay with some gravel, and very dense cobbles and boulders to the maximum depth 
explored of about 50 feet below the existing ground surface. Our boring located at 
TMK: (2) 3-4-020: 888 (Boring No. 2) generally encountered a 36-inch concrete armor 
stone layer underlain by 2 feet of fill consisting of very stiff clayey silt. The armor stone 
and fill layer were underlain by alluvial deposits consisting dense to very dense cobbles 
and boulders with interbedded layers of loose silty sand and dense clayey sand, 
extending to the maximum depth explored of 50.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface.  
We encountered groundwater in the borings drilled at the time of our field 
exploration at depths varying from approximately 21 to 29 feet below the existing ground 
surface. The groundwater levels encountered generally correspond to about Elevations 
+174 to +176 feet MSL. Considering the project is located adjacent to the Wailuku River, 
the groundwater levels may vary in response to the water level in the stream. In addition, 
it should be noted that the groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, 
seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
We recommend stabilizing and protecting the existing slopes by filling and hardening 
the slope with GRP up to the top of the existing slope. The GRP covering should be a 
minimum of 24 inches thick and consist of cobbles and boulders with voids in-filled with 
concrete placed at a slope inclination of 1.5H:1V or flatter. Prior to placement of the GRP, 
the existing slope should be lined with a woven geotextile fabric, such as Mirafi FW700 or 
equivalent. In addition, geocomposite drains should be placed between the GRP and face 
of the slope to reduce the potential for the build-up of hydrostatic pressures. A toe stone key 
should be provided at the base of the toe protection system. The edges of the GRP layer at 
the project limits should be keyed into the existing slope (minimum 4 feet deep by 2 feet 
wide) to reduce the potential for future erosion at the edge of the GRP layer.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
Maui is the second largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago. It was formed by 
the activity of two volcanoes, East Maui or Haleakala, and West Maui. The broad, gently 
sloping plain connecting the two volcanoes, known as the Maui Isthmus, was formed 
when lavas of the Haleakala Volcano banked against the already existing West Maui 
Volcano. 
The East Maui Volcano, better known as the Haleakala Mountain, makes up the 
eastern portion of the Island of Maui. Haleakala Mountain is considered to be dormant 
with the last eruption taking place on the southerly side of the mountain around 1790. 
Haleakala Mountain consists largely of the Honomanu Volcanic Series that built the 
mountain from three rift zones, probably during the Pliocene and early Pleistocene 
Epochs. 
The volcanic rocks of the West Maui Volcano have been divided into three volcanic 
series. The oldest, Wailuku Volcanic Series, is comprised of basaltic lava flows and 
associated pyroclastic and intrusive rocks, which built the major shield volcano. The 
Wailuku Volcanic Series was covered by a thin, discontinuous “frosting” of andesitic and 
trachytic flows, domes, and pyroclastic deposits known as the Honolua Volcanic Series. 
After a long period of inactivity, where erosion incised deep valleys into the volcanic flows, 
volcanic activity returned with eruptions that produced the flows and cones of the 
Lahaina Volcanic Series.

In the mountainous regions of Hawaii, erosion processes are dominated by the 
detachment of soil and rock materials from steep valley walls. The detached materials fall 
or are otherwise transported downslope toward the valley axis, primarily by gravity as 
colluvium. Once these materials reach the stream in the valley, streamflow processes 
become dominant, and the materials are further transported and may be deposited as 
alluvium.  
In general, stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy (i.e., the streams 
transmit large volumes of water for very short durations).  Because of this, the transport of 
sediment is intermittent, and the bulk of the stream's hydraulic load consists of a 
poorly-sorted mixture of boulders, cobbles, gravel, sands, and fine soil. When the erosion 
base level changes with the passage of time, the stream sediment loads are left as deposits. 
The project site is located along the Wailuku River on the lower eastern slope of the West 
Maui Volcano, approximately 1.5 miles downstream from the base of Iao Valley. Continued 
stream incision has exposed the alluvial deposits at the ground surface along the stream 
bank at the project site.

 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the 
tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to 
volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before 
or during volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the 
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not 
erupt. The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but 
most are so small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments.

In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the

Liquefaction Potential 
Based on the International Building Code, 2006 Edition, the project site may be 
subjected to seismic activity, and the potential for soil liquefaction at the project site 
will need to be evaluated. 
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the 
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build-up of excess 
pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by 
earthquakes. In this process, when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to 
vibration (such as during an earthquake), the soil tends to densify and decrease in 
volume causing an increase in pore water pressure. If drainage is unable to occur 
rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up of pore water pressure, the effective stress 
(internal strength) of the soil is reduced. Under sustained vibrations, the pore water 
pressure build-up could equal the overburden pressure, essentially reducing the soil 
shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid. During liquefaction, 
the soil acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal and vertical 
movements, and if not confined, will result in significant deformations. 
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained sands 
and loose silts with little cohesion. The major factors affecting the liquefaction 
characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows:

3.2 
Riverbank Slope Stabilization Mitigation 
As mentioned above, significant erosion has occurred on the southern banks of 
the river adjacent to existing buildings located on two lots (TMK: [2] 3-4-020: 45 and 46). 
Some undermining of the exterior wall and sidewalk has occurred at the northern corner 
of the building located at TMK: (2) 3-4-020: 45. Based on the information provided, we 
understand high flow within the river and severe storm events contributed to the erosion 
of the existing stream bank. To prevent further loss of property or potential flooding within 
the Wailuku business district, the existing riverbank slopes should be restored and 
protected. 
Based on our site reconnaissance and the topographic map provided, the 
riverbank slopes down towards the Wailuku River in a northwest orientation with 
inclinations ranging from about 0.5H:1V to 2H:1V. In general, we recommend stabilizing 
and protecting the existing slopes by filling and hardening the slope with grouted rubble 
paving (GRP). In addition, we understand the design scour depth is estimated to be about 
11.1 feet below the existing ground surface at the toe of the existing slope. Therefore, we 
recommend constructing a concrete and boulder key at the toe of the riverbank slope 
beneath the GRP, as shown on Plate 4.

 Scour Mitigation 
To reduce the potential for additional erosion at the slope toe from future flood 
events, we recommend installing a slope toe protection system consisting of a 
concrete key filled with boulders. We understand the design scour depth is 
estimated to be about 11.1 feet below the existing ground surface. Therefore, we 
recommend extending the boulder filled concrete key to a minimum depth of 
11.1 feet beneath the toe of the hardened slope, as shown on Plate 4. 
The boulder filled concrete key should be at least 4 feet wide and extend to or 
below the design scour depth. Special care should be taken to avoid inadvertent 
overly-intensive cobble and boulder removal during key excavation, which may 
result in additional slope instability. The material generated during excavation of 
the concrete key should be used for a temporary berm placed on the stream side 
of the excavation to reduce the potential for inadvertent stream flow into the 
excavation. Key excavation should be limited to 50 linear feet at a time. The key 
concrete should have a minimum compressive strength at 28 days of 4,000 psi. In 
addition, an anti-wash admixture should be used in the concrete. The key concrete 
should be placed by tremie method. 
To reduce lateral encroachment within the existing river, a drilled shaft secant or 
tangent pile cutoff wall was also evaluated for scour mitigation. Secant and tangent 
pile cutoff walls are formed by installing a row of overlapping (secant) or closely 
spaced (tangent) drilled shafts to a specified depth. In general, drilled shafts are 
constructed by drilling a hole down to a specified depth, placing reinforcing steel, 
and then pumping high slump concrete to fill up the hole. Due to site accessibility 
and difficult drilling conditions anticipated within cobbles and boulders encountered 
at the project site, we believe a secant or tangent pile cutoff wall would be very 
time consuming and expensive to construct and is therefore not a feasible option 
for scour mitigation.

 Slope Hardening 
Based on our slope stability analysis, and the anticipated high velocities and deep 
scour depths anticipated during a 100-year storm event, the options of leaving the 
existing riverbank slopes as-is and/or vegetation of the existing riverbank slopes 
are not feasible. Therefore, slope hardening will be required to mitigate future slope 
stability and erosion issues. 
To mitigate future slope stability and erosion issues, we recommend restoring and 
hardening the eroded riverbank slopes with GRP. In general, we recommend 
extending the GRP up to the top of the existing slope. The extension of the GRP 
to the top of the slope will prevent future erosion and increase the stability of the 
existing slope. Prior to placement of the GRP, the existing slope should be lined 
with a woven geotextile fabric, such as Mirafi FW700 or equivalent. 
The GRP covering should be a minimum of 24 inches thick and consist of cobbles 
and boulders with voids in-filled with sand-cement grout or concrete placed at a 
slope inclination of 1.5H:1V or flatter. A toe stone key should be provided at the 
base of the toe protection system. The toe stone should consist of 3 to 5-ton 
boulders with a nominal diameter of about 4 to 5 feet and should be set a minimum 
depth of 1 foot into the concrete key. The edges of the GRP layer at the project 
limits should be keyed into the existing slope (minimum 4 feet deep by 2 feet wide) 
to reduce the potential for future erosion at the edge of the GRP layer. Typical plan 
details of the GRP slope hardening system are presented on Plate 4. 
 Drainage 
The GRP slope hardening system should be well-drained to reduce the potential 
for build-up of hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of 2 
to 3-foot wide strips of a prefabricated drainage composite product 
(geocomposite drains) between the face of the slope and GRP with a 
center-to-center spacing of about 8 feet placed on the face of the slope. The face 
of the slope should be relatively flat without sharp points for the placement of the 
prefabricated drainage product. The prefabricated drainage composite product 
should extend from the top of the slope to the base of the slope and should be

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 50 and 
50.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped 
with continuous flight augers and coring tools.  
Our geologists classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-2.2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the Standard 
Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained by 
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, Diamond 
Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow 
bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit is 
mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were described in 
general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods 
for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International 
Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from 
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of material is
