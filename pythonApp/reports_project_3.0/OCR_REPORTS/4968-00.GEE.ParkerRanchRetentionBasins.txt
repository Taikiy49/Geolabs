SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Parker Ranch Retention Basins as part of the Parker Ranch
2020 Town Center Improvements in Waimea on the Island of Hawaii The project site is
located to the southwest of the confluence of Kamuela and Lanimaumau Streams and to
the south of the Parker Ranch Support Facilities The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended to assist in the design of the drainage
channel and retention berms only The findings and recommendations presented herein
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
As part of the planned Parker Ranch development Kamuela and Lanimaumau
Streams were diverted through a drainage channel to a percolation retention basin This
previous work involved the excavation of channels and construction of berms to divert and
retain the storm water runoff A previous geotechnical exploration was conducted by
others for the project In order to comply with Federal Emergency Management Agency
FEMA requirements for revision of flood plain boundaries additional geotechnical
engineering analyses are required to document the stability of the existing berms and
retention basin The additional information required includes the following
Observation of some of the previously placed fills adjacent to the existing
drainage channel
Testing of the existing fill material to evaluate soil classification and
engineering characteristics such as shear strength and compressibility
Seepage analyses including piping and filtration
e Slope stability and settlement analyses

percolation retention basin does not have sufficient capacity Therefore it is desired to
expand the system with new basins to the west of the existing basin Current plans call for

retention berms No other site grading is anticipated

SECTION 2 0 SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from five 5 shield
volcanoes Kohala Mauna Kea and Kilauea The oldest volcano
Kohala is estimated to be middle Pleistocene in age and forms the northern lobe of the
island The next younger shield Mauna Kea is estimated to be Pleistocene in age and
shows evidence of glaciation during the Ice Age The remaining shields have been
active in recent history
The site is situated in a saddle between Kohala and Mauna Kea where Mauna Kea
lava flows banked against the older Kohala rocks Based on available geological
information and geomorphological interpretation the site is located on Mauna Kea rocks
near the surface contact with Kohala
Following the cessation of activity in Kohala and Mauna Kea activity from the other
shields resulted in widespread aerial fall of ash over much of the island This ash has
weathered rapidly into a fine silty soil which is typified by low in situ densities and high field
moisture contents In some locales when the field moisture content is sufficiently high the
ash soil exhibits thixotropic properties i e the soil temporarily loses strength when
remolded and regains its strength after internal pore pressures dissipate In more arid
areas the ash soil exists in a dry friable state even though field moisture contents can
approach 100 percent of dry weight
Our exploration indicates that the site is underlain by a surface mantle of this ash
soil which grades with depth to basalt clinker consisting of coarse gravel sized fragments
The surface ash and clinker sequence is underlain by competent basalt lava rock at
relatively shallow depths

2 2 Existing Site Conditions
The project site is located on the southwest side of the town of Waimea in the
District of Waimea on the Island of Hawaii The project site begins on the western side of
the existing Parker Ranch retention pond and extends to the west towards the Parker
Ranch race track The project area is bounded to the north by the Parker Ranch Support
Facilities to the east by the existing retention pond open pasture land to the south and to
the west by the Parker Ranch administrative offices and race track
In general the proposed retention pond area is a shallow swale between two lava
flow ridges opening to a shallow basin in the western half of the site Topographic relief
within the swale and basin is very low with an overall regional slope trending down towards
the west The ground elevations range from 2 628 feet Mean Sea Level MSL at the
western end of the project site to about 2 698 feet MSL at the existing retention pond
The site is currently used as pasture land and is covered by a thick growth of grass
Occasional boulders occur on the surface and emergent lava pressure ridges are found
around the site particularly in the eastern area on higher ground Locally drainage
consists of sheet flow within the swale and basin

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the results of our exploration the site is underlain by volcanic ash soils
with basalt rock formation at relatively shallow depths These subsurface conditions
should provide adequate support for the proposed retention basin project provided that the
recommendations presented herein are followed
Based on our exploration it is anticipated that conventional earthmoving equipment
may be used for excavation of the near surface portions of the new drainage channel It is
anticipated that deeper portions of the channel excavation will encounter in situ rock
formation For removal of this rock formation hard excavation methods such as hoerams
and blasting may be required
Our laboratory testing on the silty ash soils at the site indicated that these soils are
non expansive but have relatively low strength characteristics such as shear strength and
California Bearing Ratio CBR value Proper site preparation and earthwork procedures
are necessary to provide adequate support for the fills planned at the project site
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering design
recommendations are presented in the following sections of this report

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris deleterious materials and
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site or
stockpiled in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the
excavated materials Since the existing vegetation at the site consists of
well established grass consideration should be given to stripping the sod and
retaining it for re planting on finished slopes
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should
be backfilled with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft soils may be
used as fill provided that it meets the requirements for fill material
Areas to receive fill should be scarified to a depth of 12 inches moisture
conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to at
least 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the
same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight corresponding to
the maximum dry density We wish to emphasize that moisture conditioning of
the fill materials is an integral part of the fill placement recommendations
provided herein Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers
vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment The

Keying and Benching
Although the average slope on the site is on the order of about eight horizontal to
one vertical 8H 1V or flatter there are local areas of steeper slopes Therefore
keying and benching is recommended for fills placed at the site in areas where the
existing slope is steeper than 5H 1V These keys and benches should be firmly
seated in the ash soils or basalt rock formation underlying the site
Based on the site conditions encountered we recommend that prior to the
placement of fill the keys and benches be moisture conditioned and proof rolled
with a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar heavy equipment for a minimum of
six passes to help verify that adequate support for the fill has been achieved
The proof rolling operations should be performed in the presence of a
representative from Geolabs Yielding areas loose areas or cavities disclosed
during clearing and proof rolling operations should be backfilled with compacted fill
materials
Fills and Backfills
Due to the intended use of most of the fills to be placed for this project i e
channellizing flow and retention of water we recommend that fine grained fill with
proper slope and channel protection should be utilized Fill materials should consist
of non expansive materials less than 3 inches in largest dimension with a minimum
CBR value of 10 The fill material should also be free of vegetation and deleterious
materials Imported fill materials should be observed and or tested by Geolabs for
their suitability prior to being transported to the site for the intended use

Cutand Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration it appears
that most of the cut slopes would expose the ashy soils consisting of stiff but friable
non plastic silts For normal construction 2H 1V inclinations are usually
appropriate However due to the anticipated flow conditions we believe that flatter
cut slopes into the soil material of 3H 1V or flatter should be used Cut slopes in
rock may be made at an inclination of or flatter To provide consistent
channel slopes the design should consider the 3H 1V slope throughout the project
Fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 3H 1V or flatter Current
plans indicate a maximum vertical height of about 9 feet for level slopes on the
project If the existing ground surface is steeper that 5H 1V fills should be keyed
and benched into the existing slope as discussed above to provide stability of the
new fill against sliding The filling operations should start at the lowest point and
continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill
placement recommendations
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope
ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face Track rolling of slopes should not be
accepted In the event that over cutting of a slope occurs keying and benching
requirements should be implemented instead of backfilling the slope to the design

Slope and Channel Protection
Due to the friable nature of the on site ash soils and to reduce seepage through
the retention berms the use of an impermeable membrane liner is recommended
for the interior berm slope face subject to water ponding Slope protection should
be considered for slopes that will be exposed to running water e g the interior
channel walls the upstream faces of the retention berms and the spillway
between Basins 2 and 3 or continued maintenance should be anticipated
The impermeable membrane liner should consist of a flexible geomembrane
such as textured HDPE The liner should be installed in accordance with the
manufacturer s specifications including anchoring at the top and bottom of the
slope
Slope protection such as riprap should be considered over the liner to protect
the liner from exposure to weathering and possible mechanical damage
Alternatively the liner could be covered with about 2 feet of soil and grassed
with the understanding that maintenance will be required to replace the soil cover
following heavy flows For the channel walls where the slopes will be subjected
to an estimated 6 cubic foot per second cfs flow the slope protection should
consist of a minimum of 3 feet of riprap constructed of boulders ranging from 12
to 24 inches in diameter For the retention berms the slope protection should
consist of a riprap constructed of boulders ranging between 6 and 12 inches in
diameter and a minimum of 2 feet thick To reduce the potential of damage to
the liner during installation of the riprap it is recommended that a cushion type
geotextile such as Geocushion Mat be placed over the liner prior to placement
of the armor stone The cushion material should not be extended to the toe of

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by excavating and sampling six
6 test pits designated as Test Pits Nos 1 through 6 to depths of about 3 5 to 10 feet
below the existing ground surface Six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6
were drilled to depths of about 20 to 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate locations of the test pits and borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The test pits were excavated using a Caterpillar 430 backhoe The borings were drilled
using truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers and
coring tools
The materials encountered in the test pits and borings were classified by visual and
textural examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the excavation and drilling
operations on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed
visually and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with
the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Logs of materials
encountered during Test Pit excavation are provided on Plates A 1 an A 2 Graphic
representations of the materials encountered during drilling are provided on the Logs of
Borings Plates A 3 through A 8
Soil samples from the test pits were obtained as grab samples of the excavated
materials and placed in Whirl Pak plastic bags for transportation to the laboratory
Consistencies textures and relative moisture contents were determined subjectively in
the field during the exploration
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and
drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations ASTM D 2937
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Two direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested Results of the direct
shear tests are presented on Plates B 1 and B 2
Three sieve analysis tests ASTM C 117 ASTM C 136 were performed on
selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in
soil classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on
Plate B 3
One consolidation test with time rates ASTM D 2435 was performed on a
sample of the potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility
characteristics of the materials encountered Results of the consolidation test are
presented on Plate B 4
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557A were performed on the
near surface soil to determine the maximum dry density and optimum moisture of the
on site soil Results of these tests are presented on Plates B 5 and B 6
Two permeability tests ASTM D 2434 were performed on remolded samples of
the near surface soils to evaluate the hydraulic conductivity of the compacted fill The
test results are summarized on Plate B 7
