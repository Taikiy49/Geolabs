incorporate a concrete bus lane in each travel direction. The original pavement design was
based on the “Guide for Design of Pavement Structures (1993)” developed by American
Association of State Highway and Transportation Officials (AASHTO).

roadway. However, due to the potential of the loss of support between the concrete bus

rigid pavement section.

allows a fairly wide range of J values (2.5 to 3.1) for this condition. As a result, the final
pavement thickness could vary based on the selection of J value.

SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
New pavements will be constructed as part of the Kapiolani Boulevard 
reconstruction project, which will also include concrete bus lanes, driveway aprons, curbs, 
gutters, storm drain structures, sidewalks and utility manholes. Our field exploration shows 
that fill materials, consisting of silty sand with some gravel, were encountered below the 
existing pavement layer. Loose marsh deposit and lagoonal deposit were then 
encountered below the fill layer and overlaying coralline detritus and alluvium.  
Currently, this section of Kapiolani Boulevard carries a total of six lanes with 
three lanes in each traffic direction. Based on the information provided, we understand that 
a concrete bus lane is required for both traffic directions.  The remaining lanes will be 
reconstructed using flexible pavements. 
Our pavement design procedures used in determining the new pavement section is 
based on the “Guide for Design of Pavement Structures (1993)” developed by the 
American Association of State Highway and Transportation Officials (AASHTO). Based on 
the traffic data provided, the CBR value of the subgrade soils (CBR=32), and the 
assumptions above, a thickened-edge rigid pavement section consisting of up to 14 inches 
of Portland cement concrete pavement is recommended for the new bus lane. It should be 
noted that a rigid pavement section requires significant amount of roadway closures for the 
concrete pavement to cure and harden compared to a similar flexible pavement section.  
Our borings indicated that existing pavement sections contain AC pavement over 
PCC pavement at most areas across the project site. We understand that it is desired to 
remove the existing PCC pavement and provide a full reconstruction. The flexible 
pavement design consisting of 2-inch of AC with 6-inch asphalt concrete base over 6-inch 
minimum aggregate subbase course could be applied to the remaining lanes.  
An alternative flexible pavement section consisting of 2-inch of AC with 4-inch ACB 
over the existing AC or PCC was also evaluated. 
A critical consideration in this alternative pavement design is the condition of the 
previous concrete pavement below the current asphalt pavement. If this concrete 
pavement is in relatively good condition (i.e. intact with minor cracking), it can provide 
good support for the new flexible pavement section. If the concrete pavement is cracked, 
reflection cracking would normally be visible at the asphalt surface. Unfortunately, 
Kapiolani Boulevard was recently paved, making it difficult to determine whether reflection 
cracking was present.  Due to the uncertain condition of the underlying concrete 
pavement, this alternative was not selected.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of 
the western third of the island while the younger Koolau Volcano forms the majority of 
the eastern two-thirds of the island. It is believed that Waianae became extinct while 
Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in 
Central Oahu. 
The Koolau Volcanic Shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by 
thinly-bedded basaltic lava flows. The main shield-building activities ceased 
approximately 2.5 million years ago. Evidence from drilled wells indicates that the Island 
of Oahu has subsided by as much as 1,200 feet since the cessation of this early 
volcanic activity (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970). 
During that period of subsidence, coral-algal reefs began to grow on the southern 
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across the mouth of the bays. The 
growth of the reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence. A series of 
lagoons formed behind the barrier reefs, and both terrigenous and marine sediments 
accumulated in the lagoons (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970). 
During the later Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred 
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great 
continental glaciers advanced, the level of the ocean fell since there was less water 
available to fill the oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded or melted, global 
sea levels rose because more water was available. The landmass of Oahu remained 
essentially stable during these changes, and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. 
These glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea, which were both higher 
and lower relative to the present sea level of Oahu. 
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of 
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at

distinct ledges of reef limestone at elevations roughly conforming to the level of the sea. 
About 15,000 years ago, a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred. During that rise, 
the valleys in the project area were submerged. In the last 10,000 years or so, the sea 
level has adjusted to its present stand. Terrigenous and marine sediments have 
continued to accumulate in low-energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in 
thick deposits of soft/loose sediments along the coast in areas that were formerly 
valleys and drainageways. 
2.2 
Soil Survey 
Soil survey maps were also used to evaluate the existing subsurface conditions 
at the project site. The soil survey maps were obtained from the publication entitled 
“Soil Survey of Islands of Kauai, Oahu, Maui, Molokai, and Lanai, State of Hawaii” dated 
August 1972 by the United States Department of Agriculture, Soil Conservation Service. 
Description of the different soil classifications are provided below. 
MkA (Makiki Clay Loam) 
The Makiki clay loam is on smooth fans and terraces with a slope between 0 to 
2 percent. This soil is generally dark brown clay loam that has subangular blocky 
structure. It contains cinders and rock fragments. The soil has a moderate 
permeability and the erosion hazard is slight.  
FL (Fill Land, Mixed) 
This land type occurs mostly near Pearl Harbor and in Honolulu, adjacent to the 
ocean. It consists of area filled with material dredged from the ocean or hauled 
from nearby areas, garbage, and general material from other sources.

Design Subgrade Conditions

Based on our recommendation above, one concrete bus lane is planned for each 
travel direction. We anticipate that the new concrete bus lane might require minor 
grading up to 1 foot. In general, we recommend removing existing AC and PCC 
encountered during the subgrade preparation. The area within the contract grading 
limits should be cleared and grubbed thoroughly at the on-set of earthwork. To reduce 
the potential for contaminating the excavated materials, vegetation, debris, deleterious 
material and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly 
off-site or in a designated area.  
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and preparation below 
pavement areas should be over-excavated to expose stiff and/or dense materials. The 
depth of over-excavation should be extended until dense underlying materials are 
exposed or a maximum depth of 18 inches.  If soft and/or loose material is still present, 
a high strength geotextile, such as Mirafi HP570, should be placed over the soft soils 
prior to back filling.  The resulting excavation should be backfilled with aggregate 
subbase materials. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be properly disposed 
of off-site. 
After clearing and grubbing, the future pavement areas should be excavated to 
the pavement subgrade level. Our field exploration indicate that the subgrade materials 
at the design depth is generally medium dense to dense. Due to the relatively shallow 
depth of the utility lines, the traditional pavement subgrade scarification might not be 
feasible at the project roadway, therefore should be waived.

The pavement subgrade soils should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, and compacted to no less than 95 percent relative 
compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as 
a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance 
with AASHTO T 180 (ASTM D 1557). Optimum moisture is the water content 
(percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
If the subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade preparation, the pumping 
subgrade conditions may be stabilized by using cement treatment. As a guide, 
stabilization by cement treatment may consist of one sack of cement for approximately 
25 square feet of subgrade area. Geolabs should be contacted to evaluate the pumping 
subgrade conditions and to evaluate whether the pavement sections need to be revised 
and/or modified based on the exposed subgrade conditions. 
Where shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade, we 
recommend preparing again as recommended above. Saturation and subsequent 
yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may 
require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well-compacted fill. CBR 
and field density tests should be performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered 
during construction to confirm the adequacy of the pavement design. If the CBR and 
density test results vary from our design assumptions, adjustments of pavement 
sections and/or additional compaction efforts might be required. Contract documents 
should include additive and deductive unit prices for over-excavation and compacted fill 
placement to account for variations in the over-excavation quantities.

3.7 
Pavement Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the base, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, special attention 
should be given to the surface drainage of the pavements. Pavement sub-drainage is 
usually desirable; however, due to relatively shallow utility lines at the project roadway, 
over-excavation into the existing subgrade soil to install the drainage layers might risk 
damaging those lines. As a minimum, the pavement surface should be sloped and 
drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to 
appropriate drainage structures. Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the 
site during or after construction. 
3.8 
Pavement Joints 
We understand a concrete bus lane is proposed for each direction of traffic. 
Portland cement concrete pavement will require longitudinal joints to prevent cracking 
caused by concrete shrinkage/expansion. 
We recommend placing the joints at a maximum 15-foot spacing in both 
directions. The slabs created by the joints should have a maximum length to width ratio 
of 1.25. Those joints should be constructed in accordance with Section 37 of the 
Standard Specification and subsequent amendments.

3.10 Underground Utility Lines 
We envision that new utility lines and utility connections, such as water and 
sewer lines, will likely be required for the proposed project. In general, granular bedding

wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) below the bedding layer 
for more uniform support.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling seven borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 7, to a maximum depth of about 21.5 feet below 
the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with continuous flight auger and rotary coring tools. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.5.

examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified 
Soil Classification System, as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-7.

some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with

OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) 
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification 
and evaluation of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings 
at the appropriate sample depths. 
Six Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 & C 136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is presented on 
Plates B-1 and B-2. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) tests (ASTM D 1883) were 
performed on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics and swelling potential of the soils. Test results are presented on 
Plates B-3 and B-4. 
Two Modified Proctor Compaction tests (ASTM D 1557) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships. The test results are presented on Plates B-5 and B-6.
