REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
of middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be of late Pliocene to early
W O 3620 00
Page6
Pleistocene in age As volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from Koolau
banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as the
Schofield Plateau
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of this plateau generated
sediments which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and to
the south these sediments accumulated and interbedded with marine sediments and
coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge
The thickness of the
sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1 Highway to
over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the
confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present
Agricultural development within the last 100 years and recent mass grading work have
brought the Villages of Kapolei area to its present form

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration indicates that the project site is generally underlain by very
stiff to hard silty clays overlying medium dense to dense coral sands and gravels
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 21 5 feet below the existing ground
surface Based on the subsoil conditions encountered at the project site it is our opinion
that a shallow foundation system may be used to provide support for the proposed
residential structures However the near surface silty clay soils exhibit moderate to high
shrink swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents
As a result we recommend that the expansive clayey soils be capped with a minimum of
12 inches of non expansive select capping fill material below all building slab areas In
addition special attention should be given to the recommended procedures for subgrade
soil preparation below slabs and pavements in order to further reduce the potential
shrink swell effects of the near surface clayey soils
Our geotechnical engineering recommendations pertaining to the design of site
grading residential structure foundations and pavements are further discussed in the
following sections

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
General fill materials and the non expansive select granular fill material should be
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture placed in
level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction
Select borrow and base course materials
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts

Page 12
not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the clayey subgrade soils are
critical elements of earthwork observations and soil density tests should be
performed by Geolabs Hawaii during site grading to assist the contractor in
obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper moisture content on
each fill lift Where compaction is less than required additional compactive effort
should be applied with adjustment of moisture content as necessary to obtain the
specified compaction It should be noted that the moisture requirement of the
on site fills and subgrades at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture is an
important requirement considering the moderately to highly expansive nature of
the on site clayey soils

Slab On Grade Foundations
In general we envision that the proposed development will consist of one to
Slab on grade foundations with thickened edge footings may be used to support the
proposed one to two story structures anticipated for the subject project Our exploration
indicates that the site is generally underlain by moderately to highly expansive clayey
soils Unless slab on grade foundations supported by these expansive soils are properly
designed there is potential for future distress to the residential structures resulting from
wetting and subsequent swelling of the expansive clayey soils
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot p s f may
be utilized for the design of shallow foundations bearing on the recompacted in situ clay
soils or engineered fill material This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil Resistance due to
passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of
300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f This assumes that the soil around
footings is well compacted or undisturbed A coefficient of friction of 0 3 may be used for
footings bearing on the recompacted in situ clay soils or engineered fill materials Unless
W

covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil
should be neglected
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1 H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines
This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement If foundations are designed in accordance with our recommendations we
estimate total settlements to be less than 1 inch with differential settlements on the order
of½ inch

on site expansive soils we recommend that non expansive select capping fill material be

Prior to placement of the select capping fill the clayey subgrades should be
scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent

Page 16
above the underlying potentially expansive subgrade soil thereby reducing the loss of
moisture from the clayey subgrade soils In our opinion the reduction in potential drying
and wetting of the subgrade soils should reduce the impact of shrink swell effects on the
slab due to potential heaving of the underlying expansive clayey soils when wetted
The slab subgrades should be kept moist prior to placement of cushion fill and
concrete To reduce the potential for drying of the subgrade soils and to reduce the costs
of form construction we recommend that the slab edges be cast neat against the soil
excavations The slab edges should incorporate a sufficient amount of top and bottom
longitudinal steel reinforcement The top and bottom steel reinforcement bars should be
connected by stir ups The ends of the stir ups should extend into the floor slab area and
should be tied to the welded wire mesh The welded wire mesh in the slab on grade
should be embedded at the proper height in the concrete to provide adequate slab
reinforcement Blocks or saddles may be used to raise the wire mesh A stiff grade of
wire mesh is thus recommended A structural engineer should be consulted for design
details of this thickened edge which is intended to function both as a moisture barrier and
as a perimeter bearing wall footing
We recommend that a minimum 4 inch layer of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation be incorporated below the concrete slab as a cushion layer The slab
subgrade should be thoroughly wetted prior to concrete placement to allow the select
capping fill layer to remain in a moist condition To further reduce moisture infiltration a
plastic vapor barrier may be considered below the slab A 2 inch thick moist fine sand
layer should be placed above the plastic sheet to aid in the curing of the concrete
Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered since they

pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation

discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes
As an alternative a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used
instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also
be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall

12 inches of backfill should consist of non expansive impervious material to reduce

will generally consist of the near surface silty clays as described in our borings If site
grading exposes soils other than those assumed additional tests should be performed to
confirm or revise the recommended pavement sections for actual field conditions Based
on the above assumptions we recommend the following pavement sections be used for
preliminary design and budgeting purposes
Flexible Pavements
Main Roadways 56 foot Right of Way
Asphaltic Concrete
Base Course 95 percent relative compaction
Select Borrow 95 percent relative compaction
Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade
Secondary Roadways 44 foot or less Right of Way and Parking Areas
Asphaltic Concrete
Base Course 95 percent relative compaction
Select Borrow 95 percent relative compaction
Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade
Based on the results of our laboratory tests the near surface soils exhibit a
moderate to high expansion potential when subjected to changes in the moisture content
Therefore we recommend that the subgrade be properly prepared and kept moist prior
to placement of subbase and base course material The pavement subgrades should be
W O 3620 00
Page 22
scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above
the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction
All subgrades should be thoroughly moistened and kept moist until covered by the
subbase course If shrinkage cracks appear on the subgrade soils prior to placement of
the subbase course the subgrade soils should be scarified to a minimum depth of
8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content
and recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction The subbase and
base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all
surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed anywhere on the
site during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in
or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend the curbs extend a minimum of
2 inches into the road subgrade level to reduce migration of landscape water into the
pavement section Alternatively a subdrain system is recommended to collect excess
water from landscaping irrigation
For improved long term performance a subdrain
system constructed adjacent to paved landscape areas should be considered
Corrosion Protection for Water Lines
Five sets of pH and minimum resistivity tests were performed on the generally
clayey soils at the site Four of the five tests indicate minimum resistivity values of less
than 1 000 ohm cm which corresponds to a Corrosion Rating No 1 based on the
guidelines provided by the City County of Honolulu Board of Water Supply One of
the five tests indicate a minimum resistivity value of 1 500 ohm cm corresponding to a
W O 3620 00
Page 23
Corrosion Rating No 2 Based on these preliminary indications of corrosion potential
additional corrosion protection evaluation will be needed at a maximum spacing of
300 feet after the water line alignment is set to satisfy the requirements for corrosion
potential evaluation by the Board of Water Supply
Utility Trench
It is anticipated that the utilities for the proposed housing development will consist
primarily of water drainage and sewer In general good construction practices should
be utilized for the installation and backfilling of the trench for the proposed utilities The
method and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by the contractor
subject to practical limits and safety considerations The excavation should comply with
all applicable local state and federal safety requirements Trench shoring design and
installation should be the responsibility of the contractor Trench shoring and bracing
should conform to the appropriate health and safety requirements
Based on the results of our field exploration it appears that in general
conventional excavation methods may be utilized for utility line trenching at the site The
near surface clayey soils encountered at the site are generally stiff to hard However
deeper excavation may encounter medium dense to dense coral sands and gravels and
boulders as described in the logs of borings
In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used
under the pipes The initial backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of
free draining backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging
the pipes from compaction of the backfill It is critical that a free draining granular material
W O 3620 00
Page 24
be used to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to
provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes The use of on site clayey soils as
backfill around utility pipes is not recommended
We recommend that a minimum cover of 3 feet be provided between the top of
utility pipes and the finished grade to facilitate construction of the pavement structure and
compaction of the trench backfill The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level
1 foot above the pipes to the finish subgrade should consist of the approved on site soils
or select granular material The backfill material should be moisture conditioned to at
least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding
8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence The upper 2 feet of the
trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to not less than
95 percent relative compaction Mechanical compaction equipment should be used to
compact the materials at the project site Water tamping jetting or ponding should not
be used to compact the backfill material considering the expansive nature of the on site
soils
Drainage
The finished grades outside the structures should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations
and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
W O 3620 00
Page 25
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce water infiltration In addition drainage swales should be provided
as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away from the
slab and footing foundations

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on selected soil
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample depths
Eighteen 1 inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the soils in the natural and
compacted state The results of these tests are summarized on Plates B 1 through B 3
Seven Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 4
Five sets of pH ASTM D 2976 and minimum resistivity ASTM G 57 tests were
performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration The results of
these tests are summarized on Plate B 5
