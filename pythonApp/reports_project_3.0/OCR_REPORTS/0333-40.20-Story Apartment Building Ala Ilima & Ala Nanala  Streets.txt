I INTRODUCTION
General
This report presents the results of a soils investigation
for the proposed project The study consisted of subsurface
borings laboratory testing and engineering analyses as
outlined in our proposal dated March 21 1972 Based on the
results of our engineering studies conclusions and recom
mendations pertinent to site development are presented
Project Description
The property is located on the northeast corner of Ala Nanala
and Ala Iiima Streets in the Salt Lake area of Honolulu
_ The site is approximately 215 feet long by 160 feet wide
with a gentle slope to the north
A 20 story condominium tower and parking facilities are
presently planned The twenty story tower located along
the south end of the property is proposed to be a concrete
structure approximately 80 feet long by 40 feet wide Rela
tively heavy column and or wall loading is anticipated The
parking area covers approximately the north two thirds of
the property

II FIELD EXPLORATION
The subsurface exploration consisted of six 6 borings ranging
in depth from 21 5 to 44 feet The drilling was accomplished
using truck mounted rotary drilling equipment with continuous
flight hollow stem auger and NX core equipment The borings
were continuously inspected by an engineer or geologist from
our firn
The approximate boring locations are shown on Plate 1 and the
boring logs are presented in Appendix A
In all borings either Standard Penetration Test or 2 5 inch O D
ring samples were taken at selected depth intervals not exceed
ing 5 feet The Standard Penetration Test consists of driving
hammer free falling a distance of 30 inches The number of
blows required to drive the sampler the last 12 inches is termed
Standard Penetration Resistance N and is an approximate
measure of the relative density or consistency of a soil These
resistance values are plotted on the boring logs Samples obtained
were visually classified in the field and representative material
was placed in air tight containers and returned to our laboratory
for inspection and testing

Geologically the site is located in the Salt Lake Crater and
is underlain by a dense partially cemented volcanic TUFF
mudrock The tuff is exposed in the high bluffs about
400 feet south of the subject site as thin bedded gray and black
moderately well cemented sand volcanic ash This ash was
blown out of Salt Lake Crater several thousand years ago and
NO
deposited in thin layers and subsequently hardened by weathering
processes After the tuff was deposited coral limestone was

A lake was later formed within the crater and the ancient shore
line may have extended south of Ala Ilima Street This is
postulated on the presence of organic clay and silt at about
15 to 20 foot depth found in all the drill holes In some
holes coral limestone was encountered below the clay and above
the mudrock At the time of this exploration a deep trench was
excavated approximately 60 feet south Qf Ala Ilima opposite the
end of Ala Nanala Street At the bottom of the trench mudrock
was exposed overlain with more than 6 feet of pinkish coral
limestone and about 2 to 3 feet of dark gray organic clay and
silt Above the silt was 6 to 12 feet of fill material composed
of angular blocks of mudrock silty sand and mudrock fragments
of various sizes The materials exposed in this trench agree

IV ENGINEERING RECOMMENDATIONS
A General
The exploration program disclosed relatively poor subsurface
soil conditions within the property The site is unsuitable
for supporting heavy structural loads on conventional spread
or continuous footing Light structural loads could be
supported on conventional footings provided a certain amount
of settlement can be tolerated
The alternate foundation systems available for transferring
heavy structural loads to competent bearing material are
1 removal of existing fill and compressible material with
a compacted structural fill 2 deep spread and or continuous
footings 3 a pile or caisson foundation system to transfer
the heavy loads to a competent bearing strata
Based on discussion with the architect and structural engineer
the structural fill and deep footing alternate would not be
economically feasible for this project We have therefore
concentrated our studies on the pile foundation alternate

Based on the subsurface information the average pile length
should be 30 feet Predrilling will be required for all
piles due to the nature of the overlying fill material
A static load test should be performed on a test pile prior
to ordering production piles in order to establish pile
driving criteria and to confirm the calculated pile capacity
Settlement behavior of the pile would also be determined
during the load test The pile to be tested should be placed
as close as possible to one of the subsurface borings and
the pile could be used for the structure provided the test
is not carried out to failure
All piles should be designed and driven in accordance with
the governing building codes and should be driven under the
Supervision of a qualified soils engineer
Settlements
Settlements of pile foundations are difficult to predict
without a static load test However we anticipate settle
ments of less than 1 2 inch for the recommended pile support
system
We recommend that all piles be driven prior to construction
3
of the spread footings in order to eliminate the possibility
of settlements caused by pile driving vibrations
Lateral Earth Pressure
Basement or retaining walls should be designed for an equi
valent fluid pressure of 35 pounds per cubic foot provided
only moderate compaction of backfill is required If a
high degree of backfill compaction is required or the walls
cannot yield even slightly an equivalent fluid pressure of
60 pounds per cubic foot should be used Surcharges due to
adjacent footings hydrostatic pressure construction equip
ment etc must be added to the above values
Structural Fill
A six inch layer of compacted free draining granular fill
should be placed beneath all floor slabs Backfill for walls
and utility trenches etc should also be compacted free
draining granular material Compacted structural fill
should be placed in 6 inch layers and compacted to 90 of
the laboratory maximum density using ASTM D 1557 70 as a
standard

Laboratory Testing
G
1 Visual Classification All soil samples obtained from the
borings were brought to our Laboratory where they were visually
reclassified to confirm po modify the field classification prior
to finalizing the Logs of Borings
2 Water Content Determinations In addition to visual classi __
ication the natural moisture content was determined for all
samples to aid in soil classification and evaluating soil prop
erties The water content values based upon the dry weight of
the soils are shown on the Boring Logs
3 Unconfined Compression Tests Selected split spoon soil
samples were tested for unconfined strength Length to diameter
patio of 2 was maintained throughout the tests to avoid scatter
of test results and to facilitate evaluation of shear strength
values for design purposes Strength values designated Quy are
shown on the Boring Logs
4 Consolidation Tests Consolidation tests were performed on
selected samples of the compressible soils to provide basic
data for making settlement calculations In this test un
disturbed samples were carefully trimmed and fitted into a rigid
ving Porous stones were Placed on the top and bottom of the
samples to allow drainage Vertical loads were then applied in
increments with each load increment being allowed to consolidate
prior to adding the next increment Measurements of the time
compression were obtained during each load increment and rebound
was measured during the unloading portion
5 Shear Tests Shear tests were performed in the Direct
Shear Machine which is of the strain control type The rate of
deformation is approximately 0 025 inches per minute Each
_ sample is sheared under varying confining loads in order to
determine the Coulomb shear strength parameter cohesion and
angle of internal friction Samples are tested at the field
moisture and in an artificially saturated condition depending
upon the sample location and future site conditions The
oe results are plotted on the Shear Test Diagram
