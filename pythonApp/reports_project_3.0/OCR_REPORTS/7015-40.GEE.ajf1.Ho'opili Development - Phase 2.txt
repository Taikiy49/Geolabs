SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered surficial fills and 
cultivated soils about 1 to 2 feet thick overlying very stiff to hard silty and clayey alluvial 
soils extending down to the maximum depth explored of about 21.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface. We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of 
our field exploration. 
Based on our field observations, the surface soils at the project site are dry and 
friable. In addition, we observed shrinkage cracks up to about 6 inches deep at the 
existing ground surface at various locations throughout the project site. Our laboratory 
tests indicate that the near-surface clayey soils exhibit moderate to high shrink/swell 
characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents. When 
considering the lightly loaded structures planned for the Phase 2 development, it is 
anticipated that the moderate to high shrink-swell potential of the on-site soils could 
result in structural distresses to the new buildings and to pavements unless special 
subgrade preparations are implemented during construction. 
We recommend the site grading preparation consist of the following procedures 
for creating a reworked soil layer within the entire grading limits at the project site. 
1. 
Removal of 12 inches of the near surface cultivated soils after the clearing 
and grubbing operation. These soils should be considered “unsuitable” 
because they contain an appreciable amount of organic materials. These 
soils should be disposed properly off-site or used in landscaping areas, if 
appropriate. 
2. 
Over-excavate and remove an additional 12 inches of soil and moisture 
condition to 2 to 4 percent above the optimum moisture content for use as 
general fill, provided the soils are free of vegetation, debris, deleterious 
materials, and other unsuitable materials. 
3. 
Prior to replacing the moisture-conditioned soil as fill, the bottom of the 
over-excavation should be scarified to a depth of 12 inches, moisture 
conditioned to 2 to 4 percent above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction. 
The intent of the above site preparation is to create a 2-foot thick reworked soil 
layer within the entire grading limits. The reworked soil serves to close-up the shrinkage 
cracks observed throughout the site. It would reduce the future soil swelling/heaving 
potential by preparing the clayey soils with elevated moisture contents and reduced 
compaction requirements. In addition, it decreases the affinity of the clayey soil for

Generally, we anticipate the earthwork would likely involve two primary types of

and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The non-expansive select

moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted to at least 
90 percent relative compaction (or 95 percent relative compaction for finished subgrades 
subjected to vehicular traffic). 
The on-site clayey soils should not be allowed to dry out and should be protected to 
retain the moisture by keeping it moist or immediately capping it with new fill. If the on-site 
clayey soils dry out and shrinkage cracks develop, the soils should be re-scarified, 
moisture-conditioned, and recompacted again. 
Based on our field exploration and analyses, we believe the planned building 
structures may be supported on a shallow Wafflemat foundation system bearing on the 
2-foot reworked soil layer or general fills placed below the finished pad grade. The 
Wafflemat is a post-tensioned slab-on-grade foundation system. We recommend using 
12-inch high Waffleboxes to provide a stiffer foundation system in order to reduce 
differential movement and resulting angular distortion. We also recommend installing a 
vapor barrier underneath the Waffleboxes extending a minimum of 2 feet beyond the 
slab perimeter to reduce water infiltration to the slab foundations. 
If conventional concrete slabs-on-grade with thickened edge footings are used 
in lieu of the Wafflemat foundation system, we recommend providing 3 feet of 
non-expansive, select granular fill material beneath the concrete slabs-on-grade and 
extending beyond the perimeter of the slab a minimum of 3 feet. 
The individual homeowners and Homeowner’s Associations should be strongly 
cautioned against over-irrigation or landscaping within 3 feet around the house structures, 
which could adversely affect proper performance of the foundation system. In addition, a 
qualified geotechnical engineer should be retained to provide the recommendations to 
individual homeowner or Homeowner’s Association if improvements or expansions are 
planned beyond the original floor area.

SECTION 2. SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age. As the volcanic activity in Waianae Volcano ceased, lava flows 
from Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau, 
now known as the Schofield Plateau. The project site is on the southeastern flank of the 
Waianae Volcano. 
Physical and chemical weathering, followed by erosion of the Schofield Plateau, 
generated sediments which were transported to the coast. In the project vicinity and to 
the south, these sediments have accumulated and are interbedded with marine 
sediments and coral/algal reef formations, forming a sedimentary wedge. The 
thickness of the sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate 
Route H-1 Highway to over 1,000 feet at Ewa Beach. This wedge forms the Ewa Plain 
and serves as the confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of 
Southern Oahu. Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time 
through the present.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to 
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. 
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that 
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and

Subsurface Conditions

designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 119, extending to depths ranging from about 
16.5 to 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, six bulk samples of the 
near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-101 through Bulk-106, were obtained to evaluate

In general, the project site is underlain by surficial fills and cultivated soils 
approximately 1 to 2 feet thick over the majority of the project site. However, in some 
areas, the cultivated soils extended to a depth of about 5.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. The surficial fills and cultivated soils were generally underlain by clayey 
alluvial soils extending down to the maximum depth explored of approximately 21.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. 
The surficial fills generally consisted of stiff clays and dense silty coralline sand 
and gravel, and were generally encountered below the various access roads that 
traverse the project site. The cultivated soils generally consisted of silty and clayey soils 
with organics and agricultural debris that varied from soft in the tilled areas near the 
ground surface to very stiff below a depth of about 1 to 2 feet. In some areas, the 
cultivated soils extended to a depth of about 5.5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
However, it should be noted that the tilled, organic portion was generally limited to the 
upper 1 to 2 feet of the cultivated soils, while traces of agricultural debris consisting of 
black plastic fragments were observed in some of the borings extending to a depth of 
about 5.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  
The alluvial soils encountered underlying the surficial fills and cultivated soils 
generally consisted of very stiff to hard silty and clayey soils. Dense silty gravel with 
cobbles was encountered in multiple borings throughout the project site at depths 
ranging from about 13 to 19 feet below the existing ground surface in various thickness 
from about 1 to 5 feet. It should be noted that the alluvial silty and clayey soils are 
moderately to highly expansive when subjected to moisture fluctuations.  
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change 
due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other 
factors.

SECTION 3. DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the Ho`opili Development – Phase 2 project site is 
generally underlain by surficial fills and cultivated soils about 1 to 2 feet thick overlying 
very stiff to hard silty and clayey alluvial soils extending down to the maximum depth 
explored of about 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Field observations during 
our exploration indicated the surface soils at the project site are dry and friable. In

to pavements, due to the moderate to high shrink/swell potential of the on-site soils, 
unless special subgrade preparations are implemented during mass grading 
construction. Therefore, special attention should be given to the site grading preparation 
and foundation design to reduce risk of the structural distresses due to the moderately 
to highly expansive soil conditions.

to create a reworked soil layer within the entire grading limits at the project site.

appropriate. 
2.

condition to 2 to 4 percent above the optimum moisture content for use as 
general fill, provided the soils are free of vegetation, debris, deleterious

3.1.2 Fills and Backfills 
Generally, we anticipate the earthwork would likely involve two primary types of 
materials: select granular fill and general fill. Select granular fill should consist of 
non-expansive granular material, such as crushed coralline and/or basaltic 
materials. The material should be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no 
larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 
30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have a 
laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent 
or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. Geolabs should test 
imported fill materials for conformance with these recommendations prior to 
delivery to the project site for the intended use. 
General fills may consist of the on-site soils with a maximum particle size of 
6 inches and should be free of deleterious materials. In general, the excavated 
on-site materials (not including the 12 inches of soil materials removed as part of 
clearing and grubbing) may be re-used as a source of general fill materials if the 
materials are free of vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and other unsuitable 
materials.

3.1.5 Cut and Fill Slopes 
We envision the cut slopes at the project site generally will expose the stiff to 
hard silty and clayey soils encountered in the borings drilled. In general, cut 
slopes and permanent fill slopes constructed of the on-site soils may be designed 
with a slope inclination of two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. Fills 
placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the 
existing slope to provide stability of the new fill embankment against sliding. The 
filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level 
horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above general fill placement 
recommendations.  
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design 
slope ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. Surface water should be 
diverted away from the tops of slopes, and slope planting should be provided as 
soon as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes.

3.5.3 Drainage 
The retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the potential for build-up 
of hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide 
zone of permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67

3.7 
Corrosion Potential 
Three sets of laboratory corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum 
Resistivity (ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content 
(EPA 300.0), were performed by Test America Laboratories, Inc. to evaluate the 
corrosivity of the on-site soils on selected soil samples obtained from our field 
exploration. Results of the corrosivity tests are presented in Appendix B.  
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil 
characteristics with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In 
general, the lower the resistivity the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried 
metallic structure. Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will 
contribute to corrosion of metallic objects.

On the basis of the laboratory resistivity and pH results, the subsurface soils at

Design of metallic substructures, such as water line pipes or other metallic piping,

ground surface are low on the order of 120 parts per million (ppm) or less, while the 
sulfate content is relatively low, less than 70 ppm. Based on the chloride and sulfate

It may be appropriate to consult with a professional corrosion engineer to review the test 
results and provide detailed recommendations for corrosion protection.

3.8.3 Design Subgrade Conditions 
Based on our field exploration, the pavement subgrades along the proposed new 
roadway alignments are generally underlain by stiff to hard clay. Laboratory testing 
performed on the surface clayey soils at the project site generally indicated an 
average CBR value of about 7 and an average swell of about 3 percent.  
Due to the relatively poor pavement support characteristics of the on-site clayey 
soils, we recommend placing the pavement sections on a minimum 18-inch thick 
layer of non-expansive, select granular fill. It should be noted that the thickness 
of non-expansive, select granular fill material should be increased to 24 inches 
for the retention basin access roads due to the anticipated loading from 
maintenance equipment.  
Alternatively, hydrated lime treatment of the upper 12 inches of roadway 
subgrade could be used in lieu of the minimum 18-inch thick layer of 
non-expansive, select granular fill as described above. It is our opinion that the 
soil-lime stabilization is capable of mitigating in-situ clayey soil expansion when

3.8.5 Site Preparation Below Pavement Areas 
As mentioned previously, the near-surface clayey soils at the project site are dry 
and friable and exhibit moderate to high shrink/swell characteristics when 
subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents. Therefore, we recommend 
implementing special subgrade preparations during mass grading construction, 
as described in Section 3.1.1 – Site Preparation of this report.  
Where minor shrinkage cracks are observed after subgrade preparation, we 
recommend thoroughly moistening the soil to close the cracks prior to 
re-compacting. Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due 
to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft 
areas and replacement with well-compacted fill. 
3.8.6 Fill Materials Below Pavement Areas 
Select granular fill should consist of non-expansive granular material, such as 
crushed coralline and/or basaltic materials. The material should be well-graded 
from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and 
should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. 
The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have 
a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with 
ASTM D1883.  
Aggregate base course and subbase course should meet the material 
requirements for Base Course and Subbase Course as specified in 
Sections 31 and 30, respectively, of the Standard Specifications for Public Works 
Construction, City & County of Honolulu, September 1986. Geolabs should test 
imported fill materials for conformance with these recommendations prior to 
delivery to the project site for the intended use. 
3.8.7 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
The non-expansive, select granular fill should be placed in level lifts of about 
8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The

compaction requirement for the final lift of the select granular fill should be 
increased to at least 95 percent relative compaction. Aggregate base course and 
subbase course should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction.  
The clayey subgrades below the non-expansive, select granular fill should be 
scarified to a depth of about 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to 2 to 4 percent 
above the optimum moisture, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content 
(percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
As an additional check for stability and uniform compaction, we recommend 
proof-rolling the non-expansive, select granular fill material prior to placing the 
aggregate base course (for flexible pavement section) or the aggregate subbase 
course (for rigid pavement section) materials using a pneumatic-tired vehicle with 
a gross vehicle weight of at least 30,000 pounds, such as a fully-loaded water 
truck. The equipment used for proof-rolling should be operated at a speed of 
about 300 feet per minute and make at least two passes over each area 
designated for proof-rolling. Proof-rolling should also be performed on successive 
lifts of aggregate base course or subbase course materials. Areas with excessive 
rutting and/or pumping should be over-excavated to expose firm material, and 
the resulting excavation should be backfilled with either well-compacted fill or 
aggregate base/subbase material.

3.9 
Erosion Control Measures (Landscape) 
As indicated previously, we observed shrinkage cracks at the existing ground 
surface of the project site. In general, shrinkage cracks will develop in the ground due to 
the lack of maintenance of the soil moisture content as the soils start to dry out under 
the sun and dry weather conditions. The development of shrinkage cracks in the ground 
was apparently intensified by the prolonged dry climatic conditions in the Ewa area 
resulting in large fluctuations in the soil moisture content. 
We recommend grassing the graded surfaces (including cut and fill slopes) to 
reduce the potential for significant soil erosion soon after completion of the site grading. 
The relatively dry climatic conditions of the Ewa area would likely require controlled 
irrigation of the graded surfaces to maintain the moisture contents and to keep graded 
surfaces free of large shrinkage cracks. However, over-watering of the graded surfaces 
should be avoided to reduce the potential for saturation and softening of the surface 
soils.

water away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after construction. It 
is highly recommended to install gutter systems around the buildings and to divert 
discharge away from the foundation and slab areas. Excessive landscape watering near 
the foundations and slabs (within 3 feet of the building perimeter) should be avoided. 
Planters next to foundations also should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and 
drains to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration into the subsoils. 
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the 
building foundations and slabs-on-grade because ponded water could cause subsurface 
soil saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength. The foundation excavations 
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of

A P E N D I X   A 
Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
nineteen borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 119, extending to depths 
ranging from about 16.5 to 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Six bulk samples 
of the near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-101 through Bulk-106, were obtained to 
evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the near-surface soils. The approximate 
boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 and 2.2. The 
borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-19. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts 
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Ten Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of the test 
results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Four Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) including three hydrometer tests 
(ASTM D422) were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation 
characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. Graphic presentation of the 
grain size distributions are provided on Plate B-2. 
Thirteen one-inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the on-site soils. The test results 
are summarized on Plate B-3. 
Four Expansion Index of Soil tests were performed on selected samples in 
general accordance with ASTM D4829. The test is a standardized test used to 
determine the expansive characteristics of the soil. The test results are summarized on 
Plate B-4. 
Eight one-dimensional swell or settlement potential tests (ASTM D4546 
Method A) were performed to evaluate the swell and/or compressibility characteristics of 
the remolded soil samples under different surcharge pressures. A soil sample is 
remolded to approximately 90 percent relative compaction (based on ASTM D1557) to 
simulate the anticipated field conditions. The remolded soil sample is allowed to settle 
until it has stabilized under a surcharge pressure. Water is then added to the soil 
sample to evaluate its collapse or swell potential in a saturated condition. The test 
results are presented on Plates B-5 and B-6. 
Two Consolidation tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the 
compressibility characteristics of the on-site soils. The tests were performed in 
accordance with ASTM D2435, and the test results are plotted on Plates B-7 and B-8. 
To evaluate the undrained shear strength of the soils, four Unconsolidated 
Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXUU) tests were performed on selected in-situ soil 
samples in accordance with ASTM D2850. The approximate in-situ effective overburden 
pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the “undisturbed” samples. The 
test results and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-9 through B-12.

Field Infiltration Tests 
Twelve field infiltration tests were conducted at the site to evaluate the infiltration 
characteristics of the subsurface materials encountered at the boring locations. The 
infiltration tests were performed in Boring Nos. 101, 103, 104, 107 through 109, 111, 
112, 114, and 117 through 119 at the approximate boring locations shown on the 
Site Plans, Plates 2.1 and 2.2. These tests were performed in general accordance with 
the procedures in Appendix D of the State of Maryland Department of the Environment 
“Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I and II” (rev. 2009). These procedures are 
consistent with other state’s procedures and may generally be considered an industry 
standard. 
The field infiltration tests were performed by augering a boring to the selected test 
depths ranging from about 3.5 to 15 feet below the existing ground surface. Upon 
reaching the test depth, a 4-inch diameter PVC solid casing was set to the bottom of the 
drilled hole to allow infiltration only through the soil exposed on the bottom of the boring. 
Falling head infiltration tests were performed to determine the average infiltration rates of 
the underlying subsurface materials. Each test consisted of four trials of filling the casing 
with 24 inches of water and taking periodic readings over a one-hour trial period. The 
infiltration rates are then calculated based on the result of the fourth and last trial for each 
test location. The calculated infiltration rates at each test location are summarized in the 
following table. The results of our field infiltration tests are presented on Plates C-1 through 
C-12 of Appendix C.
