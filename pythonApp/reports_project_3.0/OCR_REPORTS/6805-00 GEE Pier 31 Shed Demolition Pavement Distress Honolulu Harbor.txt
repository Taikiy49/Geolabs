SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered AC paving of about 7 to 8 inches thick underlain 
by loose to medium dense coralline sand and gravel fill that ranged in thickness from about 
1.3 to 4.3 feet. A horizon of harbor deposits consisting of very soft silty clay and clayey silt 
underlies the fill layer extending to a depth of about 10 feet below the existing pavement 
surface. Coralline detritus (mostly dense coralline sand) was encountered from about 10 to 
15 feet underlain by coral formation to the maximum depth drilled of 26.5 feet.  
We encountered groundwater at depths of about 6.2 to                                6.5 feet 
below ground surface, in all three borings. 
We understand that the project involves demolition of a portion of the existing shed 
to provide additional cargo operating area and storage space to support roll-on and roll-off 
(Ro/Ro) operations. The existing asphaltic concrete (AC) pavement is in generally fair to 
good condition. However, there are a few areas with significant depressions in the 
pavement surface on the order of about 2 to 3 inches.  
Based on the results of our geotechnical exploration and analysis, we envision that 
the depressions in the flexible pavement came about as a consequence of sustained heavy 
static loads placed over the affected areas. The very soft and compressible near-surface 
soil underlying the AC pavement may have consolidated and settled under prolonged 
heavy surcharge conditions. 
For Ro/Ro operations, we anticipate light transient loads consisting of cars and 
pick-up trucks and occasional trailer trucks that transport these vehicles to dealerships 
around the Island of Oahu and other destinations. It is our opinion that the existing 
pavement is sufficient to support the above-anticipated loading conditions. However, if 
sustained static heavy loads are required, such as with heavy crane loading and unloading 
operations and stacking of loaded containers that sit for relatively long periods of time, the 
concentrated loads will need to be distributed across a wider area on a rigid slab or 
structurally supported on deep foundations to avoid localized settlement of the underlying 
very soft clay that can lead to an uneven pavement surface. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration and 
summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations derived from our field 
exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, 
Plate 1. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project involves demolition of a portion of the existing shed to provide additional 
cargo operating area and storage space to support Matson’s Ro/Ro operations. The 
existing asphaltic concrete pavement is in generally fair to good condition. However, there 
are a few areas with significant depressions in the pavement surface.  
Our geotechnical exploration focused on the subsurface conditions beneath these 
depressed areas to evaluate the carrying condition of the pavement. To achieve these 
objectives, two borings (Boring Nos. 1 and 2) were drilled in the depressed pavement and 
one boring (Boring No. 3) was drilled in a relatively “good” pavement area for comparison. 
The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drilling rig to depths of about 11.5 to 
26.5 feet below the pavement surface. Soil samples were collected using split-spoon 
samplers. The soil samples were brought to our laboratory for classification and testing. 
1.3 
Purpose and Scope 
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the subsurface 
conditions at the project site to develop an idealized soil/rock data set to formulate 
geotechnical engineering recommendations for the repair of existing pavements that 
appear to be in distress due to observed localized settlements.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late 
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age.   A long period of volcanic inactivity followed during 
which time erosion formed deep gullies in the Koolau shield. Volcanic activity resumed with 
a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formation known as the Honolulu 
volcanic series.  
The project site lies on the coastal plain of southern Oahu which was built on the 
eroded flanks of the Koolau Volcano. Koolau forms the eastern two thirds of the Island of 
Oahu.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. Most of the coastal plains were developed during the 
Pleistocene Epoch when the sea levels fluctuated significantly.  As the glaciers grew and 
advanced, less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below 
the present stands of the sea.  When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water 
became available such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic  sea 
level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations.  
The project site is generally underlain by deposits of harbor sediments and coralline 
formation at greater depths. A surface layer of man-made fill was placed over these 
deposits to extend the shoreline for the development of Honolulu Harbor within the last 
century.

2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is at Pier 31, about 1,600 feet south of North Nimitz Highway and 
Alakawa Street intersection in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The site is bounded 
by Pier 32 on the northwest, Pier 30 to the southeast, Honolulu Harbor to the southwest 
and the Sea Engineering complex to the northeast. The project site encompasses an area 
of approximately 100,000 square feet. The entire site is generally paved with AC and 
currently under the roof of a large steel-framed shed that continues into the adjacent 
Piers 30 and 32.  
2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling one 26.5-foot deep 
boring (Boring No.1) and two 11.5–foot deep borings (Boring Nos. 2 and 3) within the study 
area for pavement subgrade characterization. The approximate boring locations are shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2. Boring Nos. 1 and 2 were drilled in the depressed pavement area 
while Boring No. 3 was drilled in a relatively “good” area for comparison. 
Generally, the borings encountered about 7 to 8 inches of AC over 1.5 to 4.5 feet of 
fill consisting of loose to medium dense coralline sand with gravel. This fill layer is underlain 
by very soft silty clay harbor deposits extending down to about 10 feet below the existing 
ground surface. This soft compressible clayey horizon rests on dense/hard coral formation 
occurring below 10 feet in the three borings extending to the maximum depth drilled of 
26.5 feet in Boring No. 1. We encountered groundwater at a depth of about 6.5 feet below 
ground surface in all the borings at the time of our drilling. 
Detailed descriptions of the field exploration methodology are presented in 
Appendix A. Descriptions and graphic representations of the materials encountered in the 
borings are presented on the Logs of Borings in Appendix A. Laboratory tests were 
performed on selected soil samples, and the results are presented in Appendix B.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our limited field exploration, the borings encountered  a pavement section 
of 7 to 8 inches of AC over fill consisting of loose to medium dense coralline sand with 
gravel and soft to medium stiff silty clay and clayey silt extending down to about 5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. Soft compressible clayey harbor deposit was 
encountered from about 5 to 10-foot depth over dense coral detritus and coral formation 
extending to the maximum depth drilled of 26.5 feet in Boring No. 1. We encountered 
groundwater at a depth of about 6.5 feet below ground surface in all the borings at the time 
of our drilling. 
Boring No. 3 was drilled in a relatively “good” pavement area to compare the 
subsurface conditions with the depressed areas. The subsurface conditions in Boring No. 3 
were very similar to those encountered in Boring Nos. 1 and 2 (depressed areas). 
Therefore we conclude that the cause of the pavement depression is excessive loading on 
the pavement surface from stacking loads or possibly crane reaction loads. 
3.1 
Pavement Distress  
Existing site conditions indicate the presence of localized pavement distress 
characterized by about 3 to 4-inch deep (near the center) concave depressions with visible 
shearing failure of the pavement along the perimeter of the affected areas. Based on site 
observations and analysis of the underlying stratigraphic conditions, It appears that the 
distress zones on the pavement were likely caused by prolonged heavy surface loading 
exceeding the allowable bearing capacity of the foundation soils and/or consolidation of the 
soft harbor deposits. We recommend avoiding prolonged stationary surface loads greater 
than about 500 psf pavement as much as possible or these may be distributed over a wider 
bearing area on the pavement such as with steel plates or by rigid slab on grade specially 
designed for this purpose. 
3.2 
Subgrade Strength Characterization  
Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) tests were performed in all three boring 
locations in accordance with ASTM D 6951 to obtain indicative subsoil strength

characteristics such as bearing ratio and bearing value. The tests were generally performed 
from the bottom of the AC (top of base course) through the subgrade to a depth of 4 feet. 
The DCP device consists of two 16mm-diameter stainless steel rods with the lower 
rod containing an anvil, a replaceable 60o pointed tip and depth markings. The upper rod 
holds a 17.6-pound drop hammer with a 22.6 inch drop distance. At the beginning of a test, 
the cone is “seated” by dropping the hammer until the widest part of the cone is just below 
the testing surface at which point the initial penetration is recorded as 0. The operator then 
lifts and drops the hammer one or more times depending on the strength of the soil at the 
test location. Following each sequence of hammer drops, a penetration reading is taken. 
The process is continued until the desired depth of testing is reached or the full length of 
the lower rod is buried. 
Data from our DCP testing is presented as the penetration index (PI) which is the 
penetration distance of the cone for each drop of the hammer expressed in millimeters per 
blow. The penetration indices obtained from our tests were correlated with common 
pavement design parameters such as California Bearing Ratio (CBR) and bearing value.

3.3 
Pavement Recommendations  
Specific loading conditions were not available at the time of this report. However, 
preliminary information provided by CH2M Hill indicated that Matson’s proposed Ro/Ro 
locations at Pier 31 will be similar to the adjacent use of facilities at Piers 30, 32 to 34, 
which will generally involve loading and unloading of light vehicles with a temporary holding 
and storage area, therefore we anticipate relatively light to moderate transient loads 
consisting of cars, SUV’s and pick-up trucks and occasional trailer trucks that haul these 
vehicles overland to dealerships and other destinations. Based on these assumptions, it is 
our opinion that the existing pavement is sufficient to support these anticipated loading 
conditions. 
3.3.1 Repair of Depressed Pavement Areas. 
To restore the depressed portions of the pavement at Pier 31, we recommend the 
removal of the old pavement above the depression by saw-cutting at least 2 feet 
beyond the perimeter of the affected area. The repair area should be over 
excavated to a depth of 14 inches below the existing grade. The exposed subgrade 
should be proof rolled (with a minimum of 5–ton capacity roller) to determine the 
presence of soft and yielding soils. Soft and yielding soils should be over excavated 
to a maximum depth of 12 inches below subgrade or until firm materials are

of its maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D1557. The compacted

such observations. If the actual soil conditions encountered during construction are different 
from those assumed or considered in this report, then appropriate design modifications 
should be made.

SECTION 4. LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from the field borings. Variations of subsoil conditions between and 
beyond the borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not 
become evident until construction is underway.  If variations then appear evident, it will be 
necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations provided herein. 
The locations of the field borings indicated in this report are approximate, having 
been estimated by taping from the features shown on the Site Plan provided by CH2M Hill. 
The elevations shown on the borings were obtained from GPS readings at the time of the 
field exploration. The physical locations and elevations of the borings should be considered 
accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict 
the approximate boundaries between soil types and, as such, may denote a gradual 
transition.  Water level data presented in the text of this report were based on 
measurements from the borings at the time of the exploration. These data have been 
reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report.  However, it must be 
noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in tides, temperature, and other factors.  
This report has been prepared for the exclusive use of CH2M Hill, Inc and their 
consultants for specific application to the proposed shed demolition and pavement distress 
evaluation for Matson’s new facility at Piers 31 in accordance with generally accepted 
geotechnical engineering principles and practices.  No warranty is expressed or implied. 
This report has been prepared solely for the purpose of assisting the architect and 
engineer in the design of the proposed project.  Therefore, this report may not contain 
sufficient data, or the proper information, to serve as the basis for preparation of 
construction cost estimates.  A contractor wishing to bid on this project is urged to retain a 
competent geotechnical engineer to assist in the interpretation of this report and/or in the 
performance of additional site-specific exploration for bid estimating purposes.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling 
two 11.5-foot deep borings and one 26.5-foot deep boring within the existing pavement. 
The approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The 
borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous-flight 
solid-stem augers. Dynamic Cone Penetration tests were performed in all borings for 
pavement subgrade characterization. 
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural 
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing 
in the laboratory.  Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil 
Classification System as shown on Plate A-0.1.  Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop.  The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the 
field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive 
strength of the soil sample.  Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on the Logs 
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Dynamic Cone Penetration (DCP) tests (ASTM D 6951) were performed in all 
three boring locations to obtain indicative subsoil strength characteristics such as California 
Bearing Ratio and corresponding bearing value for pavement evaluation. The tests were 
performed using a Salem Dual Mass Dynamic Cone Penetrometer from the top of the base 
course through the subgrade to a depth of about 4 feet. The test results are discussed in 
the text of this report.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Three Sieve Analysis (ASTM C 117 & ASTM C 136) tests were performed on 
selected samples from the borings to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the surface 
fills and to aid in soil classification.  A graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is 
provided on Plate B-1.  
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth. Graphic 
representation of the test results is provided on Plate B-2.
