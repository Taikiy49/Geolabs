house structures may be supported on shallow foundations provided they are embedded

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated
to be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still
active and its eastern flank is partially buried below the Koolau lavas in central Oahu
During the Pleistocene Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles
of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available
to fill the ocean basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea
When glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the
sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacioeustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
These sea level fluctuations created drowned river valley systems and the
development of coral reefs The coral reefs and lithified sand dunes may be observed
as exposed subaerially on Oahu and also buried in submarine deposits well off shore of
the island The project site geology is typified by a section of recent alluvium and
calcareous marine deposits overlying the Koolau basalt basement rock

3 5 Field Percolation Testing
Field percolation tests were performed in the borings in general accordance with
Wastewater Systems and Chapter 10 of the Ten States Standards To evaluate the
subsoil profile and permeability of the subsurface soils for wastewater disposal the
percolation tests were performed by drilling 4 5 inch diameter boreholes extending to
depths ranging from about 5 to 7 feet below the existing ground surface A total of
three percolation tests were performed at the locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Two percolation tests were performed within the surface alluvial layer and one test was
performed within the borehole that extended into the underlying sandstone deposit
Prior to testing the test holes were presoaked for at least 4 hours During
testing the holes were carefully filled with water to a minimum height of 6 inches above
the bottom of the hole The time intervals and water drops were then recorded to
provide data upon which calculation of the percolation rates were based Based on the
test results the percolation rates ranging from about 30 to 300 minutes per inch were
measured within the surface clayey soils For the percolation test performed within the
underlying sandstone deposit a percolation rate of about 20 minutes per inch was
measured The results of the individual percolation tests are presented on Plates A 4
through A 6 of this report
Based on the results of the field percolation tests performed within the surface
clayey soils we believe that the upper alluvial layer is not suitable for a shallow leaching
field system Based on the test results performed within the underlying sandstone
deposit it appears that the project site may be suitable for siting the new septic system
Seepage pits extending into the underlying sandstone layer may be considered for the
new septic system

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 to depths of about 15 9 to 16 5 feet
below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with auger
equipment In addition three field percolation tests were performed in the drilled borings
to depths ranging from 5 to 7 feet below the existing ground surface
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
