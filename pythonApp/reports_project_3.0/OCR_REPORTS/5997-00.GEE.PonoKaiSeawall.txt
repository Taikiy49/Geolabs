This letter report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Pono Kai Seawall project at Kapaa on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. The
project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
This letter report summarizes our findings and preliminary geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration, laboratory tests, and engineering
analyses. These preliminary recommendations are intended for the planning and
preliminary design of the proposed new seawall. The findings and _ preliminary
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
letter report.
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located at Kapaa Beach Park in Kapaa on the Island of Kauai,
Hawaii. We understand that the backfill (which consist of beach sand) behind the
existing rock seawall is passing through possible voids within the seawall.
As a result of the loss of backfill behind the seawall, sinkholes and settlement of
the existing ground surface behind the seawall have occurred. A portion of the seawall
is also tilting. Ve understand that it is planned to replace the existing seawall with a
new seawall.

geotechnical engineering recommendations for the planning and preliminary design of the

proposal dated September 28, 2007. Our scope of work included the following tasks and

SITE DESCRIPTION
The site is located at Kapaa Beach Park in Kapaa on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii.
The location of the project is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The existing seawall is
constructed of basaltic boulders. The seawall is about 600 feet long and about 4 to
8 feet high. A concrete bike path traverses behind the seawall.
The existing ground surface behind the seawall is relatively level with elevations
ranging from about +10 to +12 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). The existing ground
surface elevations fronting the seawall ranges from about +4 to +6 feet MSL.
At the time of our field exploration, ground settlement behind the seawall
appeared to be as much as about 2.5 feet at the north end of the seawall. In addition to
ground settlement, sinkholes were observed behind the seawall. We observed tilting of
the seawall along the northern half of the alignment. Erosion of the beach sand was
observed at both ends of the seawall.

FIELD EXPLORATION
A field exploration consisting of drilling three borings was conducted on
February 20, 2008. The three borings, designated as Borings Nos. 1 through 3, were
drilled to depths of about 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate
locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted
drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools.
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operations on a near-
continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System, as shown on Plate 3. Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings, Plates 4 through 6.
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586-84, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch
O.D. standard penetration sampler or a 3-inch O.D. Modified California sampler with a
140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths.
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling three borings,
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, extending to depths of about 21.5 feet below the
existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan,
Plate 2.
The borings generally encountered a thin layer of fill about 1 foot thick. The fill
consisted of medium dense silty sand. Below the fill, medium dense to dense coralline
sand was encountered in the borings and extended to the maximum depths explored.
Boring No. 3 encountered dense sandy gravel at a depth of about 17.5 feet below the
existing ground surface and extended to the maximum depth explored.
We encountered groundwater at depths of about 10.4 and 12 feet below the
existing ground surface, which correspond to about +1.4 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL), at
the time of our field exploration. It should be noted that groundwater levels are expected
to fluctuate with variation in tides, rainfall, surface water runoff, groundwater withdrawal,
and other factors.

LABORATORY TESTING
The following laboratory tests were performed on selected soil samples obtained
from our field exploration.
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and determinations were performed as an aid in
the classification and evaluation of soil properties. Test results are presented on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
Five Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 and C 136) were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics and to aid in soil classification.
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate 7.
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
We understand that the proposed new seawall may have to retain backfill as high
as 15 feet. Since the new seawall will be relatively close to the shoreline, there is a high
potential for foundation scour of the new seawall. The new seawall will have to be
designed to take into consideration scour of its foundations. The two options that were
considered for the project include the following:
e Conventional Reinforced Concrete Retaining Wall with Sheet Pile Cutoff
° Drilled Shaft Retaining Wall
Detailed discussions and preliminary recommendations for the planning and
preliminary design of the proposed new seawall are presented in the following sections
herein.
Reinforced Concrete Retaining Wall
A conventional reinforced concrete retaining wall may be used for the proposed
new seawall. Construction of the conventional wall will require temporary shoring and
dewatering of the foundation excavation. Sheet piles extending below the anticipated
scour line may be installed to provide temporary shoring and also to provide a cutoff wall
for dewatering purposes. After construction of the retaining wall, the front side sheet pile
should be cut above the bottom of foundation and left in place to act as a cutoff wall
reducing the potential for loss of foundation support due to scour. It should be noted that
there may be a potential for encountering boulders and cobbles behind the existing
seawall; therefore, it may be difficult to install the sheet piles if boulders and cobbles are
encountered during the installation operations.
The advantages and disadvantages of using a reinforced concrete retaining wall for
the project are presented in the following table.

Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, a preliminary allowable
bearing pressure of 2,500 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for design of the
retaining wall foundations bearing on the coralline sand. This bearing value is for
dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as
those caused by wind or seismic forces.
Lateral loads acting on the retaining wall may be resisted by friction developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure
acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A coefficient of friction of
0.30 and passive earth pressures of 300 and 125 pounds per square foot per foot of depth
(pcf) may be used for above and below groundwater conditions, respectively. This
assumes that the soils around the footings are well compacted. Unless covered by
pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be
neglected.
The retaining wall should be designed to resist lateral earth pressures due to the
adjacent soils and surcharge effects caused by loads adjacent to the retaining wall. The
recommended active lateral earth pressures for design of the retaining wall, expressed
in equivalent fluid pressures are 40 and 82 pcf for above and below groundwater
conditions, respectively.
Drilled Shaft Retaining Wall

along the proposed wall alignment, may be used for the proposed new seawall. The top of
the drilled shafts will extend up to the proposed finish grades to retain backfill placed
behind the drilled shafts. To reduce the potential of loss of foundation support due to

LIMITATIONS
This preliminary geotechnical engineering exploration report is prepared for
planning and preliminary design purposes only. It is not intended for use in final design
and/or preparation of construction documents for this project. An additional design-level
geotechnical engineering exploration of the proposed project should be conducted for final
design.
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon
information obtained from the field borings. Variations of subsoil conditions between and
beyond the field borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not
become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, Geolabs
should be contacted to re-evaluate the preliminary recommendations presented in this
report.
The test boring locations are approximate, having been estimated by taping from
reference points and visible features shown on the Topographic Map provided by Oceanit
on February 13, 2008. The elevations of the borings were interpolated from the contour
lines shown on the Topographic Map provided by Oceanit on May 9, 2008. The physical
locations and elevations of the field borings should be considered accurate only to the
degree implied by the methods used.
