PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project involves the development of a proposed wind turbine electrical 
generation facility in the Kahuku area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project site is 
located in the general vicinity of Kahuku town as shown on the Project Location Map, 
Plate 1.  
The proposed project consists of nine (9) Wind Turbine Generators (WTG) 
designated as WTG-1 through WTG-9. In addition, alternate locations are proposed for 
WTG-1 through WTG-3 and WTG-5. The alternate locations are designated as 
WTG-1A, WTG-2A, WTG-3A, and WTG-5A and are adjacent to the primary location 
sites. A substation/switchyard, operation and maintenance (O&M) building, transmission 
lines, access roadways to each WTG location, and other site improvements are also 
planned.  The substation/switchyard and O&M building are planned at the eastern 
portion of the site near WTG-8 and WTG-9.  
For discussion purposes, the WTG project site may be separated into 
two geographic regions comprising the eastern and western portions of the project site. 
The western portion of the project will include WTG-1 through WTG-5 and their 
respective adjacent alternate locations. The main existing access road serving the

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was formed by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late 
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The younger shield, 
Koolau Volcano, is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. Waianae Volcano became extinct 
while Koolau Volcano was still active, and the eastern flank of Waianae Volcano was 
partially buried below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank. 
Based on our review of available geological information, the upper elevation hills at 
the western and southern portions of the project site are generally composed of weathered 
basaltic rock and derived soil products resulting from lava flows of the Koolau Volcanic 
Series. Weathering of the parent basaltic rock has developed a near-surface horizon of 
residual and saprolitic soils. In general, the residual and saprolitic soils are primarily 
composed of silty and clayey materials that are typical of the deep tropical weathering of 
the parent volcanic rock. In general, the stiff saprolitic soils grade to hard basaltic rock 
formation with increasing depth.  Embedded within the saprolitic soils are occasional hard 
basaltic boulders that remain intact and more resistant to chemical weathering processes. 
We observed some of these basaltic boulders sporadically at the ground surface in the 
higher elevations of the hilly terrain at the western and southern portions of the project site. 
The existing boulders represent erosional remnant “corestones” of the parent rock 
material.  The hard and typically spherical shaped boulders are mainly present on the hill 
top summits and ridgelines of the project site. The remnant boulders typically occur as 
embedded rocks, partial surface outcroppings, and as isolated surface rocks. 
Based on our review of the available geological information, the eastern and 
northern portions of the project site are underlain by clayey alluvial soils, lithified 
calcareous dune sand (sandstone), and some unconsolidated calcareous dune sand. In 
places, a generally thin mantle of alluvial soils covers the buried sandstone and dune sand 
deposits. In general, the eastern and northern portions of the project site contain flattened 
plateaus and low relief landforms (terraces and broad gently sloped hills) underlain by the 
alluvial soils and calcareous sand deposits. 
The calcareous dune sands were formed by the deposition of calcareous beach 
sand blown inland from ancient beaches.  The dune sands were lithified into medium 
dense to dense sandstone material by percolating rain water carrying weak acids. Cross

GENERAL SITE CONDITIONS 
The project site is south and west of Kahuku town adjacent to existing rural 
agricultural farmland. The project site and location of the proposed improvements are 
shown on the General Site Plan, Plate 2.  In general, the proposed WTG’s are clustered 
at two separate portions of the project site, previously referred to as the western and 
eastern areas of the proposed WTG development. 
The western portion of the WTG development contains WTG-1 through WTG-5 
and their respective alternate sites, which are generally within the large parcel identified 
as TMK: 5-6-008: 006.  The main existing paved access route to the western portion of 
the development is via the existing paved farmland access roads westerly of 
Pualalea Street.  The eastern portion of the development contains WTG-6 through 
WTG-9, which is generally within the large parcel identified as TMK: 5-6-006: 018. The 
main existing access to WTG-6 through WTG-9 is via the existing dirt road identified as 
Malaekahana Valley Road.  
Western WTG Development 
The proposed WTG development within TMK: 5-6-008: 006 at the western 
portion of the project site generally resides within existing undeveloped hilly forested 
lands that are generally densely vegetated with trees, shrubs, and grasses. The western 
portion is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 3.1. The proposed development at the western 
portion of the project is bounded by existing agricultural farms toward the east and 
undeveloped natural hills and gullies toward the north, west, and south of the proposed 
development. Some existing dirt access trails occupy the hills within the WTG 
development area. 
The western WTG development resides within hilly terrain generally composed of 
residual and saprolitic soils containing sporadic basaltic boulders.  The existing boulders 
occur in generally natural and stable slope settings because they represent isolated 
in-situ erosional remnant expressions of the parent rock material. Based on our 
observations, the natural hillslopes appear to stand at average inclinations of about 
one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V) with some localized steepened slopes 
approaching 0.5H:1V. The ground surface elevations at the proposed WTG locations 
generally range between about +290 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) and +430 feet MSL. 
Potential rockfall hazard conditions involving the natural boulder deposit settings were 
not observed. Existing overt visible signs of earth movement including landslide and 
debris flow were not encountered on the slopes during our site reconnaissance and 
review of available aerial imagery.

SITE RECONNAISSANCE 
A geological reconnaissance of the study area was conducted to obtain a visual 
overview of the existing site conditions with respect to the occurrence of potential natural 
hazards such as existing slope instability, past debris flow activity, and potential rockfall 
source areas. Our geological reconnaissance included visual observations and mapping of 
pertinent features recorded during a foot-traverse of the existing slopes within the 
proposed development study area. Our geologists performed the site reconnaissance 
under dry weather and ground conditions using the available field reference staking 
supplemented with a handheld Garmin Global Positioning System (GPS).  
The primary purpose for the geological reconnaissance was to obtain an overview 
of the general location, size, spatial distribution, and setting of existing surface boulders 
and rock outcroppings residing on sloping terrain within the proposed development areas 
of the project site.  
Based on our site reconnaissance, visible evidence of active surface slope 
instability or visible evidence of past landslide/debris flow deposits was not observed. The 
existing slopes appear generally stable and obvious visible signs of previous deep-seated 
landslide earth movement or debris flow activity were not encountered. We observed

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the soil/rock conditions encountered at the site, we believe the following 
potential slope hazards should be addressed for the proposed site grading at the western 
and eastern WTG developments. 
Cut and Fill Slope Stability 
Rockfall Hazard 
Detailed discussions of the potential slope hazards are presented in the following 
sections. 
Cut and Fill Slope Stability 
Based on the borings drilled and our reconnaissance of the areas, we anticipate 
that the planned cut slope face may expose both residual/saprolitic soils consisting of stiff 
to hard clayey soils and highly weathered basalt rock at the western development and at 
WTG-6.  The proposed cut slopes within the area of WTG-7 would likely expose stiff to 
hard alluvial deposits.  Based on our evaluation, we recommend designing the proposed 
cut slopes in these areas exposing the residual and/or saprolitic soils, highly weathered 
basalt, and alluvial deposits with a slope inclination of 1.5H:1V or flatter. 
The planned cut slopes within the area of WTG-8 and WTG-9, and the 
substation/switchyard would likely expose weathered to hard coralline sandstone and 
aeolian deposits consisting of medium dense sands.  Based on our evaluation, we 
recommend designing the proposed cut slopes in these areas exposing the coralline 
sandstone and medium dense sands with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. 
New fill slopes consisting of the compacted on-site clayey soils should be designed 
with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter.  
Slope stability analyses consisting of two-dimensional slope cross sections using 
Bishop’s simplified method of limit equilibrium analysis were performed on the proposed 
cut slope and fill slope inclinations as recommended above.  Based on the results of our 
engineering analyses, it is our opinion that the new cut and fill slopes planned for the 
project would have a factor of safety against global slope instability greater than 1.3 and 
1.5, the normal standard for short and long-term conditions, respectively.  The results of 
our slope stability analyses are provided in Appendix B. 
Rockfall Hazard 
The existing surface boulders observed at the western portion of the development 
(WTG-1 through WTG-5 and alternate sites WTG-1A, 2A, 3A, and 5A) were generally 
observed in areas where cuts to the existing slopes are planned. These surface boulders 
should be removed during the site development grading for the WTG pads and access

Basaltic “corestone” boulders potentially exposed at the cut slope face may be 
removed or trimmed to match the finish slope inclination to reduce the potential for future 
rockfall condition.  
However, the existing surface boulders that were uprooted and loosely pushed to

Finally, a portion of the existing knoll adjacent to Malaekahana Valley Road is 
proposed for some slope cutback.  The existing craggy sandstone outcroppings on the

outcrops.  Due to the shapes of the sandstone outcrops, the risk of movement of these 
outcrops is relatively low.  However, to maintain stability of the sandstone material, a 
minimum distance of about 30 feet should be maintained between the planned top of cut 
slope and the sandstone outcrops. If this distance cannot be maintained, we recommend 
completely trimming the sandstone outcroppings to match the grades of the existing slope 
face. 
As mentioned above, no overt visible signs of existing slope instability or past 
debris flow, landslide, or rockfall activity was encountered during our site reconnaissance. 
LIMITATIONS 
The findings and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from visual site observations and the borings drilled at the site.  
Variation in the surface and subsurface conditions between our observation points may 
occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until 
additional field exploration or construction is underway.  If variations then appear evident, it 
will be necessary to re-evaluate the findings and recommendations provided herein. 
It should be noted that slopes composed of rock materials deteriorate with the 
passage of time due to natural weathering processes, wet-dry and hot-cold cycles, and 
erosion conditions.  Due to the inherent deterioration of rock slopes resulting from natural 
processes (weathering, wet-dry and hot-cold cycles, and erosion conditions), the potential 
for rockfall hazard at any site changes with the passage of time.  Therefore, the findings 
and recommendations contained in this letter report may be used only within a reasonable 
time from the date of issuance of this letter report.  Land use, site conditions, and/or other 
factors may change with the passage of time.  Therefore, additional work to evaluate the 
applicability of the findings and preliminary recommendations contained in this letter report 
due to changes with the passage of time will be required.  Finally, there are no guarantees 
in the professional engineering and architectural design fields with respect to potential
