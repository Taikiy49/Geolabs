SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by stiff to very 
stiff clayey silt. These soils inherently contain high moisture, which is generally associated 
with poor workability during construction. Therefore, special site preparation and earthwork 
procedures are necessary to provide adequate compaction of these borrow materials 
during construction including compaction of the finished grades at the borrow site. 
Based on our study, we believe that the ridge from where the borrow materials will 
come from may be cut at a slope of 1.5H:1V. The cut slope should be provided with 
minimum 8-foot wide benches at predetermined vertical height intervals. Permanent fill 
slopes should be designed with a slope inclination no greater than 2H:1V.  
Detailed discussion and other geotechnical engineering design recommendations 
are presented in the following sections of this report.

Project Considerations

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island.  The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age 
and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  Waianae became extinct 
while Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau 
lavas banking against its eastern flank.  These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad 
plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii, the erosion processes are dominated by 
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls, which are then transported 
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium.  Once these 
materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley, alluvial processes become 
dominant, and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium.  In general, 
stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy, such that the stream flows transmit 
large volumes of water for very short durations.  Because of this, transport of sediments is 
intermittent, and the bulk of the stream's hydraulic load consists of a poorly sorted mixture 
of boulders, cobbles, gravel, sands, and fines.  When the erosion base levels change, 
these sediment loads are left as deposits. 
When deposits are left in-place for long periods of time, chemical processes 
begin to alter the materials, simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the 
material. Chemical processes also cause induration, or cementation, of the 
coarse-grained portion of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock, or 
conglomerate. Simultaneously, erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors 
and upstream in headwaters.  This continued erosion generates material which is 
transported downslope covering the older alluvial deposits.  Depending on the local 
base level and rate of transport, these new sediments are generally transient in terms of 
geologic time. In addition, their consistency and density are generally less than those of 
older, partially consolidated deposits.

The project site is situated at the base of the Koolau Pali, a high cliff that extends 
along most of the windward side of the Koolau Range on the Island of Oahu.  The 
climate along the Pali is typified by a high to very high average annual rainfall.  
Following the main shield building stage of the Koolau volcano in the late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene Epoch, stream erosion, landslide and deep weathering of the basalt 
rock became the dominant agents affecting the morphology of the area. 
The project site is on a large colluvial apron deposited near the base of the 
Koolau Range.  The colluvial apron formed as a result of the accumulation of soil and 
rock materials derived from large-scale erosion and landslide along the Koolau Pali. 
Subsequently, stream erosion incised these deposits to create the present day 
topography of the region as evidenced by the occurrence of multiple hill and gully 
terrain. 
The project site generally exists as a ridgeline generally composed of weathered 
and indurated colluvium. The western perimeter of the study area is generally 
surrounded by a tributary of Ahuimanu Stream that incised a drainage gully skirting 
around the base of the ridge as it flows downstream in the general direction of the 
Pacific Ocean. 
2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The borrow site is located at the eastern portion of the Valley of the Temples 
Memorial Park in Kaneohe, District of Koolaupoko, on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. It 
currently exist as an undeveloped, densely vegetated hill that lies immediately south of 
Alii View Garden. It is bordered along its southern and western perimeter by a stream 
channel that separates the site from the Garden of Patriotism.  
In general, the site comprises the lower end of a ridge trending west from the 
foothills of the Koolau Pali. The axis of this ridge slopes at a gradient of about two and 
one-half horizontal to one vertical (2.5H:1V) or approximately 40 percent within the study 
area. The ground elevations range from about +210 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) at the 
western end of the project site to about +290 feet MSL at the upper eastern limits of the 
borrow site.

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were characterized by test pit 
exploration and field observations of the exposed soil conditions within a 10 to 15-foot 
deep road cut at the northeast portion of the site. 
Two 6-foot deep test pits, designated as Test Pit. Nos. 1 and 2 were excavated 
near the western and eastern boundaries of the project site. Bulk and tube samples 
were obtained from the test pits for laboratory testing to evaluate their engineering 
properties. The approximate test pit locations relative to the current grading plan are 
reflected on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The existing near-vertical road cuts exposes reddish to yellowish brown clayey 
silt soils interbedded with highly to completely weathered cobbles and boulders within 
the soil structure.  
Similarly, our test pit exploration encountered colluvium generally consisting of 
reddish brown medium stiff to very stiff clayey silts, interbedded with highly to 
completely weathered cobbles and boulders. The materials obtained have high in-situ 
moisture contents generally ranging from about 54 to 60 percent. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the test pits at the time of our field 
exploration. It should be noted however, that the Kaneohe area tends to exhibit variable 
groundwater levels related to seepage and perched water table conditions. 
Furthermore, the water level at the project site could be influenced by variations in 
seasonal precipitation, and other factors occurring outside the project site. 
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered in our test pits are presented 
on the Logs of Test Pits, Plate A-1 of Appendix A. Results of the laboratory tests 
performed on selected soil samples are presented in Appendix B.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration indicate that the borrow site is generally underlain by 
colluvial soils, consisting of medium stiff to very stiff clayey silts, with interbeds of highly 
to completely weathered gravel, cobbles and boulders.  
In general, the main purpose of this study is to determine the post construction 
stability of the cut slopes within the borrow site in conjunction with the proposed 
excavation plans provided as shown in the Site Plan “Future Soil Site Area” (Plate 2). 
Based on this Plan, the borrow operations will generally involve excavating the bulk of 
the borrow materials from the lower end of the ridge. The uphill side of the excavation 
will be cut at a design grade of about 1.5H:1V. In addition, two intermediate benches, 
10 and 8 foot-wide, will be constructed along the cut slope at a vertical interval of 
25 feet. Excavation at the upper half of the slope will be minimal and will generally 
involve cuts of less than 5 feet from the existing surface.  
Using soil parameters obtained from our field exploration and laboratory testing, 
we performed a stability analysis of the proposed cut slope using limit equilibrium 
methods. Our analysis indicates a global minimum factor of safety (FS) against slip 
failure greater than 1.5. In general, it is our opinion that the proposed borrow site design 
configuration is feasible.  
Our laboratory testing on the in-situ clayey silt materials indicated relatively high 
moisture content, which is generally associated with poor workability. Therefore, proper 
site preparation and earthwork procedures are necessary to provide stability of the 
exposed cut ground surfaces at the project site after borrow operations are completed.  
Due to the inherent high rainfall frequency in the area, substantial aeration of the 
exposed in-situ soils prior to compaction may not be feasible during construction. 
Therefore the normal 90 percent minimum compaction requirement may not be 
practical. Based on our experience with similar high moisture tropical soils, we believe 
that a reduced compaction criterion of 85 percent relative compaction may be used in 
order to facilitate compaction of the exposed finished grades within the borrow site 
including grading fills, if necessary.

3.1.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly.  Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and 
other unsuitable materials, should be removed and disposed of properly off-site or 
stockpiled in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the 
excavated borrow materials. Due to the proximity of the borrow site to a natural 
stream channel, extreme care should be exercised during the clearing and 
grubbing to prevent deposition of debris within the stream channel. 
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be 
over-excavated (to exclude problematic soils from the borrow materials) until firm 
natural material is exposed. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be 
disposed of off-site and/or stockpiled on site for future use in landscape areas, 
where appropriate.

3.1.5 Fills and Backfills 
As discussed above, the extensive use of fill is not anticipated for this project. 
However, if general fill materials are required, the fills may consist of the excavated 
on-site soils provided that they are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, and 
rock fragments greater than 6 inches in maximum dimension. Since the project site 
is in a high rainfall area, the on-site materials have very high in-situ moisture 
contents of about 15 to 30 percent above optimum. Therefore, significant drying of 
excavated materials will be required prior to their use as fill and/or backfill material. 
3.1.6 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Due to the relatively high moisture contents in the subsoils at the site, substantial 
aeration of the high moisture soils is essential to achieve a 90 percent relative 
compaction normally required. This field method of reducing the moisture content in 
the soil may not be feasible at all times due to the high rainfall frequency 
experienced at the project site.  As a result, the compaction requirements for 
general fill placement will have to be reduced in order to aid in the earthwork 
construction using the high moisture tropical soils in this wet environment.  Based 
on our experience with similar high moisture tropical soils, we believe that a 
reduced compaction requirement of 85 percent relative compaction may be used to 
facilitate placement of fills and to reduce the potential of inducing pumping 
conditions. 
General fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 10 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction.  Fill 
should be compacted to form a dense, stable mass.  Relative compaction refers to 
the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry 
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum 
moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density.  Based on experience with similar soils, a shrinkage factor of 
about 20 percent due to the differences between the natural and compacted soil 
densities may be considered for on-site cut and fill quantities.

3.1.7 Cut and Fill Slopes 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration we believe 
that the planned cut slopes will expose stiff clayey materials. In general, the cut 
slopes planned at the project site may be designed with a slope inclination not to 
exceed 1.5H:1V. 
Based on the recent plans provided, we do not anticipate fill slope construction at 
the borrow site. However, if permanent fill slopes will be required for the project 
after the borrow operations, the permanent fill slopes should be designed with a 
slope inclination not to exceed 2H:1V using the on-site materials. Due to the 
reduced compaction requirements allowed for this project, permanent fill slopes 
constructed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the 
existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding. The filling operations 
should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers

3.2 
Slope Hazards 
Section 14-14.2(d)(2) of the Revised Ordinances of Honolulu requires a slope 
hazard evaluation for grading that involves modification to an existing slope with cuts 
greater than 15 feet in height and a slope grade steeper than 40%. The proposed 
development fall under this category, and therefore, a slope hazard evaluation was 
performed which included: 
1. 
Hazards posed by potential rock, soil and debris flow into the proposed 
development and, 
2. 
Hazards posed to adjacent existing properties or buildings by the 
proposed grading activity.  
This hazard report includes mitigating measures to minimize the identified 
hazards as well as the threat the development poses to the adjoining properties. 
3.2.1 Rockfall Hazards 
Rockfall is a type of natural mass wasting process that is in-part responsible for the 
evolution of mountain slopes. Rockfall is a common natural hazard due to the steep 
volcanic terrain comprising the mountains and valleys of the Islands of Hawaii. 
Rockfall activity involves the detachment of a rock mass from the slope face and 
the fall of rock materials under the force of gravity. Rockfall may involve a single

unstable block of rock outcrop or it may involve a rockslide which encompasses 
multiple rock components.  
Falling rock behavior can be complicated by the varying character of the ground 
surface and topography encountered along the rock fall line. Rocks may either roll, 
bounce, or have combined movement vectors. Falling rock may also hang up 
temporarily or permanently on the slope without reaching the bottom under the 
effects of terrain roughness, topography, vegetation, and the presence of natural 
features such as berms and terraces formed by other rock outcroppings. 
Based on observed general site conditions, the uphill region of the borrow site does 
not contain surface rocks or boulders that can potentially roll down into the project 
during and after construction. In addition, based on our field exploration and the 
geology of the project site, we anticipate that colluvial rocks contained in the 
planned upper portions of the borrow site excavation have completely decomposed 
as residual soils, and therefore, it is our opinion that the hazard of rock fall is not an 
issue at the proposed project site. Unless considerable deposits of fresh cobbles 
and boulders are exposed at the slopes during excavation, no rock fall hazard 
mitigation is recommended at this stage. 
3.2.2 Hazards of Debris Flow Into Project Site 
The project site generally exists as a ridgeline formed by erosion and essentially 
composed of colluvium of medium stiff to very stiff clayey silts, interbedded with 
highly to completely weathered and indurated gravel, cobbles and boulders. The 
ridge is separated by an incised drainage gully to the south and west of the project 
site and merges into the developed landscape of the existing Alii View Garden to 
the north. No rock outcrops were observed at the uphill side of the project during 
our field exploration. 
In general, the area above the project is covered with a well established tropical 
vegetation. This vegetation cover provides a natural protection against surface 
erosion.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon 
information obtained from a limited number of test pits, visual observations and 
geological interpretations at the site. Variations of subsurface soil conditions between 
and beyond the test pits may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may 
not become evident until excavation is underway.  If variations then appear evident, it 
will be necessary to reevaluate the recommendations presented in this report. 
The test pit locations indicated herein are approximate; having been established 
from identifiable features on site relative to the Plan entitled “Future Soil Site Area” 
transmitted to our office by Lyon Associates, Inc. on April 23, 2013. The locations of the

A P P E N D I X   A 
Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by excavating and 
sampling two test pits, designated as Test Pit Nos. 1 and 2, each extending to a depth 
of about 6 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate locations of the test 
pits relative to the new grading plan are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  The test pits 
were excavated using The Valley of the Temples in-house Case 580 Hoptoe equipment 
operated by their staff. 
The materials encountered in the test pits were classified by visual and textural 
examination in the field by our engineer, who monitored the excavation on a continuous 
basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in the 
laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil 
Classification System. The materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Test 
Pits, Plate A-1. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on the walls of the test pits at various 
depths. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indicative measure of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the on-site soils. Pocket penetrometer test results are shown 
on the Logs of Test Pits at the corresponding testing depths. 
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the test pits by driving a 
thin-walled 2.5-inch outside diameter by 6-inch long brass tubing into the exposed 
surface of the excavation at selected depths. Upon sampling, the brass tubes were 
sealed with air tight plastic caps at both ends, labeled, and transported to our laboratory 
for testing.
