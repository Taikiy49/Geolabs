PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
Disney's Grand Hawaiian Resort & Spa project site is within the existing Ko Olina
Resort development in the District of Ewa on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii (as shown on the
General Site Plan, Plate 2). The project site encompasses two parcels of land (Lot Nos. 13
and 15) fronting Lagoon No. 1 of the Ko Olina Resort. The project site is also adjacent to
the existing Marriott Ihilani Resort Hotel (to the northwest). The two parcels are to the
southwest of Ali inui Drive, which is the main entry road into the resort.
We understand the plans for the project call for developing several tower structures
of up to about 10 to 15 stories high. We also understand that two to four-level parking
structures (with one to two level basements), restaurant, swimming pools, and other
support facilities will be required.
Based on the topographic survey map provided, the existing ground elevations at
the project site range from about +17 to +30 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). We envision
the majority of site grading will consist of cuts and fills of less than about 5 feet in height
for the project development. Excavations on the order of about 15 to 20 feet deep will
be required for construction of the basement parking levels. In addition, areas near the
porte cochere will require substantial fills of up to about 20 feet in height to achieve the
design grades.

SURFACE AND SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Based on our site reconnaissance, the project site generally is graded to a
near-level condition (previous mass grading). Portions of the project site are grassed with
an irrigation network. Based on the available maps and our observations, the ground
surface elevations at the project site range from about +17 to +30 feet MSL. The makai
(seaward) portion (west side) of the project site slopes down from east to west toward the
shoreline at a slope inclination of about two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V). The
existing ground elevations near Lagoon No. 1 are at about +10 feet MSL.
Based on our research and review of available in-house information, fills of over
20 feet thick may have been placed at the project site during the initial development of
Ko Olina Resort. The fills placed at the project site appear to decrease in thickness when
progressing toward the mauka portion (east side) of the project site. In addition, some
areas of the project site toward the mauka side were mass graded to a cut condition.
Our field exploration program at the project site consisted of drilling and sampling
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to about 80 feet below the
ground surface. In addition, twenty additional borings were drilled for design of
foundations for the various building structures (not included in this letter report). The
locations of the two borings drilled for the project are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 3.
The borings drilled generally encountered surface fills overlying coral formation,
coralline detritus, alluvium, and basalt formation extending down to the maximum depth
explored. The surface fills generally consisted of very stiff to hard silty clays and medium
dense to very dense silty sands and gravel. As mentioned previously, the thickness of the
surface fills generally increased when progressing from mauka to makai (from east to
west) of the site, ranging from less than 1 foot to about 23 feet. In general, the thickness
of the silty clay fills was limited to less than about 3 feet. It should be noted that cobbles
and boulders also were encountered within the sandy and gravelly fill layer in some of the
borings.
The fill materials generally were underlain by coral formation in most of the borings.
The coral formation encountered generally was highly weathered and varied from soft to
hard. It should be noted that the depth, thickness, and consistency of the coral formation
was highly variable across the project site. The coral formation was encountered as
shallow as about 1 foot below the ground surface and as deep as about 23 feet below the
ground surface. The coral formation generally extended down to about 16 to 46 feet
below the ground surface.
Coralline detritus generally was encountered below the coral formation in the
borings. The coralline detritus encountered generally consisted of medium dense silty
sands and gravel and extended down to about 26 to 60 feet below the ground surface. In
some instances, some loose beach sands and coralline detritus were encountered in the

The coral formation and coralline detritus materials generally were underlain by

Site Preparation
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, existing
structures to be demolished, and other unsuitable materials, should be removed and
disposed of properly off-site to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated
materials.
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas
designated to receive fill and/or future improvements should be over-excavated to expose
firm natural material, and the resulting excavation should be _ backfilled with
well-compacted fill. The excavated soft soils should not be re-used as fill materials and
should be disposed of properly off-site.
In general, subgrades in cut areas and areas designated to receive fills should be
scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content, and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of
the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water
content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.
Where hard coral formation is exposed, the subgrades in cut areas and areas
designated to receive fills should be proof-rolled with a large vibratory drum roller
(minimum 10 tons static weight) for a minimum of eight passes to help detect and collapse
near-surface cavities and/or voids. The vibratory drum roller should be operated at a
speed of about 300 feet per minute.
Yielding areas, loose areas, or cavities disclosed during the clearing and
proof-rolling operations should be over-excavated and backfilled with compacted fill
materials. The depth of over-excavation should be extended until dense underlying
materials are exposed and should be evaluated in the field by a Geolabs representative.
Fills and Backfills
In general, the coralline granular materials encountered during our field exploration
should be suitable for use as general fill materials, provided that the maximum particle size
is less than 3 inches in largest dimension. The on-site granular materials generated from
the excavations may be used as a source of general fill or backfill materials, provided that
they are screened of the over-sized materials and/or processed to meet the above
gradation requirements (less than 3 inches in largest dimension). The silty clay surface fill
materials encountered in the borings generally exhibit a slight to moderate expansion
potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations. Therefore, on-site materials consisting

of silty clay soils only should be re-used as a source of fill materials in landscape and other
non-structural areas.
Imported materials to be used as select granular fill should be non-expansive
granular materials, such as crushed coral, basalt, or cinder sands. The select granular fill
should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest
dimension. The material should have a California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or
higher, and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1883. The material also should contain between 10 and 30 percent
particles passing the No. 200 sieve. Imported fill materials should be tested for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the
intended use.
It is not anticipated that the proposed excavations for the project will encounter
groundwater except in localized areas, such as the drilled shaft caps and elevator pits.
Where groundwater is encountered within the excavations, backfill materials should
consist of free-draining granular materials, such as ASTMC 33, No. 67 gradation,
wrapped on all sides with non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent). The
free-draining granular materials should be used up to a level about 12 inches above the
groundwater level to facilitate compaction of the fill materials above the free-draining
granular materials.
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
In general, fills and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The compaction requirement for the
final subgrades of improvements subjected to vehicular traffic, such as the pavements and
parking structure ground floor level, should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D 1557.
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the
maximum dry density. Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers,
vibratory rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping,
jetting, or ponding should not be allowed to compact the fills.
Excavation
Based on our field exploration, the project site generally is underlain by fill materials
at the ground surface. Medium hard to hard coral formation is present near the ground
surface in some areas. It is anticipated that the near-surface fill materials may be
excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment, such as excavating with a backhoe
excavator. However, it should be noted that some hard coral formation was encountered
in the borings at shallow depths. Therefore, it should be noted that the contractor likely will

Cut and Fill Slopes
We envision that cut slopes at the project site generally will expose the surface fills
and/or medium hard to hard coral formation encountered in our borings. In general, cut
slopes and permanent fill slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal
to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be
keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding.
Construction of earth berms, interceptor ditches, and the use of geotextile fabrics
over the fill slope face should be considered to reduce the potential for significant erosion,
thus enhancing the long-term stability of the fill slopes. Appropriate slope planting or other
erosion control measures to reduce the potential for significant erosion of the exposed
slopes (including a permanent irrigation system) should be implemented as soon as
possible after the finished slope faces are completed.
LIMITATIONS
The analyses and earthwork recommendations submitted in this letter report are
based in part upon information obtained from our site reconnaissance and field exploration
and from our past experience in the vicinity of the project site. Variations of the subsurface
conditions may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become
evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be
necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations presented in this letter report.

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, at the project site. In
addition, twenty additional borings (not included in this letter report) were drilled for
design of the tower structure foundations. The approximate locations of the two borings
drilled for the project are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 3. The borings were drilled
using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools.
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by a geologist, who monitored the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic representations of the materials
encountered in the due diligence study are provided on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1
and A-2.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving
a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches.
Some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample
depths.
Pocket penetrometer and torvane shear tests were performed on selected
cohesive soil samples retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an
indication of the unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample. The torvane shear
test provides a quick estimate of the undrained shear strength of the soil sample.
Pocket penetrometer and the torvane shear test results are presented on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
Core samples of the coral formations encountered at the site were obtained
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113, Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the coral formation. The material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description.

Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities, discounting drilling
induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5.0-foot
core run, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock,
based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals."
