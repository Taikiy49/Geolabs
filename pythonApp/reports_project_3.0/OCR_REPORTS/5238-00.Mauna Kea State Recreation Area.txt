SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration, the subsurface conditions at the project site
generally consist of boulder fill materials starting at the ground surface extending down to
about 7 to 16 feet below the existing ground surface. The fill materials encountered
generally consisted of cobbles and boulders with some gravel and sands. Dense
pahoehoe basalt formation and clinker materials generally were encountered below the fill
materials extending down to the maximum depths explored of about 20.5 feet below the
ground surface. Groundwater was not encountered in the borings drilled during our field
exploration.
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we envision
that a shallow foundation system, such as a concrete slab-on-grade with a thickened-edge
perimeter footing, may be used to support the new reservoir tank planned at the project
site. To provide uniform bearing conditions for the interior concrete floor of the reservoir
tank, we recommend that a 12-inch thick layer of compacted aggregate subbase (3-inch
minus granular materials) be provided below the concrete floor of the reservoir. An
allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,500 psf may be used for design of shallow
foundations bearing on the aggregate subbase.
Due to the presence of undocumented boulder fills at the project site, we
recommend that the undocumented fills below the reservoir footprint be over-excavated by
a minimum depth of 36 inches below the aggregate subbase layer. The over-excavation
below the reservoir footprint should extend at least 3 feet laterally beyond the edge of the
reservoir limits. The exposed subgrade after the over-excavation should be proof-rolled
with a minimum 15-ton vibratory drum roller a minimum of eight passes. After the
proof-rolling operations, the over-excavation should be backfilled with compacted
structural fill materials (granular fills with a maximum particle size of 6 inches).
Due to the potential for presence of voids in the boulder fills and cavities in the
basaltic lava flows, we recommend that a program of probing and grouting of the
foundation locations of the reservoir tank structure be implemented during construction.
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific
recommendations for design of the new reservoir tank project.

2.1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii, the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago, covers an area of
approximately 4,030 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of five shield
voicanoes including the following: Kohala, which is long extinct; Mauna Kea, which has
had activity during recent geologic times; Hualalai, which last erupted in the Year 1801;
and Mauna Loa and Kilauea, both of which are still active.
The project site is located near the Pohakuloa Training Area (PTA). PTA is
located on the western side of the Humuula Saddle, which forms the central upland
portion of the Island of Hawaii, located between the Mauna Kea and Mauna Loa
Volcanoes. The Humuula Saddle is a broad relatively flat to gently sloping plateau
situated between the Mauna Kea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes. The regional terrain rises
from the east (Hilo side) and from the west (Waikoloa side) to the crest at the Humuula
Saddle, which is located just to the west of the intersection of the existing Saddle Road
and the Mauna Kea Observatory access road. The Humuula Saddle formed as a result
of the coalescence of lava flows erupted from the Mauna Kea and Mauna Loa
Volcanoes.
In general, the project site is at the foot of the steeper south and southwest
facing slopes comprising the upper mass of Mauna Kea Volcano. The south and
southwest facing slopes of Mauna Kea rise abruptly from the surrounding flatter terrain
of the Humuula Saddle. Several prominent cinder cone vents composed of bedded
tephra deposits are located within and adjacent to the project site. The circular-shape
cinder cones rise above the surrounding terrain and represent the volcanic vents from
which the younger age volcanic ash and cinder (pyroclastic tephra) deposits were
emitted. The cinder cone venis are typically oriented along the axes of the rift zones
composing Mauna Kea Volcano.
Most of the geologic materials at the project site are derived from the late-stage
eruption and erosion of the alkalic a’a lavas and cinder materials emitted from

Mauna Kea Volcano. The cinder materials were observed to range in size from fine

The a’a lava typically consists of a rubbly and irregular clinker surface consisting
of loosely indurated basalt rock fragments with layer thickness on the order of about 3 to

“plue rock” may be encountered. The dense rock contains the core of the a’a lava flow,
which may range to several tens of feet in thickness.
Most of the project site has accumulated a ground surface veneer of geologic
materials that represent both pyroclastic air-fall deposits, such as volcanic ash and
cinder sand emitted from the late-stage eruption of cinder cones, and alluvial materials
derived from glaciation and erosion of the upper slopes of Mauna Kea Volcano. The
surface veneer of pyroclastic and sedimentary materials generally overlies hard basalt
rock. The mantling effect of the surface deposits has created a subdued surface
topographic character, which masks the usually observed irregular and undulating
topography of a’a lava flows. As a result, the depth from the ground surface to the top
of the buried hard basalt rock surfaces varies considerably within relatively short
distances.

. 2.4 Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by
shifts in the tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily
to volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or
during volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The Island of
Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most of the earthquakes
are so small that only sensitive instruments can detect the minor earthquakes.
However, some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor
to moderate damage.
In general, earthquakes (associated with volcanic activity) are most common on
the Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the
Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over 90 percent of
earthquakes) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree of
ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the southern portion of the
Island of Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater
than Magnitude 6 (M6+). Based on information obtained from the United Siates
Geological Survey (USGS) Bulletin 2006, the following is a list of destructive

3.2. Foundation Probing
Based on our exploration and experience in the area, voids may be present in
the boulder fills encountered at the project site. In addition, cavities are commonly
encountered in the basaltic lava flows below the fills at the project site. To reduce the
potential for loss of foundation support resulting from migration of fine materials into the
voids in the fill materials below the foundations, we recommend that a program of
probing and grouting of the reservoir tank structure foundation be implemented during
construction. In addition, probing and grouting of the reservoir tank foundations also
would reduce the potential for collapse of near-surface cavities in the basalt formations
underlying the boulder fills at the project site. Foundation probing and grouting may be
omitted for the other minor structures planned at the site.
For estimating purposes, we recommend that probe holes be drilled at each
isolated spread footing (thickened slab footing) location and at 10-foot on centers for the
perimeter thickened-edge footing of the reservoir. Probe holes also should be drilled

The exposed ground below the over-excavation should be proof-rolled with a minimum
15-ton (static weight) vibratory drum roller for a minimum of eight passes to assist in
detecting and possibly collapsing near-surface cavities and/or voids. The vibratory
drum roller used for the proof-rolling operations should be operated at a speed of about
300 feet per minute.
The proof-rolling operations should be performed in the presence of a
representative from Geolabs. Yielding areas, loose areas, or cavities disclosed during
the clearing and proof-rolling operations should be over-excavated and backfilled with
compacted fill materials. Therefore, contract documents should include additive and
deductive unit prices for over-excavation of yielding and loose areas and backfill with
compacted fill (structural fill materials) to account for variations in the over-excavation
and backfill quantities.
In general, we recommend that structural fill materials be used to fill the project
site from the existing ground surface to achieve the design subgrades or finished
grades due to the relatively minor fills (less than 2 feet thick) required for the project.
Structural fill materials should consist of well-graded granular materials of 6 inches in
largest dimension with sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the
compacted mass. The excavated on-site materials may be re-used as a source of
structural fill materials, provided that the materials are processed to meet the maximum
particle size and gradation requirements.
If the excavated on-site materials do not contain sufficient fines to produce a
well-graded material, off-site borrow or on-site rock crushing of large-sized rock

fragments or boulders should be considered to provide the required gradation and
particle sizes to develop a well-graded material. Imported materials, if necessary,
should be observed and/or tested by Geolabs for suitability for the intended use prior to
being transported to the site.
Aggregate subbase and structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not
exceeding 12 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content, and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction. Relative
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557. Optimum moisture content is the water content (percentage by
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. Compaction should be
accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or other types of acceptable
compaction equipment.
Conventional compaction testing generally is not practical in fills composed of
large rock fragments and/or cobbles. Instead, a testing program to evaluate the number
of passes by a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be
conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs. Based on this testing program, the number of passes
may then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction.
For preliminary estimating purposes, compaction may be accomplished by using
a large vibratory drum roller having a static weight of at least 15 tons traveling at a
speed of about 300 feet per minute. The roller should pass over the fill surface of each
lift at least eight times for aggregate subbase and structural fill.

In general, we believe that the cut slopes may be designed with a slope

3.5 Pipelines
) We envision that a water pipeline will be installed at the project site. In general,

Free-draining granular materials, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33,
No. 67 gradation), should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches
above the pipes. It is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the
sides of the pipes. Improper backfill material around the pipes and improper placement
of the backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes.
As an alternative to the open-graded gravel, the granular bedding and the initial
trench backfill (up to about 12 inches above the top of the pipes) may consist of 1-inch
minus, well-graded granular materials (aggregate base course). Where used, the
1-inch minus, well-graded granular bedding and the initial trench backfill should be
moisture-conditioned and compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction.
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of well-graded granular
materials less than G6Ginches in maximum. size. The backfill should be
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in about 12-inch loose lifts,
and mechanically compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction.

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling three borings designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3. The borings were
advanced to maximum depths of about 20.5 feet below the existing ground surface
using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with coring tools. The approximate locations of
the borings drilled for the project are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
The materials encountered in the borings drilled were classified by visual and
textural examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operations
on a near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the
Unified Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer
falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586, Penetration Test and
Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
Core samples of the harder materials and/or rock formations encountered at the
project site were obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance
with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113, Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation.
Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock
formation. The material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for
examination and description.
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.

The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities, discounting drilling
induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5.0-foot
core run, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock,
based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals."

relative hardness of the rock, its relative quality, brittleness, and fissile characteristics.
Dense basalt formations with high RQD values would be very difficult to rip
(or excavate) and would probably require more arduous methods or longer duration of
excavation.
