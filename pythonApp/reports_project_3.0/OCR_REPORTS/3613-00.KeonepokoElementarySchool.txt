INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Cafetorium Multi Purpose Building at Keonepoko Elementary
School in Pahoa on the island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project
site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for the proposed
project These recommendations are intended for design input only and are subject to
the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located within the existing Keonepoko Elementary School in the
Pahoa area on the island of Hawaii It is proposed to construct a new cafetorium multi
purpose building as part of the development plan of the Keonepoko Elementary School
Based on the information provided the new cafetorium multi purpose building will
be located east of the existing playfield and north of the existing parking lot The new
building will be a one story concrete structure covering approximately 10 000 square feet
in area A small custodial service building located to the north of the proposed cafetorium
is also planned

Based on the existing topography and the proposed finished floor elevation of
365 30 feet Mean Sea Level MSL for the cafetorium minor cuts and fills on the order
of about 3 feet are anticipated for the project grading work Based on the information
provided us maximum column loads are estimated to be on the order of about 55 kips
per column with wall loads of approximately 1 5 kips per lineal foot Our previous
experience in the area suggest that the project site may be underlain by a surficial layer of
fill soils over basalt formation at shallow depths Special attention was given to the
potential presence of near surface voids cavities in the basalt rock formation which may
collapse under structural loads

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These include the following Kohala which has been long extinct Mauna
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3613 00 Page 5
Kea which has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in
1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is located on the eastern flank of the Mauna Loa Shield Volcano
which has been built up by basaltic lava flows These flows are of Pleistocene and
Recent Age and belong to the Ka u volcanic series The lava flows were subsequently
covered by volcanic ash deposits
The lava formation encountered at the site appears to be of pahoehoe flow which
is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of
vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lavas These
cavities were formed when the lava was still in a molten state and represent both lava
tubes intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava tubes
are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like
structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow
cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling four borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 to a depth of approximately 20 feet below the
existing ground surface The approximate locations of the drilled borings are shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our field exploration encountered a surface layer of topsoil over dense
sandy basalt gravels and cobbles extending to depths of about 1 to 3 5 feet below the
existing ground surface The surficial gravel layer was underlain by hard basalt rock
formation extending to the maximum depth drilled of approximately 20 feet below the
existing ground surface
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field
exploration However groundwater levels can fluctuate depending on factors such as
seasonal rainfall groundwater withdrawal and or injection and other factors
For detailed description of the materials encountered please refer to the Logs of
Borings presented on Plates A 1 through A 4 of Appendix A
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered a very thin surficial layer of topsoil and
basalt gravels overlying hard basalt rock formation at shallow depths Based on the
relatively competent subsoil conditions encountered in our borings and the anticipated
structural loads it is our opinion that shallow spread and or continuous footings may be
used for support of the proposed cafetorium and custodial service building Based on the
GEOLABS HAWAII

existing topography and proposed finished floor elevations for the project we anticipate
that the new cafetorium will straddle across cut and fill areas In order to provide more
uniform support for the foundation and to reduce the potential for foundation distress
resulting from differing subgrade conditions we recommend that the footings be extended
through the surficial topsoil or fill materials and bear on the underlying basalt rock
formation for support of the proposed cafetorium For more uniform support of slabs on
grade we recommend that concrete slabs be supported on at least 12 inches of granular
fill material
Due to the potential presence of cavities as commonly encountered in basaltic
lava flows we recommend that the slab subgrades be proof rolled with a minimum 20 ton

surface cavities In addition a program of probing and grouting the footing locations
should be implemented during the project construction A representative of
Geolabs Hawaii should observe the probing and grouting operation to evaluate the extent
of cavities if found Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering
design recommendations are presented in the following sections of this report
Building Foundations
Based on the competent subsoil conditions encountered and the structural loads
anticipated for the subject project it is our opinion that shallow foundations consisting of
spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the proposed buildings
Based on the existing topography and the proposed finish floor elevation we anticipate
that the cafetorium will straddle across cut and fill areas In order to provide more uniform
support for the foundation and to reduce the potential for foundation distress resulting
from differing subgrade conditions we recommend that the footings be extended through
the surficial topsoil or fill materials and bear on the underlying basalt rock formation for
support of the proposed building

An allowable bearing pressure of up to 4 000 pounds per square foot p s f may
be used for the design of footing foundations bearing on the basalt rock formation
encountered at shallow depths This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces
The footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade Depending on the depths of the basalt rock formation deeper
excavations may be required at localized areas to provide a competent bearing surface
for the footing foundations If foundations are located next to utility trenches or
easements they should be embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward
from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
If the footings are designed in accordance with the recommendations presented
herein total settlement of footings is estimated to be on the order of inch with
differential settlements on the order of inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of
footings A coefficient of friction of 0 45 may be used for footings bearing on the
underlying basalt rock formation Resistance to the lateral loads due to passive pressure
may be calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per
foot of depth p cf These values assume that the concrete for footings is cast neat
against the basalt rock Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in
the upper 12 inches below finished grade should be neglected

Foundation Probing
Based on our previous experience in the area voids and or cavities may be
present in the basalt rock formation encountered at the project site To reduce the
potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of the cavities below a
footing we recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting be implemented
during construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that probes be drilled at each spread
footing location and 10 feet on centers for continuous footings The probe holes should
be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend at least 10 feet below the planned
bottom of footing If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during the
probing operation additional probes should be drilled at closer spacings to help delineate
the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes and any cavities
discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected at low to moderate
pressures A typical sand cement grout would consist of 1 part sand 1 part cement and
0 7 part water
Because of the potential for encountering large cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during
bidding In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and
grouting operation is complete The contractor should also be made aware that a longer
lag time between probing grouting operations and foundation construction may likely be
required in the construction schedule

The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs Hawaii This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of
cavities and to allow additional recommendations to be made if excess grout take and or
changed conditions are observed
Slabs On Grade
As previously indicated the proposed cafetorium will straddle cut and fill
transitions To reduce the potential for differential settlements at the cut and fill transition
due to differing subgrade materials we recommend that concrete slabs be supported on
at least 12 inches of granular fill material The intent of the 12 inch granular fill layer is to
reduce the potential for slab cracking due to differential settlements at the cut and fill
transition Prior to the placement of the 12 inch granular fill the subgrade soils should be
prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented in the Site Preparation
section of this report
In addition to the 12 inches of granular fill a basaltic termite barrier BTB should
be incorporated into the slab on grade design of the proposed buildings in accordance
with the requirements of D A G S Technical Memorandum No 61 dated
Therefore for the interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend
that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of BTB be placed below the
slabs to serve as a termite barrier and to provide more uniform support of the slabs To
reduce future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an
impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is recommended on top
of the BTB cushion layer Two inches of BTB should be placed above the plastic
membrane to provide protection for the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab
concrete
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3613 00 Page 11
Where the slab will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as forklifts a 6 inch layer
of base course is recommended in lieu of the 4 inch BTB cushion layer The 2 inch BTB
and vapor barrier may be omitted for these slabs The base course should consist of
crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
For the design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of about 250 pounds
per square inch per inch of deflection p c i may be used for the compacted base
course A minimum slab thickness of 6 inches may be used for preliminary design
purposes Provisions should be made for proper load transfer across the slab joints
which are subjected to vehicular traffic Thickened edges of slabs adjacent to any
unpaved area should be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project We
recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer of BTB be provided below the
concrete walkway slab Construction joints should be provided at intervals equal to the
width of the walkways with expansion joints at right angle intersections
Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and the anticipated finished floor elevation of the
proposed buildings we anticipate that minor site grading work will be required for the
project construction At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits
should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site The surficial
topsoil should be removed and may be stockpiled for use in landscaped areas if
appropriate Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be
over excavated to expose firm material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled
with well compacted engineered fill
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3613 00 Page 12
Slabs and foundations of the existing structures that are to be demolished should
be completely removed Any over excavation resulting from the demolition should be
backfilled with compacted granular fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned should
be removed and the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with select
granular fill material placed in 8 inch loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction Utilities that are to be abandoned in place under the
proposed structure should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete under low pressure
After clearing and grubbing the areas within the proposed building limits should be
over excavated where necessary to provide the minimum 12 inch thick capping layer of
granular fill material Due to the potential presence of cavities as commonly encountered
in basaltic lava flows we recommend that the over excavated subgrades be proof rolled
with a minimum 20 ton vibratory drum roller for a minimum of 6 passes prior to fill
placement to assist in detecting and collapsing near surface cavities Any loose areas or
cavities disclosed during clearing and proof rolling operations should be excavated to
expose firm materials and backfilled with compacted select fill Therefore contract
documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and
engineered fill placement to account for variations in the over excavation and backfill
quantities The proof rolling operations should be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii to evaluate the presence of voids and or cavities below the subgrade
level
The on site excavated basalt rock materials may be used as fill or backfill materials
provided they are processed and reduced to a relatively well graded select granular
material with a maximum size of about 6 inches The 12 inch granular capping fill
recommended under the building slabs should consist of imported materials such as
select borrow crushed coral or basalt gravel The material should be well graded from

coarse to fine with no particles greater than 3 inches in largest dimension The material
should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 20 or higher and a
swell potential of not more than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1883 It should contain between 10 and 30 percent passing the
No 200 sieve Any imported material should be observed and or tested by

However excavations into the deeper formations may require the use of hoerams
chipping or blasting

Utility Lines
We envision that new utility lines i e water and sewer lines will be required for
development of this project It is anticipated that most of the trenches for utilities will be
excavated in hard basalt rock A granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended below the pipes Free draining
granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be
used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate
support around the pipes and to reduce compaction of the backfill thus reducing the
possibility of damaging the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill should consist of granular material
generally less than 6 inches in maximum size compacted to at least 90 percent relative
compaction Where trenches will be located in paved areas the upper 2 feet of the
trench backfill below the road subgrade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent
relative compaction Where the utility line crosses beneath the footings the utility pipes
should be concrete jacketed
Since basaltic rock formation was encountered at shallow depths during our field
exploration it is our opinion that trench work for utility installation may involve excavation
in the basaltic rock formation and will require hard ripping or hoerams Care must be
exercised by the contractor to avoid over ripping which would disrupt the structure of the
rock formation resulting in a potential loss of bearing strength for footings in its vicinity

Drainage
The finished grades outside the structures should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Also it is advised that gutter
systems be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs
should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete
bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab
footings immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce water infiltration Drainage
swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain all
surface run off away from the slabs and footing foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings to a depth of approximately 20 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using
truck mounted drilling equipment
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of
the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
Very Poor 0 25
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100
