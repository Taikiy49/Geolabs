INTRODUCTION
The purpose of this study is to survey the subsurface condi
tions of the Waimanalo Area of Windward Oahu see Project
Location Map Plate 1 with respect to the disposal of sewage
effluent by means of deep injection wells The limits of the
area of study are delineated on the attached Geologic Map
Plate 2
Previous investigators have made Studies of the feasibility of
injection of effluent in the Waimanalo area and on the basis of
some of the earlier studies a Sewage treatment plant with
three 15 inch diameter injection wells was constructed in the
late 1960 s
This report will examine the existing available reports and
data regarding these injection wells In addition the
geologic conditions within the area of the study will be
Surveyed to develop alternate locations and other areas in
Waimanalo for new disposal wells
GEOLABS HAWAII
GEOLOGIC SETTING
Considerable detail has been written by previous authors
Tegarding the geologic history of the Island of Oahu and in
particular the stratigraphy of the Waimanalo area This
report will only deal briefly with the regional geologic
history of southeastern Oahu
During the Tertiary period successive eruptions of basaltic
lavas built up the Koolau Volcano which forms the base of
eastern Oahu The main shield building phase came to an end at
the beginning of Pleistocene epoch
Following the end of the main eruptive phase a long period of
erosion began which dissected deep valleys into the eastern
flank of the Koolau Volcano
In the area which extends from the north side of Kaneohe Bay to
Waimanalo was the central caldera of the Koolau Volcano
Chemical action by rising volcanic steam and gases altered the
caldera filling basalts into an easily eroded material which
further contributed to the deep dissection of the southeastern
portion of the volcano Macdonald and Abbott 1970
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1095 00 Page 3
There is evidence that the island of Oahu has subsided as much
as 1800 feet below the present sea level after the initial
period of erosion Stearns 1946 and this subsidence has
resulted in the filling of the original valleys with inter
bedded terrigenous and marine sediments
During the Quaternary period i e the Pleistocene epoch a
complex series of glacioeustatic sea level changes resulted in
the deposition of a sequence of interbedded coralline lime
stone beach conglomerates and eolian sandstones Lum and
Stearns 1970 Locally these deposits are referred to as
coral and are used as aggregate for construction These
calcareous formations are generally very permeable due to their
porous structure
A Holocene sequence of denudation and alluviation has partially
buried these calcareous formations under a mantle of terri
genous alluvium composed primarily of clays and silts Over
lying these terrigenous sediments in the lower seaward areas
are deposits of recent dune and beach sand This sequence of
erosion in the upland areas and deposition in the low areas has
brought the terrain to its present configuration

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
In a general sense the Waimanalo area can be separated into
two areas the inland portion and the seaward portion
Generally speaking the demarcation between these areas is the
30 foot elevation contour
Inland Portion
In the inland portion the subsurface materials consist
predominately of alluvial clays and silts with some gravel
interbeds This layer is about 150 feet or more in thickness
and is underlain by basaltic bedrock The alluvial materials
generally have very low permeability and are not capable of
transmitting significant volumes of water
Seaward Portion 7
Subsurface conditions in the Seaward portion are more complex
At or near the surface there is a layer of sands and poorly
consolidated sandstones which extends to depths of approxi
mately 20 to 30 feet below the surface and may be thinly
mantled with recent alluvial clays in localized areas This
stratum represents a sequence of recent dune and beach deposits
and is generally very permeable and capable of transmitting
large volumes of water
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1095 00 Page 5
Underlying this sand stratum is a layer of alluvium and other
impermeable sediments which extend from 20 to 50 feet to depths
varying between 35 feet to 110 feet and represents alluvial and
lagoonal deposits laid down during a lower stand of the sea
This sequence is generally impermeable and is not capable of
transmitting water
Following this impermeable layer is a thick stratum of calca
reous sediments consisting of dune limestones from a lower
stand of the sea over coral and associated limestones from a
higher stand of the sea Lum and Stearns 1970 This layer is
quite porous in structure and therefore is readily permeable
and capable of transmitting large amounts of water This is
the permeable zone which has been used as the injection
interval for the existing disposal wells 7
Below this permeable layer of calcareous sediments is another
sequence of alluvial and other impermeable sediments deposited
during very low stands of the sea possibly during the subsi
dence of the island of Oahu This Sequence is relatively
impermeable and incapable of transmitting water and serves as a
basal confining layer to the overlying calcareous sediments
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1095 00 Page 6
There is no record of any well penetrating this basal confining
Sequence to the underlying basaltic bedrock and it can only be
assumed that the basement rock lies at great depth exceeding
1000 feet below the Waimanalo area
Also encountered within the area of study were small portions
of the post erosional Honolulu Volcanic Series Most of this
unit is tuff and cinder on two islands situated off Makapuu
Beach however approximately fifty feet of basaltic lava flows
overlay calcareous sediments at Sea Life Park These lava
flows are of moderate to low permeability and must be pene
trated to reach the underlying highly permeable limestones
PREVIOUS INVESTIGATIONS
7
A review has been made of available data concerning previous
investigations and logs of existing and abandoned wells
Our study indicates that the seaward portion of the Waimanalo
area is underlain by permeable calcareous formations at shallow
depths from Wailea Point to Makapuu Point The locations of
those investigations and wells are also shown on the Geologic
Map Plate 2 along with the approximate boundary of the under
lying permeable formations The generalized logs are presented
in the Appendix

A detailed study of the permeable formations was made by the
State of Hawaii Department of Land and Natural Resources
Division of Water and Land Development between 1965 and 1968
The findings of this study are summarized in Preliminary
Report on GEOHYDROLOGIC EXPLORATION FOR DEEP WELL DISPOSAL OF
EFFLUENT Waimanalo Sewage Treatment Plant Oahu by Daniel Lun
In this study injection testing and core drilling was per
formed to examine the feasibility of deep well effluent dispo
Sal as opposed to an offshore outfall
The initial injection test performed in an unused well 2042 01
old number 408 6 approximately 500 feet north of the Waima
nalo Sewage Treatment Plant indicated that the underlying
porous formations would accept of clean fresh water
Following this initial injection test a series of eight 8 NX
size core holes were drilled during 1966 in the Waimanalo Beach
area to investigate the subsurface conditions with respect to
deep well disposal of effluent These test borings generally
encountered about 20 feet of recent sands and alluvium under
lain by 3 to 87 feet of impermeable lagoonal littoral and
terrigenous sediments Below these sediments the porous for
mations extend to a depth of approximately 270 feet and consist
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1095 00 Page 8
of interbedded dune littoral and reef deposits underlain by
impermeable marine and alluvial sediments Lum and Stearns
1970
It was found in the course of this study that the lower porous
limestone formations are saturated with saline water of similar
chloride content and temperature to that of ocean water The
water level in the drilled holes varied with the tidal fluctua
tions of the nearby open ocean The tidal efficiency was not
specified however examination of U S Geologic Survey well
files show that wells in this area have an average tidal effi
ciency of approximately 70 percent This information would
lead to the conjecture that the porous limestone formation is
hydraulically connected to the open ocean When one considers
that the porous formation is confined between two relatively
impermeable clay layers yet is still hydraulically connnected
to the ocean its use as a disposal zone of treated effluent
becomes attractive
On the basis of the findings of this study three 15 inch dia
meter injection wells were drilled and tested at the present
sewage treatment plant The initial testing of these wells
showed a minimum injection capacity of for the three
wells with an injection head of 2 to 5 feet using clean fresh
water Lum 1969
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1095 00 Page 9
Following the construction of these wells they were put into
service for disposal of treated effluent from the Waimanalo
Sewage Treatment Plant
Review of other data sources leads to the conjecture that with
a few exceptions the permeable formations would not be encoun
tered at a reasonable depth for either cesspools or injection
wells at a site above the 30 foot elevation contour The
exceptions to this are the area locally known as Coral Hill
where lithified dune sand is exposed to elevation 162 and the
area of Sea Life Park at Makapuu where the disposal wells
penetrate approximately 50 feet of Honolulu Volcanic Series
basalts prior to encountering the limestone formation
DISCUSSIONS AND CONCLUSIONS 7
General
It has been shown that there are calcareous formations which
are sufficiently permeable for deep well disposal of treated
effluent underlying the seaward portion of the Waimanalo area
It is our opinion that there may be near surficial sands in the
central and southern Waimanalo area which may be sufficiently
permeable for effluent disposal through individual cesspools
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1095 00 Page 10
Review of the available subsurface data indicates the feasibi
lity of using either disposal method is limited to the areas
below 30 feet in elevation with the exceptions previously noted
in this report
Future Disposal
Continued development in the Waimanalo area will increase the
need of effluent disposal either through cesspools or deep
wells As previously stated in this report the most feasible
areas for either method of disposal are those areas below
Elevation 30 feet At higher elevations permeable strata for
disposal may not be encountered or may be too deep to be
economically reached
In the lowlying areas over the seaward portion of the study
area there are near surface sands which may be sufficiently
permeable for disposal through the use of cesspools There
are however man made boundaries one existing and one
proposed that may limit the areas of underground disposal
The existing Board of Water Supply No Pass line boundary is a
geohydrologic boundary with Slight modifications along secular
boundaries The Underground Injection Control UIC boundary
is currently being proposed by the State of Hawaii Department
of Health and is similarly developed but with more stringent
specifications
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1095 00 Page 11
Both boundaries are intended to be the dividing line between
allowable and disallowable areas for underground disposal
Underground disposal will normally be allowed only on the sea
wardside of the boundaries The two boundaries through the
area of study are shown on the Geologic Map Plate 2
As can be seen from the illustration the proposed UIC line if
enacted will limit the area available for underground disposal
in the area of study Substantial amounts of the areas avai
lable for future development are above either the boundary or
the 30 foot contour which will limit the use of individual
cesspools
Disposal by Cesspool
It is anticipated that the alluvial soils in the inland portion
of the study area will limit the use of cesspools for disposal
of effluent
For the seaward portion we anticipate that there are surface
or near surface soils which are sufficiently permeable for
cess pools to be utilized An estimated boundary between
these areas is shown on the Geologic Map Plate 2 We feel
that cesspool disposal could be used to the seaward Side of
this
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1095 00 Page 12
boundary however this demarcation is an estimate The actual
feasibility of cesspool disposal will have to be field tested
during the subsurface investigation phase of any proposed
development in this area
In addition disposal of effluent through cesspools in the
lower areas may lead to pollution related problems Cesspools
are used for the disposal of untreated effluent The surfi
cial sands are confined at their lower margin by impermeable
Clays that may cause a perching of these untreated pollutants
Occurrence of a perching condition may allow pollutants to
leach into the streams of the area or at the ocean margin
thereby contaminating surface waters and or recreational beach
areas
Disposal by Injection Well
In view of the possible limitations of available disposal areas
and the potential pollution hazard it appears that the best
solution for effluent disposal in the Waimanalo area is by
means of central treatment plants and deep well injection of
treated effluent The deeper permeable limestone are effec
tively confined by low permeability strata above and below yet
Still hydraulically connected to the open ocean This confine
ment greatly reduces the hazards of contamination to either
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1095 00 Page 13
ground water or surface water in the area and efficient treat
ment will lower the amount of pollutants that may reach the
open ocean
The best areas for any future treatment plants would be in the
lower elevations so that gravity flow may be used to bring the
Taw sewage to the plant This would also be below the maximum
30 foot elevation The location of the existing treatment
plant appears to be geographically and elevationally optimum
for the Waimanalo Area Any new facilities should most likely
be located in this area The Bellows Air Force Base area also
appears to be good with respect to elevation although it is
not as well located geographically
Any new disposal wells should be maintained with a regular r
program of surge flushing and pumping to inhibit failure of the
wells due to possible cave ins accumulated solids and
microbiological growth Even with a good maintenance program
it may still be necessary to periodically construct new
disposal wells as the honey comb like porous structure of the
calcareous sediments may not be able to accept treated effluent
with its suspended solid content for a long term period of
injection
