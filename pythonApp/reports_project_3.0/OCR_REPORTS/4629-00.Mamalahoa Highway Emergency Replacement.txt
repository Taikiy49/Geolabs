REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes including the following Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea some activity during
recent geologic time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 and is considered to be dormant and
Mauna Loa and Kilauea both are still active The project site is situated on the southeast
flank of the Mauna Loa Shield Volcano
The volume of the Mauna Loa Shield Volcano extending from the ocean floor is
estimated to be on the order of about 10 000 cubic miles making it possibly the largest
volcanic mountain on earth Furthermore it is estimated from current lava production that
Mauna Loa may have been forming for the past 1 to 2 million years Mauna Loa is actually
composed of two separate shield volcanoes Mauna Loa and Ninole The latter having been
covered by Mauna Loa lavas left only a few localized surface expressions visible at present
The Mauna Loa shield has been built by volcanic eruptions along two principal rift zones that
extend southwestward and east northeastward from the summit caldera
The Kilauea Volcano caldera is located approximately 14 miles toward the northeast
of the project site The eruptive rift zones radiating from the caldera are located toward the
east southwest rift zone and northeast east rift zone of the project site The present
GEOLABS INC
W O 4629 00 Page 6
eruption of Kilauea Volcano is located along the east rift zone in the vicinity of the Pu u O o
vent
The majority of the surface rock exposures at Mauna Loa consist of lavas and
volcanic deposits belonging to the Kau Volcanic Series Pleistocene and Holocene age and
the older Kahuku Volcanic Series Pleistocene age In addition a regional ash deposit
identified as Pahala Ash Pleistocene age forms a soil mantle overlying and interbedded
within the rocks of the Kau Volcanic Series The ash deposits typically occur as kipukas
isolated deposits with some significant layer accumulations ranging up to about 30 feet in
thickness
Pahala Ash is a low plasticity silt derived from the weathering of ash cinder and
Pele s Hair Pahala Ash generally has low shear strength and when the moisture content
is high enough it loses significant strength when it is remolded becomes thixotropic
Therefore the typical Pahala Ash may be potentially liquefiable during seismic events
The Kaalaala Stream Replacement Bridge project site is located on land that has
been mapped by others as covered with Kau Volcanic Series lava Holocene and
Pleistocene age at the ground surface The map symbols k and k3 that are shown on
the Geologic Map Plate 3 identify the general location of the Kau Volcanic Series lava flows
The Kau Volcanic Series unit k3 essentially consists of lava flows composed of tholeiitic
basait that were deposited approximately 750 to 1 500 years ago The Kau Volcanic Series
unit k also consists of tholeiitic basalt mixed with some volcanic ash and was deposited in
excess of 10 000 years ago making it the older geologic unit of the area Based on the
relative age of the rock units encountered at the project site it is possible that an extensive
regional deposit of Pahala Ash volcanic ash may underlie the near surface lava flows at the
site
The project site is also located near some younger pahoehoe lava flows that were
erupted from Kilauea Volcano These younger lava flows are on the order of approximately
400 to 750 years in age and form extensive regional coverage of the ground surface The

the project site and are mapped as part of the Puna Volcanic Series map unit p4o The

Based on our site reconnaissance Kaalaala Stream incises through the Kau Volcanic
Series basalt rock originating from Mauna Loa Volcano The exposed basalt rock comprising
the Kaalaala Stream banks and channel bottom appears to be composed of pahoehoe type
lava The basalt rock appears to be thinly layered and consists of vesicular basalt that is
generally closely to moderately fractured and slightly weathered Thin brownish colored
seams of scoriaceous strongly vesicular rock separate the individual lava flow layers
Overlying the basalt rock several feet of alluvial soils consisting of silty volcanic ash with
sands and gravel were observed The terrain in the vicinity of the project site consists of
gently undulating topography covered by the surface soil deposits
Based on our site reconnaissance and a review of previous geotechnical data in the
vicinity of the project site the basalt rock underlying the project site generally consists of
vesicular pahoehoe type lava Pahoehoe basalt rock is generally the result of thinner more
fluid lava flows The feeding river lobes of a pahoehoe flow crust over to develop a
continuous roof leaving the interior lava stream to flow through a tunnel of its own making
These remnant flow tunnels or voids are known as lava tubes The lava tubes feed the
advancing lava flow front and margins As a result the lava tube may branch or converge
generally in a downhill direction to maintain the lava flow In the waning stages of an
eruption the lava may drain from the main tube system leaving open tunnels ranging from
several feet to over 40 feet in diameter The open tubes resemble tunnels with arched roofs
and nearly flat floors The main lava tubes may extend downslope for several miles
occasionally reaching all the way to the coastline The borings drilled for this project did not
encounter large lava tubes at the location of our subsurface explorations
Another type of void space that is typically encountered in pahoehoe lava rock is a
pocket blister Rock blisters are typically isolated closed void structures formed during
GEOLABS INC
W O 4629 00 Page 8
cooling by trapped volcanic gas within the flow interior Some blisters may extend a short
distance downhill before pinching out and terminating
The surface of pahoehoe type lava flows are generally smoother than their rough and
irregular counterpart the a a lava flow A a lava flows form from generally more viscous
slower moving lavas and are expressed by a solid rock core sandwiched between an upper
and a lower less dense clinker layer The pahoehoe flow surface may be expressed by
broad dome shaped hillocks created by the heaving and buckling of the flow crust as it
approaches obstructions
FAULTING AND SEISMICITY
Based on our review of the available geologic information the Kaalaala Stream Bridge
Replacement project site is located between two unnamed geologic normal faults that were
mapped as concealed buried faults in the subsurface The concealed faults bound the
project site and are located toward the northwest and southeast from the bridge site Based
on the available geologic map of the area the concealed fault located toward the north
through west of the site appears to be located approximately 1 200 feet toward the northwest
at its closest approach The second concealed fault appears to be located approximately
500 feet in the southeasterly direction from the site at its closest approach The faults are
mapped as being approximately 1 mile in length and are oriented in a northeast to southwest
direction The project site is located adjacent to the central portion of both fault alignments
The approximate locations of the faults are shown on the Geologic Map Plate 3
Furthermore the project site is located easterly of multiple northeast to southwest
oriented normal faults associaied with the regional Kaoiki Honuapo Fault System which is
located toward the west and southwest of the site The faults previously mentioned are also
part of the Kaoiki Honuapo Fault System Some additional ground instability earthquake
potential may also be associated with the east rift zone of Kilauea Volcano which is located
approximately 2 miles toward the east of the site Therefore the project site should be
considered to be located in a region of potentially high seismic activity The general location

Due to the close proximity of the project site to the mapped fault zones the project
site is considered to be located in a seismic zone of potential recurring activity Earthquake
activity and fault rupture at the ground surface associated with future fault movement are
possibilities However the potential for surface fault rupture at the ground surface of the
project site is not known at this time

Scour Potential
Scour is the result of the erosive action of flowing water excavating and carrying away
material from the bed and banks of the stream Total scour over a period of time generally
consists of three components 1 Aggregation and Degradation 2 Contraction Scour and
3 Local Scour The rates of scour depend on a number of factors such as the shape and
dimensions of a abutment or pier depth of flow velocity of approach flow size and gradation
of streambed material and bed configuration
One of the factors affecting the scour depth is the grain size characteristics of the
stream bed material The median diameter of the stream bed material Dso in conjunction
with the depth of flow and flow velocity is used to calculate the flow velocity of the stream
bed materials in scour depth analysis Based on the materials encountered in the borings
drilled for the project we anticipate that moderately fractured basalt formation with relatively
high RQD values greater than 50 percent will be encountered at the stream bed and
foundation level Based on the guidelines on scour potential provided by the Federal
Highway Administration the basalt formation at the site may be considered to be a
non erodible material and would not be subjected to stream scour

Foundation Probing
Based on our experience in the area and observation of the core samples retrieved
voids and or cavities may be anticipated in the basalt rock formation at the project site In
addition small voids were encountered in the basalt rock formation encountered in the one
of the borings drilled To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from
the collapse of cavities below a footing we recommend that a program of cavity probing and
grouting at the foundation locations abutments and piers be implemented during
construction Probing and grouting of the site retaining wall foundations may be omitted due
to the less critical nature of these structures
For estimating purposes we recommend thai a probe hole be drilled for every
100 square feet of foundation area or at 10 feet on center along the pier and abutment
foundations The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to
a depth of at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of footing elevation If cavities and or
voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operation additional probeholes
should be drilled at closer spacing to aid in delineating the vertical and lateral extent of the
cavity and or void The probe holes and cavities discovered should be backfilled with
sand cement grout injected at low to moderate pressures We recommend that a low
strength sand cement grout mixture with a slump range of 6 to 9 inches be utilized for the
grouting operations As an alternative to sand cement grout Controlled Low Sirength
Material CLSM may also be used for the grouting operations
Because of the potential for encountering large cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that the probe drill be made available on site until the probing and grouting
operations are complete In addition it should be noted that a longer lag time between the
probing grouting operations and the foundation construction might be required in the
