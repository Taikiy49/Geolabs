SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered a pavement structure consisting of about 
3 to 11 inches of asphaltic concrete overlying sandy gravel base course material. The 
exception was in Boring No. 4, which encountered a pavement structure consisting of 
about 6.5 inches of Portland cement concrete. The pavement structure was generally 
underlain by fills, saprolites, and weathered basaltic materials, consisting of stiff to very 
stiff silty clays and dense to very dense clayey gravel, extending to the maximum depth 
explored of about 11.5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Based on our field exploration, running groundwater was encountered in 
Boring No. 6 at a depth of about 4.8 feet. It is suspected that the impermeable basalt 
ledge is preventing the groundwater from percolating into the underlying subsoils. It 
should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, time of 
year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. We believe that the 
new water mains may generally be installed without the need for dewatering. However, 
limited dewatering may be required, depending on the weather conditions during 
construction.  
Based on the results obtained from our field exploration and laboratory testing, it 
appears that some of the on-site soils have relatively high in-situ moisture contents. We 
believe that the majority of the excavated on-site soils will be suitable for use as trench 
backfill; however, drying or aerating of some of the excavated materials may be 
necessary prior to their use as trench backfill. Aeration to lower the soil moisture and 
more compaction effort along with thinner backfill lifts to achieve the specified 
compaction may be necessary and generally would reduce the rate of backfill 
placement for the project.  
Design of metallic substructures, such as water line pipes or other metallic 
piping, should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure.  
On the basis of the field resistivity survey results, the subsurface soils at the project site 
have resistivity values ranging from about 938 to 16,661 ohm-cm, corresponding to 
corrosion ratings of 1 to 5, respectively. It should be noted that the resistivity values of 
the subsurface soils varied substantially with depth and location across the project area; 
therefore, special attention should be given by the corrosion engineer when using the 
resistivity results provided in this report.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late 
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which 
time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a 
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These younger volcanic 
series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
worldwide cycles of continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less 
water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present 
stands of the sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became 
available such that the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations and within the previously carved depressions. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii and in the heads of valleys, the erosional 
processes are dominated by detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls 
and are transported downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as 
colluvium. Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley, 
alluvial processes become dominant, and the sediments are transported and deposited 
as alluvium. 
In addition to colluvial and alluvial soil deposits, the project area is generally 
underlain at deeper depths by basaltic rock built by extrusion of the lavas of the Koolau 
Volcanic Series. These rocks are generally characterized by flows of jointed dense 
vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers. In-situ chemical weathering of the

3.2 
Trench Excavation Support 
We anticipate excavation depths of up to about 4 to 5 feet below the pavement 
surface will be required for the installation of the new water mains. Deeper excavations 
may be required where the water mains traverse existing underground utilities. Where 
deeper excavations (greater than 5 feet in depth) are planned, temporary shoring or 
sloping and benching should be implemented for the trench excavations. Based on our 
site observations, we believe that temporary cut slopes for open cut excavations are not 
practical. In this case, trench walls would have to be cut vertically necessitating the use 
of shoring during construction. 
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable 
safety requirements. The contractor should be solely responsible for the adequacy and 
safety of the shoring installation. The contractor’s representative should be on-site at all 
times during excavation and construction work for the opportunity to promptly observe 
changing or unforeseen conditions, such as high groundwater, inappropriate 
construction sequence or techniques, etc., which may affect the shoring stability. 
Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal 
to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential 
for excessive ground movement. 
It is important to install adequate shoring and to maintain it tight against the 
excavation walls with proper bracing during construction. The properly braced shoring is

3.3 
Pipe Bedding 
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the 
load supporting capacity of the pipe. Therefore, the pipe bedding is an important design 
consideration. For pipes bearing on the stiff clayey soils, a minimum of 6 inches of 
compacted granular bedding material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation) should be provided below the pipe. Where soft and/or loose soils are 
encountered at or near the invert of the pipes, a stabilization layer consisting of 
additional 18 inches of open-graded gravel wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric 
(Mirafi 180N or equivalent) should be provided below the bedding layer for uniform 
support.  
The granular bedding should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width of 
one-fourth (1/4)  the outside pipe diameter and should extend half way up the sides of 
the pipe. Before the placement of bedding material, a Geolabs representative should 
observe the excavated trench bottom to confirm that firm materials are exposed at the 
bottom of the trench or whether a stabilization layer should be provided below the 
bedding layer.

3.4 
Trench Backfill 
In general, the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the Water 
System Standards, State of Hawaii 2002. The first zone of backfill extending from the 
bedding material to at least 12 inches above the top of the pipes should consist of select 
free draining granular material (ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation) to reduce the compaction 
effort required and resulting stresses on the pipe. In addition, it is critical to use this 
free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches 
of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench 
backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the pavement subgrade should consist of the excavated on-site soils or imported fill 
materials that are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, and clay lumps and rock 
fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension. It should be noted that the fill 
requirements presented herein are intended as guidelines only and may be modified 
based on Geolabs additional laboratory testing and field observations on the available 
fill materials. 
Imported fill materials used for trench backfill should be well graded from coarse 
to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension. The material should 
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material 
should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher and a 
swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. 
As an alternative, to facilitate the backfilling and reduce the time required for 
applying compactive effort, Controlled Low Strength Material (CLSM) may be used to 
backfill the trenches. The use of CLSM would also reduce the potential for future 
settlement of the trench backfill under the repeated wheel loading from traffic on the 
roadway. 
Aggregate base course materials required for the flexible pavement restoration 
section should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates and should conform to Section 31 
of the City and County of Honolulu Standard Specifications (September, 1986).

3.5 
Trench Backfill Compaction and Placement Requirements 
Backfill materials consisting of the on-site soils should be moisture-conditioned to 
at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 
8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction. The upper 3 feet below the pavement grade should be compacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.   
Imported fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture, placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Aggregate base course and subbase 
materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed 
in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of 
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or 
other types of acceptable compaction equipment.  Water tamping, jetting, or ponding 
should not be allowed to compact the on-site clayey soils. 
Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during backfilling to 
assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper 
moisture content. Where compaction is less than required, additional compactive effort 
should be applied with adjustment of moisture content as necessary, to obtain the 
specified compaction.

Thrust Blocks 
We envision that thrust blocks will be required at some locations to provide 
resistance to the thrust forces due to the pressurized pipes. The thrust force acting on 
the pipes may be resisted by passive earth pressure acting against the face of the thrust 
block. We anticipate that the majority of the planned inverts would be about 4 to 5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. However, some of the thrust blocks may need to be 
located at deeper depths of about 10 to 15 feet below the pavement surface in areas 
where the new water mains traverse the existing underground utility lines.   
Based on our field exploration, stiff to very stiff silty clays and dense to very 
dense clayey gravel were generally encountered throughout the depths explored. We 
recommend using lateral bearing pressures of 1,000 pounds per square foot (psf) for 
the design of thrust blocks bearing in the stiff to very stiff silty clays and dense to very 
dense clayey gravel underlying the site. 
3.7 
Settlements 
Primary settlement of the water mains is normally caused by the difference in the 
unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted backfill 
material placed over the pipes. The net increase in loading will cause settlement of the 
underlying subsoils below the trench invert. Based on our engineering analyses, 
settlement on the order of less than ¼ inch is anticipated for the project. 
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good 
workmanship will be engaged during construction. Additional settlement could occur if 
improper trench support is used. 
3.8 
Corrosion Potential 
Design of metallic substructures, such as water line pipes or other metallic 
piping, should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure.  
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics with 
regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the 
resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure.

Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of 
metallic objects. 
A total of 32 field resistivity surveys were conducted at a maximum of about 
9-foot intervals at eight locations, designated as R-1 through R-8, using the in-situ 
Wenner 4-Pin Method and a Nilsson Model 400 resistance meter manufactured by 
Nilsson Electrical Laboratory, Inc. The method utilizes four surface probes or pins 
(electrodes) to evaluate the average resistivity (an indication of corrosivity) of the 
underlying soil mass. The field resistivity survey was used to evaluate the average 
resistivity values of the soil mass at the approximate survey locations. Results of the 
field resistivity surveys are presented on Plates C-1 through C-4 of Appendix C. It 
should be noted that the field resistivity survey may be influenced by temperature, 
weather conditions, soil moisture, existing underground utilities and other factors. 
On the basis of the field resistivity survey results, the subsurface soils at the 
project site have resistivity values ranging from about 938 to 16,661 ohm-cm, 
corresponding to corrosion ratings of 1 to 5, respectively. It should be noted that the 
resistivity values of the subsurface soils varied substantially with depth and location 
across the project area; therefore, special attention should be given by the corrosion 
engineer when using the resistivity results provided in this report.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling six borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 6, to depths of about 10.1 to 11.5 feet below the 
existing pavement surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
We used a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers to advance the 
borings. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-6. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to 
drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized 
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations of the test 
results are provided on Plate B-1. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM C117 & C136) was performed on a selected soil 
sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plate B-2. 
To evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the on-site clayey soils, 
one Unconfined Compression test was performed on a selected in-situ sample in 
accordance with ASTM D2166.  The test results are shown on the Logs of Borings at 
the appropriate sample depth. Individual stress-strain curve of the unconfined 
compression test is presented on Plate B-3. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested.  Test results are 
presented on Plates B-4 and B-5. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-6 and B-7. 
One Modified Proctor compaction test (ASTM D1557) was performed on a bulk 
sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationship.  The test results are presented on Plate B-8.

Field Resistivity Survey 
Geophysical survey equipment (i.e. resistance meter) was mobilized to the project 
site on February 13, 2015 for the field resistivity survey.  Thirty-two soil resistivity surveys 
were conducted at eight locations, designated as R-1 through R-8, using the in-situ 
Wenner 4-Pin Method and a Nilsson Model 400 resistance meter manufactured by Nilsson 
Electrical Laboratory, Inc. The method utilizes four surface probes or pins (electrodes) to 
evaluate the average resistivity of the underlying soil mass. The average resistivity values 
of the soil mass, extending from the existing ground surface to the test depth, was 
assessed by using this method at the approximate survey locations shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The soil resistivity surveys were performed along the existing ground surface at the 
approximate survey locations shown on the site plan. The average soil resistance along 
the proposed alignment was evaluated at each survey location with the electrodes aligned 
at equal spacing of generally 3, 5, 7, and 9 feet. The depth of penetration of a resistivity 
sounding is a function of the electrode spacing. 
The resistance meter (Nilsson Model 400) provides a voltage between the outer 
electrodes, causing an electrical current to flow between them. The voltage drop between 
the inner electrodes is then measured by a sensitive, built-in voltmeter. As a result, the soil 
resistance (in ohms) is obtained from the voltmeter dial reading. The soil resistivity 
(in ohm-cm), which is directly related to both resistance and spacing, can then be 
computed. The evaluated soil resistances and computed resistivity values are presented 
on Plates C-1 and C-3. In addition, the computed resistivity values with pin spacing at the 
test locations along each pipe line alignment are presented in the graphs on Plates C-2 
and C-4. At the time of resistivity surveying, the temperature was about 85º F. The existing 
ground surface was damp to moist. It should be noted that the soil resistivity may be 
influenced by temperature, weather conditions, existing underground facilities and other 
factors.
