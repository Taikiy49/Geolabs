REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene 
to early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a 
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are 
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii, the erosional processes are dominated by 
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported 
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium. Once these 
materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley, alluvial processes become 
dominant, and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium. 
In general, stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy, such that the 
stream flows transmit large volumes of water for very short durations. Because of this, 
transport of sediments is intermittent, and the bulk of the stream’s hydraulic load 
consists of a poorly-sorted mixture of boulders, cobbles, gravel, sands, and fines. When 
the erosional basal level change, these sediment loads are left as deposits. 
The project site is on the lower gently to moderately inclined valley slopes on the 
eastern side of Nuuanu Valley; which, represents the eroded southern flank of the 
Koolau Volcano. The project site appears to be underlain by clayey alluvial and colluvial 
soils with embedded hard boulders overlying basalt rock at depth. Hard rock 
outcroppings reside on the higher elevation mountain slopes above the project site. 
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
Our field reconnaissance was conducted on May 28, 2019 by our geologist under 
fair weather conditions. Our site reconnaissance generally consisted of traversing the 
westerly facing lower mountain hillslopes above the proposed home development site 
within Units 17 & 18. Our upslope reconnaissance extended from the proposed building 
area, about 300 to 350 linear feet uphill towards the east and included the slopes on the 
uphill and downhill sides of an existing rockfall impact barrier, previously installed on the 
slope. 
We observed an area of generally level ground surfaces covered by thick ground 
cover vegetation immediately adjacent to the Ragsdale Place driveway entrance.

Towards the east, dense forest growth consisting of large trees and scattered shrubbery 
cover the natural hillside terrain bordering the existing level ground area. An existing 
rockfall impact barrier fence was encountered about 200 linear feet upslope from the 
existing level ground area. 
The rockfall impact barrier is about 100 linear feet in length and spans across the 
hillside with limited change in elevation at the ends of the fence. The barrier appears to 
be located about 200 feet upslope from the proposed building area and is about 6 feet 
in height above the ground. The steel barrier appears to be in generally fair condition 
but may require cleaning of entrained forest debris from the bottom of the net to 
maintain the desired catchment height. In addition, some of the post footings may need 
to be rehabilitated to maintain the foundation integrity of the barrier system. 
The forested hillside generally slopes westerly at about a 2H:1V inclination and 
appears to be composed of stiff clayey soils containing some scattered basalt boulders. 
We observed boulders at the ground surface that range between about 1 and 3 feet in 
larger dimension. The boulders are isolated (not nested) and appear to be in generally 
stable ground settings. Visible evidence of potential boulder instability or active ground 
erosion was not observed.  
Above the existing rock impact barrier, more widely scattered surface boulders 
and cobble deposits were encountered including some boulder nests and 
accumulations of loose cobbles upslope from the barrier alignment. At about 350 feet 
upslope from the proposed building area, a low height, semi-continuous, cross-slope 
trending basalt rock shelf was encountered. Visible signs of potential rock instability 
associated with the outcropping was not observed. 
Based on our observations, the rockfall barrier was constructed across a section 
of generally uniform westerly facing slope located upslope from the proposed new 
building site. A deep drainage ravine with associated steep soil covered slopes was 
observed towards the north of the barrier alignment. Similarly, another shallower 
drainage gully resides towards the south of the barrier alignment. Due to the presence 
of the gullies located near both ends of the barrier alignment, the natural slope and 
probable boulder fall-line direction at the ends of the existing fence alignment shifts 
away from the proposed building site. As a result of the hillside topographic conditions, 
the existing barrier appears to be located appropriately to provide rockfall protection on 
the generally westerly facing slope located above the proposed new building site. 
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our site reconnaissance, potential rockfall hazards emanating from 
within the Units 17 & 18 slopes appear to be very limited in the existing hillside 
condition. The natural terrain and the presence of the existing rockfall impact barrier on 
the hillside appear to provide a satisfactory buffer against falling rock encroachment.

Based on our review of the current lot development plan, we believe that this 
rockfall potential inspection satisfies the Condition 1, that requires retaining a licensed 
geotechnical engineer to conduct a boulder survey within the individual lots or within 
areas directly upslope of the individual lots is disturbed due to manmade of natural 
causes. We also believe that this lot development plan satisfies the Condition 2, that no 
development shall occur south of the setback line. 
As mentioned above, the proposed building location was changed from the initial 
proposed location on the lot. Based on our site reconnaissance and evaluation of the 
existing site conditions, the new building location does not appear to be exposed to the 
anticipated low risk potential rockfall hazards observed on the hillside. The existing 
rockfall barrier should protect the new building site due to the position of the barrier with 
respect to the probable slope fall-line direction and the existing surface boulder deposits 
on the hillside.  
Although a greater volume of surface rocks was observed upslope from the 
barrier alignment, the deposits did not appear to be substantially precarious or have 
significant potential for uncontrolled rockfall initiation. Based on our observations, 
potential rockfall emanating from the upper slopes above the barrier should either fall 
toward the bounding ravines or into the rockfall barrier on the slope. Thus, the potential 
rockfall exposure is limited in the present slope condition.  
The potential rockfall hazard exposure in the post-development slope condition 
should remain limited as we recommend that the new reconstructed slope be regraded 
to remove rocks larger than about 1-foot in larger dimension from the finished slope 
face. 
Finally, the existing rockfall impact barrier system and components should be 
periodically inspected and maintained by an experienced rockfall specialty contractor to 
provide the intended design rockfall interception capacity. The recommended 
maintenance may include cleaning of the existing entrained forest debris from the 
bottom of the net to increase the effective rock capture height and the rehabilitation of 
some of the above-ground GRP fence post foundations. 
Future slope hazard evaluations by a qualified engineering consultant should be 
performed at about 5- to 10-year intervals or when substantial disturbance of the 
upslopes occurs as a result of construction activity and changes to the landscape 
resulting from extraordinary events such as a strong earthquake, intense stormwater 
runoff, or other significant activity occurring on the upslope terrain.

LIMITATIONS 
The assessments and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, 
upon information obtained from a visual project site reconnaissance. Variations of 
ground conditions between and beyond our observations may occur, and the nature and 
extent of these variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If 
variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations 
provided herein. 
It should be noted that slopes composed of rock materials (rock slopes) 
deteriorate with the passage of time due to natural weathering processes, wet-dry and 
hot-cold cycles, and erosion conditions. Due to the inherent deterioration of rock slopes 
resulting from natural processes (weathering, wet-dry and hot-cold cycles, and erosion 
conditions), the potential for rockfall hazard at any site changes with the passage of 
time. Therefore, the findings and recommendations contained herein may be used only 
within a reasonable time from the date of issuance of this letter report. Land use, site 
conditions, and/or other factors may change with the passage of time. Therefore, 
additional work to evaluate the applicability of the findings and recommendations 
contained in this letter report due to changes with the passage of time will be required. 
Finally, there are no guarantees in the professional engineering and architectural design 
fields with respect to potential rockfall hazards due in large part to the unpredictable 
nature of rockfall activity, which is affected by many external variables including natural 
and man-induced causes.
