INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of a soils and foundation
investigation for the proposed 3 story high commercial building
with 2 level basement parking The study consisted of subsur
face borings laboratory testing and engineering analyses as
outlined in our proposal dated May 29 1973 Based on the results
of our engineering studies conclusions and recommendations per
tinent to site development are presented
PROJECT DESCRIPTION
The property is located at the northwest intersection of East
Manoa Road with Manoa Stream in Manoa Valley The building site
location is indicated on the attached site plan Plate 1 The
site drains towards the stream The site is partly covered
with light grass to heavy vegetation cover and partly asphalt
paved There is a gasoline station on the southwest area of
the property
It is our understanding that the structure would be a reinforced
concrete structure mainly supported on columns Presently the
magnitude of the columniloads is not known As presently planned
the western portion of the basement would be about 23 feet
below present ground The eastern portion portion of the base
ment is set back from the existing Manoa Stream channel wall and

the top of existing channel wall A relatively small portion
of the building in southeast corner ramp area will have partial
basement The northern portion of the building will have only
one story above ground while the southern portion will have
three stories above ground
FIELD EXPLORATION
The subsurface exploration consisted of four 4 borings ranging
in depth from 30 to 37 feet In all of the borings obstructions
were encountered at relatively shallow depths and borings were
moved to new locations The drilling was accomplished using
truck mounted rotary drilling equipment with 9 inch diameter
hollow stem continuous flight auger The basalt rock was cored
using a diamond bit The borings were continuously inspected by
an engineer from our firm The drilling contractor was Northwest
Exploration Services Inc
The approximate boring locations are shown on Plate 1 and the
boring logs are presented in Appendix A

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
During the late Tertiary Period the Koolau Volcanic Mountain
Range which forms the elongated northeastern portion of Oahu
was built mainly by eruptions of basaltic lava along a northwest
trending rift zone
Subsequent to the period of mountain building in eastern Oahu
a prolonged period of erosion produced the steep sided Manoa
Valley Subsequent changes of sea level either eustatic or
tectonic changed the depositional base level and considerable
infilling of the valley by clastic debris took place
Page 4
W O 478 00
Approximately 10 000 to 20 000 years ago lava welled up out of
a vent on the flank of sugarloaf on the western side of Manoa
Valley This late phase eruption may have been caused by the
unloading of the magma chamber beneath Oahu due to the previous
intense period of erosion The sugarloaf flow spread across
Manoa Valley forcing Manoa Stream out of its former channel in
the center of the valley The stream now flows along the eastern
edge of the flow and eastern edge of the project site The
flow filled the lower end of the valley and raised the base level
i e reduced the gradient of the stream and caused it to deposit
alluvium on the upstream portion of the valley Continued depo
sition of alluvial debris in the valley has caused its prograda
tion out over the lava flow so that the flow on site is buried
by about 15 22 feet of alluvial debris
Subsequent channelizing of Manoa Stream and landfill operations
have partly covered the site with fill

Groundwater
At this stage it is rather difficult to say whether water table
on the entire site is at more or less one elevation or is vari
able In B 1 groundwater was encountered at 8 foot depth while
drilling After pulling out the augers the free water was at
1 foot 8 inch depth which was gradually lowered to 4 feet 6 inches
in 3 days when the boring was backfilled This water may be
drilling water or water under artesian pressure In B 2 water
table appears to have more or less stabilized at about 15 foot
depth as per reading taken inside the inch diameter PVC
pipe which is installed to study water table fluctuations
Water table readings should be taken at frequent intervals in
this pipe to study the fluctuations in water table In B 3 water
was noted to be at 19 foot 6 inch depth after pulling out the

Footing Design
All footings should be founded on massive basalt rock to
support the proposed structure In areas where there is no
or shallow basement short piers resting on massive basalt
rock should be used This is to reduce the possibility of

Basement Excavation
Large boulder should be anticipated to be encountered during
basement excavation Wéathered basalt should not be very
difficult to excavate using heavy equipment Excavation in
massive basalt rock will be very difficult and blasting may
have to be used In B 2 and B 4 relatively shallow massive
rock was encountered and it is difficult to conclude if
these are large boulders or a layer of rock It will be
advisable to excavate a backhoe trench in the north central
portion of the site to study excavation difficulties and
determine if there is a relatively shallow layer of rock or
not This will also provide useful information on the
groundwater table Unsurcharged excavation sides should
be sloped at 1 1 gradient All safety regulations should be
observed for grading bracing of excavation and blasting

Dewatering
Dewatering of the basement excavation will be required and
it should be continuous during basement construction and
as long as necessary to ensure safe and satisfactory construc
tion The method of dewatering may be left open to the
contractor provided the excavation stability and satisfactory
drawdown are maintained and nearby structures are not damaged
due to loss of soil
Basement Walls
Basement walls should be designed to resist lateral earth
pressures which will depend on construction procedure and
position of water table Following lateral pressures are
for unsurcharged walls only and any surcharge such as fills
structure loads construction equipment etc should be
added to following values Two cases are discussed below
for design lateral pressures
Case l
In this case the excavation behind the wallsshould be back
filled prior to connecting the top of the wall to the
structural components such as cross beams columns super
structure slab etc The portion of the wall above the
highest groundwater elevation should be designed for the
Page 12
W O 478 00
pressure exerted by a fluid of density equal to 35 pcf
For the portion of wall below the groundwater table an
equivalent fluid of density equal to 80 pcf should be
assumed for lateral pressure
Case II
If the excavation behind the wall is to be backfilled
after the wall has been connected to the rest of the
structure then the wall should be designed for higher
pressures In this case the equivalent fluid densities
will be equal to 55 pcf and 90 pcf respectively for the
portion of the wall above and below the highest ground
water table
Resistance to the lateral load may be provided by a
combination of passive pressure and friction Passive
pressure may be taken as that exerted by a fluid of
density equal to 320 pcf Surface one foot of soil should
be neglected for passive pressure unless it is confined
beneath a concrete floor slab Friction may be taken as
normal dead load times a coefficient of friction equal to
0 6 six tenth If the wall footing is poured in at least
one foot deep trench in massive rock then the wall base
may be considered as fixed for lateral movement

LABORATORY TESTING
A laboratory testing program was developed to establish the
engineering properties of the soils and rocks encountered at
the project site The tests where applicable to the subject
project are included in this Appendix

Visual Classification All soil samples obtained from

field classification prior to finalizing the Logs of

Water Content Determinations In addition to visual
classification typical samples were tested for natural
water content as an aid in soil classification and in
evaluating soil properties The water content values
are based upon the dry weight of the soil
Atterberg Limits Atterberg limit tests were performed
on selected clay and silt samples obtained from the
borings for the purposes of identification and correla
tion of soils Standard procedures ASTM D 424 for the
plastic limit and ASTM D 423 for the liquid limit were
used in the performance of these tests

Grain Size Analysis Sieve analysis were performed on
granular soils For fine grained soils the hydrometer
test was performed to determine the distribution of
the grain sizes beyond the No 200 sieve
Shear Strength Tests
a
b
Unconfined Compression Tests Intact core and split
spoon soil samples which apparently exhibited high
plasticity were tested for unconfined strength Length
to diameter ratio of 2 was maintained throughout the
tests to avoid scatter of test results and to facilitate
evaluation of shear strength values for design purposes
Direct Shear Test In general soil samples were tested
for shear strength using a 2 41 I D direct shear box
Each set consisted of at least two tests each test run
under different normal loads The results are plotted
as Normal Stress vs Shear Strength with a failure envelope
drawn to determine values of cohesion c and angle of
frictional resistance f
Consolidation Test Consolidation tests were performed on
selected undisturbed ring samples of the compressible
soils to provide basic data for making settlement calculations
Porous stones were placed on the top and bottom of the samples
to allow drainage Vertical loads were applied in increments
with each load increment being allowed to consolidate prior
2
to adding the next increment Measurements of the time
and consolidation were obtained during each load increment
and rebound was measured during the unloading portion
Consolidation test results are plotted in terms of percent
settlement versus applied loads
Swell Test The ring sampleswas placed between porous
stones and a 60 psf load was applied to the sample for a
24 hour period The difference between the initial and
final sample heights is the amount of swell which is expressed
as a percentage of the initial height of the sample
Maximum Density Test Samples of soils are compacted in a
mold of given size with a 10 pound rammer dropped 18 inches
to determine the relationship between moisture content and
density of the soil At least three trial points at dif
ferent moisture content are run to determine the maximum
density and optimum moisture content The test is run in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 70
