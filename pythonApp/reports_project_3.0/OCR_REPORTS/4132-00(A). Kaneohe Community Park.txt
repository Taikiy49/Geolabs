INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Reconstruct Lighting System project within the existing
Kaneohe Community Park in the Kaneohe area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu
Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input
only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion
incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacioeustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4132 00 A Page 5
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported downslope
toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these materials reach
the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become dominant and the
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the stream
flows transmit large volumes of water for very short durations Because of this transport
of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load consists of a
poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When the erosional
base levels change these sediment loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes begin
to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the material
Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the coarse grained portion
of the sediment resulting in a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate
Simultaneously erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors and upstream in
headwaters This continued erosion generates material which is transported downslope
covering the older alluvial deposits
Depending on the local base level and rate of transport these newer sediments
are generally transient in terms of geologic time In addition their consistency and
density are generally less than those of the older partially consolidated deposits

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration indicates that the proposed light pole locations are generally
underlain by stiff to very stiff silty clays and clayey silts throughout the depths explored In
consideration of the lateral loads and moments acting on the foundations of the proposed
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4132 00 A Page 7
light pole foundations we recommend that drilled shafts embedded in the stiff clayey soils
be used to support the proposed lighting system We recommend that the drilled shafts
be embedded a minimum of 12 and 13 feet below the lowest adjacent finished grades for
the proposed light pole foundations located on level and sloping grounds respectively
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering recommendations
are presented in the following sections of this report
Drilled Shaft Foundations
In order to develop the required bearing and lateral load resistances the proposed
new light pole structures may be supported by a foundation system consisting of
cast in place concrete drilled shafts Based on the base plate dimensions for the new
light pole structures we understand that a minimum diameter of 42 inches should be
used for the proposed drilled shaft foundations Based on the anticipated structural loads
and the subsoil conditions encountered at the project site we recommend that drilled
shaft foundations with an embedment length of not less than 12 feet below the design
finished grades be used for the new light pole structures located on level ground
conditions For the new light pole structures located on sloping ground in the outfield
areas the drilled shaft embedment length should be increased to a minimum of 13 feet
below the lowest adjacent finished grades
The cast in place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally
from skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding soils An allowable
compressive load capacity of up to 20 kips per shaft may be used for dead plus live loads
and may be increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces
Lateral loads imposed on the light pole foundations may be resisted by resistance
of the shaft soil system to deflect Lateral load resistance of the drilled shaft is a function
of the stiffness of the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable deflection at the
top of shaft and induced moment in the shaft Based on the information provided each
drilled shaft will be subjected to a lateral load of 4 2 kips and an overturning moment of
about 213 kip feet at the ground surface Based on our foundation analyses the

depth of about 1 foot below the top of the shaft Our lateral load analyses indicated that
the estimated deflection at the top of the drilled shaft should be less than inch
The anticipated settlements of the light pole foundations are estimated to be on the
order of less than inch We believe that a significant portion of the settlements is elastic
and should occur as the loads are applied
Construction Considerations
Drilled shafts are desirable for the light pole foundations because of the significant
increase in lateral and uplift load capacities when compared to shallow foundations
However the performance of the drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the
contractor s method of construction and construction procedures In our opinion the
following conditions would have a substantial effect on the effectiveness and cost of the
drilled shaft foundations
Dense Boulders
Based on our field exploration the proposed new light pole locations are generally
underlain by saprolite materials consisting of stiff to very stiff clayey soils extending
to a depth of about 31 5 feet below the existing ground surface However hard
drilling conditions were encountered at shallow depths in the vicinity of Boring
No 2 during our field exploration Therefore coring into potential dense boulders
may be required during the drilled shaft construction Appropriate drilling
equipment and coring tools should be utilized by the drilled shaft contractor to
install the drilled shafts to the depths and dimensions recommended herein
Workmanship
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in
drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4132 00 A Page 9
A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 6 to 9 inch range should be used to
provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soil The
concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce segregation of the
aggregates from the concrete mix If pre cast concrete pole bases are used the
gap between the pole base and the surrounding soils should be backfilled with
concrete or lean concrete A representative of Geolabs Hawaii should be present
to observe the installation of drilled shafts during construction to confirm the
subsoil conditions and embedment depths
Utility Trench
We envision that new electrical duct lines will be required to service the proposed
new lighting system In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of clean sand or
S4C is recommended under the duct lines Free draining granular materials such as
clean sand or S4C should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 6 inches
above the duct lines to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the
compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is
critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Improper backfill material and placement procedures could result in settlement of the
backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 6 inches above the duct lines
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on site soils or imported
select granular fill material The backfill should be placed in maximum 6 inch level loose
lifts moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet
of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established

content corresponding to the maximum dry density

Field Exploration

four borings to depths ranging from about 16 5 to 31 5 feet below the existing ground
surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were
drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers

examination in the field by our engineer who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer tests provide an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil samples Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Two direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the shear strength parameters The test results are presented on Plates B 1
and B 2
