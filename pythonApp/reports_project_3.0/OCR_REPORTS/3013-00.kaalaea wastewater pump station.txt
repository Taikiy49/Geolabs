INTRODUCTION
In accordance with the request of R M Towill Corporation we have conducted a
geotechnical engineering exploration for the subject wastewater pump station and sewer
force main project located in the Kahaluu area of the island of Oahu Hawaii The
approximate location of the site and its vicinity is shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1 This report presents our findings and recommendations pertaining to the design
of the proposed project
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is situated in the Kahaluu area on the windward side of the island
of Oahu Hawaii It is proposed to construct a new wastewater pump station and force
main to connect residences in the vicinity of Wailenua Road with the existing Kahaluu
Wastewater Pump Station
Based on the information provided to us we anticipate that the new pump station
will be about 35 by 50 feet in plan dimension with bottom floor elevation and influent
sewer invert elevation approximately 27 and 20 feet below the existing ground surface
respectively and that the invert of the new force main will be between about 5 to 10 feet
below the existing ground surface The force main will cross the Kahaluu Stream
Ahuimanu Stream estuary by either a pipe bridge or by an inverted siphon Plans call for
a 1000 gallon underground fuel storage tank on the site A Venturi Box Pit approximately
10 by 12 feet in plan dimension and 4 to 5 feet deep is also planned
Primary areas of concern for the design and construction of the project are as
follows
Foundation support for the proposed wastewater pump station and pipe
bridges for the crossing of Haiamoa and Kahaluu Streams

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active and
its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in Central Oahu
The project site is located on the easterly flank of Koolau and its geomorphology
and subsurface conditions are related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of sea level during
the Pleistocene and the location of the central caldera complex of Koolau The base of
the stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt
The Koolau volcanic shield was built during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene
by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities ceased
approximately 2 5 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicates that the island
of Oahu has subsided as much as 1200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic
activity Macdonald and Abbott 1970
The central caldera complex of the volcano extended approximately from the area
of Mount Olomana northwesterly to the vicinity of Kualoa During the active phase of
Koolau hydrothermal activity by rising gases and fluids altered the basaltic rock into easily
eroded materials
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3013 00 Page 7
During the Pleistocene Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a result
of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to
fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower
relative to present sea level on Oahu
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the volcanic dome of
Koolau The headward erosion of these valleys reached the easily eroded materials in
the caldera complex and carved broad amphitheater headed valleys The heads of these
valleys coalesced to form a long steep cliff or pali
When sea level rose again sediments accumulated in the valleys In the vicinity
of the project site these sediments accumulated in a marshy low energy environment
along the shore of Kaneohe Bay
The project site is situated in the lower portions of Waihee Valley When this area
was developed man made fill was placed over the soft marshy sediments
Subsoil Conditions

encountered soft to very soft organic silts and clays to depths of about 50 feet below the
existing ground surface where loose alluvial sands and gravels were encountered

Groundwater Conditions
Groundwater was encountered at shallow depths in all of the borings drilled for our
exploration Water levels observed during our exploration exhibited positive heads above
sea level The borings drilled at the proposed pump station site produced artesian flows
after completion
These observations indicate that there is positive artesian pressure in the
sediments underlying the proposed wastewater pump station site and along the proposed
sewer force main alignment
These artesian heads may vary in response to precipitation in the recharge area
and in response to tidal fluctuations in the nearby ocean

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
As previously indicated our borings encountered soft to very soft subsoils at the
anticipated invert elevation of the pump station Specific information pertaining to the
loads which will be generated by the new site improvements were not available for use
in the preparation of this report It is our opinion that unless very low bearing pressures
are used for the design of the pump station foundations and unless settlements in excess
of one inch 1 are tolerable pile foundations will be required to support the pump
station
Along the proposed sewer force main alignment our exploration encountered soft
to very soft subsoils and high groundwater table elevations Installation of the new force
main will require shoring and bracing to support the excavated trench and to aid in
dewatering
Pipe bridges for the crossing of Haiamoa and Kahaluu Streams will also require
deep pile foundations to support the force main due to the significant thickness of soft
soils As an alternate consideration should be given to the use of directional drilling or
micro tunnelling for the installation of a flexible pipe such as high density polyethylene
Pump Station Site Preparation
Details of the proposed site grading for the proposed wastewater pump station site
were not available at the time this report was prepared However it is anticipated that the
earthwork at the site will generally consist of cutting down the ground surface on the
westerly part of the site to create a level pad at about elevation 7 5 feet MSL and the
placement of less than about one foot 1 of fill on the easterly portion of the site

Foundation Support
Our field exploration indicates that the pump station site is underlain by a thin crust
of man made fill underlain by about 40 feet of soft compressible organic sediments The
excavations for the proposed pump station and venturi pit and possibly the underground
fuel storage tank will penetrate through this crust and the bases of these structures will
rest on the soft organic sediments
Our exploration similarly indicates that there are soft compressible soils to depths
of 65 feet at Haiamoa Stream A review of the pile driving records for the waterline
crossing of Kahaluu Stream indicates that embedment depths ranging between about 66
and 115 feet were required for 50 ton capacity piles
We believe that the significant thickness of soft compressible soils will not provide
adequate support for spread footing or mat foundations without the structures undergoing
appreciable settlements Therefore pile foundations are recommended to support the
proposed pump station structures and pipe bridges
The piles would develop their axial load bearing capacity primarily from frictional
resistance between the pile shafts and the stiff and dense alluvial soils underlying the soft
organic soils Based on our field exploration and analyses a 16 5 inch octagonal pile
would develop an allowable axial load bearing capacity of about 50 tons at a depth of
about 75 feet below the existing ground surface at the pump station site Pile embedment
depths in the range of about 85 to 90 feet are anticipated for the stream crossings for an
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3013 00 Page 12
allowable pile capacity of 50 tons Piles should be spaced center to center a minimum
of 2 5 times the diameter or width of the pile to avoid potential reduction of pile capacity
due to group action
Prior to construction of foundation slabs and after driving of the piles for the pump
station venturi pit and fuel tank the subgrade at each structure location should be
covered with a layer of filter fabric such as Mirafi 140N or equivalent The fabric should
be lapped a minimum of 24 inches at the end of each roll and along the sides and
preferably should be stitched together at all overlaps A minimum two foot 2 thick
gravel blanket should be placed over the filter fabric The blanket should consist of clean
granular material such as No 3B Fine Gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 Gradation The fabric
should be form fitted around the piles so that the soft subgrade soils do not contaminate
the gravel blanket
Lateral load resistance of a driven pile is a function of the stiffness of the
surrounding soil the stiffness of the pile length and diameter of the pile and the allowable
deflection at the top of the pile The lateral load capacity of a 16 inch octagonal pile is
estimated to be on the order of 10 kips per pile The above lateral load capacity assumes
a lateral deflection of inch at the top of the pile and that the piles are spaced center
to center a minimum of 2 5 times the pile diameter
Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a minimum rated
energy of 50 000 foot pounds The hammer should be equipped with an energy
level control so that driving within the soft compressible soil may be controlled at
a reduced energy to reduce the tensile stress development within the piles which
could damage or cause cracking of the piles
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3013 00 Page 13
The contractor should forward pile and driving equipment data forms to Geolabs
Hawaii prior to bidding for evaluation Geolabs Hawaii will review the submittal and
assess the driveability of piles with the use of the selected hammer Due to
possible variation in the length of the piles unit prices should be obtained during
bidding for add ons shorter piles etc
Pile Driving Criteria
Each pile should be driven continuously without interruption with the exception of
splicing if needed A pile may be rejected or its design capacity may be reduced
when the driving resistance is interrupted for more than four hours and the pile
cannot be driven to the required depth
During soft driving i e when the pile tip is within soft loose soils the hammer
stroke or energy should be carefully controlled such that significant tensile
stresses do not develop in the pile
Because local variations in soil strength characteristics are likely to exist in the
bearing soils the supporting capacity of each pile must be confirmed in the field
by our office during driving This may be accomplished by using a driving
resistance procedure such as the Engineering News Record ENR formula or the
more sophisticated Wave Equation Analysis to evaluate pile bearing capacity
For preliminary design purposes driving may be terminated when the whole pile
length is driven down to the finished tip elevations or the blow counts steadily
increase with depth and reach 5 blows per inch for the last 6 inches of final pile
penetration or when aggregated blow counts are 40 blows for the last foot
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3013 00 Page 14
Appropriate driving criteria will be developed after selection of the pile driving
equipment
Test Pile Program
A test pile program which includes at least one load test should be undertaken to
fulfill the following objectives
1 To examine the driveability of piles with the use of the selected
hammer
2 To estimate the production pile lengths for each structure
3 To assess the test pile driving criteria which may be modified for use
as the production driving criteria
The pile load test should be performed in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1143 81 loading the test pile to a maximum of 200 percent of the
anticipated design load We recommend that a representative from Geolabs
Hawaii be present during all pile driving operations to observe the actual driving
behavior and to further evaluate the field performance It should be noted that the
pile design and driving criteria recommended were developed from our pile
analysis using the field exploration data Therefore observation of the pile driving
operations by Geolabs Hawaii is necessary to confirm our design assumptions
Obstructions in Fill
There is a possibility that the piles will encounter obstructions in the existing fill
which is generally about five feet 5 thick The piles should either be pre drilled

to a depth of five feet 5 below the existing ground surface or the upper five feet

Foundation Settlements
Settlements of pile foundations will result from the compression of the pile member
and the subgrade response of the bearing older alluvium layer We estimate that
the total settlement of the pile supported foundations will be less than one inch
1 with differential settlements between piles not exceeding about one half that
amount We believe these settlements will be essentially elastic and should occur
as the loads are applied
It is strongly recommended that the production pile driving be observed by
Geolabs Hawaii so that driving resistances tip elevations and axial load bearing
capacity may be confirmed
Buoyant Forces
Due to the high groundwater levels and artesian conditions encountered in our
borings drilled and sampled at the proposed pump station site it is anticipated that the
wastewater pump station will be subjected to buoyant uplift forces when there is no fluid
in the structure In general buoyant uplift forces may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the surface area of the structure and the surrounding soils by dead weight of
the structure or by uplift resistance from the pile foundations
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3013 00 Page 16
Our exploration at the proposed pump station site encountered relatively poor
subsoil conditions extending to depths in excess of 50 feet below the existing ground
surface It is our opinion that these soft subsoils will not develop sufficient frictional
resistance between the structure and the soils to resist the buoyant forces
We also believe that due to the anticipated length of the piles required to support
the structure splicing of the piles may be required Therefore it will generally not be
feasible to develop tensional resistance in the piles
We believe that the structure should be designed to resist uplift forces by dead
weight alone For development of the uplift loading a high water level elevation of 10
feet MSL should be used for design since groundwater levels were observed to be above
the ground surface at the locations of Boring Nos 2 and 3
Wall Design
We anticipate that the walls of the Pump Station and Venturi Pit will be rigid
structures and therefore should be designed to resist at rest pressures The walls should
be designed to resist the lateral earth pressures due to adjacent soils and surcharge
effects The recommended at rest lateral earth pressure for design expressed in
equivalent fluid pressure is 85 pounds per cubic foot p c f for an assumed level backfill
The value provided above is for on site soils or imported granular soils Generally
walls whose tops are not free to deflect beyond about 0 5 percent of the wall height or
are restrained should be designed for the at rest condition This lateral earth pressure
includes hydrostatic pressures
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges line loads and point loads within a
horizontal distance equal to the height of the wall should be considered in the design
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3013 00 Page 17
For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the wall a rectangular
distribution with uniform pressure equal to 45 percent of the surcharge pressure acting
on the entire height of the wall which is restrained at the top may be used in design
Additional analyses during design may be needed to evaluate the surcharge effects of
point loads and line loads

Excavation Support
Because of the soft subsoil conditions and high groundwater levels encountered
in our exploration it is our opinion that continuous excavation support will be required for
the pump station venturi pit underground fuel storage tank and for installation of the
gravity sewer and force main In general we believe that interlocking steel sheet piling
with horizontal bracing may be used It is important that adequate sheet piling be
installed prior to the excavation and maintained tight against the trench walls to reduce
potential lateral movements of the adjacent ground toward the excavation Lateral
movements of the sheetpiles would result in potential settlements or distress to adjacent
structures or other improvements such as roads or utilities
The steel sheet piling should be driven properly with a suitable hammer to a depth
sufficient to provide stability of the excavation bottom and to reduce the possibility of
bottom heaving or quick conditions which may be created by upward seepage pressure
during dewatering Based on our estimate the sheeting should be driven to a minimum
penetration depth of eight feet 8 below the trench bottom along the proposed alignment
of the gravity sewer and force main for this purpose A minimum penetration depth of 25
feet will be required below the base of the pump station excavation to reduce the potential
for bottom heaving Similarly a minimum penetration depth of five feet 5 will be
required below the base of the venturi pit and underground fuel storage tank excavations
A factor of safety of 1 5 is assumed for estimating the design sheet pile penetration
The above recommended sheet pile penetration depth is based on the assumption
that sump pumping inside the excavations is used to dewater the excavations and the
further assumption that there will be no groundwater drawdown outside the excavations
The use of a deep well system outside the excavation to draw down the natural
groundwater level to reduce the required sheet pile embedment is not recommended
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3013 00 Page 19
The sheet piling and bracing system used to support the trenches must comply
with all applicable safety requirements The adequacy and safety of the sheet piling and
bracing installation operations should be made the responsibility of the contractor His
representative who should be required to continually monitor the excavation and
dewatering work at the site would therefore have the opportunity for observing changing
conditions which might affect the excavation stability during construction
For estimating earth loads in the struts of the trench bracing the apparent pressure
diagram presented on Plate 9 may be used as a guide An appropriate safety factor
should be included by the shoring designer
Appropriate loads due to surcharge equipment traffic or sloping ground conditions
adjacent to the excavations should be added to the recommended earth pressures for

Even if good construction procedures are followed some movement of the

stresses during excavation Movements depend greatly on workmanship e g how

depth and width of excavation and the type of support In view of the complexity of the

of a bracing system and the adjacent ground Therefore it is important to realize that the
excavation support should be installed as early as practicable and that measurements of
vertical and horizontal movements are required especially in areas where ground
movements may result in damage to the existing adjacent structures
The ground adjacent to excavations should be continuously monitored for cracks
or dips and other indications of movements and possible sloughing of the excavation

walls Such monitoring is particularly critical in areas adjacent to existing structures and

Excavation Dewatering
Dewatering of the pump station venturi pit underground fuel storage tank and
trench excavations will be necessary since the groundwater levels encountered in our field
exploration were above the proposed pump station venturi pit gravity sewer line and
force main inverts Dewatering for construction is the sole responsibility of the contractor
The dewatering operations should be coordinated with the trench shoring support so that
the stability of the excavations is not jeopardized The operations should be carried out
without softening the bottom of the excavations
It is our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents should
be written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural groundwater table
and to exclude the water from the excavations to permit construction of the proposed
pump station venturi pit and underground fuel storage tank structures and gravity sewer
and force main under safe and dry conditions Therefore these works or systems should
include but not be limited to pumping
Dewatering Method
The method and equipment to be used for the excavation dewatering should be
determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations
We suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in selecting a
suitable method of dewatering
1 The method should result in the least disturbance or damage to the
adjacent existing structures
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3013 00 Page 21
2 The method should maintain stability of and also provide safe and
dry working conditions in the excavations
3 The method should be sufficiently flexible to allow modifications to
accommodate various ground conditions
Dewatering by means of a well point system along the outside of the
excavations is generally not recommended even though this method
could reduce the required sheetpile penetration The resultant areal
depression of the natural groundwater table could induce
consolidation of subsoils resulting in potential ground settlements
affecting the existing roadways and structures
Construction Precautions
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to
dewatering It is also recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be
retained by the contractor to design and evaluate the dewatering system used
The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His qualified representative who should be
required to be continuously present at the site will have the best opportunity to
promptly observe the effects of dewatering during construction and to implement
as soon as possible necessary precautionary or remedial measures including but
not limited to stopping or slowing down the dewatering operations
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3013 00 Page 22
Where encountered at the excavation bottoms permeable granular subsoils may
be susceptible to piping and quick conditions Therefore the project dewatering
operations should be done without pumping out soil fines bumping clear water
only and should be coordinated with shoring installation so that the excavation
stability is not jeopardized The dewatering operations should be carried out
without creating a quick condition or softening at the excavation bottoms
Extra caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering utility trenches that connect
to the excavations The granular bedding and or backfill in the utility trenches
could act as subdrains and cause significant areal groundwater withdrawals which
could induce settlements and cause damage to utility lines and or other existing
adjacent structures
Force Main Installation
Poor soil conditions were encountered near the proposed invert elevations for the
new sewer force main Groundwater levels were encountered above the proposed
anticipated force main invert elevations Therefore proper dewatering trench shoring
with interlocking steel sheet piling and bracing will be needed to reduce lateral ground
movements which could result in potential damage to existing facilities
Earth Pressure Loads on Pipes
Loads on buried conduits are influenced by the width of the trench the size of the
pipes the unit weight of backfill material and the frictional resistance between the
backfill material and the trench walls To calculate the vertical loads on the buried
sewer pipes an average unit weight of 120 p c f for backfill material and a
coefficient of friction of 0 25 are recommended For rigid pipe earth forces upon
the pipe increase rapidly with the width of the trench and therefore the width of
the sewerline trench should be kept to a minimum Flexible pipes deflect under
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3013 00 Page 23
loading and passive earth pressures are mobilized to support the sides of the pipe
while soil arching tends to pick up most of the vertical soil load
Traffic loads on the buried pipes should also be considered for the portions of the
force main located in roadway areas
If the calculated loads on the sewer force main are excessive for rigid pipe the use
of flexible plastic pipe such as PVC or HDPE or jacketing with concrete should
be considered
Pipe Settlements
Primary settlement of new pipe installation is generally caused by the difference in
the unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted
backfill material placed over the sewer structures The net increase in loading may
cause settlement of the underlying subsoils below the trench invert Based on our
exploration and analyses small primary settlements are anticipated for the project
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good work
manship will be provided Additional settlement could occur if improper trench
support or dewatering procedures are allowed
Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a great effect upon the
load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the pipe bedding is an important
design consideration
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3013 00 Page 24
For rigid pipes bearing on stiff to medium dense alluvial deposits a minimum of six
inches 6 of compacted granular bedding material should be placed below the
pipe The granular bedding should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width
of one forth 4 the outside pipe diameter and it also should extend half way up
the pipe sides The bedding material should consist of clean granular material
such as 3B Fine ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation gravel
For flexible pipes in either soft or stiff soils a minimum of 12 inches of clean
granular material should be provided between the pipe and trench walls this
material should be carried up to a height of at least 12 inches above the top of the
pipe
For both rigid and flexible pipes bearing on the soft organic soils encountered at
most locations in our exploration an additional 18 inch layer of clean granular
material contained by a geotextile such as Mirafi 140N or equivalent should be
provided below the pipe and bedding layer to provide more uniform support for the
pipe
Typical sections showing the recommended beddings for rigid and flexible pipes
are presented on Plates 10 and 11 respectively
Backfill
In general trench backfill should be in accordance with the Standard Specifica
tions for Public Works Construction dated September 1986
The first zone of backfill extends from the bedding material to at least 12 inches
above the top of the pipe Select free draining well graded granular material is
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3013 00 Page 25
recommended for the first zone of backfill to reduce the compactive effort required
and resulting stresses on the pipe The granular material should be well graded
less than 1 inch in maximum size and have no more than ten percent passing
No 200 sieve Compaction by water ponding or jetting should not be allowed for
this project
The trench backfill from 12 inches above the top of the pipes to within 2 feet of the
existing ground elevation may consist of excavated on site soils with the exception
of organic soils no rocks or clods greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension
should be permitted in the trench backfill The backfill should be placed in thin lifts
8 inches loose thickness with each lift mechanically compacted to not less than
90 percent of its maximum dry density as determined in accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1557 91 Trenches should be kept free of water during
backfilling
In paved areas within both the City and State Right of Ways the trench backfill up
to a level within 2 feet of the design pavement surface elevation should consist of
select borrow compacted to a minimum 95 percent of its maximum dry density as
determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
Typical pipe trench sections are presented on Plates 10 and 11
Precautions should be taken against the occurrence of pipeline flotation before and
during trench backfilling due to possible influx of large quantities of groundwater
into the trenches The uplift buoyant forces due to groundwater influx may lift
empty or even partially filled water pipes

Joints and Connections
Due to the various subsoil conditions along the proposed sewer line alignment we

Stream Crossings
The proposed force main alignment crosses two streams Haiamoa and Kahaluu
As previously discussed it will be necessary for the force main to cross these streams
by either pipe bridge or inverted siphon
Our exploration indicates that the crossings are underlain by thick deposits of soft
organic soils Therefore deep pile foundations would be required to support pipe
bridges Recommendations for the design of pile foundations for the pipe bridges are
discussed in a preceding section of this report
It should be noted that the 42 inch water main crossing of Kahaluu Stream which
is currently under construction was designed as an inverted siphon This siphon is
designed with an invert elevation of 14 5 feet MSL and is being constructed with a
continuous concrete jacket The siphon is supported by piles embedded to depths of
about 66 to 115 feet below the ground surface
We believe that such a crossing could present significant construction difficulty and
would be relatively expensive to install It is our recommendation that consideration be
given to using directional drilling or micro tunnelling to install the stream crossings
Directional drilling has been employed on the Mainland for the installation of pipelines
across rivers and utilities in congested_areas
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3013 00 Page 27
This technique uses a special drilling rig to navigate the drill string in an inverted
arc under the river or obstructions A small pilot hole is drilled and then reamed to the
proper diameter for installation of the pipe The pipe is prefabricated for the crossing and
pulled through the drilled hole
For this project the use of flexible pipe such as high density polyethylene could
aid in making the stream crossings in relatively small short distances with small radii on
the pipe
Retaining Walls
We understand that some low CMU retaining walls may be constructed for this
project Provided that the retaining walls are supported by the stiff fill soils an allowable
bearing value of 1 500 pounds per square foot p s f may be used for design The
allowable bearing value may be increased by one third for transient loading conditions
such as wind and or seismic forces Lateral forces may be resisted by a combination of
passive earth pressures against vertical or near vertical faces and friction between the
base of structures and the bearing soil A passive pressure corresponding to an
equivalent fluid weighing 300 p c f may be used for design Wall foundations should be
imbedded at least two feet 2 below the lowest adjacent finished grade Unless covered
by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below finished
grade should be neglected The retaining walls should be designed to resist active lateral
earth pressures developed by an equivalent fluid weighing 35 p c f This assumes that
retaining wall backfill is level and that the CMU walls are free to deflect

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling 10 borings to depths
of about 16 5 to 110 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations
shown on the Site Plans Plate 2 A truck mounted rig was used to drill the borings
The materials encountered in the borings were examined and classified in general
accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2488 84 Description and Identification of
Soils Visual Manual Procedure by a geologist from our firm The logs of the borings
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in accordance with ASTM
Standard Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 2 inch standard split spoon sampler or a 3 inch O D split barrel sampler with
a 140 pound hammer free falling 30 inches Soil samples were also obtained by
hydraulically pushing a 3 inch Shelby tube sampler The blow counts and hydraulic
pressures to advance the sampler the last 12 inches are shown on the Boring Logs at the
approximate sample depths
Core samples of soil and rock materials encountered at the site were obtained
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material
left in the annulus of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination classification and
testing
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses This designation is only applicable to cores retrieved from in situ
rock formations RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material
that is in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks if 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core
run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring Logs as RQD
50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was developed to schedule appropriate tests
to aid in determining pertinent engineering properties of the soil and or rock materials
sampled
Classification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Moisture Content ASTM Test Method D 2216 80 and Unit Weight Determinations
are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid in determining certain properties such
as relative saturation The moisture content expressed as a percentage of the dry weight
of the tested sample is shown at the appropriate sample depths on the Boring Logs
Atterberg Limits tests ASTM Test Method D 4318 84 are used to determine the
plasticity of the fine grained portion of soils The Plasticity Index of the material is used
to classify the soil under the Unified Soil Classification System The results of these tests
are shown at the appropriate sample depths on the Boring Logs
Sieve Analysis tests ASTM Test Method D 422 are used to determine the grain
size distribution of soils The distribution is used to classify the soil under the Unified Soil
Classification System and may be correlated to other engineering properties The results
of these tests are shown on Plates B 1 through B 3
Physical Property Tests
Tests are performed to develop a correlative index of certain physical parameters
of a soil These physical parameters may be used in engineering analyses pertaining to
the performance or suitability of a soil
Direct Shear tests ASTM Test Method D 3080 72 are used to determine the angle
of internal friction and the coefficient of cohesion in a soil sample These data are used
in various engineering calculations The result of the direct shear tests are presented on
Plates B 4 and B 5
Consolidation tests ASTM Test Method D 2435 80 are used to evaluate the
consolidation characteristics of the soil The result of the tests are summarized on Plate
B 6 and B 7
