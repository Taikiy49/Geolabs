1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Hapuna Irrigation Well No 4 project for the Mauna Kea
Resort located in the District of South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii The project
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the proposed irrigation
well project only These recommendations are intended for the design of site grading
well pads pavements and thrust blocks only The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The Hapuna Irrigation Well No 4 project is located on the mauka side of the
existing Mauna Kea Resort within the District of South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii
A new irrigation well will be constructed as part of the master water system of the
Mauna Kea Resort The site will be situated about 700 feet north of the existing Hapuna
Irrigation Well No 3 site
The project will include a well site about 1 000 lineal feet of new water lines and
an access road The new water lines and access road will connect Irrigation Well No 3
and Irrigation Well No 4 The access road will traverse the Waiulaula Gulch therefore
a ford crossing is planned for the gulch crossing The majority of the access road will be
paved with asphaltic concrete while the ford crossing will be paved with Portland
cement concrete

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of the following
five shield volcanoes Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea activity during recent geologic
time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both are still active
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield
Volcano which has formed the west central portion of the island The project site
appears to be underlain by basaltic lava flows of the Hamakua Volcanic Series of
Mauna Kea Shield Volcano which were deposited during the Pleistocene Epoch
Stearns and Macdonald 1946
The Hamakua Volcanic Series typically is composed of basaltic lava flows which
are mantled by a relatively thin layer of palagonitized ash locally referred to as
Pahala Ash The volcanic ash soil at the project site differs from Pahala Ash found in
other portions of the Island of Hawaii in that the in situ moisture content is generally
lower and the soil exists in a dry friable state The volcanic ash soils at the site have
been subjected to wind erosion which has resulted in the formation of a desert
pavement on the surface This desert pavement is a surface layer of cobbles and
gravel which were left behind as the finer volcanic ash soils were blown away
The lava formation at the project site appears to be of a a and pahoehoe basalt
type flows which spread and ponded as they approached the ocean A a lava are
typically characterized by a porous rough and irregular flow surface resembling a
jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders Typically a
more dense and layered lava rock material is contained within the lava flow core
Pahoehoe lava is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal
structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe
lavas Cavities form when the lavas are in a molten state and represent both lava tubes
intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes form when

2 2 Site Description
The project site is located to the east mauka of Queen Ka ahumanu Highway
within the Mauna Kea Resort in the District of South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii The
Hapuna Irrigation Well No 4 site is located about 700 feet north of the existing Irrigation
Well No 3 site The project site is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general the project site slopes upward from south to north with localized
undulating topography The existing ground surface elevations at the proposed well site
range from about 727 to 731 feet Mean Sea Level MSL The ground surface at the
well site consists of a graded gravel pad A small stream was observed just south of the
well pad Flowing water from east to west was observed within the stream at the time
of our exploration
The existing ground surface elevations along the proposed access roadway
range from about 700 to 726 feet MSL The proposed access road traverses the
Waiulaula Gulch The gulch generally slopes downward from east to west The
sidewalls and bottom of the gulch consists of basalt formation Flowing water was
observed within the southern portion of the gulch at the time of our exploration
In general the project site is located on vacant undeveloped land situated in a
relatively arid climate The site was lightly vegetated with dried wild grasses and
bushes and a scattering of kiawe trees At the time of our field exploration vehicular
access to the Irrigation Well No 4 project site was made via through an existing gravel
road located on the north side of the proposed well pad

Fills and Backfills
In general we recommend that structural fill materials be used to fill the areas from
the existing ground surface to achieve the design subgrades due to the relatively
minor fills required for the project Structural fill materials should consist of
well graded granular materials generally less than 6 inches in maximum
dimension with sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted
mass The excavated on site materials may be re used as a source of structural
fill materials provided that the materials are processed to meet the maximum
particle size and gradation requirements presented above
If the excavated on site materials do not contain sufficient fines to produce a
well graded material for structural fill off site borrow or on site rock crushing of
large sized rock fragments or boulders should be considered to provide the
required gradation and particle sizes to develop a well graded material
Imported structural fill materials if required should be free of organics and
deleterious materials and should be suitable for the intended use structural fill In
addition Geolabs should observe and or test imported fill materials a minimum of
seven days prior to being transported to the project site for the intended use
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in
loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to
the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum
moisture content is the water content percentage by weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers
vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment

3 2 Concrete Slab On Grade
We envision that a concrete slab on grade would be constructed at the well pad
area Our field exploration at the well pad area encountered a layer of gravelly fill about

Foundations
We anticipate utilizing a shallow foundation system for support of the planned
retaining walls for the culvert structures Based on the subsurface conditions
encountered in our field exploration we envision that the new retaining structures
would probably be supported on shallow foundations such as continuous footings
We recommend providing a 6 inch thick layer of granular bedding consisting of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation material between the bottom of
box culvert and retaining structure foundations and the underlying subgrade
materials to provide for uniform bearing support
We recommend utilizing an allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per
square foot psf for the design of footings This bearing value is for dead plus live
loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those
caused by wind or seismic forces
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by
passive earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation

Drainage
In general retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the build up of
hydrostatic pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide
zone of permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67

As an alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or
EnkaDrain may be used instead of the permeable drainage material The
prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a

3 6 Access Road
The access roadway will mainly consist of an asphaltic concrete pavement
section The ford crossing will consist of a rigid pavement section We envision that the
new access road generally will be used for the maintenance of the water line and
irrigation well Therefore we have assumed that the traffic loading on this access road
would be relatively light with some occasional heavy trucks In addition we have
assumed that the pavement subgrade will consist of volcanic ash and clinker and or fill
materials Based on these assumptions we recommend using the following flexible
pavement design for the project
Flexible Pavement Section
2 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
8 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade
For the design of new ford crossing the following rigid pavement design may be
used for the project
Rigid Pavement Section
6 0 Inch Portland Cement Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Subbase Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
12 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade
Due to the potential presence of flowing water over the ford crossing we
recommend extending that the edges of the concrete ford crossing a minimum of
12 inches below the subbase layer to reduce the potential for excessive migration of
water into the pavement section
The access road subgrades should be proof rolled with a minimum 15 ton
vibratory drum roller for a minimum of eight passes prior to the water line installation
We envision that the access road subgrades may become disturbed during the
installation of the water line In areas where the access road subgrades become
disturbed and loose materials are present at the surface we recommend that additional
proof rolling be conducted with a minimum 10 ton vibratory drum roller for a minimum of
six passes prior to roadway surfacing or the construction of the pavement section

APPENDIX A
Test Boring Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions were explored by drilling and sampling three borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 The borings were advanced to a maximum
depth of about 16 5 to 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with coring tools The approximate boring locations are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of the rock formations encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
