PHASE I
In accordance with your request we have performed a geotechnical
engineering investigation for the above referenced project The
approximate location of the proposed site is shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 1 Our work was done in general accordance with our proposal
of May 9 1986
This report summarizes our findings and recommendations
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of this investigation was to explore the subsurface soil
and water conditions at the site to provide soil parameters for use in the
site grading and foundation design of the proposed development

The scope of our investigation included drilling five 5 shallow
borings along the access road one 1 boring at the pumping station and
one 1 boring along the cold ocean line between the pumping station and
the ocean
Also included were engineering evaluation of the field data
development of design recommendations and report preparation
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration are presented in the
Appendix A of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The pertinent improvements for the Phase I portion of the
development include
1 Widening of the existing access road to the NEIH site and
installation of utilities along the road
2 Construction of a cold water pump station near the shore The
pump station will be a concrete structure approximately 29 by 35
feet in plan and about 32 feet below the existing ground
approximately minus 22 feet below MSL
3 A 28 inch diameter cold ocean water inlet pipe extending from
the pump station to the shoreline

STTE DESCRIPTION
The proposed host park will eventually encompass about 547 acres of
land adjacent to and south of the Keahole Airport and NEIH The site
extends from Queen Kaahumanu Highway to the ocean
The ground surface condition consists of rugged lava flows sloping
down towards the west The ground elevation generally varies from about
120 feet along Queen Kaahumanu Highway to about 8 feet at the pump station
location near the shoreline
Access into the area is by way of an existing paved road which leads
to the NEIH facility
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Boring Nos 1 through 5 were drilled along the existing access road
to depths of 10 to 20 feet below the existing ground surface The borings
encountered hard vesicular basalt with thin clinker zones to the depths
drilled Groundwater was not encountered in Boring 3 and 5
In Boring 4 water level was encountered at 8 feet depth below the
existing ground surface
Boring No 6 was drilled at the pump station site to a depth of 50
feet below the existing ground surface The boring encountered hard
vesicular basalt with thin clinker zones to the depth drilled
Groundwater was encountered at a depth of 7 feet below the existing ground
surface

DISCUSSTON AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The field exploration and site reconnaissance disclosed that the
project area is underlain by lava flows consisting of hard vesicular
basalt with some clinker zones Hard excavation is anticipated for the
utilities and pump station construction
Cold Ocean Water Pump Station
Excavation to about minus 20 feet or about 30 feet from the existing
ground surface is anticipated
Excavation
Hard excavation utilizing hoe rams and probably blasting will be
needed for efficient excavation
The rock formation is generally hard and may be cut at a slope of
about for the pump station construction Some wall supports
may be necessary to prevent loose rocks in the slope from falling
into the excavation
The actual extent of shoring necessary would be affected by
condition of the excavation walls after blasting and the dewatering
method utilized

The excavation will extend about 20 feet below the groundwater
level therefore dewatering is required
Generally low to moderate flows are anticipated in hard intact rock
formation However large volumes of water may be pumped if
cavities or thick clinker layers are encountered
If large voids or cavities are present it may not be practical to
dewater the excavation with normal pumping methods therefore
provisions should be made to include subsurface grouting where very
porous zones or cavities are encountered
Due to the moderate permeability of the subsoils the depth to the
water table and the proposed depth to the excavation floor a deep
well system which is installed deeper than the excavation floor
may be an effective method to dewater the excavation A deep well
system should be capable of pumping relatively large volumes of
water Placing the deep dewatering wells around the perimeter of
the excavation is considered to contribute to a less congested
excavation site Typically a deep well dewatering system consists
of a series of wells installed in holes which have been drilled to a

Lateral Earth Pressure Loads
The lateral pressures that may act on the walls of the pump station
will include hydrostatic pressure as well as earth pressures The
intact rock formation has high strength and would transmit minimal
lateral loads to the wall Therefore lateral soil pressure would
depend on the disturbance of the intact rock by the excavation
blasting and the extent of the backfill behind the wall We have
assumed that the excavation may be relatively large and substantial
backfill will be required Because the finished walls will be
relatively non yielding the earth pressures will be relatively high
at rest condition The recommended earth pressures including
hyrostatic pressures where applicable are as follows

Bearing Capacity and Settlement
The basalt rock formation underlying the pump station have excellent
bearing capacity Also the net loading which is the weight of the
proposed pump station reduced by the weight of the soil excavated is
minimal therefore significant settlements are not anticipated if
proper construction techniques are utilized
Backfill will be required between the pump station walls and the
excavation
Well graded granular material generally less than 6 inches in size
and with less than 20 passing the 200 sieve should be used for the
backfill
The backfill should be compacted to a dry density of at least 90 of
the maximum density determined in accordance with the ASTM D 1557 78
test procedure
Cold Ocean Water Line

water line alignment consists of hard basalt lava flow
Excavation dewatering and backfill recommendations are similar to
those presented for the pump station

Backfill
In general we recommend that well graded granular material such as
sands borken clinker or crusher waste should be used for the
backfill up to about one foot above the pipe to reduce the
compactive effort required and possible damage to the pipe We
believe water tamping can be used to compact the select granular
backfill materials If larger size voids are encountered they
should be backfilled with a choking layer consisting of
intermediate sizes of cobbles and coarse gravel to minimize loss of
fines into the open voids of the basaltic formation
Some of the excavated on site soils less than 8 inches in diameter
can be re used as the upper portion of the trench backfill from
above one foot over the pipe Backfill should be mechanically
compacted to a minimum of 90 of its maximum dry density established
by ASTM D 1557 78

Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the trench excavation be inspected for
cavities or lava tubes and backfill be observed by the soils engineer
during construction to see that adequate compaction has been obtained and
that the subsurface conditions are consistent with the findings of our
investigation
Any imported material required should be non expansive tested and
approved by the soils engineer prior to hauling to the site
Our drilled borings did not encountered large voids such as cavities
or lava tubes If actual exposed rock soil conditions encountered during
construction are different from those presented in this report then
appropriate modifications to the design should be made
INVESTIGATION LIMITATIONS
Our services consist of professional opinions and recommendations
made in accordance with generally accepted soil and foundation engineering
principles and practices This warranty is in lieu of all other
warranties either expressed or implied
The analysis and recommendations submitted in this report are based
on soil information derived from boring data obtained from this site
investigation
Unanticipated soil conditions are commonly encountered and cannot be
fully determined by taking soil samples and test borings These
unforeseen soil conditions such as hard layers or cavities may occur in
localized areas and may require additional probing or corrections to
attain a properly constructed project Some contingency fund is thus
recommended to accommodate these possible costs
