2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built up during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene
Epoch by the activities of two basaltic shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau Koolau
Volcano is the younger of the two volcanoes and reached the end of its main shield
building phase about 2 million years ago Koolau Volcano was built by the extrusion of
basaltic lava from northwest to southeast trending rift zones These lava flows are
interbedded pahoehoe and a a flows with pahoehoe being the dominant form After the
cessation of the main shield building phase of Koolau Volcano there was a long period
of erosion
During the Pleistocene Epoch there was a renewal of volcanic activity with the
eruptions of vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series Along the crest of the ridge on the
westerly side of Manoa Valley there are the Tantalus Roundtop and Sugarloaf vents
When these vents were active one or more lava flows from these vents spilled down
the westerly wall of the valley and moved across the valley floor The lava flows ponded
and filled the lower portions of the valley The ponding allowed the lava to densify as it
cooled forming dense massive rock
Other eruptive events of the Honolulu Volcanic Series mantled these lava flows
with pyroclastic material particularly sand sized cinder materials Subsequent fluvial
action has transported minor amounts of alluvial soils onto the site During the
residential housing development man made fills were placed to grade the site to its
present condition

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Imported fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Imported fill and backfill materials
under the footings should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density Compaction should be accomplished by sheeps foot rollers vibratory
rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling two borings extending to depths of approximately 1 8 and 20 1 feet below the
existing ground surface The borings were drilled at the approximate locations shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with hollow stem augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and
Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration
Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained by using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollew bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
