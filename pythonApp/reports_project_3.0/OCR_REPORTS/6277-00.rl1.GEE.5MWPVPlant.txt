Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
the Waianae and Koolau Shield Volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be
late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age As volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano
ceased lava flows from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope
forming a broad plateau now known as the Schofield Plateau The project site is on the
southwestern flank of the Waianae Volcano
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of the Schofield Plateau
generated sediments which were transported to the coast These sediments
accumulated and interbedded with marine sediments and coral algal reef formations to
form a sedimentary wedge The thickness of the sedimentary wedge ranges from zero
in the area of the Interstate Route H 1 Highway to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This
wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation or caprock over
the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu Deposition of sediments has continued
from earlier geologic time through the present
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea
When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits
at correspondingly higher elevations and deposited lagoonal and or marine sediments
in the quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to
carve valleys in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments
and calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration
of the calcareous reef materials Placement of man made fills and commercial

Site Description
We understand a new power plant is desired at the project site in the
Campbell Industrial Park area of Kapolei on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project site is
bounded by the Tesoro Refinery to the west and south and a drainage canal runs along

road measuring about one quarter of a mile long runs in the east west direction across
the middle of the site and terminates at the canal

the site is accessible only via the existing gravel road and the gravel road was completely

Soil Survey
Soil survey maps were also used to evaluate the existing subsurface conditions
at the project site The soil survey maps reviewed were obtained from the publication
entitled Soil Survey of Islands of Molokai and Lanai State of
Hawaii issued August 1972 by the U S Department of Agriculture Soil Conservation
Service Description of the soil classification is described as follows
Coral outcrop consists of coral or cemented calcareous sand formed in shallow
ocean water during the time the ocean stand was at a higher level It is exposed on the
ocean shore on the coastal plains and at the foot of the uplands Coral outcrop makes
up about 80 to 90 percent of the acreage The remaining 10 to 20 percent consists of a
thin layer of friable red soil material in cracks crevices and depressions within the
coral outcrop

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The project involves constructing a Power Plant which will occupy
approximately 20 acres of undeveloped land at the Campbell Industrial Park One of
the current design concepts involves constructing self supporting self ballasted concrete
strip footings to support the PV panels In addition two other foundation schemes
involving shallow concrete drilled piers and steel ground screws are under consideration
as alternatives for the project
Based on our field exploration the project site is covered by heavy vegetation
with relatively tall kiawe trees Our borings indicate that the project site is underlain by
variable thickness of topsoil overlying highly variable coral algal limestone Numerous
sinkholes also were observed throughout the project site In addition cavities and or
voids with relatively thin roof cover were observed during our field exploration
It should be noted that the strength characteristics of coralline materials are
highly variable In addition the depositional nature of the coralline limestone formation
may create localized areas of hard crystalline limestone formation intermixed with soft
severely fractured coralline detritus debris Furthermore it should be noted that
limestone formation frequently is cavernous or contain pockets or voids as encountered
in some of the drilled borings across the project site
To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of
cavities and or voids below the future PV panels we recommend conducting a program
of ripping the existing ground to at least 3 feet below the finished subgrade elevation
and proof rolling with a minimum 10 ton vibratory drum roller for a minimum eight
passes to detect the presence of near surface cavities and or loose zones
We encountered groundwater in the drilled borings at depths varying from
approximately 7 3 to 16 3 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field
exploration Based on the existing site conditions and the above discussions we
recommend utilizing the following geotechnical recommendations for design of foundations
and site preparation

Foundations
As mentioned previously we understand the gravity loads imposed on the
foundations by the PV panels are relatively light Design of the foundation system is
governed primarily by resistance to uplift forces acting on the PV panels caused by
wind Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site and the
relatively light gravity loads acting on the foundations we believe the new PV panels for
the power plant may be supported on strip footings In addition we also evaluated the
concrete drilled pier and steel ground screw options Detailed discussions and
recommendations for foundation designs are presented in the following subsections
Shallow Strip Footings
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we recommend an allowable
bearing pressure of up to 2 000 pounds per square foot psf for design of the
strip footings bearing on the prepared ground surface minimal embedment
depth This bearing value is for supporting dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces
We anticipate the new strip footing precast or cast in place concrete will be
placed directly over the compacted subgrade with minimum embedment below
the ground surface Therefore dead weight of the panel structures and the
weight of the concrete strip footings may be used to provide the required uplift
load resistance In addition drainage of the ground surface around the footings
should be controlled properly to reduce the potential for erosion and undermining
of the footings long term
Typically foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded
below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward
from the bottom edge of the utility trench or the footing should be extended to a
depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines
If the foundations are designed and constructed in strict accordance with our
recommendations we estimate the total settlements of the foundations to be less

be used in the design This value includes the weight of the drilled pier and
should be used for transient loads only
For sustained uplift loads acting on the foundations the provided uplift load
capacities should be reduced by a factor of safety of 2 0 The project structural
engineer should check the structural capacity of the shaft member in tension
Settlement of the drilled pier foundation will result from elastic compression of the
shaft and subgrade response of the foundation embedded in the soils
encountered at the site Total settlements of the drilled piers are estimated to be
on the order of 0 5 inches and the potential differential settlements between
adjacent drilled piers may be on the order of 0 25 inches
The performance of the drilled pier will depend largely upon the contractor s method
of construction and construction procedures As a result a Geolabs representative
should be present to monitor the installation of the drilled piers during construction
In our opinion the following may have a significant impact on the effectiveness and
cost of the drilled pier foundations
The load carrying capacity of the drilled pier depends to a large extent on
the friction between the shaft and the surrounding soil Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care
in drilling or excavating the shaft hole and in placing concrete in the hole
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site some
loose cohesionless materials will likely be encountered during the pier

Some voids and clayey silty soil pockets were encountered within the

still may be encountered during the pier drilling Therefore some

Ground Screws
We understand that a steel ground screw foundation system has been
successfully used in similar applications in other locales and is being considered
for this project site Based on the product details provided we understand the
steel ground screw system involves a vertical steel pipe with a diameter of
approximately 3 inches The steel pipe is equipped with continuous auger flights
and is about 5 to 6 feet long with a sharp end
In general a special rig will be used to screw the pipe with the auger flights into
the ground The steel pipe will displace the soil and squeeze the soil around
Passive earth pressures will be built up to lock the screw in place This
foundation system benefits from minimal excavation work minimal concrete
work and the screw would be in service immediately after installation Based on
the manufacturer s literature the steel ground screw foundation system can be
installed into all soil types including rock
Our experience with a similar type of foundation system installed in a coralline
ground environment does not provide the required capacity to resist the uplift

We anticipate some underground utility trenches might be needed for the new

Free draining granular materials such as open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67

Field Resistivity Survey and Corrosion Protection
Geophysical survey equipment i e ground resistance testing equipment was
mobilized to the project site for the field resistivity survey A total of 24 resistivity tests
were conducted at two selected locations designated as R 1 and R 2 using the in situ
Wenner 4 Pin Method and an AEMC Model 4610 ground resistance testing equipment
manufactured by AEMC Instruments The method utilized four surface probes or pins
electrodes to evaluate the average resistivity of the underlying mass The average
resistivity values of the subsurface mass extending from the existing ground surface to
the test depth was assessed by using this method along the two test reference lines
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The electrical resistivity survey test results are
presented on Plate C 1 in Appendix C
The in situ resistivity surveys were performed at the existing ground surface
along the reference lines shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 At the R 1 location
two perpendicular directions North South and East West were tested while only the
East West direction was tested at the R 2 location due to the property boundary
constraints The field resistivity tests were performed at various electrode spacings
ranging from 2 to 80 feet In general the survey was conducted with electrodes placed
as far as practicable limited by the field conditions sloping terrain uneven ground
surface heavy vegetation etc The average soil resistance along the proposed
reference lines was evaluated at each survey location with the electrodes aligned at
equal spacings as shown on Plate C 1 It should be noted that the depth of penetration
of a resistivity sounding is a function of the electrode spacing
The ground resistance testing equipment AEMC Model 4610 provides a voltage
between the outer electrodes causing an electrical current to flow between them The
voltage drop between the inner electrodes is then measured by a sensitive built in
voltmeter As a result the soil resistance in ohms is obtained from the voltmeter dial

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
14 borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 14 The borings generally extended to
depths of about 14 to 17 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring
locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous full time basis These classifications were further reviewed visually
and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with
ASTM D 2487 Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1
Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of
Borings Plates A 1 through A 14
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some samples
were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D 1586
Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths The
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows
required for the specific sampler type used The blow counts may need to be factored to
obtain the Standard Penetration Test SPT blow counts
Core samples of the coralline materials encountered at the project site were
obtained by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the formation The material left in the hollow core
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description Rock cores were
