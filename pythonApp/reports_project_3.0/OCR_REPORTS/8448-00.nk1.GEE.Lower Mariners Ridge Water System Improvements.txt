SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered a pavement structure consisting of 
about 2 to 6 inches of AC overlying about 3 to 24 inches of coralline fill material. Four 
borings encountered a pavement structure consisting of about 6 inches of PCC pavement 
directly overlying fill material or the in-situ residual soil. Beneath the pavement structure, 
fill material consisting of medium stiff to hard clayey/sandy silt and silty clay was 
encountered to depths of about 1.5 to 8 feet below the existing pavement surface. 
Residual soil and saprolite, consisting of stiff to hard clayey silt/silty clay and medium 
dense to dense sand/gravel, respectively, were encountered to depths of about 2 to 11.5 
feet below the existing ground surface. Basalt rock formation was encountered in 14 of 
our borings at depths of about 0.75 feet (directly beneath the pavement) to about 10 feet 
and extended to the maximum depth explored of about 11.5 feet. The basalt rock 
formation was generally severely to moderately fractured, moderately weathered, and 
medium hard to hard. Weathered basalt clinker was encountered in some of our borings 
and generally consisted of medium to very dense silty sand.  
We encountered groundwater in one of our borings at a depth of about 6.6 feet 
below the existing ground surface, corresponding to an elevation of about +356.4 feet 
MSL. Based on our field observations and the geology of the area, we believe this may 
have been a localized area of perched groundwater. However, it should be noted that 
groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal 
precipitation, surface water runoff, perched groundwater, and other factors. 
We anticipate that the new water main inverts will be about 4 to 12 feet below the 
existing pavement surface. However, deeper excavations may be required where the new 
water main traverses under existing utilities. Installation of the proposed water mains will 
involve excavation into the underlying fills, residual soil, saprolite, weathered clinker, 
cobbles and boulders, and hard basalt formation. In general, we anticipate that the in-situ 
soils along the proposed water main alignments may be excavated with normal heavy 
excavation equipment. However, trench excavations into the underlying basalt rock 
formation and/or cobbles and boulders may require chipping and/or the use of hoerams 
to remove the materials and to achieve the design invert elevations. 
Where deep excavations (greater than 5 feet in depth) are planned, temporary 
shoring or sloping and benching should be implemented for the trench excavations. 
Based on our site observations, we believe that temporary cut slopes for open cut 
excavations are not feasible. Therefore, trench walls would have to be cut near vertical, 
necessitating the use of temporary shoring during construction.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Lower Mariners Ridge Water System Improvements project in Honolulu 
on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering 
analyses for the water system improvements project. These recommendations are 
intended for the design of trench excavation, backfilling, pavement restoration, thrust 
block/vault design, and corrosion potential only. The findings and recommendations 
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The Lower Mariners Ridge Water System Improvements project is located in the 
lower portion of the Mariners Ridge development east of Hahaione Valley on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii.  The project is part of the Mariners Ridge 500 system. The project consists 
of the installation of approximately 9,041 linear feet of waterline replacement with pipe 
diameters of 4, 8, and 12 inches along the following roadways: Kaluanui Road, Kaloaloa 
Street, Kaahue Street, Kawaiki Place, Kauku Place, Kapoho Place, Kapapala Place, Kaohe 
Place, Kamoi Place, Kakiwa Place, Kakapa Place, Kaipuhaa Place, Kahuwai Place, 
Kahauloa Place, Kaluanui Place, and Kaluanui Way. The scope of work requires that the 
system be designed in accordance with the Honolulu Board of Water Supply (BWS) 
standards, including corrosion control. 
We understand that the corrosion testing should be performed at every 500-foot 
interval instead of 300-foot interval specified in the Standards. For this project, a minimum 
of about 30 tests would be required. The invert elevations of the waterlines were not 
available at the time this report was prepared. However, we anticipate that the new 
waterline pipe invert will be about 4 feet from the existing ground surface.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Volcano, volcanic activity returned with a 
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are referred 
to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. The project site is located on the southern flank of the 
Koolau Mountain Range. 
The project area is located on Mariners Ridge and is, therefore, generally underlain 
by basaltic rock built by extrusion of the lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series. These rocks 
are generally characterized by flows of jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded 
thin clinker layers. In-situ chemical weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred in 
the last 1 to 2 million years. The weathering process has formed a mantle of residual and 
saprolitic soils. In general, saprolite is composed mainly of silty material, while residual 
soils are more clayey. Both residual and saprolitic soils are typical of the tropical 
weathering of volcanic rocks. The residual and saprolitic soils grade to a basaltic rock 
formation with increased depth. 
2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is located in the lower portion of the Mariners Ridge development 
east of Hahaione Valley. Within the project limits, most of the roadways are paved with 
asphaltic concrete (AC) pavement. However, the northern portion of Kaluanui Road and 
the southern portion of Kaahue Street are paved with Portland cement concrete (PCC) 
pavement, likely due to the steep grade of the road. The remaining roads within the project 
limits branch out from these two main roads and end at cul-de-sacs.  
In general, the topography of the project site is relatively steep, sloping downward 
from north to south. Roadway inclinations range from nearly level on the branching 
residential streets to about 14 percent on Kaluanui Road and Kaahue Street. Based on 
the topographic survey map provided, the existing top-of-pavement elevations range from

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The proposed waterline alignment will traverse roadways underlain by fill, residual 
soil, saprolite, weathered clinker, and basalt rock formation. Cobbles were encountered 
within the residual soil and saprolitic soil layers. Basalt rock formation was encountered 
in 14 of our borings at depths of about 0.75 feet (directly beneath the pavement) to about 
10 feet and extended to the maximum depth explored of about 11.5 feet. We encountered 
groundwater in one of our borings at a depth of about 6.6 feet below the existing ground 
surface. Based on our field observations and the geology of the area, we believe this may 
have been a localized area of perched groundwater. 
We understand that the construction of the water mains will generally consist of 
trench excavation, pipe placement, trench backfill, and pavement restoration. Detailed 
discussions for the design of the water system improvements project, including trench 
excavation, trench excavation support, backfilling, pavement restoration, thrust 
block/vault design, corrosion protection, and other geotechnical aspects of the project, 
are presented in the following sections. 
3.1 
Trench Excavation 
Based on the information provided, we understand that the invert elevations of the 
new waterlines will be about 4 to 12 feet below the existing pavement surface. Installation 
of the proposed water mains will involve excavation into the underlying fills, residual soil, 
saprolite, weathered clinker, and medium hard to hard basalt rock formation. In general, 
we anticipate that the in-situ soils along the proposed water main alignments may be 
excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment. However, it should be noted that 
cobbles were encountered in the residual and saprolitic soils, and the hard basalt 
formation was encountered close to the existing ground surface in multiple borings. 
Therefore, excavations extending through cobbles and boulders and into basalt rock 
formation within the trench depths should be anticipated. Trench excavations into the 
basalt rock formation and through cobbles and boulders may require chipping and/or the 
use of hoerams to remove the materials and achieve the design invert elevations. The 
contractor must exercise care to avoid over-ripping, which would disrupt the structure of

3.2 
Trench Excavation Support 
We anticipate that excavations up to about 4 to 12 feet below the pavement 
surface will be required for the installation of the new water mains and manhole structures. 
Deeper excavations may be required where the water mains traverse existing 
underground utilities. Where deeper excavations (greater than 5 feet in depth) are 
planned, temporary shoring or sloping and benching should be implemented for the 
trench excavations. Based on our site observations, we believe that temporary cut slopes 
for open cut excavations are not feasible. Therefore, trench walls would have to be cut 
near vertical, necessitating the use of temporary shoring during construction. 
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable 
safety requirements. The contractor should be solely responsible for the adequacy and 
safety of the shoring installation. The contractor’s representative should be on-site at all 
times during excavation and construction work for the opportunity to promptly observe 
changing or unforeseen conditions, such as high groundwater, inappropriate construction 
sequence or techniques, etc., which may affect the shoring stability. 
Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal 
to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential for 
excessive ground movement. 
It is important to install adequate shoring and to maintain it tight against the 
excavation walls with proper bracing during construction. The properly braced shoring is 
essential to reduce the potential for appreciable lateral movements of the adjacent ground 
into the excavation, which may result in potential settlement or distress to adjacent 
structures or other improvements. It should be noted that relatively thick layers of granular

3.3 
Pipe Cushion 
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the 
load supporting capacity of the pipe. Therefore, the pipe cushion is an important design 
consideration. For pipes bearing on the underlying fills, residual soil, saprolite, weathered 
clinker, or basalt rock formation, a minimum of 6 inches of granular cushion material, such 
as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation) should be provided below the pipe. 
Where soft and/or loose soils are encountered at or near the invert of the pipes, a 
stabilization layer consisting of an additional 18 inches of open-graded gravel, such as 
No. 3B Fine gravel, wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) 
should be provided below the cushion layer for uniform support. 
The granular cushion layer should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width 
of one-fourth (¼) of the outside pipe diameter or trench width, whichever is greater and 
should extend 12 inches above the pipe. Before the placement of cushion material, a 
Geolabs representative should observe the excavated trench bottom to confirm that firm 
materials are exposed at the bottom of the trench or whether a stabilization layer should 
be provided below the cushion layer.

3.4 
Trench Backfill 
In general, the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the 
Water System Standards, State of Hawaii 2002. The first zone of backfill extending from 
the cushion material to at least 12 inches above the top of the pipes (or 12 inches above 
the groundwater level, whichever is greater) should consist of select free-draining 
granular material (ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation) to reduce the compaction effort required 
and resulting stresses on the pipe. In addition, it is critical to use this free-draining material 
to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide 
adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill could result in backfill 
settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the pavement subgrade should consist of the excavated on-site soils or imported fill 
materials that are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, organic material, and clay 
lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension. It should be 
noted that the fill requirements presented herein are intended as guidelines only and may 
be modified based on Geolabs additional laboratory testing and field observations on the 
available fill materials. 
Imported fill materials used for trench backfill should be well-graded from coarse 
to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in the largest dimension. The material should 
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material 
should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher and a swell 
potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. Geolabs 
should test the imported fill materials for conformance with these recommendations. 
As an alternative, to facilitate the backfilling and reduce the time required for 
applying compactive effort, Controlled Low Strength Material (CLSM) may be used to 
backfill the trenches. The use of CLSM would also reduce the potential for future 
settlement of the trench backfill under the repeated wheel loading from traffic on the 
roadway.

3.5 
Trench Backfill Compaction and Placement Requirements 
Backfill materials consisting of the on-site soils should be moisture-conditioned to 
at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches 
in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. The 
upper 3 feet below the pavement grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction. 
Imported fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture, placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Aggregate base course and subbase 
materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed 
in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of 
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established 
in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by 
dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or 
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or ponding 
should not be allowed. 
Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during backfilling to 
assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper 
moisture content. Where compaction is less than required, additional compactive effort 
should be applied with adjustment of moisture content as necessary to obtain the 
specified compaction.

3.9 
Settlements 
Primary settlement of the water mains is normally caused by the difference in the 
unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted backfill 
material placed over the pipes. The net increase in loading will cause settlement of the 
underlying subsoils below the trench invert. Based on our engineering analyses, 
settlement on the order of less than ½ inch is anticipated for the project. 
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good 
workmanship will be engaged during construction. Additional settlement could occur if 
improper trench support is used. 
3.10 Corrosion Potential 
Design of metallic substructures, such as waterline pipes or other metallic piping, 
should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure. 
Twenty-eight sets of laboratory corrosion tests, including pH and minimum resistivity, 
were performed by our office to evaluate the corrosivity of the on-site soils encountered. 
The test results are summarized on Plates B-5 and B-6 in Appendix B. Detailed results of 
the Chloride Content (EPA 300.0) and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0) performed by 
Eurofins TestAmerica are presented in Appendix D. 
Based on the updated version of the BWS, City and County of Honolulu Water 
System External Corrosion Control Standards dated July 28, 2021, there are two soil 
corrosivity categories. Category A (Moderately to Severely Corrosive) applies to soil 
meeting any one of the following conditions. Corrosion Category B (Negligibly to Mildly 
Corrosive) is applicable when none of the following conditions are met. The updated 
version of the standards is effective as of the publishing date for Oahu only. 
• Resistivity ≤ 5,000 ohm-cm 
• pH ≤ 6.5 
• Water-soluble chloride concentration ≥ 500 ppm 
• Water-soluble sulfate concentration ≥ 1,000 ppm 
• Within fluctuating saltwater table

Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
regarding the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the 
resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to the corrosion 
of metallic objects. Results of the laboratory testing indicate that the on-site soils have 
resistivity values of about 200 to 3,800 ohm-cm with pH values of about 6.5 to 10.1. 
Therefore, the on-site near-surface soils may be considered as Corrosion Category A 
(Moderately to Severely Corrosive) based on the updated BWS External Corrosion 
Control Standards dated July 28, 2021. It should be noted that there was not enough soil 
material recovered from our samples to perform resistivity testing at Boring Nos. 16, 17, 
22, and 25. 
In addition, chloride content and sulfate content tests were performed by Eurofins 
TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. to evaluate the corrosivity of the on-site soils encountered. 
Based on the chloride and sulfate content tests performed on the on-site soils, the test 
values are generally relatively low. It may be appropriate to consult with a professional 
corrosion engineer to review the test results and provide detailed recommendations for 
corrosion protection.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling 30 borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 30, extending to depths of about 7.5 to 11.5 feet 
below the existing pavement surface at the approximate locations shown on the Overall 
Site Plan, Plate 2, and Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.4. We used a truck-mounted drill 
rig equipped with continuous flight augers to advance the borings. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock samples were described in general 
accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate 
A-0.3. Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs 
of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-30. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, Penetration 
Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration 
sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler 
the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the

Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. 
For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any 
drilling, mechanical, and handling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material 
is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Five Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The graphic presentation of the test 
results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Five Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. The graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on 
Plate B-2. 
To evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the on-site clayey soil, one 
Unconfined Compression test was performed on a selected soil sample in accordance 
with ASTM D2166.  The test results are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depth. The stress-strain curve of the unconfined compression test is presented 
on Plate B-3. 
Seventeen Uniaxial Compression tests (ASTM D7012 Method C) were performed 
on selected rock cores to evaluate their unconfined compressive strength of the rock 
formation encountered.  Results of the unconfined compression tests are presented on 
Plate B-4. 
Twenty-eight sets of pH (ASTM G51) and Minimum Resistivity (ASTM G57) tests 
were performed by our office, and Chloride (EPA 300) and Sulfate Content (EPA 300) 
tests were performed by TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on selected soil samples 
obtained from our field exploration. The test results are presented on Plates B-5 and B-6. 
Four laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-7 through B-10.
