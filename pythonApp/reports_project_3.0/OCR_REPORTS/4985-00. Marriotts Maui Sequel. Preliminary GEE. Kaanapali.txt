2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Maui is comprised of two major volcanoes the older Tertiary Epoch
West Maui and the more recent Pleistocene Epoch East Maui also known as
Haleakala Pleistocene Epoch The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow gently sloping plain
located between these two volcanoes The project site is located near the northern end of
the island or on the lower northern slope of the East Maui Volcano Haleakala
Haleakala was generally built in three distinguishable series of lava flows The
lower unit the Honomanu Volcanic Series late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Epoch
consists of thinly bedded basaltic pahoehoe and a a lava These lava tend to be very
permeable and carry basal water Overlying this unit is the Kula Volcanic Series early
Pleistocene Epoch composed primarily of thicker alkalic a a flows which may contain
many inter stratified thin ash soil layers A long quiescent period followed during which
time canyons were carved in the volcano although a few eruptions may have occurred
during this erosion interval in addition to erosion due to eustatic changes higher and lower
stands of the sea level At the end of the rest period copious flows erupted along the
southwest and east rifts This is the third and upper rock unit known as the Hana Volcanic
Series Pleistocene Epoch The lava range in composition from ultra basic olivine
porphyries to non porphyritic alkalic basalts
The project site is located in an area associated with the
association This association consists of deep nearly level to moderately sloping
well drained and excessively drained soils that have a moderately textured subsoil or
underlying material

2 4 Seismic Design Considerations
Based on the 1997 Uniform Building Code UBC seismic zonation current code
used by the County of Maui the Island of Maui is located within Seismic Zone 2B with an
effective peak ground acceleration of 0 20g Therefore the project site may be subjected
to seismic activity and seismic design considerations will need to be addressed for the
project The following sections provide discussions on the seismicity of the Island of Maui
the soil profile for seismic design at the project site and the potential for liquefaction at the
site
Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by shifts
in the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before
or during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not
erupt The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but

Hawaii California

most of the earthquakes are so small that they can only be detected by
instruments However some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and
a few cause minor to moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii over
90 percent of earthquakes are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic
activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the
Island of Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5 M5 however earthquakes are not confined only to the
Island of Hawaii
To a lesser degree the Island of Maui has experienced numerous earthquakes

Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and a design peak ground
acceleration of 0 20g liquefaction may occur resulting in appreciable settlements to
the ground surface Therefore the effects of potential liquefaction should be taken
into consideration in the design of the proposed development
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess
pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by
earthquakes In this process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected
to vibration such as during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease
in volume causing an increase in pore water pressure If drainage is unable to
occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build up of pore water pressure the effective
stress internal strength of the soil is reduced Under sustained vibrations the
pore water pressure build up could equal the overburden pressure essentially
reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid
During liquefaction the soil acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal
and vertical movements and if not confined will result in significant deformations
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion It is generally acknowledged that
liquefaction may not occur if the deposit is greater than 40 to 50 feet below the
ground surface In deeper deposits the greater overburden pressure is generally
sufficient to prevent liquefaction from occurring The major factors affecting the
liquefaction characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows
1 Grain Size Distribution Fine and uniform sands and silts are more
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2 Initial Relative Density Loose sands and silts are most susceptible to
liquefaction Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to relative
density
3 Magnitude and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is directly
proportional to the magnitude and duration of the earthquake

3 1 Pile Foundations
As mentioned previously the project site could be subjected to appreciable ground
settlements in the event of liquefaction caused by a moderate seismic event M6
Seismically induced ground settlements on the order of about 3 to 4 inches may be
anticipated and the buildings may sustain some damage that would disrupt the operation
of the resort facility To reduce the potential for appreciable damage to the building
structures resulting from soil liquefaction during an earthquake we recommend that the
proposed building structures be supported on a deep foundation system consisting of
driven piles
Based on the subsurface conditions at the site we recommend that 16 5 inch
octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles be used to support the proposed building
and parking structures The driven piles would consist of end bearing piles bearing on the
underlying basalt rock formation encountered between about 100 and 110 feet below the
ground level at the Lahaina Building and between about 155 and 175 feet below the
ground surface at the Napili Building
The 16 5 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles may be designed with
an allowable compressive load capacity of up to 100 tons per pile The allowable
compressive load capacity is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third
1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces In general piles
should be spaced a minimum of 3 5 feet from center to center to avoid reduction in vertical
capacity due to group action

We anticipate that splicing of pile sections would be required to extend the driven

In the event of seismically induced liquefaction settlement of the loose sandy
deposits will occur at the site When the loose lagoonal deposits move downward relative
to the pile the downward movement will result in negative skin friction forces generally
known as downdrag forces acting on the pile shaft The magnitude of the downdrag force
depends on the thickness of the liquefiable layer relative density of the potentially
liquefiable lagoonal deposits and the pile perimeter Due to the potential for liquefaction of
the loose lagoonal deposits at the site settlement of the loose lagoonal deposits will result
in downdrag loading of the deep foundations extending through the potentially liquefiable
materials Therefore the allowable compressive load capacities of the piles will need to
be reduced by the expected downdrag loads in the pile foundation analyses Based on
our foundation analyses we estimate that the downdrag loads imposed on the vertical
piles due to the potential for liquefaction of the loose sand and gravel deposits may be on
the order of about 50 kips per pile The allowable compressive load capacity of up to
100 tons per pile recommended above has already taken into account these downdrag
loads

Uplift Load Resistance
Uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the dead weight of the driven pile
and shear along the pile surface and the adjacent soils In general an uplift load
capacity of up to 30 kips per pile may be used for preliminary design The uplift
load capacities provided above include the weight of the piles The uplift load
capacity of the piles provided is intended for transient load applications Therefore
the uplift load capacities should not be further increased for transient load
applications We recommend that the uplift load capacity provided above be
reduced by 50 percent to resist sustained uplift loads The capacity of the pile
member in tension should be checked by the project structural engineer
Lateral Load Resistance
Lateral loads imposed on the structures may be resisted by a combination of the
lateral load capacity of the driven piles and the passive pressure acting against the
vertical faces of the pile caps grade beams Passive earth pressure against the
vertical faces of the pile cap grade beam may be estimated using an equivalent
fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf above the
groundwater level and 150 pcf below the groundwater level The groundwater level
at the site may be assumed to be at about Elevation 3 feet MSL

and the induced moment in the pile The lateral load analyses were performed

a finite difference laterally loaded pile program originally developed at the

Pile Foundation Settlements
Settlements of the pile foundations will result primarily from elastic compression of
the pile member and subgrade response We estimate the total settlement of the
pile supported foundations to be less than 0 75 inches with differential settlements
between columns supported on piles not exceeding about 0 5 inches We believe
that these settlements are essentially elastic and should occur as the loads are
applied
Pile Hammer Selection
In general we recommend that the piles be driven with a hammer capable of
delivering a minimum rated energy of 32 000 foot pounds in the field The hammer
should be equipped with an energy level control such that driving within the
soft loose soils may be controlled at a lower energy to reduce the potential for
tensile stress development which could damage or cause cracking of the piles
Selection of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the matching of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be
driven This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy
to the top of the pile and also reducing the potential for damage to the piles The
project geotechnical engineer should review the pile driving equipment proposed to
evaluate whether the equipment is capable of installing the piles to the
recommended capacity The contractor should forward pile and driving equipment
data forms to the project geotechnical engineer prior to construction for evaluation

controlled such that significant tensile stresses do not develop within the pile

Construction Considerations
The performance of drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor s
method of construction and construction procedures As a result of these potential
variations we recommend that a qualified geotechnical engineer be present to
observe the installation of drilled shafts during construction In our opinion the
following may have a significant impact on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled
shaft foundations
Based on our field exploration the project site is underlain by loose beach lagoonal
deposits below water extending to depths of over 100 feet below the existing
ground surface Due to the granular nature of these materials there is a potential
for caving in of the materials during the drilling operations To reduce the potential

Workmanship
The load carrying capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in
drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids is generally not recommended Because
of the groundwater table anticipated within the depths of the drilled shaft
excavations concrete placement by tremie method will be required during
construction of the drilled shafts under such conditions The concrete should be
placed in a suitable manner by displacing the water in an upward fashion from the
bottom of the drilled hole A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 7 to 9 inch
slump range should be used to provide close contact between the drilled shafts

3 3 Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that the floor slab for the proposed building construction will consist
of concrete slabs on grade Based on the existing topography and the anticipated design
finished floor elevation we envision that the slab on grade for the proposed structures will
be supported on the existing ground or shallow new compacted fills placed to raise the
existing ground surface to the finished subgrades at the building pad area
As mentioned previously the project site could be subjected to appreciable ground
settlements in the event of liquefaction caused by a moderate seismic event M6
Seismically induced ground settlements on the order of about 3 to 4 inches may be
anticipated and the building interior may sustain some damage that would disrupt the
operation of the facility Therefore there exists the potential for damage and distress to
the building slabs on grade under the driven pile foundation support system However we
believe that the potential for damage may be significantly reduced by increasing the
building slab thickness and by incorporating steel reinforcement into the concrete slab
As discussed above concrete slabs on grade may be subjected to ground
settlements and distress in the event of seismically induced soil liquefaction As an
alternative to slabs on grade consideration may be given to structurally supporting the
ground floor slab on the pile foundations to reduce the potential for damage to the interior
of the building in the event of seismically induced ground settlements
New fills needed to raise the site to the design grades should be placed in general
accordance with the recommendations provided in the Site Preparation section of this
report New fills needed to raise the site to the finished subgrades will induce ground
settlements as a result of consolidation of the underlying loose sand deposits Therefore
we recommend that the new fills be placed as soon as practical to allow the anticipated
ground settlements to occur prior to building and slab on grade construction
For interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that a
minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of open graded gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation be placed below the slabs The open graded gravel cushion fill would

deposits underlying the site Therefore it is important that the new fills be placed as soon
as practical to allow the ground settlements to occur prior to construction of improvements
such as the pile foundations and slab construction to reduce the potential for adverse

At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation construction debris and other deleterious
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft and or yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fills should

After clearing and grubbing areas at grade or areas designated to receive fills

optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test Designation
D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight corresponding to
the maximum dry density

The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural

near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered in the test borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through

Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2118 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description

quality of soil rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the

SCOPE
The Consultant shall conduct a Preliminary Geotechnical Engineering Exploration to obtain
information pertaining to the subsurface conditions at the project site The subsurface
information will be used to formulate a summary of the soil rock conditions for the preliminary
design of the proposed structures Additional exploration will be required prior to final design
In order to accomplish these objectives the Consultant shall conduct an exploration generally
consisting of the following tasks and work efforts
1 Mobilization and demobilization of a truck mounted drill rig and two operators to the site
and back
2 Drilling and sampling of three borings to depths of approximately 150 feet below the
existing ground suface The proposed borings are as follows
Lahaina Building 1 boring to 150 feet
Napili Building 2 borings to 150 feet
3 Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the borings by a field
engineer geologist from the Consuitant s firm
4 Laboratory testing of selected samples obtained during the field exploration as an aid in
classifying the materials encountered and evaluating their engineering properties
5 Analyses of the field and laboratory data to develop geotechnical design
recommendations pertaining to the proposed structures
6 Preparation of six copies of a formal report summarizing the Consultants work on the
project and presenting their findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
7 Coordination of the Consultants work on the project by a project engineer of the firm
8 Quality assurance of the overall work on the project and client design team consultation
by a principal engineer of the Consultant s firm
9 Miscellaneous work efforts such as drafting word processing clerical support and
reproductions

during this erosion interval in addition to erosion due to eustatic changes higher and lower
stands of the sea level At the end of the rest period copious flows erupted along the
southwest and east rifts This is the third and upper rock unit known as the Hana Volcanic
Series Pleistocene Epoch The lava range in composition from ultra basic olivine
porphyries to non porphyritic alkalic basalts

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by shifts
in the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before
or during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not
erupt The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but
W O INC Page 6
Hawaii California

most of the earthquakes are so small that they can only be detected by
instruments However some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and
a few cause minor to moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii over
90 percent of earthquakes are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic
activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the
Island of Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes

To a lesser degree the Island of Maui has experienced numerous earthquakes

Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and a design peak ground
acceleration of 0 20g liquefaction may occur resulting in appreciable settlements to
the ground surface Therefore the effects of potential liquefaction should be taken
into consideration in the design of the proposed development
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess
pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by
earthquakes In this process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected
to vibration such as during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease
in volume causing an increase in pore water pressure If drainage is unable to
occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build up of pore water pressure the effective
stress internal strength of the soil is reduced Under sustained vibrations the
pore water pressure build up could equal the overburden pressure essentially
reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid
During liquefaction the soil acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal
and vertical movements and if not confined will result in significant deformations
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion It is generally acknowledged that
liquefaction may not occur if the deposit is greater than 40 to 50 feet below the
ground surface In deeper deposits the greater overburden pressure is generally
sufficient to prevent liquefaction from occurring The major factors affecting the
liquefaction characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows
1 Grain Size Distribution Fine and uniform sands and silts are more
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2 Initial Relative Density Loose sands and silts are most susceptible to
liquefaction Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to relative
density
3 Magnitude and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is directly
proportional to the magnitude and duration of the earthquake

3 1 Pile Foundations

1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces In general piles

capacity due to group action

Pile Hammer Selection
In general we recommend that the piles be driven with a hammer capable of
delivering a minimum rated energy of 32 000 foot pounds in the field The hammer
should be equipped with an energy level control such that driving within the
soft loose soils may be controlled at a lower energy to reduce the potential for
tensile stress development which could damage or cause cracking of the piles
Selection of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the matching of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be
driven This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy
to the top of the pile and also reducing the potential for damage to the piles The
project geotechnical engineer should review the pile driving equipment proposed to

Construction Considerations
The performance of drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor s
method of construction and construction procedures As a result of these potential
variations we recommend that a qualified geotechnical engineer be present to
observe the installation of drilled shafts during construction In our opinion the
following may have a significant impact on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled
shaft foundations
Based on our field exploration the project site is underlain by loose beach lagoonal
deposits below water extending to depths of over 100 feet below the existing
ground surface Due to the granular nature of these materials there is a potential
for caving in of the materials during the drilling operations To reduce the potential
for caving in of the drilled holes full depth permanent casing of the drilled holes will
be required during drilled shaft installation In addition the groundwater level at the
site may also pose construction difficulties since proper observation of the sides
and bottoms of the drilled shaft may not be possible

Workmanship
The load carrying capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in
drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids is generally not recommended Because
of the groundwater table anticipated within the depths of the drilled shaft
excavations concrete placement by tremie method will be required during
construction of the drilled shafts under such conditions The concrete should be
placed in a suitable manner by displacing the water in an upward fashion from the
bottom of the drilled hole A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 7 to 9 inch
slump range should be used to provide close contact between the drilled shafts
and the surrounding soils The concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to
reduce the potential for segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix A
qualified geotechnical engineer should be present to observe the installation of
drilled shafts during construction especially since high capacities are recommended
for these drilled shafts

3 3 Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that the floor slab for the proposed building construction will consist
of concrete slabs on grade Based on the existing topography and the anticipated design
finished floor elevation we envision that the slab on grade for the proposed structures will
be supported on the existing ground or shallow new compacted fills placed to raise the
existing ground surface to the finished subgrades at the building pad area
As mentioned previously the project site could be subjected to appreciable ground
settlements in the event of liquefaction caused by a moderate seismic event M6
Seismically induced ground settlements on the order of about 3 to 4 inches may be
anticipated and the building interior may sustain some damage that would disrupt the
operation of the facility Therefore there exists the potential for damage and distress to
the building slabs on grade under the driven pile foundation support system However we
believe that the potential for damage may be significantly reduced by increasing the
building slab thickness and by incorporating steel reinforcement into the concrete slab
As discussed above concrete slabs on grade may be subjected to ground
settlements and distress in the event of seismically induced soil liquefaction As an
alternative to slabs on grade consideration may be given to structurally supporting the
ground floor slab on the pile foundations to reduce the potential for damage to the interior
of the building in the event of seismically induced ground settlements
New fills needed to raise the site to the design grades should be placed in general
accordance with the recommendations provided in the Site Preparation section of this
report New fills needed to raise the site to the finished subgrades will induce ground
settlements as a result of consolidation of the underlying loose sand deposits Therefore
we recommend that the new fills be placed as soon as practical to allow the anticipated
ground settlements to occur prior to building and slab on grade construction
For interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that a
minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of open graded gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation be placed below the slabs The open graded gravel cushion fill would

To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration through the slab and subsequent

At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be

materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft and or yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fills should

percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test Designation

the maximum dry density

Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice

hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description

would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound

SCOPE
The Consultant shall conduct a Preliminary Geotechnical Engineering Exploration to obtain
information pertaining to the subsurface conditions at the project site The subsurface
information will be used to formulate a summary of the soil rock conditions for the preliminary
design of the proposed structures Additional exploration will be required prior to final design
In order to accomplish these objectives the Consultant shall conduct an exploration generally
consisting of the following tasks and work efforts
1 Mobilization and demobilization of a truck mounted drill rig and two operators to the site
and back
2 Drilling and sampling of three borings to depths of approximately 150 feet below the
existing ground suface The proposed borings are as follows
Lahaina Building 1 boring to 150 feet
Napili Building 2 borings to 150 feet
3 Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the borings by a field
engineer geologist from the Consuitant s firm
4 Laboratory testing of selected samples obtained during the field exploration as an aid in
classifying the materials encountered and evaluating their engineering properties
5 Analyses of the field and laboratory data to develop geotechnical design
recommendations pertaining to the proposed structures
6 Preparation of six copies of a formal report summarizing the Consultants work on the
project and presenting their findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
7 Coordination of the Consultants work on the project by a project engineer of the firm
8 Quality assurance of the overall work on the project and client design team consultation
by a principal engineer of the Consultant s firm
9 Miscellaneous work efforts such as drafting word processing clerical support and
reproductions
