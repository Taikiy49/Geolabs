Regional Geology
The Island of Lanai is a single shield volcano built by eruptions at the summit and
along three rift zones about 1 2 to 1 5 million years ago in Geologic Time Scale during
the Pleistocene Epoch of the Quaternary Period of the Cenozoic Era The primary rift
zone trends northwestward as a broad ridge and is responsible for the elongation of the
island in that direction The less conspicuous bulge on the southern side of the island is
a result of building of the southwest rift zone The summit of the shield collapsed to form
a caldera from which a shallow graben or rift valley bordered by a series of step faults
extends south southeast toward Manele Bay Numerous dikes exposed in the sea cliff
indicate that this Manele Graben lies along another rift zone
The caldera was largely filled by lava flows and the present Palawai Basin is a
remnant of the caldera Just to the west the mile wide Miki Basin is a partially filled pit
crater The top of the ridge between them is about 140 feet above the floor of the
Palawai Basin On the south side the floor of Palawai Basin merges with that of the
Manele Graben through which the last lava flows in the caldera overflowed onto the
outer slope of the volcano On the northeastern side Palawai Basin is bordered by a
steep slope about 500 feet high beyond which lies a nearly level bench of about
one mile wide The thick massive character of the lava flows in this bench indicates that
it is a part of the floor of the filled caldera Beyond the bench another steep slope rises
to Lanaihale Summit 3 316 feet altitude Southwest of the Lanaihale Summit at an
elevation of 1 000 to 2 000 feet is the Central Plateau of Lanai The soils consist of
well drained fine textured and moderately fine textured soils developed in volcanic ash
and in residual soils derived from the in situ weathering of basaltic igneous rocks
All of the lavas of Lanai are theolitic basalts rich in Olivine and Feldspar
minerals with Pahoehoe dominating near the vents and A a type lava flows more
abundant on the lower slopes The lava flows range from about 1 foot to 100 feet in
thickness but average about 20 feet and seldom exceed 50 feet except where they
have been ponded in depressions

Lanai Island is in the rain shadow of Maui and Molokai and therefore is mostly
very dry with seasonal average rainfall of only 38 inches per year The north side of the
island is subject to strong wind forces resulting in sand dunes along the coast but is
sheltered from wave erosion The south facing side is dominated by tall sea cliffs typical
of areas exposed to strong ocean forces Marine deposits found in a small gulch near
Kaluakapo Crater at elevations of over 1 000 feet above sea level indicate that a
massive tsunami submerged this portion of the island approximately 350 000 years ago
The project site is on the northwest side of Miki Basin Most of the soils in the
project area are residual and saprolitic soils In general residual and saprolitic soils
grade to weathered basalt formations with depth

Site Description
The project area is at the south end and around the southeast perimeter of the
Lanai Airport on the Island of Lanai Hawaii The project location is shown on the
General Site Plan Plate 2
The site generally slopes upward gradually from southwest to northeast with
ground surface elevations ranging from 1 220 to 1 310 feet above Mean Sea Level
MSL within the proposed limits of grading A steeper slope exists down hill of the
southwestern apron of the runway approach where the terrain rises from Kaupili Road
up to the runway apron at an average gradient of sixteen horizontal to one vertical
16H 1V Just outside of the perimeter fencing a 20 foot high 2H 1V slope is present
below the runway approach corridor
The surface materials observed at the site consist mainly of reddish brown clayey
silt exposures of residual soil Several spheroidally weathered basalt boulders and
cobbles are present throughout the area These boulders and cobbles were observed
to be in piles and in eroded swales Piles of soil material were observed just east of the
airport terminal The soil materials consist of clayey silt with varying amounts of
man made debris Aside from the many dirt roads that transect the site the area is
moderately vegetated with flora ranging from tall grasses to small trees and shrubs

Subsurface Conditions
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling
eight borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 8 to depths ranging from about
10 5 to 40 5 feet below the ground surface In addition twenty test pits designated as
Test Pit Nos 1 through 20 were excavated to depths of about 9 0 to 11 0 feet below the
existing ground surface The approximate boring and test pit locations are shown on
the Enlarged Site Plans Plates 3 1 through 3 3
The subsurface conditions at the proposed runway safety area generally consist
of a layer of fill with agricultural debris about 1 5 feet thick Below the fill residual soils
consisting of very stiff to hard clayey silt were encountered in the borings The residual
soils graded to saprolitic soils at depths of about 21 to 34 feet below the existing ground
surface and extended to the maximum depths explored of about 30 5 to 40 5 feet below
the existing ground surface
The subsurface conditions at the potential borrow areas generally consist of a
layer of fill with agricultural debris about 0 3 to 1 5 feet thick The fill was underlain by
residual and saprolitic soils consisting of very stiff clayey silt extending to the maximum
depths explored of about 9 0 to 11 0 feet below the existing ground surface Some
boulders and cobbles were encountered within the residual and saprolitic soil layers
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings and test pits at the time of our
field exploration However it should be noted that groundwater levels may fluctuate
depending on seasonal precipitation and other factors
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of
Borings in Appendix A and the Logs of Test Pits in Appendix B Laboratory tests were
performed on selected soil samples obtained during our field exploration and the test
results are presented in Appendix C

Ground Settlement
Based on the grading plans provided we understand that significant fills will be
placed for the runway safety area In general there will be about 10 feet and 40 feet of
fills placed on the south and north sides respectively over the existing ground surface
to achieve the design grades for the runway safety area
Ground settlements are anticipated when new fills are placed over the existing
ground to raise the site to the finished elevations These settlements will affect
earthwork quantity estimates for the project Based on the results of our exploration
the anticipated ground settlements are primarily the result of the following
two processes
compression of the fill material under its own weight
consolidation of the in situ soils induced by the new fill loads
Based on our analyses total settlement from compression of the fill material
under its own weight and settlement of the in situ residual and saprolitic soils induced
by the new fill loads are on the order of about 6 inches or less
3 2
New Fill Slopes
Based on our slope stability analyses permanent fill slopes planned for the
runway safety area embankment may be designed with a maximum slope inclination of
2H 1V A factor of safety for overall slope stability greater than 1 5 for both short term
and long term conditions was achieved with a fill slope inclination of 2H 1V All fills
placed on existing slopes with inclinations steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and
benched into existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling
operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal
compacted layers in accordance with the fill placement recommendations presented in
the Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements subsection herein Fill slopes
should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain

3 3
New Cut Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site we believe that new
cut slopes may expose residual and saprolitic soils Based on our slope stability
analyses a cut slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter may be used for design of the
planned cut slopes exposing residual and saprolitic soils A factor of safety for overall
stability of 1 5 was achieved for both short term and long term conditions with a cut
slope inclination of 2H 1V In the event that over cutting of a slope occurs keying and
benching requirements should be implemented instead of backfilling the slope to the
design grade with sliver fills
Excessive surface runoff over the slope face exposing residual and saprolitic
soils may cause erosion of the exposed soils thus jeopardizing the long term stability
and performance of the cut slopes Therefore it is our opinion that cut slopes should be
protected by appropriate slope planting or ECM immediately after the slope is
excavated to reduce the potential for erosion of the exposed soils

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly Vegetation debris deleterious material and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to reduce the
potential for contamination of the excavated materials Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill should
be over excavated to expose firm and natural material and the resulting excavation
should be backfilled with engineered fill The excavated soft soils should be properly
disposed of off site
As previously mentioned the project site is situated on land that was used
previously for agricultural purposes Traditionally the fields are tilled after the crops are
harvested This process results in mixing of the remaining organic materials with the
soils Based on our field exploration we anticipate that the top 12 to 18 inches of the
ground surface may contain substantial organic matter mixed in with the soils
Therefore we recommend over excavating the soils to a depth of about 18 inches
below the current ground surface to remove organic materials contained within the
upper soils prior to fill placement
In general the excavated subgrades and areas designated to receive fills should
be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of the soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content corresponding
to the maximum dry density Where shrinkage cracks are observed after preparation of
the subgrade we recommend thoroughly moistening the soil to close the cracks and
then recompacting Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due
to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas
and replacement with engineered fill

Fills and Backfills
In general we believe that the excavated soil materials and the soils
encountered at the potential borrow areas as indicated on the General Site Plan
Plate 2 may be used as a source of fill materials provided that the materials are
properly processed and placed in accordance with the Fill Placement and Compaction
Requirements subsection herein The excavated on site soils to be used as fills should
be free of vegetation deleterious materials and rock fragments greater than 6 inches in
maximum dimension Based on our field exploration and laboratory test results it
appears that the on site soils have moderate to high in situ moisture contents grading
with depth Therefore drying or aerating the excavated materials may be necessary
prior to use as general fill
Imported fill materials needed for the site filling may consist of materials with a
low expansion potential Imported general fill materials should consist of materials with
particle sizes of 3 inches or less in maximum dimension and should be free of
deleterious materials The material should contain between 10 and 60 percent particles
passing the No 200 Sieve The material should have a laboratory California Bearing
Ratio CBR value of 12 or higher and a swell potential of not more than 2 percent when
tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883
It should be noted that remnant boulders were encountered in the test pits In
addition we observed weathered boulders and cobbles at various locations across the
project site Therefore some over sized materials should be expected in the excavated
materials Boulders less than 3 feet in maximum dimension may be used as fill in areas
where there is more than 10 feet of fill The top of the boulder fill layer should be placed
at least 10 feet from the finished subgrade and at least 8 feet from the finished grade at
the slope face In general the placement of boulders in fills should be performed in
accordance with the procedures recommended in the Boulder Fill Placement section

Excavation
Based on our field exploration and the current grading plans site grading work
for the project will involve cuts into the residual and saprolitic soils It is anticipated that
the soils may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment However
cobbles and boulders were encountered within the residual and saprolitic soil layers in
several of the test pits
There is a high potential for encountering hard basalt rock in the form of
relatively unweathered cobbles and large boulders within the residual and saprolitic soil
layers considering the large areal extent of the project site Therefore grading
contractors for the project should be cautioned that hard basalt rock may be
encountered in the excavations Excavations encountering large boulders and hard
basalt rock may require chipping and or the use of hoerams to excavate the materials
and to achieve the finished grades of cut slopes
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are
based on field data obtained from the borings performed at the project site and our
experience in the project vicinity Contractors bidding on this project should be
encouraged to review and understand the geologic environment of the project site and
to examine the site conditions and soil data to make their own interpretation

Boulder Fill Placement
Boulders i e rock fragments larger than 1 foot in maximum size but less than
3 feet in largest dimension may be used as fills provided that the following
recommendations are followed
1
Boulders less than 3 feet in largest dimension may be utilized as fills
provided that the top of the boulder fill layer is placed at least 10 feet
below the finished subgrades and 8 feet from the finished grades at the
slope face Boulders larger than 3 feet in maximum dimension should not
be used as fill unless reduced in size
2
Boulders may be placed in a lift of about 3 feet in thickness The maximum
lift thickness is determined by the maximum size of the material i e 12 inch
lift thickness for 12 inch maximum boulder size
3
Boulders should be spread and compacted with a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer
or larger for a minimum of 6 to 8 passes to facilitate seating of the
boulders The boulder fill should be watered heavily by water trucks
traversing in front of the current boulder lift face and sprayed with water
continuously during placement
4
A minimum 18 inch thick choking layer should be spread on the top of the
boulder fill layer and worked into the voids with a large bulldozer or other
heavy compaction equipment to seal off the voids of the boulder fill The
choking layer should consist of well graded granular fill materials with
particle size of less than 6 inches in dimension The choking layer of
well graded granular fill should be watered heavily during placement and
compaction
5
Care must be exercised to avoid placement of boulders within the depths of
the planned utility lines to reduce the potential for encountering the boulders
during excavation for the utility trenches
6
The lateral extent and elevations of the top of the boulder fill should be
surveyed for future reference
It should be noted that the use of boulders as fills will require that the entire
boulder fill mass be compacted and properly choked off at the surface and each layer
of boulder fill to reduce the potential for migration of fines through the voids between
boulders

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling eight borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 8 to depths of about 10 5 to 40 5 feet below the
existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the General Site Plan
Plate 2 We used a truck mounted drill rig equipped with auger equipment
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field on a near continuous basis These classifications were
further reviewed visually and by laboratory testing Soils were classified in general
conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 8
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Pocket penetrometer tests results are summarized on
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight determinations ASTM D 2937
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Eight Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths The
test results are provided on Plate C 1
Three Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested The test results are
presented on Plates C 2 through C 4
Three unconsolidated undrained Triaxial Compression tests ASTM D 2850
were performed on selected samples of the soils to evaluate the undrained shear
strength of the soils The results of the unconsolidated undrained triaxial compression
tests are presented on Plates C 5 through C 7
Two Consolidation tests ASTM D 2435 with time rates were performed on soil
samples to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the encountered materials
The test results are presented on Plates C 8 and C 9
Four Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates C 10 through C 13
Four laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were
performed on bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils The test results are presented on Plates C 14 through C 17
