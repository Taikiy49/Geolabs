SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration encountered fills consisting of soft uncompacted silty clay 
within the northern middle third portion of the project site extending to depths of about 2 to 
5 feet underlain by medium stiff to hard silty clay soils. The back edge of the proposed 
small cemetery model complex is bounded by the edge of a ravine that drops at a 
relatively steep angle of about one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V) to the bottom of a 
stream gully approximately 50 to 60 feet below the project site. Our field observations 
reveal that the edge of the ravine is regularly used as a disposal site of excess soil 
materials from landscaping operations at the memorial park complex. Over time, the 
slivers of fill accumulated into an unconsolidated mass near the rim of the slope. 
Laboratory tests of the on-site soils obtained from the exploratory pits indicated relatively 
high moisture content, which is generally associated with poor workability and less 
favorable support characteristics. Special site preparation and earthwork procedures are 
necessary to provide adequate support for the structures planned at the project site. 
Based on conceptual plans provided by the project architects, Clark and Green 
Associates, low garden walls and granite pre-fabricated mausoleum type structures are 
proposed along the back-edge of the site where most of the unconsolidated fills are 
located. To improve stiffness and bearing capacity of the subgrade materials within this 
zone, we recommend excavating the unconsolidated soils and replacing these with 
properly compacted fill. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,000 psf may be utilized in 
the design of foundations bearing on compacted structural fill over the in-situ soils. A 
minimum setback distance of at least 8 feet measured horizontally from the outside edge 
of a footing to the edge of the existing slope should be observed. Surface drains should be 
incorporated into the project design to reduce the potential for ponding and saturation of 
the subsoils. Runoff should not be allowed to discharge at the slope behind the project site 
to reduce the risk of gullying that can potentially migrate under the structures near the 
edge of the slope. 
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering design 
recommendations are presented in the following sections of this report.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island.  The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age 
and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  Waianae became extinct 
while Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau 
lavas banking against its eastern flank. These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad 
plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii, the erosion processes are dominated by 
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls, which are then transported 
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium. Once these 
materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley, alluvial processes become 
dominant, and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium.  In general, 
stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy, such that the stream flows transmit 
large volumes of water for very short durations. Because of this, transport of sediments is 
intermittent, and the bulk of the stream's hydraulic load consists of a poorly sorted mixture 
of boulders, cobbles, gravel, sands, and fines.  When the erosion base levels change, 
these sediment loads are left as deposits. 
When deposits are left in-place for long periods of time, chemical processes 
begin to alter the materials, simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the 
material. Chemical processes also cause induration, or cementation, of the 
coarse-grained portion of the sediment into a poorly-consolidated sedimentary rock, or 
conglomerate. Simultaneously, erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors 
and upstream in headwaters. This continued erosion generates material which is 
transported downslope covering the older alluvial deposits. Depending on the local base 
level and rate of transport, these new sediments are generally transient in terms of 
geologic time. In addition, their consistency and density are generally less than those of 
older, partially consolidated deposits.

The project site is situated near the base of the Koolau Pali, a high cliff that 
extends along most of the windward side of the Koolau Range on the Island of Oahu.  
The climate along the Pali is typified by a high to very high average annual rainfall.  
Following the main shield building stage of the Koolau volcano in the late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene Epoch, stream erosion, landslide and deep weathering of the basalt 
rock became the dominant agents affecting the morphology of the area. 
The study area forms part of a large colluvial apron deposited near the base of 
the Koolau Range. This apron formed as a result of the accumulation of soil and rock 
materials derived from large-scale erosion and landslide along the Koolau Pali. 
Subsequently, stream erosion incised these deposits to create the present day 
topography of the region as evidenced by the occurrence of multiple hill/ridge and gully 
terrain. The project lies above the end of a northeast trending ridge separated by an 
incised well-developed stream gully along the northwest side of the ridge. Previous 
development on the ridge created the gently sloping transitions in the terrain as the 
ridge merges gradually into the surrounding landscape. 
2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project lies at the western terminus of a prominent northwest trending ridge 
dividing the northern and southern portions of the Valley of the Temples Memorial Park 
complex.  
The site, which includes an old fill area, is relatively flat with a moderate slope 
towards the northwest in the general direction of the gully. The site is covered with well 
maintained lawn grass bordered with some ornamental trees consisting of betel nut 
palm and Norfolk Pine. A steep slope (about 1H:1V) covered with dense vegetation of 
Mangrove, Java Plum, Ironwood, Mango and other tropical trees with an undergrowth of 
fern borders the northern side of the site. 
Ground elevations range from about + 152 and 160 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) 
near the southern and northern ends of the project site, respectively.

Subsurface Conditions 
The subsurface conditions were characterized by test pit exploration. Four test 
pits, designated as Test Pits Nos. 1 through 4, were excavated to depths of about 4 to 
7 feet below the existing ground surface with “CASE 580” backhoe equipment. Soil 
samples were obtained from each of the test pits for index property and relevant 
engineering characteristics evaluation. The approximate test pit locations are shown on 
the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our field exploration generally encountered fill underlain by native colluvial soils 
at relatively shallow depths within the project site. The fill encountered near the crest of 
the gully consisted of soft, uncompacted clay and organic materials. The underlying 
natural soils explored consist of colluvium of very stiff to hard clayey silt. These on-site 
materials generally have very high in-situ moisture contents ranging from 30 percent to 
more than 60 percent as measured in the laboratory tests. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the test pits at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it should be noted that the water level at the project site could be 
influenced by variations in seasonal precipitation, and other factors occurring outside 
the project site.

2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic 
plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic

activity; therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground 
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The 
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so 
small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of 
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate 
damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on 
the Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of earthquakes in Hawaii 
(over 90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and 
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of 
Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than 
Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of 
Hawaii. 
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes 
greater than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have 
occurred in the County of Maui. The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Islands 
of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu. For example, small 
landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of 
1938 (M6.8). Some houses on the Island of Oahu were reportedly damaged as a 
result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 (M7+). 
In the last 150 years of recorded history, we are not aware of earthquakes greater 
than Magnitude 6 that have occurred on the Island of Oahu. An earthquake of 
Magnitude 4.8 to 5.0 occurred along the Diamond Head Fault in 1948 on the Island 
of Oahu. The moderate tremor resulted in broken store windows, ruptured building 
walls, and broken underground water mains.

2.4.3 Liquefaction Potential 
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils near the ground 
surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build-up of excess pore 
water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes. In 
this process, when loose saturated sand deposits are subjected to vibration 
(such as during an earthquake), the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume 
causing an increase in pore water pressure. If drainage is unable to occur rapidly 
enough to dissipate the build-up of pore water pressure, the effective stress

pressure build-up could equal the overburden pressure, essentially reducing the

vertical movements, and if not confined, will result in significant deformations. Soils

loose silts with little cohesion. The major factors affecting the liquefaction

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration indicated that the project site includes uncompacted fills 
near the edge of the slope underlain by native soils of stiff to hard silty clays. 
Preliminary conceptual plans indicate that most of the structures for the proposed 
development are located close to the edge of the gully where the majority of the 
uncompacted fills are anticipated. Therefore, proper site preparation and earthwork 
procedures including removal of the uncompacted fill, are necessary to provide 
adequate support for the planned structures for the project. Based on our evaluation, we 
believe that the in-situ soils and new fills should provide adequate support for the 
proposed improvements provided that the recommendations presented herein are 
implemented during the design and construction of the project. 
Preliminary plans for the project include structures consisting of a dry deck 
fountain feature, paved walkway, low concrete garden walls, granite mausoleum, and 
similar structures.  
Our laboratory testing of the near-surface in-situ soils indicated relatively high 
moisture content, which is generally associated with poor workability when used as 
construction fill and consequently have less favorable support characteristics. To 
improve bearing support, we recommend providing a minimum of 12 inches of structural 
fill under the proposed structures. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may 
be utilized for the design of foundations bearing on 12 inches of structural fill compacted 
to 90 percent of maximum dry density over the in-situ soils or recompacted fills.  
A properly designed surface drain should be incorporated into the project design 
to reduce the potential for saturation of the subsoils and build-up of hydrostatic pressure 
behind the walls.

3.1 
Site Grading 
Based on the conceptual plan for the proposed small cemetery model complex 
and our discussions with the project architect, Clark and Green, the development will 
adapt to the existing grade, and therefore, we anticipate that grading will be minimal and 
limited within the proposed improvements. Majority of the improvements are located 
near the edge of the development where most of the unconsolidated fills occur. To 
improve bearing support and increase slope stability in this area, we recommend 
excavating the loose uncompacted fill materials and replacing with engineered fill. Items 
of grading that are addressed in the following subsections include: (1) Site Preparation; 
(2) Fills and Backfills; (3) Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements; (4) Cut and Fill 
Slopes; (5) Excavation. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor site grading operations to observe 
whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation process and to 
confirm whether the exposed soil conditions are similar to those encountered in our field 
exploration. 
3.1.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly.  Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and 
other unsuitable materials, should be removed and disposed of properly off-site. 
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below finished 
subgrades and areas designated to receive fill should be over-excavated to expose 
firm natural material.  The resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
well-compacted general fills.   
The soft and/or organic uncompacted fill soils should be removed and disposed of 
off-site and/or used in landscape areas, where appropriate. 
After clearing and grubbing, the contractor should proof-roll the over-excavated 
subgrades, finished subgrades, and areas designated to receive fills and compact 
to a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the 
in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density

3.1.2 Fills and Backfills 
General fill materials may consist of excavated on-site soils provided that they are 
free of vegetation, deleterious materials, and rock fragments greater than 6 inches 
in maximum dimension. Since the project site is in a high rainfall area, the on-site 
materials have very high in-situ moisture contents of up to 30 percent above 
optimum. Therefore, significant drying of excavated materials will be required prior 
to their use as fill and/or backfill to attain the relative moisture and density 
relationship requirements during compaction. 
Imported materials to be used as structural fill should be non-expansive granular 
material, such as crushed coral or basalt. The structural fill should be well graded 
from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension. The 
material should have a California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher and a 
swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883. 
The material should also contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the

3.1.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Due to the relatively high moisture contents of the on-site soils, substantial aeration 
is required to achieve a 90 percent relative compaction normally required. This field 
method of reducing the moisture content in the soil may not be feasible at all times 
due to the high rainfall frequency experienced at the project site. As a result, the 
compaction requirements for general fill placement will have to be reduced in order 
to aid in the earthwork construction using the high moisture tropical soils in this wet 
environment. Based on our experience with similar high moisture tropical soils, we 
believe that a reduced compaction requirement of 85 percent relative compaction 
may be used to facilitate fill placement and to reduce the potential of pumping 
conditions. 
General fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction.  Fill 
should be compacted to form a dense, stable mass. The contractor working on this 
project should be made aware that aeration to lower the soil moisture and 
additional effort of thinner fill lifts to achieve the specified compaction will reduce the 
rate of fill placement for this project. Even with this reduced 85 percent minimum 
compaction requirement, we believe substantial moisture reduction would still be 
required along with the use of light compaction/construction equipment. 
Compaction should be accomplished by acceptable types of compaction 
equipment. Due to the high rainfall environment at the project site, we believe that 
good construction practices, such as sealing and grading of the ground surface to 
provide positive drainage at the end of each work day, to reduce the potential for 
ponding and saturation are essential to the earthwork operations for the proposed 
project. Backfill to replace the unsuitable uncompacted fill must be properly keyed 
and benched into the existing stiff in-situ soil.

3.1.4 Cut and Fill Slopes 
Based on our discussion with the project architect, the development will make use 
of the existing grades, and therefore, no mass grading fills are anticipated. 
However, if fills are required for this project, such as to create a level pad for the 
small structures, permanent fill slopes constructed using the on-site materials 
should be designed with a slope inclination not to exceed 2H:1V. The filling 
operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal 
compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations. 
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope 
ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. Slope planting should be provided as 
soon as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes. 
3.1.5 Excavation 
The proposed earthwork at the site will involve excavation to remove unsuitable 
materials such as uncompacted fills to expose firm subgrade to receive fill under 
the proposed structures. In general, it is anticipated that these materials may be 
excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment.  
The above discussion on the rippability of the soil materials are based on field data 
obtained from the test pits excavated and sampled at the project site. Contractors 
bidding on this project should be encouraged to examine the site conditions and 
soil data to make their own interpretation. 
The contractor should take special precautions to divert water away from 
excavations to reduce the potential for saturation of the subsoils, which may affect 
their stability. Also, the contractor must closely monitor the condition of relatively 
deep i.e., greater than 4 feet temporary cuts, if required, for signs of instability. 
Depending on the contractor’s sequence of work and schedule, temporary 
excavation support and shoring system may be prudent and should be the 
responsibility of the Contractor. Temporary excavation and shoring support must 
comply with all applicable safety requirements.

3.2 
Foundations 
Based on the current concept, the following structures are planned at the site: 
A 5-foot tall horse shoe-shaped CMU with stone veneer wall having 
a unit dead load estimated at about 750 psf. 
A 1.5-foot wide x 8.5-foot x 28-foot long niche wall with an 
estimated dead load of about 500 psf. 
Three 4-foot x 6-foot x 7-foot-high shrines with an estimated dead 
load of about 460 psf. 
Granite cremation pedestals with an estimated dead load of about 
250 psf. 
Low granite walls with an estimated dead load of about 300 psf. 
Granite monuments with a 6-foot x 1.2-foot footprint and an 
estimated dead load of about 450 psf. 
The following general guidelines may be used for the design of foundation for 
these structures. 
3.2.1 Footing Foundation  
In general, we believe that the foundations for the CMU wall, granite shrine, and 
other structures may be designed with an allowable bearing pressure of up to 
2,000 psf bearing on 12 inches of structural fill compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction. The structural fill would provide a more competent 
bearing surface over the in-situ high moisture soils.  This bearing value is for 
dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, 
such as those caused by wind or seismic forces.  In addition, the structural fill 
should extend a minimum of 1 foot beyond the edges of the footing. 
The in-situ soils below the 12-inch structural fill should be scarified to a depth of 
about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned or aerated to about 2 percent above the 
optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 85 percent relative 
compaction. If soft and/or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the 
select borrow layer, the soft/loose materials should be over-excavated to expose

W.O. 6822-00 
GEOLABS, INC. 
Page 16

the underlying firm materials. The over-excavation should be backfilled with 
imported structural fill material compacted to 90 percent relative compaction. 
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction developed 
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth 
pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system.  A 
coefficient of friction of 0.40 may be used for footings bearing on the compacted 
select borrow material. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated 
using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth 
(pcf).  This assumes that the soil around footings are well-compacted.  Unless 
covered by pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of 
soil should be neglected. 
Wall footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches or at least twice the width 
of the wall. Wall foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded a 
minimum depth of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade. For sloping 
ground conditions, the footing should extend deeper to obtain a minimum 8-foot 
setback distance measured horizontally from the outside edge of the footing to the 
face of the slope.  Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should 
be constructed in steps. 
Footings next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a 
45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility 
trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines.  This requirement is necessary to 
avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with additional structural loads 
and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement. 
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate that total settlements of footings bearing on the 
12-inch structural fill select borrow over the in-situ soils to be on the order of less 
than 1 inch. We estimate that differential settlement along the wall footings to be on 
the order of about 0.5 inches.

personnel. Our preliminary pavement design assumes that the pavement subgrade soil

12 and swell potential of 1.5 percent or less.  Based on the above assumptions, we 
recommend the following rigid pavement section for preliminary design purposes: 
Rigid Pavements 
  4.0-Inch Portland Cement Concrete 
  6.0-Inch Aggregate Subbase (95 Percent Relative Compaction) 
10.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness over Moist Compacted Subgrade

City and County of Honolulu, September 1986.  CBR and field density tests should be 
performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered during construction to confirm the

3.4 
Utilities 
It is anticipated that some utilities, such as drainage, irrigation and possibly 
electrical service, will be constructed for the proposed project. The contractor should 
determine the methods and equipment to be used for excavation, subject to practical 
limits and safety considerations.  The excavation should comply with all applicable local, 
state, and federal safety requirements.  The contractor should be responsible for trench 
shoring design and installation if necessary. Trench shoring and bracing should conform 
to the appropriate health and safety requirements.  
In general, for support of the utility lines, we recommend using granular bedding 
consisting of 6 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) under the 
pipes.  The initial backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of 
free-draining backfills, such as No. 3B Fine gravel, to reduce the potential for damaging 
the pipes from compaction of the backfill.  It is critical to use a free-draining granular 
material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and 
to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes.  The use of on-site soils as 
backfill directly around utility pipes is not recommended. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to 
the finish subgrade should consist of approved on-site fill soils or structural fill material. 
The backfill material should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to between 85 and 90 percent relative compaction to reduce 
the potential for future ground subsidence. The upper 2 feet of trench backfills below 
pavement subgrades should be compacted to no less than 90 percent relative 
compaction. Mechanical compaction equipment should be used to compact the

3.5 
Drainage 
Since the project site is in a high rainfall area, special attention should be given 
to site drainage during and after construction.  The finished grades adjacent to the 
structures should be sloped to shed water away from the foundations and to reduce the 
potential for ponding.  Excessive landscape watering and use of gravel strips near the 
foundations should also be avoided.  Planters next to foundations should be avoided or 
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the 
subsoils. These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the 
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil 
saturation and subsequent loss of strength of the near-surface soils. The foundation 
excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting 
of the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration.  Drainage swales should be 
provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface run-off away 
from walls and foundations.  
3.6 
Stability of the Existing Slope 
A stability analysis of the existing slope in relation to the anticipated loading 
conditions from the proposed structures was performed using limit equilibrium 
methods. Our analysis indicates a stability factor (FS) of about 1.5 against sliding. It is 
our opinion that it is feasible to build the proposed structures near the edge of the slope 
provided that our foundation and minimum setback distance recommendations are 
followed during construction. 
3.7 
Slope Hazards 
Section 14-14.2(d)(2) of the Revised Ordinances of Honolulu requires a slope 
hazard evaluation for grading that involves modification to an existing slope with cuts 
greater than 15 feet in height and a slope grade steeper than 40 percent. 
The proposed development does not fall under any of the above categories and 
therefore a slope hazard evaluation is not included in this report.
