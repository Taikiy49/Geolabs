SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered alluvium and interbedded lagoonal 
deposits predominately consisting of silty clay, extending to the maximum depth drilled of 
approximately 31.5 feet below the ground surface. We did not encounter groundwater in 
the borings drilled at the proposed KIUC substation site at the time of our field exploration. 
However, groundwater was encountered between about 1.3 and 8.3 feet below the 
existing ground surface at the AES PV array sites. It should be noted groundwater levels 
are subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water 
runoff, and other factors. 
Based on the anticipated light structural loads and the generally stiff subsurface 
conditions, we recommend using shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings to 
support the new buildings. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 and 
2,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the design of shallow foundations at 
the KIUC substation and AES PV array sites, respectively. For footings bearing on the 
recompacted on-site soils and/or new compacted fills, the bottom of the footing 
excavations should be recompacted to at least 90% relative compaction to provide a 
relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to the placement of reinforcing steel 
and/or concrete. 
Based on our laboratory tests, the on-site clayey soils exhibit a high to very high 
shrink/swell potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations. To reduce the potential for 
future distress to the lightly loaded slabs-on-grade resulting from shrinking and swelling 
of the clayey soils, we recommend embedding the thickened-edge footings a minimum of 
18 inches below the lowest adjacent grade. We also recommend properly preparing and 
capping the subgrade soils with a 36-inch thick layer of non-expansive select granular fill 
materials. The non-expansive select granular fill should extend a minimum of 3 feet 
beyond the edge of the structures.  
To provide sufficient uplift and lateral load resistances, the solar panel array 
structures may be supported on a deep foundation system consisting of driven piles. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, we recommend using an ultimate skin 
friction of 0.5 kips per square foot (ksf) for the alluvium and lagoonal deposits 
encountered at the site to determine the ultimate compressive and uplift load capacity. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and 
recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL

conducted for the AES West Kauai Energy Project in the West Kauai area on the Island

findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at

1.1 
Project Considerations 
We understand as part of the AES West Kauai Energy project, it is desired to 
develop a substation and photovoltaic (PV) solar farms in the West Kauai area on the 
Island of Kauai, Hawaii. Based on the information provided, the substation will be 
constructed on approximately 2 acres of land northeast of the PV solar farms that will 
encompass approximately 285 acres of former farmland. We anticipate various 
equipment pads and electrical circuits will be required for the project.

support of the design of building foundations, slabs-on-grade, solar array foundations, 
retaining structures, pavements, and other geotechnical aspects. This geotechnical 
investigation excludes the electrical distribution system as the utility line alignments were 
not available at the time this report was prepared. 
Detailed information pertaining to the project layouts and the solar array foundation 
supports and loading were not available at the time this report was prepared. However, 
we envision solar panel arrays will be elevated and extending about 5 to 10 feet above 
the existing grade with associated support facilities.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Kaua`i is a dissected basaltic dome that was initially formed by a 
single large shield volcano, which has subsequently been deeply eroded and partially 
covered with later (younger) volcanic materials. The Island of Kaua`i is the oldest of the 
main Hawaiian Islands and today encompasses approximately 1,456 square kilometers 
(562 square miles) in area with a maximum elevation of about 1,569 meters (5,148 feet) 
above Mean Sea Level (MSL) at Mt. Wai’ale’ale near the center of the island. It is believed 
that during the Tertiary Period (about 5 to 6 million years ago in the Pliocene Epoch), 
basaltic lavas belonging to the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series built a roughly circular 
island volcano from the ocean floor that was estimated to have been about 6,100 meters 
(20,000 feet) deep at the time. Near the end of the Pliocene Epoch, this main shield 
building volcanic phase came to a halt. 
Following a long period of erosion and island mass subsidence, volcanism was 
renewed during the early Pleistocene Epoch (about 1.5 million years ago) with the 
eruption of the Koloa Volcanic Series from multiple vents throughout the island. This post-
erosional volcanic activity laid a veneer of fresh lava and interbedded volcanic sediments 
over the older deposits of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series. Because of the long 
period of erosion between the eruption of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series and the 
Koloa Volcanic Series, deposits of the Koloa Volcanic Series typically filled the erosional 
valleys and depressions that existed at that time. Consequently, the basaltic rocks of the 
Koloa Volcanic Series are commonly found overlying thick residual soil and alluvial 
deposits. 
The dominant geologic processes of weathering, landform erosion, and sediment 
re-deposition followed during the late Pleistocene and Recent Epochs. Combined with 
the erosional and depositional effects of the Pleistocene sea-level fluctuations, which 
occurred in response to the worldwide advance and retreat of the continental ice sheets, 
the Island of Kaua`i has evolved to its present form.

The project site is on a low-lying coastal area of Mana Plain on the western side 
of the island.  Mana Plain was below sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and is 
characterized by both terrigenous alluvium and marine sediments. The marine sediments 
have continued to accumulate in a low energy lagoonal environment resulting in thick 
deposits of soft and/or loose sediments along the coast in areas that were formerly valleys 
and drainageways. 
2.2 
Site Description 
The project site is located on former agricultural lands in the West Kauai area on 
the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. It is generally located just south of Mana and about 1.5 miles 
inland (northeast) of the coastline as shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.  
Based on our field observations, the parcels of land appear to be relatively flat with 
a gradual slope down to the south. Both KIUC substation and PV array sites were 
generally covered by overgrown brushes and small to large trees with varying degrees of 
canopy density. Several unpaved and cleared access roads/trails ran throughout the 
project limits during our field exploration. It should be noted several irrigation channels 
were observed throughout the PV array sites.

3.2 
Slabs-On-Grade 
We anticipate new slabs-on-grade will be constructed for the project. Based on our 
laboratory tests, the subsurface clayey soils at the project site generally exhibit a high to 
very high shrink/swell potential when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture content. 
To reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs-on-grade resulting 
from shrinking and swelling of the expansive clayey soils, we recommend embedding the 
thickened-edge footings a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent grade. We 
also recommend properly preparing and capping the subgrade soils with a 36-inch thick 
layer of non-expansive select granular fill materials. The non-expansive select granular 
fill should extend a minimum of 3 feet beyond the edge of the structures. Prior to 
placement of the non-expansive select granular fill, the subgrade soils should be scarified 
to a depth of about 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to about 2 to 4 percent above the 
optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction. The non-expansive select granular fill also should be compacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
For interior building slabs (not subjected to vehicular traffic or machinery vibration), 
we recommend placing a minimum 4-inch layer of cushion fill below the concrete slab. 
The cushion fill should consist of free-draining materials, such as No. 3 Fine gravel (ASTM 
C33, No. 67 gradation), which would also serve as a capillary moisture break. To reduce 
the potential for excessive moisture infiltration through the slab and subsequent damage

We anticipate that the solar panel arrays will be supported on driven steel piles 
consisting of C-Pile or Z-Pile sections to develop the required bearing, uplift, and lateral 
load resistance. Details of the driven steel piles were not provided at the time this report 
was prepared. 
The steel piles would derive vertical support primarily from skin friction between 
the pile and the surrounding soils. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, we 
recommend using an ultimate skin friction of 0.5 kips per square foot (ksf) for the alluvium 
and lagoonal deposits encountered at the site to determine the ultimate compressive load 
capacity. A factor of safety of 2.0 should be used to determine the allowable compressive 
load capacity of the steel piles.  The allowable compressive load capacity is for supporting 
dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as 
those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
It should be noted that the actual pile capacity should be performed by a qualified 
professional civil engineer specialized in geotechnical engineering, who understands the 
soil bearing behavior developed by the impact hammer driving. Calculation of the pile 
bearing capacity should consider the soil plug effects due to closed or semi-closed pile 
section. 
Uplift loads imposed on the piles may be resisted by a combination of the dead 
weight of the pile and skin friction between the pile surface and the adjacent soils.  An 
ultimate uplift skin friction of 0.5 ksf for the alluvium and lagoonal deposits may be used 
to achieve the uplift load capacity for transient loading.  The uplift load capacities should 
not be further increased for transient load applications.  For sustained uplift loads, we 
recommend reducing the uplift load capacity by 50 percent to resist sustained uplift loads.

3.5.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared thoroughly. Surface vegetation and other unsuitable materials should be 
removed and disposed of properly off-site. Soft and yielding areas encountered 
during clearing below areas designated to receive fill and/or future improvements 
should be over-excavated to expose firm material and the resulting excavation 
should be backfilled with well-compacted fill. The excavated soft soils should be 
properly disposed of off-site and/or used in landscape areas, where appropriate. 
After clearing, the exposed subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 
8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2% above the optimum moisture, and 
recompacted to a minimum of 90% relative compaction. The compaction 
requirements for finished subgrades subjected to vehicular traffic, such as the 
pavement subgrades, should be increased to a minimum of 95% relative compaction. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.  
If the subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade preparation, the subgrade may 
be proof-rolled and a woven geotextile fabric, such as Mirafi 500X or equivalent, 
should be placed on the subgrade prior to placement of subsequent lifts of fill.

materials provided that the maximum particle size is less than 3 inches in largest 
dimension. The on-site materials generated from the excavations may be used as a 
source of general fill or backfill materials provided that they are screened of the over-

Imported materials required for site filling should consist of select granular fill

3.5.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Fill materials consisting of the on-site soils should be moisture-conditioned to at least

Corrosion Potential 
Design of metallic substructures, such as electrical conduits or other metallic 
piping, should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure.  
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
regarding the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the 
resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of 
metallic objects.

A total of 156 soil resistivity tests were conducted at six locations, designated as 
R-1 through R-6, using the in-situ Wenner 4-Pin Method and an AEMC Model 6472 
resistance meter manufactured by AEMC Instruments. Each field resistivity test was 
conducted at thirteen selected pin spacings with a maximum pin spacing of about 300 
feet, and then repeated in the orthogonal (90-degree) orientation. 
The Wenner 4-Pin Method utilizes four surface probes or pins (electrodes) to 
evaluate the average resistivity (an indication of corrosivity) of the underlying soil mass. 
The field resistivity survey was used to evaluate the average resistivity values of the soil 
mass at the approximate survey locations. Results of the field resistivity surveys are 
presented in Appendix D. It should be noted that the field resistivity survey may be 
influenced by temperature, weather conditions, soil moisture, existing underground 
utilities and other factors. 
Based on the results of our field resistivity survey, the subsurface soils at the 
project site have resistivity values ranging from about 77 to 7,082 ohm-cm, corresponding 
to corrosion ratings of 1 to 4, respectively. It should be noted that the resistivity values of 
the subsurface soils varied substantially with depth and location across the project area. 
Therefore, special attention should be given by the corrosion engineer when using the 
resistivity results provided in this report.

The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 30 
boreholes, designated as Borings B-1 through B-26 and I-1 through I-4, extending to depths 
of approximately 5 to 31.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plate 3.1 through 3.3. The borings were drilled using 
a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-30. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the Standard 
Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the 
field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive 
strength of the sample. The pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on the Logs 
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Fifteen Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations of the test 
results are provided on Plates B-1 and B-2. 
Eight Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plate B-3 and B-4. 
Four one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples 
to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils.  The test results are 
summarized on Plate B-5. 
Four Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-6 through B-9. 
Seven 
Unconsolidated 
Undrained 
Triaxial 
Compression 
(TXUU) 
tests 
(ASTM D2850) were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear 
strength of the clayey silt soils encountered.  The approximate in-situ effective overburden 
pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the relatively “undisturbed” soil 
sample.  The test results and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-10 
through B-16. 
Six Unconfined Compression (UC) tests (ASTM D2166) were performed on 
selected in-situ samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the on-site 
cohesive soils. The test results are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. Individual stress-strain curves of the unconfined compression tests are 
presented on Plates B-17 through B-22. 
Four laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils.  The test results are presented on Plates B-23 through B-26. 
One Modified Proctor compaction test (ASTM D1557) was performed on a bulk 
sample of the near-surface soil to evaluate the relationship between the moisture content

Field Infiltration Tests 
Four field infiltration tests were conducted at the site to evaluate the infiltration 
characteristics of the subsurface materials encountered. The infiltration tests were 
performed at four selected locations, Borings I-1 through I-4, as shown on the Site Plans, 
Plates 3.1 through 3.3. These tests were performed in general accordance with the 
procedures in Appendix D of the State of Maryland, Department of the Environment 
“Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I and II” (rev. 2009). These procedures are 
consistent with other state’s procedures and may generally be considered an industry 
standard. 
The field infiltration tests were performed by augering boreholes to a selected test 
depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface. Upon reaching the test depth, a 
solid casing was set to the bottom of the drilled hole to allow infiltration only through the 
soil exposed on the bottom of the boring. Falling head infiltration tests were performed to 
determine the infiltration rate of the underlying subsurface materials. Each test consisted 
of four trials of filling the casing with 24 inches of water and taking periodic readings over 
a one-hour trial period. The infiltration rates are then calculated based on the result of the 
fourth and last trial for each test location. The test results are presented on Plates C-1 
through C-4.

Field Resistivity Surveys 
Geophysical survey equipment (i.e. resistance meter) was mobilized to the project 
site on November 2, 2021 for the field resistivity survey. A total of 156 soil resistivity surveys 
were conducted at six locations, designated as R-1 through R-6, using the in-situ Wenner 
4-Pin Method and an AEMC Model 6472 resistance meter manufactured by AEMC 
Instruments. The method utilizes four surface probes or pins (electrodes) to evaluate the 
average resistivity of the underlying soil mass. The average resistivity values of the soil 
mass, extending from the existing ground surface to the test depth, was assessed by using 
this method at the approximate survey locations shown on the Overall Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The soil resistivity surveys were performed along the existing ground surface at the 
approximate survey locations and then repeated in the orthogonal direction, as shown on 
the site plan. The average soil resistance at the project site was evaluated at each survey 
location with the electrodes aligned at equal spacing of generally 1, 2, 3, 5, 10, 25, 50, 75, 
100, 150, 200, 250 and 300 feet. The depth of penetration of a resistivity sounding is a 
function of the electrode spacing. 
The resistance meter (AEMC Model 6472) provides a voltage between the outer 
electrodes, causing an electrical current to flow between them. The voltage drop between 
the inner electrodes is then measured by a sensitive, built-in voltmeter. As a result, the soil 
resistance (in ohms) is obtained from the voltmeter dial reading. The soil resistivity (in ohm-
cm), which is directly related to both resistance and spacing, can then be computed. The 
evaluated soil resistances and computed resistivity values are presented on Plates D-1 
through D-6. In addition, the computed resistivity values with pin spacing at the test locations 
are presented in a graph on Plate D-7. At the time of resistivity surveying, the temperature 
was about 75º to 80º F. The existing ground surface was damp to moist. It should be noted 
that the soil resistivity may be influenced by temperature, weather conditions, existing 
underground utilities and other factors.
