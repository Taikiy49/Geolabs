This letter report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
for the proposed emergency road repairs project at 3707 Round Top Drive in Honolulu on 
the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This letter report summarizes the results of our findings and geotechnical

analyses performed. The design recommendations presented in this letter report are 
intended for the soil nail retaining wall only. The findings and recommendations presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report. 
Project Considerations 
We understand that the existing terrace rock walls collapsed during the heavy 
rain storm, which exposed saturated soft/loose steep soil slope encroaching the existing 
road shoulder. The steep failed slope was about 30 feet long along the property of 
3707 Round Top Drive. It was proposed to evaluate and design the alternatives of the 
emergency road repairs.  
Several possible stabilization alternatives were evaluated for feasibility and 
constructability, including drilled shaft wall, conventional retaining wall, soldier pile 
concrete lagging wall and soil nail retaining wall. Each alternate was evaluated for slope 
stability and construction cost estimate. The drilled shaft wall system was determined as 
non-economical solution to mitigate this relatively shallow slope failure. The 
conventional retaining wall system requiring extensive grading is not a desirable 
mitigation solution. The soldier pile concrete lagging wall system may have difficulty for 
construction. The soil nail retaining wall system was believe to be a viable mitigation 
solution and selected for final design and construction.

Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield 
volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a series 
of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone eruptions. These later eruptions are referred 
to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present sea level. 
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the 
sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic sea 
level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations. 
Following the extrusion of lavas in the early Pleistocene Epoch, the island 
underwent a long cycle of erosion and weathering forming the prominent ridgelines and 
summits as we know today. During the erosion period, the interfluvial ridges, and areas 
such as the Schofield Plateau, were subjected to deep chemical weathering, forming a 
mantle of residual soil and saprolite which grades with depth to saprolite and weathered 
basalt rock formation.

During the Pleistocene Epoch, renewal of volcanic activity occurred with the 
eruptions of vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series. Along the crest of the ridge along the 
westerly side of Manoa Valley, there are the Tantalus, Roundtop, and Sugarloaf vents. 
The project site is situated on an interfluvial ridge on the southeastern flank of Koolau. The 
majority of the project area is underlain by clayey soils which grade with depth to basalt 
rock formation. 
Subsurface Condition 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling one boring 
extending to a depth of approximately 41.5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Approximate boring location is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  
In general, the project site is underlain by stiff surface fills, loose cinder sands and 
stiff clayey residual soils overlying weathered basalt rock formation at greater depth. It 
should be noted that the cinder sand may be weakly cemented with less than 10 percent 
fine content (passing No. 200 sieve). 
In addition, six (6) dynamic cone penetrometer (DCP) tests were conducted near 
the edge of the slope and on the slope, where the drill rig was not able to access. The 
DCP tests were extended to depths of about 6 to 9 feet below the ground surface. The 
results of DCP tests indicate 4 to 6 feet of loose debris/fill overlying stiff native ground. 
Based on review of the topography map provided, we anticipate that the loose debris/fill 
from the slope collapse may be extended up to an elevation of about +1230 feet Mean 
Sea Level (MSL). 
We did not encounter groundwater in the boring drilled at the time of our field 
exploration.  It should be noted that groundwater levels can significantly vary depending on 
rainfall, temperature, surface runoff, groundwater withdrawal, and other factors.

Discussion and Recommendations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration and 
existing topographic conditions, we believe that soil nail retaining wall system may be a 
viable mitigation solution. To reduce risk of future undermining and/or erosion, we 
recommend removal of the loose debris/fill from the previous slope failure by extending 
the bottom of the soil nail retaining wall to an elevation of about +1,230 feet MSL. We 
also recommend installing erosion control matting with hydro-mulching on face of the 
slope in front of the soil nail retaining wall.

The soil nails would provide lateral resistance to the new retaining wall in order to

Soil Nail Retaining Walls 
A soil nail retaining wall system consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars 
grouted into drilled holes to stabilize an excavation or slope. A shotcrete facing is 
generally applied to the face of the excavation providing as either temporary support or 
the permanent retaining wall facing. The reinforcing bars provide both additional tensile 
and shear strengths to the soil and reinforce the sidewall of the excavation. The 
reinforced soil mass behaves in a manner similar to a gravity retaining wall but lacks a 
footing. In general, the face of the soil nail retaining wall system is slightly battered at a 
slope inclination of about one horizontal to twelve vertical (1H:12V). 
Soil nail retaining walls are normally installed in a cut condition only. In the event 
that the proposed retaining walls may require partial fills, we recommend that the fills be 
placed first using shotcrete or flowable fill, to allow construction of the soil nail retaining 
wall entirely in a cut condition. 
Design Parameters 
Design of the soil nail retaining wall system will need to consider both the internal 
and external stability of the reinforced soil mass. The design of the internal 
stability includes establishing the size, spacing, orientation, and length of the 
grouted reinforcing bars. The external stability includes overall slope stability of 
the reinforced slope. The geotechnical design parameters needed for evaluating 
the internal and external stability of the soil nail retaining wall system are 
provided in the following table.

The bond stress, which is the pullout resistance per unit area of the grout/soil 
interface contact, is variable depending on the type of soil and grout, the 
overburden stress, and the construction procedures used in installing these 
grouted reinforcing bars. For a rough estimate, an average bond stress of 
1,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the on-site sandy soils. The 
bond stress used in the design will need to be confirmed in the field during 
construction. 
Based on our preliminary slope stability analyses, the length of the soil nails may 
be about 100 percent of the height of the wall depending on the geometry of the 
slope. The soil nail retaining wall system of up to about 20 feet high should have 
a factor of safety of 1.5 or greater for the combined internal and external stability 
for the service loading condition. For the seismic loading condition, a factor of 
safety of 1.0 or greater for the combined internal and external stability should be 
achieved. 
Design Considerations 
It should be noted that our soil nail retaining wall evaluation is based on the 
following assumptions. We recommend that the soil nails should require some form 
of corrosion protection, such as a fully encapsulated double corrosion protection 
system. 
Soil nail of 8-inch diameter with #9 reinforcing bar; 
Soil nail inclination of 15-degree from horizontal; 
Soil nail pattern with 5-foot vertical and horizontal spacing 
(center-to-center); 
Design bar yield stress of 36 ksi and punching shear of 45 kips at bar 
head; 
First layer of soil nail at 3 feet below top of the retaining wall; 
Surcharge of 150 psf behind the retaining wall and earthquake 
loading condition under a horizontal acceleration of 0.22g. 
The design of the soil nail retaining wall system does not include hydrostatic 
pressures that might be caused by groundwater or water trapped behind the 
walls. Therefore, the soil nail retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the 
build-up of hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of 
2-foot wide strips of a prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or 
EnkaDrain, placed on the face of the excavation between the soil nails. The 
prefabricated drainage product should extend from the top to the base of the wall 
and should be hydraulically connected to a weephole at the base of the wall, as 
shown on Plate 3. The weepholes should discharge to appropriate outlets.

Soil Nail Testing 
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated behind the soil nail retaining 
walls, we recommend performing at least one verification test on the production 
soil nails. In addition, proof tests should be performed on at least 10 percent of 
the production soil nails during construction to confirm the bond stresses used in 
the design. 
The verification test should be loaded gradually to 150 percent of the design load 
for the creep test and subsequently to the maximum test load of about 
200 percent of the design load. In addition, the load at every loading increment 
should be held for at least 10 minutes, and creep tests should be held for at least 
60 minutes. The proof tests should consist of subjecting the soil nail to at least 
150 percent of the design loads and the load should be held for at least 
10 minutes (until stable). The test nails may be incorporated into the permanent 
soil nail retaining wall provided that they satisfy the test criteria. Pullout tests and 
creep tests on the soil nails are integral components of the design for the soil nail 
retaining wall system. Therefore, we recommend conducting pullout tests and 
creep tests in the presence of a Geolabs representative. 
Workmanship 
Construction of the permanent soil nail retaining wall should be performed by a 
specialty contractor experienced in the construction of soil nail retaining walls. 
Due to the specialized nature of the soil nail retaining wall construction, a 
Geolabs representative should be present to observe the geotechnical aspects of 
the soil nail retaining wall construction and testing of soil nails. 
Due to the loose consistency of sandy fill materials behind the existing retaining 
wall as encountered in the boring, caving-in and/or sloughing may occur during the 
soil nail drilling. To reduce the potential for caving-in of the drilled holes, temporary 
casing of the drilled holes may be required during the soil nail installation. 
It is common that some large boulder or cobble may be encountered in the fill 
materials. Therefore, hard drilling conditions may be encountered in the localized 
areas. In addition, due to potential variation of dense basalt rock outcrop behind the 
retaining wall, hard drilling conditions may also be anticipated. 
Drilling fluids including mineral and polymer slurry should not be allowed for the soil 
nail installation. If utilizing air pressure during the drilling, the contractor should be 
cautious when drilling is advanced into the loose sandy material to prevent

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling one borehole, designated as Boring No. 1, extending to a depth of about 
41.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  The approximate boring location is shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  The boring was drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary wash drilling/coring tools. 
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous basis.  These classifications were further reviewed visually and by 
testing in the laboratory.  Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1.  Deviations made 
to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Boring, Plates A-1.1 and A-1.2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches.  In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop.  The blow counts

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content and Unit Weight tests were performed on selected soil samples 
to aid in defining the interface between the various soil layers encountered and 
correlating the layers in the boring.  The moisture content tests were performed in 
general accordance with ASTM D2216.  The unit weight tests were performed in 
general accordance with ASTM D2937.  The test results are presented on the Logs of 
Boring at the appropriate sample depths in Appendix A. 
One Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D4318) was performed on a selected soil 
sample to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification.  The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depth. Graphic 
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM C117 & C136) was performed to evaluate the 
gradation characteristics and to aid in soil classification. The test was performed in 
accordance with ASTM C117 and C136. Graphic presentation of the test results is 
provided on Plate B-2. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested.  Direct shear test 
results are presented on Plates B-3 and B-4.

Dynamic Cone Penetrometer Tests 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were also explored by conducting 
Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) testing at six locations, designated as DCP-1 
through DCP-6, extending to depths of about 6 to 9 feet below the existing ground 
surface. The approximate DCP locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Portable Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) was used for probing by driving an 
8-mm diameter 60° steel cone tip with an 8-kg hammer falling 575-mm in vertical height. 
The blow counts were recorded per every or near 75-mm (3-inch) penetration and 
converted to standard penetration resistance (SPT, ASTM D1586) using correlation 
between Penetration Index (PI) and SPT, developed by Sowers and Hedges and later 
Livneh and Ishai. Both PI and SPT blow counts are shown on the Logs of DCP at the 
appropriate depths, presented on the Logs of DCP, Plates C-1.1 through C-6.2.
