SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
During our site reconnaissance, volcanic tuff formation was observed along the trail
surface and at the exposed face of the upper and lower slopes adjacent to the trail. The
volcanic tuff exposed along the trail ranged from soft to hard. Due to the large amount of
foot traffic along the existing trail, the surface of the volcanic tuff along portions of the trail
was weathered to silty sand. Seepage water at the ground surface was not observed at
the time of our site visit.
Fill placement is not desired for this project due to the steep existing ground
conditions. Therefore, the majority of the site grading will involve cut slopes and
excavation. If required, fills should consist of lean concrete. Excavation into the adjacent
slope will be required to provide a minimum 3-foot wide path in an area along the trail. We
believe that cut slopes into the hard volcanic tuff may be cut at an inclination of near
vertical. After slope cutting, rock slope scaling should be performed to remove loose
material on the cut slope face and above the top of the cut to reduce the poteniial for
future rockfall hazards. The condition of the slope face above the trail should be
monitored periodically during the year and after heavy rainfall events.
The path pavement will consist of a structural concrete slab-on-grade with the new
railing embedded into the concrete slab. Because of the sloping ground conditions and
the lateral loading at the edge of the slab, we recommend supporting the new concrete
slab and railing system by rock anchors. Based on our analysis, preliminary anchor length
of 12 feet may be used with minimum hole diameter of 2.25 inches. The anchor holes
should be filled with a 1.25-inch diameter, Grade 75 threaded stainless steel bar
(Type 316) and non-shrink cement grout. Because of the difficulties of mobilizing and
demobilizing large construction equipment to and from the project site and the limited
construction space available along the existing path, the size and depth of the anchor
holes were minimized so more portable equipment may be used for the anchor installation.
Preconstruction testing on one sacrificial rock anchor under tension loading should
be performed prior to ordering materials for the production rock anchors. Extended creep
test should be performed on the preconstruction anchor. Proof testing should be
performed on the production anchors.
The text of this report should be referred to for more detailed discussions and
specitic site preparation recommendations.

SECTION 1. GENERAL
1.1. Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering study performed
for the Diamond Head State Monument, Ledge Trail project in the Honolulu District on
the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the
Project Location Map, Plate 1.
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our site reconnaissance and engineering analyses.
These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations, slabs-on-grade,
and site preparation only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report.
1.2. Project Considerations
The Diamond Head State Monument, Ledge Trail project site is within Diamond
Head Crater on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. We understand that preliminary drawings
for the Ledge Trail as part of the Summit Trail Improvements project have been
developed by others. Furthermore, we understand that it is desired to re-evaluate and
re-design the preliminary design of the Ledge Trail.
Based on the preliminary plans for the Summit Trail Improvements project, the
proposed new Ledge Trail consists of a steel framed structure supported by micropiles.
The micropiles are 4 inches in diameter with a minimum depth of 15 feet. Reinforced
concrete slab-on-grade with rock anchors and dowels are located adjacent to the steel
structure for portions of the trail. The rock anchors are battered into the existing slope
and are 4 inches in diameter with minimum 10 feet length. The use of vertical and
horizontal dowels with a diameter of 2 inches and with minimum length of 12 inches are
shown for the concrete slab.
The Ledge Trail is about 90 feet in length and extends from the existing Fire
Control Station to the Lookout Landing. We understand that the new trail will consist of

SECTION 2. SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2.1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of the
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger Koolau Volcano forms the majority of
the eastern two-thirds of the islands. It is believed that Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in
central Oahu.
Volcanic activity from Koolau Volcano ceased for nearly 2 million years. Volcanic
activity once again resumed from mid-Pleistocene Epoch and has lasted through recent
geologic time. These volcanic events, known as the Honolulu Volcanic Series, have
formed cinder, spatter, tuff and ash cones throughout the southern portion of the Island
of Oahu.
One of the most prominent vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series is Diamond
Head. Diamond Head is a tuff cone and was generally formed by hydromagmatic
explosions. Materials within the tuff cone consist of magmatic debris, blocks of Koolau
basalt, and fragments of coral limestone torn from the underlying reef. The project site is
located on the western slope of Diamond Head Crater.
2.2 Site Description
The project site is within the western portion of Diamond Head Crater on the
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The existing trail from the Fire Control Station to the metal
stairway to the summit lookout is unpaved. The width of the existing trail varies from
about 2 to 8 feet. Steel pipe railing with chain link fencing was noted on the downslope
side of the trail. Low to tall brush was noted along the flatter portions of the upper slope
area and along the lower slope area.
The existing elevations along the trail range from about +727 to +731 feet Mean
Sea Level (MSL). The existing slope above the trail range from about one horizontal to

3.1. Site Grading
The site grading work will generally consist of cuts and fills of less than 1 foot for
the project site. In addition, slope excavation up to about 7 feet in height is anticipated
for a portion of the new trail to accommodate a minimum 3-foot wide path.
A Geolabs representative should monitor site grading operations to observe
whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation process and to
confirm whether the exposed rock conditions are similar to those observed during our
site reconnaissance.
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site or stockpiled
in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated
materials.

Soft and yielding areas encountered during the clearing and grubbing should be
over-excavated to expose firm natural material, and the resulting excavation should be

Fills and backfills, if required, should consist of lean concrete.
Based on preliminary grading plans and our observations, the trail excavation

Based on our site observations, it appears that most of the anticipated cut slopes
would expose material consisting of hard volcanic tuff. In general, we believe that cut
slopes into the hard volcanic tuff may be cut at an inclination of near vertical. After slope
cutting, rock slope scaling to remove loose material on the cut slope face and above the
top of cut should be performed to reduce future rockfall hazards.
3.2 Rock Slope Scaling
Rock slope scaling is typically performed to reduce the potential for rockfall
activity on slopes composed mainly of rock material. Rock slope scaling is performed to
remove loose fractured rock material from the slope face. The work is typically
performed manually by skilled scaling crews, who may be suspended on the hillside
using climbing gear. Hand tools such as pry bars are most commonly used to scale the
loose rock material from the slope face. The work is performed in a methodical
controlled manner from the top to bottom to move the scaled material toward the bottom
of the slope for collection and disposal. Temporary protection of existing down slope
structures and persons is required during the scaling work.
Rock slope scaling may need to be repeated as the rock material continues to
weather, which may produce additional loose rock as time progresses. In some cases
where the rock is weak and broken with many closely spaced fractures, rock slope
scaling may need to be repeated at shorter 5 to 10 year intervals. The condition of the

3.3 Path Pavement
Rigid pavement will be used for the new Ledge Trail path. Based on our field
observations, we anticipate the path pavement will be underlain by soft to hard volcanic
tuff formation. We understand that a structural concrete slab-on-grade will be used for
the path pavement. In addition, we understand that the new railing will be embedded
into the structural concrete slab system.
Because of the sloping ground conditions and the lateral loading at the edge of
the slab, we recommend supporting the new concrete slab and railing system by rock
anchors. The rock anchors will help resist the anticipated vertical and lateral loading.
Discussion on the rock anchors is provided below.
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry
surface water off-site. Surface water ponding should not be allowed on-site during or
after construction.
Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of the slab excavations
should be over-excavated to expose the underlying firm materials. The over-excavation
should be backfilled with lean concrete. The slab should be poured neat against the
near-vertical excavation within the volcanic tuff formation to help resist future
erosion/undermining.
3.4 Rock Anchors
A rock anchor is constructed by drilling a borehole, installing a steel tendon or
reinforcing bar into the borehole, and grouting the borehole. The basic components of a
grouted rock anchor consist of the anchorage and the bonded length of the steel tendon
or reinforcing bar. The bonded length of the rock anchor is the portion of the steel
tendon (or reinforcing bar) that is directly bonded to the grout allowing it to transfer the
applied tensile load into the ground.

We recommend locating the top of the lower bonded length about 2 feet below
the bottom of the footing level. Subsequent to anchor testing, the upper bonded length
should be grouted up to the bottom of footing level.
Because of the difficulties of mobilizing and demobilizing large construction
equipment to and from the project site and the limited construction space available
along the existing path, the size and depth of the anchor holes were minimized so more
portable equipment may be used for the anchor installation.
In general, the rock anchors would derive support principally from the bond
between the grout and the soft to hard volcanic tuff formation observed at the project
site. The bond stress, which is the pullout resistance per unit area of the grout/rock

stress, and the construction procedures used in installing the rock anchors. An average
bond stress of 5,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the soft to hard
volcanic tuff formation. The bond stress used in the design will need to be confirmed in

The performance of the rock anchors depends significantly upon the contractor's
method of construction and construction procedures. As a result of the potential

variations in the subsurface materials encountered at the project site, a Geolabs
representative should be present to monitor the installation of the rock anchors
during construction. A specialty contractor experienced in the construction of rock
anchors should perform construction of the rock anchors.
Grouting of the rock anchors has a major impact on the load carrying capacities of
the rock anchors. A neat-cement grout or sand-cement grout mix should be used.
Placement of grout should be by tremie methods beginning from the bottom of the
hole. The grout should be placed through the tremie pipe by pressure methods.
The grouting operations should be conducted prompily (within 8 hours) after drilling
to reduce the potential for caving-in of the sides of the drilled holes.
3.4.2 Rock Anchor Testing
In general, we recommend performing preconstruction testing on one sacrificial
rock anchor under tension loading before ordering materials for the production rock
anchors. Extended creep testing should be performed on the preconstruction
anchor. The extended creep test consists of subjecting the rock anchor to at least
133 percent of the design loads, and the load should be held for at least 5 hours.
Pullout tests (proof tests) should be performed on the production rock anchors
during construction to confirm the bond stresses used in the design. We
recommend testing all of the constructed production rock anchors for pullout. The
pullout tests should consist of subjecting the rock anchors to at least 133 percent of
the design loads and the load should be held for at least 10 minutes.
Creep and pullout tests on the rock anchors are integral parts of the design.
Therefore, we recommend conducting the creep and pullout tests under the
observation of a Geolabs representative.
