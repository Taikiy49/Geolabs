Subsoil Conditions
The borings indicate that the subsoil conditions in the subject study
areas generally conform with the typical subsoil conditions encountered in
the Woodlawn area along the southern hillside of Manoa Valley
Generally speaking the borings encountered a clayey or sandy surface
fill layer over adobe clay which was then underlain by silty gravelly
and boulderly colluvium to the drilled depths of the borings The fill
and clay layers vary in consistency and thickness with location and
underground water conditions but the clay layer generally increases in
thickness towards the bottom of the valley The fill material was found
to be a variable mixture of sand gravel and clayey silt generally in a
fairly competent condition The clay alluvium is generally a weak soil
particularly when saturated The colluvium layer generally has a very
stiff to hard consistency and is considered a competent formation
The subsoil conditions and the observed major earth movement damages
of the study areas are discussed in further detail below

Mechanism of Earth Movements
The subject study areas are underlain by various thicknesses of
adobe clay soils which are known for their low strengths particularly
when saturated Since the observed damage distribution does not
correspond directly with the associated subsoil conditions it appears
that the thickness of the adobe clay layer is not the primary factor in
the mechanisms of the earth movements Therefore the role of soft wet
zones within the alluvial clay layer in the observed earth movement areas
was carefully studied
Based on a review of the damage history the observed existing damage
patterns and the associated groundwater conditions it appears that the
earth movements may have been initiated by a gradual downhill creep of the
clay soils due to groundwater seepage This initial small creep movement
would eventually trigger utility line leakage in the upper study areas
such as Alani and Paty Drives Paty Extension and Hale Manoa As water
found underground drainage channels and or seepage paths downslope the
alluvial clays along the water passages were further saturated and
weakened This would cause further sliding under gravity and hydraulic
forces and probably result in additional utility line leakages In some
critical areas the subsoils could then be eroded away into the broken
utilities
These underground water and earth movements would continue to induce
more utility leakage downslope and the additional leakage further
aggravated the earth movements Eventually the underground earth
movements were propagated upwards and manifested in the observable forms
of scarps and toes cracks and settlements etc on the ground surface

Since the patterns of the earth movements would be strongly
controlled by the subsurface drainage channels and or seepage paths of the
underground water this would explain why soft and wet zones were
encountered only in some of the drilled borings and the observed damage
varies significantly for locations in the same general area with basically
similar subsoil conditions In other words the observed damaged areas
are probably an indication of the path of the subsurface water

Remedial Work
Since the primary mechanism of the observed earth movements is
underground water flow seepage from various sources mostly in the upper
study areas it is our opinion that the initial remedial work should
include repair of all of the leaking or damaged lines which is currently
underway and an adequate trench drain system in these areas to intercept
and divert the subsurface water before it flows downslope in an
uncontrollable and damaging pattern
Without these first steps we feel that other remedial work will have
very little chance of success
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1964 00 Page 14
Dk No 49
After installation of the initial remedial work a long term
monitoring program should be employed to observe its performance before
the commencement of repair of the existing major damages in a subsequent
phase of the remedial work We believe that a one year observation period
may be sufficient for this purpose
If a remedial scheme to affect an immediate reduction of movements is
preferred it is our opinion that the alluvial clays should be completely
removed in conjunction with the installation of a trench drain system in
the upper study areas However this upgraded scheme would unavoidably be
much more expensive It may also involve construction difficulties such
as the maintenance of through traffic flow and the risk of undermining
adjacent house lots
It should be noted that the already damaged house lots are
especially vulnerable to further ground movements which may be induced by
the remedial earthwork operations
Should more funding be available it is our opinion that additional
retaining structures such as soil anchors crib walls and gabion walls
etc should be installed to help stabilize the already damaged house lots
in the upper areas and also to reduce the potential of further long term
downslope earth movements from these areas
For the abandoned or severely damaged private house lots we suggest
that the houses be evaluated for structural safety by qualified
consultants before any remedial measures are proposed for the houses It
appears that an alternative may be to remove some of the houses to allow
removal of the underlying adobe clay then reconstruct replace the
houses It also appears advantageous to remove some of the houses
permanently to provide access for the remedial work

There are many possible schemes for the remedial work at the site
Due to possible funding limitations as well as legal considerations the
various schemes have been presented in terms of a Basic Remedial Scheme
which provides the minimum work which we feel is necessary to allow a
reasonable chance of slowing down the movements Generally the basic
remedial work involves construction only within the City s right of way
However recognizing the damage to the adjacent private property and the
difficulty of working adjacent to these damaged areas Optional Remedial

Due to the variations in surface topography the localized subsoil

Alani and Paty Drives
Basic Remedial Scheme Since water seepage from natural sources as
well as utility line leakage appear to be the prime source of the
problem we believe remedial provisions should be made to intercept
underground seepage It is our opinion that an efficient method to
provide drainage would be the installation of trench drains under the
roads It appears that there may be many voids seepage channels
under the pavement section In order to attempt to seal these and to
create a firmer material to allow trenching of the trench drains we
recommend that pressure grouting under the road section be performed
first Following the grouting a double line of trench drains is
recommended The trench drain on the lower side of the road would
intercept future utility line leakage while the drain on the upper
side of the road would intercept natural hillside seepage
The trenches should extend down through the adobe clay layer or toa
maximum depth of 20 feet below the existing ground surface to
intercept and drain subsurface water A typical section of such a

LIMITATIONS
The general site is underlain by adobe clay soils which are subject
to long term creep movements The recommendations presented in this
report should help to reduce drastic movements however creep movements
may continue
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based
in part upon information obtained from field data points such as borings
slope indicators and observation wells Variations of conditions between
the field data points may occur and the nature and extent of these
variations may not become evident until construction If variations then
appear evident it will be necessary to re evaluate the recommendations
given in this report
