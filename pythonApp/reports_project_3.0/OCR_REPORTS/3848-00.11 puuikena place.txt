INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Ting Residence at 11 Puuikena Place in East Honolulu on
the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses of the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input
only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located at 11 Puuikena Place in the Hawaii Loa Ridge
Subdivision on the island of Oahu Hawaii Based on the information provided to us the
subject lot has an area of approximately 13 119 square feet It is proposed to build a
two story home We understand that a two car garage at the north end of the lot will have
a proposed finish grade of approximately 10 feet below the existing grade The north side
of the house will be supported on a cut condition at the proposed finish grade of the
garage We also understand that fills will not be placed on the south side of the house
and the house will be supported on post and beam foundation Detailed information
pertaining to the structural loads were not available at the time this report was prepared
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the subsurface
conditions at the site to develop a soil rock data set for the formulation of geotechnical
engineering recommendations pertinent to the proposed project To accomplish this our
exploration program consisted of the following tasks and work efforts

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age and forms
the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lava banking
against its eastern flank
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3848 00 Page 4
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of the Koolau Range and is
composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lava from the Koolau Volcanic Series
These rocks are generally characterized by flows of jointed dense vesicular basalt
interbedded with thin clinker layers

The project site is located at 11 Puuikena Place in the Hawaii Loa Ridge

House Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site it is our
opinion that a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings
may be used to support the proposed new residence An allowable bearing pressure of
up to 4 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be used for the design of footings bearing
on the basalt cobbles and boulders encountered at shallow depths This bearing value is
for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such
as those caused by wind or seismic forces
The bottom of the footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below
the lowest adjacent finish grade Where massive basalt rock is encountered the footing
embedment may be reduced to 10 inches For footings constructed near the top of
slopes or on slopes the footings should be embedded deep enough such that the
horizontal distance from the outside edge at the base of the footing to the slope face is
not less than 8 feet Continuous foundations running parallel to the direction of the slope
should be constructed in stepped footings
Where foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should
be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3848 00 Page 7
The bottom of the footing excavations should be recompacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage
by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density If soft and or loose materials
are encountered at the bottom of footing excavations they should be over excavated to
expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation should be backfilled with
select granular material compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the
footing bottom may be extended down to the underlying competent material
If the foundations are designed in accordance with our recommendations we
estimate total settlements to be less than 1 inch with differential settlements on the order
of inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil Resistance due to
passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of
350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f This assumes that the soil around
footings is well compacted A coefficient of friction of 0 4 may be used for footings
bearing on recompacted boulders and cobbles Unless covered by pavements or slabs
the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the
foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depths

Cavity Detection
Based on our field exploration and past experience in the area voids and or
cavities may be present in the basalt rock formation encountered at the project site To
reduce the potential for loss of building foundation support resulting from collapse of the
cavities below a footing we recommend that a program of either GPR survey or probing
and grouting be implemented during construction The following subsections address
issues regarding the cavity detection programs
Ground Penetrating Radar GPR
The use of Ground Penetrating Radar GPR to assist in the location of buried
objects structures or voids is one of several available geophysical non intrusive
type surveys GPR is used to locate anomalies based on the electrical properties
of the materials surveyed
As in many geophysical surveys the result of the survey is subject to professional
interpretation of the data recorded by the radar machine Therefore the GPR
survey program should be conducted under the direction of a representative from
Geolabs Hawaii This would allow our firm to make interpretations of the data
obtained and conduct additional surveys as deemed necessary to monitor the
presence of cavities
Any shallow cavities discovered should be exposed and backfilled with
compacted fill or sand cement grout injected at low to moderate pressures We
recommend that a low strength sand cement grout mixture with a slump range of
6 to 9 inches be utilized for the grouting operations
GEOLABS HAWAII

Probing and Grouting
For estimating purposes we recommend that probes be drilled at each spread
footing location and at 10 foot on centers for continuous footings The probe holes
should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below
the planned bottom of footing If cavities and or voids are encountered or
suspected during the probing operation additional probes should be drilled at
closer spacings to help delineate the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or
void The probe holes and any cavities discovered should be backfilled with sand
cement grout injected at low to moderate pressures We recommend that a low
strength sand cement grout mixture with a slump range of 6 to 9 inches be utilized
for the grouting operations
Because of the potential for encountering large cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during
bidding In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing
and grouting operation is complete The contractor should also be made aware
that a longer lag time between probing grouting operations and foundation
construction may be required in the construction schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs Hawaii This would allow our firm to monitor the
presence of cavities and to allow additional recommendations to be made if
excess grout take and or changed conditions are observed
Slabs On Grade
Concrete slabs on grade are anticipated for the proposed residence For the
interior building slabs which are not subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that a
minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation or Basaltic Termite Barrier BTB be placed below the slabs to provide
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3848 00 Page 10
more uniform support of the slabs and to serve as a capillary break To reduce future
moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture
vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is recommended on top of the gravel or BTB
cushion layer Two inches of moist sand or BTB should be placed above the plastic
membrane to provide protection for the membrane and to aid in curing the slab concrete
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project In general
we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer of gravel BTB be provided
below the concrete walkway slab Contraction joints should be provided at intervals equal
to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at right angle intersections
Garage Slab and Concrete Driveway
For slabs on grade that will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as the garage
slab and the driveway a 6 inch thick layer of base course is recommended in lieu of the
cushion fill layer in order to provide more uniform support The base course should
consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction We recommend that the concrete slab be designed by a structural engineer
for the intended loads and should be reinforced as a structural slab to accommodate
some amount of differential settlement For design of structural slabs a modulus of
subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per cubic inch p c i may be used for the compacted
base course Provisions should be made for proper load transfer across the slab joints
which are subjected to vehicular traffic The subgrade soils beneath the garage and
driveway slabs should be prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented in
the Site Preparation section of this report

Drainage

pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the wall
discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used

upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of non expansive impervious material to

Fills and Backfills
Fill and backfill materials to be used as general and structural fill up to the slab or
pavement subgrade level should consist of well graded granular materials less
than 6 inches in largest dimension The on site cut materials may be used as fill or
backfill materials provided they are processed and reduced to a relatively
well graded select granular material with a maximum size of about 6 inches Any
imported material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles
greater than 3 inches in largest dimension The material should have a laboratory
California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of
1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883
Any imported material should be observed and or tested by Geolabs Hawaii for its
suitability prior to being transported to the site
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91

Excavation
Based on our field exploration the proposed residential site is underlain by basalt
cobbles and boulders at shallow depths It is anticipated that the near surface
cobbles and boulders may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment
such as ripping with large bulldozers However excavations into the deeper basalt
formations may require the use of hoerams or chipping
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based
on our visual observation of the existing rock formations and field data from the
borings performed at the site We recommend that all contractors proposing to bid
on this project be encouraged to examine the site conditions and the boring data to
make their own interpretation
Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings it appears that
most of the cut slopes would expose the basalt cobbles and boulders In general
we believe that cut slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or
flatter Where the cut slopes expose dense basalt formation at the slope face the
cut slope may be steepened to a slope inclination of 1H 1V Any cavities exposed
on the cut slope face should be backfilled and or grouted if necessary
Permanent fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or
flatter All fills to be placed on slopes with inclinations steeper than 5H 1V should
be keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill

The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling four
borings to depths of approximately 10 to 20 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings
were drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer geologist who monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Spilt Barrel Sampling of Soils
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
