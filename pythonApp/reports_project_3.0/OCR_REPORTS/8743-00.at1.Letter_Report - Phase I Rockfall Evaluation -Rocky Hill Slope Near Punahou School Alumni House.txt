In accordance with our fee proposal dated July 26, 2023, we conducted a Phase I 
evaluation of the potential rockfall hazard conditions at the project site and developed 
possible long-term mitigation measures for the site located near the Alumni House at 
Punahou School in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and 
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. A discussion of the 
observed site conditions and recommendations on rockfall mitigation measures are 
presented for the existing slopes on the southern side of the project site.  
This report presents our findings and preliminary recommendations from our visual 
observations of the existing site conditions at the project site. This report is based on our 
site reconnaissance, geological analysis, and engineering analysis of the potential natural 
hazards and hillslope deficiencies at the project site. The findings and preliminary 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located near Punahou School Alumni House on Kakela Place in 
Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The rocky hill slope is located east of the Alumni 
House, with an existing residential home at the toe of the hill slope. 
Loose rocks, debris, and cut pieces of trees were observed on a relatively level 
area between the upper and lower slopes. Additionally, exposed rock outcroppings were 
observed at the lower slope face, which is relatively close to the property line of the 
residential home. We understand that the residence at the home is concerned about the 
potential rockfall above his home.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield 
volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau.  The older volcano, Waianae, is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age, and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age. 
After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time erosion incised deep valleys into 
the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder 
and tuff cone eruptions. These later eruptions are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic 
Series. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available 
to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea.  When 
the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic sea 
level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was correspondingly 
lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. When the sea level 
was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments accumulated at higher 
elevations. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii, the erosional processes are dominated by 
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported downslope 
toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium. Once these materials reach 
the stream in the central portion of a valley, alluvial processes become dominant, and the 
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium. 
The project site is located near the foot of Roundtop Crater near the valley floor. 
The project site is underlain by thin deposits of mixed alluvial and colluvial materials 
consisting of clay with sand over cinder sand.  Basalt rock formation of the Koolau 
Volcanic Series forms the stratigraphic base of the region and is believed to underlie the 
near-surface deposits. 
GENERAL SITE CONDITIONS 
The subject slope is located at the northeastern corner of the Punahou School 
property boundary near the Alumni House. Based on our observations and the 
information provided, we understand that previous vegetation cutting operation along the 
slope has divided the slope into an upper uncut area and a lower cut area. 
Based on our field measurements and the topographic survey map provided by 
Punahou School, the lower slope and the upper slope are approximately 7 and 20 feet 
tall, respectively. The slope face inclinations range from about one-and-a-half horizontal 
to one vertical (1.5H:1V) on the south side to 5H:1V towards the north end. It should be 
noted that some near-vertical sections on the upper slope were observed.

SITE RECONNAISSANCE 
A visual site reconnaissance was conducted by two staff engineers on 
August 31, 2023. The purpose of our field reconnaissance was to observe the existing 
slope conditions and evaluate potential rockfall hazards. Site reconnaissance 
photographs depicting the observed conditions at the project site are presented on 
Plates 3.1 through 3.5. 
On the relatively flat area between lower and upper slopes, surface vegetation 
consisting of grass, shrubs, and cut down tree trunks and branches was observed (see 
Photograph No. 1, Plate 3.1). The slope material generally consisted of weathered 
basaltic cobbles, sand, and gravel. Cobbles and boulders observed on the face of the 
lower slope are generally about 6-inch to 2-foot in diameter, and due to the relatively flat 
inclination of the area, they pose low to moderate rockfall hazard potential (see 
Photograph No. 2, Plate 3.1). Additionally, construction debris, such as CMU and 
concrete blocks, was also observed on the slope face (see Photograph No. 3, Plate 3.2). 
It should be noted that the construction debris can pose a similar hazard potential as the 
observed boulders. 
Moderately to highly weathered rock outcrop was observed near the toe of the 
lower slope at the south end of the site (see Photograph No. 4, Plate 3.2). The outcrop 
was generally well embedded and near the toe of the slope; however, exposed sections 
with moderate undermining were observed. As shown in Photographs Nos. 5 and 6 
(Plate 3.3), a system of wire rope and a small bolt has been used to secure the exposed 
section of the outcrop. The approximate location of this outcrop is shown on the Site Plan 
(Plate 2). 
Moving north along the plateau between the upper and lower slope, Photograph 
No. 7 (Plate 3.4) depicts the surface conditions consisting of cut down tree trunks and 
branches and moderate vegetation growth. Rock blocks about 0.5 to 1 foot in diameter 
were noted. Additionally, construction debris, including concrete blocks and bricks, was 
observed on the slope face. We understand that two Kiawe trees were cut down and 
removed from this section of the slope (see Photograph No. 8, Plate 3.4). Along the 
northern property line, we observed a slightly weathered rock outcrop that was mostly 
embedded into the relatively flat slope. In addition, smaller loose concrete blocks were 
observed in the area (see Photograph No. 9, Plate 3.5).  
Loose cobbles and boulders ranging between 0.5 to 1.5 feet in size were observed 
along the upper slope; however, due to heavy vegetation growth on the slope face, closer 
observation of the slope face was not possible (see Photograph No. 10, Plate 3.5).

Rockfall Mitigation Design  
Based on our site reconnaissance and engineering analyses, we believe there are 
potential rockfall hazards at the project site. These rockfall hazards include 0.5 to 2 feet 
sized loose cobbles and boulders and construction debris throughout the upper slope and 
the relatively flatter area above the lower slope. Additionally, two partially detached and 
highly weathered boulders near the toe of the lower slope present a moderate rockfall 
hazard to the adjacent property. Therefore, we believe measures should be taken to 
mitigate the potential rockfall hazards at the project site. 
As previously mentioned, some geometric characteristics of the slopes include 
relatively flat inclination, relatively shorter slope heights, and a narrow distance between 
the toe of the slope and the property line. Additionally, the majority of the rockfall hazards 
observed on the slopes include loose rocks and debris that can be removed from the 
slope face. Therefore, we believe that mitigation measures appropriate for the project site 
include vegetation and debris clearing, boulder sweep, rock outcrop demolition, slope 
scaling, and drainage improvements.  
The following discussions of the rockfall mitigation measures provide additional 
details on the mitigation measures mentioned above. 
Slope Vegetation and Debris Clearing 
Based on our observations, the existing slopes are generally moderately to heavily 
vegetated with low-lying grass and shrubs, small to medium-sized trees, and a 
large Opiuma tree. Additionally, construction debris, such as concrete blocks and 
metal pipes, is spread along the slopes. 
Based on our evaluation, the existing vegetation and debris should be cleared and 
removed. All existing woody stem shrubbery and trees on the slope face should 
be cut flush with the finished ground surface and poisoned to reduce the potential 
for undesired regrowth on the slope face. The existing Opiuma tree near the south 
end of the slope should be cut close to the existing ground surface and poisoned. 
Subsurface tree trunk remnants should not be excavated (i.e., left in-place).

In addition, existing ground cover vegetation should be removed to completely 
expose the slope surface in preparation for slope scaling and boulder removal. 
The construction debris consisting of Concrete Masonry Unit (CMU), concrete 
blocks, and metal pipes should be removed. In addition, dead wood cuttings and 
branches should be removed. 
Boulder Sweep 
Boulder sweep consists of the removal of loose surface boulders (non-embedded 
to partly embedded) by heavy machinery and manual labor to move the targeted 
material off the slope. As previously mentioned, loose cobbles and boulders were 
observed throughout the upper and lower slopes and along the relatively flatter 
area in between the slopes.  Larger boulders in sensitive slope settings may be 
broken into smaller pieces by hoe ram chipping for easier handling and control of 
the material. 
Rock Outcrop Demolition 
Mechanical demolition of potential rockfall hazards by hoe ram chipping and 
excavation may be needed to separate some exposed rock outcroppings from 
parent bodies of rock buried in the subsurface. Rock outcrop demolition may be 
implemented to completely remove a surface rock outcropping or reduce the 
volume of the above-ground outcrop relief (protrusion) to stabilize the in-situ rock 
mass. In general, rock outcroppings containing open fractures and loose outcrop 
subcomponents should be completely removed, whereas solid, intact, and 
un-fractured rock may only require a reduction in mass to achieve a more stable 
remnant rock outcropping. The approximate location of the exposed outcropping 
is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Slope Scaling 
Slope scaling consists of experienced crews employing manual labor and hand 
tools to remove loose rocks and precariously positioned rock fragments from the 
slope face. The scaling crews performing the rock slope scaling should be 
experienced in the performance of rock slope scaling by rappelling and/or working 
from crane baskets. Slope scaling work typically proceeds by starting from the top 
of the slope and working downward in a controlled and orderly manner in 
conjunction with the combined progress of all the scaling crews working on the 
slope face.  
As previously mentioned, adequate temporary rockfall barriers should be installed 
near the base of the slopes prior to commencing the rock slope scaling work. The 
temporary barriers should be able to resist the anticipated kinetic energy of the 
falling boulders and be constructed to an adequate height to catch boulders that 
may bounce an appreciable height.

Drainage Improvements 
Embedded cobbles and boulders may potentially be exposed and create a rockfall 
hazard after heavy rain events. To reduce the potential for the above rockfall 
hazard, we recommend that drainage improvements be implemented. The 
drainage improvement should consist of the installation of a drainage swale or 
structure along the top of the slope to carry surface water away from the slope 
face.
