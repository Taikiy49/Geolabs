1 1 Introduction

performed for the A J W Office and Warehouse Building project located in the
vicinity of the intersection of Kalihi Street and Silva Street in Honolulu on the Island of
Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for
the A J W Office and Warehouse Building project These recommendations are
intended for the design of building foundations site preparation and cavity probing and
grouting only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated
to be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age As the volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano ceased lava flows
from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad
plateau now known as the Schofield Plateau
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea
When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits
at correspondingly higher elevations and deposited lagoonal marine sediments in the
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of
the calcareous reef materials Placement of man made fills and commercial
development within the last 30 years have brought the project site to its present form

3 2 Foundation Probing
Based on our field exploration voids were not encountered in our borings drilled
at the project site However our experience suggests that cavities and or voids are
commonly encountered in the coralline deposits in the vicinity of the project site To
reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of the cavities
and or sinkholes below foundations we recommend that a program of cavity probing
and grouting at the foundation locations be implemented during construction
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that probe holes be drilled at each spread footing location one probe hole
for every 50 square feet of footing area and at 10 feet on center along wall foundations
The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least
10 feet below the planned bottom of foundation elevation If cavities and or voids are
encountered or suspected during the probing operation additional probe holes should
be drilled at closer spacing to aid in delineating the vertical and lateral extent of the
cavity and or void The probe holes and cavities discovered should be backfilled with
sand cement grout injected at low to moderate pressures We recommend that a low
strength sand cement grout with a slump range of 6 to 9 inches be utilized for the
grouting operations In lieu of the sand cement grout fluid lean concrete such as
Controlled Low Strength Material flowable fill may also be used for the grouting
operation

3 3 Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be required for the construction of
the new building Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions we recommend that
6 inches of non expansive select granular fill material be provided below the slab
cushion for more uniform support
We recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill be placed below
the slabs to provide more uniform support The cushion fill should consist of open
graded gravel such as No 3 Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation which would
also serve as a Capillary break To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration
and subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture barrier is
recommended on top of the cushion layer
Where the concrete slabs on grade are subjected to future vehicular traffic or
machinery vibrations such as the loading zone or equipment pads a 6 inch layer of
aggregate subbase is recommended in lieu of the 4 inch thick gravel cushion layer
The aggregate subbase should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction We recommend that the concrete slabs be
designed by a structural engineer for the intended loads and should be reinforced as a
structural slab to accommodate some amount of differential movement
For design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of about
250 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection pci may be used for the compacted
aggregate subbase Where slabs are intended to function as rigid pavements a
minimum slab thickness of 6 inches may be used for preliminary design purposes

3 5 Drainage

from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter

swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface
run off away from the slabs and footing foundations

The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths of about 25 5 and

The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2

with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetratio0On sampler using the same hammer
and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches
