INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed 0 5 Million Gallon Reservoir of the Kalaheo Water System in
the Kalaheo area on the island of Kauai Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses for the proposed new reservoir project These recommendations are intended
for the design of foundations site grading and pavements only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located at the upper end of Poohiwi Road in the Kalaheo area on
the island of Kauai Hawaii Based on the information provided we understand that it is
proposed to construct a new 0 5 Million Gallon MG reservoir as part of the Kalaheo
Water System development The general layout of the new reservoir is shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2
Based on the plans provided the new reservoir will be of reinforced concrete
construction with a concrete floor slab The reservoir measuring approximately 75 feet in
diameter and 20 feet high will be located on the sloping hillside to the northeast of the
existing booster pump station The finish floor elevation for the new reservoir will be at
approximately 888 feet Mean Sea Level MSL with a minimum 6 inch thick reinforced
concrete slab
As part of the project construction a single story control building and connecting
pipelines will also be required to serve the new reservoir In addition a 12 foot wide
asphalt concrete paved service road will be provided around the reservoir structure

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lava of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene Epoch
more than 2 million years before present Following the cessation of this main shield
building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lava of
the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the alluvial
sediments of the Palikea Formation
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series are generally characterized as thick flows of
dense basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various
locales Associated with the vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low cinder
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4105 00 Page 5
cones at the vent Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of
the island of Kauai
During the Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to fill
the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels rose
because more water was available The land mass of Kauai remained essentially stable
during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower relative to the
present sea level on Kauai
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs Most of the soils in the project area are residual and saprolite
soils derived from the in situ weathering of igneous rocks

stiff silty clay and clayey silt soils extending to the maximum depth explored of about
56 5 feet below the existing ground surface Based on the subsoil conditions encountered
at the project site we believe that shallow foundations consisting of a perimeter ring
footing bearing on 12 inches of compacted base course over the in situ stiff clayey soils
may be used for support of the proposed new reservoir
Based on the laboratory test results we recommend that the on site clayey soils be
capped with a minimum of 12 inches of compacted base course below the reservoir slab

Reservoir Foundations
Based on the current design concept and the generally stiff subsoil conditions
encountered at the site we believe that shallow foundations may be used for support of
the proposed new reservoir An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per
square foot psf may be used for the design of a perimeter ring footing bearing on
12 inches of compacted base course over stiff clayey soils anticipated at the project site
This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for
transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
In order to reduce the potential for differential settlements as a result of differing
subgrade conditions it is our opinion that the new reservoir structure should be located
entirely on cuts and should not straddle across cut and fill zones
For footings bearing on the compacted base course over the in situ stiff soils the
bottom of the footing excavations should be recompacted to at least 90 percent relative
compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to placement of
the base course material If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of
the footing excavations they should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 4105 00 Page 8
materials The over excavation may be backfilled with on site soils compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom may be extended down
to the underlying competent material
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 3 may be used for foundations bearing on the in situ stiff soils Resistance due
to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300
pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soil around footings
is well compacted The passive pressure should be reduced for foundations located on
slopes Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper
12 inches of soil should be neglected
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grades For footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping ground
the footings should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal set back
distance of 8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the slope face
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the
utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to
avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for foundation settlement
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for footings
bearing on the recompacted in situ stiff soils as recommended herein to be less than
1 inch total with differential settlements on the order of 0 5 inches

Slab On Grade
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by highly
plastic clayey soils with plasticity index ranging from 37 to 76 To reduce the potential for
future distress to the slab on grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of these clayey
soils due to changes in the moisture content we recommend that the subgrade soils
below the slab concrete be properly prepared and capped with at least 12 inches of base
course compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction The 12 inch
compacted base course will also serve to provide more uniform support for the reservoir
slab The base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction For the design of structural slabs a modulus
of subgrade reaction of about 250 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection pci may
be used for the compacted base course The area adjacent to the reservoir slab should
be backfilled tightly against the slab with compacted on site soils and graded to divert
water away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the slab and
foundation
In accordance with the design guidelines by the Department of Water we
understand that it is generally desired to conduct a foundation probing program to aid in
the detection of cavities and or lava tubes Based on the very stiff clayey subsoil
conditions encountered in our field exploration and the current grading concept we believe
that this requirement would not be necessary for the proposed Kalaheo Reservoir project

Retaining Walls
Based on the current design concept we envision that retaining walls up to about
20 feet high may be required to provide grade separation between the new reservoir and
the existing hillside The following general guidelines may be used for preliminary design

Wall Foundations
In general we believe that retaining wall foundations may be designed in
accordance with the recommendations presented in the Reservoir Foundations
section of this report Wall footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches
Wall foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded a minimum depth of
24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade For sloping ground conditions
the footing should extend deeper to obtain a minimum 8 foot set back distance
measured horizontally from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope
Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should be constructed in
stepped footings

Site Grading
Based on the existing topography and the design finish floor elevation of the new
reservoir we anticipate that the majority of the site grading work for the proposed
construction will consist of cuts up to about 20 feet deep into the existing hillside
At the on set of earthwork the areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft and or organic soils
should be properly disposed of off site or used in landscaping areas if appropriate
After clearing and grubbing areas beneath future slabs should be over excavated
where necessary to provide the minimum 6 and 12 inch thick compacted base course
layer below the slab concrete Prior to placement of the base course layer the subgrade
soils should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least
2 percent above the optimum moisture and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction Where shrinkage cracks are noted after preparation of the subgrade

we recommend that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks and

If the subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade preparation the pumping
subgrade may be stabilized by cement treatment to reduce the potential for pumping

immediately around water line pipes is not recommended

Drainage
The finished grades outside the reservoir structure should be sloped to shed water
away from foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive
landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be
properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce water infiltration In addition drainage swales should be provided as
soon as possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away from slabs
and foundations

three borings to depths ranging from about 16 5 to 56 5 feet below the existing ground

flight augers and coring tools

examination in the field by our engineer who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials

sample depths

retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket

depths
