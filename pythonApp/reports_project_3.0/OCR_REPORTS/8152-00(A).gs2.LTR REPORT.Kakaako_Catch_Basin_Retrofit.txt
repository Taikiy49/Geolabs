REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Volcano, volcanic activity returned with a 
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are referred 
to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
The project site is on the coastal plain of Southern Oahu. The coastal plain was 
built on the eroded flanks of the Koolau Volcano, which forms the eastern two-thirds of the 
Island of Oahu. The coastal plain was built by extensive accumulation of alluvium derived 
from erosion of the volcano, interbedded with coral reefs and associated deposits. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. Most of the coastal plains were developed during the 
Pleistocene Epoch when the sea levels fluctuated significantly. As the glaciers grew and 
advanced, less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below 
the present stands of the sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water 
became available such that the sea levels rose to above its present level.

The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic sea 
level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations. 
The project sites generally are underlain by lagoonal deposits and coralline detritus 
consisting of calcareous sediments. A surface layer of man-made fill was placed over 
these deposits for the development of the Kakaako area within the last century. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITION 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the proposed new GSI locations by 
drilling and sampling five borings, designated as Boring Nos. 2, 3, 6, 7, and 8, extending to 
a depth of about 16.5 feet below the existing pavement/ground surface.  The approximate 
boring locations are shown on the General Site Plan, Plate 2, and Site Plans, Plates 3.1 
through 3.5. 
In general, our drilled borings encountered 2 to 9 inches of asphaltic concrete over 
base course and/or fill material extending to depths of about 1.5 to 8 feet below the 
existing pavement/ground surface.  The base course and/or fill materials generally 
consisted of very loose to dense silty sands, gravelly sands, and sandy gravel.  Below the 
fill materials, lagoonal deposits and/or coralline detritus consisting of very loose to dense 
silty and clayey sands, as well as soft to very soft sandy and gravelly clays, were 
encountered to the maximum depth explored of about 16.5 feet below the existing 
pavement/ground surface. 
We encountered groundwater at depths ranging from about 3.7 to 6.5 feet below 
the existing pavement/ground surface. The groundwater levels encountered generally 
correspond to elevations of about -0.6 to +1.0 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) at the time of 
our field exploration. Due to the proximity of the project site to the Pacific Ocean, water 
levels are expected to vary with tidal fluctuations and storm events. It should be noted that 
water levels may also vary with seasonal rainfall, time of the year, and other factors. 
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration methodology and the Logs of Borings 
are presented in Appendix A. Results of the laboratory tests performed on selected soil 
samples obtained from our field exploration are presented in Appendix B.

Based on the results of our field exploration, we anticipate the proposed new GSIs 
will likely bear on the soft and/or loose lagoonal deposits and/or coralline detritus materials 
underlying the project sites. Therefore, we recommend providing a subgrade stabilization 
layer below the bedding layer for uniform support of the new GSIs.  Detailed discussions 
and recommendations for the design of the new GSIs are presented in the following 
section. 
New Green Stormwater Infrastructures 
Based on the information provided, we understand retaining structures, such as the 
new GSIs, are planned for the project. Therefore, the following general guidelines are 
provided and may be used for the design of the retaining structures at the project sites. 
Retaining Structure Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration, we 
envision the new GSIs will likely be underlain by the soft and/or loose lagoonal 
deposits and/or coralline detritus underlying the project sites. An allowable bearing 
pressure of up to 2,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the design of 
the GSIs, provided that the bedding layer is underlain by a subgrade stabilization 
layer as described below.  This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may 
be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or 
seismic forces. 
As mentioned above, soft and/or loose materials are anticipated at the bottom of 
the new GSIs.  Therefore, we recommend providing a subgrade stabilization layer 
consisting of 24 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) 
wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) below the 6-inch 
bedding layer for uniform support of the new GSIs. The stabilization layer should 
also extend beyond the sides of the GSIs a minimum of 18 inches. 
Lateral loads acting on the GSIs may be resisted by friction between the bottom of 
the foundation and the bearing soil, and by passive earth pressure acting against 
the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A coefficient of friction of 0.35 may 
be used for footings bearing on the stabilization layer over the soft and/or loose 
soils. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an 
equivalent fluid pressure of 150 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf).  
Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in the upper 
12 inches of soil should be neglected.  For the design of retaining structures, the 
normal high groundwater level may be assumed at an elevation of +3 feet MSL. 
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate total settlements of the GSIs to be less than 1 inch.  
We envision a majority of the settlements would occur during the GSI installation. 
A Geolabs representative should observe the GSI excavations to confirm the 
foundation bearing conditions and the required over-excavation depths.

Static Lateral Earth Pressures 
Retaining structures, such as the new GSIs, should be designed to resist the 
lateral earth pressures due to the adjacent soils and surcharge effects. An at-rest 
lateral earth pressure, including hydrostatic pressure, of 90 pcf may be used for 
below groundwater condition. 
The value provided above assumes that select granular materials will be used to 
backfill around the GSI above the groundwater level. It is assumed that the 
backfill around the GSI will be compacted to between 90 and 95 percent relative 
compaction. Over-compaction of the backfill should be avoided.  Backfill below 
the groundwater level should consist of No. 3B Fine gravel. 
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges, line loads, and point loads within a 
horizontal distance equal to the depth of the wall should be considered in the 
design. For walls that are restrained, such as the GSIs, a rectangular distribution 
equal to 50 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure acting over the entire 
height of the GSI wall may be used for the design. Additional analyses during 
design may be needed to evaluate the surcharge effects of point loads and line 
loads.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the proposed new GSI locations were explored by 
drilling and sampling five exploratory borings, designated as Boring Nos. 2, 3, 6, 7, and 8, 
extending to a depth of about 16.5 feet below the existing pavement/ground surface. The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the General Site Plan, Plate 2, and Site Plans, 
Plates 3.1 through 3.5. The borings were drilled with a truck-mounted drill rig equipped 
with 4-inch OD solid-stem augers. 
Our field engineer/geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by 
visual and textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, 
Standard Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling 
operations on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further 
reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general 
accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for 
Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log 
Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with 
ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-5. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are
