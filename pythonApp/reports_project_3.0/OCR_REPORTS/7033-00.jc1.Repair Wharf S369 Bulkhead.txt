PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is along the south shore of Ford Island in the Pearl Harbor area 
on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The design-build project involves stabilizing the backfill 
behind the existing bulkhead (concrete curtain wall) and providing a safe area for facility 
users at Wharf S369. 
The existing Wharf S369 is about 600 feet long and 50 feet wide.  The wharf 
structure is supported on concrete vertical and battered piles.  Based on the information 
provided in the RFP documents, we understand the existing 10-foot deep concrete curtain 
wall or bulkhead is undermined at some locations.  Voids caused by the undermining 
range from about 18 to 36 inches in height and extend about 24 to 36 inches behind the 
curtain wall.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau Shield Volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island.  The 
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) 
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  Waianae Volcano 
became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank was partially 
buried below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank.  The Koolau Volcano 
reached the end of its main shield-building phase about 2 million years ago. 
The geologic conditions underlying the project site are the result of a complex 
series of deposition and erosional events brought on by glacio-eustatic sea level 
changes and post-erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch. Following 
the cessation of activity of the Koolau Volcano, alluvial soil materials were deposited in 
a deltaic or estuarine environment in the Pearl Harbor area. 
The sea level fluctuated several times, receding perhaps 300 feet below and 
rising about 100 feet above the present sea level. Concurrent with a rise in sea level 
during the late Pleistocene era, eruption of the Salt Lake-Makalapa member of the 
Honolulu Volcanic Series mantled the older alluvium with thick deposits of volcanic ash, 
which consolidated to form volcanic tuff (locally known as “mudrock”) layers. 
Subsequent erosion dissected and thinned mantles of those volcanic tuff.

Ford Island is a remnant of the hard reef rock that once covered a large portion 
of the embayment and was subsequently carved away in many places by streams 
during lower stands of the sea. During the Recent Epoch, as sea level rose to its current 
stand, the area was covered with soft and loose lagoonal marine deposits and 
additional sediments have continued to accumulate along the lochs and channels of the 
harbor. These deposits were subsequently covered with man-made fills placed during 
the past 40 to 60 years for land development along the shoreline. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITION 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the existing Wharf S369 project site 
by drilling and sampling three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 103, 
extending to depths of about 53 to 101.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, the subsurface conditions at the site consist of about 7 to 12 feet of fill 
materials overlying coralline and marine deposits.  The fill materials consisted of stiff silty 
clay and loose granular soils.  The coralline deposits are generally loose to medium dense.  
Below the stiff marine deposit, we encountered weathered tuff formation and/or stiff 
alluvium extending to the maximum depth drilled of about 101.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. 
We encountered groundwater at depths of about 6.6 to 8.4 feet below the ground 
surface at the time of our field exploration.  The groundwater levels measured generally 
correspond to about Elevations +0.9 to +2.7 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).  Due to the 
proximity of the project site to Pacific Ocean, water levels are expected to vary with tidal 
fluctuations.  It should be noted that groundwater levels at the site may also vary with 
seasonal precipitation, storm surge conditions, and other factors. 
Detailed descriptions of the field exploration methodology and graphic 
representations of the materials encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of 
Borings in Appendix A.  We performed laboratory tests on selected soil and rock samples 
obtained during our field exploration and the test results are presented in Appendix B.  
Results of the laboratory corrositivity tests performed by others on selected soil samples 
retrieved from our field exploration are presented in Appendix D.

subjected to seismic activity, and seismic design considerations will need to be addressed

of Oahu and the soil profile for seismic design.

Based on the IBC 2012 and ASCE 7-10, the project site may be subjected to 
seismic activity and should be evaluated for potential soil liquefaction.  Based on the 
seismic design classification (Site Class “C”), the seismic design horizontal peak ground 
acceleration is determined to be approximately 0.20g in accordance with IBC 2012 for

opinion that the liquefaction hazard at the site may be ignored due to the computed factor 
of safety against liquefaction to be greater than 1.1. Therefore, the potential for liquefaction 
is not a design consideration at this project site.

Lateral Earth Pressures 
Due to the relatively shallow retained depth of the new bulkhead, we envision that 
the new bulkhead will likely consist of a cantilevered sheet pile bulkhead system.   In 
general, the bulkhead should be designed to resist the lateral earth pressures due to the 
adjacent soils and surcharge effects.  Therefore, we recommend designing the new sheet 
pile bulkhead for lateral earth pressures due to the in-situ soils, which include the existing 
fills and coralline deposits.  The recommended lateral earth pressures for design of the 
new sheet pile bulkhead are provided on the attached Lateral Earth Pressure Diagram, 
Plate 3. 
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges, line loads, and point loads within a 
horizontal distance equal to the depth of the sheet pile bulkhead should be considered in 
the design.  For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the bulkhead, a 
rectangular distribution with uniform pressure equal to 30 percent of the vertical surcharge 
pressure acting on the entire height of the bulkhead may be used in design.  Additional 
analyses during design may be needed to evaluate the surcharge effects of point loads 
and line loads. 
It should be noted that we utilized the general limit equilibrium analysis method to 
idealize the active earth pressures acting on the new bulkhead sheet pile to simplify 
calculation. 
Dynamic lateral earth forces due to seismic loading (a = 0.20g) may be estimated 
by using 4.0H2 pounds per lineal foot of wall length for level backfill conditions, where H is 
the height of the sheet pile wall in feet. It should be noted that the dynamic lateral earth 
forces provided assume that the wall will be allowed to move laterally up to about 2 inches 
in the event of an earthquake. For restrained condition (less than 0.5 inches of lateral 
movement), forces caused by dynamic lateral earth pressures due to seismic loading may 
be estimated using 10.0H2 pounds per linear foot of wall length for level backfill conditions. 
Unbalanced Hydraulic Pressures 
For waterfront structures such as sheet pile-supported piers, the maximum lateral 
pressures may be expected when the tide is at its lowest level.  A receding tide may cause 
higher water levels behind the sheet pile than in front of it, depending on the type of soils 
behind the sheet piles.  The high water level behind the sheet pile wall will introduce 
additional seepage pressure due to hydrostatic load.  For soils encountered at the project 
site, a hydrostatic pressure estimated based on the maximum tide difference 
(conservative) may be used for the sheet pile design.

Overall Slope Stability 
Based on the anticipated sheet pile depth of about 30 feet below the existing 
ground surface, we have evaluated the overall slope stability of the proposed sheet pile 
bulkhead.  Slope stability analyses consisting of two-dimensional slope cross sections 
using Bishop's simplified method of limit equilibrium analysis were performed on the new 
sheet pile bulkhead.  Based on our analyses, the factors of safety for the sheet pile 
bulkhead under static and seismic conditions are greater than 1.5 and 1.1, which are the 
minimum factors of safety normally recommended. 
Corrosion Protection 
Due to the exposure to salt water, we envision that the new sheet pile bulkheads 
would be subjected to a highly corrosive environment.  Therefore, we recommend 
designing metallic substructures for protection against potential corrosion. Consideration 
may be given to the use of other types of sheet piles, such as vinyl, composite, or other 
types of non-corrosive materials, in lieu of the conventional steel sheet piles.  If a steel 
sheet pile system is elected for the project, a corrosion engineer should be consulted for 
detailed recommendations of corrosion protection of the steel sheet pile bulkhead. 
Construction Considerations 
Our field exploration indicated that some concrete debris and/or cobbles may be 
present within the fill layer, as well as hard zones of coralline deposits.  The potential for 
the presence of concrete debris and/or cobbles and hard zones of siltstone/limestone 
deposits may pose some difficulties to the contractor to maintain the plumbness of the 
sheet piles.  To facilitate installation of the sheet pile, consideration may be given to 
pre-drilling and/or spudding along the sheet pile bulkhead alignment to clear the path of 
obstructions on an as-needed basis.  After clearing the path of the potential obstructions 
(as needed), the sheet piles then may be installed to the desired depth without significant 
resistance due to obstructions. 
SETTLEMENT MONITORING 
Ground settlement in existing pavements and/or adjacent structures was observed 
in the past during other wharf construction in the Pearl Harbor area, due to excessive 
vibration induced by sheet pile driving when a vibratory hammer was used.  Therefore, it is 
critical to reduce potential risk of ground settlement during sheet pile installation and/or 
dewatering operations (if applicable), that may cause damage on the existing pavements, 
structures and/or underground utilities. 
The contractor should select the sheet pile driving equipment that is capable of 
installing the sheet piles to the design tip elevations, without inducing ground settlement 
and causing damage to the existing facility.  An impact hammer may be used for installing 
the sheet piles, in lieu to a vibratory hammer, to reduce potential risk of excessive 
vibration.

To reduce potential ground settlement, we recommend conducting a monitoring 
test program, consisting of settlement and vibration monitoring during sheet pile driving 
and/or dewatering operations (if applicable).  The monitoring test program should be 
performed at the beginning of the construction, to examine the proposed construction 
equipment and to evaluate the vibration effect/level induced by the construction activities. 
Survey monitoring points should be established along the existing concrete wharf 
structure, existing man-hole structures, and adjacent existing buildings and pavements to 
be remained.  All monitoring points should be referenced to a stable bench mark that will 
not be affected by the construction activities at the site for a baseline reading prior to the 
construction activity.  The settlement monitoring should be conducted at the beginning, 
during and at the end of the monitoring test program by a licensed surveyor. 
Vibration monitoring should be conducted at multiple locations to measure peak 
particle velocity induced by the sheet pile driving operation during the monitoring test 
program.  Background vibration readings should be measured prior to the sheet pile 
driving operation.  A graphic relationship for a given energy source attenuate vibration 
amplitude with increasing distance should be established.  Based on the relatively older 
structures adjacent to the construction site, we believe that ground motion of 1.0 inch per 
second may be selected as threshold of damage criteria in accordance with publication 
literature by Chae, Y.S., 1978 (Design of Excavation Blasts to Present Damage). 
CORROSION POTENTIAL 
Two sets of laboratory corrosion tests, including pH (SW 9045C), Minimum 
Resistivity (SM 2510B), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), 
were performed by TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. to evaluate the corrosivity of the on-site 
soils encountered. The test results are summarized on Plate B-8 of Appendix D. Based on 
the chloride and sulfate content tests performed on the on-site soils, the tested values are 
generally relatively low. Therefore, we believe that either Type I or Type II cement may be 
used for the concrete in contact with the ground planned for the project. It may be 
appropriate to consult with a professional corrosion engineer to review the test results and 
provide detailed recommendations for corrosion protection. 
The resistivity value of the near-surface soil sample tested generally was on the 
order of 3,900 ohm-cm (based on the soluble soil sample prepared in accordance with 
Standard Methods for the Examination of Water and Wastewater SM 2510B). The 
minimum resistivity value corresponds to Corrosion Rating of 3 (corrosive) based on the 
guidelines provided by the City & County of Honolulu – Board of Water Supply.

Based on the current design concept, we anticipate that excavations will be needed 
for the installation of utility lines at the proposed wharf site.  Based on our field exploration, 
excavations for these substructures for the project likely will encounter groundwater and, 
possibly, soft soil conditions and/or obstructions. 
In general, the contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used 
for excavation, subject to practical limits and safety considerations.  Based on our field 
exploration, the existing fill materials and loose coralline deposits encountered in the 
borings may be excavated with normal excavators and/or backhoes.  However, hard 
ripping or special excavating methods may be required to remove obstructions present in 
the subsurface. 
Excavation for installation of utility lines may need to be adequately shored to 
reduce the potential for adjacent ground movement.  The excavated materials should not 
be stockpiled adjacent to the planned excavation within a horizontal distance equal to the 
depth of the excavation to reduce the potential for ground movement.  The excavation 
support and shoring system used are the responsibility of the contractor and must comply 
with all applicable safety requirements. 
The contractor should be solely responsible for the adequacy and safety of the 
shoring installation. Therefore, it is recommended for the contractor to retain a qualified 
engineer to design and evaluate the shoring system used.  His/her representative, who 
would be present on site during excavation and construction work, will have the best 
opportunity to promptly observe changing conditions during construction, such as 
unforeseen subsoils, unexpectedly high groundwater table, inappropriate construction 
sequence or techniques, etc., which may affect shoring stability.  However, it must be 
noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and the adjacent ground still 
may occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation.  Due to the complexity of 
the stress changes, it is difficult to accurately estimate the magnitude of movement.  The 
magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship such as how quickly and tightly the 
shoring and bracing supports are installed, the subsoil conditions, the size of the 
excavation, and the rate of excavation. 
It is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed properly and 
as early as practical and that the adjacent ground should be continuously monitored for 
cracks, dips and/or other indications of movements with instruments.

We envision that new utility lines will be required for the project.  In general, we 
recommend providing granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of free-draining materials, 
such as bedding sand or open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), below the 
pipes for uniform support.  If soft and/or loose soils are encountered at the proposed pipe

mechanically compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the 
potential for appreciable future ground subsidence.  Where trenches are below pavement 
areas, the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be 
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling 
three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 103, extending to depths of 
approximately 53 to 101.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  We drilled the borings using truck-mounted 
drilling equipment equipped with rotary coring tools. 
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural 
examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis.  Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil 
Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1.  Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1.1 through A-3.2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches.  In addition, we obtained 
some samples from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop.  The blow counts needed to 
drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the coral/rock formations encountered at the site were obtained 
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation.  Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the coral/rock formation.  The material left in the hollow core 
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description.  Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2.  The Rock Description System is based on the 
publication “Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in 
Rock Masses” by International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of coral/rock masses.  Recovery is defined as the actual length of material 
recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt.  For example, if 
3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 
74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.

The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of coral/rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities, discounting drilling 
induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5.0-foot 
core run, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as 
RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock, 
based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals."

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content and Dry Density tests were performed on selected soil samples 
to aid in defining the interface between the various soil layers encountered and 
correlating the layers between test borings. The moisture content tests were performed 
in general accordance with ASTM D2216. The test results are presented on the Logs of 
Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Five (5) Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid limit (LL), plastic limit (PL), and plasticity index (PI), as an 
aid in classifying the soils. The test results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at 
the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of the test results are provided on 
Plate B-1. 
Four (4) Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 and C136) were performed to 
evaluate the gradation characteristics and to aid in soil classification. Graphic 
presentation of the test results are provided on Plate B-2. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected sample to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. The direct shear test 
results are presented on Plate B-3. 
Four (4) Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXUU) tests 
(ASTM D2850) were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained 
shear strength of the silty and clayey soils encountered. The approximate in-situ 
effective overburden pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the 
relatively “undisturbed” soil sample. The test results and the stress-strain curves are 
presented on Plates B-4 and B-7. 
Two sets of Corrosion tests, including one pH (SW 9045C), Minimum Resistivity 
(SM 2510B), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0) tests, 
were performed by TestAmerica Analytical Testing Corporation on selected soil 
samples obtained from our field exploration.  The test results are summarized on 
Plate B-8. 
Two (2) Unconfined Compression tests (ASTM D7012 Method C) were 
performed on selected rock cores to evaluate their unconfined compressive strength of 
the rock formation encountered.  Unconfined compression test results are presented on 
Plate B-9. 
One California Bearing Ratio (CBR) test (ASTM D1883) was performed on a bulk 
sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the strength characteristics.  CBR test 
results are presented on Plate B-10.

Seismic Cone Penetration Test 
Due to the nature of the subsurface conditions at the project site, we performed 
seismic shear wave velocity profiling using Cone Penetration Testing (CPT) equipment 
in an effort to more closely analyze the seismic design considerations for the project 
site.  Seismic shear wave velocity tests using seismic cone penetration testing 
equipment were performed in Boring No. 102.  The approximate boring location is 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  The probe extended to a depth of about 101.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface using seismic cone penetration testing (SCPT) 
equipment. 
After the boring was drilled, the bored hole was backfilled with bentonite pellets, 
and the bentonite pellets were allowed to hydrate.  The seismic shear wave velocity test 
consists of hydraulically pushing a 10-ton steel electronic subtraction cone with an apex 
angle of 60 degrees and an area of 1.55 square inches (10 square centimeters) through 
the borehole backfilled with bentonite. 
The cone carries a uniaxial horizontal geophone to detect the arrival of a shear 
wave propagated from the ground surface.  The seismic measurements are made when 
the SCPT has stopped and a shear wave is sent into the subsurface.  A shear wave is 
generated at the surface by striking a loaded plank with a switched hammer.  The 
propagation time of the wave from the hammer blow to the cone was measured at each 
depth interval.  The vector difference of these depths divided by the time difference 
provided the average shear wave velocity over the depth interval. 
The shear wave velocities measured and the weighted average shear wave 
velocity for the top 100 feet of the soil profile at the probe location are presented on 
Plate C-2. The average shear wave velocity was calculated based on the average shear 
wave velocity method of the International Building Code (Equation 16-41).
