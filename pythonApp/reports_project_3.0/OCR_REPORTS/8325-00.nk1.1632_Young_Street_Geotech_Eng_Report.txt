SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The subsurface conditions at the project site are variable and complex. The field 
exploration generally encountered alternating layers of terrigenous soils and coralline 
deposits. The two borings performed at the project site generally encountered about 29 feet 
of very loose to medium dense basaltic silty sand (cinder sand deposits). Underlying the 
silty sand, interbedded layers of soft to very stiff silts and/or clays and very loose to medium 
dense silty sands were encountered to about 89 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Interbedded layers of relatively stiff silts and/or clays and medium dense coralline sands and 
gravel were encountered to the maximum depth explored of about 173.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface. Based on the subsurface conditions at the site and the anticipated 
structural loads, we envision the proposed structure may be supported on deep foundation 
system consisting of either micropiles or augered cast-in-place (ACIP) piles. 
We understand the first-floor level of the proposed residential tower is set at 
+26.5 feet MSL and the bottom of the basement mat foundation will be at +14.5 feet MSL. 
Therefore, the top of the residential tower deep foundation system will be approximately 10 
to 12 feet below the existing grade. Due to the size of the equipment needed to install ACIP 
piles and the limited size of the lot, installing ACIP piles for the project will need to be from 
near the existing ground surface of the project site. Installing the ACIP piles from the existing 
ground surface generally will be quicker and easier to construct. However, it will involve 
extra grout to build up the ACIP pile to the existing ground surface followed by chipping the 
excess pile to the cutoff elevation. Therefore, ACIP piles for this site with a basement 
condition will consist of some wasted materials and should be accounted for in the cost of 
the ACIP pile foundation system. Installing micropile foundations in a basement condition of 
the limited lot size would not be as impacted and would not be at an appreciable premium 
over standard installations. 
Cavities and voids are commonly encountered in the coralline materials in the project 
vicinity. Therefore, the actual volume of grout required to fill the ACIP piles may be 
appreciably more than the theoretical grout volume. Based on our experience in the area, 
we envision that an average grout factor on the order of about 170 to 200 percent over the 
theoretical grout volume may be required for the ACIP piles. The text of this report should 
be referred to for detailed findings and recommendation.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
Of interest to our geotechnical analysis for foundation design of the mid-rise 
building planned for this project are the subsurface materials encountered at the project 
site, the engineering properties of the materials encountered, and the variability of the 
subsurface conditions across the project site. Therefore, the following subsections 
provide a description of the geologic setting of the project site, the subsurface conditions 
encountered at the site, and a discussion on the items needed for seismic design. 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
Extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the Waianae and Koolau Shield 
Volcanoes built the Island of Oahu. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle 
to late Pliocene Epoch in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the Island of 
Oahu. The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene 
(latest Ice Age) in age and forms the bulk of the eastern two-thirds of the Island of Oahu. 
Following the coalescence of the two volcanoes and after a long period of volcanic 
inactivity, during which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic 
activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. 
These late series of eruptions are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series, which are 
confined to scattered areas of the Koolau Volcano footprint. The Honolulu Volcanic 
Series, which began approximately 700,000 years ago (MacDonald and Abbott, 1970), 
produced several prominent cinder and tuff cone structures with some basaltic lava flows 
that became inter-layered with the developing Southern Oahu coastal plain deposits. 
The project site is on the lower southern flank of the Koolau Volcano. As a result, 
the geomorphology and subsurface conditions of the area are directly related to 
glacio-eustatic fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis 
of the Honolulu Coastal Plain. The subsurface conditions in the Moiliili area include 
various marsh land and alluvial soil deposits overlying fossil Pleistocene Age coral/algal 
limestone reef and related marine/lagoonal deposits laid during higher stands of the sea. 
These deeper marine deposits are intercalated with other terrigenous derived sediments. 
The base of the stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt.

Most of the Honolulu Coastal Plain developed during the Pleistocene Epoch when 
the sea level experienced significant fluctuations related to the continental glacial stages. 
As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill the oceanic basins 
such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When the glaciers melted 
and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea levels rose to above 
the present sea level. The processes of landform erosion, sediment deposition, and reef 
development were affected by these glacio-eustatic sea level fluctuations. 
When the sea level was relatively lower, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower and stream valleys were carved deeper into the island basaltic 
rock, the fringing coastal sediments, and the offshore reef deposits. Also, during periods 
of relatively lower sea level, the sub-aerial exposure of calcareous marine sediments 
caused consolidation and cementation of the deposits to form hardened calcareous 
deposits. In contrast, when the sea level was relatively higher, the erosional base level 
was raised such that marine sediments and coral reef formations filled the pre-existing 
depressions and accumulated as reef platforms at correspondingly higher elevations. The 
higher sea levels also caused the formation of deltas and alluvial fans composed of 
terrigenous sediment in the head region of old bays. 
Finally, the placement of near-surface man-made fills associated with the 
development of the urban area within the last century has brought the Honolulu Coastal 
Plain to its present form.

2.3 
Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the International Building Code 2012 Edition (IBC 2012), the project site 
would be subjected to seismic activity, and seismic design considerations will need to be 
addressed, as well as evaluating the potential for soil liquefaction. 
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils near the ground 
surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build-up of excess pore water 
pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes. Soils most 
susceptible to liquefaction are loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained sands and loose silts 
with little cohesion. 
Based on the available borings, the subsurface conditions at the site generally 
consist of alternating layers of very loose to medium dense silty sand and soft to very stiff 
silts and clays. Laboratory testing performed by MFA suggest that the fine-grained 
material within the on-site silty sands have relatively high liquid limits and plasticity 
indices. Therefore, we believe the on-site very loose to medium dense silty sands beneath 
the encountered groundwater table levels are not susceptible to soil liquefaction, and the 
phenomenon of soil liquefaction is not a design consideration.

The following sections discuss two potential deep foundation systems that can be

3.1 
Micropile Foundation 
If it is desired to perform the basement excavation before installing the residential 
tower foundation system, we recommend using a micropile foundation system. The 
micropile foundation installation equipment is generally smaller and more maneuverable 
than other deep foundation equipment. This allows the equipment to be lowered in and 
out of the excavation and maneuver around obstructions within the excavation. The load 
supporting capacity of the micropile foundation would be derived primarily from skin 
friction between the micropile bonded zone and the relatively stiff and medium dense 
terrigenous and coralline deposits encountered at the project site.  
A micropile consists of a small diameter (usually less than 12 inches) drilled and 
grouted pile with steel reinforcing. The micropile foundation typically is constructed by 
drilling a borehole, placing reinforcing steel in the hole, and grouting the borehole. 
Micropiles are desirable because they can be installed readily in access restrictive 
environments and in numerous soil types and ground conditions. In addition, installation 
of the micropiles generally causes minimal disturbance to adjacent structures, the 
adjacent soils, and the environment. 
Based on the availability of local drilling equipment, we envision that a micropile 
system with a minimum grout bulb diameter of 7.625 inches may be used. We recommend 
utilizing a combination of micropiles with allowable compressive load capacities of 100 
and 200 kips per micropile. The allowable compressive load capacity of the micropiles is 
for supporting dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one third (⅓) when 
considering transient loads, such as wind or seismic forces. 
The load supporting capacity of the micropile foundation element would be derived 
primarily from skin friction between the micropile bonded zone and the underlying 
relatively stiff and medium dense terrigenous and coralline deposits encountered at 
depths starting from about 20 feet below the bottom of pile cap or footing.

3.1.1 Uplift Load Resistance 
Uplift loads imposed on the micropiles may be resisted by skin friction along the 
grout bulb (bonded zone) and the adjacent soils. In general, an allowable uplift 
load capacity equal to the allowable compressive load capacity may be achieved 
for each respective micropile. The allowable uplift load capacity provided is 
intended for transient load applications only. Therefore, the allowable uplift load 
capacity should not be further increased for transient load applications. The 
capacity of the pile member in tension should be checked by the project structural 
engineer. 
3.1.2 Lateral Load Resistance 
Lateral load resistance contribution from the micropile should be discounted due 
to the relatively small diameter of the foundation element. Passive earth pressure 
acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation caps and/or grade beams 
may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 and 150 pounds per 
square foot per foot of depth (pcf) for above and below groundwater conditions, 
respectively. The static groundwater level at the project site may be assumed to 
be at about Elevation +6 feet MSL for design purposes. 
3.1.3 Micropile Foundation Settlements 
Settlements of the micropiles will result primarily from elastic compression of the 
micropile member and subgrade response. We estimate the total settlement of the 
micropile-supported foundations to be 0.5 inches or less with differential 
settlements between micropiles not exceeding about one-half of the total 
settlement. We believe these settlements are essentially elastic and should occur 
as the loads are applied.

3.2 
Augered Cast-In-Place (ACIP) Piles 
ACIP piles generally have a faster production rate than micropiles and, therefore, 
are more economical in most situations. However, due to the size of the equipment 
needed to install the ACIP piles and the limited space within the basement excavation, 
installation of ACIP piles for this project will need to be installed from the existing ground 
surface. As a result, ACIP piles for this site will incur some added costs associated with 
the extra grout to build up the ACIP pile to the existing ground surface followed by 
chipping the excess pile to the cutoff elevation. In addition, the contractor will need to take 
extra care in the basement excavation as not to damage the ACIP piles Therefore, ACIP 
piles for this site with a basement condition will involve some wasted materials and 
additional work efforts. These added costs should be accounted for in the cost of the 
ACIP pile foundation system. 
It is important to note that the ACIP grout must be placed up to the existing ground 
surface at the time of installation; the grout level should not be terminated at the pile cutoff 
elevation in an effort to reduce the amount grout wasted or to avoid the pile chipping.

Having clean grout return to the existing ground surface during the ACIP pile installation 
is critical to creating a properly constructed pile. 
The ACIP piles would derive vertical support primarily from skin friction between 
the foundation element and the relatively stiff terrigenous deposits and medium dense 
coralline deposits underlying the project site. In general, the end bearing component of 
ACIP piles is a small component of the total compression load capacity and has been 
discounted in our analyses. 
ACIP piles are constructed by rotating a continuous flight hollow-stem auger 
(generally 10 to 24 inches in diameter) into the ground until either the planned pile tip 
depth or termination (refusal) criterion is achieved. At the termination depth, grout of a 
specified consistency is pumped into the augered hole through an orifice located in the 
drilling bit at the tip of the hollow-stem auger. The auger is slowly withdrawn from the hole 
and the pile shaft is formed as a continuous flow of grout replaces the soil removed by 
the auger. After the auger stem is removed, the reinforcing steel cage is then inserted 
into the grouted hole (through the fluid grout) within the set-time window allowed by the 
grout. 
Based on our engineering analyses, we recommend supporting the new mid-rise 
buildings on 18-inch diameter ACIP piles. In general, ACIP piles installed in groups should 
have a minimum center-to-center spacing of three times the pile diameter to avoid 
reduction in vertical load capacity due to group action and to facilitate drilling of the ACIP 
piles. Our recommendations pertaining to the ACIP pile capacities are presented in the

3.2.2 Lateral Load Resistance 
Lateral loads imposed on the building foundations may be resisted by a 
combination of the passive earth pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of 
the foundation caps and/or grade beams and the lateral load capacity of the ACIP 
piles. Passive earth pressure against the near-vertical faces of the foundation caps 
and/or grade beams may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 
300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf) and 150 pcf for above and below 
groundwater conditions, respectively. For the design of lateral load resistance of 
the new structure, the groundwater level may be assumed at an elevation of +6 
feet MSL. 
In general, lateral load resistance of the ACIP piles is a function of the stiffness of 
the surrounding soil, the stiffness of the ACIP pile, allowable deflection at the top 
of pile, and induced moment in the pile. The ACIP piles should be spaced at a 
minimum of three (3) times the diameter of the pile from center-to-center. 
The lateral load analyses were performed using the program LPILE v2019 for 
Windows, which is a microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference, laterally 
loaded pile program originally developed at the University of Texas at Austin.

Total settlements of the 60-foot to 75-foot deep ACIP piles are estimated to be

3.2.4 ACIP Pile Construction Considerations 
Proper installation of the ACIP piles is dependent upon the procedures and 
equipment used by the foundation subcontractor. It should be noted that the load 
bearing capacity of the ACIP piles will depend largely on the relative density/quality 
of the terrigenous and coralline deposits within the bearing strata. Therefore, the 
foundation subcontractor’s personnel on the project site who will be responsible 
for the installation should have the relevant experience with the augering and 
pumping equipment, experience with placing ACIP piles under similar subsurface 
conditions, as well as experience with handling the special grout mixes, 
admixtures, etc.

3.5.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
thoroughly cleared and grubbed. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, existing 
structures to be demolished, and other unsuitable materials, should be removed 
and disposed of properly off-site to reduce the potential for contamination of the 
excavated materials. 
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill and/or future improvements should be over-excavated to 
expose firm natural material, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
well-compacted fill. The excavated soft soils should not be re-used as fill materials 
and should be disposed of properly off-site. 
In general, the subgrades in cut areas and areas designated to receive fills should 
be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by 
ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density.  
Yielding and/or loose areas should be over-excavated and backfilled with 
compacted fill materials. The depth of over-excavation should be extended until 
dense underlying materials are exposed and should be evaluated in the field by a 
Geolabs representative.
