2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau Volcano forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae Volcano became extinct while
Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau
Volcano lavas in Central Oahu
The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be of the middle to late Pliocene in
age and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be of late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age
The main shield building activities of Koolau Volcano ceased approximately 2 5 million
years ago After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which erosion incised deep
valleys into the Koolau Volcanic Shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series
The project site is located on the lower reaches of the junction of Nuuanu and
Pauoa Valleys which were formed by deep erosion of the Koolau Shield Volcano The
period of erosion was followed by filling of the valley with alluvial deposits then covered
by the lava flows of the Honolulu Volcanic Series whose episodes were spread out over
a considerable period of time
The top of the lower basalt rock formation which was encountered in the borings
drilled at approximately 40 feet below the existing ground surface may be associated
with the Luakaha Vent an earlier Nuuanu Flow of the Honolulu Volcanic Series The
alluvial soil layer above the lower basalt rock formation was deposited by streams
during a cessation of volcanic activity This alluvial soil layer was later covered by the
upper relatively thin Nuuanu Basalt Flow cemented cinder which appear to have been
from the Makaku cone and conglomerate materials river deposits Continued erosion
of the upper reaches of the ridges and deposition of sediments by the intermittent

3 1 Underpinning Using Micropile Foundation System
Because of settlement of the load bearing wall footings we recommend that the
wall footings be underpinned by a micropile foundation system supported on the upper
basalt rock layer In general the micropile foundation system consists of a small
diameter usually less than 12 inches drilled and grouted pile with steel reinforcing
The micropile foundation typically is constructed by drilling a hole with or without
casing placing reinforcing steel in the hole and grouting the hole Micropiles are
desirable because they can be installed readily in access restrictive environments and
in numerous soil types and ground conditions In addition installation of the micropiles
generally causes minimal disturbance to adjacent structures the adjacent soils and the
environment
We estimate that a micropile system with a grout bulb diameter of 5 inches may
be used to support the structural footing loads We understand that an allowable
compressive load capacity of 50 kips per micropile will be required to support the
footings assuming that 12 micropiles will be used per affected footing The allowable
compressive load capacity for the piles is for dead plus live loads and may be increased
by 50 percent for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces To support the
allowable compressive load capacity of 50 kips per micropile we recommend that the
micropiles extend a minimum of 30 inches into the upper basalt rock formation Based
on the borings drilled we anticipate the micropiles for the project likely will vary from
about 5 to 15 feet in depth below the bottom of footing elevation
It should be noted that the micropile system provides low lateral load resistance
due to the relatively small diameter of the micropile Therefore the lateral load
resistance contributed by the micropiles should be neglected unless the micropiles are
battered at an angle to take advantage of the lateral load component of the micropile
Lateral loads imposed on the building structure may be resisted primarily by passive

Due to the high shrink swell potential of the clayey soils encountered at shallow
depths at the project site the clay likely will swell and lose substantial strength
when saturated and will shrink appreciably when subjected to drying In general
the mechanism of shrink swell in expansive soils is a complex phenomenon and is
influenced by a number of factors including soil characteristics and environmental
factors
Expansive soil characteristics generally can be measured by engineering properties
such as soil plasticity and density to evaluate the shrink swell potential of soils In
general the soil plasticity reflects the capacity of the clay minerals in the soil to
retain large amounts of water while maintaining a coherent structure Therefore a
highly plastic soil such as those found at the project site will have a greater
potential for volume change shrink and swell due to the loss or absorption of
moisture under cycles of drying and wetting In addition increased soil density
through compaction or natural depositional history such as desiccation generally
has greater shrink swell potential
Environmental factors comprise of the initial moisture content of the soil and
moisture variations over time The initial moisture content generally impact the
potential of a soil to absorb or expel water from the soil system In general a dry
soil will have a higher capacity to absorb water and swell while a wet soil will lose
water more readily upon exposure to dry conditions and will shrink more Moisture
variations generally can be caused by climate surface drainage vegetation
permeability etc
The on site clayey soils had relatively high moisture contents at the time our field
exploration was conducted Therefore we believe that the potential for additional
swelling of the already high moisture clay soils to be low However we believe that
the potential for moisture variations in the future may result in the potential for
shrinkage of the high moisture clayey soils However we believe that the potential
for drying out of the high moisture clayey soils to be low Therefore we

Wall footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches In addition wall footings
on relatively flat areas should be embedded a minimum depth of 24 inches below the
lowest adjacent finished grade In addition the embedment of the shallow footings should
be limited to a maximum of 30 inches below the lowest adjacent grade The bottom of the
footing excavations should be recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to the placement
of reinforcing steel or concrete
lf soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing
excavations the soft and or loose materials should be over excavated to expose the
underlying firm and or dense materials The over excavation may be backfilled with the
granular on site soils compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the
bottom of footing may be extended down to the underlying competent materials
Lateral loads acting on the retaining walls may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 80 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted surface fill soils
and or clay soils Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an

Core samples of rock materials encountered in the borings drilled at the site were
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core
drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation
The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for
examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of soil rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Fifteen One Inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed soil
samples and remolded soil samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near
surface clayey soils under a surcharge pressure of 55 psf The results of these tests
Two Consolidation tests with time rates ASTM D 2435 were performed on
samples of the potentially compressible near surface soils to evaluate the
compressibility characteristics of the materials encountered Results of the
consolidation tests are presented on Plates B 2 and B 3
