for the proposed American T Shirt Company Warehouse located at 1217 North King Street
in the Kalihi area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
derived from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses The
recommendations presented herein are intended for the design of foundations
slabs on grade site grading and pavements only The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the
western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern
two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still
active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in Central Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill the
oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea levels
rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of accumulated
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet
waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys
in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials The project site is situated in an area where the
subsurface soil deposits are dominated by calcareous reef and lagoonal deposits Land
development and reclamation projects within the last century have brought the project area
to its present form

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
As previously mentioned our field exploration generally encountered stiff to very stiff
clayey fill materials overlying very stiff alluvial soils The alluvial soils were underlain by
medium dense sandy and gravelly materials or stiff silty materials with some coral detritus
Groundwater was encountered at a depth of about 11 feet below the existing ground surface
at the time of our field exploration
Based on the stiff to very stiff subsurface soil conditions encountered in our borings
we believe that shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the
proposed warehouse structure Considering the proximity of the new structure to the existing
GEOLABS HAWAII

Our laboratory tests indicate that the clayey fill soils at the site exhibit a moderate

mentioned we anticipate that approximately 1 to 2 feet of fill will be placed at the site in order

Foundations
Based on the stiff to very stiff subsoil conditions encountered at the site we believe
that shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the proposed
warehouse structure An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot
psf may be used for the design of foundations bearing on the recompacted on site soils or
imported fill needed to achieve the design finished grades This bearing value is for dead
plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those
caused by wind or seismic forces
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent grade New footings to be located adjacent to the existing buildings at the south
should be lowered to the same depths as the existing building footings or slightly deeper
to avoid surcharging the existing foundations with additional structural loads In addition
the new footings should not extend deeper than the existing footings by more than about
6 inches to avoid undermining the existing footings Where footings are to be located
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4276 00 Page 8
adjacent to the existing retaining wall at the southwest or utilities the footings should be
embedded to a depth below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane
projected upward from the bottom edge of the existing retaining wall footing utility trench or
as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging
adjacent below grade structures and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
The bottom of the footing excavations should be recompacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete In
addition the footing subgrade should be dense firm and unyielding If soft and or loose
materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations they should be
over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation should be
backfilled with excavated on site materials compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction or the footing may be extended down to the underlying competent material
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate that the total footing settlements under the anticipated
design loads to be on the order of 1 inch Differential settlements between adjacent
footings should be on the order of about 0 5 inches
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 35 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted on site soils or imported
fill Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soils
around the footings are well compacted The passive pressure should be reduced for
foundations located on slopes or adjacent to retaining walls Unless covered by pavements
or slabs the passive pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected

Site Grading
Based on the information provided we understand that the existing site will need to
be raised by placement of approximately 1 to 2 feet of fill in order to achieve the design
finished grades Based on the existing grades at the site we envision that the fill materials
needed to raise the site to the design finished grades will need to be imported In general
we recommend that the following guidelines be used for the site grading work anticipated
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials within the
grading limits should be removed and disposed of properly off site The existing broken
concrete slabs and asphalt concrete pavements at the site should also be removed to
expose the underlying soils The construction debris generated from the demolition
operations should be properly disposed of off site
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to
receive fill or future improvements should be over excavated to expose firm or dense
material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill The
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4276 00 Page 13
excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site Existing utilities
to be abandoned should be removed and the resulting excavation should be properly
backfilled with well compacted fill Contract documents should include additive and
deductive unit prices for over excavation and compacted fill placement to account for
variation in the over excavation quantities
After clearing and demolition areas beneath the future warehouse structure and
parking areas should be over excavated where necessary to provide the slab on grade or
pavement section Prior to placement of the new fills the existing subgrade soils should be
scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of
the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91
test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend
that the subgrade soil be prepared again as recommended above Saturation and
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage
may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted fill
The new fill materials needed to raise the site to the design finished grades should
consist of imported granular materials Imported granular materials should consist of
non expansive select granular fill such as crushed coral basalt gravel or cinder sand The
material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in
largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the
No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should
have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less The materials should be tested by
Geolabs Hawaii for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project
site for the intended use

Drainage
The finished grades outside the warehouse structure should be sloped to shed water
away from the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that
gutter systems be installed around the structure and that the discharge be diverted away
from the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the
foundations and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be
avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into
the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be properly
backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce
the potential for water infiltration In addition drainage swales should be provided as soon
as possible and should be maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the
foundations and slabs

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings extending to depths of about 16 5 to 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface
at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using
a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers In addition a
bulk sample shown as Bulk 1 on Plate 2 of the near surface soils was retrieved from the
site for laboratory testing
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 2 inch OD Standard Penetration sampler or a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler
with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler
the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the
field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive
strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Two 1 inch ring swell tests were performed on the natural soil samples to evaluate the
swelling potential of the near surface soils The test results are presented on Plate B 1
Three Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of the
tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the moisture content and dry density
relationships The results of the tests are presented on Plate B 3
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM D 1883 was performed
on a bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The results of the tests are presented on Plate B 4
