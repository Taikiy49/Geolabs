encountered fill material consisting of medium dense cobbles gravel and silty sands

drainage at the site In addition the relatively thin and poor quality base course material

pavements Therefore we recommend that longitudinal edge drains be provided along
the sides of the roadway reconstruction alignment Based on the relatively light traffic

due to the near vertical cut of the slopes Remedial measures to reduce the potential
instability of the near vertical cut slopes include flattening the existing cut slopes
constructing a retaining wall in front of the cut slopes or lining the cut slopes with
grouted rubble paving

2 1 Regional Geology
American Samoa is a group of seven islands including Tutuila Aunu u Ofu
Olosega Ta u Rose and Swains It has a total area of about 76 square miles and is
located approximately 2 276 miles south southwest of the State of Hawaii The Island of
Tutuila was built during the Pliocene and early Pleistocene age by the extrusion of basaltic
lava and pyroclastic materials from five volcanoes Following the cessation of the main
shield building activity there was a long period of erosion accompanied by the growth of
fringing coral reefs During the late Quaternary Age volcanism was renewed by eruptions
of lava and pyroclastic materials at several vents on the south side of the island forming
the Tafuna Leone Plain
The main mountain mass of Tutuila was divided into seven formations The rocks
are believed to be of Pliocene Epoch consisting of thinly bedded to massive flows of
basalt andesitic basalt and andesite These massive rock formations are interbedded
with cinder cones and tuff beds with thrachytic dikes plugs and crater fills The rock units
were erupted from as many as five eruptive centers but the principal volcano was near the
middle of the island Pago Pago Harbor is believed to occupy part of the collapsed caldera
of that principal volcano Erosion along a major fault along the caldera wall formed the
near vertical cliff on the northeast side of the harbor
Prior to the eruption of the Leone Volcano beach and lagoonal deposits
accumulated on and behind a submerged barrier reef at the present site of the Leone
Plain These deposits were later buried under several hundred feet of volcanic
materials These older beach and lagoonal deposits consist partially of sand and
coralline gravel
Three tuff cones and a thick sequence of lava flow form the plain between Tafuna
and Leone The rock is believed to be of Holocene Age possibly only a few thousand

3 2 Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms observed in pavement structures is
pumping due to saturation of the base course and or subgrade soils In addition free
water voids in the pavement section repeated heavy wheel loads and an erodible
base can also cause pumping
In general the primary source of free water in the pavement section is surface
water infiltration through cracks and joints in the pavement A major source of infiltrated
moisture is the longitudinal pavement shoulder joint Water also enters the pavement
section from shallow ditches To reduce moisture infiltration into the pavement
structure all pavement joints and cracks should be sealed to reduce infiltration and
pavement drainage systems should be installed to collect and divert free water from the
pavement section Therefore considering the high rainfall in this area longitudinal
edge drains should be considered along alignments with relatively flat gradients and
transverse drains should be provided at low spots along the roadway alignment
Schematic details of the edge drains are presented on Plate 3 As _ previously
mentioned the pavement surface and the subgrade should be sloped and drainage
gradients maintained to carry all surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage
structures

3 3 Pipe Culverts
We understand that existing pipe culverts will be replaced with RCP and HDPE
pipe culverts and additional RCP and HDPE pipe culverts will be installed In general
a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of free draining granular material such as
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipe
culverts If soft and or loose soils are encountered at the proposed pipe inverts an
additional 18 inches of free draining granular material wrapped in a non woven
geotextile such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent should be provided below the bedding
layer for more uniform support The free draining granular material should also be used
for the initial backfill up to the springline of the pipe 0 5 times the pipe diameter to
provide adequate support around the pipes For larger pipe culverts exceeding
24 inches in diameter we believe that the initial backfill may be reduced to 0 25 times
the outside pipe diameter measured from the top of the bedding layer
Where groundwater is present above the pipe free draining granular material
should be used as backfill up to at least 12 inches above the water level It is critical
that free draining granular materials be used for the initial backfill to reduce the potential
for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for
the sides of the pipes which could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the
pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level of the springline of the
pipes or above the initial backfill level as recommended for larger diameter pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of general fill with a maximum
particle size of less than 3 inches The backfill material should be moisture conditioned
to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture placed in maximum 8 inch level loose
lifts and mechanically compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
A minimum cover of 3 feet should be maintained above the top of the pipes to the
finished grades in order to facilitate construction of the pavement structures

3 4 Retaining Structures
Retaining structures such as retaining walls and headwall structures should be
designed to resist the lateral earth pressures due to the adjacent soils and surcharge
effects The following general guidelines may be used for preliminary design of retaining
structures
Retaining Structure Foundations
Retaining structures may be supported on shallow continuous footings bearing on
the stiff clayey soils or the extremely to highly weathered basalt encountered in our
test pits An allowable bearing pressure of 2 500 pounds per square foot psf may
be used for design of footings bearing on the stiff clayey soils or extremely to highly
weathered basalt This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third 1 3 when considering transient loads such as wind or
seismic forces
Retaining structure foundations should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches
below the lowest adjacent finished grade If soft and or loose materials are
exposed at the footing subgrade the soft and or loose materials should be
over excavated to expose firm or dense material The resulting over excavation
should be backfilled with fill material compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction Where footings are located adjacent to slopes or on slopes
the footings should be embedded to provide a minimum horizontal set back
distance of at least 6 feet measured from the edge of the footing to the face of
the slope Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should be
constructed in stepped footings
Footings located next to utility trenches and or easements should be embedded
below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the
utility trench or embedded to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with

Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges line loads and point loads within a
horizontal distance equal to the depth of the structure should be considered in
the design For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the
structure a rectangular distribution with uniform pressure equal to 36 percent of
the vertical surcharge pressure acting on the entire height of the structure which
is free to deflect cantilever may be used for design For structures that are
restrained a rectangular distribution equal to 53 percent of the vertical surcharge
pressure acting over the entire height of the structure may be used for design
Additional analyses during design may be needed to evaluate the surcharge
effects of point loads and line loads
Drainage

weepholes should be spaced 6 feet apart or jess

Existing Cut Slopes
As previously mentioned the existing cut slopes along the right side of the roadway
appeared to be unstable due to the near vertical cut of the slopes The instability of the
slope appeared to be localized near the face of the cut slopes and do not seem to be
deep seated Some old localized slope failures were observed along the cut slopes as
evident by head scarps and fragments of extremely to highly weathered basalt fragments
at the toe of the cut slopes In addition some potential localized slope failures were
observed along the cut slopes Remedial measures to reduce the potential instability of
the existing cut slopes include flattening the existing cut slopes constructing a retaining
wall along the face of the existing cut slopes or lining the existing cut slopes with grouted
rubble paving GRP
Flattening Existing Cut Slopes
Flattening the existing cut slopes is one option to increase the stability of the cut
slopes The cut slopes may be flattened to a ratio of one and one half horizontal to
one vertical or flatter in cut slopes consisting of soils or extremely
weathered basalt saprolite The slopes may be flattened to a ratio of 1H 1V in
slopes consisting of highly to moderately weathered basalt

cut slopes However due to the limited amount of space along the roadway

turbulence at the drop off area
