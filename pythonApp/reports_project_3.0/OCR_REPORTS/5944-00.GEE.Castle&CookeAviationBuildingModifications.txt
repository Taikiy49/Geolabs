1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Castle Cooke Aviation Building Modification
project in Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The project
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and _ geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and
engineering analyses These recommendations are intended to assist in the design of
the new office spaces and an elevator shaft pit only The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The project site is on the eastern side of the Honolulu International Airport The
project will involve the addition of new office space on the first floor of the Castle
Cooke Aviation Building formerly Circle Rainbow at the mauka side of the intersection
of Lagoon Drive and Kapalulu Place in Honolulu on the Island of 1
43 The existing two story building covers most of the lot with parking areas
in the front and rear of the site Office spaces are planned along the front north
western side of the existing building and rear south eastern side over the existing
parking lot A new elevator is also planned at the rear side of the project site The
building location is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Koolau
Volcano reached the end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago
The project site is on the southern flank of Koolau Volcano and its geomorphology
and subsurface conditions are directly related to the glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea
level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain
During the later Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles
of continental glaciation As the continental glaciers advanced less water was available to
fill the oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell to below its present stand When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea
levels rose to above its present level
Glacio eustatic sea level fluctuations affected the processes of erosion and
deposition The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine sediments in the
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and
calcareous materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal
deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial
erosion of the upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under
relatively high energy conditions
During periods of insignificant sea level changes continued stream action extended
the alluvial delta and fans seaward by deposition of alluvium over the previously deposited

lagoonal sediments Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued to accumulate in
low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in thick deposits of soft harbor
sediments along the coast in areas that were formerly valleys and drainageways
The geologic history of the area has been further complicated by the deposition of
recent pyroclastic materials and lava flows from eruptions of the vents of members of the
Honolulu Volcanic Series including the Aliamanu Salt Lake Makalapa vents These
events were contemporaneous with the Pleistocene sea level fluctuations The deposits of
pyroclastics have consolidated to form volcanic tuff locally referred to as mudrock
These eruptions caused a diversion of Moanalua Stream from its original course to its
current location
The proposed project is at the western side of Keehi Lagoon which is a drowned
stream channel at the mouth of Kalihi and Moanalua Streams The area is generally
underlain by old lagoonal finger coral reefs that have become filled with organic clays
and silts During the mid to late 20 Century urban development and reclamation
projects have altered the natural formation of the area by the placement of man made
fill over the lagoonal deposits

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the current design we understand that rental office spaces are planed
at both the front and side of the existing two story building Based on the ground floor
plan provided we believe the office spaces at the front side will be modified within the
existing building structures Meanwhile the office spaces at the side will be constructed
over the existing parking lot An elevator will also be installed within the side of the office
spaces
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by stiff clayey
fill over loose lagoonal deposit and coralline detritus at deeper depth Our laboratory
testing on the on site soils indicated low to moderate expansion potential when
subjected to changes in the moisture content We encountered shallow groundwater
levels at the time of our field exploration
Based on our field exploration and laboratory test results we recommend using
shallow footing foundations and slabs on grade to support the proposed structure The
shallow footing foundations should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the
finished grade and bear on properly compacted fill or selected stiff in situ material An
allowable bearing pressure of 2 500 pounds per square foot psf may be utilized for the
design of the new office space shallow footing foundations bearing on in situ stiff clayey
for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
To reduce the potential for structural distress to the future slabs on grade
resulting from the potential swelling of the surface clayey soils we recommend
supporting concrete slabs on grade on 4 inches of gravel cushion over at least 8 inches
of structural fill materials Special attention should be given to the subgrade preparation
requirements presented in the Site Preparation section herein to reduce the potential
for shrink swell effects on concrete slabs
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering design

3 1 Site Preparation
Based on the current condition of the existing building we anticipate that minimal
grading is required However areas within the contract grading limits should be cleared
and grubbed thoroughly Old pavement vegetation debris deleterious material and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off site or stockpiled in a
designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials
Slabs and foundations of the existing structures to be demolished should be
completely removed Over excavation resulting from the demolition should be backfilled
with compacted granular fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned should be
removed and the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with select granular fill
material moisture conditioned to above optimum moisture content placed in 6 to 8 inch
level loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Utilities to
be abandoned in place under the proposed structure should be backfilled by pumping lean
concrete under low pressure Soft and yielding areas encountered should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with well compacted engineered fill
After clearing and demolition the excavated subgrades underneath future slab
areas should be scarified to a depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to about 2 percent
above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Where shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend thoroughly moistening the soil to close cracks or preparing again as
recommended above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to
inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and
replacement with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft soils may be used as
fill provided that it meets fill material requirements Relative compaction refers to the
in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the
same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density

3 2 Building Foundations
Shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the
proposed office spaces provided they are embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the
finished grade to reduce the effects of future soil expansion
An allowable bearing pressure of may be utilized for the design of the
new office space foundations bearing on existing stiff clayey fill This bearing value is for
those caused by wind or seismic forces Footings should have a minimum width of
12 inches and should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the finished grade
We estimate that settlements for footings bearing on properly compacted fill or stiff
on site clayey fill as recommended herein to be less than 1 inch total Differential
settlement is estimated to be less than 0 5 inch The foundation system shall be designed
to resist the total and differential settlements
Soft and or loose materials encountered at the bottom of footing excavations should
be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation should be
backfilled with select granular material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction or the footing bottom may be deepened to the underlying firm materials
Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a
one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom
edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is
necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural
loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by friction developed

3 3 Slabs On Grade
We recommend supporting the new office spaces with concrete slabs on grade
Based on subsurface information and our laboratory test results the project site is
underlain by fill material with low to moderate expansion potential when subjected to
moisture fluctuations In general we recommend providing a minimum of 8 inches of
low expansive select granular structural fill material below the building slab areas The
structural fill should be moisture conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Additionally to reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded
slabs on grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of the soils due to changes in the
moisture content we recommend scarifying the excavated subgrades underneath future
slab areas to a depth of 8 inches moisture conditioning to about 2 percent above the
optimum moisture and compacting to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction

3 6 Utilities
We envision that new utility lines and utility connections may be required for the
proposed project For support of the utility lines we recommend using a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation under the
utilities The initial backfill up to about 12 inches above the utilities should consist of
free draining material such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for utility
damage from compaction of the backfill It is critical to use a free draining granular material
to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the utilities and to provide adequate
support for the sides of the utilities The use of on site soils as backfill directly around
utilities is not recommended
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the utilities
to the finish subgrade should consist of the approved on site soils or select granular
material The backfill material should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in
loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to
reduce the potential for future ground subsidence The upper 2 feet of the trench backfill
below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to no less than 95 percent relative
compaction Mechanical compaction equipment should be used to compact the

3 7 Drainage
The finished grades adjacent to the structures should be sloped to shed water away
from the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised to install
gutter systems around the structures and to divert the discharge away from the foundation
and pavement areas Blockage or clogging of drainage system could cause ponding of
water at the building structure and foundations resulting in foundation and building
distress
Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should be avoided
Gravel strips adjacent to slabs and foundations should also be avoided Planters next to
foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water
infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent loss of strength of the near surface soils The foundation
excavations should be backfilled properly against the footings immediately after setting
of the concrete to reduce water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon
as possible and should be maintained to drain surface run off away from walls and
foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling
three borings to 21 and 20 3 foot depths below the existing ground surface using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with auger equipment at the approximate locations
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field and observed the field exploration operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual
observation and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 3
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 and D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler
the second and third 6 inches of an 18 to 24 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings
at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight determinations ASTM D 2937
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Two one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively natural and remolded
samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the soils under surcharge pressures These
tests were run on air dried samples The test results are presented on Plate B 1
One Unconfined Compression test ASTM D 2166 was performed on a selected
sample to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the surface clayey fill
encountered Results of the unconfined compression test are presented on Plate B 2
