FIELD EXPLORATION
The subsurface conditions to date were explored by drilling and sampling seven
test borings designated as Boring Nos 101 through 105 107 and 109 Due to limited site
access conditions only seven borings have been completed to date Additional borings
will be drilled after the demolition of the existing hotel The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary wash coring tools
and generally extended to depths of about 31 to 51 5 feet below the existing ground
surface The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered were classified by visual and textural examination in the
field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a near continuous basis
These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory Soils
were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System as
shown on the Boring Log Legend Plate 4 Graphic representations of the materials
encountered and water levels observed in the borings drilled are provided on the attached
Logs of Borings Plates 5 through 11
Soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with
ASTM _ D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some samples
were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTMD 1586
Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests
are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the basalt formations encountered at the project site were
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the basalt formation The material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
As mentioned previously a field exploration program consisting of drilling
seven test borings extending to depths of about 31 to 51 5 feet below the existing ground
surface was conducted for the project to date The boring locations are shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2 In addition the subsurface conditions also were evaluated based on
past field explorations at the project site conducted by others and provided for our use
Our field exploration generally encountered surface fills underlain by cinder sands
a a basalt and dense basalt formation The fill materials generally consisted of very stiff to
hard sandy clays and medium dense to dense silty sands and gravel extending down to
depths of about 1 to 25 feet below the ground surface Cinder sands consisting of medium
dense sands were encountered in some of the borings drilled below the surface fills
The fill materials and cinder sands were generally underlain by interbedded layers
of medium dense a a basalt and dense basalt formation extending to the maximum depth
explored of about 51 5 feet below the ground surface The dense basalt formations
encountered generally were hard to very hard and relatively unweathered to moderately
weathered Based on the borings drilled the depths at which dense basalt formations
were encountered ranged from about 1 foot below the existing ground surface in Boring
No 107 to about 43 5 feet below the existing ground surface in Boring No 103 The
following table summarizes the estimated depth to dense basalt formation for the borings

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris deleterious materials old foundations
of the existing structures walkways and other unsuitable materials should be removed
and disposed properly off site to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated
materials
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas
designated to receive fill and or future improvements should be over excavated to expose
firm materials and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill
The excavated soft soils should not be re used as a source of fill materials and should be
disposed properly off site
In general the subgrades in cut areas and areas designated to receive fills should
be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of
the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Where hard basalt formation is exposed the subgrades in cut areas and areas
designated to receive fills should be proof rolled with a large vibratory drum roller
minimum 10 tons static weight for a minimum of eight passes to help detect and collapse
near surface cavities and or voids The vibratory drum roller should be operated at a
speed of about 300 feet per minute
Yielding areas loose areas or cavities disclosed during the clearing and
proof rolling operations should be over excavated and backfilled with compacted fill
materials The depth of over excavation should be extended until dense underlying
materials are exposed and should be evaluated in the field by a Geolabs representative

Fills and Backfills
In general the excavated on site materials may be re used as a source of
general fill materials except excavated soft soils provided that the materials are free of
vegetation deleterious materials and clay lumps and rock fragments greater than
6 inches in maximum dimension It should be noted that the excavated rock materials
will need to be processed to reduce the particle size to 6 inches or less in order to be
re used as a source of fill materials
Imported materials to be used as select granular fill should be non expansive
granular materials such as crushed coral basalt or cinder sands The select granular fill
should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest
dimension The material should have a California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 20 or
higher and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with
ASTM D 1883 The material also should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles
passing the No 200 sieve Imported fill materials should be tested for conformance with
these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
In general fills and backfills should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Compaction requirements for the
subgrades of improvements subjected to vehicular traffic such as the ground surface of
the parking structure driveways and pavements should be increased to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by
ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction of the fill materials should be accomplished by utilizing sheepsfoot
rollers vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment Water
tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed to compact the fills in lieu of mechanical
compaction of the fill materials
Cut and Fill Slopes
We envision that cut slopes at the project site will generally expose the surface fills
cinder sands a a basalt and or medium hard to very hard basalt formation encountered in
the borings In general cut slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of
two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter In general we believe cut slopes that
expose dense basalt formation may be designed with slope inclination steeper than
2H 1V However due to the variable ground conditions and potential presence of loose
clinker and porous pahoehoe basalt proposed cut slopes with inclinations steeper than
2H 1V should be evaluated in case by case basis In addition it is important that a

Permanent fill slopes constructed using the excavated on site soils or imported
materials may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fills placed on
slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to
provide stability of the new fill against sliding A typical slope detail is shown on Plate 3
Construction of earth berms interceptor ditches and the use of geotextile fabrics
over the fill slope face should be considered to reduce the potential for significant erosion
of the slope face thus enhancing the long term stability of the fill slopes Appropriate slope
planting or other erosion control measures to reduce the potential for significant erosion of
the exposed slopes including a permanent irrigation system should be implemented as
soon as possible after the finished slope faces are completed
Excavation
Based on our field exploration the project site generally is underlain by fill materials
at the ground surface Medium hard to very hard basalt formation is present near the
ground surface in some areas It is anticipated that the near surface fills cinder sands and
a a basalt materials may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment such as
excavating with a backhoe excavator However it should be noted that the contractor likely
will encounter some difficult excavation conditions requiring the use of hoe ramming or
the need for drilling and blasting during construction where the hard basalt formation is
encountered
The above discussions regarding the excavation of the subsurface materials are
based on our visual observation of the existing site conditions and field data from the
borings drilled at the project site We recommend that contractors proposing to bid on this
project be encouraged to examine the site conditions and the boring data to make their
own interpretation
