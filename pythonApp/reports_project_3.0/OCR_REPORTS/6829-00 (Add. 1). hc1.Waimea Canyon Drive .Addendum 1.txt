This addendum to our report entitled “Geotechnical Engineering Exploration, 
Waimea Canyon Drive/Kokee Road Improvements, Phase II, Waimea, Kauai, Hawaii,” 
dated September 11, 2013, provides additional geotechnical recommendations for the 
soil-nailed wall design to stabilize the two eroded embankments along Kokee Road in the 
vicinity of Mileposts 9.3 and 9.4, next to an existing shallow, unlined irrigation ditch.  
Soil-Nailed Retaining Wall System  
We understand that it is desired to stabilize the two eroded embankments in the 
vicinity of Mileposts 9.3 and 9.4 with a new retaining wall system. Based on the existing 
site conditions and construction considerations, we understand that a soil-nailed 
retaining wall system is proposed to reinforce the two eroded slopes along Kokee Road.

Construction of the permanent soil-nailed retaining wall system should be 
performed by a specialty contractor experienced in the construction of soil-nailed 
retaining walls. Due to the specialized nature of the construction, a Geolabs 
representative should be present to observe the geotechnical aspects of the soil-nailed 
retaining wall and testing of the soil nails.  
Wall Preparation 
At the on-set of construction, surface vegetation, loose material, and deleterious 
material should be removed from the embankment slopes, dry-stack rock wall face and 
adjacent areas. The existing dry-stack boulders in the vicinity of Milepost 9.4 should not 
be removed from the wall face to avoid causing slope instability. Care should be taken 
in removing material so that the stability of the existing wall and slope is not 
compromised.  
Soil-Nailed Wall 
A soil-nailed retaining wall system consists of a series of individual reinforcing 
bars grouted into drilled holes to stabilize an excavation or slope. A shotcrete facing is 
generally applied to the face of the wall or slope, providing the appearance of a 
conventional concrete retaining wall. To provide a natural rock appearance, the 
shotcrete facing may be sculpted and stained to match the appearance of the 
surrounding environment. The reinforcing bars provide additional tensile and shear 
strength to the wall. The reinforced soil mass behaves in a manner similar to a gravity 
retaining wall but lacks a footing. 
The soil nails should be installed by drilling a minimum 5.5-inch diameter hole 
with an inclination of approximately 15 degrees from horizontal. The inclination of the 
nails may be increased to avoid existing underground structures. The soil nail bar 
should consist of an ASTM A615 Grade 75 threaded bar with a minimum bar diameter 
of 1.25 inches. We recommend using a double corrosion protection system for the nails 
consisting of epoxy-coated bars surrounded by neat cement grout or sand-cement 
mixture with a minimum 28-day compressive strength of 4,000 pounds per square inch 
(psi). 
Based on our soil nail analysis, we recommend using design nail lengths of 
11 feet extending into the residual soil encountered in our field exploration. The soil 
nails should be spaced 4 feet on-center vertically and 6 feet on-center horizontally. The 
first nail should be installed about 2 feet below the top of the slope.

Based on the above design parameters, the soil-nailed retaining wall system 
should have a factor of safety of 1.5 or greater for the combined internal and external 
stability for the static loading condition considering a surcharge load of 250 pounds per 
square foot (psf) on the roadway travel lanes.  
Soil Nail Installation 
To facilitate the installation of the soil nails through the existing dry-stack rock 
wall, a shotcrete flash coat should first be applied to the face of the dry-stack wall. Soil 
nail installation activities should avoid causing instability to the existing wall.  
Potentially difficult drilling conditions may be encountered during the installation 
of the soil nails due to the presence of the dry-stack rock wall and boulders (core 
stones) in the residual soil. Utilizing a temporary casing may be required to maintain an 
open hole for the soil nail installation. 
Soil Nail Testing 
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated behind the soil-nailed retaining 
wall, we recommend performing a minimum of one pre-production verification test on a 
sacrificial soil nail installed near the top of the slope on the southern end of the existing 
near-vertical dry-stack retaining wall. In addition, proof tests should be performed on at 
least 10 percent of the production soil nails during construction. 
The verification test should be loaded gradually to 150 percent of the design load 
(23 kips) for the creep test and subsequently to the maximum test load of about 
200 percent of the design load. In addition, the load at every loading increment should 
be held for at least 10 minutes, and creep tests should be held for at least 60 minutes. 
The proof tests should consist of subjecting the soil nail to at least 150 percent of the 
design loads and the load should be held stable for at least 10 minutes. The proof test 
nails may be incorporated into the permanent soil nail retaining wall provided they 
satisfy the test criteria. Pullout tests and creep tests on the soil nails are integral 
components of the design for the soil nail retaining wall system. Therefore, we 
recommend conducting pullout tests and creep tests in the presence of a Geolabs 
representative. 
Shotcrete Facing 
Shotcrete placement should be performed by an experienced nozzleman. Prior to 
production shotcreting, it is recommended that unreinforced test panels of shotcrete be 
constructed for inspection. We understand that the shotcrete facing may be sculptured. 
Therefore, adequate thickness of the final facing should be provided to accommodate 
the sculptured surfacing.

Drainage
The soil-nailed retaining wall should be well-drained to reduce the potential for
the build-up of hydrostatic pressures. Drainage elements consisting of 2-foot wide strips
of a prefabricated drainage composite product may be used on the face of the slope
before application of a shotcrete flash coat used for temporary stabilization. The
prefabricated drainage composite product should extend from the top of the slope to the
base of the slope and hydraulically connected to a weep hole near the base of the wall.
