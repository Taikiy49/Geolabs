INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Sea Cliff Development at Mauna Lani Resort in the Anaehoomalu area
within the District of South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity
of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations based on our
review and analyses of the site conditions the grading plan and the subsurface information
obtained for this project The geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of
the site preparation grading underground utilities and pavements for the subject project
only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations
noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is situated on the northern flank of Hualalai Mountain The lava
formation encountered at the site appears to be of a a type flow which spread downslope
from vents further up on the mountain flank A a lavas are typically characterized by a
porous rough and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock
fragments including cobbles and boulders Typically a more dense and layered lava rock
material is contained within the lava flow core
Cavities such as lava tubes or other void space are commonly encountered buried
within a a lavas Many of these cavities were formed when the still molten lava created the
structures by means of flow and ponding of the lava The cavities represent both lava tubes
intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava tubes are
W O 4208 00 Page 5
formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure
which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are
generally smaller in horizontal extent
The a a basalt lava rock and clinkers encountered at the site is generally considered
to be relatively permeable rock and may transmit water readily in both horizontal and vertical
directions Water is transmitted through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures
inter flow contacts cavities and along embedded clinker layers The permeability of the
clinker and cavities is high therefore they serve as the major water transmission features

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Structural fill materials 6 inch minus fills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding
12 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction General fills and backfills
should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 24 inches in loose thickness
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum
dry density We wish to emphasize that moisture conditioning of the fill materials is
an integral part of the fill placement recommendations provided herein Compaction
should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other types of
acceptable compaction equipment
Rock fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of the
rocks or 5 feet whichever is less Placement should be by haul trucks traversing
previously placed lifts and dumping at the edge of the currently placed lift Spreading
of each layer of the rock fill should be performed by a bulldozer to facilitate seating
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 4208 00 Page 13
of the rock The rock fill should be watered heavily during placement by water trucks
traversing in front of the current rock lift face and spraying water continuously during
placement Compaction equipment such as a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar
heavy compaction equipment providing suitable energy to achieve the required
compaction should be utilized to compact the rock fill The number of passes to be
made to achieve proper compaction should be determined at the on set of site
grading by the performance of a trial compaction test section or by plate bearing tests
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of
rocks boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number
of passes by a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should
be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation
of a representative from Geolabs Hawaii Based on this testing program the number
of passes may then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction

Pavement Design
Based on the information provided asphalt concrete pavements will be used for the
proposed roadways and parking areas planned In general we anticipate that the vehicle
loading for the proposed roadways and parking areas for this project would consist primarily
of passenger vehicles and light trucks Therefore we have assumed light to medium traffic
loading conditions for pavement design purposes We understand that the following
pavement section is generally required by the County of Hawaii Department of Public Works
County of Hawaii Standard Detail R 34
2 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
4 0 Inch Base Course
6 0 Inch Select Borrow Subbase
12 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness over Subgrade
In general the purpose of the subbase course required in the County of Hawaii
Standard section is to serve as a foundation or stabilizing course such that the subsequent
base and surfacing layers may be constructed on top of an inferior subgrade soil The
standard pavement section does not take into consideration good quality subgrade
conditions Since the subgrade conditions along the proposed roadway alignment at the
proposed Sea Cliff Development at Mauna Lani Resort will consist primarily of compacted
granular fill material it is our opinion that a subbase course for stabilization foundation
purposes is not necessary due to the high quality of the subgrade materials anticipated at
the subject site If the roadway subgrade exposes poorer subgrade soils at the site we
recommend that the poorer subgrade soils be removed and be replaced with structural fill
material 6 inch minus material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
If a subbase course is mandatory we believe that the compacted rock fill below the
base course layer can be considered as a subbase material since its pavement support
characteristics with CBR value over 60 would be equal to or better than that of the select

Asphalt Concrete Pavement
2 0 Inch Asphalt Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
8 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Subgrade
It is our opinion that the strength and performance of the proposed pavement section
of 2 inches of asphalt concrete over 6 inches of base course is no less substantial than the
County of Hawaii Standard section for this site
The subgrade soils under the pavement area should be scarified to a minimum depth
of 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to at least
95 percent relative compaction CBR tests and or field observations should be performed
on the actual subgrade soils during construction to confirm that the above design sections
are adequate The base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to
a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry surface
water off the pavements Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or
after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or adjacent to
the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs extend a minimum of 2 inches into the
subgrade soil to reduce the potential for migration of landscape water into the pavement
section Alternatively a subdrain system could be constructed to collect excessive water
from landscaping irrigation For long term performance we recommend that a subdrain
system be constructed adjacent to paved landscaped areas

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling six
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 at the approximate locations shown on the
General Site Plan Plate 2 and Site Plans Plates 3 1 and 3 2 The depths of our borings
ranged from about 15 to 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were
drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with rotary coring tools
The subsurface conditions at the site were also explored by excavating 17 test pits
extending to depths of about 5 to 10 feet below the existing ground surface Logs of the
materials encountered in the test pits are provided on Plates B 1 through B 5 of Appendix B
Locations of the test pits excavated are also shown on Plates and 3 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is recovered
from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs
of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
