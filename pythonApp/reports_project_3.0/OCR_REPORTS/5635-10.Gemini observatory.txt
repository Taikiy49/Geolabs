In accordance with the Purchase Order P270956AN and the scope of services
outlined in our electronic transmittal dated July 27, 2007, we have conducted a rockfall
hazard assessment for the cut slopes along the rear parking lot at the Gemini Observatory
- Hilo Base Facility Expansion project. This letter report summarizes our findings and
presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations resulting from our engineering
evaluation and assessment.
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is within the existing Gemini Hilo Base Facility at 670 North
A‘ohoku Street in the Hilo area on the Island of Hawaii. It is situated between the existing
Gemini Hilo Base Facility (Buildings A and B) to the east and Komohana Street to the west
at the southwestern corner of the base facility. -
Based on the existing topography and the grading concept, cuts on the order of
about 15 feet in depth into the existing hillside were required to attain the design finish
grades for the new parking lot construction. Due to the severe fracturing of the exposed
rock slope and its proximity to the new parking lot, there exists the potential for rockfall
events that may cause personal injury and/or property damage. Therefore, it is desired to
conduct an assessment of the cut slope conditions and to recommend some form of
rockfall protection to reduce the hazardous condition at the site, if deemed necessary.
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
A site reconnaissance was made on August 3, 2007 to observe the existing
conditions of the rear cut slopes at the above-referenced site. At the time of our site visit,
the existing hillside at the rear end of the property had been generally graded to a design
cut slope ratio of about 0.5H:1V. Based on our field observations, the majority of the
exposed cut slopes generally consist of medium hard to hard basalt rock formation that is
moderately weathered and severely fractured.

We believe that the basalt rock formation underlying the cut slopes is generally
competent and stable in terms of slope stability. However, the severe fracturing of the rock
materials exposed at the slope face may cause unraveling of small (generally cobble size)
pieces of rock materials due to erosion resulting from heavy rainstorms, etc. Due to the
proximity of the cut slopes to the planned parking lot, falling rock that bounces, rolls, or
falls directly onto the parking lot is a real problem with a significant potential to cause
personal injury and/or property damage. To reduce the potential for these hazardous
rockfall events along the cut slopes, we believe that it is necessary to implement some
form of rockfall protection at the existing hillside adjacent to the new parking lot.
Based on our site reconnaissance and engineering evaluation, we believe that the
rockfall mitigation program for the subject hillside may consist of the following measures:
(1) cutting back the existing rock slope to provide a rockfall catchment ditch along the toe
area; (2) installation of a rockfall impact barrier along the toe area of the cut slope; and
(3) installation of a draped wire mesh cover over the cut slope face to control falling rocks
from encroaching into the parking lot. Detailed discussions and feasibility of these rockfall
mitigation measures are presented below:
Rockfall Catchment Ditch
Since only cobble size rock materials are anticipated from the unraveling of the
slope face, we believe that one of the construction alternatives that may be
implemented for rockfall hazard mitigation is the provision of a rockfall catchment
ditch along the toe area of the cut slopes. Based on our field observations, the toe
area of the existing hillside will be butted against the new parking lot separated only
by a 2-foot wide drainage swale. In addition, the crest of the existing hillside is in
proximity to the existing Komohana Street. Therefore, significant cutting back of the
rock slope to provide a rockfall catchment ditch does not appear to be feasible nor
practical since the majority of the hillside grading work has already been performed.
Rockfall Impact Barrier
In lieu of a rockfall catchment ditch, a low-energy rockfall impact barrier may be
considered along the toe area of the hillside to prevent the falling rocks from
bouncing off and/or rolling into the parking lot area. Based on the construction
drawings provided, we understand that the toe of the existing rock slope is at the
edge of the new parking lot pavement separated by a 2-foot wide drainage swale.
Due to the limited space available, re-designing the parking lot or cutting into the
toe area of the existing hillside will be required in order to install the rockfall impact
barrier for this approach. Furthermore, we understand that erection of a rockfall
impact barrier is aesthetically undesirable to the users of the base facility.
Therefore, this rockfall mitigation option is not further pursued in our evaluation.

Draped Wire Mesh
Since a rockfall catchment ditch or rockfall impact barrier cannot be developed or
constructed due to physical limitations at the project site, we believe that covering
the cut slopes with a draped wire mesh is a viable solution to the potential rockfall
hazards at the site. Draped wire mesh is a slope treatment that involves the
installation of wire mesh panels and supporting cables on the slope face in an effort
to control the fall of rock material down the slope face toward the base of the slope.
As previously mentioned, the draped wire mesh cover should assist in the
controlled release of loose rock that is shed from the slope face during weathering
and erosion. Therefore, it is not the primary intent to closely pin the wire mesh to
the slope face as a retention system. The draped wire mesh should provide positive
rockfall control for the useful life of the wire mesh material. It is anticipated that
some periodic maintenance will be necessary to remove the natural accumulation
of soil and rock debris at the bottom of the draped mesh.
The installation of a draped wire mesh covering the slope face serves to control the
release of loose rocks that may develop on the slope face in the years following the
slope cutting work. Following the slope cutting effort, the majority of the large blocks
of rock and boulders would have been removed. Therefore, the draped wire mesh
should help retain some loose rocks on the slope, but more importantly, it should
allow for the controlled release of fallen rock toward the base of the slope at the
parking lot level. The draped wire mesh cover essentially contains the broken rock
material against the slope face as the fallen rock materials move downward by
gravity. In this way, the rock fragments are deposited along the mesh outlet at the
base of the slope.
Normally, the wire mesh panels should not be pinned to the slope face. The bottom
of the panels should remain open to discharge fallen rock material at the base of
the slope. A narrow containment area along the toe of the hillside should be
provided to collect the accumulation of debris and allow for periodic cleanup. Where
a catchment ditch is provided, the mesh panels should terminate at about 1 to
2 feet above the ground surface to allow for maintenance of the catchment ditch
and clean-out of fallen material with heavy construction equipment. If a dedicated
catchment ditch is not provided (as in this situation), the panels should terminate
closer to the ground surface to provide additional control of the fallen/accumulated
debris.
Various types of mesh are available based on the desired mesh strength, protection
from corrosion, and colorization. The wire mesh material is generally available as
galvanized or stainless steel wire, which would be covered with colored PVC
coating. The galvanized wire mesh panels are anticipated to have a life expectancy
of about 10 to 15 years in an aggressive corrosion environment before
maintenance or replacement of worn mesh panels may be needed. The life

mesh and hardware should be corrosion resistant. Therefore, we propose that the

Once the rock slope cutting work has been completed, installation of the draped
wire mesh may proceed. Grouted rock anchors of about 5 to 10 feet in depth are
grouted into the solid rock about 5 to 10 feet beyond the crest of the rock slope.
The grouted rock anchors are generally installed at about 20 feet on centers along
the slope crest. The manufacturer of the specific type of draped mesh to be used
for the project should be further consulted for detailed installation requirements.
Galvanized steel wire rope is strung continuously along the top of the slope through
the additional grouted rock anchors known as lateral anchors at the corners and at
the bends in the alignment of the steel wire rope. The wire mesh is attached and
draped from the cable ropes to cover the entire rock slope. In general, a minimum
of about 1-foot of overlap between adjacent rolls of wire mesh is provided. It should
be noted that the wire mesh may be colored to approximate the general coloration
of the natural slope.
The rolls of wire mesh are usually draped starting from the top of the slope. The
wire mesh is hung to within about 1 foot above the base level to allow for removal
of loose rock that may fall from behind the draped wire mesh during installation.
The draped lengths of wire mesh are then tied together by the rock slope scalers to
complete the installation.
Based on our field observations, existing isolated boulders at the top of the cut
slopes should be removed as soon as practical to reduce the potential for rolling down the
existing hillside. In addition, we also recommend that re-vegetation of the cut slopes with
appropriate ground cover following the draped mesh installation be implemented.
