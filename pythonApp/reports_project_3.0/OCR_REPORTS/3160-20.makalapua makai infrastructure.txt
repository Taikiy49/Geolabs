INTRODUCTION
In accordance with our revised fee proposal of April 15th and the Underground
Injection Control U I C permit application No UH 1867 we have completed our visual
observations during construction of one of the total of three new drywells planned at
the Makalapua Makai Infrastructure project site In addition we have performed one
injection test for the new drywells This report has been prepared in support of
Underground Injection Control permit application No UH 1867 The approximate
location of the project site and its vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate
1 This report summarizes findings resulting from our field observation and testing of
the new drywell at the project site
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Makalapua Makai Infrastructure project site is located approximately mile
west of the Kona industrial subdivision in the town of Kailua Kona District of North
Kona on the island of Hawaii The project consists of approximately 0 9 mile of new
roadway Makala Boulevard and roadway extensions Luhia Street as well as
drainage improvements at the intersection of Kuakini Highway and Kaiwi Street The
County of Hawaii requires that new development create no net increase in discharge
of surface or storm water runoff The general practice on the island of Hawaii is to
utilize drywells as a method of disposal of surface runoff

A total of three new drywells have been constructed at the Makalapua Makai
Infrastructure project site for disposal of surface and storm water runoff under UIC
permit No UH 1867 Under the conditions set forth in the approval to construct the
drywells the Department of Health has required that one of the new drywell
excavations be logged for geologic lithology The same drywell was selected to
undergo short term injection testing to evaluate the operational status after construction

PHYSICAL CHARACTERISTICS
The site is located in Kailua Kona District of North Kona on the island of Hawaii
The climate of the area is mild with an average annual temperature of about 74 F and
annual rainfall ranging from about 20 to about 40 inches per year Rainfall at the site
typically occurs as daily light afternoon showers except during periods of stormy
weather where heavier rainfall can be expected
The site is situated on the lower western flank of Mount Hualalai The
topography is relatively gentle sloping down toward the the ocean westward from the
central portion of the island Elevations across the site range from about 20 to about
70 feet Mean Sea tevet MSL

Formerly the site had been undeveloped rocky land covered with brush and
scattered small trees At the time of the drywell injection testing the roadways had
been paved and the three drywells were completed and in a functional state
Regional Geology
The island of Hawaii is composed of five basalt shield volcanoes built by the
extrusion of lavas during the Pleistocene and the Holocene Epochs The site is located
on the western flank of Mount Hualalai a now dormant volcano which last erupted
between 1801 to 1803 This mountain is thought to be of an intermediate age between
Mauna Kea and Mauna Loa its larger neighboring mountains Hualalai appears to
have entered the late stage of the eruptive cycle Rocks of Hualalai Volcano cover a
portion of the western side of the island of Hawaii Most of its lavas are alkalic olivine
basalt and physically are composed of both Pahoehoe and A a flow types
Earthquake Conditions
The island of Hawaii is designated in the 1991 Uniform Building Code as
Seismic Zone 3 Seismic Zone 3 is considered to be an active zone of earthquake
occurrence Most of the earthquakes that occur on the island of Hawaii are related to
subsurface magma movement and are therefore termed volcanic earthquakes
The drywells were constructed in a relatively competent layered rock formation
with some rock fill The drywells generally consist of simple excavated pits lined with
perforated precast concrete rings and catch basin inlets at the ground surface A
typical cross section of a drywell is shown on Plate 3 Due to the below grade and
interlocking construction of the precast drywell rings seismic conditions are not
considered significant

Flood and Tsunami
Site grading to provide positive drainage of the site has been constructed In
addition the site is located inland of and at a higher elevation than the expected
tsunami inundation zone for this portion of the island of Hawaii Therefore it is
anticipated that tsunami flooding should not affect the operation of the drywells
Land Use
The site is situated below the Underground Injection Control Line for this portion
of the island of Hawaii and is in a suburban area zoned for mixed commercial and
residential development The project consists of approximately 0 9 miles of new
roadway and roadway extensions in support of the new commercial development in the
area Construction and use of drywells at the site is considered to be consistent with
this use
SYSTEM OPERATION
It is proposed to use the new drywells for the disposal of surface and storm
water runoff flowing from the paved roadways and graded areas of the site Use of the
drywells will be intermittent and will be dependent upon weather conditions and storm
water runoff
The influent will not be treated prior to disposal and the volume and rate of
injection will vary according to the intensity of rainfall The design maximum injection
rate is 6 cubic feet per second per drywell Injection will be under gravity pressure i e
the head pressure that builds up within the drywell Since the new drywells are
intended for intermittent disposal of storm water runoff only there is no additional
standby system nor are monitoring wells planned

GEOHYDROLOGIC CONSIDERATIONS
The site is located at Latitude North and Longitude West
The ground surface elevations at the site range from approximately 20 to about 70
feet MSL Subsurface conditions are basalt rock formation generally consisting of
interbedded clinker and massive to fractured rock Groundwater was not observed in
the drywell selected for observation during our field work
The regional characteristics of groundwater in the area is fresh to brackish water
in a basal volcanic aquifer of relatively high permeability Published data indicates that
the groundwater table slopes towards the ocean at a gradient of about one foot per
mile Regional discharge is generally along the coastline
The lithology of the logged drywell excavation is presented in Appendix A of this
report All depths reported on the log were measured from the existing ground surface
to the bottom of the drywel during consiruction

Based on observations of the selected drywell the entire depth of the drywell
was lined with precast perforated concrete rings with dimensions of six feet diameter
and four feet in height each Crushed rock and cobbles were used as backfill behind
the concrete rings and sidewalls During construction the excavated drywells ranged
in depth from about 12 to 27 feet below the existing ground surface The drywell
locations were based on area runoff calculations
INJECTION TESTING
The subsurface permeability condition at the site was tested by the injection of
clear fresh water from a water tank truck with a capacity of approximately 4 000
gallons Injection rates were measured by use of an in line paddle wheel flow sensor
During testing clean water was injected at rates of about 150 to 180 gallons per minute
for a period of about 20 minutes During the injection of water the variations of the
water level in the drywell was recorded with respect to time and the rate of injection
The test indicates that the basalt formation has an approximate coefficient of
permeability k of approximately 865 feet per day Groundwater was not observed in
the drywell prior to the beginning of the test The results of the injection test is
presented in Appendix B
