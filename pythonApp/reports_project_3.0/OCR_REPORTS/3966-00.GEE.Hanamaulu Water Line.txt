INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Hanamaulu Water Line project in the Lihue area on the island
of Kauai Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design of
foundations site grading and pavements only and are subject to the limitations noted at
the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located on Maalo Road near the intersection of Kuhio Highway
in the Lihue area on the island of Kauai Hawaii We understand that it is proposed to
construct a new 16 inch water line crossing at the existing Kapaia Bridge The new water
line will cross Hanamaulu Stream Bridge on the upstream side of Kapaia Bridge
Based on the available information the new water line will span approximately
62 feet across the Kapaia Bridge The water line will be supported on a concrete beam
with abutments on each side of the bridge We understand that the pipeline will have
both horizontal and vertical bends near the stream crossing Based on the information
provided the dead load reaction at each abutment will be about 65 kips The internal
pipe pressure of 150 pounds per square inch psi will generate vertical and horizontal
thrust forces of about 30 1 kips
We understand that the new water line will extend from Kuhio Highway up along
Maalo Road Our field exploration was only limited to the water line crossing at the

Hanamaulu Stream Therefore this report will cover geotechnical engineering
recommendation pertaining to the stream crossing only
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the subsurface
conditions at the site to develop a soil data set for the formulation of geotechnical
engineering recommendations pertinent to the proposed project In order to accomplish
this we conducted an exploration program consisting of the following tasks and work
efforts
1 Mobilization and demobilization of a truck mounted drill rig and
two operators to the project site from Honolulu and back Application for
drilling permits toning for utilities and installation of safety and traffic signs
prior to drilling works
2 Drilling and sampling of two boreholes to depths of approximately 73 and
78 feet below the existing ground surface using a truck mounted drilling rig
3 Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the borings by a
Field Engineer from our firm
4 Laboratory testing of selected samples obtained during the field exploration
as an aid in classifying the materials encountered and evaluating their
engineering properties
5 Analyses of the field and laboratory data for the formulation of geotechnical
engineering recommendations pertinent to the design of the bridge
foundation for the water line crossing
6 Preparation of this report summarizing our work on the project and
presenting our findings and recommendations
7 Coordination of our overall work on the project and client design team
consultation by a Principal Engineer of our firm
8 Miscellaneous work efforts such as drafting word processing and clerical
support

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of the lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene
more than 2 million years before present age Following the cessation of this main
shield building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic
lavas of the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of alluvial
sediments of the Palikea Formation
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic series cover most of the eastern half of the island of
Kauai Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic series are generally characterized as thick flows of
dense basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various
locales Associated with the vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low
cinder cones at the vent
During the Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to fill
the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose The land mass of Kauai remained essentially stable during these changes and the
fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands
of the sea which were both higher and lower relative to the present sea level on Kauai
The soils in the project area appear to consist of alluvial soils from the Hanamaulu
Stream and residual and saprolite soils derived from the in situ weathering of igneous
rocks In general residual and saprolite soils grade to weathered basalt formations with
depth

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located on Maalo Road approximately 200 feet north of the
intersection with Kuhio Highway in the Lihue area on the island of Kauai Hawaii
The existing Kapaia Bridge is a single span bridge supported on a shallow foundation
system with a span length of approximately 30 feet as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The Hanamaulu Stream has a narrow channel with very steep banks near the
Kapaia Bridge crossing The bottom of the stream channel is approximately 12 feet
below the existing ground surface At the time of our field exploration the Hanamaulu
Stream was flowing moderately to strongly in a west to east direction In general the
stream banks are vegetated with some wild grasses Where the soil is exposed along the
stream banks cobbles and boulders embedded in silty and clayey materials were
observed Cobbles and boulders as well as branches leaves and rubbish were also
visible at the bottom of the stream
The Maalo Road is a two lane roadway with narrow shoulders on both sides of the
road At the time of our field exploration traffic flow along this stretch of roadway was
light to moderate In general the surrounding areas at the western side of the road near
the proposed water line crossing are moderately to heavily vegetated The vegetation at
the site generally consists of wild grasses small shrubs and trees
Based on our field reconnaissance and a topographic map provided the majority
of Maalo Road at the stream crossing is relatively level The areas at the western side of
the road near the proposed water line crossing slope down toward the west at an
inclination of up to about one and one half horizontal to one vertical
The elevation at the project site ranges from approximately 86 feet Mean Sea Level
MSL at the Hanamaulu Stream channel bottom to about 98 feet MSL along Maalo
Road

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration at the project site consisted of drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths of about 78 and
73 feet below the existing ground surface respectively The approximate locations of the
borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our field exploration generally encountered medium stiff to stiff clayey silts and
silty clays to depths of about 41 to 46 below the existing ground surface The clayey silts
and silty clays were underlain by highly weathered to unweathered medium hard to hard
basalt formation to the maximum depth explored of approximately 73 to 78 feet below the
existing ground surface _
Groundwater was encountered in the borings at depths of about 26 to 28 feet
below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration However it should
be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to seasonal precipitation
run off and other factors

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the structural loads
provided we recommend that a deep foundation system consisting of a single 24 inch
diameter cast in place drilled shaft be used at each abutment location to support the
proposed waiter line crossing at the Hanamaulu Stream The drilled shafts should extend
a minimum of 30 feet below the bottom of footing to derive the necessary support for the
design loads

Drilled Shaft Foundation
We recommend that a deep foundation system consisting of a single 24 inch
diameter cast in place drilled shaft extending to a depth of 30 feet below the bottom
of footing elevation be used to support the proposed water line at each abutment
The cast in place drilled shafts would derive vertical support primarily from skin friction
The end bearing component of the drilled shafts has been discounted due to difficulties
associated with obtaining a clean bottom below the groundwater level during
construction
Our foundation analysis was based on a subsurface soil conditions encountered
c on the assumption that the soil in front of the water line abutment is protected from
SCOU
Based on our analysis an axial compression load capacity of 100 kips may be
used for the design of the new water line abutments The above axial compression load
capacity is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third for transient loads
such as those caused by wind or seismic forces A safety factor of 3 0 was used in
arriving at the allowable compression load capacity In addition these values are net
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3966 00 Page 8
allowable compression load capacities therefore the weight of the drilled shaft need not
be included in the structural calculations
Uplift loads imposed on the foundations may be resisted by a combination of the
dead weight of the drilled shaft and by shear along the shaft surface and the adjacent
surrounding soils For intermittent uplift forces an ultimate uplift capacity of 100 kips per
shaft may be used to resist uplift loads The structural capacity of the drilled shaft in
tension should be checked by the project Structural Engineer
The load bearing capacities of the drilled shafts will depend largely on the skin
friction between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Because local variations in
the subsurface conditions may be encountered at the site a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii should be present to confirm the subsurface conditions anticipated and
to observe the installation of the drilled shafts during construction In addition contract
documents should include provisions for extension of the drilled shaft during construction
if needed We also recommend that provisions be made for extending the reinforcing
steel cages for the drilled shafts to account for variations of the subsurface conditions
Lateral Load Resistance
In general lateral loads imposed on the foundations may be resisted by a
combination of the following 1 resistance of the drilled shaft soil system to deflect and
2 passive resistance of soils against the vertical faces of footings where applicable
Lateral load resistance of drilled shafts is a function of the stiffness of the surrounding soil
the stiffness of the shafts the allowable deflection at the top of the shaft and the induced
moment in the shafts
Based on the subsoil conditions encountered we have evaluated the lateral load
resistance of the shafts For the purpose of our analyses we have assumed that a single
24 inch diameter drilled shaft will be used at each abutment locations with no

Foundation Settlements
Settlement of the drilled shaft foundation will result from elastic compression of the
7 shaft and subgrade response of the foundation embedded in the basalt rock Total drilled
shaft settlements are estimated to be on the order of less than 1 inch with differential
settlements between the abutments estimated to be less than 0 5 inches We believe a
significant portion of the settlement is elastic and should occur as the loads are applied
Post construction foundation settlements should be approximately 0 5 inches or less
Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations
In general the performance of drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the
contractor s method of installation and construction procedures The load bearing
capacities of drilled shafts depend on the friction resistance between the shaft and the
surrounding soil Therefore the Contractor should exercise care in drilling the shaft holes
and in placing concrete into the holes

Boulders and cobbles were observed along the stream banks and channel
bottoms during our field exploration Therefore possible difficult drilling conditions may
be encountered In addition due to the presence of groundwater and medium stiff soils
encountered cave in of the material may occur during the drilling operation To reduce
the potential for caving in of the holes temporary casing may be needed Due to the
close proximity of the project to the Hanamaulu Stream seasonal precipitation run off
and other factors may influence the groundwater levels Therefore groundwater levels
may also pose further construction difficulties since proper observation of the sides and
bottom of the drilled shafts would be difficult Therefore a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii should be present at the site to observe the drilling and installation of
drilled shafts during construction Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids is not
recommended because use of these fluids may reduce the friction between the concrete
shaft and surrounding soils
A low shrink high slump 7 to 9 inch slump range concrete mix should be used to
provide close contact between the drilled shafts surrounding soils and basalt formation
The concrete should be placed promptly after the completion of drilling within 24 hours
to reduce the potential for caving in The concrete should also be placed in a suitable
manner to reduce segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix
Since groundwater will likely be encountered in the drilled holes placement of concrete
by tremie methods is recommended During concrete placement a minimum of 10 feet of
concrete head should be maintained above the bottom of the tremie pipe at all times
Thrust Blocks and Earth Pressure on Pipes
We envision that thrust blocks will be required at some locations to provide
resistance to the thrust forces due to the pressurized pipe The thrust force acting on the
pipe may be resisted by passive earth pressure acting against the face of the thrust block
Resistance due to passive pressure may be calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure
of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f for thrust blocks embedded in the
in situ soil To estimate the frictional resistance between the thrust block and surrounding
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3966 00 Page 11
soils a coefficient of friction of 0 8 may be used These values assume that the soil
around the block will be well compacted and the top of the thrust block will be embedded
a minimum of 3 feet below the ground surface
Loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the trench the size of the
pipes the unit weight of the backfill material and the frictional resistance between the
backfill material and the trench walls For a preliminary estimate of the vertical loads on
the buried water pipe an average unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic foot p c f for the
backfill material and a coefficient of friction of 0 3 between the backfill and trench
sidewalls may be used Earth pressure forces upon the pipe increase rapidly with
increased width of the trench Therefore the width of the water line trench should be kept
to a minimum Traffic loads on the buried pipe should also be considered where the
proposed water line alignment is located within traveled ways

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to reduce the
potential for contamination of the excavated materials If soft or wet soils are
encountered during clearing and grubbing over excavation should be required to
expose firm soils The over excavation should be backfilled properly with structural
fill
After clearing and grubbing the exposed subgrade should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 8 inches properly moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
Fills and Backfills
The on site materials may be reused as a source of fill and backfill materials
provided that they are free of organic materials and contain no lumps or fragments
larger than 3 inches in the largest dimension Cobbles and boulders if
encountered should not be used as fill or backfill materials
Granular fill materials to be used as structural fill should consist of crushed coral
mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded from coarse
to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension In addition the
fill material should contain a maximum of 20 percent particles passing the No 200
sieve

We understand that imported fill material will be needed for the project
The imported fill material should conform to the above mentioned requirement and

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by
dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Excavations
In order to install the new water line excavations on the order of about 4 to 8 feet
relative to the existing ground surface may be required Generally it is the
responsibility of the Contractor to provide a safe and stable excavation for the
construction workers in accordance with applicable State and Federal regulations
Generally medium stiff clays and silts with gravel were encountered near the
existing ground surface during our field exploration We anticipate that
conventional excavation techniques may be used for the proposed excavation
However boulders were observed along the stream bank walls and channel
bottoms at the time of our field exploration Therefore excavation in localized hard
areas may require the use of hoerams or chipping

No 3B Fine gravel is recommended under the new water pipe Free draining granular

The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings to depths of approximately 73 and 78 feet below the existing ground surface
The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous
flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Spilt Barre Sampling of Soils
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the logs of borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of
the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
Two unconsolidated undrained triaxial compression tests TXUU ASTM D 2850
were performed on selected samples to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the
cohesive soils The approximate in situ effective overburden pressure was used as the
applied confining pressure Results of the tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings
at the appropriate sample depths The test results and stress strain curves are presented
on Plates B 2 and B 3
One consolidation test ASTM D 2435 was performed on a selected sample of the
soils to evaluate compressibility characteristics of the on site soils Results of the
consolidation test are presented on Plate B 4
