INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Waterline Improvements from Kaohe Subdivision to Kaukaiau Subdivision
in the Paauilo area on the Island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site
are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the proposed
project These recommendations are intended for the design of the waterline improvements
project and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Waterline Improvements from Kaohe Subdivision to Kaukaiau Subdivision is
located in the Paauilo area of the District of Hamakua on the Island of Hawaii as shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1 Based on the information provided it is proposed to install
approximately 3 000 lineal feet of 6 inch diameter waterlines along the shoulder area of the
Hawaii Belt Road in the Paauilo area In general we anticipate the waterline inverts to be up
to about 5 feet below the existing ground surface In addition the new waterline will traverse
Kawailii Gulch which is spanned by a vehicular bridge Based on the information provided
we understand that the waterline will be fastened to the existing bridge Therefore a new
bridge crossing or other means of crossing the gulch will not be required as part of this project

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago with an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes Kohala which has long been extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity during
recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both
of which are still active
GEOLABS INC
W O 4476 00 Page 4
The project site is located on the northern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield Volcano
which has been built up by successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and pyroclastic
materials These flows are believed to be late Pleistocene in age and belong to the Hamakua
Volcanic Series
The lava formation at the site appears to include both a a and pahoehoe flows A a
flows are characterized as dense blocky lava with a rough clinker surface while pahoehoe
flows are characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of
vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lava flows These
cavities are formed when the lava is still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes
intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes are formed when
molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend
for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Blisters and pockets inter flow cavities are
generally smaller in horizontal extent
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The proposed Waterline Improvements project from Kaohe Subdivision to Kaukaiau
Subdivision is located along Hawaii Belt Road in the Paauilo area on the Island of Hawaii The
waterline project spans between the Aamanu and Kainehe Gulches as shown on the Site
Plans Plates 3 1 through 3 3
A large portion of the waterline project will be located in the shoulder area of the Hawaii
Belt Road north side of the road Based on our site reconnaissance Hawaii Belt Road is a
two lane highway with paved and unpaved shoulders It should be noted that overhead power
and communication lines were observed on both sides of the roadway The shoulder areas
on both sides of the roadway consisted of both paved asphaltic concrete and grass
Based on our site observations of Kawailii Bridge the existing bridge is a concrete
structure of about 40 feet long and about 25 feet wide The banks of the gulch near Kawailii
Bridge were moderately vegetated with wild grasses and shrubs At the time of our field
exploration Kawailii Gulch was generally dry

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling a total of five borings
two borings at the Kawailii Gulch crossing and three borings along the waterline alignment
extending to depths ranging from about 6 3 to 26 feet below the existing ground surface The
locations of the drilled borings are shown on the General Site Plan Plate 2 Enlarged site
plans showing the approximate boring locations are provided on Plates 3 1 through 3 3
Our borings along the waterline alignment generally encountered 1 to 2 inches of
asphaltic concrete pavement The asphaltic concrete pavement was underlain by fill materials
consisting of dense silty basalt gravel and sand extending to depths of about 2 to 4 5 feet
below the existing ground surface The fill materials were underlain by volcanic ash materials
consisting of stiff clayey silts extending to the maximum depths explored of about 6 3 to 8 feet
below the existing ground surface
Our borings at the gulch crossing Kawailii Gulch generally encountered a 1 inch thick
layer of asphaltic concrete pavement The asphaltic concrete was underlain by fill materials
consisting of silty basalt gravel with some sand extending to depths of about 1 to 2 feet below
the existing ground surface The fill materials were underlain by volcanic ash consisting of stiff
clayey silts extending to depths of about 5 to 8 feet below the existing ground surface The
volcanic ash was underlain by alluvial soil deposits consisting of silty sands and gravel
extending to depths of about 13 5 to 15 feet below the existing ground surface The alluvial
soil deposits were underlain by medium hard to hard basalt interbedded with clinker materials
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 26 feet below the existing ground surface
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings at the time of our field exploration
However it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to seasonal
precipitation storm surge conditions and other factors

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our borings the project site is generally underlain by a relatively thin layer of
asphaltic concrete pavement underlain by dense gravelly fill materials The fill materials were
underlain by volcanic ash materials consisting of clayey silts extending to the depths of about
8 feet below the existing ground surface Based on the deeper borings drilled near Kawailii
Gulch the fill and volcanic ash materials were underlain by alluvial soil deposits and basalt
formation extending to the maximum depth explored of about 26 feet below the existing
ground surface
Based on the proposed waterline alignment the new waterline will traverse Kawailii
Gulch Based on our site reconnaissance the gulch was heavily vegetated and the bottom
of the gulch appeared to consist of basalt formation In general the methods that may be
used to install pipelines across an existing stream and or gulch include fastening the pipeline
to the existing bridge traversing the gulch construction of a separate waterline bridge
crossing conventional cut and cover methods and trenchless methods such as horizontal
directional drilling and or microtunneling Because of the relatively small diameter of the new
waterline we understand that the new waterline will be fastened to the existing Kawailii
Bridge in order to traverse the existing gulch
We understand that construction of the remainder of the waterline project will generally
consist of trench excavation pipe placement and trench backfill Detailed discussions of the
geotechnical aspects of the project and our recommendations for design of the waterlines are
presented in the following sections of this report

Trench Excavation
Based on the information provided we understand that the inverts of the proposed
6 inch waterline will range from about 3 5 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface As
previously mentioned dense silty gravel fills and stiff clayey silts volcanic ash were
encountered below the asphaltic concrete pavement within the depths of the proposed new
waterline Therefore we envision that most of the trenches for the new waterlines will likely
be excavated in the near surface soil materials encountered in the borings However it should
be noted that Boring No 1 encountered highly to extremely weathered basalt formation at a
depth of about 5 5 feet below the existing ground surface Therefore some hard excavation
in localized areas should be anticipated for the project and may require the use of hoerams
or chipping
In general the method and equipment to be used for trench excavation should be
determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations The
excavated soils from the trench excavation should be stockpiled no closer than a horizontal
distance equal to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the
potential for appreciable ground movement The trench excavation should comply with all
applicable local state and federal safety requirements
Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the load
supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the pipe bedding is an important design
consideration In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 1 inch minus well graded
granular material aggregate base course is recommended under the pipes The granular
bedding should extend beyond the sides of the pipe by a minimum width of one fourth the
outside diameter of the pipe Before the placement of bedding material the excavated trench
bottom should be observed by a representative from Geolabs to confirm whether firm
materials are exposed at the bottom of the trench or if the bedding material should be
extended deeper
GEOLASBS INC
W O 4476 00 Page 8
Trench Backfill
The first zone of backfill initial trench backfill extending from the bedding material to
at least 12 inches above the top of the pipes should consist of 1 inch minus well graded
granular materials aggregate base course The initial trench backfill should be moisture
conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to at least 90 percent
relative compaction Itis critical that the initial trench backfill be properly placed and
compacted to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to
provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill around the pipes
could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes Compaction of the initial
trench backfill by water ponding or jetting should not be allowed
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
about 2 feet below the pavement subgrade should consist of granular materials generally less
than 6 inches in maximum particle size The backfill material should be moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture content placed in relatively thin lifts normally 10 to 12 inches
in level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction to reduce the potential for significant future ground subsidence Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding
to the maximum dry density
It should be noted that some of the volcanic ash materials encountered in the borings
have very high in situ moisture contents in excess of 100 percent Therefore excavation and
subsequent re use of these high moisture soils would be limited because compaction of these
materials to the specified compaction requirements will be difficult Therefore consideration
should be given to importing the trench backfill materials for backfilling of the waterline
trenches
GEOLABS INC
W O 4476 00 Page 9
The trench backfill from about 2 feet below the pavement subgrade elevation up to the
subgrade elevation should consist of select borrow subbase material 3 inch minus granular
material compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction Since the waterline
trench will be within paved areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement
grade should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction
Thrust Blocks
We envision that thrust blocks will be required at some locations to provide resistance
to the thrust forces due to the bends in the pressurized pipes The thrust force acting on the
pipes may be resisted by passive earth pressure acting against the face of the thrust block
Based on the available information the planned inverts are anticipated at about 3 5 to 5 feet
below the existing ground surface Based on our field exploration granular fills and stiff clayey
silts were encountered at shallow depths Therefore we recommend that a lateral bearing
pressure of 2 000 pounds per square foot psf be used for the design of thrust blocks
Settlements
Primary settlement of the new waterline construction is normally caused by the
difference in the unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted
backfill material placed over the pipes The net increase in loading will cause some minor
settlement of the underlying subsoils below the trench invert Based on our calculations
primary settlement of less than 0 5 inches is anticipated for the project The above estimate
assumes that proper construction procedures and good workmanship will be engaged during
construction
Pavement Restoration
It is anticipated that asphaltic concrete pavements will be used to repave the waterline
trenches along the waterline alignment As previously mentioned select borrow subbase
material should be placed in the trench backfill within the upper 2 feet below the pavement
subgrade The select borrow subbase material should be placed in 8 inch level loose lifts
moisture conditioned and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction In general
the new pavements along the waterline trenches should consist of a minimum of 2 inches of

asphaltic concrete or they should match the thickness of the existing pavement sections In

match the thickness of the existing aggregate base course
Corrosion Tests
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics with
regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects In general the lower the
resistivity the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure Conversely
the higher the resistivity the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of metallic objects
Two pH tests and two minimum resistivity tests using the Soil Box Method were
performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration Based on the
minimum resistivity values of the subsurface materials at the site greater than 5 000 ohm cm
the subsurface materials at the site may be classified as moderately corrosive to buried
metallic structures

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions along the waterline alignment were explored by drilling and
sampling five borings extending to depths ranging from about 6 3 to 26 feet below the existing
ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plans Plates
3 1 through 3 3 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight augers and rotary coring equipment
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials encountered are
presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 5
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches in addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the
field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive
strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the Logs
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of basalt rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method
that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative quality
of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from a coring
attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material is recovered
from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs
of Borings as REC 74

of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced fractures or
breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would be
50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the
following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the Practical Handbook
of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of the
soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The results of the
test are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
Two pH tests ASTM G 51 and two minimum resistivity tests using the Soil Box
Method ASTM G 57 78 were performed on selected soil samples retrieved from our field
exploration program to determine the pH and minimum resistivity values of the soils The
results of the tests are presented on Plate B 2
