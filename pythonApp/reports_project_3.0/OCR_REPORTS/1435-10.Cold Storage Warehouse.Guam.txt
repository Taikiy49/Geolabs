INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our additional soils
investigation performed for the proposed Cold Storage Warehouse Addition
P 110 at the U S Naval Supply Depot Guam Mariana Islands The
general location of the site and its vicinity are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
Our previous investigation disclosed that the site is underlain by
compressible lagoonal deposits Also located about 72 feet below the
ground surface at the central portion of the site a foot cavity
was encountered
W O 1435 10 Page 2
In view of the anticipated large settlement due to heavy structural
loads deep pile foundation was recommended In order to achieve a high
design capacity piles would require either a large embedment to generate
sufficient skin resistance or in some areas especially those where
cavities were encountered a hard bearing stratum
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of this investigation was to provide additional sub
surface information within the development site for the formulation of
recommendations for the deep pile foundation design of the proposed
construction Particular attention would be directed to locate the hard
coral stratum for end bearing piles Also attempts would be made to
provide additional information about the cavity which was encountered
during our previous exploration
The scope of work included drilling and sampling three 3 borings
each to a depth of 150 feet below the surface laboratory testings
engineering evaluation of the field and laboratory findings and report
preparation
Detailed description of our field exploration laboratory testing
and boring logs are presented in the appendices The approximate
locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2

This report summarizes our findings and recommendations
PROJECT CONSIDERATION
It is proposed to construct a one story 25 640 square foot
reinforced concrete addition to the existing cold storage warehouse
It was originally planned to construct the new cold storage warehouse
adjacent to the existing warehouse However due to the anticipated
ground settlement caused by the new fill and the resulting effect on the
existing warehouse it was decided to separate the new and existing
structures The general layout of the project is shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2
The design live load over the warehouse floor will be 450 p s f
with small forklift truck operations The cold storage floor slab system
will consist of a 6 inch concrete slab over 4 inches of insulation on top
of a 10 inch thick reinforced concrete structural slab which will be
supported by piles The finished flcor grade is planned at elevation
12 0 feet
A truck apron area consisting of concrete pavement with asphalt
concrete pavement transition areas will be constructed to the east of
the warehouse addition for truck loading
Site grading involving small cut and fill along with instal
lation of supporting utilities will be required for the concrete truck
apron areas

SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is near the eastern shore of the Inner Apra
Harbor Islands
The site is located on a relatively flat asphalt concrete paved
area at the northern end of the existing cold storage warehouse
building The general ground surface ranged from about elevation 11 5
feet along the wharf at the western boundary to about elevation 8 5 feet
near the drain inlet catch basin located at the northeastern corner of
the existing cold storage building
The site drainage generally runs from the higher ground at the
western end towards the eastern portion of the site
SUBSURFACE SOIL CONDITION
In general the soil conditions at the upper 80 feet of the site
was found to be similar to those disclosed during our previous
investigation On this investigation the site was found to be underlain
by a 6 to 10 foot fill layer consisting of dense to medium dense coral
sand and gravel overlying soft and loose compressible lagoonal clay and
lcose sand deposits of various thickness ranging frem about 6 feet at
Boring 6 to about 26 feet at Boring 8
The compressible lagoonal deposits were in turn underlain by medium
to very dense coral sand and gravel deposits to a depth of about 120 to
125 feet where highly weathered coral formation was encountered

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATTONS
General
The three additional borings drilled confirmed the presence of soft
or loose lagoonal deposits below the site As discussed in our previous
report due to the relatively high column and floor loads anticipated
pile foundations are reconmended for the proposed building and slab
Fills of about 12 to 18 inches high may be required on the east end
of the new building Estimated settlements due to the weight of the new
fill are on the order of one to two inch The ground settlements would
create a downdrag force on the new piles thereby reducing the net
allowable capacity In order to minimize the downdrag force we
recommend that the fill area be surcharged prior to installation of the
pile foundations Details of the surcharge fill are covered under a
subsequent section
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1435 10 Page 6
Below the loose lagoonal deposits the borings encountered dense
coral sand over a weathered coral formation at about 120 feet depth
Generally the dense sand or coral formation could provide suitable
bearing for the pile foundation
Cavity
In our previous exploration a cavity was encountered in the dense
coral sand layer in Boring 5 which was drilled near the center of the
proposed building site The cavity was encountered from a depth of 71 5
feet to 78 feet Boring 7 was drilled about 10 feet away from the
previous Boring 5 to evaluate the extent of the cavity A cavity was
encountered in this new boring from 76 to 88 feet depth
Cavities are formed in coral formation or cemented sand due to the
presence of fresh water streams during the growth stages of the coral
which prevent the growth of the coral or can disolve the cementing agents
in cemented sands Therefore it is likely that the cavity may be part
of our old stream and therefore may extend horizontally through the
site rather than being an isolated occurance
The presence of the cavity presents significant problems to the
pile foundation Piles which are stopped above the cavity could be
affected by future collapse of the cavity Therefore we recommend that
all the piles extend down to a depth below the bottom of the cavity
GEOLABS HAWAI
W Page 7
However our borings encountered the presence of very dense
partially cemented coral sand above the cavities therefore very hard
driving and possibly pre drilling may be required to drive the piles to
the bottom of the cavity Another concern would be the possibility of
damage to the piles while driving through the cavity If a pile is
subjected to the full impact of the pile driving hammer while penetrating
the cavity excessive tensile stresses could develop near the pile tip
resulting in a broken pile This problem can be minimized by reducing
the energy of the hammer when driving through the cavity
Pile Foundation
It is obvious from the above discussion that the presence of the
cavity has a significant effect on the pile foundations Two systems of
piles are needed one for areas where the cavity is not encountered and
one for areas where the cavity is encountered Since the extent of the
cavity will be unknown until the piles are driven the pile design must
be flexible to acconmodate both conditions
Based on Borings 5 and 7 it appears that if present the cavity
should be encountered from depths of 70 to 90 feet Therefore if a pile
is driven down to a depth of 90 feet and does not encounter the cavity
it can be assumed that the cavity is not present at that location For a
pile driven to a depth of 90 feet below the existing ground surface into
the dense coral sand an allowable pile capacity of 100 tons may be used
GEOLABS HAWAII
W Page 8
Where a cavity is encountered the pile should be driven to a depth
of at least 120 feet to weathered coral formation or 35 feet below the
bottom of the cavity whichever is less to obtain a 100 ton capacity
Therefore a suitable design approach would be to specify 90 foot
long piles one section at each location If the pile is driven down to
90 feet and a cavity is not encountered a design capacity of 100 tons
may be used If a cavity is encountered near the bottom of the 90 foot
section a 35 foot long section should be spliced to the first pile and
ariven to the above criteria to maintain the 100 ton capacity
Sixteen and one half inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete
piles are recommended
Pile Driving
Based on the results of our wave equation analysis as shown on
Plates C 1 through C 4 the piles should be driven with a hanmer
with maximum rated energy of at least 27 000 foot pounds
The recommended pile driving criteria are as follows
1 The driving process should be continuous without interruption
for each pile A pile may be rejected or its design capacity
modified when the driving process is interrupted for more than
4 hours
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1435 10 Page 9
2 In those areas where cavities are encountered the piles
should be embedded at least 120 feet below the ground surface
or 35 feet below the bottom of the cavity whichever is less
Where no cavity is encountered the required embedment is 90
feet
3 The pile driving may be terminated when the blow counts are
steadily increasing with depth and reach 15 blows per inch
for each of the last 6 inches or aggregated blow counts of
180 blows for the last foot
Criteria 3 is based on the use of a diesal driving hammer
with maximum rated energy of 27 500 foot pounds to drive a
16 5 inch octagonal concrete pile 90 feet long with a net
design load of 100 tons This criteria should be modified
according to the actual rated energy of the hammer used
Test Pile Program
A test pile program should be undertaken to fulfill the following
main objectives
1 To examine the drivability of piles with the use of one or a
few selected hammers
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1435 10 Page 10
2 to help to delineate the area and maximum depth from ground
surface where cavities are likely to be encountered
3 to determine if a driving shoe is required
4 to determine if pre drilling is necessary in the cavity or
other areas within the construction site
5 to study the damage to the pile when it is driven at full
thrust of the hanmer from very dense layer into the cavity
6 to help evaluate and modify if necessary the driving
criteria
To achieve these objectives we recommend that at least ten 10
piles be test driven around the central part of the site where
cavities were encountered during our exploration Five more piles
should be test driven into the soil at various parts of the
construction site One of these five should be at close vicinity
to Boring 4 where the thickest layer of lagoonal deposit was
encountered These test piles can be incorporated into actual
foundation later
Pile segments of 90 feet long should be used At least four 35
feet long sections and four splice cans should be provided at the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1435 10 Page 11
site during the test driving period Splice cans may be fabricated
with 1 4 inch thick high grade steel and should be at least 4 feet
long
As a part of the test pile program two pile load tests one at the
cavity area and the other at any remaining part of the site are
recommended The two test piles should be load tested to 200 tons
in accordance with the ASTM D 1143 test procedure
The test piles should be driven with the same hammer which is to be
used for production pile driving The test pile program should be
conducted under close and continuous observation of the soils
engineer who will determine when piles may be stopped and to
provide guidance should additional measures be required due to
unforeseen driving conditions He should evaluate the final load
carrying pile capacity and production pile embedment lengths based
on the results of the test pile driving and the pile load tests
Spread Footing Foundation
Equipment pads and footings to support a canopy are planned
The actual loads on these pads have not been determined yet but are
anticipated to be light An allowable bearing value of 2 000 p s f may
be used for these foundations bearing on the near surface coral fill
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1435 10 Page 12
The amount of settlement experienced by the footings and pads would
depend on the actual location since the thickness of the soft subsoil
varies The estimated settlement for a 10 kip load would be on the order
of less than one inch Refinements to the above settlement estimate may
be made when details of the structures are available
Loading Dock Wall Retaining Wall
A short retaining wall generally less than 3 feet high is planned
along the northern side of the building to provide the necessary grade
separation for use as a loading dock
The wall should be supported on pile foundations as specified in a
previous section
Well graded granular material such as base course rock sand or
equivalent should be used as backfill material directly behind the
walls This would minimize the compactive effort required for the
backfill and reduce the build up of additional lateral pressures on the
walls
We reconmend that the walls should be designed to resist lateral
earth pressures as follows
Unrestrained at top equivalent
active condition fluid pressure
Restrained at top equivalent
at rest condition fluid pressure

Additional loads due to surcharge or traffic
should be included where applicable
A subsurface drainage system should be used to prevent the build
up of hydrostatic pressure behind the walls
Pavements
It is our understanding that a concrete truck apron is planned in
front of the loading dock area to the east of the cold storage warehouse
addition To provide sufficient dock height and to allow for new truck
apron pavement section up to 3 feet of excavation below the existing
grade will be required Asphalt concrete pavements are planned for
transition zones on both northern and southern sides of the truck apron
area
The traffic loading is not well defined at this time For design
purpose we have selected a medium highway traffic load with Design Index
of 3 according to NAVFAC Design Manual 5 4 This traffic load consists
of 21 to 75 daily repetition of 18 kip equivalent axle load
Based on our borings the existing upper fill is coral sand and
gravel which can generally previde good subgrade support For the new
pavement construction it is recommended that the existing fill be
overexcavated down to at least 18 inches below the finished grade of the
new pavement The top 6 inches of soil below the overexcavated bottom
should be recompacted to a minimum of 95 relative campaction per the
ASTM D 1557 78 maximum density

Surcharge Program
With the current proposed structure offset by 30 feet from the
existing cold storage facility we believe that a surcharge program can
be used to accelerate the ground settlement at the site caused by
additional fill
In order to minimize the amount of disturbance to the pile
foundation of the existing cold storage facility the surcharge thickness
should not exceed 3 feet and should be placed at least 30 feet from the
existing cold storage building
With the use of 3 feet of surcharge the estimated time pericd for
the completion of ground settlement due to the additional fill is
approximately one 1 month
Periodic optical survey readings to monitor settlement should be
taken by the project surveyor and transmitted to the soils engineer to
determine the actual surcharge time required

Wave Equation Analysis
a A A EE Om a a A CN
The wave equation analysis for pile driving is based on using a
finite element method with input of the stress wave generated during the
impact of the hammer on to the pile
The primary objectives of the analysis are
1 To analyze the driving stresses in the pile
2 to determine the suitable driving system to be used for
the pile driving and
3 to predict the ultimate dynamic capacity of the pile
The wave equation analyses for this investigation were
performed by utilizing the computer prooram WEAPG
Two cases were analyzed for this project they included
1 Single pile segment 90 feet long
2 two pile segments 30 and 90 feet each with a splice can
installed at 30 feet from ground surface
Input data are shown on Plates C and C 3 along with bearing
graphs for different hammers Plates C 2 and C 4 show the relationship
of blow counts and driving stresses
A typical computer print out is presented on Plates C 5 through
C 11
