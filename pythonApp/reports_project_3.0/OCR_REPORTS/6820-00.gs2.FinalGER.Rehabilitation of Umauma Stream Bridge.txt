Introduction 
This geotechnical engineering report presents the results of our analyses and 
geotechnical recommendations for the Value Engineering Cost Proposal, Rehabilitation 
of Umauma Stream Bridge project in the North Hilo area on the Island of Hawaii. The 
project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our

and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of

1.2 
Project Considerations 
The bridge rehabilitation project is along Hawaii Belt Road at the Umauma 
Stream crossing north of Hilo on the Island of Hawaii.  A Value Engineering Cost 
Proposal (VECP) which includes redesign of the pier and abutment structures, and the 
installation of a temporary bypass road upstream of the existing bridge structure is 
proposed.  
The existing bridge structure was initially constructed in 1911 with two abutments 
and five bent structures.  Subsequent to bridge construction, the bridge was widened in 
1949.  The bridge is about 35 feet wide and 280 feet long, and consists of one traffic 
lane in each direction. 
The existing bridge structure will be rehabilitated with the installation of two new 
pier column structures located between existing Bent Nos. 1 and 2 and between 
existing Bent Nos. 3 and 4.  We understand that existing Bent No. 5 will not carry 
structural loading.  At the existing abutments, geotextile fabric reinforced walls will be 
constructed behind the abutments.  In addition, new wingwalls will be constructed.  The 
new bridge foundations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
Hawaii, the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago, covers an area of 
approximately 4,000 square miles. This island was formed by the activity of the 
following five shield volcanoes:  Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea (activity during recent 
geologic time), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801), and Mauna Loa and Kilauea, (both still 
active). 
The project site is located on the eastern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield Volcano, 
which had been built up by successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and 
pyroclastic materials. The most recent lava flows that comprise the ground surface and 
shallow subsurface at the project site are believed to be Pliocene to Pleistocene in age 
(approximately 1.2 million years to 2 million years before present time) and belong to 
the upper member of the Hamakua Volcanic Series. 
The lava flows were subsequently covered by volcanic ash deposits, locally 
referred to as Pahala Ash. The Pahala Ash is an air-laid deposit of volcanic ash from 
the Hamakua Volcanic Series of Mauna Loa Mountain during the Pleistocene Epoch. 
The Pahala Ash in certain locations has high in-situ moisture contents and low in-situ 
densities. It generally has low shear strength, and when the moisture content is high 
enough, it becomes thixotrophic, i.e. it loses strength when remolded. Therefore, this 
type of Pahala Ash may be potentially liquefiable during seismic events.   
The lava formation appears to be of pahoehoe flow that is characterized by a 
smooth, rope-like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular (porous) rock. 
Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lavas. Cavities are formed when the 
lava is still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes (intra-flow cavities) and 
inter-flow cavities (blisters and pockets). Lava tubes are formed when molten lava 
drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube-like structure that extend for a large 
longitudinal distance along the flow. Inter-flow cavities are generally smaller in 
horizontal extent.

2.2 
Site Description 
The bridge rehabilitation project site is north of Hilo on the Island of Hawaii.  The 
project is located on Hawaii Belt Road near Mile Post 16.02 at the Umauma Stream 
crossing. 
Umauma Stream is about 120 feet below the existing bridge deck.  The side 
slopes of the stream are steep with inclination ranging from about one horizontal to 
one and two-tenths vertical (1H:1.2V) to near vertical in some areas.  Based on the 
topographic plan, the existing ground elevations at the bridge alignment range from 
about +66 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) at the stream level up to about +186 feet MSL at 
the bridge deck. 
The slopes are heavily vegetated with brush and trees.  Large boulders are 
visible along the stream banks.  Water was observed flowing in the stream during our 
site visits.

3.1.1 Bridge Foundation 
Based on the available boring information, we anticipate the footing at Abutment 
No. 1 will bear on a thin layer of fill underlain by completely weathered rock.  For 
Abutment No. 2, completely weathered rock is anticipated at the footing level. 
For the abutment structures, bearing pressures of up to 6,000 and 13,000 pounds 
per square foot (psf) may be used for sizing footings bearing on the medium stiff 
and medium dense fill and completely weathered rock for the Strength Limit State 
and Extreme Event Limit State, respectively. For the Service Limit State, an 
allowable bearing pressure of up to 4,000 psf may be used to check the 
foundations for settlement considerations.   
Based on the available borings, we anticipate the footings at Pier Nos. 1 and 2 will 
bear on hard basalt rock formation.  For the pier structures, bearing pressures of up 
to 13,000 and 30,000 psf may be used for footings bearing on the hard basalt rock 
formation for the Strength Limit State and Extreme Event Limit States, respectively.  
For the Service Limit State, a bearing pressure of up to 10,000 psf may be used. 
Pier footings should be poured neat and embedded a minimum of 1 foot into the 
hard basalt rock formation. In addition, the footings should maintain a 5-foot 
horizontal setback distance from the outside edge of the footing to the face of a 
slope.  To resist sliding, friction factors of 0.45 and 0.53 for footings bearing on the 
completely weathered rock may be used for the Strength Limit State and Extreme 
Event Limit State, respectively.  For footings bearing on the hard basalt rock 
formation, friction factors of 0.60 and 0.70 may be used for the Strength Limit State 
and Extreme Event Limit State, respectively.

abutment and pier structures, respectively, we estimate total settlements for 
footings bearing on the completely weathered rock and hard basalt formation to be

3.1.2 Foundation Probing 
Based on our experience in the area and the field exploration, cavities and/or voids 
commonly exist in the basalt rock formation. To reduce the potential for loss of 
foundation support resulting from the collapse of cavities below a footing, we 
recommend implementing a cavity probing and grouting program at the pier 
foundation locations for the bridge structures during construction. 
We recommend drilling a probe hole for every 100 square feet of foundation area 
for the pier footings. The probe holes should be at least 2 inches in diameter and 
should extend to depths of at least 10 feet below the bottom of footing elevation. If 
cavities and/or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operation, 
additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacing to aid in delineating the

3.1.3 Lateral Load Resistance of Abutment Reinforced Fills 
The abutment structure may be considered as a large footing bearing on the 
abutment reinforced fill soils for resistance of lateral loads during a seismic event in 
the longitudinal direction. For lateral loads imposed on the abutments, the stiffness 
of the abutment reinforced fill soils will generally be mobilized prior to the lateral 
resistance of its footings. 
Provided that intimate contact exists between the backfill soil and the abutments or 
back wall, a soil stiffness spring equal to approximately 4 ksf per inch of deflection 
may be used in resisting lateral loads. To reduce the potential for shear failure in 
the abutment fill soils, the lateral deflection should be limited to 1.25 inches or less. 
Therefore, the maximum lateral load resistance of the abutment structure or back 
wall should be limited to 5 kips per square foot of abutment or back wall face. The 
structural engineer should check the structure displacement required in order to 
engage the abutment fill soils for lateral load resistance. 
Please note that the stiffness of the abutment fill soils only applies to forces that 
cause the abutment wall to move into the backfill soil (longitudinal direction normal 
to the abutment wall face). Therefore, only the lateral load resistance of the 
abutment foundation and wing walls may be utilized to resist the lateral loads in the 
transverse direction.

3.3 
Geotextile Fabric Retaining Wall System 
Based on the site conditions, we believe constructing a geotextile fabric retaining 
wall behind the existing abutment structures would be beneficial. The geotextile fabric 
retaining wall will reduce the loading to the existing abutment structures.  The geotextile 
fabric retaining wall system is a composite wall system, which utilizes woven geotextile 
fabric to reinforce the backfill zone and to improve the shear strength of the reinforced 
soil zone.  This composite system essentially forms a gravity wall structure with an 
ability to carry appreciable structural loads.  In addition, geotextile fabric retaining walls 
are desirable due to the flexibility of the wall, ease of construction, high load carrying 
capacity, and economy. 
The vertical spacing of the geotextile fabric should be about 12 inches.  Since the 
front face of the wall will be battered outwards up to 14 degrees with the vertical, the 
front face of the geotextile fabric should be wrapped at least 10 feet from the face of the 
wall.  We understand that the wall height is about 16 feet.  Based on our analysis, the 
reinforced zone of the geotextile fabric retaining wall should extend laterally behind the

3.4 
Shoring Micropiles 
The footing for Pier No. 2 will be constructed adjacent and below the existing 
footings for Bent No. 4.  Prior to footing excavation for Pier No. 2, we recommend that 
the existing Bent No. 4 footing be shored with a micropile foundation system. 
In general, a micropile foundation system consists of a small diameter (usually 
less than 12 inches), drilled and grouted, pile with steel reinforcing.  The micropile 
foundation typically is constructed by drilling a borehole, placing reinforcing steel in the 
hole, and grouting the borehole.  Temporary steel casing may be used.  Micropiles are 
desirable because they can be installed readily in access restrictive environments and 
in numerous soil types and ground conditions.  In addition, installation of the micropiles 
generally causes minimal disturbance to adjacent structures, the adjacent soils, and the 
environment.   
We believe that a micropile system consisting of two vertical micropiles at the 
ends of the two existing footings for a total of eight micropiles may be used.  The 
existing footing will need to be widened to incorporate the micropiles for foundation 
support.  Micropiles with a minimum grout bulb diameter of about 8 inches and 
extending a minimum of 12 feet below the bottom of footing and embedded into the 
underlying basalt rock may be used to support the existing bent footing.  The micropiles 
will derive its support primarily from side resistance within the underlying hard rock 
formation.  The micropiles should be spaced at least 4 feet center-to-center.  The 
minimum pile spacing recommended would avoid reduction in vertical capacity due to 
group action and would also facilitate pile installation in the pile group.   
It should be noted that the compressive load capacity of the micropiles is highly 
dependent on the drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed by the 
contractor to install the micropile.  Therefore, proof testing should be performed on at 
least two of the production micropiles.  Based on experience, we believe that the proof 
load test should be conducted no earlier than 7 days after completion of the micropile 
installation or until the micropile grout has attained at least 75 percent of the specified 
28-day compressive strength.

3.5 
Pavement Design 
We understand that short sections of new pavement will be required behind the 
bridge approach slabs and along the new shoulders.  Since the new pavement sections 
are short and the existing roadway consists of a flexible pavement, we recommend that 
a flexible pavement be used for the new pavement sections. 
The flexible pavement section presented herein was generally determined based 
on the methodology described in Chapter 3 of the revised Pavement Design Manual 
dated March 2002 prepared by the State of Hawaii – Department of Transportation, 
Highways Division, Material Testing and Research Brach.  The pavement design

We anticipate constructing the majority of the new roadway pavements at or near 
the existing grades or in a cut condition. In general, the area within the contract grading 
limits should be cleared and grubbed thoroughly at the on-set of earthwork. Vegetation, 
debris, deleterious materials, and other unsuitable materials should be removed and 
properly disposed off-site or disposed in a designated area to reduce the potential for 
contaminating the excavated materials.  
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over-excavated to expose 
stiff and/or dense materials. The resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
aggregate subbase materials. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be 
properly disposed off-site. 
After clearing and grubbing, the future pavement areas should be excavated, 
where necessary, to the pavement subgrade level (i.e., bottom of the aggregate 
subbase course layer). The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of 
at least 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers 
to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry 
density of the same soil determined in accordance with AASHTO T 180 (ASTM D1557). 
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density. 
In general, fill and backfill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Aggregate 
subbase materials required for the new pavements should conform to the requirements 
stipulated in Subsection 703.17 of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for Road 
and Bridge Construction, 2005 (HSS). The aggregate subbase should be 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in 8-inch level 
loose lifts, and compacted to no less than 95 percent of the maximum density according 
to AASHTO T 180 (ASTM D1557).
