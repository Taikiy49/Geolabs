INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed dining hall addition adjacent to the existing Airmen s Dining
Hall within the Hickam Air Force Base on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location
and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design level
input only and may not be appropriate to form the basis for construction cost estimates
nor for construction bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to expand the existing Airmen s Dining Hall facility with the
construction of a new office and dining hall building adjacent to the existing structure It
is Our understanding that the existing dining hall structure is being supported on pile
foundations
The new office and dining hall addition will be a one story structure covering a floor
area of approximately 435 and 625 square feet in plan dimension respectively Based
on the proposed finish floor elevation of about 10 5 feet Mean Sea Level MSL grading
consisting of minor cuts and fills on the order of 1 foot 1 is anticipated for the proposed
construction Special considerations for the project will include the potential swelling
characteristics of the near surface soils on the future floor slabs supported on grade
Based on the information provided to us by the project structural engineer
maximum column loads are estimated to be on the order of 20 kips per column with wall
GEOLABS HAWAII

loads of approximately 6 kips per lineal foot Special attention has to be given to the
settlement characteristics of the underlying subsoils under the anticipated loading
imposed by the new dining hall facility

The proposed project site is located adjacent to the existing Airmen s Dining Hall
Building 1860 within the Hickam Air Force Base on the island of Oahu Hawaii The
existing dining hall building is bounded by Westover Street to the east Andrew Street to
the north and McChord Street to the west
The existing dining hall is a one story structure with concrete walls and slab on
grade concrete floors It is our understanding that the structure is supported on pile

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our exploration program was conducted on August 12 1993 The exploration
program consisted of drilling and sampling two 2 borings to depths of 25 and 28 5 feet
below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our exploration encountered a surficial layer of fill material approximately
six 6 to eight feet 8 thick consisting of very stiff clayey silt material overlying
approximately three feet 3 of soft compressible lagoonal deposit of silty clay This was
underlain by coral deposits of varying degrees of density to depths of approximately 20
feet below the existing ground surface Very dense volcanic tuff deposits mudrock was
then encountered extending to the maximum depths explored
Groundwater was encountered in the drilled borings at depths of approximately 9
feet below the existing ground surface during the time of our field exploration These
depths generally correspond to an elevation of approximately one foot above MSL Due
to the proximity of the shoreline and the Pacific Ocean it is likely that the groundwater
levels measured in the borings during our field exploration may reflect neither the highest
nor the lowest possible levels at the locations measured The groundwater levels at the
project site may be influenced by tidal fluctuations seasonal precipitation and storm surge
conditions

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
On the basis of our geotechnical engineering evaluation we believe that due to the
presence of soft compressible subsoils there is a potential for ground settlements at the
proposed dining hall addition We understand that structural connections between the
existing pile supported Airmen Dining Hall structure and the proposed new addition are
not finalized We anticipate that there will be some differential settlements between the
existing pile supported structure and the proposed new addition if a shallow spread
footing foundation system is used to support the one story addition Based on our
evaluation we estimate total settlements on the order of three quarter of an inch 3
could occur for the new addition supported on shallow foundation systems Since the
existing adjacent structure is supported on piles differential settlements between the
existing structure and proposed addition could be up to about inch If the estimated
differential settlements are not acceptable then a deep foundation system should be
considered for support of the proposed dining hall addition
Shallow Foundations
Our field exploration indicate that the site is underlain by a compressible layer of
soft silty clay and loose coral deposits below the near surface crust Based on the
subsoil conditions and the anticipated structural loadings it is our opinion that shallow
footing foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings well tied together
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3125 00 Page 7
could be used for support of the proposed dining hall addition provided that the estimated
differential building settlements between the two adjacent structures are acceptable
An allowable bearing pressure of 2 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be
used for design of spread footing foundations bearing on the upper stiff clayey silt fill
material encountered near the existing ground surface This bearing value is for dead
plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 for transient loads such as those
caused by wind or seismic forces
The footings should be embedded a minimum of eighteen inches 18 below the
lowest adjacent finish grade We understand that the some of the renovation work
performed at the existing dining hall after its completion were supported on shallow
foundations We recommend that new footings adjacent to these existing shallow
foundations should extend at least to the same depth as the existing footings or deeper
to avoid surcharging the existing shallow foundations The actual depths of the existing
shallow footings should be confirmed in the field during construction
We estimate the footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for footings
bearing on the very stiff in situ soils to be less than inch total and inch differential
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil Resistance due
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3125 00 Page 8
to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300
pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f This assumes that the soil around
footings is well compacted or undisturbed Unless covered by pavements or slabs the
passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected A coefficient of
friction of 0 3 may be used for footings bearing on the dense in situ soils
It is recommended that the bottom of all footing excavations be compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction prior to placement of reinforcing steel and
concrete Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
procedures described in ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 If soft and or loose
materials are encountered at the bottom of footing excavations they should be over
excavated to expose firm materials The over excavation should either be backfilled with
granular material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction or the
footing bottom be extended down to the compacted firm in situ soil
Footing excavations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii
prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm foundation bearing
conditions and the embedment depths
Concrete Pile Foundations
Since the existing adjacent dining hall building is supported on concrete pile
foundations ideally a similar driven pile foundation system could be considered for the
new dining hall addition However due to the proximity of the new dining hall addition
to the existing structure vibration generated during the pile driving operation may cause
distress to the existing dining hall structure
In addition the mobilization cost of the pile driving equipment would be relatively
high in comparison with the scope of work involved for the subject project Based on
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3125 00 Page 9
these considerations it appears that the use of a similar driven concrete pile foundation
system may not be practical and economical for the proposed addition
Drilled piers were also considered in lieu of piles however installation of drilled
piers will require temporary casing and tremie concrete placement due to the presence
of loose granular soils and high groundwater table
Micro Pile Foundations
If the estimated settlements are not acceptable for the project construction micro
pile foundations consisting of small diameter steel pipe piles with closed ends may be
used to support the proposed new dining hall The pipe piles will be filled with structural
concrete after completion of the pile driving operations These micro piles would provide
adequate foundation support with relatively small settlements and can be installed with a
small pile driving hammer or vibratory hammer
The steel pipe piles should have a minimum inside diameter of ten inches 10
A design capacity of 15 kips per pile may be used for piles bearing on the dense coral
formation or the volcanic tuff deposits encountered at a deeper depth Based on our field
exploration data pile length on the order of 20 feet may be anticipated for the project
construction A minimum pile spacing of three feet 3 center to center is recommended
A lateral load capacity of nine 9 kips per pile with a maximum induced moment
of fourteen 14 kips feet occurring at a depth of three feet 3 below the top of the pile
may be used to resist lateral loads An allowable uplift capacity of five 5 kips per pile
may also be used for the design of the pile foundation
Pile settlements are estimated to be on the order of less than one half inch 4
Settlements should be elastic and should occur as the loads are applied
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3125 00 Page 10
The pipe piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a minimum
rated energy of about 15 000 foot pounds Because local variations in soil strength
characteristics are likely to exist in the bearing soils the supporting capacity of each pile
must be confirmed in the field during driving This will be accomplished by using a driving
resistance procedure such as the Engineering News Record ENR formula or the more
sophisticated Wave Equation Analysis to evaluate pile bearing capacity The driving
criteria will be developed after the selection of the pile driving equipment
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the matching of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be driven
This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy to the top of
the pile and also reducing damage to the pile Geolabs Hawaii should review the pile
driving equipment proposed to evaluate if the equipment is capable of installing the piles
to the recommended capacity Due to possible variation in the length of the piles unit
prices should be obtained during bidding for add ons additional welds if used at
connection etc
Predrilling may be required to penetrate through the hard fill layer in the upper five
feet to facilitate the pile driving operation
Special attention has to be given to the potential deterioration of the proposed steel
pipe pile foundations due to the anticipated corrosive subsoils encountered at the project
site A thicker gauge steel pipe pile with steel reinforcements at the center of the pile may
be used to reduce the potential effects of the corrosive subsoils on the pipe pile
foundations However the use of a thicker gauge steel assumes that the structure has
a finite useful life A high density concrete mix should be used for backfill inside the pipe
pile

Slab On Grade
The project site is underlain by a layer of clayey silt soils which are considered to
be moderately expansive when subjected to moisture fluctuations Unless slab supported
by these soils are properly designed there is a potential for future distress to lightly
loaded slab on grade construction resulting from the shrinking and swelling of these soils
due to changes in the moisture content To reduce the potential for structural distress
resulting from the swelling of the on site moderately expansive soils we recommend that
the concrete slabs be supported on 12 inches of non expansive select fill material The
non expansive select fill material should extend at least two feet 2 beyond the
perimeter of the proposed structure
The select fill material should be placed as soon as possible after the scarification
and recompaction of the existing subgrade soils The prepared capping fill and the
underlying subgrade soil should be wetted and kept moist prior to final placement of
concrete Subgrade soils should be prepared in accordance with the recommendations
presented in the Site Preparation section of this report The select fill would serve as
a capping fill layer of moist soil above the underlying potentially expansive subgrade soils
and this capping fill would also reduce the potential loss of moisture from the above
prepared moist subgrade soils The reduction in future subgrade saturation should
reduce the potential swelling of the underlying moderately expansive clayey soils
Compaction requirements for the subgrade soils and select fill are discussed in the Site
Preparation section of this report
We recommend that a minimum four inch 4 thick layer of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation cushion layer be installed below the slab to provide
uniform support for the slab

To reduce future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings
an impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is recommended on
top of gravel cushion layer One inch 1 of moist fine sand may be placed above the
plastic membrane to help concrete curing
Site Preparation
We anticipate that site grading for the proposed project will consist of minor cuts
and fills on the order of about one foot 1 to provide a level building pad for the
proposed construction
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of off site
After demolition clearing and grubbing all areas beneath future slabs areas and
two feet 2 beyond should be over excavated to a depth of at least 12 inches below the
future slab subgrade level such as bottom of No 3B Fine gravel cushion layer If zones
of soft loose or saturated soils are encountered at the over excavated subgrade level
deeper over excavation may be required to expose firm material The resulting excavation
should be backfilled with compacted select granular fill material The clayey material
resulting from the over excavation should be removed and properly disposed of off site
Based on the existing topography and the proposed finish floor elevation of the structure
we anticipate that a relatively small amount of over excavation of the surficial expansive
soils would be required
Areas to receive fills should be scarified to a depth of eight inches 8 moisture
conditioned to at least two percent 2 above optimum moisture content and
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction Select granular fill material should
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3125 00 Page 13
be placed in level lifts not exceeding ten inches 10 in loose thickness and compacted
to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
The select granular fill material should consist of imported granular material such
as crushed coral basalt gravel or cinder sand The material should be well graded from
coarse to fine with no particles greater than three inches 3 in largest dimension The
material should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 20 or higher
and a swell potential of not more than one percent 1 when tested in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1883 It should contain less than 20 percent passing the No
200 sieve Any imported fill material should be tested and approved for its intended use
by our firm prior to delivery to the project site
Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines may be required for the project A granular
bedding consisting of six inches 6 of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
is recommended under the pipes Self compacting granular materials such as No 3B
Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the trench backfill up
to about one foot 1 above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and
to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging
the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level one foot 1 above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select granular material
The backfill should be placed in maximum ten inch 10 level loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper two feet
2 of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to 95
percent relative compaction

Drainage
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Also it is advised that gutter
systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations
and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be
properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce water infiltration
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be
maintained to drain all surface run off away from the slab and footing foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling two
2 borings to depths of 25 and 28 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig
The material encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System A graphic presentation of the materials encountered are presented
on the Boring Logs Plates A 1 and A 2
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the
Boring Logs at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of four inches 4 in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0
foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring Logs as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content unit weight and Atterberg Limits determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Boring Logs at the appropriate sample
depths
One 1 one inch ring swell test was performed on selected samples to evaluate
the swelling potential of the subsoils The test results are summarized on Plate B 1
One 1 Atterberg Limits test was performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate their liquid and plastic limits The test results are shown on Plate B 2
Two 2 unconfined compression tests were performed on selected samples to
evaluate their shear strength characteristics The test results are also presented on
Plate B 2
