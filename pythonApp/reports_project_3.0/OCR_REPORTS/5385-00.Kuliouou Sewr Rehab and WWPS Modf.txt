2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated
to be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age When the building of the Waianae and Koolau volcanoes came to
an end only a slight embayment existed on the southern coast of Oahu
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of the Koolau Range and its
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to the glacio eustatic
fluctuations of sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age The base of the
stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt The Koolau volcanic shield was built
by thinly bedded lava flows during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epochs The
main shield building activities ceased approximately 2 million years ago
After a long period of cessation of the shield building volcanic activity returned
with the formation of a series of lava flows cinder cones and tuff cones such as those
associated with Koko Crater Many of the eruptions were rather explosive in nature due
to the interaction of groundwater with the rising magma resulting in steam explosions
which expelled large quantities of pyroclastic material predominantly ash and cinder
Some explosive eruptions produced ash that cemented together to form a firm rock
known as tuff locally known as mudrock
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea
When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits
at correspondingly higher elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine sediments in

3 4 Cured In Place Pipe Rehabilitation
The Cured In Place Pipe CIPP system can be used to eliminate or reduce the
inflow of storm water infiltration of groundwater exfiltration of pollutants surface
settlement caused by soil migration into the pipe corrosion attack and pipe
irregularities defects joints in the sewer collection systems The CIPP technique is
normally preferred over other alternatives to rehabilitate the existing pipelines under the
following conditions
e The pipeline has adequate flow capacity during the expected life of the
liner
e The pipe diameter ranging from 4 to 108 inches and length up to
1 000 to 3 000 feet of the rehabilitation run are within the general
envelope of capabilities of the CIPP system
e The host pipe is not severely damaged broken or deformed There are
no flat surfaces or corners significant changes in pipe diameter from
manhole to manhole and sharp bends or tees
e Flow bypassing is feasible or unnecessary due to low flows
CIPP is a liquid thermosetting resin saturated material that is inserted into the
existing pipeline through the existing manholes by hydrostatic inversion air inversion or
by mechanically pulling with a winch and cable The water inversion process utilizes
water pressure to expand the lining against the walls of the host pipe The air inversion
W O INC Page 26
Hawaii California
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
process employs air pressure to invert the lining With the mechanical winching process
the lining is winched into place and expanded using a calibration hose inverted into the
lining under water pressure After insertion the resin saturated material cures by
application of hot water or steam to form a new pipe of slightly smaller inside diameter
but of the same general shape as the original pipe The liner is designed to fit snugly
against the wall of the host pipe
There are several advantages of the CIPP method over other methods of
trenchless pipeline rehabilitation Grouting is not normally required due to the tight fit of
the CIPP against the existing pipe The CIPP has no joints and has a very smooth
interior surface which typically improves flow capacity despite the slight decrease in
diameter Non circular shapes can be accommodated often without a decrease in flow
capacity The lining is capable of accommodating bends and some existing pipe
deformations Entry is possible via existing manholes or through minor excavations and
the total installation can take place rapidly Remote control internal lateral connection is
possible The cost of CIPP has been found to be competitive with all other methods of
construction and rehabilitation particularly for larger projects with multiple segments
and where social and environmental considerations are significant
Compared with other rehabilitation techniques CIPP liners require expertise to
ensure that installations result in a quality product A trained crew and operators with
special equipment are required which may cost more for small jobs High set up costs
on small projects may reduce the effectiveness of this method The tube or hose must
be specially constructed for each project The existing flow must be bypassed or
plugged upstream of the section to be rehabilitated during the installation process
Sealing may be required at liner ends or cuts to prevent inflow or outflow The curing
period could take 5 hours or more under certain circumstances Finally the rehabilitated
pipe will take the shape of the host pipe including deformations

In order to install the underground fuel storage tank flow tube box and
replacement sewers we anticipate that excavations below the existing ground surface
will be required for the project construction Based on the soft and or loose subsurface
conditions encountered in the upper 30 feet of subsoils we envision that temporary
shoring of the excavations will be required for the project construction
Our field exploration at the project area generally encountered a surface layer of
man made fill underlain by very loose sands and soft clays extending to depths of about
22 feet below the existing ground surface Therefore we anticipate that conventional
excavation techniques may be used for the planned excavations In general we believe
that interlocking steel sheet piling may be used for temporary shoring purposes
especially where dewatering of the excavations will be necessary For shallow
excavations and excavations in open areas we believe that a cantilever sheet pile
shoring may be considered For deeper excavations and excavations located adjacent
to buildings utilities and pavements we recommend that interlocking steel sheet piling
with horizontal bracing be used for excavation support in order to reduce the potential
for significant adjacent ground movement
A monitoring program should be developed and implemented by the contractor to
detect ground movement and or subsidence adjacent to the excavations which may

Excavation Method
In general the method and equipment to be used for excavation should be
determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations
Based on our field exploration the surface fill soils and underlying soft loose
deposits encountered in the borings may be excavated with conventional
earthmoving equipment
The excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance
equal to the depth of the excavation measured from the outside edge of the
excavation in order to reduce the potential for appreciable ground movement In
addition the excavated soils should be stockpiled at least 15 feet away from
underground utilities to reduce the potential for ground movement or subsidence
which may damage the existing underground utility lines
Excavation Support
We anticipate that excavation depths up to about 11 to 13 feet may be required for
the project construction Based on the subsoil conditions encountered in our
borings we believe that shoring of the sides of the excavations will be necessary to
protect personnel working in the excavations Temporary shoring of the excavations
using steel sheet pile shoring especially where dewatering of the excavations will
be necessary may be considered However it should be noted that the planned
excavations may likely traverse other utility lines therefore care should be
exercised to avoid truncating the existing utility lines if sheet piles are to be used as
shoring
One possible method of shoring would be to use interlocking sheet pile shoring

The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and
Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration
Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
