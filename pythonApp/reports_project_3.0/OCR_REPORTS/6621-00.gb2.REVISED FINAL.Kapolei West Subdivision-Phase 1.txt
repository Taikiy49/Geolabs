SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The subsurface conditions at the north and east sides of the site generally consists 
of a relatively thick layer of alluvial deposits.  The south and west sides of the site 
generally consist of a relatively thin layer of alluvial deposits.  Below the alluvial deposits, 
our borings encountered coralline detritus interbedded with alluvial deposits extending to 
the maximum depths explored. We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the 
time of our field exploration. 
Based on the information provided, majority of the site will be filled to achieve the 
design finished grades.  We understand that the site will be mass graded to approximately 
24 inches below the finished subgrades using the on-site soils as fill material.  Our field 
exploration disclosed that majority of the project site is generally underlain by near-surface 
clayey soils.  Based on our laboratory tests, the clayey soils have a moderate to high 
swelling potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations. After the site has been mass 
graded to approximately 24 inches below the finished subgrades, it may be sometime 
before the site is filled up to the finished subgrades.  This could allow the soils at the mass 
graded elevations to dry out.  If the clayey soils are allowed to dry, shrinkage cracks may 
develop and the subgrade will have to be prepared again in accordance with our site 
preparation recommendations provided in this report prior to fine grading. We believe that it 
is critical to maintain the in-situ moisture of the clayey soils to at least 2 percent above the 
optimum moisture content. 
Since the clayey soils encountered at the site have a moderate to high swell 
potential, we recommend using structural fill material to raise the site from the mass graded 
elevations up to the finished subgrade elevations.  The thickness of the structural fill layer 
will depend on the type of foundation and slab system used for future building structures 
constructed at the site.  Since the land areas will be developed by others, it is not known as 
to what type of foundation and slab system will be used for the future building structures. 
If the new building structures will consist of conventional foundations and slabs-on-
grade, a minimum 36-inch thick layer of structural fill should be used to raise the building 
pad areas.  Depending on the finished grade elevation, the mass graded areas may have 
to be over-excavated to provide a minimum 36 inches of structural fill.  If alternative 
foundation and slab systems are used, such as post-tensioned waffle slabs, the structural 
fill layer thickness could be reduced. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
design recommendations.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from 
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to 
be middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. 
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene 
(Ice Age) in age and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. As 
volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano ceased, lava flows from the Koolau Volcano 
banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau, known as the 
Schofield Plateau. 
Physical and chemical weathering, followed by erosion of this plateau, generated 
sediments, which were transported to the coast. In the project vicinity and to the south, 
these sediments accumulated and interbedded with marine sediments and coral/algal 
reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge. The thickness of the sedimentary wedge 
ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H-1 Highway to over 1,000 feet at 
Ewa Beach. This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation, or 
"caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu. Deposition of sediments 
has continued from earlier geologic time through the present. Agricultural development 
within the last 100 years has brought the project area to its present form.

3.1.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly.  Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and 
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off-site.  Soft 
and/or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill should be over-excavated to expose firm materials, and 
the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fills.  The 
excavated soft and/or organic soils should be disposed properly off-site and/or used 
in landscape areas, where appropriate.  Contract documents should include 
additive and deductive unit prices for over-excavation and compacted fill placement 
to account for variations in the over-excavation quantities. 
Abundant shrinkage cracks are present at the existing ground surface across the 
project site.  In general, shrinkage cracks will develop in the ground due to the lack 
of maintenance of the soil moisture content as the surface soils dry out under the 
sun and dry weather conditions.  The development of shrinkage cracks in the 
ground was apparently intensified by the prolonged dry climatic conditions that are 
characteristic of the Kapolei area resulting in large fluctuations of the soil moisture 
content. 
After clearing and grubbing, the exposed subgrades should be proof-rolled with a 
minimum 10-ton vibratory drum roller a minimum of six passes to assist in detecting 
and collapsing near-surface voids and loose zones created by the shrinkage cracks. 
The exposed subgrades should be proof-rolled without moisture-conditioning the 
subgrade soils (at the pre-existing moisture contents).  Loose areas disclosed during 
the proof-rolling operation should be backfilled with general fill material compacted to 
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  A Geolabs representative should 
observe the proof-rolling operations on a near-continuous basis.

Subsequent to the proof-rolling operations, the subgrades should then be scarified 
to a minimum depth of 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above 
the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction.  Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil 
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Where shrinkage cracks develop in the subgrade soil after compaction, we 
recommend preparing the subgrade soil again as recommended above.  Saturation 
and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and 
poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with 
well-compacted fill.  The need for over-excavation due to soft subgrade soil 
conditions should be evaluated in the field by a Geolabs representative. 
3.1.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, we believe that the excavated soil materials may be used as a source of 
fill materials provided that the materials are properly processed and placed in 
accordance with the “Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements” subsection 
herein.  The excavated on-site soils to be used as fills should be free of vegetation, 
deleterious materials, and rock fragments greater than 6 inches in maximum 
dimension.  
Imported fill materials needed for the site filling may consist of materials with a low 
expansion potential.  Imported general fill materials should consist of materials with 
particle sizes of 6 inches or less in maximum dimension and should be free of 
deleterious materials.  The material should contain between 10 and 60 percent 
particles passing the No. 200 Sieve.  The material should have a laboratory 
California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 12 or higher and a swell potential of not 
more than 2 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883. 
Structural fill material used to raise the mass graded elevations to the finished 
subgrade elevations should consist of crushed coralline or basaltic materials. The

3.1.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
General fills and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to about 2 percent above 
the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction.  
Structural fill should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, 
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to at 
least 90 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, 
multiple-wheel pneumatic-tired rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction 
equipment. 
Because moisture-conditioning and compaction of the clayey soils are critical 
elements of earthwork, Geolabs should conduct observations and perform soil 
density tests during site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the required 
degree of compaction and the proper moisture content. Where compaction is less 
than required, additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of

3.1.4 Excavation 
Based on our field exploration, excavations at the site will involve cuts into the very 
stiff to hard alluvial deposits and medium dense to dense coralline detritus. It is 
anticipated that the alluvial deposits and coralline detritus may be excavated with 
normal heavy excavation equipment. However, excavations in the coralline detritus 
may encounter localized hard zones, which may require the use of hoerams.   
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based 
on field data obtained from the borings performed at the project site. Contractors 
bidding on this project should be encouraged to examine the site conditions and 
soil data to make their own interpretation. 
3.1.5 Cut and Fill Slopes 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration, it appears 
that most of the cut slopes would expose medium dense to dense sand and gravel 
and very stiff to hard silty clay.  In general, we believe that cut slopes may be made 
at an inclination of 2H:1V or flatter.  Cut slopes at the proposed drainage channel 
may be steepened to an inclination of 1.5H:1V provided that the cut slopes are 
lined with Grouted Rubble Paving (GRP) or concrete. 
Fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter.  If 
necessary, slope benches are recommended at maximum 30-foot vertical height 
intervals.  If the existing ground surface is steeper that 5H:1V, fills should be keyed 
and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding.  
The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level 
horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement

3.2.3 Dynamic Lateral Earth Pressures 
Dynamic lateral earth forces due to seismic loading (amax= 0.17g) may be estimated 
by using 7.7H2 pounds per lineal foot of wall length for level backfill conditions, 
where H is the height of the wall in feet.  The dynamic lateral earth force presented 
above is in addition to the static lateral earth pressures.  The resultant force should 
be assumed to act through the mid-height of the wall.  An appropriately reduced 
factor of safety may be used when dynamic lateral earth forces are accounted for in 
the design of the retaining structures. 
It should be noted the forces due to dynamic lateral earth pressures provided 
assume that the retaining structure will be allowed to move laterally by up to about 
2 to 4 inches in the event of an earthquake. If this amount of lateral movement is 
not acceptable, the retaining structure should be designed with higher dynamic 
lateral forces for a semi-restrained wall condition. For a semi-restrained wall 
condition, the forces due to dynamic lateral earth pressures may be estimated 
using 11.7H2 pounds per linear foot of wall for level backfill conditions. 
3.2.4 Drainage 
Headwall structures should be well drained to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic 
pressures.  A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide zone of 
permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), 
placed directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at the base of 
the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes.  As an alternative, a 
prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used 
instead of the drainage material.  The prefabricated drainage product should also 
be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the structure. 
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of compacted on-site 
materials or granular compacted fills, where specified by the designer.  Unless 
covered by concrete slabs, the upper 12 inches of backfill should also consist of 
low-expansion, relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential for excessive 
water infiltration behind the walls.

3.4 
Drainage Channel 
We understand that a drainage channel is planned for the project.  Based on the 
information provided, the drainage channel will be about 15 to 30 feet deep.  The field 
exploration disclosed that the site is underlain by alluvial deposits over coralline detritus. 
The alluvium is generally about 1 to 3 feet thick at the south and west sides of the site 
and about 8 to 30 feet thick at the north and east sides of the site.  The alluvial deposits 
consist of clayey soils and the coralline detritus consists of silty sand and gravel.   
The alluvial deposits and coralline detritus are susceptible to erosion from water 
flow; therefore, we recommend protecting the channel walls and bottom with GRP or 
Portland cement concrete lining.  We understand that coralline cobbles and boulders 
may be available near the site.  The coralline cobbles and boulders may be used as a 
GRP lining along the drainage channel sidewalls.

Corrosion Potential 
Corrosivity tests (consisting of pH and minimum resistivity tests) were performed 
on the silty clay soils at the project site. Corrosivity test results are presented in 
Appendix B.  
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil 
characteristics with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In 
general, the lower the resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried 
metallic structure. Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will 
contribute to corrosion of metallic objects. 
Based on the results of the laboratory resistivity tests, we believe that an average 
corrosion rating of 2 may be used for the proposed project. Design of metallic 
substructures, such as water line pipes or other metallic piping, should consider the 
effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling twenty eight borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 28, advanced to 
maximum depths of about 16.5 to 46.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The

tools. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural 
examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified 
Soil Classification System, as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings,

sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) 
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification 
and evaluation of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings 
at the appropriate sample depths. 
Twelve one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on undisturbed and remolded 
samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils. The test results are 
summarized on Plate B-1. 
Four pH (ASTM G 51) and four Minimum Resistivity (ASTM G 57) tests were 
performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration.  The test results 
are provided on Plate B-2. 
Fourteen Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plates B-3 and B-4. 
Ten Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 & C 136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is presented on 
Plates B-5 and B-6. 
Ten Direct Shear tests (ASTM D 3080) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics.  Direct shear test results are presented 
on Plates B-7 through B-16. 
Five Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D 1557) were performed on the 
near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content relationships. The 
test results are presented on Plates B-17 through B-21.  
Five laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) tests (ASTM D 1883) were 
performed on the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-22 through B-26. 
Six laboratory Resistance (R) Value tests (ASTM D 2844) were performed on the 
near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils. The test 
results are presented on Plates B-27 through B-32.
