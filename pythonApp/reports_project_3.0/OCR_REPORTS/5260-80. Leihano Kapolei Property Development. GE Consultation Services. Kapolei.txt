Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of the
western third of the island while the younger Koolau Volcano forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below the Koolau lavas in central
Oahu forming a broad plateau known as Schofield Plateau
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of this plateau generated
sediments which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and to the

Subsurface Conditions
Subsurface conditions at the project site were evaluated based on literature
research of in house reports plans maps and other information Based on the geologic
setting of the project site and our experience in the vicinity we anticipate that the
subsurface materials generally would consist of a surface fill layer underlain by alluvial
deposits with coralline deposits within the alluvium
We anticipate the fill materials placed during the mass grading work for the
development generally would extend to about 1 to 2 feet below the ground surface The fill
materials will generally consist of dense coralline soils Below the fill materials we
anticipate that alluvial deposits consisting of stiff to hard clay and silty clay may be present
at the site The alluvial deposits extended to the maximum available boring depth in the
area of about 31 5 feet below the existing ground surface
Groundwater should be anticipated at elevations ranging from about 2 to 3 feet
Mean Sea Level MSL It should be noted that groundwater levels may be influenced by
tidal fluctuations seasonal precipitation surface water runoff and other factors at the
project site

Site Grading
Based on available subsurface information the project site is underlain by
moderate to highly expansive clay soils During the mass grading work the clayey
subgrade soils were moisture conditioned and about 1 to 2 feet of coralline fill material was
placed over the clayey subgrade at the project site
Since the project site has been exposed for an extended period of time after mass
grading the moisture condition of the clayey subgrade should be verified prior to fill
placement Should drying of the clayey subgrade be encountered the clayey subgrade
would need to be scarified moisture conditioned and compacted to the minimum
compaction requirements
We anticipate that about 3 to 4 feet of fill would be needed to raise the site to the
street level Non expansive select granular fill material should be used below the building
and exterior slabs on grade
Building Foundations
Details of the proposed improvements were not available at the time this letter
report was prepared As mentioned previously the site may be underlain by stiff to hard
alluvial clay deposits
For the proposed 14 story structure we envision column loads of about 1 000 to
1 500 kips Due to the high column loading we believe that a deep foundation system
consisting of drilled shafts or augered cast in place ACIP piles may be considered
Drilled shafts with diameters of 24 and 36 inches and preliminary shaft lengths ranging
from 50 to 65 feet long would be required to achieve an allowable compressive load
capacity of 500 to 1 500 kips For the ACIP piles with diameters of 18 and 24 inches and
preliminary pile lengths ranging from 40 to 75 feet long allowable compressive load
capacities range from about 300 to 1 200 kips The size and depth of the foundations
would need to be determined based on the type of new structures planned and the result
of a design level field exploration program
