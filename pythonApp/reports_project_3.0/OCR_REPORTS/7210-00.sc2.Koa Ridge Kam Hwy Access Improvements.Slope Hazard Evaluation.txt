PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The proposed Koa Ridge residential subdivision project site is along the northern 
side of Kamehameha Highway in the Waipio area of the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. Part of 
the development involves some improvements to a section of Kamehameha Highway to 
accommodate the future subdivision Road “C” intersection with the existing highway. The 
intersection improvements include some highway widening and development of a new 
flatter cut slope along the mauka side of the highway between about Sta. 14+00 and about 
Sta. 20+00 to accommodate the additional outbound lane improvements. 
We understand it is proposed to develop the new cut slope by flattening the existing 
cut slope through the length of the highway improvement zone.  The existing highway cut 
slope stands at an average inclination of about one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V) 
where as the planned cut slope is proposed at a flatter 2H:1V inclination. Therefore, there 
will be a slope inclination transition zone spanning between about Sta. 19+00 and about

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
The cut slope project site is in the Waipio area of the Island of Oahu, Hawaii, as 
shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. The proposed cut slope flattening is along 
the eastern side of Kamehameha Highway, northerly of the Ka Uka Boulevard 
intersection with the highway and southerly from the existing highway bridge crossing 
within Kipapa Gulch. The cut slope project site is bounded by gently sloping former 
agricultural land towards the east at the top of the slope above the highway. The former 
agricultural lands are proposed for development of the Koa Ridge residential 
subdivision. The project is bounded towards the west by existing steep natural slopes 
dropping into Kipapa Gulch. The existing conditions and location of the subject cut slope 
are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  
Based on our observations at the time of our site visit, the existing cut slope was 
generally moderately to densely vegetated with brush and shrubbery. The existing cut 
slope appears to stand at inclinations of about 1H:1V with some steepened slope 
segments approaching 0.5H:1V. The existing cut slope height ranges between about 
10 feet at about Sta. 15+00 and about 35 feet at about Sta. 20+00.  
We did not observe visible overt signs of slope instability, such as earth slump 
and falling rock, within the cut slope project limits. Fallen rock material was not 
encountered along the toe of the existing cut slope at the time of our visit. However, a 
review of available time-lapse images from Google Earth indicates that occasional fallen 
rock material, including small hard boulders, has occurred in the past along the shoulder 
of the highway. 
Based on our site reconnaissance, the segment of the existing cut slope 
spanning between about Sta. 14+00 and about Sta. 20+00 is generally composed of 
reddish brown silty and clayey residual/saprolitic soils with some embedded boulders 
and scattered zones of weathered rock material. The embedded boulders and 
weathered rock represent erosion-remnant rocky material associated with the deep 
weathering and decomposition of the parent basalt rock.

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on a review of the available project boring logs, the slope appears to be 
composed of some near-surface stiff/loose fill soils overlying very stiff residual soils at 
depth. A project boring drilled at the top of the slope adjacent to the subject slope 
inclination transition zone did not encounter buried rock material; however, we did observe 
some generally isolated embedded boulders and scattered zones of weathered basalt 
rock embedded within the existing cut slope face in the vicinity of the slope inclination 
transition zone. 
Based on our site reconnaissance, visible evidence of active slope instability or 
significant landslide/debris flow deposits was not observed.  However, it appears that the 
steepness of the existing cut slope may contribute to some surface soil loss by erosion 
and possible shallow sloughing of the near-surface soils comprising the slope face.  
Based on our reconnaissance, hard rock material exposure at the existing cut slope 
face appears to be limited to some embedded hard boulders and zones of highly 
weathered and decomposed rock material.  Extensive outcropping of hard basalt rock 
formation material was not observed in the exposures of the cut slope face within the 
project limits.  
Potential Slope Hazard & Rockfall Conditions 
Based on our observations, it appears that the proposed flattening of the existing 
cut slope to a 2H:1V cut slope inclination is appropriate to reduce the potential for slope 
erosion and production of potential falling rock hazards.  However, the transition zone for 
the new cut slope inclination to match the existing cut slope inclination at the northern limit 
of the project site appears to have some potential for slope surface erosion and the 
possible production of potential falling rock hazards due to the steeper slope inclination 
and the increased potential for erosion of the cut slope face. 
To reduce the potential for future cut slope instability and the possible production of 
falling rock hazards from the steepened cut slope transition zone located between 
Sta. 19+00 and Sta. 20+00, the installation of an anchored wire mesh system on the cut 
slope face is recommended. The purpose for the anchored wire mesh is to stabilize and 
prevent the production of falling rock hazards from the steepened slope face. We envision

that a row of ground anchors will be constructed across the top and bottom of the slope to 
support the wire mesh system. In addition, the wire mesh panels will be pattern-pinned to 
the slope face with grouted anchors drilled at selected intervals across the slope.   
In addition to preventing the fall of loose rocks from the slope face, the anchored 
wire mesh system provides resistance against possible slope face erosion and 
shallow-depth earth failure. Finally, the anchored wire mesh system should help to 
promote the re-growth of slope vegetation through the wire mesh material. 
Based on our observations, it is our professional opinion that the cut slope flattening 
and provision of an anchored wire mesh system within the cut slope inclination transition 
zone should provide for a stabilized finished cut slope. We recommend the installation of 
erosion control matting beneath the wire mesh to reduce the potential for surface soil 
erosion following construction until the native slope vegetation becomes established. 
LIMITATIONS 
The findings and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from visual site observations and the borings drilled at the site. 
Variation in the surface and subsurface conditions between our observation points may 
occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until 
additional field exploration or construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it 
will be necessary to re-evaluate the findings and recommendations provided herein. 
It should be noted that slopes composed of rock materials deteriorate with the 
passage of time due to natural weathering processes, wet-dry and hot-cold cycles, and 
erosion conditions. Due to the inherent deterioration of rock slopes resulting from natural 
processes (weathering, wet-dry and hot-cold cycles, and erosion conditions), the potential 
of rockfall hazard at any site changes with the passage of time. Therefore, the findings and 
recommendations contained in this letter report may be used only within a reasonable time 
from the date of issuance. Land use, site conditions, and/or other factors may change with 
the passage of time. Therefore, additional work to evaluate the applicability of the findings 
and preliminary recommendations contained in this letter report due to changes with the 
passage of time will be required. Finally, there are no guarantees in the professional 
engineering and architectural design fields with respect to potential rockfall hazards due in 
large part to the unpredictable nature of rockfall activity, which is affected by many external 
variables including natural and man-induced causes.
