the island of Kauai Hawaii Seismic refraction methods were selected to perform an initial

SEISMIC REFRACTION METHODS
Seismic refraction surveys involve applying energy to the ground and measuring
the rate that the energy in the form of elastic waves moves through the soil and rock
In general the elastic waves travel slowly through soft low density materials such as soil

The elastic waves travel rapidly through high density material such as rock The
boundaries between layers of different velocities can be determined by analysis of the
travel times to geophones at different distances from the energy source
Using Seismic refraction methods successive layers of increasing velocity
corresponding to increasing density can be measured Low velocity layers overlain by
high velocity layers are masked and can not be detected by this method
As a general rule seismic refraction methods measure the velocities of the
subsurface materials to a depth equal to about 1 3 the length of the survey line Survey
lines for this study were about 220 feet long therefore seismic velocities of the rock were
measured to depths of about 70 feet
The complexity of volcanic formations can lead to difficulties in accurate
interpretation of seismic refraction data Not only does the rock generally become less
weathered witn depth volcanic formations tend to have discontinuous layers of dense
rock and less dense clinker or vesicular rock A substantial amount of judgement must
be used to interpret what kind of rock a given seismic velocity layer may represent
Therefore the preliminary interpretations described in this report should be confirmed by
subsurface exploration such as borings
Survey Methods Used
For this investigation an 8 lb hammer was used as the energy source The
hammer was used to apply energy to the ground at shot points located 3 feet from both
ends of the geophone array The geophone array consisted of 12 geophones with a
spacing of 20 feet between geophones Energy arrivals at the geophones were

measured and recorded with a Bison Model 5012 12 channel signal enhancing

A total of 21 seismic refraction lines were performed at three sites Lines 1 through
8 were performed at a site located southeast of the existing quarry lines 9 through 14
were located in an area referred to as Mauka Quarry and lines 15 through 21 were
located in an area called B Quarry
The seismic refraction data collected in the field was transferred to a computer and
analyzed using a program called Refract The program assists the geophysist by plotting
charts showing elastic wave arrival times at the geophones vs distance from the energy
source The program also facilitates interpretation of the data as a layered model
Area Geology
Regional geologic maps indicate that the study area is underlain by Koloa Series
volcanic lava flows McDonald 1960 consisting of basaltic rock In the study area the
basaltic rock is overlain by residual soil and a layer of highly weathered to decomposed
rock
In the Mauka Quarry residual soils consisting of red brown clayey silt are typically
about 2 feet thick Beneath the residual soil highly weathered saprolitic rock was
exposed ranging from about 10 to 20 feet thick Beneath the saprolitic rock less
weathered basalt was exposed consisting of interbedded dense basalt and clinker basalt

Survey Findings
The attached Geologic Layer Profiles represent an interpretation of the seismic
refraction data that was collected on October 12th through October 14th The data
interpretation was made without the benefit of nearby boring data It should be noted that
the data from line 13 was judged to be inadequate for interpretation of a layer profile
The Geologic Layer Profiles show the interpreted thickness of layers with certain
seismic velocities in feet per second fps In general a surfical soil layer was interpreted
with a velocity of about 500 to Underlying the surficial soil a decomposed
saprolitic rock was interpreted with a velocity of about 1 300 to Underlying the
decomposed saprolitic rock less weather rock that probably includes dense basalt
and or clinker was interpreted Where velocities of about or higher were found
the majority of the rock mass is interpreted to be dense basalt Where velocities of less
than about were found the majority of the rock mass was probably clinker
Conditions where dense basalt can be found at relatively shallow depths below the
existing ground surface are desirable from a quarry resource standpoint Based on the
findings of the seismic fraction survey areas that appear most likely to meet these criteria
and therefore may warrant exploratory drilling include the areas near Lines 11
14 20 and 21
At Lines 16 17 and 19 the survey suggests that the saprolite could be
relatively thin generally less than about 25 feet thick however the velocities of the
underlying rock suggest that the rock mass probably contains a significant percentage
of clinker However we recommend that borings be made to further verify the above
interpretation
