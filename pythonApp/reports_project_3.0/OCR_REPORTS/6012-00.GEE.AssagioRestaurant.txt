SECTION 1.0 - GENERAL
1.1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Assagio Restaurant project in Honolulu on the Island of
Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project
Location Map, Plate 1.
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering
recommendations derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering
analyses. These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations,
slabs-on-grade, site grading, and pavements only. The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report.
1.2. Project Considerations
The project involves the construction of Assagio Restaurant at the corner of
Waialae Avenue and Kilauea Avenue, TMK: (1) 3-5-017: 003, in the Kahala area on the
Island of Oahu, Hawaii, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
A one-story building with 30 parking stalls is currently planned. The site is
generally flat and only minimal grading is anticipated. Details for the planned structures
were not available at the time this report was prepared.
The site was formerly used as a Chevron gas station; therefore, backfill for the
former underground tanks and other foundations is present. Based on the field borings,
the backfill materials vary from silty clay to cobbles and boulders.

geotechnical engineering recommendations for the design of foundations,

SECTION 2.0 - SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2.1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) and
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas
banking against its eastern flank.
The project area is located on the southern flank of Koolau and is composed of
basaltic rock built by the extrusion of the lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series. These
rocks are generally characterized by flows of jointed, dense vesicular basalt with
interbedded thin clinker layers. For the last 1 to 2 million years, in-situ chemical
weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred, forming a mantle of residual soils,
which grade to saprolite with depth. In general, saprolite is composed mainly of silty
material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks. The saprolite grades
to basaltic rock formation with depth. Grading operations for the developments in the
area have brought the site to its present form.
2.2 Existing Site Conditions
The project site is along Waialae Avenue in the Kahala area, in Honolulu on the
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The site is located on the corner of Waialae Avenue and
Kilauea Avenue, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
Based on our field observation, the site is an open lot, partially paved with
asphaltic concrete, and partially covered by grass. It appears that the site was
generally flat with surface water ponding in grassy areas. Therefore, minimal site
grading is anticipated.

SECTION 3.0 - DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration indicated that the project site is generally blanketed by a fill
layer consisting of silty clay and cobbles and boulders, extending to the maximum depth
explored of about 15 feet below the existing ground surface. We did not encounter
groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration.
Due to the presence of variable materials ranging from clay to cobbles and
boulders encountered in our borings, we recommend supporting the building slabs and
foundations on at least 2 feet of compacted structural fill for more uniform support. We
envision that the proposed restaurant building may be supported on a shallow
foundation system consisting of spread or continuous footings.
Our geotechnical engineering recommendations for the design of foundations,
slabs-on-grade, site grading, and pavements are further discussed in the following
sections herein.
3.1. Building Foundations
Due to the presence of variable materials ranging from clay to cobbles and
boulders encountered in our borings, we recommend supporting the building slabs and
foundations on at least 2 feet of compacted structural fill for more uniform support. We
believe that a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and/or continuous
footings may be used to support the new commercial building bearing on 24 inches of
structural fill.
If soft soils are encountered below the 2-foot structural fill layer, we recommend
removing the soft soils to expose the underlying firm materials. The over-excavation
should be backfilled with compacted structural fill. The over-excavation for the
compacted structural fill should extend beyond the outside edges of the perimeter
footing a minimum of 3 feet. The structural fill materials should consist of imported,
non-expansive, select granular materials compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction.

For design of foundations bearing on the compacted structural fill materials, we
recommend using an allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,500 pounds per square foot
(psf). This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third
(1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces.
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by friction between the
bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure acting
against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A coefficient of friction of 0.40
may be used for foundations bearing on the structural fill materials. Resistance due to
passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of
350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf). This assumes that the soils around
footings are well compacted. The passive pressure should be reduced for foundations
located on slopes. Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in the
upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected.
In general, footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the
lowest adjacent finish grades. Footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping
ground should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal set-back
distance of 6 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the slope face.
A Geolabs representative should observe the footing excavations prior to the
placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the foundation bearing conditions
and the required embedment depths.

3.2.1 Site Preparation
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly, if necessary. Vegetation, debris, deleterious
material and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly
off-site. Soft and/or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below
areas designated to receive fill should be over-excavated to expose firm material,
and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fills. The
excavated soft and/or organic soils should be disposed properly off-site and/or used
in landscape areas, where appropriate. Contract documents should include additive
and deductive unit prices for over-excavation and compacted fill placement to
account for variations in the over-excavation quantities.
After clearing and grubbing, subgrades, finished subgrades, and areas designated
to receive fills should then be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches,
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the
in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture
is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry
density.
3.2.2 Fill Materials
General fill materials required to raise the existing ground surface to the proposed
finished subgrades may consist of the excavated on-site soils, provided that they
are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, and rock fragments greater than
6 inches in maximum dimension.
Structural fill required to backfill 24 inches below the building should consist of
granular materials with particle sizes of 3 inches or less in maximum dimension and

with between 10 and 30 percent fines should be free of deleterious materials, and

Aggregate base course materials required for the pavement sections should consist

3.2.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture,
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the
in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture
is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry
density.
Aggregate base course materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.
Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during the site grading
operations to assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction
and the proper moisture content on each fill lift. Where compaction is less than
required, additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture
content, as necessary, to obtain the specified compaction.

3.2.5 Cutand Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration, we believe
that cut slopes for this project will expose stiff clay materials and cobbles and
boulders. In general, the cut slopes planned may be designed with a slope
inclination of two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter.
Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the
existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding. The filling operations
should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers
in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations. Fill slopes should be
constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a
well-compacted slope face. In addition, slope planting should be provided as soon
as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes.
3.3. Slab-On-Grade
We anticipate that a concrete slab-on-grade will be used for the proposed
building. We recommend placing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting
of No. 3 Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) below the slabs. The open-graded
gravel cushion fill would serve as a capillary moisture break and would provide uniform
support of the slabs. To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration and
subsequent damage to the floor coverings, an impervious moisture barrier is
recommended on top of the gravel cushion layer.

In general, granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel
(ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) should be used under the pipes for support of the utility
lines. The initial trench backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of
free-draining granular backfills, such as No. 3B Fine gravel, to reduce the potential for
pipe damage from compaction of the backfill. It is critical to use a free-draining granular
material as the initial trench backfill to reduce the potential for formation of voids below
the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. We
do not recommend the use of clayey soils as initial trench backfill directly around utility
pipes.
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to
the finished subgrade should consist of the approved on-site soils or general fill
materials with a maximum particle size of 6 inches or less. The backfill material should
be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence. The upper
3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to no less
than 95 percent relative compaction. Mechanical compaction equipment should be used
to compact the backfill materials at the project site. Compaction efforts by water
tamping, jetting, or ponding should not be allowed at the site.
3.6 Drainage
As previously indicated, proper surface drainage is critical to the performance of
foundations and slabs. Therefore, the finished grades outside the structure should be
sloped to shed water away from the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding.
It is also advised to install gutter systems around the building and to divert the discharge
away from the foundation and pavement areas. Excessive landscape watering near the
foundations and slabs should also be avoided.
The foundation excavations should be backfilled properly against the walls or
slab edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for water

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling
four borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, extending to depths of
approximately 14.6 to 15 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate
boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. We used a truck-mounted drill rig
equipped with rotary coring tools.
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field. He monitored the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-4.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths.

APPENDIX B

Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) determinations were performed on selected
