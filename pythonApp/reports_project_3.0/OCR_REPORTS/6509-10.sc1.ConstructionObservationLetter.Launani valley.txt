In November 2018 we responded to a reported incident at the condominium complex
involving stormwater derived accumulations of soil deposited along the toe of the slopes
adjacent to Building M At that time we were also requested to check the existing condition
of Boulder No 6 as it was reported to be in a precarious setting on the slope and appears
to be a danger Based on our 2018 evaluation of the boulder condition we observed some
possible encroaching soil erosion around the downslope perimeter of the boulder which
could permit destabilization of the boulder with the passage of time and perhaps allow the
rock to slide forward on the ridgeline facing Building M Despite the rock being rather
tabular shaped and located on a localized flattened area of the ridgeline slope the
encroaching ground erosion was justification for the removal of the potential hazard
A follow up site visit was conducted in May 2019 with Royal Contracting to review
the boulder and assess the appropriate method for controlled removal from the hillslope
Boulder demolition using a rock cracking expansion compound and pneumatic chipping to
reduce the boulder to hand carried fragments was selected
BOULDER DEMOLITION
We observed the rock demolition and removal of the potential rockfall hazard from
the slope above Building M at the Launani Ridge condominium complex between August
12 and 14 2019 The work was performed by Royal Contracting The work was performed
under dry weather and ground conditions The work generally consisted of the mobilization
and the setup of a temporary safety containment system around the rock rock demolition
scattering of rock fragments in the slope vicinity and demobilization from the site
Access to the boulder was via rope assist and a foot trail leading up the nose of the
ridgeline that the boulder was located on A section of steel wire mesh was secured around
the downslope perimeter of the boulder to serve as a containment system for the generated
rock fragments
The rock demolition commenced with the drilling of about 30 1¼ inch diameter
boreholes to a depth of about 12 inches into the boulder top surface The holes were
predrilled with an electric hammer drill using a ½ inch pilot bit The holes were blown free of
dust and 5kg of rock cracking compound was mixed and poured into about 20 of the
boreholes The compound was allowed to harden and expand overnight in an effort to
induce slow cracking within the boulder to facilitate the mechanical breaking into rock
fragments less than 1 to 2 feet in largest dimension
Examination of the boulder the following morning indicated that the compound did
not perform as anticipated No induced rock fracturing was observed and the treatment did
not assist to expedite the mechanical breaking process We observed the mechanical rock
chipping using a 185 CFM air compressor powering pneumatic hammers used in
conjunction with the electric hammer drill to create pilot holes to facilitate the rock chipping

The rock chipping progressed from the top and sides inward toward the boulder core
We observed the rock to be very hard massive blue rock with limited rock vesicularity
therefore the rock chipping was intensive and generally generated tabular and angular rock
fragments that could be carried by hand We observed that the broken rock pieces
generated were generally tabular plate shaped thin and very angular in character The
pieces generated for disposal generally ranged between about 6 and 18 inches in largest
dimension with variable thickness though generally less than about 3 to 6 inches Due to
the platy and irregular shape character of the produced rock fragments a significant rock
volume expansion was observed as a result of the breaking into rock fragments The rock
chipping effort alone took almost 2 days
The basalt rock fragments generated for on site disposal were generally less than
about 15 inches in largest dimension and have a generally flat and tabular fragment shape
The controlled rock demolition resulted in the complete reduction of the single piece boulder
to eliminate the potentially dangerous boulder hazard
The rocks were generally scattered and placed in the vicinity of the rock demolition
work area Some rock fragments were hand carried and placed on existing stable ground
areas with a general flattened slope of about four horizontal to one vertical 4H 1V located
uphill and toward the southwest from the rock demolition site Other rock fragments were
widely distributed on the densely vegetated down sloping terrain facing the gully bounding
the southern perimeter of the demolition site Manual efforts were taken to position the
tabular and angular rock fragments in generally stable ground settings with good ground
contact Care was taken to avoid rock fragment dispersal on the steeper slopes and slopes
facing toward the northern bounding gully including down the ridge fall line toward Building
M of the complex

BOULDER CLUSTER NO 8
At the request of the condominium management we reviewed the existing hillside
condition of a previously documented three boulder deposit referred to as Boulder Cluster
No 8 in our above referenced rockfall potential and slope hazard evaluation report Boulder
Cluster No 8 was initially evaluated for rockfall potential by Geolabs Inc in 2011
Based on our current review of the existing boulders and their slope setting we
observed no visible adverse change in the stable condition of the boulders and no visible
evidence of active or beginning ground erosion that would reasonably approach the deposit
location Based on our observations the average width of the flattened ridge surface in the
vicinity of the boulder cluster is over 20 feet in width The partly embedded subangular
boulders reside on about a 5H 1V slope inclination with very good ground contact and
seating It is our opinion that the existing boulder cluster does not represent a potential

LIMITATIONS

simultaneously on a construction site therefore some work may have occurred without our
observation However we believe the level of construction observation services provided is
reasonable in reducing the geotechnical risks to the project Our services were performed
in accordance with generally accepted standards of geotechnical engineering practice no
warranty is expressed or implied Provision of construction monitoring by an engineer is not
insurance nor does it guarantee construction of any type The contractor shall retain
responsibility for the quality of work and for repairing defects regardless of when they are
found
The findings and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon
information obtained from visual site observations and computer simulation and statistical
analysis of potential rockfall activity only Variation in the surface and subsurface conditions
between our observations and analysis points may occur and the nature and extent of these
variations may not become evident until additional field exploration or construction is
underway If variations then appear evident it will be necessary to re evaluate the findings
and recommendations provided herein

natural processes weathering wet dry and hot cold cycles and erosion conditions the
potential for rockfall hazard at any site changes with the passage of time Therefore the
