Existing Site Conditions
Based on our reconnaissance of the project site generally shallow depth basalt rock
conditions were observed throughout the site Some stiff silty and clayey light colored
residual and saprolitic soils were observed at the northern portion of the project site near
the top of the ridgeline The saprolitic soils were observed to grade with highly weathered
basalt rock in exposed slope cuts
Elsewhere the surface soils were observed to generally consist of dark brown
adobe clay overlying shallow depth basalt rock Based on observations of the ground
surface within the residential lots the clay soils appear to range generally between 2 and
5 feet in thickness with widespread areas of exposed basalt rock comprising the ground
surface The thicker surface soil deposits appear to be concentrated at the western and
southern corners of the project site where existing grades are less steep The thinner
surface soils and exposed basalt rock condition is more prevalent at the northern central
and eastern portions of the project site

Based on our laboratory testing the dark brown clay soils encountered at the project
site may have a moderate to high shrink swell potential when exposed to moisture
fluctuations as evidenced by the highly plastic properties of the soils Observation of some
existing retaining walls and other fixed structures located throughout the site exhibit
evidence of some deformation possibly related to the expansive soil character and slow
soil creep movement in response to gravity acting upon the hillslopes In addition
desiccation cracks were observed in the dark brown soils during periods of dry weather
The basalt rock observed at existing slope cuts located in the backyard and roadway
areas of the project site appears to be layered pahoehoe with some interbedded thin
clinker seams Often the rock outcroppings exhibit a stepped character of steep rock
banks separated by flatter slope segments containing some alluvial and colluvial soil
accumulation
Based on our observation and a review of the available construction plans for the
Papakolea development it appears that the existing roadways were constructed mostly in
cut conditions that followed the general trend of the existing topography Areas of
significant fill placement appear to be less extensive In general it may be anticipated that
the roads paralleling the slope contour were constructed by cutting of the inside of the
slope and filling along the outer edge to create the road base Therefore shallower soils
overlying basalt rock may be anticipated at the inside edge of the existing roadways that
parallel the contour of the slope Thicker fill materials may be encountered more frequently
along the outer edge of the roadways
Observation of the general condition of the ground surface within the individual lots
located on sloping ground indicates that only minor site grading had been performed The
slopes appear to be naturally developed with frequent exposure of layered rock formation
and some soil deposits composing the flatter slope segments
Subsurface Conditions
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling eight borings designated as
Boring Nos 1 through 8 that were spread across the project site The borings extended
to depths of about 15 feet below the existing ground surface In addition two bulk samples
of the near surface soils designated as Bulk 1 and Bulk 2 were collected at selected
locations within the project site
Our subsurface exploration indicates that the majority of the project site is generally
underlain by a near surface layer of expansive soils consisting of stiff to very stiff adobe
clay extending to depths of about 2 to 4 feet below the existing ground surface Because
the borings were drilled within the city street right of way the adobe clay soils were
encountered below the pavement and base sections of the roadways

Below the surficial adobe clay soils our field exploration generally encountered
dense saprolitic material and highly to moderately weathered vesicular basalt rock The
depth to the weathered rock materials varied from about 2 to 4 feet below the existing
ground surface The weathered basalt rock extended to the maximum depth explored of
about 15 feet below the ground surface
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field
exploration which was conducted under generally dry weather conditions In general the
site is located well above the level of the basal groundwater table however localized
perched water conditions are possible Furthermore groundwater conditions can vary
significantly depending upon rainfall temperature surface runoff groundwater withdrawal
and other factors
Site Grading
Based on the preliminary information provided the site grading is anticipated to
consist mainly of fills ranging up to about 10 feet in height The proposed fills will be retained
by walls and will serve to flatten portions of the existing sloping ground surface to provide for
the redirection of storm water runoff and drainage control Items of grading that are
addressed in the following subsections include 1 Site Preparation 2 Fills and Backfills
and 3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Site grading operations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Inc It
is important that a representative from our office observe the site grading operations to
evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the site preparation process
and to confirm that the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those encountered in our
field exploration
Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area to receive the select borrow subbase material
should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation organic debris construction
debris and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly
off site or in a designated area
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should
be backfilled with properly compacted select granular fill The soft soils should not be
reused as fill material
The subgrade should be scarified a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density

Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend
that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close cracks or prepared again as
recommended above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade
due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft
areas and replacement with well compacted engineered fill
Fills and Backfills
Select granular fill should consist of non expansive granular material such as
crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded
from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and
should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The
material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a
maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1883 The on site clay and silty clay materials are unsuitable for use
as select granular fill
Fills placed on sloping ground steeper than 5H 1V should be properly keyed and
benched into the natural ground to bond the fill to the slope
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optinum moisture content
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Base course and select borrow subbase
materials should be moisture conditioned to near the optimum moisture placed in
level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by vibratory rollers multiple wheel pneumatic
tired rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment Because moisture
conditioning and compaction of soils are critical elements of earthwork observations and soil
density tests should be performed by Geolabs Inc during site grading to assist the
contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper moisture content
Where compaction is less than required additional compactive effort should be applied with
adjustment of moisture content as necessary to obtain the specified compaction

Paved Swale Interceptor Ditch
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by near surface
clayey soils with moderate to high expansion potential when subjected to moisture
fluctuations To reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded concrete
swale ditch resulting from shrinking and swelling of the clayey soils due to changes in the
moisture content we recommend that the subgrade soils be properly prepared and capped
with at least 12 inches of non expansive select granular fill capping fill material below the
concrete swale ditch The capping fill should also extend beyond the new swale ditch a
minimum of 12 inches Since cobbles and boulders were encountered at various depths
below the existing ground surface across the project site the 12 inch capping fill will also
serve to provide more uniform support for the swale ditch that may otherwise bear directly
on the cobbles and boulders
The non expansive select granular capping fill should be placed as soon as possible
after the subgrade soils are properly prepared The prepared capping fill and the underlying
subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist prior to the final placement of concrete
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that
the soils be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks Saturation and subsequent yielding
of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted engineered fill

Where the swales may be subjected to vehicular traffic or equipment operation an

Utility Lines
We understand that underground utility lines drainage will be required for the
proposed project It is anticipated that most of the trenches for utilities will be excavated
in the near surface soils and or hard basalt rock Based on the boring information it is
anticipated that the shallow excavations will encounter expansive clay soils The
expansive clay soils should not be reused as trench backfill
In general good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and
backfilling of the trenches for the proposed utilities The method and equipment to be used
for excavation should be determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety
considerations The excavation should comply with the applicable local state and federal
safety requirements Trench shoring design and installation should be the responsibility
of the contractor Trench shoring and bracing should also conform to the appropriate
health and safety requirements
In general we recommend that granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No
3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under the pipes for support of the
utility lines The initial trench backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of
free draining backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging the
pipes from compaction of the backfill It is critical that a free draining granular material be
used as the initial trench backfill to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Use of
clayey soils as initial trench backfill immediately around utility pipes is not recommended
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to the
finished subgrade should consist of the approved on site soils or general fill material The
backfill material should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground
subsidence The upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction Mechanical compaction
equipment should be used to compact the backfill materials at the project site Compaction
efforts by water tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed considering the
expansive soil conditions at the site
