PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is situated at the Natural Energy Laboratory of Hawaii Hawaii
Ocean Science and Technology Park NELH HOST at Keahole Point in the district of
North Kona on the island of Hawaii An open disposal trench has been constructed to
handle mixed sea water flows of approximately 17 000 gallons per minute from the
Department of Energy Net Power Producing Experiment NPPE
In keeping with the current groundwater monitoring program in place at the facility
it was desired to design and install groundwater monitoring wells in the vicinity of the
disposal trench to monitor the impacts of the disposal activities on the groundwater below
the site and to aid in monitoring the impacts on the adjacent coastal waters
Since the site is underlain by a thin stratified Gnyben Herzberg lens it was
recommended that the monitoring wells be constructed as clusters with varying
completion depths to allow discrete monitoring of different intervals of the lens This was
similar to the existing monitoring wells currently being used in the NELH HOST complex
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our work on the project was to design drill and install
groundwater monitoring wells for the NPPE disposal trench and to provide documentation
of the construction of the wells for inclusion in the NELH HOST groundwater monitoring
program

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The project site is located on the western flank of Mt Hualalai one of the five 5
volcanoes comprising the island of Hawaii Hualalai is generally considered to be the
third oldest of the volcanoes having an age ranging from the late Pleistocene to Recent
with its most recent activity around the year 1801 Since 1801 Hualalai has been
dormant but occasional earthquakes suggest it remains capable of erupting to this date
The project site is situated to the south and west of the main northwest southeast
trending rift zone of Hualalai This rift zone forms a structural and hydrological boundary
which is expressed physically at the surface as a line of small cinder and spatter cones
trending to the northwest from the summit area
Subsurface Geologic Conditions
In general Well Nos 7 and 8 penetrated 25 to 35 feet of relatively dense to very
dense pahoehoe basalt This upper flow unit appeared to be widely fractured with few
highly vesicular or clinkery zones Beneath this dense surface layer the wells generally
encountered clinker or closely to severely fractured pillow lavas and littoral scree deposits
of high porosity These deposits extend from a depth of approximately 35 to 60 feet
below the existing ground surface This clinkery zone is believed to have formed by
molten lava entering the ocean and causing steam explosions which broke the lava at the
flow front into sand and gravel sized fragments This pillow lava and scree deposit then
formed a base for the lava flow which continued to advance seaward covering the
fragmental material Below this littoral deposit at a depth of approximately 57 feet below
the existing ground surface Well No W 8 encountered relatively dense vugular basalt to

Hydrologic Conditions
Groundwater moves from areas of recharge to areas of discharge in response to
the driving force of the hydraulic head imposed by the recharge Generally the project
site is considered to be in a discharge area since the groundwater moves from the
recharge area on the upper slopes of Hualalai to discharge at the coastline
Tidal fluctuations observed in previously drilled wells at the project site and
previous HOST Park pumping station construction dewatering efforts indicate that the
subsurface rocks underlying the site are highly permeable and that the flux of the
groundwater below the site is rapid Test data from the disposal trench indicates that the
net permeability of the upper lava flows is on the order of about four hundred feet per
day It is estimated that the permeability of the underlying littoral deposit is much greater
Generally in volcanic aquifers groundwater flow and solute transport is
stratigraphically controlled Preferential flow paths occur in voids within and between the
lava strata in fractures and clinker strata Installation of monitoring wells with continuous
screened intervals may yield spurious data and may also cause cross contamination
between discreet flow tubes within the aquifer Therefore we proposed to construct a
cluster of wells with short screened intervals at each monitoring site This would allow
more discreet sampling of the individual stratigraphically controlled flow paths at each
monitoring site
DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Well Locations
The general locations of the monitoring wells were chosen by representatives of
NELH HOST Subsequently the sites were adjusted somewhat in the field to improve

accessibility of the monitoring wells during installation and during the later monitoring
program The approximate locations of the wells are marked on the Site Plan Plate 2
The actual locations should be surveyed to establish their true horizontal and vertical
locations
Well Drilling and Installation
Our effort consisted of drilling and installing six 6 monitoring wells to depths of
20 to 65 feet to monitor separate and distinct zones within the groundwater column
These six 6 wells were installed as two 2 clusters of three 8 wells each The
approximate well locations are plotted on the Site Plan Plate 2 Well and 7B
were installed downgradient of the disposal trench to attempt to monitor the plume of
mixed seawater from the trench Well and 8B were installed upgradient of the
trench to attempt to provide baseline and background data on existing groundwater
conditions
Well Nos 7 and 8 were drilled using a truck mounted rotary drilling machine
advancing HQ wireline coring tools By utilizing this method rock core samples were
recovered to aid in the logging of subsurface materials encountered These pilot holes
were then enlarged utilizing a downhole air percussion hammer for the installation of the
well The remaining monitoring wells in each well cluster were drilled entirely by the air
percussion method
The wells were drilled and constructed in general accordance with current EPA
guidelines governing the installation of monitoring wells The wells were also constructed
in such a manner as they are compatible with the existing monitoring program and
sampling equipment In addition barrier posts were installed around the well clusters to
minimize the possibility of accidental damage to the well heads

Studies of monitoring well construction have shown that monitoring wells may be
contaminated during construction and thereby provide false or spurious base line data
Potential sources of contamination are drilling fluids and the materials and methods used
in development Therefore potable water and compressed air were used as drilling fluids
to minimize the potential introduction of contaminants during drilling The drilling tools
were decontaminated by washing with a trisodium phosphate solution followed by a
potable water rinse prior to the commencement of drilling
At each of the two 2 well cluster locations an initial well was cored to a depth of
about 60 feet below the average groundwater table to obtain rock core samples for
description of the formation characteristics Two 2 additional shallow wells of variable
depths were drilled next to each of the deeper pilot holes Two inch PVC well casings
were installed with the screened portions isolated in discrete zones in order to sample
All wells were packed with clean well graded gravel and sealed using bentonite and
cement grout Concrete wellheads with steel locking caps were constructed for each well
to provide protection from potential tampering or vandalism In addition steel barrier
posts were installed around the well clusters for protection from vehicular traffic
Additional Monitoring Wells
We recommend that additional monitoring wells be added to the groundwater
monitoring program as the development of the HOST Park NELH complex continues in
the future Particular attention should be given to potential point sources of groundwater
contamination such as disposal trenches and tenants who mobilize large volumes of
water through their facilities Attention should also be given to tenants who develop high
tech facilities which may use solvents or other chemicals and materials that may have
potential impacts if released into the environment

LIMITATIONS
The analysis and recommendations submitted in this report are based in part upon
information obtained from field data points such as borings and or observation wells
Variations of conditions between the field data points may occur and the nature and
extent of these variations may not become evident until the actual monitoring program is
implemented If variations then appear evident it will be necessary to re evaluate the
findings and recommendations given in this report
The location of the field data points were approximately determined by taping from
previously surveyed points Elevations of the data points were approximately determined
from the Site Plan The physical location and elevation of the field data points should be
considered accurate only to the degree implied by the method used
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the field data points
depict the approximate boundaries between rock soil types and as such may denote
a gradual transition The strata lines shown on profiles or cross sections are based upon
interpolation between field data points and may not represent actual subsurface
conditions
Water level data from the field data points was collected at the times given in the
text of this report This data has been reviewed and interpretations made in the
formulation of this report However it must be noted that fluctuation may occur due to
variations in rainfall tides temperature and other factors

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by the drilling and sampling
of two 2 borings to depths of about 60 0 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 3 The borings were drilled by a
truck mounted rotary drill rig advancing wireline core drilling tools
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the annulus of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination classification and testing
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a five foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material that is in excess of four 4 inches in length without any discontinuities
discounting any drilling induced fractures or breaks if 2 5 feet of sound material is
recovered from a five foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown
on the Boring Logs as REC 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative
quality based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
Very Poor 0 25
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100
The materials encountered in the borings were examined and classified in general
accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2488 84 Description and Identification of
Soils Visual Manual Procedure by a geologist from our firm The logs of the borings
After the drilling of the exploratory borings the holes were reamed using air rotary
percussion drilling tools Groundwater monitoring wells were installed in the boreholes
The remaining wells were drilled by the air rotary percussion method
