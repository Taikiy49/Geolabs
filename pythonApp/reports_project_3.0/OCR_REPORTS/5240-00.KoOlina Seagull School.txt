SECTION 1.0 - GENERAL
1.1. Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Ko’Olina Seagull School project located along Aliinui Drive
(TMK: 9-1-56: 16) in Ewa on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and
engineering analyses. These recommendations are intended for the design of
foundations, site preparation, and pavements only. The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report.
1.2. Project Considerations
The project site is located at Al’inui Drive in Ewa on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.
Based on the Topographic Survey Map, the project site slopes downward from about
Elevation +28 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) on the north side to about Elevation +23 feet
MSL on the south side of the project as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
The parcel is approximately 1 acre in area. It is proposed to construct buildings
and parking facilities on one-half of the property. The project consists of one
administration building and three classroom buildings around an open space area, a
covered multi-purpose area, a 22-stall parking lot, and other site improvements.
A grading plan was not available at the time of our field exploration; however, we
anticipate that site grading would be limited to a few feet of fill.

SECTION 2.0 - SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2.1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from
two shield volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau. The older volcano, Waianae Volcano, is
estimated to be middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be
late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age. As the volcanic activity in the Waianae
Volcano ceased, lava flows from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern
slope forming a broad plateau, now known as the Schofield Plateau. The project site is
located on the southwestern flank of the Waianae Volcano.
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of Schofield Plateau
generated sediments, which were transported to the coast. In the vicinity of the project
site and to the south, these sediments accumulated and are interbedded with marine
sediments and coral/algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge. The thickness
of the sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H-1
Highway to over 1,000 feet at Ewa Beach. This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves
as the confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of southern
Oahu. Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the
present.
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea.
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that
the sea levels rose to above the present sea.
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef
deposits at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine
sediments in the quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused

SECTION 3.0 - DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the subsoil conditions encountered at the project site, we believe that
shallow foundations consisting of spread and/or continuous footings bearing on
compacted structural fill may be used to support the proposed building.
Based on our laboratory tests, the in-situ clayey soils exhibit moderate to high
shrink/swell potential when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents. To
reduce the potential for distress to the future buildings resulting from the swelling of the
near-surface soils, we recommend that the on-site clayey soils be capped with a
minimum of 24 inches of non-expansive structural fill (capping fill) material below slab
and foundation areas. In general, the subgrade soils should be compacted to provide a
relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to the placement of fill material. The
slab subgrade should be kept moist and free of shrinkage cracks prior to placement of
capping fill, cushion fill, and/or slab concrete. Our geotechnical engineering
recommendations pertaining to the design of foundations, retaining walls, site
preparation, and pavements are further discussed in the following sections of this
report.
3.1 Building Foundations
Based on the generally stiff subsoil conditions encountered at the site, we
believe that shallow spread and/or continuous footings may be used for support of the
proposed buildings. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,500 pounds per square
foot (psf) may be used for the design of shallow foundations bearing over the structural
fill. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by
one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces.
If soft and/or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing
over-excavations, the material should be over-excavated to expose the underlying firm
materials. The resulting over-excavations should be backfilled with on-site material to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.

Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing structural fill and by
passive earth pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system.
A coefficient of friction of 0.35 may be used for footings bearing on the structural fill
material. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf). This
assumes that the soil around footings is well-compacted. The passive pressure should
be reduced for foundations located on slopes. Unless covered by pavements or slabs,
the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected.
In general, footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the
lowest adjacent finish grades. Footings located adjacent to retaining walls should be
embedded deep enough to avoid surcharging the retaining wall foundations, or the
retaining walls should be designed to resist the additional structural loads.
Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded
below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility
trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This requirement is necessary to avoid
surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce
the potential for foundation settlement.
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for
footings bearing on the compacted structural fill material over the in-situ stiff clayey soils
as recommended herein to be less than 1 inch total with differential settlements on the
order of 0.5 inches.
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative from
Geolabs prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation
bearing conditions and the required embedment depths.

3.2 Slabs-On-Grade
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by clayey
soils with moderate to high expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations.
To reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs-on-grade resulting
from shrinking and swelling of the clayey soils due to changes in the moisture content,
we recommend that the subgrade soils be properly prepared and capped with at least
24 inches of non-expansive structural fill (capping fill) material below the slab cushion.
The non-expansive structural capping fill should be placed as soon as possible
after the subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented
in the “Site Preparation” section of this report. The prepared capping fill and the
underlying subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist prior to the final placement of
concrete. Where shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade, we
recommend that the soils be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks. Saturation and
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor
drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well-compacted engineered fill.
For interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic, we recommend that a
minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33,
No. 67 gradation) be placed below the slabs to serve as a capillary break and to provide
more uniform support of the slabs. To reduce moisture infiltration and subsequent
damage to floor coverings, an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the
No. 3B Fine gravel. Flexible floor coverings, such as carpet or sheet vinyl, should be
considered since they can better mask minor slab cracking.
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project. Since the
walkways may tolerate some movement, we recommend that concrete walkways be
supported on 24 inches of non-expansive structural fill. The structural fill should be
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Control joints should be provided
at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at right-angle
intersections.

3.4 Retaining Walls
Retaining walls will be constructed as part of the site improvements. The
following general guidelines may be used for preliminary design of retaining structures.
3.4.1 Wall Foundations
In general, we believe that retaining wall foundations may be designed in
accordance with the recommendations presented in the “Building Foundations”
section of this report. It is our opinion that foundations for the building retaining
walls should also bear on compacted structural fill as recommended above. Wall
footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches. Wall foundations on relatively
flat areas should be embedded a minimum depth of 24 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade. For sloping ground conditions, the footing should extend
deeper to obtain a minimum 8-foot setback distance measured horizontally from
the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope. Wall footings oriented
parallel to the direction of the slope should be constructed in stepped footings.

Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic
pressures behind the walls. A typical drainage system should consist of about
1 cubic foot of permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33,
No. 67 gradation), wrapped with non-woven, filter fabric at each of the weephole
locations. The weepholes should be spaced not more than 6 feet apart. Unless
covered by concrete or asphaltic concrete, the upper 12 inches of backfill should
consist of relatively impervious materials, such as the on-site soils, to reduce the
potential for significant water infiltration behind the walls.

3.5 Site Preparation
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of off-site. Soft and yielding
areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill
or future development should be over-excavated to expose firm material, and the
resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted engineered fill. The
excavated soft and/or organic soils should be properly disposed of off-site. Contract
documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over-excavation and
engineered fill placement to account for variations in the over-excavation quantities.
After clearing and grubbing, the areas within the future building and pavement
limits should be over-excavated, where necessary, to provide the minimum 24-inch
thick layer of non-expansive structural fill material. The over-excavated subgrades
should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least
2 percent above the optimum moisture content, and recompacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density
of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures. Optimum moisture is
the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry
density.
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade, we
recommend that the subgrade soil be prepared again as recommended above.
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather
and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well-compacted fill. The need for over-excavation due to soft subgrade soil conditions
should be evaluated by a representative from Geolabs in the field.
If the subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade preparation, the pumping
subgrade may be stabilized by cement treatment. As a guide, one sack of cement may
be used for approximately 25 square feet of subgrade area. Alternatively, the subgrade

Site grading operations should be observed by a representative from Geolabs. It
is important that a representative from our office observe the site grading operation to
evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the scarification
process and whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar to those encountered
in our field exploration.
3.6 Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines may be required for the proposed project. In
general, for support of the utility lines, we recommend that a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) be used
under the pipes. Free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel
(ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), should also be used for the trench backfill up to about
12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce
the compaction effort of the backfill, thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes.
It is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of
voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the
pipes. Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to
the pipes.
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of select fill material. The
backfill should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in
maximum 8-inch level loose lifts, and mechanically compacted to not less than
90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence.
Where trenches are below pavement areas, the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below
the pavement finish grades should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative
compaction.

3.7. Drainage
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding. It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas. Excessive landscape watering near the
foundations and slabs should also be avoided. Planters next to foundations should be
avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water
infiltration into the subsoils. Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible
and maintained to drain surface run-off away from the slabs and foundations.
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent loss of strength and/or heaving of the near-surface clayey
soils. The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings
immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration.

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
five borings to depths of about 11.5 and 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The
approximate locations of the drilled borings are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The
borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers.
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer, who monitored the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-5.
Samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by
driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound hammer falling
30 inches. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches
of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths.
