SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered a pavement structure consisting of about 3 to 
5 inches of asphaltic concrete pavement underlain by 4 to 10 inches of base material 
consisting of medium dense sandy gravel. The pavement structure generally was 
underlain by a surface fill layer consisting of soft to very stiff silty clays and clayey silts, 
and loose to dense silty sands extending to depths of approximately 0.9 to 9 feet below 
the existing pavement surface. 
The surface fill generally was underlain by residual soils and saprolitic materials 
extending to depths of about 2 to 17 feet below the existing pavement surface. The 
residual soil and saprolite layers generally were underlain by basalt formation with 
interbedded layers of clinkers in the borings located at Site No. 4, and Site Nos. 7 through 
12. The surface fill generally was underlain by colluvial deposits in the borings located at 
Site Nos. 1 through 3, 5, and 6. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. It should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to 
rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
Based on our field observations, 12 areas of potential slope hazards were 
identified. Pavement distress generally was noted at each potential slope hazard location 
along the makai side (in the direction of the ocean) of the roadway. The pavement distress 
consisted of a series of longitudinal cracks, which may be evidence of lateral ground 
movement along the lower (or fill slope) side of the roadway. 
Slope stability analyses were performed  on the existing slopes to evaluate the  
potential for slope instability. Based on the results of the global slope stability analyses 
(overall stability of the entire roadway section), the existing conditions at the identified 
potential slope hazard sites generally have minimum factors of safety of at least 1.5, which 
is the allowable factor of safety generally accepted for long-term stability. Site No. 10, 
yielded localized areas of yielding slopes along the lower (or fill side) of the roadway with 
a minimum static factor of safety of approximately 1.1, which is less than the allowable 
factor of safety of 1.5 for long-term stability; and a minimum factor of safety of  0.9 during 
a moderate seismic event, which is less than the desirable minimum factor of safety of 
1.2 for seismic slope stability. 
To increase stability and reduce further creep movement of the roadway, we 
recommend reconstruction of the existing pavement structure and incorporating a 
stabilization layer consisting of a reinforcing geogrid (such as Tensar TriAx Geogrid TX7 
or equivalent) over a permeable separator (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) between the 
aggregate base course and the underlying recompacted subgrade. Prior to placing the

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed in support of the Roadway Slope Stabilization Evaluation project at the Ka`ena 
Point Satellite Tracking Station (KPSTS) on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project 
location and general site vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.  
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses. These findings 
and geotechnical recommendations are intended to determine the probable causes of the 
pavement distresses, evaluate the global roadway slope stability, and assist in the 
remedial design of the distressed pavements against further creep movement. The 
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at 
the end of this report.  
1.1 
Project Considerations 
Based on the information provided, a major construction project requiring the 
trucking of heavy equipment and supplies along the existing access road is planned within 
the KPSTS. Due to the importance of the existing KPSTS access road, it is desired to 
perform a structural evaluation of the existing asphaltic concrete pavement and to 
evaluate the global stability of the existing roadway. The KPSTS access road is 
approximately 3.42 miles long with an entrance located off the northern terminus of 
Farrington Highway (State Route 93) near Yokohama Beach.  
A geotechnical  exploration program (including visual reconnaissance and 
subsurface borehole drilling) was conducted along the existing KPSTS access road to 
evaluate the existing site conditions and to identify areas of  potential slope stability 
hazards. To obtain the information needed for the slope stability evaluation, 22 test 
borings were drilled and sampled to depths of about 50.5 to 102 feet below the existing 
ground surface at 12 selected areas along the existing access road.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age (Ice Age) and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. Waianae Volcano became 
extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried 
below Koolau lava banking against its eastern flank. 
The Waianae and Koolau volcanic shields were built during the late Pliocene and 
early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly-bedded basaltic lava flows. The main shield-building 
activities ceased approximately 3.5 to 2.5 million years ago. Evidence from drilled wells 
indicates that the Island of Oahu has subsided as much as 1,200 feet since the cessation 
of this early volcanic activity (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970). 
In general, the two volcanoes were built by the long-term eruption of thinly bedded 
a’a and pahoehoe lava flows, which were erupted from elongated and linear trending 
volcanic vents associated with the prominent rift zones of each of the volcanoes. The 
Koolau lavas banked against the lava flows of the Waianae Volcano to form the Leilehua 
Plateau of the central interior portion of Oahu. The Waianae Mountains, which forms the 
western third of the Island of Oahu, contain the oldest basalt rock formations on the Island 
of Oahu. The project site is situated at the western end of the Waianae Mountain Range.

Based on the International Building Code (2015 Edition), the project site may be 
subject to seismic activity, and seismic design considerations will need to be addressed.

design, and the potential for liquefaction at the project site. 
2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused largely by 
shifts in the tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked 
primarily to volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally 
occurs before or during volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result 
from the underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but 
does not erupt. The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each 
year, but most of the earthquakes are so small that they can be detected only by 
sensitive instruments. However, some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be 
felt, and a few cause minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes (associated with volcanic activity) are most common on 
the Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement

on the Island of Hawaii. Because majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over

Island of Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes

It should be noted that some earthquakes in Hawaii are caused by the burden of 
the island masses overlying the lithosphere, which cause the lithosphere to sag

beneath the islands’ overburden causes some moderate to strong intensity 
earthquakes. The 1973 Honomu Earthquake, Magnitude 6.2 (M6.2), beneath the 
Hamakua Coast of the Island of Hawaii and the October 15, 2006 Kiholo Bay 
earthquake, Magnitude 6.7 (M6.7), were a result of lithosphere adjustment. Both 
of these earthquakes were widely felt throughout the Hawaiian Islands. 
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced numerous earthquakes 
greater than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have 
occurred in the County of Maui. The effects of earthquakes occurring on the 
Islands of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu. For example, several 
small landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake 
of 1938 (M6.8). In addition, some houses on the Island of Oahu were reportedly 
damaged as a result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 (M7+). 
Due to the relatively short period of documented earthquake monitoring in the 
State of Hawaii, information pertaining to earthquakes that were felt on the Island 
of Oahu may not be complete. In general, over the last 150 years of recorded 
history, we are not aware of reported earthquakes greater than Magnitude 6 
occurring on the Island of Oahu. Based on available information, we understand 
that an earthquake of about Magnitude 5.6 occurred on June 28, 1948 in the 
vicinity of the Island of Oahu, possibly along the hypothesized and controversial

2.4.2 Liquefaction Potential 
Based on the International Building Code (2015 Edition), the project site should be 
evaluated for the potential for soil liquefaction. Soil liquefaction is a condition where 
saturated cohesionless soils near the ground surface undergo a substantial loss 
of strength due to the build-up of excess pore water pressures resulting from cyclic 
stress applications induced by earthquakes. When the loose saturated sand 
deposit is subjected to vibration (such as during an earthquake), the soil tends to 
densify and decrease in volume causing an increase in pore water pressure. If 
drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up of pore water 
pressure, the effective stress (internal strength) of the soil is reduced. Under 
sustained vibrations, the pore water pressure build-up could equal the overburden 
pressure, essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to 
behave as a viscous fluid. During liquefaction, the soil acquires a mobility sufficient 
to permit both horizontal and vertical movements, and if not confined, will result in 
significant deformations. 
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained 
sands and loose silts with little cohesion. The major factors affecting the 
liquefaction characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows:

the depths explored and the presence of basalt rock formation. Therefore, the 
potential for liquefaction is not a design consideration at this project site.

3.2 
Seismic Slope Stability Evaluation 
As mentioned above, there is a potential for permanent deformation of the slopes 
following a seismic event with strong shaking at the project site. Newmark proposed a 
method in 1965 that uses both simplistic pseudo-static analysis and sophisticated 
finite-element modeling. The Newmark method of analysis models a landslide as a 
rigid-plastic friction block having a known critical acceleration, the acceleration required 
to overcome frictional resistance and initiate sliding. The analysis calculates the 
cumulative permanent displacement of the block as it is subjected to the effects of an 
earthquake ground motion time history. The calculation consists of integrating those parts 
of the ground motion time history (acceleration versus time) that lie above the critical 
acceleration resulting in a plot of velocity versus time of the sliding block. The velocity 
versus time profile is integrated further to provide a cumulative displacement of the sliding 
block. 
A simplified method of the analysis recommended by Makdisi and Seed (1977) for 
the method suggested by Newmark also was used for the evaluation of the investigated 
slopes. The method is based on the concept that an earthquake produces acceleration 
of the mass of a potential slide surface that causes shear stresses in addition to those 
SECTION 3. DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS  
W.O. 7938-00 
GEOLABS, INC. 
Page 17 
Hawaii • California 
under static loading. The additional shear stresses caused by the acceleration of the 
mass are usually for brief periods of time. This method assumes that shear stresses 
developed that are less than the ultimate shearing resistance of the soil causes elastic 
deformation while the shear stresses that are greater than the ultimate shearing 
resistance causes plastic deformation (permanent deformation). 
Plastic deformation begins to occur at the horizontal acceleration that will produce 
a factor of safety of unity (FS = 1.0). The term for a horizontal acceleration producing a 
factor of safety of unity is the “yield” acceleration. It should be noted that for each period 
of time when the ground acceleration exceeds the “yield” acceleration, permanent 
deformations of the potential sliding mass that occur are accumulated. 
The Newmark method of analysis is applicable to soils that exhibit no significant 
loss of strength due to seismic loading (strain softening). The loss of strength, known as 
liquefaction, is usually the result of the generation of pore water pressure resulting from 
seismic loading. This significant loss of strength usually occurs with loose cohesionless 
soils, such as fine-grained sands. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the 
project site, we believe that the generation of pore water pressure during a seismic event 
should not adversely affect the shear strength of the subsurface materials. Therefore, we 
do not anticipate a significant loss of shear strength during a seismic event for the soils 
encountered at the project site. 
3.2.1 Yield Accelerations 
Pseudo-static slope stability analyses were performed to evaluate the seismic 
slope stability for the project. The pseudo-static analyses calculate the yield 
acceleration (the average horizontal acceleration on a potential sliding mass that 
will produce a factor of safety of unity). The yield acceleration is expressed in units 
of gravity and is assumed to act as a continuous horizontal force at the mid-height 
of the slice. 
Based on our engineering analyses, the yield accelerations of each identified 
slopes were generally greater than the design acceleration, except for Site No. 10, 
where the design peak ground acceleration is greater than the yield acceleration.

3.2.2 Permanent Deformations 
As mentioned above, there is a potential for permanent deformation of the slopes 
at Site No. 10. Using the simplified methods and guidelines suggested by Makdisi 
and Seed (1977), we estimated that the potential total permanent deformation of 
the slopes at Site No. 10 to be on the order of about 0.5 to 8 inches during a seismic 
event of Magnitude 6.75 or greater. 
Typically, total permanent deformations of the slope on the order of about 3 to 
6 inches or less, for roadways adjacent to the slopes, may be considered 
acceptable. Because the potential permanent deformation of the slopes at Site 
No. 10 exceeds slightly the “acceptable” deformations and considering the critical 
nature of the facility at this site, some remedial measures may be appropriate to 
reduce the potential for adverse effects on the roadway following a seismic event 
with strong shaking. 
3.3 
Pavement Distress Evaluation 
Based on the results of our subsurface exploration, our site reconnaissance, and 
available existing topography information, we believe the access road was constructed 
by cutting into the upper hillside and filling the downslope side to create a near-level pad 
for the roadway. Therefore, we believe the fill increases in thickness from the upslope to 
downslope side of the roadway forming a wedge-shaped fill. 
We envision conventional compaction equipment may not have been readily 
available during the access road construction; therefore, the clayey silt and silty clay fill 
materials may not have been compacted to the desired levels appropriate for supporting 
pavements subjected to moderate to heavy vehicular traffic. As a result, the fill materials 
also exhibited reduced shear strength properties when compared to an adequately 
compacted condition. Based on laboratory test results of the fill materials, the fills placed 
along the makai side of the roadway might have been compacted to between about 75 
and 85 percent relative compaction, which is much lower than the typical 95 percent 
relative compaction requirements for roadway subgrades.

3.4 
Pavement Distress Remediation 
To increase stability and reduce further creep movement of the roadway, we 
recommend reconstruction of the existing pavement structure and incorporating a 
stabilization layer consisting of a reinforcing geogrid (such as Tensar TriAx Geogrid TX7 
or equivalent) over a permeable separator (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) between the 
aggregate base course and underlying recompacted subgrade. In general, the reinforcing 
geogrid and permeable separator should extend a minimum width of 5 feet beyond the 
visible distress in the roadway at each of the identified distressed sites, except for Site 
No. 10. We recommend extending the reinforcing geogrid and permeable separator the 
entire width of the roadway at Site No. 10.  
Prior to placing the stabilization layer, we recommend scarifying the subgrade soils 
to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioning the soils to above the optimum 
moisture content, and recompacting to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage 
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. 
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density.  Soft and/or loose materials encountered should be over-excavated 
to expose the underlying firm/dense materials. The over-excavation should be backfilled 
with select granular fill materials, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture 
content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to 
a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. A non-woven geotextile fabric, such as 
Mirafi 180N or equivalent, should be provided below the select granular fill materials. 
To maximize the benefits of the reinforcing geogrid, aggregate base course with 
nominal maximum size of 1.5 inches should be used. In general, the triaxial geogrid will 
interlock with the aggregate base course, resulting in two benefits during initial 
construction and for the life of the project: 1) lateral confinement - increasing the modulus 
of the aggregate base course, and 2) subgrade bearing capacity enhancement.

Select granular materials should consist of either aggregate base course, or 
non-expansive select granular material, such as crushed coral or basalt. The

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling 22 borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 12.2, extending to depths 
ranging from about 50.5 to 102 feet below the existing pavement surface. The borings 
were drilled with a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 4-inch solid-stem augers and PQ 
coring tools. In addition, one bulk sample of the near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-
10.1, was collected at Site No. 10. The borings were drilled at the approximate locations 
shown on the Overall Site Plan (Plate 2) and Site Plans (Plates 3.1 through 3.12).  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-22. 
Some soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance 
with ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch O.D. standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. 
In addition, some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general 
accordance with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
3-inch O.D. modified California sampler with the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample 
depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number 
of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be 
factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. The pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the

Core Recovery may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Core Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. 
For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as Core Recovery = 
74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses.  RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities, discounting 
drilling-induced fractures or breaks.  If 2.5 feet of sound rock material is recovered from 
a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the 
Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%.  Generally, the following is used to describe the relative 
quality of the rock, based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and 
Minerals."

Seismic Shear Wave Velocity Tests 
Seismic shear wave velocity profiling of the subsurface materials at the project site 
was performed using Seismic Cone Penetration Testing (SCPT) equipment. The purpose 
of the seismic shear wave velocity profiling of the subsurface materials was to more 
closely analyze the seismic design considerations for the project. Shear wave velocity 
testing using seismic cone penetration test equipment was performed within two selected 
borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 9, at the locations shown on the Overall Site 
Plan (Plate 2) and Site Plans (Plates 3.1 and 3.9). The seismic shear wave velocity 
profiling was performed at various depths extending to the maximum depths of 
approximately 100.2 and 99.9 feet below the existing pavement surface at Boring Nos. 1 
and 9, respectively. 
In order to conduct the seismic shear wave velocity test in the boring, the test 
boring was advanced utilizing rotary coring methods to the maximum depth of the boring. 
A log of the materials encountered in the boring are presented in the Logs of Borings in 
Appendix A. After the boring was advanced to the maximum depth of the borehole, the 
bored hole was backfilled with 0.25-inch diameter coated bentonite pellets. The temporary 
casing from the coring operations was used as a tremie pipe to place the bentonite pellets 
starting from the bottom and advancing upward. When the bentonite pellets are in contact 
with the groundwater in the borehole, the pellets started to hydrate slowly. As the 
bentonite pellets hydrate, they swell and soften. The probe was then pushed through the 
softened bentonite using seismic cone penetration testing equipment (SCPT). 
The seismic shear wave velocity test consists of hydraulically pushing a 10-ton 
steel electronic subtraction cone with an apex angle of 60 degrees and a projected 
surface area of 1.55 square inches (10 square centimeters) into the bored hole. The cone 
carries a uniaxial horizontal accelerator geophone to detect the arrival of a shear wave 
generated and propagated from the ground surface. The seismic measurements were 
made when the SCPT had stopped, and a shear wave was sent into the subsurface. A 
shear wave was generated at the surface by striking a loaded plank with a switched 
hammer. The propagation time of the wave from the hammer blow to the cone was 
measured at each discrete depth interval. The vector difference of these depths divided 
by the time difference for the shear wave to arrive at the various depths gave provided 
the average shear wave velocity over the depth interval. 
The seismic shear wave velocities measured, and the weighted average seismic 
shear wave velocity calculated for the top 100 feet of the soil profile at the boring locations 
are presented on Plates B-1.1 through B-2.4 in Appendix B. The weighted average shear 
wave velocity was calculated based on the average shear wave velocity method 
described in accordance with Chapter 20, Site Classification Procedure for Seismic 
Design contained in ASCE 7-10 (Minimum Design Loads for Buildings and Other 
Structures).

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Twenty-one (21) Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected 
soil samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The 
test results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths, 
and graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plates C-1 through C-3. 
Seven (7) Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected 
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. In addition, four hydrometer tests (ASTM D422) were conducted on the 
samples to further evaluate the finer grain materials of the soils.  Graphic presentations 
of the grain size distributions are provided on Plates C-4 and C-5. 
Nineteen (19) Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. The test 
results are presented on Plates C-6 through C-24. 
Two (2) Consolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression (ASTM D4767) tests by 
Geotesting Express were performed on selected in-situ soil samples to evaluate the 
drained and undrained shear strength of the clayey soils. The test results, stress-strain 
curves, and the Mohr Circles are presented on Plates C-25 and C-26. 
Thirty-three (33) Uniaxial Compression tests (ASTM D7012, Method C) were 
performed on rock core samples to evaluate the uniaxial compressive strength of the 
underlying colluvial deposits and basalt formation. The test results are presented on 
Plates C-27 and C-28. 
One Modified Proctor compaction test (ASTM D1557, Method C) was performed 
on a bulk soil sample collected at Site No. 10 to evaluate the relationship between the 
moisture content and the dry density of the near-surface soils. The test results are 
presented on Plate C-29.
