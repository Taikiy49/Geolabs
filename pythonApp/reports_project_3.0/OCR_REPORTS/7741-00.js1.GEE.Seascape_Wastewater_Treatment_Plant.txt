REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers 
an area of approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of the 
following five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea (activity during recent

geologic time), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 – 1803), and Mauna Loa and Kilauea 
(both still active). 
The project site is situated on the western flank of Hualalai Mountain, which makes 
up the west-central portion of the island. The lava formation encountered at the site 
appears to be of both a'a and pahoehoe type flows, which spread and ponded as they 
approached the ocean. A’a lava typically is characterized by a porous, rough, and irregular 
flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and 
boulders. Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth, rope-like or billowy surface and an 
internal structure of vesicular (porous) rock. 
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas, especially pahoehoe type 
lava. Cavities form when lavas are still in a molten state, and they represent both lava 
tubes (intra-flow cavities) and blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities). Lava tubes form 
when molten lava drains from the cooling flow, leaving a hollow tube-like structure, which 
may extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow. Blisters and pockets generally 
are smaller in horizontal extent. 
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite 
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions. In general, water is transmitted 
through the porous rock matrix, along joints, fractures and inter-flow contacts, cavities, and 
along clinker layers. Clinker and cavities serve as the primary water transmission features 
because of their high permeability. 
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
The project site is located within the existing Seascape Condominium complex in 
the District of North Kona on the Island of Hawaii. The existing wastewater treatment plant 
is located in the southwest corner of the condominium complex. The project location and 
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
Based on our field observations, the project site is unpaved and graded with 
crushed basalt rock. The center area of the project site contains a large mound of uniform 
gravel that was placed to cover the wastewater treatment tanks. Based on available 
topographic plans, the existing ground surface elevations of the gravel mound range 
between about +356 and +366 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).  The project area surrounding 
the gravel mound contains storage sheds and several equipment pads with ground 
surface elevations range between about +354 and +362 feet MSL.

EARTHQUAKES AND SEISMICITY 
Generally, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the 
tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic 
activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during volcanic 
eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground movement of magma 
that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The Island of Hawaii experiences 
thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so small that they can only be detected 
by sensitive instruments. However, some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, 
and a few cause minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of magma 
are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the Island of 
Hawaii. Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over 90 percent) are related to 
volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes with 
increased distance from the active volcanoes located in the southern portion of the Island 
of Hawaii.

Magnitude 6 (M6+) including the October 15, 2006 earthquakes. Based on information

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
As mentioned above, it appears that the existing tanks were placed within a gravel 
mound that may be susceptible to failure during a seismic event. Available photographs of 
the tank installation obtained by our office indicate that the tanks were not installed on a 
cradle or anchored to the ground. Therefore, the tanks are currently held in place primarily 
by the surrounding loose gravel mound. The loss of the gravel mound during a seismic 
event could result in the loss of the tanks support and movement of the tanks. In addition, 
excavation of the existing gravel mound at the location of the proposed future tank would 
likely disturb the adjacent existing Bio-Chip Reactor No. 1 tank, resulting in movement of 
the existing Bio-Chip Reactor No. 1 tank. We believe that the installation of ground 
anchors and straps to hold down and secure the existing tanks should mitigate these 
concerns and should be evaluated. 
The results of our slope stability analysis of the loose gravel mound and detailed 
discussions and recommendations for the ground anchors, hold-down straps, and 
retaining walls are presented in the following sections.  
Existing Gravel Mound Slope Stability 
Based on the available existing project drawings, the existing gravel mound along 
the eastern portion of the project site is about 10 feet in height with a slope inclination of 
two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V). 
We have evaluated the overall slope stability of the existing gravel mound 
surrounding the existing underground tanks for static and seismic conditions using the 
Simplified Bishop method. The Simplified Bishop method was used to evaluate the 
balance of forces and moments acting within and around the gravel mound. Based on our

Ground Anchor and Tie-Downs 
Based on information obtained, the existing tanks at the project site are 
approximately 10 feet in diameter and 55 feet long, with approximately 30,000 gallons of 
storage capacity and manufactured by the Xerxes Corporation. As mentioned above, the 
existing tanks were placed within a gravel mound that may be susceptible to failure during 
a seismic event. Failure of the gravel mound could result in movement of the existing 
tanks. Therefore, we recommend installation of ground anchors and tie-down straps to 
hold down and secure the existing tanks. 
A ground anchor generally consists of a reinforcing bar, such as rebars or threaded 
bars, placed in a hole drilled into the underlying rock formation and is grouted. We envision 
the permanent ground anchors required to resist uplift loads would derive support 
principally from the bond between the grout and the underlying basalt rock encountered at 
the project site. The bond stress, which is the pullout resistance per unit area of the 
grout/rock interface contact, is variable depending on the type of soil/ground and grout, the 
overburden stress, and the construction procedures used in installing the permanent 
ground anchors. The bond stress used in the design will need to be confirmed during 
construction by proof load testing. 
Based on information provided by the Xerxes Corporation, manufactured tie-down 
straps designed for use with the existing tanks are pultruded fiber reinforced polymer 
(FRP) and rated for an allowable sustained tensile load of 25 kips per strap. Based on the 
tank size and design peak ground acceleration, we envision approximately 6 tie-down 
straps spaced out along the length of each tank will be required to hold the tank in place 
during a seismic event. Each tie-down strap should be connected to two ground anchors 
on opposite sides of the tank, resulting in twelve (12) ground anchors per tank. It should be 
noted that turnbuckles will also be required to secure the tie-down straps. The contractor 
should consult with Xerxes Corporation for recommendations on the tie-down strap 
placement and installation. 
It should be noted that the existing gravel mound is loose and may not support 
heavy drilling equipment without proper ground surface preparation. In addition, we 
understand that most of the existing tanks are located approximately 1.5 to 2 feet below 
the existing ground surface. To prevent damage to the existing tanks, we recommend that 
the drilling subcontractor refrains from driving or parking any heavy equipment over the 
existing tanks. In addition, we understand that the existing tanks are interconnected with 
several utility pipes. The locations of these utility pipes will need to be taken into 
consideration during the layout of the tie-down strap locations and installation of the 
ground anchors. To alleviate some of the potential difficult drilling conditions in the loose

against corrosion.

anchors will be required during construction. A Geolabs representative should be present 
to observe the installation and testing of the uplift load resisting ground anchors. Uplift load 
resisting ground anchors should be proof tested to 133 percent of the design load and 
should be held for at least 10 minutes. 
A specialty subcontractor, experienced in the construction of ground anchors, 
should perform the ground anchor installation. The subcontractor will need to have the 
appropriate equipment and tools to drill through hard basalt rock formation and 
problematic zones of loose uniform gravel.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, extending to depths of about 17.5 to 
21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted and portable drill 
rig equipped with continuous flight augers and/or rotary coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1.  
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described

Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by 
the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). Photographs of the rock cores 
are presented in Appendix C. 
Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. 
For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any 
drilling, mechanical, and handling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material 
is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be 
shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe
