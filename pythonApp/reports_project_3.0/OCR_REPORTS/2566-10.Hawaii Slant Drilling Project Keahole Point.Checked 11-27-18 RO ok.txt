INTRODUCTION
In accordance with your request we have conducted a geotechnical engineering
reconnaissance to provide supportive information for preparation of a Request for
Proposal for directional drilling work to be conducted as part of the subject project
This report summarizes our work on the project and presents our findings pertinent
to the geology of the area of the project site The work was performed in accordance
with our revised fee proposal dated
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is situated at the Hawaii Ocean Science and Technology
Park Natural Energy Laboratory of Hawaii HOST NELH Keahole Point in the District of
North Kona on the island of Hawaii Ground surface elevations within the HOST NELH
facilities range from about 0 feet to about 160 feet above Mean Sea Level
The site is being developed as a technological and industrial area for ocean related
research and commercial activities such as ocean thermal energy conversion and
aquaculture The specific purpose of the effort initiating this report is to implement a
demonstration project of directional drilling technology Directional drilling for pipelines
is being considered as an alternate to shore deployment to reduce logistical requirements
and to provide pipelines which are better protected from hostile coastal environments

PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our work on the project in its current stage was to provide
geotechnical engineering and geological consultation in support of the preparation of the
Request for Proposals for the initial directional drilling efforts in the demonstration project
In order to accomplish these support activities we performed work including but
not limited to the following tasks
1 Review of the available geologic data published and unpublished pertinent
to the site and its vicinity including boring and well logs
2 A limited on ground reconnaissance of the site to make observations from
a geotechnical engineering and geologic point of view
3 Preparation of this report summarizing our work and presenting our
findings
GENERAL SITE DESCRIPTION
The terrain is generally rugged with some steep slopes locally approaching a ratio
of one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V The terrain typically reflects the original surface
of basaltic lava flows in both the a a and pahoehoe forms with pahoehoe being the
dominant land form The ground surface elevations range from about 0 feet above sea
level to approximately 160 feet above sea level with drainage trending towards the west
The site is generally barren rocky land with a vegetative cover that grades from
bare rock to thin grasses with some haole koa keawe naupaka and other dry land
vegetation

Most of the ground surface is exposed in situ basalt rock formations in the
pahoehoe form which is characterized by a relatively smooth rock surface with numerous
hummocks tumuli pressure ridges and cracks In some areas the pressure ridges and
cracks form very sharp topographic relief Grading with heavy equipment such as
bulldozers is generally required to provide access for wheeled vehicles
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The project site is situated on the western flank of Hualalai one of the five 5
basaltic shield volcanoes which built the island of Hawaii Shield volcanoes are
characterized as being low dome shaped mountains built by quiescent eruptions of very
fluid lava The general slope of the flanks of the domes range from about five 5 to
about fifteen 15 degrees above horizontal
Hawaiian volcanic activity is typified by an abundance of flank eruptions along rift
zones or zones of weakness in the earth s crust The northwest rift zone of Hualalai is
situated to the north of the site
In the vicinity of the project site most of the subaerial lava flows are in the a a form
which is characterized by a rough spiny or rubbly surface of fragmental clinker underlain
by a dense core of massive basalt which frequently has a thinner less continuous layer
of clinker below the massive core
Visual observations of exposures of existing road cuts in the vicinity of the project
site indicate that the clinker layers may be as much as ten feet thick with discontinuous
ledges of hard basalt interbedded within the clinker
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2566 10 Page 4
Most of the HOST NELH site is underlain by prehistoric basaltic lava flows of the
Hualalai Volcanic Series which were most likely deposited during the late Pleistocene
Stearns and Macdonald 1946 In the northern portion of the site the historic lava flow
of 1801 1803 reached the ocean The geologic map by Stearns and Macdonald shows
that this historic flow is found only in the most northern portion of the facility However
the exposed lava between the coast and the eastern boundary of the HOST NELH facility
from the vicinity of Wawaloli Beach northward to the mapped boundary of the 1801 1803
flow is very fresh and unweathered It is possible that this unweathered rock formation
may be an early phase of the historic flow which was not included in the mapping
Subsurface Conditions
Portions of the site were previously explored by the drilling of borings These
previous borings were drilled for geotechnical engineering exploration and for the
installation of groundwater monitoring wells
In general the borings were drilled by small rotary rigs such as Mobile B 40
Mobile B 61 and CME 55 rigs using diamond coring methods Both wireline and
conventional core barrels were used The depths of the borings ranged from about 8 5
to about 135 0 feet below the existing ground surface
The majority of these borings were drilled along the main access road into the
HOST NELH facility and in the immediate vicinity of the Natural Energy Laboratory
complex With the exception of the deepest boring Monitoring Well W 1 which was
drilled at the entrance to the park the previous borings encountered what appears to be
a relatively thick flow unit of pahoehoe lava
This flow unit extended from the existing ground surface to depths ranging from
approximately 30 to 50 feet below the existing surface at the individual boring locations
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2566 10 Page 5
It appears that the lava flow ponded when it was deposited creating a ledge of dense
relatively massive rock intercalated with zones of porous and fractured rock
The deeper borings which penetrated the surface flow unit encountered sand to
gravel and cobble sized basalt fragments which appear to be littoral scree deposits of
debris generated by explosions and cavitation resulting from the intense thermal
interaction between the molten lava and the cooler ocean water Examination of
recovered samples of these scree deposits indicate that the grain sizes are generally in
the sand and gravel range with occasional cobble and boulder sized fragments Visual
observations made during SCUBA dive reconnaissances of excavations at the
HOST NELH facility also indicate that the scree deposits contains some pillow lava
The available evidence leads to the conjecture that the Keahole Point area was at
one time an embayment in the coast and that the lava flow exposed at the surface
entered this lower area The underlying scree deposit was formed by the advancing front
of the flow and formed a base on which the flow continued its seaward advance forming
what is now Keahole Point and in this advance covered the scree
The previous borings which encountered the scree indicate that it ranges in
thickness from about 19 feet to more than 34 feet Only one of these wells completely
penetrated the scree deposit to encounter what appeared to be a subaerial basalt flow
The deepest boring Monitoring Well W 1 was drilled near the highway entrance
to the HOST NELH facility at an elevation of about 102 0 feet above sea level
Examination of the cores recovered from this boring indicates that it penetrated subaerial
lava flows through its full depth

During the drilling of these borings cavities were occasionally encountered Most
of these cavities were on the order of one 1 foot or less in height However it is
believed that most of these cavities have a much greater horizontal extent
Groundwater Conditions
Groundwater was encountered in the drilled borings at elevations roughly
corresponding to sea level Based on observed tidal fluctuations in the borings and pump
testing performed in some borings the basalt formation is highly permeable with
calculated coefficients of permeability as high as 750 feet per day It is not uncommon
to encounter basalt formations with coefficients of permeability on the order of 3 000 to
5 000 feet per day
DISCUSSIONS
General
By definition basalt is a volcanic rock formed by the extrusion of magma onto the
surface When the magma is extruded as lava it will move across the surface until it
cools and solidifies This solidification is actually analogous to freezing
The lava when extruded is so hot that there is little or no crystalline structure to
the melt When the lava freezes the groundmass of the resultant rock is highly vitreous
i e contains a high percentage of glass In addition Hawaiian basalts in their molten
state contain a high percentage of entrained gases and fluids These gases and fluids
are actively exsolving from the melt forming bubbles If the melt freezes before the gases
and fluids are completely exsolved the bubbles remain in the rock as vesicles and vugs
If the majority of the gases and fluids exsolve while the lava is still moving the
viscosity and density of the melt increases The surface of the flow begins to freeze into
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2566 10 Page 7
a scoria Dynamic fluid stresses and thermal stresses break up this scoria crust and
move it around the surface of the flow and down the face of the flow where it is covered
by the advancing lava This forms the spiny scoriaceous fragments referred to as clinker
which are characteristic of a a flows The viscous inner core of the flow freezes into a
dense but vitreous rock
Also since basalts are formed by the freezing of an intensely hot melt there are
considerable thermal stresses generated when the melt freezes and cools to ambient
temperatures Relief of these thermal stresses causes jointing in the rock Generally the
cooling does not occur at an even rate therefore the jointing is frequently irregular
Cavities occur in basalt as large bubbles or blisters of gases and fluids and as lava
tubes Lava tubes form when molten lava drains from the interior of a cooled portion of
the flow Cavities range in size from a few inches to large caverns which are tens of feet
in diameter and may extend for miles in length
Because of the porosity of the rock resulting from the vesicles the open structure
of clinker and the jointing of the rock mass basalts tend to be highly permeable The
open cavities also serve as conduits for fluids Therefore basalts are capable of
accepting and transmitting large quantities of fluids
Drilling Considerations
Hawaiian basalts present a unique set of problems for drilling work These
problems are related to the nature structure and certain physical characteristics of the
rocks The following discussions attempt to provide a summary of some of the major
problems encountered or observed during past experiences

Physical Properties
Basalt is considered to be a hard rock Laboratory testing on three 3 core
samples selected from previous borings at the site indicated unconfined
compressive strengths ranging from about 2 400 to about 8 300 pounds per
square inch Review of available literature indicates that a value of about 0 2 is
typical for the Poisson s Ratio of basalt rock
A review of the field notes from some of the borings drilled at the site indicated
coring penetration rates ranging from about seven 7 to over 30 minutes per foot
At other sites in Hawaii we have experienced penetration rates slower than 60
minutes per foot with diamond coring methods
The strength of the rock can be highly variable and may change in a matter of
inches We have encountered very slow penetration rates in the core section of
a a flows followed by extremely rapid penetration in clinker or highly vesicular
pahoehoe rock or vice versa This has been noted to occur within a single five 5
foot core run
Since basalt is highly permeable with fractures cavities and punky zones it can
be considered a thief formation capable of taking large quantities of drilling fluids
Review of the field notes for the borings drilled at the site indicates that circulation
was lost within the first few feet of drilling Clear water was used for the drilling of
these borings The field notes indicate that bentonite or polymer mud was used
for the deeper portions of some borings when the rotary tools began to bind in a
dirty hole or in the scree deposit Circulation was not restored by the use of
muds

Experience at other locations indicates that when using mud rotary drilling
methods the use of lost circulation materials or cementing is frequently required
to maintain circulation or to obtain adequate hole cleaning
In addition basalt has a relatively high unit weight The bulk unit weight of the
core samples tested for this report ranged from about 132 to 178 pounds per
cubic foot This high unit weight may result in a requirement for high rates of
circulation or thick muds for adequate hole cleaning
Physical Characteristics
Since basalt is generally vitreous and contains vesicles vugs and scoriaceous
zones it is an abrasive material Our own experience indicates that diamond bit
life may be as short as a few tens of feet but generally averages about 400 feet of
penetration for a surface set core bit There is also noticeable abrasion wear on
drill rods and casing when drilling in fresh unweathered basalts
Caving problems have been experienced in clinker or debris zones and in highly
fractured lava flows A situation which has been encountered in a a lava flows
when drilling in a clinker zone after penetrating the dense core section is that
clinker fragments may fall into the bore on top of the drilling tools The friction of
the fragments between the tools and the walls of the bore may lock the tools in the
hole and make retrieval of the drill string difficult
The variable strength of the rock also can create drilling problems When
advancing through either hard or soft material the transition to material of a
different strength may be irregular and cause bit deviation

Voids or cavities generally do not present significant problems for vertical drilling
other than loss of circulation and bit deviation However angle holes may require
the cementing or grouting of cavities that intersect the bore at low angles to
reduce the potential of the drill string following the cavity rather than the planned
course

Laboratory Testing
To provide additional data in support of the preparation of this report and for
the use of readers of this report laboratory testing was performed on selected rock
core samples obtained from borings previously drilled at the Keahole Point facility
Three 3 samples were selected by a representative of the Hawaii Slant Drilling
Project team from core samples stored at the site and delivered to our laboratory
The samples were prepared for testing in accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 4543 86 for compressive strength testing and for determination of the bulk
unit weight of the specimens
The bulk unit weight was determined by measuring the dimensions and weight
of the prepared samples in an air dried condition
The samples were tested to determine their uniaxial compressive strength in
accordance with ASTM Test Method D 2938 86
