EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
This basis of design report was developed in support of the use of Horizontal
Directional Drilling HDD methods to install the portion of FM A 1 between Station 17 20
and Station 20 20 which was originally planned to be installed by the open trench
method This portion of the sewer force main is located in the area near Vickers Avenue
and will extend to the northwest to the area near the Hickam Officer s Club This portion of
the sewer force main installation will be referred to as Bore 1
The installation of FM A 1 between Station 17 20 and Station 20 20 using High
Density Polyethylene HDPE pipe will consist of one HDD drive of 300 meters in length
The drive alignment will have an entry angle of about 10 degrees and exit angle of about
8 degrees The maximum ground cover of the drive is about 9 2 meters
Based on the available borings drilled at the project site the subsurface materials
anticipated during the HDD pipe installation consists of medium stiff to hard and dense
surface fill very loose to medium dense coralline deposits hard volcanic tuff stiff to very
stiff residual volcanic tuff and moderately hard coralline limestone Groundwater was
encountered in the borings ranging from about 0 9 to 2 meters below the existing ground
surface which corresponds to about 0 to 0 7 meters Mean Sea Level MSL
The HDPE pipe to be installed using HDD methods must be able to withstand the
loads imposed on the pipe during installation of the pipe and during the service life of the
pipe The short term loading conditions imposed on the pipe include tensile forces and
stresses during pipe installation and the external pressure due to the drilling fluid In
addition the drilling fluid pressure in the borehole should not exceed the hydraulic fracture
pressure of the in situ soil rock formation If hydraulic fracture of the surrounding formation
occurs there is a higher potential for heave and or blowout of the ground surface during
the drilling operations For the long term or service life loading conditions we considered
the external pressure due to earth and live loads ring deflection due to pipe buoyancy
and ring deflection due to earth and live loads In general a 50 year design life was used
in our analyses when evaluating the service life loading condition
Based on the results of our analyses an HDPE pipe with a nominal outer diameter
of inches with a Standard Dimension Ratio of 11 may be used for Bore 1
when installed using HDD methods

This basis of design report presents the results of our engineering analyses
performed for the HDD Option for installation of the proposed Sewer Force Main between
Station 17 20 and Station 20 20 Bore 1 for the FYO2 MCON Project P 701 Shipyard to
the Wastewater Treatment Plant in the Pearl Harbor area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii
The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1
This basis of design report summarizes our general assumptions design
considerations and design analyses from our review of the available geotechnical
engineering reports and literature for the proposed installation of Sewer Force Main
between Station 17 20 and Station 20 20 Bore 1 by HDD methods

SECTION 3 0 DESIGN CONSIDERATIONS
3 1 installation Considerations
The HDPE pipe must be able to safely handle loads imposed on the pipe during
installation The following items should be considered during the installation of the HDPE
pipe
Tension Force
Tensile Stresses
External Pressure Due to Drilling Fluid
Hydraulic Fracturing of the Near Surface Soils
The tension pull back force on the HDPE pipe must overcome several forms of
resisting forces during the pullback sequence The resisting forces during the pullback
sequence include the frictional resistance between the pipe and the borehole frictional
resistance between the pipe and the ground surface capstan effect force which develops
around bends and hydrokinetic drag force of the drilling fluid The most important
consideration during installation of the pipe by HDD methods is the tensile stresses
imposed on the pipe when it is pulled into place through the drilled hole High Density
Polyethylene pipes generally have low tensile strength when compared to other types of
pipe materials and may be susceptible to failure during installation Therefore the tensile
stresses acting on the HDPE pipe due to the pullback force should not exceed the safe
tensile stress of the HDPE pipe that is being pulled back
During drilling operations water only or water mixed with bentonite and or polymer
may be used as a drilling fluid During pullback the HDPE pipe may be subjected to
external pressure due to the fluid head of the drilling fluid whether it is water or a mixture
of water and bentonite and or polymer slurry The external pressure acting on the pipe
due to the drilling fluid must not exceed the buckling pressure of the pipe during pullback
Another important consideration during the drilling operations is to determine if the
drilling fluid pressure in the borehole will exceed the hydraulic fracture pressure of the
in situ soil or rock formation The drilling fluid pressure is the sum of the hydrostatic

During the service life of the HDPE pipe long term loads are imposed on the

and considered in our analyses
External Pressure Due to Earth and Live Loads

e Ring Deflection Due to Earth and Live Loads
After installation of the HDPE pipe by HDD methods the drilled hole may deform
considerably or even collapse Drilled hole deformation is normally limited when
arching in the soil is mobilized usually in semi consolidated or consolidated soils
Where arching in the soil develops the earth pressure applied to the pipe is less than
the overburden pressure Typically a depth of cover of at least five pipe diameters
above the top of the pipe would be sufficient to develop arching in the soil In
unconsolidated soils we anticipate only minimal arching in the soil to occur When
minimal arching in the soil is expected to occur the earth pressure applied to the pipe is
equal to the overburden pressure
Ring deflection is caused by the non uniform pressure acting on the HDPE pipe
due to buoyancy or from earth and live loads acting on the pipe Ring deflection
reduces the hydrostatic collapse resistance of the HDPE pipe and also produces tensile
bending stresses in the pipe wall When the HDPE pipe is submerged in drilling fluid
there is a pressure difference resulting from the difference in fluid head at the crown and
invert of the pipe The larger pressure acting at the invert of the pipe causes an upward
deflection toward the crown causing ring deflection due to buoyancy Ring deflection
due to earth and live loads occurs when soil deforms near the crown of the pipe
causing load to be applied to the crown of the pipe An opposite reaction occurs at the
invert of the pipe Because the drilling fluid does not provide side support for the pipe

External Pressure Due to Hydrostatic Pressure of Drilling Slurry
The drilling slurry within the annular space of the pipe and borehole exerts an
external hydrostatic pressure on the pipe This hydrostatic pressure is equal to
the height of the slurry head multiplied by the density of the slurry The
maximum height of slurry corresponding to the maximum hydrostatic pressure
was used in our analysis and is presented in Appendix B
Drilling Fluid Pressure versus Hydraulic Fracture Pressure
During drilling operations the drilling slurry exerts a pressure in the borehole If
this pressure exceeds the hydraulic fracture pressure of the in situ soil rock
formation then heave and or blowout of the ground surface may occur Drilling
fluid pressure is determined from the sum of the hydrostatic pressure and
circulation pressure of the drilling fluid The hydraulic fracture pressure of the soil
is the sum of the horizontal in situ stress of the soil and the shear strength of the
soil The computer output of the bottom hole pressure versus hydraulic fracture
pressure of the soil along the HDD alignment is presented in Appendix E
External Pressure Due to Earth and Live Loads
We are not aware of published reports that provide acceptable methods of
calculating the earth loads imposed on HDD installed pipes To estimate the
earth pressure on an HDD pipe a conservative method to estimate the earth
pressure on a jack and bored pipe developed by Stein et al 1989 may be used
This external pressure was compared to the critical buckling pressure of the pipe
using a factor of safety of 2 These calculations are presented in Appendix B

Magnetic Guidance System
Sharewell s new Magnetic Guidance System MGS is
a directional steering system that provides real time
information for the Horizontal Directional Drilling Industry
The MGS provides the contractor with known
positioning by producing immediate information on tool
face azimuth horizontal angle and inclination vertical
angle This data is processed at the surface by steering
software to provide information on depth course length
and distance off a predetermined azimuth
Magnetic Guidance System
The MGS system has five main components A probe
driller s console interface unit computer and printer
The probe is located in a non magnetic bottom hole
assembly that typically includes a collar orientation sub
and a bent sub with a bit when jetting or a mud motor
Information from the probe is sent up to the surface via
a single conductor wireline The information is processed
at the interface unit and simultaneously transmitted to
the driller s console and computer to provide continuous
information on the borehole orientation

TruTracker
Surface Monitoring Signal Inducing System
Sharewell s TruTracker System provides an
accurate determination of the location of the drill bit
during drilling operations even in areas of magnetic
interference
This precise location of the drill bit is independent of
the driller s survey calculations Information provided is
not affected by limited amounts of outside magnetic
interference
TruTracker is designed for application in pipeline
river crossing utility and environmental drilling It is
used in conjunction with Sharewell s downhole Magnetic
Guidance System MGS TruTracker is a surface
monitoring and signal inducing system which
communicates with the downhole MGS probe to
determine the probe s position
The TruTracker System allows the driller to operate
with known positions and remain on course in areas
which previously had to be drilled blindly
Boring with critical exits can now be performed with
confidence when using the TruTracker System

MEASURING HANDLING DISPOSAL OF DRILLING MUD
1 MEASURING SPOILS
a SPOILS WILL BE MEASURED BY THE CUBIC FOOT VOLUME IN
REFERENCE TO THE DIAMETER OF THE DRILLING PROCESS
AND BY THE LENGTH THE DRILLING OPERATION HAS
PROGRESSED INTO THE BORE HOLE
b THE SPOILS WILL BE MONITORED AS THEY FALL FROM THE
SHAKER SCREENS AND ACCUMULATE INTO MEASURABLE
PILES
c AS THE SPOILS ARE ACCUMULATED THEY WILL BE
MEASURED AGAINST THE CUBIC VOLUME DRILLED DURING
THE MEASURED DRILL STEM LENGTH
d THE FORMULA TO MEASURE SPOILS WILL BE
3 14 X R2 RADIUS OF CURRENT BORE SIZE X LENGTH
ON THE PILOT BORE OF 10 INCHES THE SPOILS GENERATION
SHOULD BE LF OF BORING THIS WOULD BE THE
MINIMUM AND WITH SOME HOLE COLLAPSE IT WILL
PROBABLY INCREASE IN VOLUME
2 HANDLING SPOILS
a AS SPOILS ARE MEASURED AND REMOVED FROM THE
RECYCLING SITE THEY WILL BE STOCK PILED AT A
DESIGNATED SITE FOR TESTING OF CONTAMINATION
3 DISPOSAL OF SPOILS
a IF THE SPOILS ARE SUITABLE FOR NORMAL HANDLING
THEY WILL BE PLACED IN AN APPROPRIATE LAND FILL
b IF THE SPOILS ARE CONTAMINATED THEY WILL BE
HANDLED WITH IN THE PROPER MANNER AS THE
CONTAMINATION IS IDENTIFIED
4 MEASUREMENT OF DRILLING MUD
a DRILL MUD WILL BE MEASURED BY THE CUBIC FOOT
VOLUME IN REFERENCE TO THE DIAMETER OF THE DRILLING
PROCESS AND BY THE LENGTH THE DRILLING OPERATION
HAS PROGRESSED INTO THE BORE HOLE
b DRILL MUD WILL BE ADDED AS THE BORE ADVANCES THE
MUD LEVELS WILL BE CHECKED ON AN HOURLY BASIS
c DURING THE CHECKING PROCESS THE MUD WILL BE TESTED
FOR PH VISCOSITY AND MUD WEIGHT
d THE FORMULA TO MEASURE DRILL MUD WILL BE
3 14 X R2 RADIUS OF CURRENT BORE SIZE X LENGTH X 7 5
ON THE PILOT BORE OF 10 INCHES THE MUD LOSSES SHOULD
BE 4 125 GAL PER LF OF BORING THIS WOULD BE THE
MINIMUM AND WITH SOME HOLE COLLAPSE IT WILL
PROBABLY INCREASE IN VOLUME
5 HANDLING OF DRILLING MUD
a
b
DRILL MUD WILL BE HANDLED IN A MANNER TO MINIMIZE
SPILLS AND INADVERTENT LOSSES
A VACUUM TANKER WILL BE USED TO TRANSFER AND
HANDLE TRANSPORTING DRILLING MUD TO AND FROM THE
SITE
TRANSFER PUMPS WILL BE USED TO PUMP MUD FROM THE
BORE PITS TO THE MUD RECYCLING EQUIPMENT
6 DISPOSAL OF DRILLING MUD
a
DRILLING MUD WILL BE STOCK PILED IN PORTABLE
STORAGE TANKS OR IN A SLURRY POND AT A DESIGNATED
LOCATION
DRILL MUD WILL BE TESTED FOR CONTAMINATION
CONTAMINATE FREE DRILL MUD WILL BE DE WATERED AND
ALLOWED TO DRY FOR DISPOSAL AS A SOLID
CONTAMINATED DRILL MUD WILL BE DISPOSED OF IN THE
PROPER MANNER AS THE CONTAMINATION DEEMS

HANDLING AND DISPOSAL OF CONTAMINATED SOILS AND
GROUND WATER
1 HANDLING AND DISPOSAL OF CONTAMINATED SOILS
a
AS SOILS ARE EXCAVATED AND REMOVED FROM THE BORE
SITE THEY WILL BE STOCK PILED AT A DESIGNATED SITE
FOR TESTING OF CONTAMINATION
THEY WILL BE PLACED ON HEAVY PLASTIC SHEETING WITH
ENOUGH OVERLAP AS TO CREATE A WATERPROOF
CONTAINMENT AREA
THEY WILL BE COVERED TO PROTECT FROM RAIN AND WIND
AFTER TESTING THEY WILL BE USED AS DEEMED FROM THE
TEST SUITABLE FOR BACKFILL OR HAUL OFF TO A
DESIGNATED DISPOSAL SITE
2 HANDLING AND DISPOSAL OF CONTAMINATED GROUND WATER
a
b
CONTAMINATED GROUND WATER WILL BE HANDLED IN A
MANNER TO MINIMIZE SPILLS AND INADVERTENT LOSSES
A VACUUM TANKER WILL BE USED TO TRANSFER AND
HANDLE TRANSPORTING CONTAMINATED GROUND WATER
TO THE DESIGNATED DISPOSAL SITE
GROUND WATER MAY BE HELD IN A PORTABLE STORAGE
TANK UNTIL TESTING IS COMPLETED AT WHICH TIME
DISPOSAL METHODS FOR SPECIFIC CONTAMINATES IS
GENERATED

CONTINGENCY PLANS
1 FLUID LOSS CONTINGENCY
a
IN THE EVENT OF EXCESSIVE DRILLING FLUID LOSS THE
PROJECT WILL STOP DRILLING AND EVALUATE THE
FORMATION CONDITIONS AT THE POINT OF LOSS RETURNS
LOSS CIRCULATION MATERIALS WILL BE ADDED TO THE
DRILLING MUD TO REGAIN CIRCULATION
THE LOST CIRCULATION MATERIALS USED WILL BE
DETERMINED BY THE FORMATION ENCOUNTERED DURING
THE LOSS
2 HYDRO LOCK CONTINGENCY
a
b
EQUIPMENT PRESSURES WILL BE MONITORED PRECISELY
AND CONTINUOUSLY DURING THE PULLBACK OPERATIONS
WEEPING SUBS WILL BE INSTALLED TO ALLOW FRESH
DRILLING MUD TO BE PUMPED ACROSS THE ENTIRE BORE
LENGTH DURING PULLBACK
MUD WEIGHTS WILL BE MEASURED PRIOR TO PULLBACK TO
INSURE A LOW LEVEL OF SOLIDS THAT CAN CAUSE HYDRO
LOCK
EQUIPMENT OF SUFFICIENT SIZE TO AIDE IN THE REMOVAL
OF THE PIPE FROM THE EXIT PIT SIDE WILL BE ONSITE THE
EQUIPMENT WILL BE USED TO ASSIST THE DRILL RIG IN
PUSHING THE PIPE OUT OF THE BORE HOLE
3 HYDRO FRAC CONTINGENCY
a
THE JOB SITE WILL HAVE VARIOUS ITEMS ON SITE TO
CONTAIN INADVERTENT RELEASE OF DRILL MUD OUTSIDE
OF THE CONTAINMENT AREAS
THE ITEMS WILL BE STRAW LOGS STRAW BALES SAND
BAGS PORTABLE PUMP EMPTY BUCKETS
IF A HYDRO FRAC IS OBSERVED DRILLING WILL STOP
IMMEDIATELY THE FRAC SITUATION WILL BE EVALUATED
TO DETERMINE THE APPROPRIATE MEASURES
SOME MEASURES WILL BE ADDING A LOST CIRCULATION
MATERIAL SUCH AS DIAMOND SEAL COTTON SEED ETC
THE POSSIBLE CHANGE IN DRILLING MUD TYPES COULD BE
ALSO A POSSIBILITY
4 STEERING ISSUES
a
STEERING THROUGH THE HARD FORMATIONS IS NOT A
CONCERN USING A MUD MOTOR WHEN THE MUD MOTOR
ENCOUNTERS THE SOFT MATERIALS IN THE HARBOR WE
WILL USE THE FOLLOWING CONTINGENCIES
1 TURN MUD FLOW TO A MINIMUM
REMOVE AND INCREASE THE BENT SUB TO A
SHARPER ANGLE TO INCREASE DEFLECTION
3 CHANGE THE JET SIZE TO REDUCE FLOW
4 REMOVE THE MUD MOTOR IN INSTALL A JETTING
ASSEMBLY
5 ADJUST THE JETTING ASSEMBLY BENT SUB TO AN
INCREASED ANGLE
5 MAGNETIC INTERFERENCE
a
b
MAGNETIC INTERFERENCE WILL BE MONITORED AND
CALIBRATED FROM LOCAL INTERFERENCE
DURING THE LONG LAND APPROACH THE TRUE TRACKER
WILL VERIFY THE EXACT LOCATION OF THE DRILL HEAD
DURING THE BORE WE WILL COMPENSATE FOR
ANY ERROR
6 PIPE YIELD FAILURE
a
b
c
IF PIPE YIELD IS ENCOUNTERED WE WILL REMOVE THE PIPE
THROUGH THE ENTRY PIT INSPECT THE SITUATION AND
TAKE APPROPRIATE REPAIR ACTIONS
WE WILL HANDLE THE PIPE IN A MANNER NOT TO INDUCE
FURTHER DAMAGE
IF THE PIPE BECOMES SEPARATED IN THE BORE HOLE WE
WILL RETRIEVE THE SECTIONS FROM BOTH ENDS
7 EQUIPMENT FAILURE
a
b
DURING PULLBACK WE WILL HAVE SUITABLE SPARE PARTS
FOR THE DRILL RIG RECYCLER AND PUMP
WE WILL BRING APPROXIMATELY 2 500 FEET OF DRILL STEM
SO WE CAN REPLACE A MAJOR PORTION OF THE DRILL STEM
IF FAILURE ENCOUNTERED
WE WILL HAVE TWO LIFTING CRANES ON SITE TO HANDLE
DRILL STEM
WE WILL HAVE TWO LOADER BACKHOES ON SITE
MOST OF THE EQUIPMENT WE ARE USING IS LESS THAN 1
YEAR IN AGE AND HAS BEEN SERVICED AS REQUIRED

Pull In Applications
When Performance Pipe OD controlled polyethylene pipe or conduit is installed using pull in techniques such
as sliplining insertion renewal horizontal directional drilling pipe bursting plowing and planting the installation
applies temporary stresses to the pipe f installation applied stresses are too great the polyethylene pipe may
be damaged beyond use or its potential service life may be compromised
Polyethylene Tensile Properties
Unlike metals that break almost immediately after yielding polyethylene will elongate several hundred percent
before breaking under tensile load Once the material yields it doesn t require greater pulling force to stretch it
until it breaks That is the pulling load that causes the pipe to yield is about the same as the load that causes it
to break but between yield and break the pipe will stretch out several hundred percent For example 400
elongation means that when the pipe yields 1 foot of pipe will stretch an additional 4 feet but while stretching
after yield the pulling load stays about the same until it drops to zero when the pipe breaks
Polyethylene is sensitive to the length of time the pulling load is applied That is the pulling load that causes
the pipe to yield and break in a few minutes is much higher than the load that causes failure in an hour or a
day This phenomenon is called creep It means that the pipe s allowable tensile load ATL for a half day pull
is lower than the ATL for a 1 hour pull Published tensile strength values are based on short term tests that
take only minutes to run Because pull in installations take longer ATL values must be much lower than the
published short term tensile yield strength The ATL is the allowable safe pulling load that can be applied to a
polyethylene pipe
Lastly as with all thermoplastic materials the strength of polyethylene decreases as the temperature
increases As a result pipe that has been warmed by the sun before pull in has a lower allowable tensile load
Pull In Installation
Weak Link Devices
A weak link or breakaway device should always be used at the leading end of the PE pipe to protect the pipe
from damage if the pulling load gets too high The breakaway strength for the weak link device should be set
so that the allowable tensile load ATL of the pipe cannot be exceeded Indicators such as drilling rig
hydraulic pressure or winch rope pulling force do not show the load at the connection to the PE pipe and do not
provide reliable protection against pull in damage
Allowable tensile loads for setting weak link devices for polyethylene pipe can be determined from ASTM F
1804 Standard Practice for Determining Allowable Tensile Load for Polyethylene PE Gas Pipe During Pull In

Pull In Installation Practices
When pull in tensile loads are at or below the ATL polyethylene pipe has elastic properties That is the pull in
load causes the pipe to elongate a small amount but the material is not permanently damaged and after the
tensile load is removed the pipe shrinks back to its original length To recover from temporary pull in
installation tensile stress effects a relaxation period is required Relaxation periods typically range from 8 to
24 hours Normal installation practice is to pull 3 5 past the exit point and leave 3 5 extra pipe length at
the entry point This allows the pipe to shrink back to its original length during the relaxation period but
afterwards the pipe ends will still be exposed for tie in connections

elongation and breakage Thus monitoring just the pulling load at the pulling equipment does not protect
against pipe damage A good practice is to observe the pipe or conduit at the eniry point as it is pulled in It
should continue to move at a steady pace If pipe movement stops or it s pace is interrupted pipe yielding may
