REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age. As the volcanic activity in Waianae Volcano ceased, lava flows 
from Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau, 
now known as the Schofield Plateau. The project site is in the lower distal flanks of the 
Waianae Range and Koolau Mountain Ranges (Ewa Plain). 
The Ewa Plain is a gently sloping land surface formed by the long-term 
accumulation of alluvial sediments consisting mainly of terrigenous clays and silts with 
some embedded cobbles and boulders derived from the weathering and decomposition 
of basaltic rock residing further upslope. Some pockets of marine sediments and 
coralline detritus materials were intercalated within the alluvial sediments as a result of 
past sea-level fluctuations. 
The Ewa Plain is dissected by various drainage gullies, which emanate in the 
upland areas and discharge at the Middle and East Lochs of Pearl Harbor. With the 
passage of time, some of the incised drainage channels meandered in course and were 
filled-in by more recent soft alluvial sediments. The deeper basaltic rock of the Waianae 
and Koolau Mountain Ranges is the primary aquifer and potable water source on the 
Island of Oahu.  The subsurface geological conditions along the roadway reflect past 
glacio-eustatic sea-level fluctuations, which occurred most substantially during the 
Pleistocene Epoch. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Our field exploration program for the project consisted of drilling and sampling 
five borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, each to a depth of about 16.5 feet 
below the existing pavement surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on 
the Site Plan, Plate 2.  
Our borings generally encountered a pavement section consisting of 8 to 
10 inches of concrete underlain by about 5 to 10 feet of fills. Below the fill layer, we 
generally encountered soft to very soft recent alluvium deposits extending to the 
maximum depth drilled of about 16.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. 
The fills generally consisted of medium stiff silty clay and/or medium dense 
sandy gravel.  The recent alluvium deposits generally comprised of highly compressible 
organic silty and clayey soil.

SUBGRADE STABILIZATION 
Based on our field observation, the first half of the concrete pavements 
(Segment-1) from Station 0+00 to Station 5+50 are generally in a poor condition and is 
experiencing structural distresses, such as settlement, transverse cracks, punching 
failure, etc.  Sections of the settling concrete panels were previously overlaid with 1 to 
2 inches of asphaltic concrete. However, the remaining half of the concrete pavements 
(Segment-2) from Station 5+50 to Station 12+61.09 are in a fair/good condition. 
Based on our field exploration, the concrete pavements in Segment-1 are 
generally underlain by about 5 feet of medium stiff to stiff clayey silt over very soft 
organic recent alluvial soil, while a relatively thicker clayey silt layer about 9 feet was 
encountered under the concrete pavements in Segment-2. 
We believe that the distressed concrete pavement conditions in Segment-1 may 
be caused by unstable subgrade conditions due to highly compressible recent alluvial 
soils encountered at relatively shallow depths of about 5 to 6 feet below the existing 
pavement surface. Therefore, we recommend installing a stabilization layer under the 
concrete pavement subgrade in Segment-1 (Station 0+00 to Station 5+50). The 
stabilization layer may consist of select granular fill with geogrid reinforcement and 
geotextile fabric permanent separator. 
Considering the existing underground utility lines within Hikimoe Street, we 
recommend extending the stabilization layer to about 2 feet below the finished 
subgrade. 
The geogrid, such as Tensar TX140 or equivalent, is a polymeric grid formed by 
a regular network of integrally connected multi-direction tensile elements with triangular 
apertures of appropriate size and shape to allow interlocking with surrounding soil or 
rock to function primarily as reinforcement. The geotextile fabric, such as Mirafi 180N or 
equivalent, should be at the bottom of the stabilization layer as a permanent separator 
between the select granular fill and in-situ soil. 
The geotextile fabric should be placed on the prepared subgrade with a minimum 
12 inches of overlapping. One layer of the geogrid should be placed on top of the 
geotextile fabric covered by the select granular fill with a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction.  Another layer of the geogrid should be placed in the middle of the

PAVEMENT DESIGN 
The pavement structural section presented herein was generally determined 
based on the methodology described in Chapter 3 of the Pavement Design Manual 
dated March 2002.  The Pavement Design Manual was prepared by the State of Hawaii 
- Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Materials Testing and Research 
Branch.  The design procedures for rigid pavements are based on the design 
procedures of the Portland Cement Association “Thickness Design for Concrete 
Highway and Street Pavements.” 
We envision the new pavement design life of 40 years is required.  The design 
traffic data was provided by Fehr & Peers dated November 11, 2016 with estimated 
annual traffic growth of 1 percent, as are summarized as follows.  
Based on a design period of 40 years, the traffic volume, and the truck 
distribution information provided, a Traffic Index (TI) of 15.0 has been determined for 
the roadway reconstruction project.  
Based on our field exploration result and the current design concept, we 
anticipate that the pavement subgrade soils will likely consist of the select granular fills 
or near-surface medium dense sandy gravel for the proposed roadway reconstruction 
project. Therefore, a design subgrade CBR of 20.0 was adopted in the pavement 
analyses for the subgrade materials. If site grading exposes soils other than those 
assumed, additional tests should be performed to confirm and/or revise the 
recommended pavement section for actual field conditions.

The subgrade soils under the new pavement structural section should be 
scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to near the optimum 
moisture, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. 
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density. 
Aggregate subbase course as required should meet the material requirements 
for Subbase Course as specified in Sections 30 of the Standard Specifications for 
Public Works Construction, City & County of Honolulu, September 1986. The materials 
should be moisture-conditioned to above optimum moisture content, placed in 8-inch 
level loose lifts, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
Imported fill materials below the subbase course layer should consist of 
non-expansive select granular material, such as crushed coral or basalt. The material 
should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in 
largest dimension. In addition, the material should contain between 10 and 30 percent 
particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have a laboratory CBR value 
of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in 
accordance with ASTM D1883. The materials should be moisture-conditioned to above 
the optimum moisture content, placed in 8-inch level loose lifts, and compacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Geolabs should test imported fill materials 
for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the 
intended use. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor the pavement subgrade preparation to 
observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and 
scarification process and to confirm whether the exposed soil conditions are similar to 
those encountered in our field exploration.  Modification to the extent of the different 
areas may be required. CBR tests and/or field observations should be performed on the 
actual subgrade soils during construction to confirm that the above design section is 
adequate. 
PAVEMENT JOINTS 
Considering the large areal extent of the rigid pavements for the roadway 
reconstruction project, an adequate amount and pattern of joints should be provided.  In 
general, joints should be provided in rigid pavements for the following reasons: 
1. 
To reduce potential curling and warping stresses in the pavement caused by 
temperature and moisture gradients across the pavement slabs. 
2. 
To reduce and control cracking due to volume changes in the concrete. 
3. 
To reduce damage to immovable structures. 
4. 
To facilitate construction.

PAVEMENT DRAINAGE 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the base, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the pavement 
surface should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry surface water off 
the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. Surface water ponding should not be 
allowed on the site during or after construction. 
Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the 
pavement areas, we recommend extending the curbs a minimum of 2 inches below the 
aggregate subbase layer to reduce the potential for migration of excessive landscape 
water into the pavement section. We strongly recommend a development of good 
shoulder drainage, along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage 
structures, to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration or premature failure of the 
pavements.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
five borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, extending to a depth of about 
16.5 feet below the existing pavement surface.  The approximate boring locations are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with continuous flight augers with rotary coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1.  
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-5. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts 
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
