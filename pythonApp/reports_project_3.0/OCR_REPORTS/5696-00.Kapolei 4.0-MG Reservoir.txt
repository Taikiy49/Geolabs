2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age
and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct
while Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau
lavas banking against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad
plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau
During the later stage of its activity the composition of the lavas from the
Waianae Volcano shifted towards andesitic or trachytic basalts A series of events along
the southerly rift zone formed several composite cinder and lava cones including
Puu Makakilo and Puu Palailai Lavas in these cones are typically very dense with a
platy structure resulting from horizontal jointing The project site is situated on the
southern facing slope of Puu Makakilo
In situ chemical weathering of the cinder and lava formed a mantle of residual
soils that grade to saprolite and then rapidly to weathered basalt rock formation with
depth In general saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is typical of the
tropical weathering of volcanic rocks
Rapid erosion of the residual soils possibly due to deforestation or agricultural
practices has stripped most of the residual soils on Puu Makakilo

2 3 Subsurface Conditions
Our work consisted of a review of available information from a previous exploration
and reconnaissance of the site to observe the current site conditions The approximate
boring locations from the previous exploration are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The previous exploration at the reservoir site generally encountered very stiff
residual clayey silt soils interbedded with saprolite from volcanic clinker and weathered

the existing reservoir at the site Kapolei 215 It should be noted that groundwater
occurrence and groundwater levels may vary significantly depending on seasonal rainfall
time of year and other factors

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the subsurface conditions at the proposed
reservoir site generally consist of very stiff to hard clayey silt residual soils in the
western portion of the site grading to saprolite in the central portion and then grading to
highly weathered basalt formation in the eastern side of the reservoir footprint Based
on our evaluation of the subsurface conditions with respect to the proposed reservoir
construction the primary geotechnical considerations for the proposed reservoir project
include the following
Foundation support for the proposed reservoir
Estimated foundation settlement of the proposed reservoir
Site preparation and grading
Pavement design
Based on the structural loads anticipated and the subsurface conditions
anticipated from this current exploration we believe that a shallow foundation system
consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be used to support the proposed
reservoir structure
Our geotechnical engineering recommendations pertaining to the design of
foundations site grading and pavements are further discussed in the following sections
of this report
3 1 Reservoir Foundations
Based on the structural loads anticipated and the subsurface conditions
anticipated from this current exploration we believe that a shallow foundation system
consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be used to support the proposed
reservoir structure
We recommend that an allowable bearing pressure of up to 4 000 pounds per
square foot psf be used on the stiff in situ material This bearing value is for
those caused by wind or seismic forces
W O INC Page 6
Hawaii California
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by friction developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 40 may be used for foundations bearing on the stiff in situ material
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 400 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the
soils around footings are well compacted The passive pressure should be reduced for
foundations on slopes Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in
the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the
lowest adjacent finish grades Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should
be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom
edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is
necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional
structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with the
recommendations presented herein total settlement of foundations is estimated to be
on the order of about 1 inch with differential settlements of less than about inch
We recommend that a Geolabs representative observe footing excavations prior
to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the foundation bearing
conditions and the required embedment depths

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris deleterious material and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off site Soft and
yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to
receive fill should be over excavated to expose firm natural material and the

and or organic soils should be properly disposed off site or used in landscaping
areas if appropriate
Fill and Backfill Materials
The on site soils encountered in cut areas may be re used as a source of general
fill material provided that they meet the requirements for general fills presented
below
General fill materials required to raise the existing ground surface to the proposed
finished subgrades outside the reservoir structure may consist of the on site
excavated soils provided that they are free of vegetation deleterious materials and
rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension Additional general fill
needed for site filling may consist of imported materials with a low to moderate
expansion potential Imported general fill material should consist of materials with
particle sizes of 3 inches or less in maximum dimension The material should have
a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 12 or higher and a swell
potential of less than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883
Aggregate base course and aggregate subbase required for the project should
consist of crushed basalt aggregate and should conform to Sections 703 06 and
703 17 of the State of Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road Bridge and Public
Works Construction 1994 Imported fill materials should be tested for conformance

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Compaction should be accomplished by using sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers
or other types of acceptable compaction equipment Water tamping jetting or
ponding should not be allowed in compacting the on site clayey soils General fill
materials should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum
dry density as determined by ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Aggregate base course and aggregate subbase materials should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts
not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction
Excavation
Our field exploration disclosed that the proposed reservoir site is generally
underlain by stiff residual soils that grade to saprolite soils that further grade to
highly weathered basalt rock formation Our opinion is that the residual and
saprolite soils at the site may be excavated with normal heavy excavation
equipment such as large backhoes or bulldozers However excavations into the
weathered basalt rock ledges will likely require the use of hoerams or chipping It
should also be noted that remnant boulders are frequently encountered in residual
and saprolite soils If encountered these remnant boulders may require additional
excavation for removal or hard excavation techniques to break through them
The above discussions regarding the excavation characteristics of the subsurface
materials are based on the field data of the borings drilled Contractors proposing to

Cut and Fill Slopes
We anticipate that the proposed cut slopes for the reservoir pad site and the new
service road would generally expose the stiff residual and saprolite soils as
encountered in the borings In general we believe that the planned cut slopes may
be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or
flatter
Excessive surface run off over the slope face may cause erosion of the exposed
soils thus potentially jeopardizing the long term stability and performance of the cut
slope Therefore appropriate slope planting or other means should be used as
soon as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes
Permanent fill slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter
Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the
existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations
should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers
in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations Fill slopes should be
constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a
well compacted slope face In addition slope planting should be provided as soon
as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes

The subgrade soils should be proof rolled with a sheepsfoot roller for a minimum
of eight passes If the subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade preparation the

recommend thoroughly moistening the soil to close the cracks and then recompacting
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather
and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted engineered fill
The base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to no

should be performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered during construction to
confirm the adequacy of the above sections The recommended sections consider only
light traffic conditions and assume that good drainage will be provided adjacent to
paved areas
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to
carry surface water off site Surface water ponding should not be allowed on site during
