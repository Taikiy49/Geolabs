REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae and
Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late

Pliocene in age 4 million years ago and forms the bulk of the western one third of
the island The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene Ice Age in age 5 million years ago and forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Waianae and Koolau shields volcanic activity
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations mainly at
the southeastern portion of the Island of Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
filled pre existing depressions and accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii and at the heads of valleys erosional
processes are dominated by detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and
transportation down slope toward a valley axis primarily by gravity as colluvial earth
materials Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley stream
flow processes become dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as
alluvial earth materials

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our visual observations it is our opinion that the northern portion of the
Lalea development encompassing the rear driveway and the existing Lalea Buildings 1
and 2 shown on the Site Plan are at risk for potentially dangerous rockfall encroachment
Based on our observations it appears that Lalea Building 1 may be exposed to greater
risk than Lalea Building 2 due to the closer proximity to the steepest section of hill slopes
and the two identified areas of potential rock instability on the hillside Based on our
evaluation of the existing site conditions we believe that some type of permanent rockfall
protection should be constructed to reduce the potential for hazardous rockfall to encroach
on the existing town house development
The permanent rockfall mitigation program must be designed to provide a high level
of protection and safety from potential rockfall events emanating from the high slopes
including Outcrop Areas A and B We believe that rockfall protection at the Lalea North

1 the construction of appropriate rockfall barrier structures down slope
from the rockfall source region or
2 the construction of rock slope stabilization improvements directly on
the high slopes encompassing the rockfall source region or
3 the combination of the construction of rock slope stabilization

Computer Rockfall Simulation Analysis
As part of our evaluation of potential rockfall activity at the site and the probable
effectiveness of the various rockfall mitigation methods we performed a computer
simulation of potential falling rock activity using input data collected during our site
reconnaissance We used the Colorado Rockfall Simulation Program CRSP Version 4 0
for our analysis Topographic information was obtained from a recent ground topographic
survey of the hillside The purpose for the CRSP analysis was to model the existing slope
and potential rockfall conditions and obtain a statistical analysis of the simulation results to
assist in the evaluation of the level of risk and design of appropriate rockfall containment
and barrier structures
The CRSP is a computer program that is a widely accepted engineering tool used
to estimate potential rockfall behavior by simulating rockfall activity based on input
parameters that are assigned on a site specific basis The input parameters for this project
were assigned based on our observations and measurements collected in the field The
program provides a statistical analysis of potential rockfall activity based on hill slope

parameters used to quantify the typical ground surface conditions Information obtained
from the CRSP analysis includes the predicted falling rock velocity bouncing height
kinetic energy and roll out distance The output information is useful to assist in the
site specific design and location of various rockfall mitigation schemes such as rockfall
impact barriers rock catchment ditches or other rockfall containment systems

Our CRSP _ analysis was performed using spherical shaped boulders
conservative scenario ranging from 2 to 6 feet in dimension initiating from the upper rock
slope area identified during the field reconnaissance For each rockfall simulation run
100 to 1 000 source rocks were utilized to develop a statistical distribution of the results
Numerical input coefficients were used which approximated the typical ground surface
relief and roughness conditions Our rockfall protection criterion is defined as the probable
interception and catchment of 90 percent of possible rockfall hazards assessed by the
computer rockfall simulation analysis CRSP The 90 percent catchment criteria is a target
that is commonly used in engineering practice for evaluation criteria that can be quantified
by a statistical and probability analysis using model data for natural occurrences such as
rockfall activity Since the analyses are based on statistics and probability a 100 percent
criterion is impractical to achieve

Option 1 Draped Netting
Option 1 considers the use of an anchored steel wire ring net drapery system
covering the upper steep slopes composed of rock formation material The drapery
would extend from the ridge summit and terminate at the base of the steep cliffs
where the net would be anchored pinned to the base of the cliffs for containment
of fallen rock beneath and within the ring net drapery The purpose of the draped
nets is to contain and control the fall of large rock materials from the steep slopes
beneath the netting for later professional controlled removal and disposal It should
be noted that some existing boulders presently embedded on the talus slopes
below the draped netting could potentially be un earthed and destabilized by future

would not be covered by the draped net system

Rockfall impact barriers are usually constructed on hillsides where the protection of
down slope areas from varied and potentially widespread sources of falling rock is
necessary Rockfall impact barriers are considered to be a viable rockfall protection
measure where a specific and limited source of potential rockfall cannot be readily
identified or where the source area may encompass disturbance sensitive land that
makes other mitigation measures less practical to implement Rockfall impact
barriers are specially designed fences consisting of steel support beams linked by
wire rope cable or steel wire ring nets which are designed to flex and absorb
rockfall impact energy The nets and beams are supported by drilled and grouted
ground anchors The barrier height is typically constructed 6 to 16 feet above
ground level and the galvanized steel components are typically colored black for
reduced visibility Rockfall barrier fences require periodic inspection and future
maintenance to remove accumulated boulder debris and replace worn or corroded
steel components The components of the barrier are generally considered heavy
duty however if a very large impact causes severe deformation portions of the
barrier may require substantial repair or replacement Ultimately the rockfall barrier
will require the replacement of components affected by natural environmental
degradation such as corrosion
We roughly estimate the service life of the barrier system as being about 30 years
at which time replacement of components such as nets ground anchors and
support beams may be required to maintain the design capacity of the system A
schematic of a typical medium high capacity 184 foot ton rockfall impact barrier is
presented on Plate 8 Photographs of similar medium high and high capacity
rockfall barriers with design capacities of 184 foot tons and 369 foot tons
respectively constructed in similar residential settings on the Island of Oahu are
presented on Plate A 6

conditions our preferred program for permanent rockfall protection at the project site is

provide a high level of effective long term protection against rockfall hazards emanating
from the adjacent hill slope

barrier
