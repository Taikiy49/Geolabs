Dear Mr. Matsuda: 
This letter report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering evaluation 
performed for the Punaluu Shore Protection project in the Punaluu area on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii.  The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location 
Map, Plate 1. 
This letter report summarizes our geotechnical engineering recommendations 
resulting from our review of the available subsurface borings provided, the report of the 
geophysical investigation by others, geotechnical engineering analysis, and previous 
experience in the vicinity.  The recommendations are for the sheet pile design parameters 
only.  The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations 
noted at the end of this letter report. 
Project Considerations 
The project involves shore protection at three general locations along Kamehameha 
Highway.  The three locations include Kaaawa, Punaluu, and Hauula where the ocean 
erosion has threatened the highway.  We understand that the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers 
(USACE), Honolulu District will provide the designs for the shore protection systems.  The 
scope of work for this contract only included topographic and geophysical surveys along the 
study areas.  The geotechnical engineering scope of work was limited to providing the sheet 
pile wall design parameters at Punaluu site only.  The other two areas do not require 
geotechnical engineering assistance. 
We understand that the design concept of the shore protection consisted of a 
cantilever sheet pile wall or sheet pile wall with tiebacks and/or deadman anchors.  Some 
shallow borings were previously drilled by others and the boring logs were provided for 
evaluation of the sheet pile wall design.  New borings were not drilled under this contract.  In 
addition, the USACE will provide the wave forces acting on the wall and the long-term 
scour/erosion depth on the ocean side of the sheet pile wall for the design.  The design 
drawings and specifications will be done by the USACE.

Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau Volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island.  The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  Waianae Volcano became 
extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried 
below Koolau Volcano lavas banking against its eastern flank.  These banked or ponded 
lavas formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau. 
The Punaluu shore protection project is in the Punaluu area of the District of 
Koolauloa on the windward side of the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The coastal area of 
Punaluu lies within the Coastal Plain province of Oahu and is located to the northeast of 
the Koolau Mountain province.  As a result, much of the generally flat land area in the

Subsurface Condition 
Shallow borings previously drilled by others indicated that the near-surface 
conditions consisted of loose sandy beach deposits, as referenced in Appendix A.  
Thirteen borings were drilled and are designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3 and 8 through 
17.  The borings were terminated at depths of about 12 to 18.5 feet below the ground 
surface within the loose sandy beach deposits. Borings were not extended into competent 
soil or rock stratum.  Details of the field exploration are contained in the report entitled 
“Preliminary Geotechnical Reconnaissance Report, Punaluu Shore Protection, Punaluu, 
Oahu, Hawaii”, by Yogi Kwong Engineers dated November 15, 2011. 
To supplement the shallow borings, geophysical surveys consisting of 
Multi-Channel Analysis of Surface Wave (MASW) and Electrical Resistivity Tomography 
(ERT) were conducted along the proposed sheet pile wall alignment by others.  The 
MASW was performed along the existing highway at about Elevation +6 feet, to estimate 
the subsurface shear wave velocity profile down to a depth of about 45 feet below the 
existing ground surface. The ERT was conducted along the beach front at about 
Elevation -4 feet, to generate subsurface resistivity profiles down to a depth of about 
80 feet below existing mudline surface.  Relevant geophysical survey profiles of MASW 
and ERT at the Punaluu site are included in Appendix B for reference.  Details of the 
geophysical surveys are contained in the report entitled “Report for the Geophysical 
Investigation, The Kaaawa, Punaluu, and Hauula Shore Protection Projects, Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii”, by Global Geophysics dated April 2, 2015.

Sheet Pile Walls 
Due to existing underground utilities in the highway and future infrastructure 
improvements, we believe that tieback and deadman anchors are not desirable for the 
lateral support to the sheet pile structure.  Therefore, we envision that the new sheet pile 
wall will likely consist of a cantilevered sheet pile system.  We assume the design mudline 
elevation to be -4 feet, so that the design cantilever length of the sheet pile wall is 10 feet. 
Details of the proposed sheet pile wall design were not available at the time this 
report was prepared.  The new sheet pile walls along the highway will serve two purposes: 
(1) to provide lateral support for the existing soils, and (2) to maintain stability of the soil 
mass.  Based on the above subsurface profiles and the interpreted soil parameters, we 
recommend the following sheet pile design lengths.  Segments of the sheet pile walls are 
summarized in the table below.  Locations of the sheet pile wall stations are referenced on 
the geophysical survey profiles in Appendix B.

Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions and design assumptions, we 
believe that the proposed sheet pile walls may consist of either PZC18/26 or AZ18/26 
sections to provide desirable stiffness to resist the bending moment demand, as 
summarized in the above table.  Details of the sheet pile wall design should be determined 
by a licensed structural engineer. 
It should be noted that the above sheet pile lengths are based on the assumption 
that cemented layers or hard coral layers that could inhibit penetration are not present.  
We recommend probing along the new sheet pile wall alignment prior to sheet pile 
installation, to validate the design parameters and confirm that hard layers are not present.  
The above design sheet pile lengths are only preliminary and subjected to modification 
based on the results of the probing. 
Design of the sheet pile walls will need to consider both overturning stability and 
overall slope stability. Geotechnical design parameters to evaluate these stability 
conditions are presented in the following subsections. 
Lateral Earth Pressures 
In general, the sheet pile wall should be designed to resist the lateral earth 
pressures due to the adjacent soils and surcharge effects.  We recommend that the new 
sheet pile wall be designed for lateral earth pressures from the in-situ soils, which include 
the existing sandy coralline materials.  The recommended lateral earth pressures for 
design of the new sheet pile wall are presented on the Apparent Lateral Earth Pressure 
Diagrams, Plates 2 through 5, for the sheet pile design category Sections 1 through 4. 
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges, line loads, and point loads within a 
horizontal distance equal to the depth of the sheet pile wall should be considered in the 
design.  We assumed a uniform surcharge stress of 250 pounds per square foot (psf) for 
traffic loads to be used for the sheet pile wall design.  For uniform surcharge stresses 
imposed on the loaded side of the wall, a rectangular distribution with uniform pressure 
acting on the entire height of the wall may be used in design, as presented on Plates 2 
through 5.  Additional analyses during design may be needed to evaluate the surcharge 
effects of point loads and line loads. 
Dynamic lateral earth forces due to seismic loading (a = 0.20g for Site Class “C” or 
a = 0.23g for Site Class “D”) may be estimated by using 3.3H2 or 3.7H2 pounds per lineal 
foot of wall length for level backfill conditions, respectively, where H is the height of the 
sheet pile wall in feet.  Based on limited subsurface information available, we believe that 
Site Class “D” may be used for design seismic consideration.  If a lower seismic design 
class is desirable for the sheet pile wall design, a supplemental exploration using seismic 
cone penetration testing (SCPT) equipment may be conducted to evaluate the average 
shear wave velocity for the subsurface materials within the upper 100 feet of the 
subsurface profile. 
The dynamic lateral earth forces are also presented on Plates 2 through 5.  It 
should be noted that the dynamic lateral earth forces provided assume that the wall will be 
allowed to move laterally up to about 2 to 2.5 inches in the event of an earthquake.  For

Unbalanced Hydraulic Pressures 
For waterfront structures such as sheet pile walls, the maximum lateral pressures 
may be expected when the tide is at its lowest level.  A receding tide may cause higher 
water levels behind the sheet pile than in front of it, depending on the type of soils behind 
the sheet piles.  The high water level behind the sheet pile wall will introduce additional 
seepage pressure due to hydrostatic load.  For sandy soils encountered at the project site, 
a hydrostatic pressure of 2 feet estimated based on the maximum tide difference 
(conservative) may be used for the sheet pile design, as presented on Plates 2 through 5. 
Overall Slope Stability 
We have evaluated the overall slope stability of the proposed sheet pile walls based 
on the anticipated soil lateral pressure parameters.  Slope stability analyses consisting of 
two-dimensional slope cross sections using Bishop's simplified method of limit equilibrium 
analysis were performed on the new sheet pile structure.  Based on our analyses, the 
factors of safety for the sheet pile bulkhead under static and seismic conditions are greater 
than 1.5 and 1.1, which are the minimum factor of safety normally recommended.  Results 
of the slope stability analysis for the static loading condition are presented on Plates 6.1 
through 6.4.  Results of the slope stability analysis for the seismic loading condition (Site 
Class “D”) are presented on Plates 7.1 through 7.4. 
Corrosion Protection 
Due to exposure to salt water, we envision that the new sheet pile walls would be 
subjected to a highly corrosive environment.  Therefore, we recommend designing metallic 
substructures for protection against potential corrosion.  If a steel sheet pile system is 
elected for the project, a corrosion engineer should be consulted for detailed 
recommendations pertaining to corrosion protection of the steel sheet pile structure. 
Construction Considerations 
The potential for the presence of cobbles and hard zones of coralline reef deposits 
may pose some difficulties for the contractor to maintain the plumbness of the sheet piles.  
To facilitate installation of the sheet pile, consideration may be given to pre-drilling and/or 
spudding along the sheet pile wall alignment to clear the path of obstructions on an 
as-needed basis.  After clearing the path of the potential obstructions (as needed), the 
sheet piles then may be installed to the desired depth without significant resistance due to 
obstructions.
