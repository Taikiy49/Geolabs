2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The
younger shield Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in
age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae Volcano
became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank was partially
buried below Koolau lava banking against its eastern flank These banked or ponded
lava formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau on which the project
site is located
The project area is composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lava of the
Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows of jointed
dense vesicular basalt rock formation with inter bedded thin clinker layers In situ
chemical weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million
years The weathering process has formed a mantle of residual soils which grade to
saprolite with depth In general saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is
typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite grades to basaltic rock
formation with increasing depth

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by shifts
in the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before
or during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not
erupt The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but
most of the earthquakes are so small that they can only be detected by
instruments However some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and
a few cause minor to moderate damage

In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii over
90 percent of earthquakes are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic
activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the
Island of Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5 M5 however earthquakes are not confined only to the
Island of Hawaii
To a lesser degree the Island of Maui has experienced numerous earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5 Therefore moderate to strong earthquakes have
occurred in the County of Maui The effects of earthquakes occurring on the
Islands of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu For example
several small landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui
Earthquake of In addition some houses on the Island of Oahu were
reportedly damaged as a result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 M7
Over the last 150 years of recorded history we are not aware of reported
earthquakes greater than Magnitude 6 occurring on the Island of Oahu We
understand that an earthquake of magnitude 4 8 to 5 0 occurred along the Diamond
Head Fault in 1948 on the Island of Oahu The moderate tremor resulted in broken
store windows ruptured building walls and broken underground water mains

Lateral Load Resistance of Abutment Fills
The abutment structure may be considered as a large footing bearing on the
abutment fill soils for resistance of lateral loads during a seismic event in the
longitudinal direction For lateral loads imposed on the abutments the stiffness of
the abutment fill soils will generally be mobilized prior to the lateral resistance of its
footings
Provided that intimate contact exists between the backfill soil and the abutments a
soil stiffness spring equal to approximately 4 ksf per inch of deflection may be used
in resisting lateral loads To reduce the potential for shear failure in the abutment fill
soils the lateral deflection should be limited to 1 25 inches or less Therefore the
maximum lateral load resistance of the abutment structure should be limited to
5 kips per square foot of abutment wall face The structural engineer should check
the structure displacement required in order to engage the abutment fill soils for
lateral load resistance
Please note that the stiffness of the abutment fill soils only applies to forces that
cause the abutment wall to move into the backfill soil longitudinal direction normal
to the abutment wall face Therefore only the lateral load resistance of the
abutment foundation and wing walls may be utilized to resist the lateral loads in the
transverse direction

retaining walls from deflecting appreciably during a seismic event resulting in potential
settlement of the bridge abutment structure
Based on the generally competent subsurface conditions assumed and the
current design concept we recommend that a permanent tieback anchor system be
used to provide the lateral support for the existing retaining walls below the Pineapple
Road Bridge structure In general the permanent tieback anchor system will be

lateral loads imposed from the heavily loaded existing abutment footings during a

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
As indicated previously the borings used for our analyses were located on the
makai side of the bridge centerline and about 30 to 120 feet away from the bridge
centerline We understand that no additional boring information was available near the
existing abutments structures at the time this report was prepared Because new
tiebacks will be installed at this bridge structure location we recommend that additional
borings be drilled at the existing abutment locations of the bridge structure to confirm
the assumed subsurface conditions and to provide subsurface information to the
prospective bidders for the tieback installation
In general the permanent tieback anchor system is constructed by drilling a
borehole installing a steel tendon or reinforcing bar into the borehole and grouting the
borehole The basic components of a grouted permanent tieback anchor consist of the
anchorage and the bonded and unbonded lengths of the steel tendon or reinforcing bar
In general the unbonded length of the tieback anchor is the portion of the steel tendon
or reinforcing bar that does not have direct contact with the grout allowing the tendon
or reinforcing bar to elongate elastically transferring the resisting force from the
structure to the bonded length The bonded length of the tieback anchor is the portion
of the steel tendon or reinforcing bar that is directly bonded to the grout allowing it to
transfer the applied load into the ground Based on the location of the existing
Pineapple Road Bridge abutment and the tieback anchor we recommend that the
unbonded length of the tieback anchor be at least 20 feet behind the concrete retaining
wall to reduce the potential for over stressing the foundation soils below the existing
abutment footing
In general the permanent tieback anchors would derive support principally from
the bond between the grout and the stiff to very stiff residual soil materials anticipated at
the project site The bond stress which is the pullout resistance per unit area of the
grout soil interface contact is variable depending on the type of soil and grout the
overburden stress and the construction procedures used in installing the permanent
tieback anchors For a rough estimate an average bond stress of 3 000 pounds per
square foot psf may be used for the stiff residual soil materials The bond stress used
in the design will need to be confirmed during construction Due to the lack of

Construction Considerations
The performance of the permanent tieback anchors depends significantly upon the
contractor s method of construction and construction procedures As a result of the
potential variations in the subsurface materials encountered at the project site we
recommend that a representative from Geolabs be present to observe the
installation of the tieback anchors during construction Construction of the tieback
anchors should be performed by a specialty contractor experienced in the
construction of permanent tieback anchors In our opinion the following may have
a significant impact on the effectiveness and cost of the tieback anchors
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions in the area it should be noted that
drilling of the tieback anchor holes may encounter gravel and large boulders within
the residual soil materials Therefore special drilling tools for drilling into and
through the gravel and potentially large boulders will be required Temporary
casing of the drilled holes for the permanent tieback anchors will be required if
caving in conditions occur during drilling of the tieback anchor holes
Grouting of the tieback anchors has a major impact on the load carrying capacities
of the tieback anchors A neat cement grout or sand cement grout mix should be
used Placement of grout should be by tremie methods beginning from the bottom
of the hole The grout should be placed through the tremie pipe by pressure
methods The grouting operations should be conducted promptly within 8 hours
after drilling to reduce the potential for significant caving in of the sides of the drilled
holes As mentioned previously we recommend that post grouting tubes be
provided in the tieback anchor system for secondary grouting of the tieback anchor
system if needed

Tieback Anchor Testing
In general we recommend that a minimum of one pre production performance test
be conducted on a sacrificial tieback anchor prior to ordering of materials for
production tiebacks and installation of production tiebacks to confirm the
contractor s installation methods and tieback pullout resistance In addition we
also recommend that a creep test be performed on the first production tieback
anchor to be installed at each concrete retaining wall before construction of the
remaining tieback anchors The creep tests should consist of subjecting the tieback
anchor to at least 150 percent of the design loads and the load should be held for
at least 8 hours to evaluate the creep effects of the tieback under sustained loads
Pullout tests proof tests should be performed on all the remaining tieback anchors
during construction to confirm the bond stresses used in the design We
recommend that all of the tieback anchors constructed be tested for pullout The
pullout tests should consist of subjecting the tieback anchors to at least 150 percent
of the design loads and the load should be held for at least 10 minutes
We wish to emphasize that creep and pullout tests on the tieback anchors are
integral parts of the design Therefore we recommend that the creep and pullout
tests be conducted under the observation of a representative from Geolabs
