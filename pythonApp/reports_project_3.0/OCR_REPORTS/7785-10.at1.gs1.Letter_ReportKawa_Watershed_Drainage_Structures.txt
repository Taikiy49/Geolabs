SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
four boreholes, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, extending to depths of about 19 to 
21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown 
on Site Plans, Plate 2.1 through 2.4. 
Boring Nos. 1 and 4 generally encountered residual soil to about 7 to 19 feet deep, 
followed by saprolite and basalt rock formation extending to the maximum depth explored 
of 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  In Boring No. 1, a surface fill layer about 
1 foot thick was encountered. 
Boring Nos. 2 and 3 generally encountered a surface fill layer about 1 and 3 feet 
thick underlain by alluvial deposits to about 8.5 and 18.5 feet deep. The alluvium was 
underlain by saprolite and basalt rock formation extending to 21.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. 
The surface fill layers generally consisted of 5 and 6 inches of asphaltic concrete, 
medium dense to dense silty sands and silty/sandy gravels. The alluvium consisted of 
soft to stiff clayey silt and silty clay with some sand and gravel. The residual soil was 
composed of medium stiff to very stiff clayey silt and silty clay.  The saprolite consisted of 
medium stiff to hard clayey/sandy silt with decomposed gravel.  The basalt rock formation 
was hard to very hard.  
We encountered groundwater in one of the borings (Boring No. 2) at a depth of 
about 5.4 feet below the existing ground surface. However, it should be noted that 
groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal 
precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
Detailed descriptions of the field exploration methodology are presented in 
Appendix A. Descriptions and graphic representations of the materials encountered in the 
borings are presented on the Logs of Borings in Appendix A. Laboratory tests were 
performed on selected soil samples and the test results are presented in Appendix B.   
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered a medium dense to dense surface fill 
layer underlain by soft to stiff alluvium and medium stiff to hard residual and saprolite 
soils, followed by basalt rock formation extending to the maximum explored depth of 
about 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Groundwater was encountered in one 
of the drilled borings (Boring No. 2) at a depth of about 5.4 feet below the ground surface.

We understand that new drainage structures will be installed for the project. Detailed

Excavation 
In order to install the proposed drainage structures, we anticipate that excavations 
below the existing ground surface will be required for the project construction. Based on 
the planned bottoms of the drainage structures, we envision that temporary shoring of the 
excavations will be required for the project. 
Our field exploration at the project site encountered a surface fill layer, alluvium, 
residual soils, saprolite and basalt rock formation. It is anticipated that most of the material 
may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment. However, deep excavations 
encountering cobbles and boulders and basalt rock formation may require the use of 
hoe-rams or chipping.  
In general, the contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used 
for excavation, subject to practical limits and safety considerations. The contractor should 
be encouraged to examine the site conditions and the subsurface data to make their own 
reasonable and prudent interpretation. 
The excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal 
to the depth of the excavation measured from the outside edge of the excavation in order to 
reduce the potential for appreciable ground movement. In addition, the excavated soils 
should be stockpiled at least 15 feet away from existing underground utilities to reduce the 
potential for appreciable ground movement or subsidence, which may damage the existing 
underground utility line. 
The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be made the sole 
responsibility of the contractor. The contractor’s representative should be required to be 
continuously present on-site during excavation and construction work. They will have the 
best opportunity to promptly observe changing conditions during construction, such as 
unforeseen subsurface soil conditions, unexpectedly high groundwater table, inappropriate 
construction sequence or techniques, etc., which may adversely affect shoring stability. 
Therefore, it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed 
properly and as early as practical, if necessary, and that the adjacent ground should be 
continuously monitored for cracks, dips and/or other indications of movements with 
instruments.

Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the contractor. The selection of 
equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor, and he/she 
should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during 
construction depending on the conditions encountered. The dewatering method selected 
should have minimal impact on the groundwater level surrounding the proposed 
excavation. The dewatering operations should be coordinated with the shoring support 
such that the stability of the excavations is not jeopardized. The operations should be 
carried out without softening the bottom of the excavations. 
Special caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering utility trenches connected 
to the excavations. If this occurs, the granular bedding and/or backfill in the utility trenches 
could act as subdrains and cause significant areal groundwater drawdown. Significant 
areal groundwater drawdown would result in ground settlements and potential damage to 
utility lines and/or other existing adjacent structures. 
Drainage Structure Foundations 
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions encountered, an allowable bearing 
pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be utilized for design of the 
drainage structures bearing on the anticipated medium stiff to stiff alluvium, residual soil, 
and saprolite, and hard to very hard basalt rock formation at the project site. To provide 
uniform bearing support for the drainage structure, we recommend providing a 6-inch 
thick layer of No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) below the bottom of the 
structure.  
Foundation subgrades should be recompacted to a firm and unyielding surface 
prior to the placement of the No. 3B Fine gravel and reinforcing steel, concrete, or precast 
box structures. Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of the excavations 
should be over-excavated to expose the underlying firm materials or maximum 2 feet 
below the bottom of the footing. The over-excavation should be backfilled with general fill 
materials compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  
We believe that the net increase in bearing pressure on the underlying medium stiff 
to stiff alluvium, residual soils, saprolite and basalt rock formation resulting from the drainage 
structure construction would be minimal. Settlements on the order of less than 1 inch are 
anticipated for the drainage structures.

recommended lateral earth pressures for design of drainage structures, expressed in

be used to backfill around the drainage structures. It is assumed that the backfill around the

Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges, traffic loads, line loads, and point loads

the bottom of the foundation and the supporting subgrade soils and passive earth pressure 
developed against the embedded near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A coefficient

material to resist lateral loads. Based on the anticipated subsoil conditions, the

Backfilling of Drainage Structure Excavations 
The drainage structure excavations will need to be properly backfilled. The excavated 
on-site soils or imported fill materials that are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, and 
clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension may be used 
as backfill above the cushion layer up to the finish grades.

Backfills using on-site soils should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent 
above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Where drainage 
structures are located below pavement areas, the upper 3 feet of the drainage structure 
backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative 
compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D1557. Optimum 
moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum 
dry density.  
Underground Drain Lines 
We anticipate that new drain lines connecting to the new drainage structures will 
be constructed for the proposed project. The methods and equipment to be used for 
excavation should be determined by the contractor, subject to practical limits and safety 
requirements. The excavation should comply with all applicable local, state, and federal 
safety requirements. Trench shoring design and installation should be the responsibility 
of the contractor. Trench shoring and bracing should conform to the appropriate health 
and safety requirements. 
In general, for the support of the underground utility lines, we recommend that a 
granular bedding layer consisting of 6 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation) be used under the pipes. The initial backfill up to about 1 foot above pipes 
should consist of free-draining backfills, such as No. 3B Fine gravel, to reduce the 
potential for damaging the pipes from compaction of the backfill. It is critical that a 
free-draining granular material be used to reduce the potential for formation of void below 
the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. The 
use of on-site soils as backfill immediately around utility pipes is not recommended.  
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to the 
finished subgrade should consist of the approved on-site granular soils or select granular 
material. The backfill material should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for 
future ground subsidence. The upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement 
subgrade should be compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction. 
Mechanical compaction equipment should be used to compact the materials at the project 
site. Water tamping, jetting, or ponding should not be used to compact the backfill 
material.
