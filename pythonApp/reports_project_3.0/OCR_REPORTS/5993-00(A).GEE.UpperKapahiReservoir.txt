SECTION 1.0 - GENERAL
1.1. Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed East Kauai Irrigation System Improvements, Upper Kapahi
Reservoir project on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. The project location and general
vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration, laboratory testings, and
engineering analyses. These recommendations are intended for the design of the pile
foundation for this project only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report.
1.2 Project Considerations
The project site is surrounded by Kainahola Road on the north, east, and south in
the Kapaa area on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. Currently, there is an existing wooden
catwalk with the intake control gates at the western side of the reservoir. Based on the
information provided, the existing catwalk is supported on timber piles, which are
severely deteriorated. A new replacement catwalk is proposed to access the intake
control gates. The approximate location of the proposed new concrete catwalk is shown
on the Site Plan, Plate 2
Based on the project location, the depth of the mudline and the subsurface
conditions, we anticipate that pile foundations will be used to support the new
replacement catwalk. The piles will be supported by both end bearing on hard basalt
formation and skin friction from stiff saprolite soils.
1.3. Purpose and Scope
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering exploration was to obtain an
overview of the subsurface conditions at the project site to develop a generalized
soil/rock data set to formulate geotechnical engineering recommendations for the

SECTION 2.0 - SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2.1 Regional Geology
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single dissected basaltic shield volcano
built by the extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series that began about
5 to 6 million years ago. The eruption of the Waimea Volcanic Series ended about
2% million years ago and was followed by a long period of erosion. Following the
cessation of this main volcano shield-building phase, about 1% million years ago,
renewed volcanic activity occurred with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of the
post-erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the thick alluvial
sediments of the Palikea Formation.
The basalt rock of the Koloa Volcanic Series is generally characterized by thick
lava flows of dense basaltic rock extruded from groups of vents aligned in north-south
trends in various locales on the eastern half of the Island of Kauai. Associated with the
Koloa Volcanic Series lava flows are some localized volcanic deposits consisting of
pyroclastic materials (volcanic ash and cinders), which are usually encountered as
surficial mantling deposits and accumulations surrounding the cinder cone vents. Rocks
of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of Kauai,
including the regional area surrounding the project site.
Based on our review of available geological mapping information, the project site
appears to be on a gently sloping plateau region along the foot of the
Makaleha Mountains as indicated on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. We anticipate
that the project site is underlain by weathered soils and basaltic rock belonging to the
Koloa Volcanic Series.
2.2 Site Description
The project site is near the intersection of Kainahola Road and Kawaihau Road
in the Kapaa area on the eastern side of the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. It is surrounded by
Kainahola road on the north, east and south. The Upper Kapahi Reservoir is part of
East Kauai Irrigation System. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the
Project Location Map, Plate 1.

3.1.1 Uplift Load Resistance
Uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the dead weight of the driven pile
and by shear along the pile surface and the adjacent soils. The recommended
allowable uplift load capacity of 17 kips per pile may be used in the design of the
catwalk. The provided uplift load capacities already include the weight of the pile
and assume that the piles will not be spliced. The project structural engineer should
check the structural capacity of the pile member in tension.
3.1.2 Lateral Load Resistance
Lateral load resistance for the driven piles is a function of the stiffness of the
surrounding soil, the stiffness of the pile, allowable deflection at the top of the pile,
and the induced moment in the pile.
The lateral load analyses were performed using the program LPILE-plus for
Windows, which is a microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference, laterally

3.1.3 Pile Foundation Settlements
Settlement of the pile foundations will result primarily from elastic compression of
the pile member. We estimate the total settlement of the pile-supported foundation
to be less than 0.5 inches with differential settlements between piles not exceeding
about one-half of that amount. We believe that these settlements are essentially
elastic and should occur as the loads are applied.
3.1.4 Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a rated energy of
up to about 40,000 foot-pounds per blow. The hammer should be equipped with an
energy control level such that driving within the loose/soft soils may be controlled at

which could damage or cause cracking of the piles.
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into

variation in the length of the piles, unit prices should be obtained during bidding for
add-ons, shorter piles, etc.
3.1.5 Pile Driving Criteria |
As mentioned above, due to the uncertainties regarding the selection of the pile
driving equipment, the driving criteria for the precast concrete piles should be
determined after a wave equation analysis is performed.
Each pile should be driven continuously without interruption. A pile may be rejected
or its design capacity may be reduced when the driving resistance is interrupted for
more than 4 hours and the pile cannot be driven to the required depth. During soft
driving, such as when the pile tip is within soft/loose soils, the hammer stroke
(or energy) should be carefully controlled such that significant tensile stresses do
not develop within the concrete pile.
Due to the limited number of driven piles anticipated for the project construction, we
believe that a test pile program would not be conducted. Because variations in soil
strength characteristics are likely to exist in the bearing soils, a Geolabs field

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling
two borings to depth of about 44 and 36.5 feet below the existing water surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a
skid-mounted drill rig equipped with casing and coring tools secured on a floating
platform (raft).
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field and observed the drilling operations on a near-continuous
basis. These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation and testing in the
laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic representation of the materials encountered is
presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-2.2.
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2.0-Inch
OD standard penetration sampler or 3.0-inch OD Split-Barrel sampler with a 140-pound
hammer falling 30 inches. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last
12 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown on the logs of borings at the appropriate
sample depths.
Core samples of the rock or consolidated materials encountered at the site were
obtained by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM D 2113, Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation or consolidated
materials. The rock (or consolidated) material left in the hollow core of the bit is
mechanically recovered for examination and description.
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D 2937)
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths.
Three Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test
results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic
representations of the test results are provided on Plate B-1.
One Unconfined Compression test (ASTM D 2166) was performed on a selected
core run to evaluate their unconfined compressive strength of the cohesive soil
encountered. Results of the unconfined compression tests are presented on Plate B-2.
