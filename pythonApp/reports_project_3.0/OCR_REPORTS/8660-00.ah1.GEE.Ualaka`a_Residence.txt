SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The subsurface conditions encountered at the site consisted of fills and cinder over 
saprolite and/or basalt rock formation at greater depths. Groundwater was not 
encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. However, it should 
be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, 
seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
It appears the backfill behind the existing CRM walls generally consists of 
loose/soft sands and silts. Considering the poor consistency of the retained soils, we 
recommend implementing a ground improvement program by utilizing the drilling and 
grouting method to strengthen the existing CRM wall west and to support the new 
residence construction. 
To improve the loose/porous subsurface condition, we recommend utilizing a 
hollow core bar drilling system to inject cement-based slurry during the drilling process to 
a depth of about 15 feet (or until hard drilling is encountered). The cement-based slurry 
would likely infiltrate and solidify the adjacent soft/loose soil column for ground 
improvements. 
Considering the relatively light structural loads of the new Ohana residential 
structure, we believe that shallow foundations consisting of spread and/or continuous strip 
footings may be used with an allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,500 psf for the design 
of footings bearing on the recompacted on-site materials and/or new compacted fills 
(minimum 90 percent relative compaction). This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads 
and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind 
or seismic forces. 
We anticipate the ground floor slabs for the new garage structure, driveway, and 
any surrounding flatwork will consist of reinforced concrete slabs-on-grade. We envision 
the slabs-on-grade generally will be supported at the existing grade or on new compacted 
fills. Beneath the garage and driveway slabs, we recommend providing a 6-inch layer of 
aggregate subbase, where the exterior flatwork slabs may be supported directly on the 
compacted subgrade.

recommendations.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield 
volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle 
to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene 
to early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a series 
of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone eruptions. These later eruptions are referred 
to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present sea level. 
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that 
the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, renewal of volcanic activity occurred with the 
eruptions of vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series. Along the crest of the ridge and the 
westerly side of Manoa Valley are the Tantalus, Roundtop, and Sugarloaf vents. The 
project site is situated on an interfluvial ridge on the southeastern flank of Koolau. 
The project site is on the eastern flank of Punchbowl Hill (Puowaina), which is a 
tuff cone near the center of Honolulu built against the end of a spur of the Koolau Range. 
The tuff is mostly brown palagonitized vitric ash and lapilli with scattered fragments of 
coral limestone and Koolau basalt.

2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused largely by 
shifts in the tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked 
primarily to volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally 
occurs before or during volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result 
from the underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but 
does not erupt. The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each 
year, but most of the earthquakes are so small that they can be detected only by

felt, and a few cause minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes (associated with volcanic activity) are most common on 
the Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement 
of magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes 
on the Island of Hawaii. Because majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over 
90 percent of earthquakes) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of high seismic 
activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the 
Island of Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes 
greater than Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not confined only to 
the Island of Hawaii. 
It should be noted that some earthquakes in Hawaii are caused by the burden of 
the island masses overlying the lithosphere, which cause the lithosphere to sag 
beneath the immense weight. The isostatic adjustment of the Pacific lithosphere 
beneath the islands’ overburden causes some moderate to strong intensity 
earthquakes. The 1973 Honomu Earthquake, Magnitude 6.2 (M6.2), beneath the 
Hamakua Coast of the Island of Hawaii and the October 15, 2006 Kiholo Bay 
earthquake, Magnitude 6.7 (M6.7), were a result of lithosphere adjustment. Both 
of these earthquakes were widely felt throughout the Hawaiian Islands. 
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced numerous earthquakes 
greater than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have 
occurred in the County of Maui. The effects of earthquakes occurring on the 
Islands of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu. For example, several 
small landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake 
of 1938 (M6.8). In addition, some houses on the Island of Oahu were reportedly 
damaged as a result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 (M7+). 
Due to the relatively short period of documented earthquake monitoring in the 
State of Hawaii, information pertaining to earthquakes that were felt on the Island 
of Oahu may not be complete. In general, over the last 150 years of recorded 
history, we are not aware of reported earthquakes greater than Magnitude 6

2.4.2 Liquefaction Potential 
Based on the IBC 2018, the project site should be evaluated for the potential for 
soil liquefaction. Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils 
near the ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build-up 
of excess pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced 
by earthquakes. When the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration 
(such as during an earthquake), the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume, 
causing an increase in pore water pressure. If drainage is unable to occur rapidly 
enough to dissipate the build-up of pore water pressure, the effective stress 
(internal strength) of the soil is reduced. Under sustained vibrations, the pore water 
pressure build-up could equal the overburden pressure, essentially reducing the 
soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid. During 
liquefaction, the soil acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal and 
vertical movements, and if not confined, will result in significant deformations. 
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained 
sands and loose silts with little cohesion. The major factors affecting the 
liquefaction characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows:

3.1 
Existing West Concrete Rubble Masonry (CRM) Wall 
Along the west boundary of the property, an existing CRM wall provides grade 
separation from the 1502 Ualaka`a Place property west of the project site. The existing 
CRM wall is approximately 10 to 15 feet tall with ledges cascading downwards. It should 
be noted that the upper section of the wall at the southern end of the wall alignment 
consists of concrete masonry units. 
It is desired to strengthen the existing CRM wall supporting the new residence 
building construction. Based on our borings near the existing wall, it appears the backfill 
behind the walls generally consists of loose/soft sands and silts. Considering the poor 
consistency of the retained soils, we recommend implementing a ground improvement 
program by utilizing the drilling and grouting method. 
3.1.1 Drilling and Grouting 
Generally, we recommend drilling and grouting holes with a triangle grid pattern in a 
spacing of 4 feet center-to-center along the wall alignment and up to an 8-foot 
setback behind the existing wall. The drill holes should be at least 3 inches in 
diameter and should extend downwards until hard drilling is encountered or to a 
depth of 15 feet below the existing ground surface. 
To improve the loose/porous subsurface condition, we recommend utilizing a hollow 
core bar drilling system to inject cement-based slurry during the drilling process. The 
cement-based slurry would likely infiltrate and solidify the adjacent soft/loose soil 
column for ground improvements. 
Due to the reaming nature of the drilling with grout/slurry, the actual volume of the 
drilled hole may exceed the theoretical volume of the drilled hole by a factor of up to 
four times. The contractor should anticipate the potential grout loss and incorporate 
the necessary costs into the unit prices during the bidding process. 
The grout slurry should consist of a neat grout mix with a water to cement ratio of 
about 0.45 and a minimum compressive strength of 100 pounds per square inch 
(psi). The grout slurry should be injected at low to moderate pressures (the slurry 
should be capable of being pumped through the hollow core bar).

program Slide2 from Rocscience Inc. The slope stability analysis was performed at 
a typical section in the middle of the wall alignment. 
Bishop’s simplified method of limit equilibrium was used to evaluate the balance of 
forces and moments acting within the slope section to determine the factors of safety 
for the stabilized wall based on the generalized subsurface profile and estimated 
shear strength parameters. The Bishop’s simplified method of slope stability analysis 
assumes a circular slip surface, which is divided into slices with finite mass. The 
following parameters were used in our global slope stability analyses to evaluate the 
rotational slope failure mechanism: 
1. 
Slope geometry. 
2. 
Density and shear strength of the in-situ fill, cinder and basalt rock 
formation based on the borings drilled at the project site and 
empirical correlations. 
3. 
Inclusion of construction surcharge of 250 psf. 
To evaluate the long-term stability of the selected slope area, effective stress soil 
parameters were determined and utilized in our analyses. Based on the results of the 
global slope stability analyses, the grouting stabilized walls generally have a 
minimum factor of safety of greater than 1.5 for the construction phase loading 
conditions.

Infiltration Test 
We conducted a falling head infiltration test at Boring LID-1 to evaluate the 
infiltration characteristics of the subsurface materials encountered. Boring LID-1 was 
generally located in the landscaping area near the northwest corner of the existing pool. 
The test was performed in general accordance with the procedures in Appendix D of the 
State of Maryland, Department of the Environment “Stormwater Design Manual, 
Volumes I and II” (rev. 2009). These procedures are consistent with other states’ 
procedures and may generally be considered an industry standard. 
The field infiltration test was performed by drilling to a depth of about 5 feet below 
the existing ground surface. Upon reaching the test depth, a 4-inch diameter PVC solid 
casing was set to the bottom of the drilled borehole to allow infiltration only through the 
soil exposed on the bottom of the borehole. The falling head infiltration test was performed 
to determine the average infiltration rate of the underlying subsurface materials. The test 
consisted of four trials of filling the casing with 24 inches of water and taking periodic 
readings over a one-hour trial period. The infiltration rate is then calculated based on the 
result of the fourth and last trial. 
Based on the infiltration testing performed at the project site, the subsurface soils 
at the selected test location have a calculated infiltration rate of about 24 inches per 
26-minute test period, equivalent to about 55 inches per hour, during the fourth and final 
trial. Based on the results of the field infiltration testing, the infiltration rate at the testing 
location indicates that the infiltration/detention system and similar methods of stormwater 
management would be feasible for the development. 
It should be noted that the infiltration value presented herein is the rate of infiltration 
through the soil exposed at the bottom of a 4-inch diameter borehole, which may not 
represent the actual infiltration condition within a typical infiltration chamber footprint or 
an open basin. Due to the variability of the subsurface conditions, the absorption capacity

3.9 
Corrosion Potential 
Two sets of laboratory corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum 
Resistivity (ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), 
were performed (by our office and Eurofins TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc.) on selected 
soil samples obtained from our field exploration. Results of the corrosivity tests are 
presented in Appendix B. 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower 
the resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to the corrosion 
of metallic objects. 
On the basis of the laboratory resistivity, the subsurface soil at the project site has 
minimum resistivity values of approximately 850 and 990 ohm-cm corresponding to a 
corrosion rating of 1 (Extremely Corrosive), based on the guidelines provided by the City 
& County of Honolulu – Board of Water Supply. Design of metallic substructures, such as 
waterline pipes or other metallic piping, should consider the effects of the corrosive 
environment on the substructure. 
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent on the pH and the chloride and sulfate contents found in the soil. 
In general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm), sulfate 
content of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH of greater than 5.0 may be considered 
“non-corrosive” to underground concrete pipelines and structures. Based on the 
laboratory-tested values of pH, chloride content, and sulfate content of the in-situ soils, 
we believe the near-surface soils at the project site may be considered “non-corrosive”; 
therefore, either Type I or Type II (Type I/II) cement may be used for the concrete in 
contact with the ground. 
It may be appropriate to consult with a professional corrosion engineer to review 
the test results and provide detailed recommendations for corrosion protection.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling six borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5 and LID-1, extending to 
depths between approximately 5 and 30 feet below the existing ground surface. The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were 
drilled using a portable drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-6. 
Some soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance 
with ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch O.D. standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. 
In addition, some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general 
accordance with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
3-inch O.D. modified California sampler with the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample 
depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number 
of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be 
factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” 
by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt.

For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any 
drilling, mechanical, and handling-induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used 
to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical 
Properties of Rocks and Minerals” by Robert S. Carmichael (1989).

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture content (ASTM D2216) and unit weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Four Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths, and a 
graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Seven Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plates B-2 and B-3. 
Two one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples 
to evaluate the swelling potential of the on-site soils. The test results are summarized on 
Plate B-4. 
Six Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the peak and/or residual shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. 
The test results are presented on Plates B-5 through B-10. 
Four Uniaxial Compression tests (ASTM D7012, Method C) were performed on 
selected core runs to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the basalt 
formations encountered. The test results are presented on Plate B-11. 
One Modified Proctor compaction test (ASTM D1557 A) was performed on a bulk 
sample of the near-surface soil to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationship.  The test results are presented on Plate B-12. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils.  The test results are presented on Plates B-13 and B-14. 
Two sets of Corrosion tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), were 
performed by our office and Eurofins TestAmerica on selected soil samples obtained from 
our field exploration. The test results are summarized on Plate B-15.

Field Infiltration Test 
A field infiltration test was conducted at the site to evaluate the infiltration 
characteristics of the subsurface materials encountered. The infiltration test was 
performed at a selected location, Boring LID-1, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. This 
test was performed in general accordance with the procedures in Appendix D of the State 
of 
Maryland, 
Department of 
the 
Environment 
“Stormwater Design 
Manual, 
Volumes I and II” (rev. 2009). These procedures are consistent with other states’ 
procedures and may generally be considered an industry standard. 
The field infiltration test was performed by drilling and sampling a borehole to a 
selected test depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface. Upon reaching the 
test depth, a 4-inch diameter PVC solid casing was set to the bottom of the drilled hole to 
allow infiltration only through the soil exposed on the bottom of the boring. The falling 
head infiltration test was performed to determine the infiltration rate of the underlying 
subsurface materials. The test consisted of four trials of filling the casing with 24 inches 
of water and taking periodic readings over a one-hour trial period or until the water 
completely drains. The infiltration rate is then calculated based on the result of the fourth 
and last trial for the test location. The test results are presented on Plate C-1.
