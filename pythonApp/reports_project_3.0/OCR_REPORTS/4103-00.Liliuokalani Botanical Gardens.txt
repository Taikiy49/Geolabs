INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed security lighting fencing and other site improvements project
at the Liliuokalani Botanical Gardens located in Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii
The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of the security light pole
foundations site grading and pavements only The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located within the existing Liliuokalani Botanical Gardens in
the Nuuanu area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii It is proposed to install
new security lights perimeter fencing and to make other improvements at the existing
park In addition the northwestern side of the existing parking lot will be expanded to
accommodate a total of about 28 parking stalls
We understand that new security light poles will be installed at the parking lot
areas Based on the information provided the new light poles will be approximately
15 feet in height The following structural loads for the lighting system were provided by
the project structural engineer for design
e Vertical Load per pole 110 pounds
e Lateral Load at the base per pole 350 pounds
e Overturning Moment at the base per pole 2 800 foot pounds
Based on the topographic map provided the project site is relatively level with
existing elevations ranging from about 63 to 67 feet Mean Sea Level MSL The
GEOLABS HAWAII

existing ground at the northwestern portion of the project site where the parking lot
expansion is planned is about 2 feet lower than the existing parking lot grade Therefore
we anticipate that construction of the parking lot expansion will involve placing fills of up to
about 2 feet to achieve the final design grades
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of the field exploration program was to obtain an overview of the
surface and subsurface conditions at the site to develop a soil rock data set for the
formulation of geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the design of the
proposed project In order to accomplish this we conducted an exploration program
consisting of the following tasks and work efforts
1 Mobilization and demobilization of a truck mounted drill rig and operators to
and from the project site
2 Drilling and sampling of two borings to depths of about 16 5 and 17 5 feet
below the existing ground surface for one day of field exploration
3 Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the boring by a geologist
from our firm
4 Laboratory testing of selected soil samples obtained during the field
exploration as an aid in classifying the materials encountered and
evaluating their engineering properties
5 Analyses of the field and laboratory data for the formulation of geotechnical
engineering recommendations pertinent to design of the security light pole
foundations site grading and pavements
6 Preparation of this formal report summarizing our work on the project and
presenting our findings and recommendations
7 Coordination of our overall work on the project by a Project Engineer of our
firm
8 Quality assurance and client design team consultation on the project by a

support

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas
banking against its eastern flank
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported downslope
toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these materials reach
the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become dominant and the
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
The project site is located in Nuuanu Valley an amphitheater headed valley
carved by erosion into Koolau Basalt Filling the base of this valley is a thick sequence of
alluvium interbedded with deposits of lava and pyroclastics of the Honolulu Volcanic
Series

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsoil conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings extending to depths of about 16 5 and 17 5 feet below the existing ground
surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Generally our field exploration encountered surficial fills consisting of medium
dense sandy gravel and silty gravel to about 1 2 feet below the existing ground surface
Below this surficial fill layer adobe soils consisting of stiff to very stiff silty clays with sands
and gravel were encountered The adobe soils were generally intermixed with
boulders and cobbles extending to the maximum depths explored of approximately
16 5 and 17 5 feet below the existing ground surface It should be noted that the adobe
soils encountered at the site are highly expansive Therefore special attention should be
given to the discussion and recommendations presented in the Site Grading and
Pavement Design sections of this report
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings at the time of our field
exploration However it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change
due to rainfall storm surge conditions time of year and other factors

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered surficial fills of about 1 2 feet in
thickness underlain by adobe soils The adobe soils generally consisted of stiff to very
stiff silty clays intermixed with sands gravels cobbles and boulders Based on our
laboratory tests the on site adobe soils may be considered highly expansive
In order to develop the required bearing and lateral load capacities for the new
light pole structures we recommend that drilled shaft foundations embedded in the stiff to
very stiff adobe soils be used to support the proposed lighting system Based on the
anticipated structural loads and the subsoil conditions encountered at the project site we
recommend that drilled shafts with a minimum diameter of 24 inches and an embedment
length of not less than 5 feet below the design finish grades be used for the proposed
light pole structures
The performance of the drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor s
method of construction construction procedures and workmanship The
recommendations and general guidelines presented in this report should be followed by
the contractor during the foundation construction work
Detailed discussions and recommendations for design of foundations site grading
pavements and other geotechnical aspects of the project are presented in the following
sections of this report
Security Light Foundations
In order to develop the required bearing and lateral load capacities the proposed
new light pole structures may be supported by a foundation system consisting of
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4103 00 Page 7
cast in place concrete drilled shafts Based on the anticipated structural loads and the
subsoil conditions encountered at the project site we recommend that drilled shafts with a
minimum diameter of 24 inches be used to support the proposed light pole structures
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site a drilled shaft
embedment length of not less than 5 feet below the design finish grades may be used for
the light pole foundations at the subject site
The cast in place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally
from skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding soils An allowable
compressive load capacity of up to 8 kips per shaft may be used for dead plus live loads
and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as wind or seismic
forces The end bearing component of the drilled shafts should be discounted due to
difficulties in obtaining a clean bottom during drilled shaft installation
Lateral loads imposed on the foundations may be resisted by a combination of the
following 1 resistance of the shaft soil system to deflect and 2 passive pressure
resistance on the vertical faces of shaft caps where applicable Lateral load resistance of
the drilled shaft is a function of the stiffness of the surrounding soil the stiffness of the
shaft allowable deflection at the top of shaft and induced moment in the shaft Based on
the information provided each drilled shaft will be subjected to a lateral load of
350 pounds with an overturning moment of about 2 800 foot pounds at the ground
surface Our lateral load analyses indicate that the estimated deflection at the top of the
drilled shaft should be less than inch
The anticipated settlements are estimated to be on the order of less than inch
We believe that a significant portion of the settlements is elastic and should occur as the
loads are applied
Construction Considerations
Drilled shafts are desirable to the subject project because of the significant
increase in lateral and uplift load capacities when compared to shallow foundations

However the performance of the drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the

Difficult Drilling Conditions
Based on our field exploration cobbles and boulders intermixed with silty clays
may be anticipated at shallow depths Therefore difficult drilling conditions may
be encountered Appropriate drilling equipment should be utilized by the drilled
shaft contractor to install the drilled shafts to the depths and dimensions
recommended herein In addition due to the proximity of the site to Nuuanu
Stream it is possible that some seepage water may be encountered during the
shaft drilling operations Therefore the contractor should exercise care when
drilling the shaft holes
Workmanship
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in
drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes
A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 6 to 9 inch range should be used to
provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soil The
concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce segregation of the
aggregates from the concrete mix if pre cast concrete pole bases are used the
gap between the pole base and the surrounding soils should be backfilled with
lean concrete A representative of Geolabs Hawaii should be present to observe
the installation of drilled shafts during construction

Site Grading
Final grading plans were not available at the time this report was prepared
However we anticipate minor grading consisting of cuts and fill of about 2 feet relative to
the existing ground surface would be needed for the proposed project Items of grading
that are addressed in the following subsections include the following 1 Site Preparation
2 Fills and Backfills and 3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Site grading operations should be observed by a _ representative of
Geolabs Hawaii It is important that a representative from our office observe whether the
exposed soil conditions during excavation are similar to those encountered in our
exploration The following recommendations are intended to provide guidelines for the
design of site grading work
Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to reduce the
potential for contamination of the excavated materials Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill
should be over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting
excavation should be _ backfilled with well compacted engineered fill
The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site or
at designated areas on site
After clearing and grubbing the exposed subgrade should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 8 inches properly moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction Due to the highly expansive nature of the on site adobe soils
it is imperative that the subgrade soils are moisture conditioned to at least
2 percent above the optimum moisture content and are not allowed to dry prior to
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4103 00 Page 10
placement of fills Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
Fills and Backfills
In general the on site granular soils generated from the excavation may be
reused as a source of general fill and backfill materials provided that the
recommendations contained herein are followed On site granular soils to be
used as fill should contain no particles greater than 3 inches in largest
dimension Over sized on site materials should be screened prior to being
re used as fill materials
If imported fill is required the materials should be well graded from coarse to fine
with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension In addition the fill
material should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the
No 200 sieve The materials should be tested and approved by Geolabs Hawaii
prior to delivery to the site to confirm that it is suitable for its intended purpose
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction The compaction requirements for fills and backfills within 3 feet of
the pavement grades should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density

Pavement Design
New flexible pavements are anticipated for the expansion of the existing parking
lot We anticipate that the vehicle loading for this project will consist primarily of
passenger vehicles light pick up trucks and occasional heavy trucks We have made
our preliminary pavement design assuming the pavement subgrade soil will be similar to
the silty clay adobe soils encountered in the borings with a California Bearing Ratio CBR
of 1 6 and a swell value of 14 9 percent Based on the above assumptions we
recommend the following pavement section be used for preliminary design purposes
Asphalt Concrete Pavement
2 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 Inch Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
18 0 Inch Select Borrow Subbase 95 Percent Relative Compaction
26 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on a Moist Compacted Subgrade
The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction If pumping of the subgrade
occurs during construction the subgrade should be proof rolled to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction The base course and select borrow subbase should
consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction
Laboratory CBR tests and field density tests should be performed on the actual
subgrade soils encountered during construction to confirm the above recommended
design section The recommended pavement section is based on the assumption that
good drainage will be provided for areas adjacent to the pavements
Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines such as electric duct lines would be required for
the proposed project In general we recommend that a granular bedding consisting of
6 inches of sand or S4C be used under the duct lines for support of the utility lines The
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4103 00 Page 12
backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes should consist of free draining materials
such as sand or S4C to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from compaction of
the backfill
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on site soils or select
granular material The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet
of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to not less than
95 percent relative compaction

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of
the test are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
One California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM D 1883 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the strength characteristics for pavement
subgrade support Results of the CBR test are presented on Plate B 2
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on the
near surface soils Results of the test are presented on Plate B 3
