We would like to thank you for allowing us the opportunity to discuss the injection
well installation and permitting process with a portion of the Board of Directors on the
evening of March 10, 1993.
It is our understanding that the Board would like us to prepare and submit a permit
application for four (4) wells but drill and test two (2) for capacity. The remaining two (2)
wells would be drilled and installed only if additional capacity is needed.
In principal, we have no problems with this plan of action and feel that it may be
prudent in terms of economic considerations. However, we would like to point out that
the well screen required for the new wells needs to be special ordered from the Mainland.
This requires a few weeks of lead time. In addition, the material is cannot be returned if
it is not used. Therefore, we must commit to a set amount for each order.
From the standpoint of our logistics and scheduling, we would not be able to leave
our drilling equipment at the site during the lag period to order additional supplies if it is
necessary to drill the additional two (2) wells. Therefore, we would have to demobilize
from the site and remobilize when the additional materials arrive. This would necessitate
our charging an additional mobilization fee of $1,000.00 plus tax.

in terms of operational prudence, we still recommend that four (4) wells be drilled
and installed. As was stated in the meeting with the Board, there are no definitive plans
at this time for the City and County to develop a regional sewer system for the Mokuleia
area and injection wells could easily lose capacity from clogging during a power outage
or for other reasons.
We also recommend the installation of four (4) wells for better operation and
maintenance. Generally, injection wells tend to lose capacity over time as a result of the
suspended solids load in the effluent. This is particularly true for wells in continuous use.
Based on research by Dr. Frank Peterson at the University of Hawaii, this clogging effect
is reduced somewhat if the wells are allowed to rest between periods of injection. It is
believed that the resting period allows microbial action to breakdown some of the
clogging solid matter at a slow enough rate that gas locking of the pores in formation is
not a problem.
By installing four (4) wells with proper valving and controls, it would be possible to
rotate the effluent disposal between the wells. This would provide the resting periods
which apparently enhance injection well life.
We appreciate the opportunity to provide these comments and look forward to
working with you on this project. If you have questions or if we may be of further
assistance, please contact the undersigned.
