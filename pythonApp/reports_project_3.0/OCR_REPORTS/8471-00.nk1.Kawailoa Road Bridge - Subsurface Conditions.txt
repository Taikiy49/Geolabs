This technical memorandum provides a summary of the subsurface conditions
encountered during our field exploration performed for the Kawailoa Road Bridge project.
A detailed summary of our recommendations will be contained in our geotechnical
engineering report. The report should be consulted when it becomes available.
REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is largely composed of the weathered remnants of the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The younger Koolau
Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and forms the
majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. It is believed that Waianae became extinct
while Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank is partially buried below the Koolau lavas
in central Oahu.
During the late Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great continental
glaciers advanced, the level of the ocean fell because less water was available to fill the
oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded or melted, global sea levels rose
because more water was available. The land mass of Oahu remained essentially stable
during these changes, and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. These glacio-eustatic
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower relative to the
present sea level of Oahu.
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations, and deposition of lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet
waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in
the sediments and reef deposits. Sub-aerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials. In addition, renewed sub-aerial erosion of the
upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively
high-energy conditions.

During periods of insignificant sea level changes, continued stream action extended 
the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal sediments. 
During these stable periods, the growth of fringing coral/algal reefs formed distinct ledges of 
reef limestone at elevations roughly conforming to the level of the sea during that period. 
About 15,000 years ago, a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred. During that sea level 
rise, the valleys in the project area were submerged. In the last 10,000 years or so, the sea 
level has adjusted to its present stand. Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued 
to accumulate in low-energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in thick deposits 
of soft and/or loose sediments along the coast in areas that were formerly valleys and 
drainageways. 
The available geological record indicates that a barrier reef, or sand bar strand, 
formed across Kailua Bay in the vicinity of the present coastline. This formed a protective 
barrier for the back of the bay allowing a quiet lagoon to develop. Experience in the area 
indicates that extensive finger coral reefs developed in this lagoon. Natural sedimentation 
within the lagoon filled the interstices in the finger coral formation with organic silts and clays. 
As the lagoon slowly filled, dune sands and beach sands eventually filled in and over the 
finger coral formation, generally bringing the area to its present configuration. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling ten borings, designated as 
Boring Nos. B-1 through B-10, extending to depths of approximately 57.5 to 58.5 feet 
below the existing bridge deck surface. Our borings generally encountered a concrete 
bridge deck about 9.5 to 11.75 inches thick, water at elevations of about +0.5 to +1.1 feet 
Mean Sea Level (MSL), mudline at elevations of about -0.4  to +1.3 feet MSL, and an 
upper coral/sandstone shelf at about -4.1 to -8.2 feet MSL. 
The existing soils from the mudline level down to the top of the coral/sandstone 
shelf generally consisted of very loose to loose sandy beach and shallow marine deposits 
with localized layers of medium dense coralline detritus. The coral/sandstone shelf was 
encountered in all our borings, except Boring No. 8, and ranged in thickness from about 
1 to 6.5 feet. This upper shelf generally consisted of severely to closely fractured, slightly 
to moderately weathered, soft to medium hard coral formation and sandstone. Underlying 
the upper shelf, shallow marine deposits, weathered sandstone, lagoonal deposits, and 
coralline detritus consisting of very loose to medium dense silty sand and gravel with 
some pockets of soft cohesive soil were encountered to elevations of about -22.1 
to -30.9 feet MSL. Coral formation and sandstone with interbedded layers of weathered 
sandstone and coralline detritus were then encountered to the maximum depth explored 
of about -50.9 feet MSL. The coral formation and sandstone were generally close to 
moderately fractured, slightly to moderately weathered, and medium hard to hard. The 
weathered sandstone and coralline detritus generally consisted of medium dense silty 
sand and gravel.

The water level within Kaelepulu Stream was encountered at depths of about 6.8 
to 7.6 feet below the top of the bridge deck, corresponding to elevations of about +0.5 to

fluctuations, seasonal rainfall, time of year, and other environmental factors.
