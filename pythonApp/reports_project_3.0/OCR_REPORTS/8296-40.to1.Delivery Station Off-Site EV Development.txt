SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered surficial fills overlying soft/loose lagoonal deposits 
and dense coralline detritus extending to the depths drilled of about 3 and 11.5 feet below 
the existing pavement surface. We encountered groundwater in the borings at depths 
ranging from about 8.8 to 9.5 feet below the existing pavement surface at the time of 
exploration. The groundwater levels will likely vary due to tidal fluctuations and the nearby 
canal, as well as rainfall, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors at 
the project site. 
Details of the proposed project were not available at the time this report was 
prepared. However, we anticipate that the new electrical conduit pipe inverts will be on 
the order of about 5 to 10 feet below the existing pavement surface. Installation of the 
proposed electrical conduit will involve excavation into the underlying fills, lagoonal 
deposits, and/or coralline detritus. In general, we anticipate that the in-situ soils along the 
proposed alignment may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment.

depth) are planned, we envision that temporary cut slopes for open-cut excavations may not 
be practical. In this case, trench walls would have to be cut vertically with the use of shoring 
during construction. Possible methods of shoring could be to use interlocking sheet pile 
shoring or a shoring box/shield. 
In general, the near-surface soils encountered during our field exploration may be 
used in the upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the pavement subgrade, provided that the materials are free of vegetation, deleterious 
materials, organic material, and clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in 
maximum dimension.

geotechnical recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Delivery Station Off-Site EV Development project in the Honolulu area 
on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project. 
These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of the 
project. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations 
noted at the end of this report.

It is proposed to develop an off-site EV facility in support of the recently completed 
Amazon delivery station operation. Based on the information provided, we understand

SECTION 2. SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. Waianae became extinct while 
Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau 
lavas banking against its eastern flank. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during 
which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned 
with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are 
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
The project site is on the coastal plain of Southern Oahu. The coastal plain was 
built on the eroded flanks of the Koolau Volcano, which forms the eastern two-thirds of 
the Island of Oahu. The coastal plain was built by extensive accumulation of alluvium 
derived from erosion of the volcano, interbedded with coral reefs and associated deposits. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. Most of the coastal plains were developed during the 
Pleistocene Epoch when the sea levels fluctuated significantly. As the glaciers grew and 
advanced, less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell 
below the present stands of the sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess 
of water became available such that the sea levels rose above the present sea. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations. 
The project site generally is underlain by deposits of calcareous sediments and 
alluvium. The deposition of soils at the site is likely associated with the nearby Kapalama

3.1 
Trench Excavation 
Based on the information provided, we understand that the off-site EV 
development will extend from the new delivery station operation site to the nearby HECO 
substation; in approximately 1,000 linear feet alignment. Details of the proposed project 
were not available at the time this report was prepared. However, we anticipate that the 
majority of the new electrical conduit pipe inverts will be on the order of about 5 to 10 feet 
below the existing ground surface. Installation of the proposed alignment will involve 
excavation into the underlying surficial fills, lagoonal deposits, and/or coralline detritus. In 
general, we anticipate that the in-situ soils along the proposed alignment may be 
excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment. 
Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal 
to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential for 
excessive ground movement. In addition, the excavated soils should be stockpiled at least 
15 feet away from existing underground utilities to reduce the potential for appreciable 
ground movement or subsidence, which may damage the existing underground utility 
lines. 
The above discussion regarding the excavation of the subsurface materials is 
based on the information obtained from our field exploration at the project site. We 
recommend encouraging contractors to examine the site conditions and soil data to make 
their own interpretation. 
3.2 
Trench Excavation Support 
We envision excavation depths of up to about 5 to 10 feet below the existing 
ground surface may be required for the installation of the new electrical conduit. Where 
deeper excavations (greater than 5 feet in depth) are planned, temporary shoring or 
sloping and benching would likely be implemented for the trench excavations. Based on 
our site observations, we believe that temporary cut slopes for open-cut excavations may 
not be practical due to congested existing underground utilities and adjacent above 
ground structures. In this case, trench walls would have to be cut vertically with the use 
of shoring during construction.

Possible methods of shoring could be to use interlocking sheet pile shoring or a 
shoring box/shield. If sheet piles are to be used, the piles should be driven with a suitable

be noted that the excavations will likely encounter very soft/loose soil deposits at the

heave and design the shoring system accordingly.  
It is important to install adequate sheeting prior to the excavation and to maintain 
it tightly against the excavation walls with proper bracing during excavation. The properly 
braced sheeting is essential to reduce appreciable lateral movements of the adjacent 
ground into the excavation, which may result in potential settlements or distress to 
adjacent structures or other improvements, such as pavement or utilities. 
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable 
safety requirements. The contractor should be solely responsible for trench excavation 
and the adequacy and safety of the shoring installation. The contractor’s representative, 
who should be required to be continuously present on-site during excavation and 
construction works, will have the best opportunity to promptly observe changing 
conditions during construction, such as unforeseen subsoils, unexpectedly high 
groundwater table, inappropriate construction sequence or techniques, etc., which may 
affect shoring stability. 
It must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and the 
adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation. Due 
to the complexity of the stress changes, it is difficult to accurately estimate the magnitude 
of movement. The magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship, such as how 
quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are installed, the subsoil conditions, 
the size of the excavation, and the rate of excavation. In addition, it should be noted that 
settlement of the existing ground may occur as a result of the vibrations generated during 
the installation and extraction of the sheet pile shoring. Therefore, the contractor should 
give special attention during the sheet pile installation and removal process to reduce the 
potential for appreciable ground settlement.

3.3 
Pipe Cushion 
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the 
load supporting capacity of the pipe. Therefore, the pipe cushion is an important design 
consideration. For pipes bearing on the stiff/dense surface fill soils, a minimum of 6 inches 
of compacted granular cushion material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
Gradation), should be provided below the pipe. As an alternative, S4C material may be 
allowed for pipe cushion where the invert of the pipe is at or lower than +4 feet Mean Sea 
Level (MSL). 
The granular cushion layer should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width 
of one-fourth (¼) of the outside pipe diameter, or 12 inches, whichever is greater, and 
should extend 12 inches above the pipe. 
3.4 
Trench Backfill 
It should be noted that loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the 
trench, the unit weight of the trench backfill material, and the friction resistance between 
the backfill material and the trench walls. To calculate the vertical loads on the buried 
pipes, we recommend an average unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic feet (pcf) for the 
trench backfill material and a coefficient of friction of 0.3. Earth forces acting upon the 
pipe generally increase rapidly with the width of the trench. Therefore, the width of the 
trench should be kept to a minimum. Traffic loads on the buried pipes should also be 
considered for the portion of the pipes located in roadway areas. 
The first zone of backfill extending from the cushion material to at least 12 inches 
above the top of the pipes should consist of select free-draining granular material 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation) or S4C material, to reduce the compaction effort required 
and resulting stresses on the pipe. In addition, it is critical to use this free-draining material 
to reduce the potential for the formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to

3.5 
Trench Backfill Compaction and Placement Requirements 
Backfill materials consisting of the on-site surface fill materials should be moisture-
conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture content for cohesive soils or 
above the optimum moisture content for cohesionless soils, placed in level lifts not 
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction. The upper 3 feet below the pavement grade should be compacted 
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
Imported fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to at least above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Aggregate base course and 
subbase materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture 
content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to 
a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place 
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same

Corrosion Potential 
Design of metallic substructures, such as electrical conduits or other metallic 
piping, should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure. 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
regarding the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the 
resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of 
metallic objects. The following evaluations of corrosion potential that are addressed in the 
subsequent subsections were conducted.

3.6.2 Laboratory Corrosivity Testing 
Three sets of corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0) were 
performed by our laboratory and CERCO Analytical, Inc. on selected near-surface 
soil samples obtained from our field exploration. A summary of the corrosivity tests 
is presented on Plate B-2 of Appendix B. 
Based on the results of the laboratory corrosivity tests, the near-surface soils at the 
project site exhibit minimum resistivity values ranging from approximately 1,200 to 
13,000 ohm-cm, indicating that the near-surface soils are very corrosive to mildly 
corrosive (Corrosion Rating of 2 to 5) to buried metallic structures. It should be noted 
that the laboratory resistivity testing was conducted under fully saturated soil samples 
and the testing results present the most extreme design condition. Therefore, we 
recommend properly designing near-surface metallic substructures (such as piping) 
for protection against the potential for corrosion.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, extending to the depths drilled of 
about 3 and 11.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations 
are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3.  
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, Penetration 
Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler 
using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second 
and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the 
Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. 
The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) 
blow counts.

Field Resistivity Surveys 
Geophysical survey equipment (i.e., resistance meter) was mobilized to the project 
site on May 7, 2024 for the field resistivity survey. Three field resistivity surveys were 
conducted, designated as R-1 through R-3, using the in-situ Wenner 4-Pin Method and an 
AEMC Model 4630 resistance meter manufactured by AEMC Instruments. The method 
utilizes four surface probes or pins (electrodes) to evaluate the average resistivity of the 
underlying soil mass. The average resistivity values of the soil mass, extending from the 
existing ground surface to the test depth were assessed by using this method at the 
approximate survey locations. 
The soil resistivity surveys were performed along the existing pavement surface at 
the selected survey locations. The average soil resistance along the proposed alignment 
was evaluated at each survey location with the electrodes aligned at an equal spacing of 
generally 1, 2, 3, 5, 10, and 20 feet. The depth of penetration of a resistivity sounding is a 
function of the electrode spacing. 
The resistance meter (AEMC Model 4630) provides a voltage between the outer 
electrodes, causing an electrical current to flow between them. The voltage drop between 
the inner electrodes is then measured by a sensitive, built-in voltmeter. As a result, the soil 
resistance (in ohms) is obtained from the voltmeter reading. The soil resistivity (in ohm-cm), 
which is directly related to both resistance and spacing, can then be computed. The 
evaluated soil resistances and computed resistivity values are presented on Plate C-1. In 
addition, the computed resistivity values with pin spacing at the test locations are presented 
in the graph on Plate C-2.  
At the time of resistivity surveying, the temperature was about 78º to 85º F. The 
existing ground surface was dry to damp. It should be noted that the field resistivity survey 
location R-2 was located near an Island Energy jet fuel pipeline. Temperature, weather 
conditions, existing underground utilities, and other factors may influence the soil resistivity.
