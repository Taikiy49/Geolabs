SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration results, the existing pavement structure within the 
project limits along Nimitz Road, Peltier Avenue, and Forest City office parking lot 
generally consists of about 2 to 4.5 inches of asphaltic concrete underlain by 4 to 
18 inches of base/subbase materials.  The subgrade soils below the existing pavement 
structure varied considerably ranging from hard clayey soils at the office parking lot to 
loose sandy gravel (trench backfill) and very dense volcanic tuff along Nimitz Road and 
Peltier Avenue.  
In general, the type and severity of the distresses exhibited in the pavement areas 
would determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction of the existing pavements should 
be performed. Existing pavements showing minor distresses and severity are generally 
rehabilitated with cold-planing and an AC overlay. Reconstruction of the existing 
pavements is usually performed for those areas where the observed distresses are likely 
caused by failures in the underlying base, subbase, and/or subgrade materials. 
Our visual pavement condition survey at the project site indicated that the Forest 
City office parking lot, the mauka lane of Nimitz Road, and the Diamond Head lane of 
Peltier Avenue under consideration for this project appear to exist in generally fair to good 
condition with some localized moderately to highly distressed areas. Based on our 
engineering evaluation and construction considerations, we recommend rehabilitating 
these pavement areas with a regular AC overlay consisting of cold-planing 2.0 inches of 
the existing AC surface and replacing with 2.0 inches of new AC overlay. 
The remaining pavement areas, including the localized distressed areas mentioned 
above, generally exhibited substantial amounts of cracking, rutting, and depressions.  We 
believe that the majority of these pavement failures are the result of ground subsidence 
from the inadequately compacted utility trench backfill consisting of loose sandy gravel as 
encountered in some of the drilled borings. Considerations should be given to reconstruct 
these failed pavement areas with a new pavement structural section consisting of 2 inches 
of AC on 4 inches of ACB over 6 inches of ASB. 
We recommend proof-rolling the bottom of the new pavement structural section 
with a minimum 10-ton vibratory drum roller a minimum of 6 passes or recompacting to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  The recompacted subgrade should be lined 
with a layer of woven geotextile fabric, such as Mirafi 500X or equivalent, prior to the 
placement of the aggregate subbase course.

Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering analyses 
performed for the Repave Nimitz Road and Portion of Peltier Avenue project in Honolulu 
on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on 
the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our 
analyses for the pavement improvement project. The findings and recommendations 
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project involves pavement improvement for approximately 1,400 lineal feet 
of Nimitz Road and about 300 lineal feet of Peltier Avenue in Honolulu on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii. The existing roadways are two-way roads with a single travel lane in 
each direction.  As part of the project construction, it is also desired to rehabilitate the 
pavements at the Forest City office parking lot located at 5173 Nimitz Road.  The 
approximate project limits are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  
Based on the information provided, Nimitz Road and Peltier Avenue are owned 
by the U.S. Government and leased to Hawaii Military Communities, LLC.  The Forest 
City office parking lot is located on U.S. Government property. Our visual pavement 
condition survey indicated that the existing pavements at these locations are generally 
in fair to poor conditions.  Therefore, it is desired to rehabilitate and/or reconstruct these 
existing pavements to support the anticipated traffic.  To reduce the potential for 
premature failures and improve the long-term performance of the new pavement 
structures, we understand that drainage improvement are also planned to alleviate 
ponding water and improve runoff into existing drainage channels connected to the City 
and State drainage systems.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. 
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene 
(Ice Age) in age and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. As the 
volcanic activity in Waianae Volcano ceased, lava flows from Koolau Volcano banked 
against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau, now known as Schofield 
Plateau. 
After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time erosion incised deep 
valleys into the Koolau Volcano Shield, volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows 
followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are referred to as the Honolulu 
Volcanic Series. 
The project site is on the south side of the Aliamanu vent and on the 
southwestern flank of the Salt Lake vent complex of the Honolulu Volcanic Series. As 
the molten magma ascended through these vents, it reacted with the groundwater by 
generating steam. This steam caused explosive eruptions, which deposited low cones 
of ash material that have consolidated to form tuff formation, locally referred to as 
"mudrock.” 
The Aliamanu tuff is an older, water-laid ash deposit that was laid down when the 
sea level was higher than the present level. The Aliamanu eruptive event deposited its 
material over alluvium originating from Halawa Stream. After this phase of volcanic 
activity ceased, sedimentary deposits accumulated over the Aliamanu tuff before the 
next phase of volcanic activity began. Then, the Salt Lake eruptions began when the 
sea level was at about 40 to 60 feet below the present level. The Salt Lake eruptive 
event deposited its material over earlier tuffs from the older Aliamanu events. 
Subsequently, man-made developments in the last century have brought the site to 
existing conditions.

2.4 
Dynamic Cone Penetration Testing 
In addition to borehole drilling and sampling, our field exploration program also 
included performance of Dynamic Cone Penetration (DCP) testing at the boring 
locations to provide additional data and information for our pavement evaluation and 
analyses. 
The Dynamic Cone Penetration test provides a measure of a material’s in-situ 
resistance to penetration. The test is performed by driving a metal cone into the ground 
by repeatedly striking it with a 17.6-lb (8-kg) weight dropped from a distance of 
22.6 inches (575 mm). The penetration of the cone is measured after each blow and is 
recorded to provide a continuous measure of shear resistance up to 61 inches below 
the pavement surface. Test results can be correlated to California Bearing Ratio, in-situ 
density, resilient modulus, and bearing capacity. 
The DCP tests were generally conducted at 1-inch (25.4-mm) discrete depth 
intervals between depths of about 15 to 61 inches below the existing pavement surface 
at the test locations.  Field logs of the DCP tests are included in Appendix C of this 
report for reference.   
Several authors have investigated the relationship between the Dynamic Cone 
Penetrometer penetration index (PI) and California Bearing Ratio (CBR).  Based on 
these studies, we have estimated the CBR value based on the penetration index 
obtained from the DCP testing.  Results are presented on the Correlations of 
Penetration Index and California Bearing Ratio, Plate 3 of this report.  Based on our 
field observations and the DCP test results obtained, we have adopted a design CBR 
value of 8 for the subgrade materials in our pavement design analyses for the 
reconstruction areas.

3.1 
Rehabilitation of Existing Pavements 
In general, the type and severity of the distresses exhibited in the pavement 
areas would determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction of the existing 
pavements should be performed.  Existing pavements with minor distresses are 
generally rehabilitated with an AC overlay with cold-planing.  Based on our visual 
pavement condition survey, these pavement areas generally include the majority of the 
Forest City office parking lot, the mauka lane of Nimitz Road, and the Diamond Head 
lane of Peltier Avenue.  Based on our evaluation, it is our opinion that these pavement 
areas with minor distresses may be rehabilitated with an AC overlay consisting of 
cold-planing 2.0 inches of the existing AC surface and replacing with 2.0 inches of new 
AC overlay. 
In general, the contractor should vacuum-sweep the pavement areas 
immediately after cold-planing to remove gravel-sized materials and dust not picked up 
by the cold-planing operation.  It should be noted that the vacuum-sweep should not 
preclude the dust control operation during cold-planing. In general, the contractor 
should begin paving no later than three working days after cold-planing begins.  Finally, 
vacuum sweeping of cold-planed surfaces should also be performed immediately prior 
to paving.

3.2 
Reconstruction of Existing Pavements 
As discussed in the “Visual Pavement Condition Survey” section, some of the 
observed pavement distresses are likely caused by failures in the underlying base, 
subbase, and/or subgrade materials. In general, these areas should be reconstructed 
with a new pavement section in lieu of an AC overlay section. In addition, these 
distressed areas should be repaired prior to the AC overlay work for the entire roadway 
under consideration. 
Design traffic information and data were not available for the preparation of this 
report.  In general, we anticipate that the vehicle loading for the parking lot and 
roadways under consideration for this project would primarily consist of passenger 
vehicles with some light trucks and occasional heavy trucks only. Therefore, we have 
assumed that these pavements may be considered as “Low Volume Pavements” with a 
Total Equivalent Single Axle Loads (Total ESALs) of less than one million over the 
pavement life.  In addition, we have adopted a CBR value of 8 for the pavement 
subgrade soils as discussed in the “Dynamic Cone Penetration Testing” section herein. 
Based on these assumptions and the construction considerations described 
above, we recommend using the following flexible pavement design section for the 
reconstruction areas. Detailed calculations and analyses of the pavement design are 
presented on Plate D-1 in Appendix D.

  2.0-Inch Asphaltic Concrete (AC) 
  4.0-Inch Asphalt Concrete Base (ACB) 
  6.0-Inch Aggregate Subbase (ASB)

section should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above 
the optimum moisture, and proof-rolled with a minimum 10-ton vibratory drum roller a 
minimum of six (6) passes or recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction.  Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with

AASHTO T-180 or ASTM D 1557.  Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by

The prepared base/subbase/subgrade should be lined with a layer of woven 
geotextile fabric, such as Mirafi 500X or equivalent, prior to the placement of the

representative should monitor the pavement subgrade preparation to observe whether

CBR tests and/or field observations should be performed on the actual subgrade 
soils during construction to confirm that the above design section is adequate. The 
aggregate subbase course (ASB) should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates 
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
Asphalt concrete base (ACB) material should consist of asphalt-treated basalt 
aggregate, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and 
compacted to no less than 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity 
determined in accordance with AASHTO T-209 or ASTM D2041. Asphaltic concrete 
(AC) material should be constructed in accordance with Section 401 Asphalt Concrete 
Pavement of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge 
Construction (2005) and its amendments. 
3.3 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development 
of good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage 
structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration 
or premature failure of the pavements.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions within the pavement areas under 
consideration by drilling and sampling five borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, 
extending to depths ranging from about 5.0 to 5.3 feet below the existing pavement 
surface.  The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The 
borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers 
and coring tools. 
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A.  
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Dynamic Cone Penetration Tests 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway improvement project 
by performing Dynamic Cone Penetration (DCP) tests at five boring locations, extending 
to depths of about 15 to 61 inches below the existing pavement surface.  The 
approximate boring locations where DCP tests were performed are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. Results are shown on the Dynamic Cone Penetrometer Data Sheet, 
Plates C-1 through C-5. 
The procedures used in our DCP tests are in general accordance with 
ASTM D6951, Standard Test Method for Use of the Dynamic Cone Penetrometer in 
Shallow Pavement Applications.  The DCP apparatus consists of a 5/8-inch diameter 
steel rod with a 60 degree conical tip. The rod is topped with an anvil that is connected 
to a second steel rod. This rod is used as a guide to allow a 17.6-lb hammer to be 
repeatedly raised and dropped from a height of 22.6 inches. The connection between 
the two rods consists of an anvil to allow for quick connections between the rods and for 
efficient energy transfer from the falling weight to the penetrating rod. 
Once the test apparatus is assembled, the DCP is placed at the test location and 
the initial penetration of the rod is recorded to provide a zeroing scale. While holding the 
rod vertically, the weight is raised to the top of the rod (22.6 inches above the anvil) and 
dropped. The penetration of the rod is measured after each drop. 
In the DCP tests conducted by Geolabs, the same equipment described above 
was used to record the number of blows required to advance the cone in 1-inch 
(25.4-mm) depth increments, as shown in the field logs provided in Appendix C.
