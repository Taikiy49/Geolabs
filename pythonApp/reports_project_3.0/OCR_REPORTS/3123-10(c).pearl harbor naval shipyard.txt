INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our preliminary geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the evaluation of the existing pile foundation system at
Piers GD 1 and GD 2 located within the Pearl Harbor Naval Shipyard on the island of
Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering findings derived from our evaluation
of the subject project
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located at Piers GD 1 and GD 2 of the Pearl Harbor Naval
Shipyard on the island of Oahu Hawaii A preliminary geotechnical engineering
evaluation has been performed for the subject piers based on the limited subsurface
information available for review and analysis during the initial stage of the subject
project Our preliminary findings and recommendations have been summarized and
transmitted to your office in our letter dated March 24 1994
It is currently desired to evaluate the existing pile foundation system at
Piers GD 1 and GD 2 to determine the allowable payload capacity of the subject piers
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3123 10 C Page 2
In order to evaluate the pile capacity of the existing pier foundations the subsurface
soil profile at the project site would need to be developed by drilling and sampling deep
borings at selected locations on the piers to depths ranging from 100 to 150 feet below
the existing deck surface
Based on the information provided we understand that test piles for the subject
piers were driven to depths ranging from about 20 to 165 feet below the cut off
elevations Actual lengths of the remaining production piles are not known for this
evaluation however the production piles are assumed to be similar in length to the test
piles driven Depending upon the field exploration results and foundation engineering
analyses field confirmation of the actual pile capacity by load testing of selected piles
may be needed after the soil exploration is performed It is also desired to evaluate the
potential for liquefaction at the subject piers

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The subject project is located at Piers GD 1 and GD 2 of the Pearl Harbor Naval
Shipyard on the island of Oahu Hawaii Piers GD 1 and GD 2 are part of the same
finger pier located within the Controlled Industrial Area of the Pearl Harbor Naval
Shipyard between and 2 Pier GD 1 is the eastern face of the finger
pier and Pier GD 2 is on the western face of the pier as shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The pier consists of a Quaywall deck of approximately 280 feet long by 50 feet
wide and is supported by 282 18 inch square precast prestressed concrete piles The
finger pier measures approximately 230 feet long by 48 feet wide and is supported by
352 18 inch square concrete piles
The pier structure consists of cast in place reinforced concrete girders beams
and slabs supported by driven precast concrete piles The pier is protected by a timber
pile fender system which is intended to distribute the berthing loads both horizontally
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3123 10 C Page 5
as well as vertically The quaywall bulkhead is constructed of steel sheetpiles with a
concrete sheet pile curtain wall in front that extends down to the mean low water
elevation
The pier is currently used as a temporary berthing area for small surface ships
and a movable caisson for the dry dock facility There are several small shacks
container sized pieces of mechanical equipment and a movable crane on the quaywall
end of the pier A 3 to 4 story temporary vacant enclosure with plan dimensions of
approximately 40 by 70 feet is situated on the seaward end of the finger pier The
metal sided structure is supported by full height scaffolding lining the interior of the
building Lateral support of the scaffolding is provided by guy wires attached to the pier
deck Activity on the pier itself appears to be light
REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The geologic conditions underlying the site are the result of a complex series of
deposition and erosional events brought on by glacio eustatic sea level changes and
post erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch Following the cessation
of activity of the Koolau Volcano alluvial materials were deposited in a deltaic or
estuarine environment in the area of Pearl Harbor
The sea level fluctuated several times receding perhaps 300 feet below and
rising about 95 feet above the present sea level Concurrent with a rise in sea level
during the late Pleistocene era eruption of the Salt Lake Makalapa member of the
Honolulu Volcanic Series mantled the older alluvium with thick deposits of volcanic ash
which consolidated to form volcanic tuff locally called mudrock layers Subsequent
erosion dissected and thinned those mantles of tuff
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3123 10 C Page 6
During the recent epoch as sea level rose to its current stand the area was
covered with soft and loose lagoonal marine deposits These deposits were sub
sequently covered by man made fill placed during the middle portion of this century for
land reclamation projects during the construction of the existing Navy Shipyard

Our field exploration at Boring No 1 near the bulkhead area of Pier GD 2
indicated that the area is underlain by loose silty sands between a depth of 11 and 16
feet below the existing deck surface We believe that it is highly likely that the dredged
area behind the steel sheetpile walls especially below the ground water level may be
backfilled with uncontrolled loose granular fill materials up to the water line prior to
capping with compacted fill to the existing ground surface Therefore it is our opinion
that over 10 feet of loose granular materials may be present at the bulkhead area of
Pier GD 2 which is potentially liquefiable under moderate to large earthquakes and
may result in appreciable settlements of the existing ground surface Lateral movement
of the pier bulkhead may also occur in the event of liquefaction
Our field exploration conducted at Boring No 2 and Probe No 3 indicated that
up to 30 feet of loose granular materials may be present along the finger pier We
anticipate that liquefaction of these loose granular soils would occur when subjected
to earthquake induced vibrations generated by an earthquake of magnitude 6 0 with a
peak horizontal ground acceleration of 0 10g or stronger Because of the anticipated
densification and settlement of the loose deposits pile foundations founded through the

load capacity of the piles will need to be reduced by the estimated downdrag loads
FINDINGS AND DISCUSSIONS
Based on our field exploration of the site loose sandy soils underlain by dense
coral formation were encountered at relatively shallow depths near the bulkhead area
of Pier GD 2 Soft harbor sediments and loose coralline deposits were encountered
along the finger pier and extended to a depth of about 80 feet below the existing deck
surface It is our opinion that the loose granular materials encountered at the subject
site are potentially liquefiable when subjected to moderately strong earthquakes with
a magnitude on the order of 6 0 and an associated peak horizontal ground acceleration
of 0 10g or stronger Liquefaction of these materials may result in appreciable
settlements of the existing ground surface at the bulkhead area and cause downdrag
loads on the pile foundations supporting the existing pier
Based on our engineering analyses and considering the downdrag loads induced
due to liquefaction a net allowable compressive load capacity of 80 tons may be
developed for an 18 inch square concrete pile driven to a depth of about 70 feet below
the existing pier deck surface near the bulkhead area of Pier GD 2 The pile would
need to be driven to a depth of about 110 and 150 feet below the existing deck surface
in order to develop a net allowable compressive load capacity of 80 tons along the
finger pier Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration
the project site may be classified as a site varying from Soil Type 2 to Type 3 from a
seismic analysis standpoint Detailed findings and discussions are presented in the
following sections of this report

Vertical Load Capacity of Piles
Based on the idealized soil profiles and soil parameters presented in the
previous section of this report engineering analyses were performed to develop the
relationship between the allowable compressive load capacity of an 18 inch square
concrete pile and the driven pile length below the existing deck surface at each of the
boring and probe hole locations Safety factors of 2 and 3 were used in our analyses
for the frictional and end bearing contributions from the subsoils respectively to obtain
the allowable compressive load capacity of the 18 inch square concrete pile Results
of our analyses are presented in the Estimated Pile Capacity Versus Depth charts
shown on Plates 4 through 6 of this report for the three soil conditions encountered in
our exploration
As previously discussed in the Liquefaction section of this report liquefaction
of the loose granular deposits would occur in the event of an earthquake The
earthquake induced liquefaction would result in significant settlements and would
impose downdrag loads on the pile foundations driven through these deposits We
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3123 10 C Page 13
estimate that downdrag loads on the order of 17 20 and 28 tons per pile may be
anticipated at the locations of Boring No 1 Probe No 3 and Boring No 2
respectively in the event of liquefaction due a moderate to large earthquake Taking
into consideration these downdrag loads it is our opinion that a net allowable
compressive load capacity of 80 tons may be developed for an 18 inch square concrete
pile driven to a depth of about 70 feet below the existing pier deck surface at the
location of Boring No 1 At the locations of Probe No 3 and Boring No 2 the pile
would need to be driven to a depth of about 110 and 150 feet respectively below the
existing deck surface in order to develop a net allowable compressive load capacity of
80 tons Since the mudline appears to be levelling off between Probe No 3 and Boring
No 2 we believe that a pile length on the order of about 150 feet 18 inch square pile
would be required to develop a net allowable compressive load capacity of 80 tons
along Bent Nos 18 through 25 of the subject pier
We understand that little information such as the pile driving records or actual
driven pile lengths for the production piles could be obtained pertaining to the existing
pile foundation system at Piers GD 1 and GD 2 for our evaluation of the existing pile
supporting capacity Therefore it is our opinion that the actual load bearing capacity
of the existing pile foundation system could only be confirmed by load testing of
selected piles in the field

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling two
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 to depths of about 83 and 163 feet below
the existing pier deck surface A third probe hole designated as Probe No 3 was
advanced by continuous penetration without sampling using a blunt point to a depth of
about 110 feet below the existing pier deck surface The borings and probe hole were
drilled using a truck mounted drill rig at the approximate locations shown on the Site
Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer geologist who monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and
by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic illustration of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings and Probe Hole Plates
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
fornia sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left
in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a

Laboratory Testing
