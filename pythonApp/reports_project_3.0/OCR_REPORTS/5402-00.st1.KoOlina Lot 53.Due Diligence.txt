Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to
early Pleistocene in age As volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano ceased lava flows

from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau
now known as the Schofield Plateau The project site is located on the southwestern flank
of the Waianae Volcano
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of Schofield Plateau
generated sediments which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project
site and to the south these sediments accumulated and are interbedded with marine
sediments and coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge The thickness of
the sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1 Highway
to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the
confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea
When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposited lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet
waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys
in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the calcareous
reef materials Placement of man made fills and commercial development of the area
within the last 20 years have brought the Ko Olina area to its present form

foundation system consisting of cast in place concrete drilled shafts or augered
cast in place concrete piles
Based on our experience at the Ko Olina Resort we envision that there may be
concerns regarding excess noise and vibrations generated during the pile driving
operations Therefore we believe that drilled shaft foundations or augered cast in place
ACIP piles may be desired for support of the proposed structures Considering the
subsurface conditions coralline detritus and or coral formation there likely is a potential
for cost savings when comparing drilled shaft foundations and augered cast in place piles
to the traditional driven pile foundations for support of the new structures at the project site
in addition to the benefits of less noise and vibrations
We envision that the new structures may be supported on 36 inch diameter drilled
shafts It is anticipated that each column would be supported on a single drilled shaft
designed to support the structural loads of the columns For preliminary estimating
purposes we envision that the drilled shafts would need to extend to depths of about 50 to
80 feet below the bottom of footing cap elevation in order to achieve an allowable
compressive load capacity of about 1 500 kips per drilled shaft
It should be noted that cavities and voids are commonly encountered in the
coralline materials in the vicinity of the project site Therefore the actual volume of
concrete required to fill the drilled shaft foundation may be appreciably more than the
theoretical concrete volume Based on our experience at the Ko Olina Resort area we
envision that concrete overpours of up to about 30 to 40 percent over the theoretical
concrete volume may be required for the drilled shaft foundations
Our limited field exploration encountered varying subsurface conditions from the
makai to mauka portions of the project site Therefore we recommend that the
subsurface conditions be evaluated further by conducting a design level field exploration
program consisting of drilling and sampling additional borings during the design phase of
the project when the type and distribution of the proposed structures are better defined

Soft and or Loose Soils
Very soft and or loose lagoonal and alluvial deposits were encountered from a
depth of about 32 to 42 feet below the ground surface in Boring No 1 Design of the new
structures and improvements should consider the presence of the soft and or loose soils at
the makai portion of the project site In addition drilled shaft excavations may require the
use of temporary casing to reduce the risk of caving of the sidewalls during construction
As mentioned previously a design level field exploration program should be conducted to
delineate the approximate limits of the soft and or loose soils present at the project site
Expansive Soils
Based on our experience in the vicinity of the project site the on site alluvial soils
may exhibit a slight to moderate shrink swell potential when subjected to moisture
fluctuations The alluvial soils were used as a source of fill materials during the initial mass
grading operations for the Ko Olina Resort development Our borings encountered a
surface layer of clayey soils of about 2 to 2 5 feet in thickness
The expansive potential of the clayey soils at the project site should be evaluated
by performing laboratory swell tests during the design phase Where expansive soils are
encountered over excavation of the clayey soils may be necessary under the concrete
slabs on grade and pavements The resulting over excavation would need to be backfilled
with non expansive select granular fill 12 to 18 inches thick
Site Preparation
The proposed grading plans were not available at the time this letter report was
prepared In general the excavated on site materials may be used as a source of fill
materials provided that they are processed to meet the gradation requirements of the
various fill types Due to the potential for presence of expansive soils special attention
should be given to the site preparation aspect of the proposed construction
Over excavation of the subgrades under structures and roadways may be necessary
depending on the expansive nature of the on site soils Fill areas may require placement
of non expansive select granular material below future improvements such as building
slabs sidewalks and or pavements
