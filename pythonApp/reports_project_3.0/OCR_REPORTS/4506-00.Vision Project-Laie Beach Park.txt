REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu encompassing approximately 604 square miles of land area may
be divided into four basic geographic provinces The provinces include the Wai anae
Mountains Ko olau Mountains Leilehua Plateau and Coastal Plain The Island of Oahu was
formed by the merging of basalt lavas erupted from the two prominent volcanoes the
Waianae and Ko olau Volcanoes Of the two volcanoes the Waianae Volcano is the older
having breached the ocean surface nearly 4 million years ago The Ko olau Volcano rose
above sea level approximately 2 7 million years ago making it the younger of the
two volcanoes
In general the two volcanoes were built by the long term eruption of thinly bedded a a
and pahoehoe lava flows which were erupted from elongate and linear trending volcanic
vents associated with the prominent rift zones of each of the volcanoes The Ko olau lavas
banked against the lava flows of the Waianae Volcano to form the Leilehua Plateau of the
central interior portion of Oahu The Wai anae Mountains which form the western third of
GEOLABS INC
W O 4506 00 Page 5
the Island of Oahu contain the oldest basalt rock formations on the Island of Oahu The
Ko olau Mountains form the eastern two thirds of the island and include the rock layers
exposed along the extending from Waimanalo to Kahuku
During the final stages of eruption and continuing well after the cessation of Wai anae
and Ko olau eruptions the island mass began to subside thousands of feet under the
tremendous weight of the rock mass In addition to the subsidence of the island the force
of stream erosion worked in conjunction with the large scale fracture and slumping of
portions of the land mass to form the topography of today Between 850 000 and
70 000 years ago volcanic activity returned to the Island of Oahu with the eruption of ash
cinder and basalt lavas from vents located throughout the eastern and southeastern portions
of the island These most recent eruptions are collectively known as the Honolulu Volcanic
Series Subsequently erosion by streams and ocean waves have continued to work on the
exposed land surfaces to form the topography of the present day
The Island of Oahu is no longer sinking beneath the sea however the effects of the
early whole island subsidence combined with the added effect of major Pleistocene age sea
level fluctuations worked to create drowned river valley systems and the development of
coral reefs The coral reefs and lithified sand dunes may be found exposed subaerially on
the Island of Oahu and also buried in submarine deposits well offshore of the island The
coral reefs and sand dune deposits were formed in shallow offshore seas during periods of
higher and lower sea levels associated with the effects of the advance and retreat of glaciers
which controlled the volume of water contained in the seas Today some of the shallow sea
coralline deposits are visible on the flanks of mountains or buried beneath terrestrial
sediments which is evidence of the higher and lower stands of the sea in prehistoric time

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
As previously mentioned our field exploration indicates that the project site is
generally underlain by loose to medium dense beach sands throughout the depths explored
Based on the subsurface soil conditions encountered at the project site we believe that a
shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be used for
support of the future comfort station building planned at the site Foundations for the comfort
station building should bear on the recompacted on site soils
In consideration of the lateral load and moment demands acting on the proposed light
pole foundations we recommend that drilled shafts embedded in the sandy soils be used to
support the proposed lighting system We recommend that the drilled shafts consist of
30 inch diameter shafts embedded a minimum of 8 feet below the lowest adjacent finished
grades for the proposed light pole foundations located on level ground conditions Detailed
discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering recommendations for design are
presented in the following sections of this report
Building Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe that
shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be used for support
of the future comfort station building planned at the site An allowable bearing pressure of
up to 2 500 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of foundations bearing
on the recompacted on site sands This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may
be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent grade If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements the
foundations should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from
the bottom edge of the utility trench or extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures
GEOLABS INC
W O 4506 00 Page 8
with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation
settlement
It should be noted that the near surface beach sands are poorly graded with a
relatively uniform particle size As such the sands may be difficult to compact particularly
when dry of the optimum moisture Therefore the bottom of the footing excavations should
be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to at least
90 percent relative compaction prior to the placement of reinforcing steel and concrete
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of
the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91
test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations
the soft and or loose materials should be removed to expose the underlying firm and or
dense materials The over excavation may be backfilled with the excavated on site sandy
materials compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate the total settlements of footings to be less than 1 inch
Differential settlements between adjacent footings should be on the order of about
0 5 inches
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 40 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted sand Resistance due
to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds
per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soils around the footings are
well compacted Unless covered by pavements or siabs the passive pressure resistance
in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected

Siabs On Grade
We envision that concrete slabs on grade will be provided for the comfort station and
surrounding sidewalks In general we recommend that the subgrade soils below the

For concrete slabs that will not be subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that

the proposed light pole structures should be supported by a foundation system consisting of
cast in place concrete drilled shafts As previously indicated we envision that the proposed
light poles will be of galvanized steel and or aluminum construction We anticipate that the
typical 25 foot high light poles would have the following load demands acting at the base of
the light poles

Based on the anticipated structural loads for the new light poles and the depth to

settlements of the light pole foundations due to compression loads are estimated to be on
the order of less than 0 25 inches We believe that the settlements are elastic and should
occur as the loads are applied

stiffness of the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable deflection at the top of

0 5 inches

It should be noted that loose to medium dense sands were encountered in our field
exploration at the site Therefore there is a potential for caving in of the loose to medium
dense sands if the sandy materials are dry or saturated To reduce the potential for caving in
of the drilled holes temporary casing may be required during the foundation construction

In addition care should be exercised during removal of the temporary casing to

Leach Field
We understand that on site sewage treatment and disposal by the leach field metho
is planned for the future comfort station Therefore field percolation tests were conducted
in our field exploration to obtain data pertaining to the preliminary design of the sewage
disposal system A total of three field percolation tests were performed at selected locations
designated as P 1 through P 3 as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
GEOLABS INC
W O 4506 00 Page 12
The field percolation tests were performed in general accordance with the State of
Systems and Chapter 10 of the Ten State s Standards to evaluate the permeability of the
subsurface soils for wastewater disposal The tests were performed by drilling 7 inch
diameter borehoies extending to depths of about 4 to 5 feet below the existing ground
surface Approximately 6 inches of gravel was then placed at the bottom of the borehole to
protect the bottom of the hole from scouring and sediments During the test the hole was
carefully filled with water to a minimum depth of 12 inches above the gravel The time
intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which the calculation of
the percolation test was based Based on the percolation tests conducted the percolation
rates at the site were generally less than 1 minute per inch Results of the individual
percolation tests are presented on Plates 3 1 through 3 3 of this report
Based on the Manual of Septic Tank Practice prepared by the U S Department of
Health Education and Welfare the proposed leach field locations with the percolation rates
obtained should be suitable for the siting of the absorption system for on site sewage effluent
disposal
Site Preparation
We envision that minimal site grading work will be required for the proposed project
construction In general we recommend that the following guidelines be used for the minor
site grading anticipated at the site At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract
grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish
asphaltic concrete pavements and other unsuitable materials within the grading limits should
be removed and disposed of properly off site
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to
receive fill or future improvements should be over excavated to expose firm or dense
material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill The
GEOLABS INC

excavated soft soils should be properly disposed of off site Contract documents should
include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and compacted fill placement
to account for variation in the over excavation quantities
Existing underground utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the resulting
excavation should be properly backfilled with on site fill materials moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content placed in 6 to 8 inch loose lifts and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
After clearing and grubbing areas beneath the future pavement areas should be
scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test
procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
The beach sands are uniformly graded and therefore may be difficult to compact
particularly when dry of optimum moisture Where the soil is unconfined such as at the
ground surface the material may be easily disturbed due to lack of cohesion and
interlocking Therefore the contractor should properly moisten the soils prior to compaction
and be careful not to disturb the final grades prior to placement of aggregate subbase base
course and or concrete
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend
that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or scarified and prepared again as
recommend above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to
inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and
replacement with well compacted fill at no additional cost to the owner

Utility Trenches
New utility lines and utility connections will likely be needed for the proposed project
In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials such as
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the initial trench
backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes
It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils The backfill
materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in
about 8 inch loose level lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground subsidence Where
trenches are located below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the
pavement grade should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction

Drainage
The finished grades outside the new comfort station should be sloped to shed water
away from the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that
gutter systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundations and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations
and pavement areas should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be
avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for significant water
infiltration into the subsurface
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be properly
backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce
the potential for significant water infiltration In addition drainage swales should be provided
as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the
foundations and pavement areas

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of about 6 5 to
16 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings drilled
are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as
the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
