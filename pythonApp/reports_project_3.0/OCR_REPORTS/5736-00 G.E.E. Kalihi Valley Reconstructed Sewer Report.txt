SECTION 2 0 SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Koolau
Volcano reached the end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago
The project site is along the southern flank of the Koolau Volcano adjacent to
Kalihi Stream The subsurface conditions at the project site are mainly composed of
terrigenous alluvial soils from Kalihi Stream deposited by continuous surface water
erosion of the Koolau volcanic dome and Koolau Basalt Land developments in the last
century and placement of man made fills have brought the project site to its present
form
2 2 Existing Site Conditions

2 3 Subsurface Conditions
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
eight test borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through and 11 The borings
extended to depths of about 21 5 to 30 feet below the ground surface The approximate
boring locations are shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 1 through 2 3
The borings drilled generally encountered a surface fill layer with thickness
ranging from about 1 to 4 5 feet The fill layer consisted of dense sandy silty gravel and
boulders The surface fill layer was underlain by alluvial residual saprolite and basalt
rock formation to 30 feet the maximum depth explored In one of the borings we
encountered a soil and cement layer about 4 feet thick below the surface fill layer
Below the surface fill layer we encountered an alluvial deposit in four of the
borings The alluvial deposit ranged from about 2 to 17 feet thick and generally
consisted of stiff to very stiff clay with gravel and cobbles We encountered residual and
saprolite deposits about 2 5 to 7 feet thick in three borings The residual and saprolite
deposits were comprised of very stiff to hard silty clay and sandy silt with gravel and
cobbles We encountered soft to very hard basalt rock formation in seven of the
eight borings
We encountered groundwater in one of the borings at a depth of about 17 5 feet
below the ground surface at the time of our field exploration The groundwater level
measured corresponds approximately to Elevation 153 5 feet MSL It should be noted
that groundwater levels are expected to vary with seasonal rainfall time of the year and
other factors

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our borings generally encountered a dense surface fill layer underlain by stiff to
very hard alluvial residual and saprolite deposits and soft to very hard basalt rock
formation to the maximum depth explored of about 30 feet below the existing ground
surface We encountered a soil and cement layer below the surface fill layer in one of
the borings We encountered groundwater in one of the borings at a depth of 17 5 feet
below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration
Based on the field exploration the replacement sewer line and manhole structures
will bear on the alluvium and basalt rock formation A granular bedding consisting of
6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel is recommended under the pipes and manhole structures
From the results of our field exploration we anticipate that hoeramming and or
chipping will be required for the basalt rock formation and for the cobbles and boulders
anticipated within the fill alluvial and saprolite deposits during the installation of the
replacement sewer line and manhole structures Detailed discussions and
recommendations for the replacement sewer line and manhole structures are further
discussed in the following sections herein
3 1 Underground Sewer Lines
The installation of the replacement sewer line will be performed using
conventional open trench method The invert depths of the new sewer line will range
from about 7 to 24 feet below the existing ground surface
Earth Pressure Loads on Pipes
Loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the trench the size of the
pipes the unit weight of backfill material and the frictional resistance between the
backfill material and the trench walls To calculate the vertical loads on the buried
sewer pipes we recommend using an average unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic
foot pcf for the backfill material and a coefficient of friction of 0 25 Earth forces
upon the pipe generally increase rapidly with the width of the trench Therefore the
width of the trench should be kept to a minimum In addition traffic loads on the

Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a great effect upon the load
supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore pipe bedding is an important design
consideration In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the sewer line pipes
The granular bedding should extend beyond the sides of the pipe a minimum width
of 24 inches A typical section of the trench detail is presented on Plate 3 for
reference
Backfill
In general free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be used for the initial trench backfill up to
about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and
to reduce the need for compaction of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of
pipe damage Compaction by water ponding or jetting should not be allowed
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of approved excavated
on site soils or select granular fill material The backfill should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in maximum
8 inch horizontal loose lifts and mechanically compacted to no less than 90 percent
relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground
subsidence
Where trenches are located below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench
backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to at least 95 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by
ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density

In order to reduce the potential for pavement distress resulting from the settlement
of utility trench backfill a temporary pavement section may be considered at the
completion of the trench work A permanent pavement section may be placed after
the backfilled area has come to equilibrium under backfill earth loads
In general backfill materials should consist of non expansive granular materials
generally less than 3 inches in maximum dimension with sufficient fines to prevent
the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass In general we believe that the
excavated on site sandy and gravelly soils with the exception of the clayey alluvial
deposits may be re used as a source of general backfill material at the site
provided that the materials are free of vegetation deleterious materials and rock
fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension
Where imported material is required as backfill the material should consist of
non expansive select granular material such as crushed coral basaltic gravel or
cinder sand Imported materials should be well graded from coarse to fine with
particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between
10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The imported fill material
should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum
swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883 Imported
materials should be tested for conformance with these recommendations prior to
delivery to the project site for the intended use
Pipe Settlement
Primary settlement of the new pipe installation is generally caused by the difference
in the unit weight of the excavated original earth and the compacted backfill
material placed over the pipeline The net increase in loading may cause settlement
of the underlying subsoils below the trench invert Based on our exploration and
analyses small primary settlements on the order of about less than 1 inch may be
anticipated for the new sewer line alignment

3 2 Excavation
In order to install the sewer lines we anticipate that excavations below the
existing ground surface will be required for the project construction Based on the
planned sewer line inverts we envision that temporary shoring of the excavations will
be required for the utility line installation
Excavation Method
In general the contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used
for excavation subject to practical limits and safety considerations Based on our
field exploration and the available information it is anticipated that hoeramming
and or chipping will be required within the hard basalt rock formation and for the
cobbles and boulders anticipated within the fill alluvial and saprolite deposits
during the sewer line and manhole installation The excavated soils should be
stockpiled no closer than a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the excavation
measured from the outside edge of the excavation in order to reduce the potential
for appreciable ground movement
Excavation Support
As previously mentioned the replacement sewer line inverts range from 7 to 24 feet
below the existing ground surface Because the replacement sewer line will be
along the roadway sloping of the sides of the trenches may not be possible due to
the limited space available Therefore temporary shoring of the sides of the
trenches may be necessary to facilitate the proposed trench excavations and to
protect personnel working in the trench
Because the site is underlain by dense and hard deposits installation of driven
sheet piles may be difficult The excavation support and shoring system used must
comply with all applicable safety requirements The contractor should retain a
qualified geotechnical engineer to design and evaluate the shoring system used
The contractor should be made solely responsible for the adequacy and safety of
the shoring installation His ner representative who should be required to be
continuously present on site during excavation and construction work will have the

Even if good construction procedures are followed some movement of the shoring

adjacent ground should be monitored continuously for cracks dips and or other
indications of movements

The soil backfill above the No 3B Fine gravel should be placed in thin lifts
normally 8 inches in loose lift thickness with each lift mechanically compacted
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to
the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Within the

Because the sewer line installation is in a developed area and hard excavation is

photographs and a crack survey to document the conditions of the existing buildings

Hard excavation is anticipated during sewer line construction within the

vibration caused during sewer line construction within the basalt rock formation

the construction methods may be necessary

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
eight borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through and 11 The borings
generally extended to depths ranging from about 21 5 to 30 feet below the existing
ground surface and were drilled at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plans
Plates 2 1 through 2 6 We drilled the borings using a truck mounted drill rig equipped
with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 8
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the site were obtained by
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings
at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests
are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are presented on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic presentation of the test
results is provided on Plate B 1
A one dimensional Consolidation test ASTM D 2435 was performed on a
selected soil sample to evaluate the consolidation characteristics of the soils Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
One Direct Shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected soil sample
to evaluate the shear strength parameters The test results are presented on Plate B 3
One Unconfined Compression test ASTM D 2166 was performed on a selected
in situ cohesive soil sample to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the
soils The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depth and graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 4
Nine Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on selected
rock core samples retrieved to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the
underlying basalt rock formation The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings
at the appropriate sample depth
