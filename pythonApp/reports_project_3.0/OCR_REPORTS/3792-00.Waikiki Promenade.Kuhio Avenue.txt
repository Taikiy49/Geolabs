INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Waikiki Promenade project to be located on Kalaimoku Street
between Kalakaua and Kuhio Avenues in the Waikiki area in the District of Honolulu on
the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommenda
tions resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses
These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations site grading and
pavements only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central
Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of Koolau and its geomorphology
and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea
level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain The
Subsurface conditions in the Waikiki area are dominated by fossil Pleistocene Age
coral algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of the sea which are
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3792 00 Page 5
intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic section in this area
is Koolau Basalt
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world These glacio
eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower
relative to present sea level on Oahu About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in
sea level occurred During that rise the valleys in the project area were drowned In the
last 10 000 years or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and
marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal
environments resulting in thick deposits of soft loose sediments along the coast in the
areas which were formerly valleys and drainageways
In the early part of this century the Waikiki area consisted of low marshy
wetlands As the city of Honolulu grew and the Waikiki area was urbanized man made
fills were placed to reclaim the marshy areas and lagoons The fill was placed upon
lagoonal deposits consisting of silty sands with coral fragments The lower clayey
material was deposited during periodic influxes of non marine alluvial debris which
apparently smothered the coral reef development Land development and reclamation
projects within the last 100 years have brought the Waikiki area to its present form Many
of the resulting fills are of poor quality in terms of supporting heavy structural loads

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 to depths ranging from
about 80 5 to 101 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of
the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our field exploration generally encountered a surface fill crust of about
2 to 3 feet thick consisting of loose to medium dense sands The near surface fill crust is
followed by soft to very soft marshy or lagoonal deposits overlying loose to very loose
granular lagoonal deposits The lagoonal deposits encountered consist of gray silty to
clayey coral sands and gravels extending to a depth of about 35 to 40 feet Below the
lagoonal deposits alluvial materials consisting of generally stiff to very stiff clayey silts
and silty clays were encountered to a depth of about 55 to 80 feet The alluvial materials
are followed by interbeds of alluvium and weathered coralline materials consisting of
medium dense to very dense sandy to gravely materials extending to the final depths of
the borings Asphalt concrete and base materials were encountered at the surface of
Boring Nos 2 and 4 drilled in the existing parking areas of the site

DI SION AND RECOMMENDATION
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered during our exploration and the
moderate to heavy structural loads of the proposed four story structures we recommend
that the proposed four story structures be supported on a deep foundation system
consisting of driven piles The driven pile foundations would extend through the soft
lagoonal deposits to bear in the stiff alluvial materials encountered starting at depths of
about 35 to 40 feet below the existing ground surface The proposed pile foundation
would derive support primarily from skin friction between the pile shaft and the stiff alluvial
materials and should extend to depths of about 55 to 70 feet below the surface in order to
develop the recommended compressive load capacity We recommend that either
12 inch square or 16 5 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles be used to
support the proposed structures
Since the site will need to be raised by filling to achieve the proposed finish floor
elevation of 9 feet MSL we envision that about 3 feet or less of new fills will be required
to achieve the proposed finish floor elevation Placement of approximately 3 feet of new
fills at the site will induce consolidation of the underlying soft loose lagoonal deposits
resulting in areal ground settlements Based on our settlement analyses we estimate
approximately 2 5 to 3 inches of ground settlements may occur due to the placement of

about 3 feet of new fills at the site Due to the high percentage of clayey materials within
the soft lagoonal deposits the estimated time needed to achieve approximately 75 to
80 percent consolidation should be about 4 to 5 months after the fills have been placed
A settlement monitoring program should be implemented to monitor the settlement
patterns and settlement rates during construction
Placement of the fill and the resulting ground settlements will also affect the
proposed pile foundations in the form of a downdrag load The magnitude of the
downdrag load can be quite significant on the order of 15 to 20 tons per pile In order to
reduce the adverse effects of the areal ground settlements on the proposed development
we recommend that construction of the proposed improvements above the new fills
including pile installation be delayed by at least 4 to 5 months after the fill placement in
order to allow the ground to stabilize under the new fill loads Otherwise the piles will
need to be designed for the significant downdrag loads
If the project construction is delayed by the estimated 4 to 5 months after the fill is
placed the ground floor slab for the proposed structures may be supported on grade As
an alternative in lieu of placing about 3 feet of new fills at the site to achieve the finish
floor elevation of 9 feet MSL consideration may be given to constructing a structural or
suspended slab for the first floor ground floor without filling the site In this way site
filling may be eliminated and settlements associated with site filling would be negligible
As a result construction of the project may proceed without the need for a settlement
waiting period of about 4 to 5 months

Pile Foundations
Our field exploration did not encounter a distinct or continuous coral formation with
sufficient strength and thickness to support end bearing piles The coral formation
30 foot coral ledge typically found in the area was not encountered in the four borings
drilled for this exploration However the coral ledge is present below the adjacent
Beachwalk Wastewater Pump Station site on the Diamond Head side of the project site
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we recommend that driven piles
developing frictional resistance in the alluvial materials be used to support the proposed
structures We believe that either 12 inch square or 16 5 inch octagonal precast
prestressed concrete piles driven into the stiff to very stiff alluvial materials may be used
to support the proposed four story structures
The 12 inch square precast prestressed concrete piles may be designed with an
allowable compressive load capacity of 45 tons with an estimated length varying between
95 to 70 feet below the surface For the 16 5 inch octagonal precast prestressed
concrete piles an allowable compressive load capacity of 60 tons may be achieved with
pile lengths of about 55 to 70 feet below the existing ground surface The allowable
compressive load capacities for the two pile types are for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces Piles should
be spaced a minimum of 2 5 times the pile width measured from center to center of the
piles to avoid reduction in vertical capacity due to group action and also to facilitate pile
driving in groups

Uplift Load Resistance
Uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the dead load of the driven pile
and by shear along the pile surface and the adjacent soils An uplift load capacity
of 50 kips may be used for the 12 inch square piles and an uplift load capacity of
70 kips may be used for the 16 5 inch octagonal piles The uplift load capacities
provided already include the weight of the pile These uplift load capacities
assume that the piles will be cast in one continuous section with no pile splicing
The capacity of the pile member in tension should be checked by the project
structural engineer

Lateral Load Resistance
Lateral loads imposed on the building structures may be resisted by a combination
of the lateral load capacity of the driven piles and the passive pressure against the
vertical faces of the pile caps grade beams Passive earth pressure against the
vertical faces of the pile cap grade beam may be estimated using an equivalent
fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f above the
groundwater level and 125 p c f below the groundwater level
The lateral load resistance of driven piles is a function of the stiffness of the
surrounding soil the stiffness of the pile allowable deflection at the top of the pile
and the induced moment in the pile The lateral load analyses were performed
using the program LPILE plus for Windows which is a microcomputer adaptation
of a finite difference laterally loaded pile program originally developed at the
University of Texas at Austin The program solves for deflection and bending
moment along a pile under lateral loads as a function of depth The analysis was
carried out with the use of internally generated non linear p y curves to represent
soil moduli The lateral deflection was then computed using the appropriate soil
moduli at various depths The analyses were performed for a laterally loaded
12 inch square pile and 16 5 inch octagonal pile
A minimum pile spacing of 10 feet is required in order to utilize the full lateral load
capacity shown for the single pile condition For piles that are spaced less than
10 feet on center the effect of group action was considered in our analysis by
reducing the effective horizontal modulus of subgrade reaction in the direction of
loading These values assume that piles in the direction of loading are spaced at
least 2 5 times the width of the pile measured from center to center If closer
spacing is required Geolabs Hawaii should be consulted for supplemental lateral
load resistance recommendations

Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a minimum energy
of 50 000 foot pounds in the field The hammer should be equipped with an
energy level control such that driving within the loose soft soils may be controlled
at a lower energy to reduce tensile stress development within the piles which
could damage or cause cracking of the piles
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the matching of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be
driven This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy
to the top of the pile and also reducing damage to the piles Geolabs Hawaii
should review the pile driving equipment proposed to evaluate if the equipment is
capable of installing the piles to the recommended capacity The contractor
should forward pile and driving equipment data forms to Geolabs Hawaii prior to
construction for evaluation Geolabs Hawaii should review the submittal and
assess the driveability of piles with the use of the selected hammer

the last 3inches This preliminary driving criteria should be confirmed after the
selection of the pile driving equipment and during the test pile driving to
accommodate variations of subsurface conditions in the field

Site Grading
Based on a proposed finish floor elevation of 9 feet MSL the site will need to be
raised by up to about 3 feet of new fills in order to attain the finish floor elevation As
previously indicated placement of new fills at this site will induce consolidation of the
soft loose lagoonal deposits underlying the site Therefore it is important that the new
fills be placed as soon as practicable to allow the ground settlements to occur first prior to
construction of any improvements such as pile installation and slab construction to
reduce the adverse effects of potential ground settlements The following
recommendations are intended to provide guidelines for the design of site grading work
After demolition of the existing structures and pavements all debris generated
from the demolition should be removed and properly disposed of off site The

Settlement Monitoring
Based on our engineering analyses we estimate about 2 5 to 3 inches of ground
settlements may occur as a result of the up to 3 feet of new fill placement at the site
Based on the consolidation test performed we believe that a settlement period of about
4to5 months after the fills are placed would be required in order to achieve
approximately 75 to 80 percent consolidation of the soft loose lagoonal deposits
underlying the site The new fills should be placed as early as possible to induce the
anticipated settlement
It should be recognized that it is difficult to accurately predict the exact time
required for the filled ground to settle since the settlement rate will be affected by
variations of the subsoil structure and the history of the subsoil deposition For the very
soft clayey lagoonal deposits we believe that the estimated settlement period could vary
as much as 50 to 100 percent from the actual settlement period required Therefore the
actual settlement rate should be monitored and provisions should be made for potential
delays in the construction schedule if a longer settlement period is required
To monitor the actual settlement rate we recommend that settlement gauges be
installed in the fill areas The number and location of the settlement gauges will be
evaluated by our office after the grading plan is finalized A typical settlement gauge is
shown on Plate 3 The gauges should be read optically by a qualified professional
surveyor and the readings should be transmitted to Geolabs Hawaii for review We
recommend that two readings one week apart for each settlement gauge be taken prior

Drainage
The finished grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations
and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent loss of strength of the near surface soils The foundation
excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as
possible and maintained to drain all surface run off away from the slabs and footing

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results
of these tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plate B 1
One direct shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected sample of the
soils to evaluate the shear strength characteristics Results of the direct shear test are
presented on Plate B 2
Two sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Results of these tests are presented on Plate B 3
One consolidation test ASTM D 2435 was performed on a selected sample of the
soils to evaluate compressibility characteristics of the on site soils Results of the
consolidation test are presented on Plate B 4
