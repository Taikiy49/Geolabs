Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene Epoch in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The
volcanic materials erupted from the Waianae Volcano is referred to as the Waianae
Volcanic Series
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the
island Volcanic materials erupted from the Koolau Volcano are referred to as the
Koolau Volcanic Series Waianae Volcano became extinct while Koolau Volcano
remained active therefore the Waianae Volcano s eastern flank was partially buried
below the younger Koolau lavas that banked against its eastern flank These banked or
ponded lavas formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau of Central
Oahu The present day form of the Island of Oahu is a result of stream dissection and
mass erosion of the steep valley walls
During the history of the evolution of the Island of Oahu multiple global
fluctuations in sea level occurred as a result of the advance and retreat of the great
continental glaciers These sea level changes occurred more substantially in the
Pleistocene Epoch and had some significant effect on the geologic evolution of the
Island of Oahu The changes in worldwide sea level affected the erosional baseline of
streams and caused local submergence and emergence of the coastal landforms with
respect to the level of the sea
The end of volcanic activity associated with the Koolau Volcano was followed by
a long period of erosion whereby deep valleys were carved into the Koolau Mountain
Range The valleys were filled with thick accumulations of alluvial sediments as the
island slowly sank under the weight of the new landmass

Based on the International Building Code 2006 Edition IBC 2006 as amended
the project site may be subject to seismic activity and seismic design considerations will
need to be addressed The following subsections provide discussions on the seismicity
and soil profile type for seismic design
Earthquake and Seismicity
In general earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic
plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic
activity therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during
volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most are so
small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments However some of

In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the

the Island of Hawaii Because majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii

Shallow Building Foundations
Based on the proposed finish elevation of 88 feet MSL and the generally
competent subsoil conditions encountered at the site we believe that shallow spread
and or continuous footings may be used for support of the proposed Pacific Buddhist
Academy Building II
An allowable bearing pressure of may be utilized for the design of
building foundations bearing on properly compacted fill or the on site very stiff to hard

pressures A typical drainage system consists of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
