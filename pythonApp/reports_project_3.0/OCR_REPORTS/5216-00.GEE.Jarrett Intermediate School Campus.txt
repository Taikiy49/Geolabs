2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older shield volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger shield Koolau Volcano is estimated to
be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity
during which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These
series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series The project site is located at the
southwestern flank of the Koolau Mountain Range
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of
the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available
such that the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii and in the heads of valleys the erosional
processes are dominated by detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and
are transported downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium
Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes
become dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium

Due to the high to very high shrink swell potential of the clayey soils encountered at
shallow depths at the project site the clay likely will swell and lose substantial
strength when saturated and will shrink appreciably when subjected to drying
resulting in shrinkage cracks In general the mechanism of shrink swell in
expansive soils is a complex phenomenon and is influenced by a number of
factors including soil characteristics and environmental factors
Expansive soil characteristics generally can be measured by engineering properties
such as soil plasticity and density to evaluate the shrink swell potential of soils In
general the soil plasticity reflects the capacity of the clay minerals in the soil to
retain large amounts of water while maintaining a coherent structure Therefore a
highly plastic soil such as those found at the project site will have a greater
potential for volume change shrink and swell due to the loss or absorption of
moisture under cycles of drying and wetting In addition increased soil density
through compaction or natural depositional history such as desiccation generally
has greater shrink swell potential
Environmental factors comprise of the initial moisture content of the soil and
moisture variations over time The initial moisture content generally impact the
potential of a soil to absorb or expel water from the soil system In general a dry

non expansive select granular fill material be provided below the building slab
areas The non expansive select granular fill should extend to the edge of the
concrete sidewalk or concrete apron surrounding the building The non expansive
select granular fill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Select granular fill should consist of non expansive select granular materials such
as crushed coral basaltic gravel or cinder sand The materials should be well

Hawaii California

graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest
dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the
No 200 sieve The select granular fill materials should have a laboratory
CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less
when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883
In addition we recommend that the subgrade soils below the select granular fill be
compacted to a firm and unyielding condition prior to the placement of the select
granular fill We believe that the subgrade soils are very moist to wet at this time
which does not permit scarifying and recompacting the subgrade soils typically
required for new construction
We also recommend that the subgrade be graded to slope toward the subdrain
system and a layer of non woven geotextile fabric Mirafi 180N or equivalent
materials be placed over the subgrade before the placement of the select granular
fill The geotextile fabric is placed under the select granular fill to prevent fines from
falling into the shrinkage cracks formed during the dry seasons and to reduce the
erosion problems under the slabs on grade
We also recommend that the interior building slabs on grades have a minimum
thickness of 6 inches and be reinforced with No 4 reinforcing bars spaced at
2inches in each direction in lieu of the standard welded wire fabric This
recommendation is provided to reduce the potential for structural distress due to
differential movements of the slabs due to the expansive nature of the on site soils
In addition we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill

moisture break To reduce the potential for appreciable future moisture infiltration

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling 16 test borings The 16 test borings drilled at the project site were distributed
as described below Six of the borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 were
drilled at the areas adjacent to the existing buildings Buildings A through F The six
test borings were extended to depths of about 25 to 36 5 feet below the existing ground
surface Ten borings designated as Boring 11 and 12
were drilled at the existing sidewalks surrounding the buildings extending to depths of
approximately 0 5 to 6 5 feet below the existing concrete floor level The approximate
locations of the borings drilled for the project are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The
borings were drilled using both truck mounted drill rig Boring Nos 1 through 6 and
portable drilling equipment Boring Nos 7 through 16 equipped with continuous flight
augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered in the borings drilled are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 16
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and
Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration
Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
