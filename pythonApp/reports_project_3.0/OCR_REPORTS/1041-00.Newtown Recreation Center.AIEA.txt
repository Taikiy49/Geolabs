As requested we have performed a soil engineering reconnais
Sance study at the above referenced project site The pur
pose of the reconnaissance was to evaluate the subsurface
soil conditions at the proposed retaining wall location in
order to provide recommendations for the design and construc
tion of the proposed wall and to evaluate the possible causes
of the reported ground subsidence at the proposed children s
playground location
Our investigation consisted of 4 borings at the proposed
retaining wall location and 9 hand auger probings in the pro
posed children s playground location
The results of our field explorations and laboratory tests
which were used in the engineering analysis to formulate our

of this report
SITE DESCRIPTION
The existing Newtown Recreation Center is Situated about 250
feet northwest of Kaahele Street approximately 2000 feet
west of Komo Mai Drive
The recreation center consists of several main one and two
story buildings with a large swimming pool tennis basket
ball and volleyball courts The site is situated along the
side slopes of a relatively deep and steep sided drainageway
The subject improvements are situated along the existing cut
slope on the eastern side of the site and at the top of the
existing natural drainageway side slope along the western
side of the site
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Subsurface Exploration
Four borings ranging in depth from 5 5 to 15 5 feet below
the existing ground surface were drilled along the proposed
retaining wall locations in order to explore the subsurface
soil conditions and to obtain samples for laboratory testing
Nine hand auger probings were drilled to depths of 3 to 10
feet in the proposed childrens playground area in order to
evaluate the consistency of the near surface soils
Soil Conditions
The borings along the proposed retaining wall location
encountered very stiff clayey silt with decomposed rock
residual soil to the depth of the borings Field obser
vations of the existing cut slope bank indicated similar
material to that encountered in the borings

tively loose
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Retaining Wall
Based on the data obtained from the field exploration very
stiff residual soil is anticipated below and behind the pro
posed retaining wall
The wall foundations should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches
below the finished grade
An allowable bearing value of 4000 p s f can be used on the
very stiff natural soil
Well graded granular material such as base course rock 3B
Fine gravel sand or equivalent should be used as backfill
material behind the walls Impervious on site silty mater
ial should be used for the top 2 feet of the backfill

We recommend that the walls should be designed to resist
lateral earth pressures as follows
1 Active condition level _ 35 p c f equivalent
backfill fluid pressure
2 Active condition Py 45 p c f
sloping backfill P 20 p c f
A friction factor of 0 45 can be used to determine the slid
ing resistance of the wall
Additionally a passive soil resistance of 300 p c f can be
used
A subsurface drainage system should be used to prevent the
build up of hydrostatic pressure behind the walls
Alternatively if sufficient space is available steeper cut
Slopes could be utilized in lieu of the wall We feel that
a cut slope ratio of 1 1 H V could be used in the resid
ual soil A steeper cut slope ratio of 1 1 H V could be
utilized if a concrete lining is also incorporated in the
design to protect the cut slope face from erosion and ravel
ling
Pavement
Asphaltic concrete pavement is planned for the new parking
areas along the proposed retaining wall

P 6
The laboratory CBR test indicates that the natural soil may
be moderately expansive The recommended preliminary pave
ment design is as follows
2 inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 inch Base Course
6 inch Select Borrow
14 inch Total Pavement Thickness
The base course should be crushed rock or other granular
material with a CBR of 85 and conforming with Section 31 2
Standard Specification for Public Work Construction or equiv
alent Non expansive select borrow should have a minimum
CBR value of 25 and consist of crushed rock mudrock coral
sand or cinder material
Base course and select borrow should be compacted to 95 of
its maximum density
Additional CBR tests should be performed on the actual sub
grade soil samples at the time of roadway construction and
the above design section can be revised accordingly
Children s Playground
The field exploration encountered relatively loose fill up
to 4 5 feet thick along the edge of the slope near Probe
Nos P 1 P 2 and P 4 locations This loose fill should be
overexcavated and recompacted to a minimum of 90 of its

should be properly keyed into the stiff natural ground and
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in thickness
Two small sinkholes about 8 inches deep were noted near
the northern edge of the pool overflow structure These
holes appear to be a result of washing out of the upper fill
This area should also be overexcavated and recompacted as

The remainder of the area appears to be relatively compacted

Structures placed near the edge of the slope should be embed

slope creep

CONSTRUCTION MONITORING
It is recommended that the soils engineer be present during
earthwork construction particularly the overexcavation and
recompaction operations
It is also recommended that the footing excavations be observed
by a soils engineer during construction to see that adequate
bearing has been obtained and that the subsurface conditions
are consistent with the findings of our subsurface investiga
tion
The recommendations given in this report are contingent upon
the above inspections

APPENDIX A
FIELD EXPLORATION
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by
drilling 4 borings from 5 5 to 15 5 feet and 9 probings from
3 0 to 10 0 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on Plates 1 and 2 Portable hand
auger drilling equipment was utilized for the probings since
the site was inaccessible for conventional drilling equipment
The soils encountered in the borings were classified by
visual and textural examination in the field by our engineer
ing geologist who continuously monitored the drilling opera
tions The classifications were reviewed by visual inspec
tion and testing in the laboratory The soils were classi
fied in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classifica
tion System A graphic presentation of the soils is presented
on the Boring and Probing Logs Plates A 1 through A 9
In the hand auger probings disturbed samples were
obtained from the auger cuttings
Soil samples were obtained by driving either a 2 4 inch
I D ring sampler or a standard penetration sampler with a
140 pound hammer free falling a distance of 30 inches
