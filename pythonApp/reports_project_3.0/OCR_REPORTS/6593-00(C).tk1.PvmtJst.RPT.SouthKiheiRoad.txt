SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration along the proposed roadway rehabilitation project indicated 
that the existing pavement structure is generally underlain by medium dense to dense 
poorly-graded sands extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 5.8 feet 
below the existing pavement surface.  We did not encounter groundwater in the shallow 
borings drilled at the time of our field exploration. 
Because the project will involve the reconstruction of the existing roadway, we have 
performed a detailed economic comparison involving both flexible and rigid pavement 
sections for the project in accordance with the latest guidelines provided by the State of 
Hawaii - Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Material Testing and Research 
Branch. 
Based on our field exploration program and the current design concept, we envision 
that the new pavements for the project will be constructed over the in-situ sandy soils in 
the shallow subsurface.  Based on the anticipated subgrade conditions, the traffic data 
provided for our pavement analyses, the economic analysis performed, and construction 
considerations, it is our opinion that new pavements for the roadway rehabilitation project 
may consist of 4 inches of AC over 6 inches of ACB on 4 inches of ASB.  It should be 
noted that CBR tests and/or field observations should be performed on the actual 
subgrade soils during construction to confirm that the above pavement design section is 
adequate. 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to 
saturation of the base, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the pavement surface 
should be sloped, and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface water off 
the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
pavement design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This pavement justification report presents the results of our geotechnical 
engineering analyses performed for the South Kihei Road Pavement Rehabilitation 
project in Kihei on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are 
shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the design of new pavements only. The findings and recommendations presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The roadway rehabilitation project is along South Kihei Road extending from 
Alanui Ke Alii Road to the south entrance of Kamaole III Park’s parking lot for a distance 
of approximately 0.8 miles. The approximate limits of the roadway rehabilitation project 
are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Based on the information provided, we understand that it is desired to reconstruct 
the existing pavements with a new pavement structural section.  We understand that the 
proposed construction will be a federally funded project. Therefore, a pavement 
justification report conforming to the requirements and guidelines of the State of Hawaii 
- Department of Transportation will be required. The new pavements for the project will 
be designed based on the methodology described in the Pavement Design Manual 
dated March 2002 prepared by the State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation, 
Highways Division, Materials Testing and Research Branch. 
Special attention was given to the potential presence of near-surface soft and/or 
loose soils, which may be a contributing factor to the pavement failures observed along 
portions of the roadway under consideration. The findings obtained from our field 
exploration and laboratory testing programs will be used as a basis to formulate 
geotechnical engineering recommendations for the pavement reconstruction for the 
project roadway.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes, the older West Maui 
(Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala (Pleistocene 
Epoch). The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain located between these 
two volcanoes. The project site is located at the northern area of this gently sloping 
plain. 
The Isthmus of Maui was created by lava flows from East Maui (Haleakala) 
ponding on West Maui. It is comprised of alluvium washed from the slopes of West Maui 
and Haleakala. The erosional processes are dominated by the detachment of soil and 
rock masses from the mountain walls, and the soil materials are transported downslope 
toward the Isthmus primarily by gravity as colluvium. Once these materials reach the 
stream in the central portion of a valley, alluvial processes become dominant, and the 
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium. In general, stream flows in Hawaii 
are intermittent and flashy, such that the stream flows transmit large volumes of water 
for very short duration. Because of this, the transport of sediments is intermittent, and 
the bulk of the stream's hydraulic load consists of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders, 
cobbles, gravel, sands, and fines. When the erosional base levels change, these 
sediment loads are left as deposits. 
When deposits are left in-place for long periods of time, chemical processes 
begin to alter the materials, simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the 
materials. Chemical processes also cause induration, or cementation, of the 
coarse-grained portion of the sediment into a poorly-consolidated sedimentary rock or 
conglomerate. Simultaneously, erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors 
and upstream in headwaters. This continued erosion generates material, which is 
transported downslope covering the older alluvial deposits. Depending on the local base 
level and rate of transport, these newer sediments are generally transient in terms of 
geologic time. In addition, their consistency and density are generally less than those of 
the older, partially consolidated deposits.

Underlying the alluvial deposits are overlapping lavas from the West Maui and 
East Maui volcanoes. The bulk of the Haleakala Shield was built during the late 
Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows of the 
Honomanu Volcanic Series. During the Pleistocene Epoch, the characteristics of the 
lavas changed to very hard, thickly bedded flows of andesitic composition. These lavas 
have been grouped as the Kula Volcanic Series. Typically, the basalt consists of thinly 
to thickly bedded a’a and pahoehoe type flows. 
2.2 
Existing Site/Pavement Conditions 
The existing South Kihei Road within the project limits is generally a two-lane 
undivided roadway except for the additional turn lanes at the intersection areas.  The 
approximate limits of the roadway are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The shoulder areas beyond the edge of pavement generally consist of short cut 
grass, side walks, traffic signs and utility posts. The surface soils along the shoulder 
areas below the vegetation generally consist of tannish brown coralline sands with silt. 
The existing South Kihei Road alignment generally slopes down from north to south, 
between the limits of Ke Alii Alanui Drive and the south entrance to the Kamaole III 
Park’s parking lot. 
Our office conducted a site reconnaissance in February 2012 to collect general 
information for the existing pavement conditions along the portion of the roadway under 
consideration. Detailed descriptions of our field surveys are presented in the following 
subsections. It should be noted that the term “Structural Defects” refers to areas where 
the pavement deterioration are believed to be caused by failures in the underlying base, 
subbase, and/or subgrade materials. The term “Ride Characteristic” refers to the 
response of a vehicle when passing at normal speed over the roadway. 
2.2.1 Areas with Minor to Moderate Distresses and Severity 
Based on our site reconnaissance, the majority of South Kihei Road within the 
existing pavements under consideration falls into this category and appear to be 
generally fair with minor signs of structural defects. Overall pavement deterioration

Methodology of Pavement Design 
The pavement structural section presented herein was generally determined 
based on the methodology described in Chapter 3 of the Pavement Design Manual 
dated March 2002. The Pavement Design Manual was prepared by the State of Hawaii

Branch. The pavement design methodology is based on the Hveem Stabilometer

The design procedures for rigid pavements are based on the design procedures of the 
Portland Cement Association “Thickness Design for Concrete Highway and Street 
Pavements.”

Our field exploration and laboratory testing indicate that the roadway under 
consideration for this project is underlain by sandy soils with little or no cohesion.  As a 
result, the subgrade may not be able to compact into a dense mass to receive 
placement of the asphalt concrete base (ACB) or Portland Cement Concrete (PCC) 
layer.  Therefore, we recommend providing a 4-inch layer of aggregate subbase 
between the upper ACB or PCC layer and the underlying sandy subgrade soils.  The 
aggregate subbase will serve to provide confinement of the sandy soils and a 
compacted surface for the placement of the ACB or PCC.  Based on this construction 
consideration, we recommend using the following flexible and rigid pavement design 
sections for the roadways under consideration for this project.

An economic analysis was performed on the two pavement design options 
presented above to evaluate the initial construction cost and life cycle cost of the 
pavement structural sections. Based on our cost comparison of the pavement design 
options, it appears that the flexible pavement structural section (Option A) would result

The exposed pavement subgrade materials should be proof-rolled with a large 
vibratory drum roller (minimum 10 tons static weight) a minimum of eight passes to help 
detect and collapse near-surface loose zones. The vibratory drum roller should be 
operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor the pavement subgrade preparation to 
observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and 
scarification process and to confirm whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar 
to those encountered in our field exploration. 
Aggregate subbase materials required for the project should consist of crushed 
basaltic aggregates and should conform to Subsection 703.17 of the State of Hawaii, 
Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction (2005).  Aggregate subbase 
material should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers 
to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry 
density of the same soil established in accordance with AASHTO T-180 or 
ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Asphalt concrete base (ACB) material should consist of asphalt-treated basalt 
aggregate, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and 
compacted to no less than 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity 
determined in accordance with AASHTO T-209 or ASTM D 2041. Asphaltic concrete 
(AC) material should be constructed in accordance with Section 401 Asphalt Concrete 
Pavement of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge 
Construction (2005) and its amendments.

3.5 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development 
of good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage 
structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration 
or premature failure of the pavements.

If the actual soil conditions encountered during construction are different from

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from the test borings, bulk samples, and laboratory test data. 
Variations of conditions between and beyond the test borings and bulk samples may 
occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until 
construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to 
re-evaluate the recommendations presented herein.

reference points and visible features shown on the Topographic Survey Map provided 
by the County of Maui - Department of Public Works, Engineering Division on 
April 12, 2012. Elevations of the field borings were estimated by interpolation from the 
contour lines shown on the same plan. The physical locations and elevations of the 
borings should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification breaks shown on the graphic representations of the borings 
depict the approximate boundaries between soil/rock types and, as such, may denote a 
gradual transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown 
on the graphic representations and/or presented in the text herein. These data have 
been reviewed and interpretations made to formulate this report. However, it must be 
noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in rainfall, temperature, and other 
factors.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway alignment by drilling and 
sampling six borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 6. The borings extended to 
depths of about 4 to 5.8 feet below the existing pavement surface. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-6. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.
