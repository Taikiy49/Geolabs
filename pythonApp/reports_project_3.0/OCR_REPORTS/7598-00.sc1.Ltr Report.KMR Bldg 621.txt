Dear Ms. Miyasato: 
In accordance with our fee proposal dated August 23, 2017, this letter report 
presents the results of our permeability testing conducted for the Hawaii Department of 
Defense Keaukaha Military Reservation Building 621, Underground Injection Control 
(UIC) project in the Hilo airport area on the Island of Hawaii. The project location and 
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes our field observations and presents the results of our 
permeability testing and engineering analyses for the project.  These findings are 
intended to support the preparation of a UIC Injection Well Status Report for Injection 
Well Nos. 1 and 2 at the project site. The findings and recommendations presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
Based on the information provided, the Hawaii Department of Defense Building 
621 facility at the Keaukaha Military Reservation (KMR) operates two (2) injection wells, 
identified as Well Nos. 1 and 2, for the on-site disposal of septic effluent. The KMR 
Building 621 facility is identified by the UIC Facility I.D. No. 8-4203.19. Injection Well 
Nos. 1 and 2 at the KMR Building 621 facility is regulated by UIC Permit No. UH-2509.  
We understand that observation and permeability testing services are desired in 
support of the preparation of a UIC Injection Well Status Report for Well Nos. 1 and 2 at 
the Hawaii Army National Guard Battery Building 621 facility at the Keaukaha Military 
Reservation in Hilo, Hawaii.

PURPOSE AND SCOPE 
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering services was to obtain an overview 
of the existing condition of the injection wells by visual observation and short-term 
permeability injection testing in support of a UIC Injection Well Status Report prepared 
by others for the project. The work was performed in general accordance with the scope 
of services outlined in our fee proposal dated August 23, 2017.  Generally, the scope of 
work included the following tasks: 
1. 
Review of available documentations pertaining to the UIC permit 
application. 
2. 
Mobilization of a water truck with one operator to the project site. 
3. 
Performance of short-term duration permeability tests to evaluate the 
disposal capacity of the existing wells. 
4. 
Analysis of the field data by our engineer. 
5. 
Preparation of a letter report summarizing our work on the project and 
presenting our findings and recommendations. 
6. 
Coordination of our overall work on the project by our geologist. 
7. 
Quality assurance of our work and client/design team consultation by our 
principal engineer. 
8. 
Miscellaneous work efforts such as drafting, word processing and clerical 
support. 
EXISTING WELL AND SITE CONDITIONS 
The location of Well Nos. 1 and 2 is within the Keaukaha Military Reservation 
adjacent to Building 621, as approximately indicated on the Project Location Map, 
Plate 1. Well No. 1 is on the southerly side of Building 621 and serves the women’s 
latrine and septic tank system. Well No. 2 is on the northerly side of Building 621 and 
serves the men’s latrine and septic tank system. Photograph Nos. 1 and 6 show the 
existing conditions at the Well No. 1 well head and vicinity. Photograph Nos. 7 and 12 
show the existing conditions at the Well No. 2 well head and vicinity. 
Both well heads are sited on generally level ground with lawn grass surrounding 
the well heads and vicinity. We observed that the lawn surface surrounding the well 
heads and vicinity was uniform in color and contained no ponded water accumulations. 
The surface completions of both wells consist of a steel manhole cover set flush with 
the ground surface. Below the manhole cover, access to the well is via a concrete plug 
cover measuring about 16 inches square. Photograph Nos. 2 and 3 show the existing 
condition of the well access manhole and the concrete plug cover at Well No. 1.

Photograph Nos. 8 and 9 show the existing condition of the well access manhole and

Our geologists conducted the assessment of Well Nos. 1 and 2 during two site 
visits which were made on November 8 and November 21, 2017. During the 
November 8 site visit, we observed that the concrete plug well covers at Well Nos. 1 
and 2 would require mechanical assistance to lift and open the plug cover from the well 
head. In addition, we observed that a lifting handle for the concrete plug at Well No. 2 
was missing and would need to be installed in order to open the well with mechanical 
assistance. Thus, inspection and testing of the wells could not be conducted on the 
November 8th site visit. 
However, because access to Well Nos. 1 and 2 was not possible during our 
November 8th site visit, we opened the manhole covers to the two septic tanks located 
upstream from Well Nos. 1 and 2. We observed that the septic tank upstream from Well 
No. 1 was nearly full but appeared to be functioning satisfactorily. We observed that the 
septic tank upstream from Well No. 2 was completely full with sludge material visible at 
the top of the tank vent pipe. The full tank suggested that the tank required a 
pumping/cleaning effort to restore capacity. We notified the operator of the facility of the 
need to service both septic tanks and provide assistance with installing a cover lift 
handle at Well No. 2 and to provide assistance to open the heavy well covers for our 
inspection and testing. 
A return site visit for the well inspection and testing was conducted on 
November 21, 2017 after being informed by the facility operator that the tanks had been 
serviced, a lift handle installed, and the well covers would be opened for us.  
Well No. 1 
Well No. 1 is on the southerly side of Building 621 in an open lawn area adjacent 
to the building. The well head is on generally firm level ground as shown in Photograph 
Nos. 1, 2, and 6. 
The well head is generally constructed flush with the existing lawn surface and 
consists of a steel manhole cover at the ground surface with a recessed access port 
consisting of a concrete plug cover measuring about 16 inches square. We observed 
that the concrete plug cover seats firmly into the concrete well head surface with 
minimal gap space. Photograph No. 3 shows the existing condition of the concrete plug 
cover. 
We observed steel manhole covers upstream from the Well No. 1 well head that 
house access ports associated with the septic tank. No other existing above-ground 
appurtenances were observed associated with the well operation. 
With the well cover open, we sensed a mild to moderate sewage odor emanating 
from the well. The existing fluid level in the well consisted of an approximate 0.65-foot

PERMEABILITY TESTING 
Our evaluation of the well permeability characteristics was conducted on 
November 21, 2017 between the hours of about 0900 and 1500 under generally dry 
weather and ground surface conditions. 
Permeability testing for Well Nos. 1 and 2 generally involved the short-term 
gravity injection of clear water from a water tanker truck with measurement of the 
increasing head followed by measurement of the falling head in the well until the well

drained to the pre-test condition. The permeability testing was conducted in an effort to 
evaluate the drainage (infiltration) functionality of the existing wells. 
Prior to the start of each of the two permeability tests, no septic effluent was 
observed discharging into the well. The tests were initiated by discharging 
approximately 1,800 gallons of water from a water tank under gravity feed through a 
2-inch diameter hose placed in the lower portion of the well.  
It should be noted that Well No. 1 contained a static head of about 8 inches in 
depth of fluid prior to the start of the test. In contrast, Well No. 2 was dry prior to the 
start of the test. No discharge effluent from the septic system was received at the wells 
during the testing of both wells. 
Well No. 1 
Based on our measurements, approximately 1,800 gallons of clear water was 
discharged to the well from the water tanker over a period of about 27 minutes, resulting 
in an average discharge flow rate of about 67 gallons per minute (gpm). The total 
accumulated head was measured at 3.05 feet above the bottom of the well at the end of 
the short-term injection portion of the test. 
When the short-term injection discharge ended, a falling head test was 
performed to record the fall of the accumulated water level within the well. Based on the 
results of the falling head test, a time elapse of about 180 minutes was needed to drain 
the well to a head depth of about 2.25 feet in the well. The resulting head at the end of 
the falling head test was approximately 1.6 feet above the pre-test head level of 
0.65 feet measured in the well. 
Based on the results of the short-term injection and falling head permeability test, 
the operation and capacity of the well appear to be good and is well within the needed 
capacity for the existing facility needs.

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the results of our visual observations and the short-term permeability 
testing of Well Nos. 1 and 2 at the KMR Building 621 facility, the wells appear to be 
operating satisfactorily with no overt indication of potential failure.   
Based on the falling head permeability testing conducted, Well No. 1 has an 
effluent capacity of about 10 gpm, equivalent to about 14,400 gallons per day (gpd), 
which exceeds the estimated design flow capacity of 3,000 gpd. Based on our 
engineering analysis, the coefficient of permeability of the subsurface ground condition 
within the bottom 3 feet of Well No. 1 was estimated to be on the order of magnitude of 
about 0.0012 feet per minute (about 0.0006 centimeters per second).  
Based on the falling head permeability testing conducted, the Well No. 2 has an 
effluent capacity of at least 250 gpm, equivalent to about 360,000 gallons per day (gpd), 
which exceeds the estimated design flow capacity of 3,000 gpd. Based on our 
engineering analysis, the coefficient of permeability of the subsurface ground condition 
within the bottom 1 foot of the Well No. 2 was estimated to be on the order of magnitude 
of about 0.08 feet per minute (about 0.04 centimeters per second).  
Details of the falling head permeability analysis are presented on Plates A-1 and 
A-2 of Appendix A. Water level measurements during the permeability testing at each 
well location are presented on Plates A-3 and A-4 for reference.

Based on our observation of the existing well site and vicinity conditions, we 
recommend the following to maintain the performance of the wells. 
Perform routine inspection of the fluid levels contained in the septic tank to 
gauge when servicing may be needed and schedule servicing to include 
the pumping and cleaning of the septic tank system on a regular basis. 
Periodically exercise any upstream flow control valves to ensure operation 
and functionality. 
Continue to conduct annual injection well inspections as per the UIC 
permit conditions. 
Review and abide by the best practice guidelines for effluent discharge 
that was provided in the Environmental Notice dated December 2016 and 
provided to the facility operator by the State of Hawaii Depart of Health 
Safe Drinking Water Branch Underground Injection Control (UIC) 
Program.
