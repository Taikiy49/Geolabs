INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Douglass Residence at Lot 23 along Puako Beach Drive Tax Map Key
06 23 in the District of South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii The general location
and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings based on the field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed residential project These recommendations are intended for the
design of house foundations slabs on grade retaining structures and site preparation only
and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located at Lot 23 along Puako Beach Drive 06 23 in the

elevate the first level of the residential structure above the flood elevation of 8 feet
Mean Sea Level MSL In addition we understand that a swimming pool will be

finished grades

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
GEOLABS INC
W O 4318 00 Page 4
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of Mauna Kea fronting the
shoreline on the western portion of the Island of Hawaii The project site is underlain by
beach sand deposits and fill materials The beach sand deposits consist of coral and
volcanic gravel and sands deposited from wave action along the shoreline The lava
formation encountered below the beach sand deposits and fill materials appear to be of
pahoehoe and a a type lava flow which spread downslope from vents further up on the
mountain flank Pahoehoe lava is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface
and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock A a lavas are typically characterized
by a porous rough and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock
fragments including cobbles and boulders Typically a denser and layered lava rock
material is contained within the lava flow core
Cavities such as lava tubes or other void spaces are quite commonly encountered
buried within pahoehoe and a a lavas Many of these cavities were formed when the still
molten lava created the structures by means of flow and ponding of the lava The cavities
represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such as blisters and
pockets Lava tubes are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a
hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the
flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent
The pahoehoe and a a basalt lava rocks and clinkers encountered at the site are
generally considered to be permeable rock and may transmit water quite readily in both
horizontal and vertical directions Water is transmitted through the porous rock matrix
along joints fractures inter flow contacts cavities and along embedded clinker layers
The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they serve as the major water
transmission features

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located at 122 Puako Beach Drive Lot in
the District of South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii The property is bounded by developed
lots to the north and south Puako Beach Drive to the east and the Pacific Ocean to the
west as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
At the time of our field exploration an abandoned one story house structure
supported on a post and beam type of foundation system was located at the northwest
corner of the lot An 8 foot high concrete retaining wall with an attached walkway is located
at the western property line In addition some existing utility lines such as overhead
telephone and electrical lines water line and septic system were noted to traverse the
project site Other underground utility lines not noted may also traverse the project site
The lot is relatively level with a slight slope for drainage purposes The existing
ground surface elevation at the site is at about 6 feet MSL At the time of our field
exploration the project site was moderately vegetated with trees shrubs and lawn grass
which covered much of the property The trees and shrubs were concentrated along the
perimeter of the lot The surface soils at the site appeared to consist of dry loose to
relatively firm silty sands with varying amounts of gravel and cobbles Very loose and dry
surface silty sands were noted at the ground surface along the northern end of the project
site

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by beach sand
deposits and surface fills consisting of loose to medium dense silty fine sands extending to
about 10 feet below the existing ground surface Some of the beach sand deposits graded
to dense basalt and coral gravel from about 5 to 6 feet below the ground surface Clinker
materials were encountered below the sandy soils in the boring nearest to Puako Beach Drive
Boring No 1 The beach sand deposits and clinker materials were underlain by medium
hard to hard basalt formation extending to the maximum depth explored of about 25 feet below
the existing ground surface Groundwater was encountered at depths of about 4 5 to 5 3 feet
below the ground surface
Based on the subsurface conditions at the project site we believe that the proposed
new residential structure may be supported on a post and beam foundation system bearing
on the recompacted silty sands encountered in the borings Due to the potential for erosion
GEOLABS INC
W O 4318 00 Page 7
of the silty fine sands as a result of wave action during storm surge conditions the new
footings for the post and beam foundations should be embedded a minimum of 36 inches
below the existing ground surface and or the lowest adjacent finished grade
Our borings encountered loose to medium dense sandy soils at depths of about 3 to
5 feet below the existing ground surface To provide a firm and unyielding base for the
footings the soils encountered at the footing subgrade level should be moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction If soft and or loose materials are encountered or the compaction requirement is
not readily achieved at the footing subgrade level the soft and or loose soils should be
over excavated a minimum of 24 inches below the footing subgrade level The
over excavation should be replaced with No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
wrapped in a non woven filter fabric such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent We anticipate that
groundwater may be encountered at the over excavated subgrade level
Due to the loose nature of the silty fine sands encountered in our borings sloughing
and caving in of the sands may occur if left in an unconfined state such as in the swimming
pool and utility line excavations Therefore it would be prudent to construct the swimming
pool utility line trenches and other below grade structures excavated in the silty fine sands
before the construction of on grade structures located within proximity to the below grade
structures Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering design
recommendations are presented in the following sections of this report
House Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe that a
post and beam foundation system may be used to support the proposed new residence The
post footings should consist of spread footings bearing on the recompacted near surface
sandy soils encountered in the borings An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 000 pounds
per square foot psf may be used for the design of footings bearing on the recompacted silty
fine sands This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third
1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces Due to the potential
GEOLABS INC
W O 4318 00 Page 8
for erosion of the silty fine sands as a result of wave action during storm surge conditions we
recommend that the footings be embedded a minimum of 36 inches below the lowest adjacent
finished grade to reduce the potential for erosion of the silty fine sands
Where foundations are located next to retaining structures utility trenches and or
easements they should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward
from the bottom edge of the retaining structure foundations utility trench or as deep as the
inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to reduce the potential for surcharging
adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for
appreciable foundation settlement
Prior to the placement of reinforcing steel and concrete the soils encountered at the
footing subgrade level should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of
the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
If soft and or loose materials are encountered or the compaction requirement cannot
be achieved at the bottom of footing excavations the soils should be over excavated a
minimum depth of 24 inches below the footing subgrade level The resulting
over excavation should then be backfilled with free draining granular material such as
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation wrapped in a non woven filter fabric
Mirafi 180N or equivalent The purpose of the filter fabric is to reduce the potential for
migration of the finer adjacent soils into the open graded granular fill resulting in future
ground settlements
lf the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate that total settlements of footings bearing on the
recompacted sandy soils may be on the order of about 1 inch We estimate that differential
GEOLABS INC
W O 4318 00 Page 9
settlements between adjacent footings supported on the recompacted sandy soils to be on
the order of about 0 5 inches
Lateral loads acting on the residence may be resisted by frictional resistance developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of friction of 0 35
may be used for footings bearing on the silty fine sand Resistance due to passive earth
pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot
per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soils around footings are well compacted
Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil
should be neglected
We recommend that the footing subgrades be observed and tested by a representative
of Geolabs prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation
bearing conditions and the required embedment depths
Garage and Driveway Slabs
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the garage and driveway
areas leading to the residence In general we recommend a minimum 6 inch layer of
aggregate subbase be placed below the slab for more uniform support The aggregate
subbase should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction Concrete slabs should be designed for the intended loads For design
of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per square inch per inch of
deflection pci may be used for the compacted aggregate subbase Provisions should be
made for proper load transfer across the slab joints that will be subjected to vehicular traffic
The slab subgrade should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction to provide a firm and unyielding base The 6 inch thick
aggregate subbase should be placed as soon as possible after preparation of the slab
subgrade If the sandy soils encountered at the slab subgrade are allowed to dry then the
GEOLABS INC
W O 4318 00 Page 10
sandy soils may revert back to a loose state Therefore the dry soils should be
moisture conditioned and re compacted as recommended above
Exterior concrete walkways that are planned for the proposed project may be
supported on a minimum 4 inch thick layer of aggregate subbase compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction Crack control joints should be provided at intervals
equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at right angle intersections
The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at
least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade Areas adjacent to the slab
should be backfilled tightly against the edges of the slab with on site soil materials and
graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around
the slab edges

Utility Lines
We envision that some new utility lines and utility line connections will be required for
construction of the new residence It is anticipated that most of the trenches for utilities will be
excavated in the near surface sandy soils encountered in the borings It should be noted that

potential for undermining of the new footings Shoring of utility trenches within the fine sandy

of the contractor and should conform to all applicable local state and federal regulations
In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation is recommended below the pipes for uniform support If soft and or loose
materials are encountered at or near the invert of the pipes an additional layer of No 3B Fine

critical to use the free draining materials to reduce the potential for formation of voids below
the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper
trench backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes

Drainage
The finished grades outside the residential structure should be sloped to shed water
away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also
recommended that gutter systems be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be
diverted away from the foundation and slab areas Excessive landscape watering near the
foundations and slabs should be avoided Planters next to foundations should also be avoided
or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the
subsoils
The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab
edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration In
addition drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained
to drain surface water runoff away from the foundations and slabs

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of approximately
15 to 25 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings
are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials encountered are
presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3 of Appendix A
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of the
bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative quality
of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from a coring
attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material is recovered
from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs
of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
Very Poor 0 25
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100
The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its
relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense basalt with a high RQD would
be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous methods of excavation
