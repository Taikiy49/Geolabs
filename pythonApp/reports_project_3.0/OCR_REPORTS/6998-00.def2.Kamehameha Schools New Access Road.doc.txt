This geotechnical engineering report presents the results of our geotechnical 
engineering exploration performed for design of the New Road from Building 35 to Athletic 
Field project at the Kamehameha Schools – Kapalama Campus in Honolulu on the Island 
of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our 
geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the design of pavements. 
The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at 
the end of this letter report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
 The project involves design and construction of a new access road extending from 
Building 35 to the Athletic Field within the Kamehameha Schools – Kapalama Campus.  
Based on the information provided, the access road will primarily be used as a walkway 
with vehicular traffic limited to light trucks and other maintenance equipment.  It is planned 
to construct the road as a Portland Cement Concrete (PCC) rigid pavement.   
Site grading for the new road construction is anticipated to be minimal with cuts and 
fills on the order of about 1 foot in depth or thickness. 
REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late 
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island.  The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and

forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  The Koolau Volcano reached the 
end of its main shield-building phase about 2 million years ago. 
The project area is generally composed of basaltic rock built by extrusion of the 
lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series. These rocks are generally characterized by flows of 
jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers. In-situ chemical 
weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years. The 
weathering process has formed a mantle of residual and saprolitic soils. In general, 
saprolite is mainly composed of silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of 
volcanic rocks. The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with increased depth. Fills 
placed during the initial development of the campus may underlie the ground surface at 
portions of the project site. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling three borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, at selected locations along the proposed road 
alignment. The borings generally extended to depths of about 5.8 to 6.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface (BGS). The approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, our borings encountered 1.5 to 6.0 feet of surface fill generally 
consisting of stiff sandy clay.  Boring No. 2 penetrated the existing sidewalk which consists 
of 4 inches of concrete over the near-surface fill material.  Below the fill, the borings 
penetrated dense clayey gravel or stiff sandy clay (saprolite) to the maximum depth 
explored of 6.5 feet BGS. In Boring No. 3, medium hard weathered basalt rock formation 
was encountered at the depth of about 4 feet BGS. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the shallow borings at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it should be noted that seepage or groundwater levels may occur 
depending on seasonal rainfall, time of year, surface water runoff, and other factors.  
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered from our field exploration are 
presented on the Logs of Borings in Appendix A. Results of the laboratory tests performed 
on selected soil samples are presented in Appendix B.  
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
The project site extends from the existing Building 35 in a southwesterly direction to 
the Athletic Field.  The alignment is currently used as a walkway between Building 35 and 
the field.  The upper portion closest to the building is a gravel footpath that extends from 
the asphaltic concrete (AC) parking area at Building 35 to a concrete sidewalk serving a 
foot bridge crossing Bishop Drive and the lower part is a grassed pathway to the athletic 
field within the Kamehameha Schools – Kapalama Campus in Honolulu on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii.  The location of the site is shown on Project Location Map, Plate 1.  The 
locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.

PAVEMENT DESIGN 
Design Subgrade Conditions 
Based on our field exploration, we anticipate that the new pavement subgrade will 
consist of silty clay (CH) soils.  Laboratory CBR testing suggest that the subgrade soils 
consisting of silty clay with a mixture of some gravel and sand exhibit a CBR value of 
about 27 and a swell of about 1.1 percent remolded at near the in-situ moisture content in 
a laboratory compacted condition. 
Pavement Section 
Based on the anticipated traffic loading and the design CBR swell value, we 
recommend utilizing the following rigid pavement section for design.   
Rigid Pavement 
6.0-Inch Portland Cement Concrete  
  6.0-Inch Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction) 
 12.0-Inch Total New Pavement Thickness  
As an economic alternative, a flexible asphaltic concrete pavement could be 
considered. The following section may be used for the flexible pavement: 
 2.0-Inch Asphaltic Concrete  
 6.0-Inch Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction)  
  6.0-Inch Aggregate Subbase Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction) 
 14.0-Inch Total New Pavement Thickness  
After removal of the existing concrete sidewalk and the existing soils to 
accommodate the new pavement structural section, the exposed subgrade soils should be 
proof-rolled with a minimum 10-ton (static weight) drum roller for a minimum of six passes 
to compact the exposed subgrade soils. The subgrade should be moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction. 
If soft soils are exposed at the pavement subgrade or the soils are pumping during 
the proof-rolling operations, we recommend providing a stabilization layer consisting of 
8 inches of aggregate base course over a layer of triaxial geogrid (such as Tensar TX7 or 
equivalent) underlain by a woven geotextile fabric (such as Mirafi HP370 or equivalent). 
A Geolabs representative should monitor the pavement subgrade preparation to 
observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and to 
confirm whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar to those encountered in our 
field exploration.

The aggregate base course should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. 
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density. 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to 
saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be maintained 
to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. Surface water 
ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development of good shoulder 
drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage structures is 
strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration or premature 
failure of the pavements. 
SITE GRADING 
Site grading for the new road construction is anticipated to be minimal with cuts and 
fills on the order of about 1 foot in depth or thickness.  The following discussion provides 
general guidance for the design of grading work required. 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, asphaltic 
concrete, and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly 
off-site. Soft and/or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill should be over-excavated to expose firm materials, and the 
resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fills. The excavated soft 
and/or organic soils should be properly disposed off-site and/or used in landscape areas, 
where appropriate. Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices 
for over-excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the 
over-excavation quantities. 
After clearing and grubbing, finished subgrades and areas designated to receive 
fills should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 
2 percent above the optimum moisture, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction.  Finished subgrades in areas subjected to vehicular traffic should be 
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.   
Where shrinkage cracks develop in the subgrade soil after compaction, we 
recommend preparing the subgrade soil again as recommended above. Saturation and 
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage 
may require over-excavation and replacement of the softened areas with well-compacted

The excavated on-site soils may be re-used as a source of general fill materials,

rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension. Imported general fill 
materials should consist of soil materials with a maximum particle size of 3 inches or less 
with a CBR value of 12 or greater and a swell of 1 percent or less when tested in 
accordance with ASTM D1883. It should be noted that the general fill requirements 
presented herein are intended as guidelines only and may be modified based on additional 
laboratory testing and field observations on the available fill materials conducted by 
Geolabs. 
The non-expansive, select granular fill required as subbase course should consist 
of crushed coral or basalt. The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with 
particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension. The material also should contain 
between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. In addition, the material 
should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 
1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. 
Fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the 
optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to 
the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of 
the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the 
water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
FILL SLOPES 
Cut or fill slopes required for the project should be designed with a slope inclination 
of two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter.  The filling operations should start at the 
lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the 
above fill placement recommendations. 
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope 
ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. Water should be diverted away from the tops 
of slopes and slope planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the 
potential for erosion of the finished slopes.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling three borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, to depths of approximately 5.8 to 6.5 feet below 
the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with solid-stem auger and 
rotary coring equipment. 
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural 
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis.  These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing 
in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil 
Classification System, as shown on Plate A-0.1. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3.  
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches.  In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop.  The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2213, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cute into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the Rock 
Log Legend, Plate A-0.2.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D 2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification.  The test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth. Graphic presentation 
of the test results is provided on Plate B-1.  
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected soil sample to 
evaluate the shear strength parameters. The test results are presented on Plate B-2. 
Two Unconfined Compression tests (ASTM D2166) were performed on selected in-
situ cohesive soil samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the soils. 
The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths 
and graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-3 and B-4. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on  
bulk samples of the subgrade soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the 
soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-5 and B-6. 
Two one-inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples of the clayey matrix soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the 
subsurface soils. The test results are summarized on Plate B-7.
