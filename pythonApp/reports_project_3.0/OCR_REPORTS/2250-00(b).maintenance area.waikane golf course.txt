INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our preliminary geotechnical
engineering exploration for the site grading and foundation design of the
proposed Waikane Golf Course Development maintenance area The project
site is located in Waikane Valley in the Koolaupoko District of windward
Oahu Hawaii The general location of the project site is shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1 Our work was performed in general
accordance with our revised proposal dated
This report summarizes our findings and recommendations
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed Waikane Golf Course development consists of a
twenty seven hole golf course and related facilities The proposed
maintenance area is a related facility and will provide a staging area for
maintenance personnel and equipment The maintenance area will consist of
several lightly loaded steel framed Butler buildings with concrete slab
on grade floors Site grading will consist of cutting dowm a low bluff
about five 5 to ten 10 feet to create a level area
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain soil rock data for the
the formulation of engineering recommendations pertinent to the design of
the site grading and foundations of the proposed maintenance facility
The scope of our work included

the surface soil conditions

GENERAL SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is situated in Waikane Valley in the Koolaupoko
District on the windward shore of the island of Oahu Hawaii The general
location of the site and its vicinity is shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 1
The Waikane Golf Course project site is rural land that is used
partially for truck farming and pasture Most of the site is fallow land
that is densely vegetated by guava christmas berry and other semitropical
trees shrubs and grasses

Regional Geology
The island of Oahu is composed of two 2 shield volcanoes Waianae
and Koolau built up by the extrusion of basaltic lavas during the late
Pliocene and Pleistocene epochs Waikane Valley is an amphitheater headed
valley which has been deeply eroded into the eastern flank of Koolau
During the Pleistocene or Ice Age global sea levels rose and fell in
response to the great continental glaciers As the glaciers grew the
global sea level fell below the existing level and as the glaciers melted
and retreated the sea level rose to heights above the current stand of
the sea
During the lower stands of the sea the land surface eroded to depths
below the current sea level The basalt floor of Waikane Valley is most
likely at one hundred or more feet below sea level Alluvial deposits
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2250 00 B Page 5
were laid down during higher stands of the sea at elevations above the
current base level Most of the project site is thickly mantled with
alluvium Some of this alluvial material has become deeply weathered and
eroded since its deposition
Soil Conditions
Based on our exploration the subsurface conditions at the project
site appear to consist primarily of stiff mottled brown brown and reddish
brown clayey silts to the maximum depths explored of 31 5 feet below the
existing ground surface representing deeply weathered alluvial
materials These soils were originally stream laid clastic deposits
consisting of boulders cobbles and gravel In situ chemical weathering
of these clastic deposits has reduced the material to stiff clayey silts
while retaining most of the structure of the original boulders cobbles
and gravel
These stiff clayey silt soils generally exhibit adequate in situ
bearing strength characteristics and will also exhibit slight lateritic
properties i e they tend to develop slightly higher strength or a
slight cementation when exposed to subaerial conditions
Groundwater was encountered in Boring Nos 101 and 102 at depths of
15 6 and 16 9 feet below the existing ground surface respectively during
our exploration These water levels will most likely fluctuate in
response to precipitation and the surface water levels occurring in the
streams within the project site
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
In general our exploration indicates that most of the site is
underlain by relatively competent subsoils It is our opinion that
modified site grading techniques should be used due to the high in situ
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2250 00 B Page 6
moisture contents of the subsoils in this high rainfall area More
detailed discussions and modified grading recommendations are presented
below
Site Grading
Areas to be graded should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed of
vegetation debris boulders and other deleterious materials Topsoil
should be removed and stockpiled for later usage and to avoid
contamination of fill materials Any tree roots or stumps greater than
three 3 inches in diameter should be removed In areas designated to
receive fill the prepared ground surface should be scarified to a minimm
depth of six 6 inches brought to near optimm moisture content and
recompacted to a minimum of 85 of its maximum dry density per ASIM Test
Method D 1557 78 Any soft or yielding areas encountered should be
over excavated and backfilled with on site soil compacted to 85 maximm
ary density
Since the project site is located in a high rainfall area the on site
subsoils generally have in place moisture contents in excess of the
optimum moisture content Therefore some amount of drying of the
excavated material will be required before its use as fill
Because of the high moisture content of the on site material and
because of the site being situated in a high rainfall area it is our
opinion that the normal 90 compaction standard used in other locales may
be modified to 85 for most of the golf course embankment fills proposed
on this project However the 90 standard i e 90 of the maximum dry
density per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 should be maintained for off site
borrow or select borrow to be utilized in structural fills structural
backfills and utility backfills below paved areas
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2250 00 B Page 7
General fills should be placed in maximm twelve inch 12 thick
loose horizontal lifts and compacted to the appropriate level of dry
density Sheepsfoot type compaction equipment is highly suited to the
materials encountered at the project site For structural fills and
trench backfills the lift thickness should be reduced to a maximm of ten
10 inches
We anticipate a shrinkage factor between 10 and 20 when the on site
soils are utilized as fill compacted to 85 maximum dry density It is
recommended that if additional off site borrow is required it should be
tested by the geotechnical engineer to determine if it is suitable for its
intended use Generally the material should be non expansive and should
not contain boulders organics or other deleterious materials
The current plans do not show any major fills in this maintenance
area However we recommend that where the existing ground surface is
steeper than five horizontal to one vertical 5H 1V keying and benching
are required to properly bond the new fill to the existing slope See
Plate 3 for details The filling operations should start at the lowest
point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance
with the above recommendations for fill placement
Subdrains should be provided at the base of the new fills to reduce
the potential of water accumulating at the interface between the fill and
the existing subsoils See Plates 4 5 Such accumulation of water
could create a plane of weakness between the fill and the original ground
potentially causing slippage or creep of the fill
In general cut slopes of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or
flatter are recommended for the site grading design Fill slopes should
be constructed at ratios of three horizontal to one vertical 3H 1V due
to the reduced compaction level and should be constructed by over filling
past the design slope and then cutting back Care should be exercised to
avoid over cutting of cut slopes

W O 2250 00 B Page 8
Water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and slope
planting utilized to reduce potential erosion and or instability of the
finished slopes
In general mid point slope benches should be provided for slopes
steeper than four horizontal to one vertical 4H 1V and having vertical
heights greater than 20 feet Modifications to the above recommended
bench interval should be reviewed by the project geotechnical engineer on
an individual basis
Foundation Design
Based upon the soil conditions encountered in our field exploration
it is our opinion that shallow spread and continuous footing foundations
bearing on in situ soils may be utilized to support the proposed
structures The bottoms of foundation excavations should be compacted
prior to steel placement and they should be free of loose debris Any
soft wet and or yielding areas encountered at footing subgrade level
should be over excavated to firm natural material and the resulting
excavation should be backfilled with non expansive select borrow granular
material compacted in thin lifts to 90 of its maximm dry density per
ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
An allowable bearing capacity of 2 000 p s f may be utilized for the
design of foundations bearing on recompacted in situ soils prepared as
recommended above A minimum footing embedment of 18 inches below the
lowest adjacent grade is recommended The above recommended bearing value
is for total dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third when
temporary wind or seismic loads are considered
Based on our exploration and laboratory testing estimated foundation
settlements are on the order of less than one half 1 2 of an inch total
and one quarter 1 4 of an inch differential
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2250 00 B Page 9
A base friction coefficient of 0 4 may be used to compute the
frictional resistance between the bottoms of the footings and the stiff
on site soils In addition a passive resistance of 300 p s f per foot
of depth of the buried vertical faces of the footings may be used to
calculate resistance to lateral loads
If a footing foundation is located next to a utility trench the
footing should be extended to the same depth as the bottom of the trench
to reduce the potential of footing distress resulting from the settlement
of the trench backfill If a continuous footing foundation crosses a
utility trench it should be designed to span the trench to reduce the
potential of foundation distress due to settlement of the backfill

Slabs On Grade
It is anticipated that slabs on grade will be utilized for the
floors of the maintenance buildings
For office and light storage spaces a minimm 4 inch thick slab
with a 4 inch thick cushion of ASTM C 67B 3B Fine gravel may be
used The slab perimeter should have a thickened edge footing which
extends a minimum of twelve 12 inches below the outside finish

grade A moisture barrier membrane should be considered between the
slab and cushion if it is desired to protect interior floor coverings

For heavy maintenance areas or storage spaces subjected to

Infrastructure
The use of underground utility lines is anticipated for this
project Based on our exploration most of the trenching will be in
stiff alluvial soils Therefore it is our opinion that conventional
excavation and backfilling techniques may be utilized
However due to the alluvial nature of the on site soils there is
a possibility that some areas of hard excavation such as well cemented
soils and or boulders may be encountered
The on site soils with proper moisture conditioning aeration
are generally suitable for use as trench backfill material Since the
project site is located in a high rainfall area the on site subsoils
generally have in place moisture contents in excess of the optimm
moisture content Therefore some amount of drying of the excavated
material may be required before its use as backfill

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based
in part upon information obtained from field data points such as
borings probes test pits and or observation wells Variations of
conditions between the field data points may occur and the nature and
extent of these variations may not become evident until construction If
variations then appear evident it will be necessary to re evaluate the
recommendations given in this report
The location of the field data points were approximately determined
by taping from reference points shown on the topographic worksheets
prepared by Community Planning Inc Elevations of the data points were
approximately determined by estimation and interpolation from the advance
sheet of Schematic Site Plan by Robert M Matsushita Associates dated
The physical location and elevation of the field data
points should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the
method used
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the
field data points depict the approximate boundaries between soil types
and as such may denote a gradual transition The strata lines shown on
profiles or cross sections are based upon interpolation between field
data points and may not represent actual subsurface conditions
Water level data from the field data points was collected at the
times given on the graphic representations and or in the text of this
report This data has been reviewed and interpretations made in the
formulation of this report However it must be noted that fluctuation
may occur due to variations in rainfall tides temperature and other
factors
