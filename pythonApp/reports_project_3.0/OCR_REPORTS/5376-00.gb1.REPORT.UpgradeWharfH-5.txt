performed in support of the design of the Repair Wharf H 5 project located at Pearl

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island
The younger shield Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the
island The Koolau Volcano reached the end of its main shield building phase about
2 million years ago
The geologic conditions underlying the Pearl Harbor area and the project site are
the result of a complex series of deposition and erosional events brought on by
glacio eustatic sea level changes and post erosional volcanic activities during the
Pleistocene Epoch Following the cessation of volcanic activity of the Koolau Volcano
alluvial soil materials were deposited in a deltaic or estuarine environment in the area of
Pearl Harbor
The sea level fluctuated several times receding perhaps 300 feet below and
rising about 100 feet above the present sea level During the recent epoch as sea level
rose to its current stand the area was covered with soft and loose lagoonal marine
deposits These deposits subsequently were covered by man made fills placed during
the middle portion of this century for land reclamation projects during construction of the
existing Pearl Harbor area

International Building Code IBC 2000 the short period spectral acceleration Ss for

Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near
the ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess
pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes
In this process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration such as
during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an
increase in pore water pressure If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to
dissipate the build up of pore water pressure the effective stress internal strength of
the soil is reduced Under sustained vibrations the pore water pressure build up could
equal the overburden pressure essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and
causing it to behave as a viscous fluid During liquefaction the soil acquires a mobility
sufficient to permit both horizontal and vertical movements and if not confined will
result in significant deformations
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion It is generally acknowledged that liquefaction
will not occur if the deposit is greater than 40 to 50 feet below the ground surface In
deeper soil deposits the greater overburden pressure is generally sufficient to prevent
liquefaction from occurring The major factors affecting the liquefaction characteristics
of a soil deposit are as follows
1
Grain Size Distribution Fine and uniform sands and silts are more
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2
Initial Relative Density Loose sands and silts are most susceptible to
liquefaction Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to relative
density
3
Magnitude and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is directly
proportional to the magnitude and duration of the earthquake
In general the subsurface information obtained from the borings indicates that
the site is underlain by soft and or loose lagoonal deposits The lagoonal deposits
consisting of soft and or loose sandy silts are potentially liquefiable during seismic
events

stability analyses consisting of two dimensional slope cross sections using Bishop s
simplified method of limit equilibrium analysis were performed on the sheet pile
bulkhead structure Based on our analyses the factor of safety for overall slope
stability of the sheet pile bulkhead in the long term condition is at least 1 5 which is
the minimum factor of safety normally recommended for long term conditions The
factor of safety for overall slope stability of the sheet pile bulkhead in the short term
condition during construction is 1 2 which should be acceptable during the
construction phase

As mentioned previously some ground settlements just behind the existing sheet
pile bulkhead were observed during the test pile driving activities and installation of
the falsework piles We believe that the ground settlements may have been

attributed to vibration during construction which could have induced a liquefaction
condition in the lagoonal deposits
As mentioned previously soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated
cohesionless soils located near the ground surface undergo a substantial loss of
strength due to the build up of excess pore water pressures resulting from cyclic
stress applications induced by vibration In this process when the loose saturated
sand and or soft silt deposits are subjected to vibration the soil tends to densify and
decrease in volume causing an increase in pore water pressure If drainage is
unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build up of pore water pressure the
effective stress internal strength of the soil is reduced Under sustained
vibrations the pore water pressure build up could equal the overburden pressure
essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a
viscous fluid
Therefore we recommend that use of a vibratory hammer to install the interlocking
steel sheet piles be avoided to reduce the potential for inducing a liquefaction
condition in the underlying lagoonal deposits which would cause additional ground
settlements of the ground adjacent to the new sheet pile bulkhead In addition
construction activities that generate sustained vibrations should be reviewed and
precautionary measures should be taken to reduce the impact of the vibrations
generated on failure of the soft and or sensitive soils underlying the project site

Seismic Piezocone Testing
Due to the nature of the subsurface soil conditions at the project site seismic
shear wave velocity profiling using Cone Penetrometer Testing CPT was performed at
the site in an effort to more closely analyze soil properties at the site Shear wave
velocity tests and cone penetration tests were performed at three probe locations
designated as CPT 1 through CPT 3 as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The probes
extended to depths of about 103 to 117 feet below the ground surface using seismic
piezocone testing equipment SCPTU
The seismic piezocone penetration test consists of hydraulically pushing a 10 ton
steel electronic subtraction cone manufactured by with an apex
angle of 60 degrees and an area of 1 55 square inches 10 square centimeters
connected to a friction sleeve with an area of 23 25 square inches 150 square
centimeters into the ground The tip and side friction resistance and pore pressure
induced immediately behind the cone tip designated as the U2 parameter during
penetration are measured by strain gages within the instrument and are recorded versus
the depth of penetration in about 2 inch 5 centimeter intervals
In addition the cone carries a uniaxial horizontal geophone to detect the arrival of
a shear wave propagated from the ground surface The seismic measurements are
made when the SCPTU has stopped and a shear wave is sent into the subsurface A
shear wave is generated at the surface by striking a loaded plank with a switched
hammer The propagation time of the wave from the hammer blow to the cone was
measured at each depth interval The vector difference of these depths divided by the
time difference provided the average shear wave velocity over the depth interval
Logs of the seismic cone penetration tests are presented on Plates B 1 through
B 3 of Appendix B In general the plots include cone tip resistance friction ratio sleeve
friction over tip resistance pore pressure differential pore pressure ratio shear wave
velocity soil behavior based on the Rf zone and interpreted soil description The values
of the tip resistance friction ratio and pore pressure may be correlated to soil strength
and classification The center of the friction sleeve is approximately 4 inches 10 cm
behind the cone tip To calculate the friction ratio the average friction resistance and tip
resistance are compared at the same depth This involves an offset of the friction
resistance by the physical distance of 4 inches
Pore pressure is presented in terms of equivalent height of water head
Differential pore pressure ratio is calculated as a ratio of excess pore pressure to cone tip
resistance expressed as a percentage The excess pore pressure was calculated by

A P P E N D I X C
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample
of soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate C 1
Two Sieve Analysis tests were performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate the gradation characteristics and to aid in soil classification The tests were
performed in accordance with ASTM Test Designations C 117 and C 136 Graphic
presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate C 2
One unconsolidated undrained triaxial compression test ASTM D 2850 was
performed on a selected sample of the soils to evaluate the undrained shear strength of
the soils Results of the unconsolidated undrained triaxial compression test are
presented on Plate C 3
One consolidated undrained triaxial compression test ASTM D 4767 was
performed on a selected sample of the soils to evaluate the long term shear strength of
the soils The results of the consolidated undrained triaxial compression test are
presented on Plate C 4
