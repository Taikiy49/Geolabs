This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Castle Junction Slide Area Emergency Repairs at
Kalanianaole Highway in the Kaneohe area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project
site is located at the northern side of the intersection of Kalanianaole Highway and
Kamehameha Highway The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing field in situ
seismic refraction geophysical surveys and engineering analyses These
recommendations are intended to assist in the design of the new cut slope and pavement
section only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two main
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older shield volcano Waianae Volcano is
estimated to be middle to late Pliocene Epoch in age and forms the bulk of the western
third of the island The younger shield volcano Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late
Pliocene Epoch to early Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age in age and forms the majority of
the eastern two thirds of the island The Waianae Volcano became extinct while the
Koolau Volcano remained active Therefore the older Waianae Volcano s eastern flank
was partially buried below the younger Koolau Volcano lavas that banked against the
Waianae s eastern flank
The Koolau Volcano produced a large caldera Koolau Caldera remnants of which
are located along the northeastern side of the Koolau Mountains extending from about
Waimanalo to Kaneohe During the later stage of eruption the Koolau Caldera was filled
with significant thickness of basaltic lavas and breccia that were intruded by vertically
oriented dikes narrow bands of distinctively fine grained dense rock material that fed the
extrusion of the lava as shown on Plate 3 The lavas and associated dikes were
intensively altered and decomposed by rising hydrothermal fluids and gases which
emanated from the active volcano As a result of the accelerated rock decomposition
induced by the hydrothermal activity much of the original caldera structure has been
eroded away by natural erosion processes The remnant decomposed Koolau
Caldera filling lavas have been previously identified and mapped as belonging to the
Kailua Volcanic Series which is actually part of the Koolau Volcanic Series

Seismic Refraction Geophysical Surveys
Seismic refraction geophysical surveys are often conducted to supplement and
enhance traditional subsurface geotechnical exploration such as test borings
The refraction surveys may provide quick and affordable general subsurface
characterization of the strata thickness and lateral continuity and an estimation
of the rock rippability for excavation However the seismic refraction methods do
rely on some simplified assumptions that may limit its application in some
settings
In general the seismic refraction geophysical exploration method involves using
a signal enhancement seismograph and seismic data processing software to
record and analyze an induced seismic energy source expressed as
compressional P waves which travel through and refract on layered subsurface
geologic mediums consisting of varying density and hardness Seismic energy
travels with a compressional velocity that is characteristic of the density porosity
structure and water content of each distinguishable geologic layer encountered
The primary constraint of the P wave refraction method assumes that the
subsurface geologic layers increase in material density generation of higher P
wave velocities with increasing depth The refraction method is not effective
where the materials P wave velocity decreases with increasing depth a
condition known as a velocity inversion Normally over 80 percent of natural
geological settings conform to increasing material density corresponding with
increasing depth of burial
Our seismic refraction survey incorporated the use of a Geometrics Smartseis
S 12 a 12 channel seismograph coupled with an array of 12 geophone sensors
The seismic energy source was induced using a 12 pound sledge hammer
striking a metal plate coupled with the ground surface at selected source shot
points The geophone array consisted of 12 individual geophones spread out at
10 foot intervals Five discreet shot locations were utilized at each survey
location to trigger the seismograph recording of the seismic energy response at
the geophones At each shot location approximately 6 to 18 hammer strikes

Serrated Cut Slope Alternative
The slide repair may be implemented by flattening the existing steep slope to a
2H 1V serrated slope Based on the current grading plan provided we
understand that the cut slope height will range from 10 to 135 feet with maximum
vertical cut up to 70 feet deep The total excavation quantity was estimated up to
240 000 cubic yards
As mentioned previously we envision that the new 2H 1V cut slope would likely
encroach into the neighboring properties beyond the existing State Right of Way
However considering the relatively lower cost shorter construction duration
conventional construction method and aesthetically pleasing appearance this
alternative would be preferable
Our slope stability analyses indicated that the proposed 2H 1V cut slope may
achieve greater than 1 5 factor of safety for the static condition and greater than
1 15 factor of safety for the seismic condition with assumed seismic acceleration
coefficient of 0 15g
Excessive surface run off over the slope face may cause erosion of the exposed
soils thus potentially jeopardizing the long term stability and performance of the
cut slope Therefore it is critical that the cut slopes be protected by appropriate
slope planting and serrating the slope face
In general slope benches are normally provided to reduce the runoff velocity and
potential for erosion However the inclusion of slope benches usually increases
the total height of slope exposed and increases the quantity and cost of the
excavation Since the runoff will be initiated by rainfall on the slope face it is an

Soil Nail Stabilization Alternative
Based on the generally competent subsurface conditions at the project site we
believe that a soil nail wall system may be used to stabilize the slide areas and to
maintain near vertical sloping geometry Based on the existing slope geometry
we believe that the soil nail walls may be constructed on the upper portion of the
slope to serve both as temporary shoring of the excavation and permanent
retaining structures The lower portion of the slope may be retained by
conventional concrete retaining walls in a stepped or benched configuration The
steps or benches can be used for planting to improve the aesthetics of the
construction Details are presented on the Conceptual Sketch of Soil Nail
Stabilization Alternative Plate 4
In general a soil nail wall system consists of a series of individual reinforcing
bars grouted into drilled holes to provide reinforcement to an excavation or slope
A shotcrete facing is generally applied to the face of the reinforced excavation to
protect the soil and provide the appearance of a conventional concrete wall The
reinforcing bars provide both additional tensile and shear strengths to the soil
and reinforce the sidewall of the excavation The reinforced soil mass behaves
in a manner similar to a gravity retaining wall but lacks a footing In general the
face of the soil nail wall system is slightly battered at a slope inclination of one
horizontal to twelve vertical 1H 12V or flatter

95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the
same soil determined in accordance with AASHTO T 180 Optimum moisture is
the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory
rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment Water tamping

Subgrade Preparation Below Pavement Section
The subgrade soils below the pavement areas should be scarified to a minimum
depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with AASHTO T 180 or
ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
CBR tests and or field observations should be performed on the actual subgrade
soils during construction to confirm that the above design section is adequate
If pumping conditions are encountered during compaction of the subgrade soils the
subgrade soils may be stabilized by cement treatment to reduce the potential for
pumping subgrade conditions As a guide the subgrade may be proof rolled and a
woven geotextile fabric such as Mirafi 500X or equivalent should be placed on the
subgrade prior to placement of the aggregate subbase layer
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement
weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and
replacement with well compacted fill Contract documents should include additive
and deductive unit prices for over excavation and compacted fill placement to
account for variations in the over excavation quantities
Pavement subgrade preparation should be observed by a representative from
Geolabs It is important that a representative from our office observe the site
preparation operation to evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered
during the excavation and scarification process and whether the exposed soil rock
conditions are similar to those encountered in our field exploration

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling three
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 to depths of about 51 to 81 feet below the
existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site
Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using truck mounted and track mounted drilling
equipment equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered during drilling are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 1 through
A 3 3
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler or a 3 inch OD split barrel sampler
with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the
sampler the second and third 6 inches a total of 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket
penetrometer tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Core samples of the rocks encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock structure The material left in the hollow core of the
bit is mechanically recovered for examination and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and would
be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80

APPENDIX C
Seismic Refraction Geophysical Survey
The subsurface conditions at the project site were also explored by the
performance of three seismic refraction survey lines performed during July 2003 The
approximate locations of the seismic refraction surveys are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 designated as SR 1 through SR 3
Refraction seismography is based on the transmission of elastic energy
compressional waves through geologic materials The seismic refraction surveys
were conducted using a 12 channel signal enhancement seismograph with a 12 pound
sledge hammer as a seismic energy source Generally the denser or more
consolidated a material is the faster the transmission of the compressional waves
These waves are refracted at interfaces between materials with contrasting densities
The refracted waves are received by geophones on the surface and recorded by the
operator
The recorded data is input into a desktop computer for processing which
involves the interpretive selection of the arrival times of the refracted waves After the
interpretive selection of arrival times the technique permits mathematical calculations
that yield approximate depths or thickness of materials below the survey lines and
provide their approximate compressional wave velocities The velocities are averaged
along the travel path of the compressional waves The calculated velocities may be
empirically related to the excavation characteristics of the materials
The typical estimated seismic velocities for the various materials underlying the
site are classified as follows A contractor wishing to bid on this project should retain a
qualified professional to assist in the interpretation of these geophysical survey results
