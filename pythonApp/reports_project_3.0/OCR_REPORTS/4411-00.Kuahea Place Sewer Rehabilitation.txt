REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age
After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion incised deep valleys into
the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and
tuff cone eruptions These later eruptions are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles
of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill
GEOLABS INC
W O 4411 00 Page 5
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea levels
rose to elevations above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was correspondingly
lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level When the sea level was
high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments accumulated at higher
elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported downslope
toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these materials reach the
stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become dominant and the
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
When alluvial and colluvial deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical
processes begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or further
weathering of the material Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the
coarse grained portion of the sediment resulting in a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock
or conglomerate Simultaneously erosion continues in areas above the valley floors and
upstream in headwaters This continued erosion generates material which is transported
downslope covering the older alluvial deposits Depending on the local base level and rate
of transport these newer sediments are generally transient in terms of geologic time In
addition their consistency and density are generally less than those of the older partially
consolidated deposits
The project site is located along the lower colluvial slope of a spur valley Waiomao
located within Palolo Valley Palolo Valley is essentially a deep erosional valley carved into
the Koolau Shield Volcano by stream processes and mass wasting of the adjacent slopes As
a result the project site is generally underlain by mixed colluvial and alluvial soil deposits

derived from the weathering and transport of eroded materials downslope toward the valley
axis Based on a review of the boring data from the vicinity there may be some significant
layers of pyroclastic material derived from the Honolulu Volcanic Series Kaau Crater
consisting of cemented sandy and gravelly material sandwiched in between the alluvial and
colluvial soil deposits Based on the boring information obtained from the site a generalized

Generalized Geologic Cross Section Plate 4 The line of the cross section is shown on the

Because the alluvial and colluvial soil deposits form as a result of the erosion

resemble a gray to brown colored silty clay and sand containing a large percentage of
embedded partially and completely weathered gravel cobble and boulder material
The steep slopes of Mauumae Ridge bound the site toward the east and are composed
of layered Koolau basalt rock sloping gently down toward the south The colluvial and alluvial
soil deposits form a wedge shaped apron deposited at the foot of the steep rock slopes The
clayey and rocky colluvial alluvial soil apron generally thickens in the direction of the valley axis
where it merges with the alluvial soil deposits of Waiomao Stream a tributary of the larger
Palolo Stream channel Based on the available deep boring data the basalt rock formation

Inclinometer Data
Inclinometers are used to monitor lateral earth movements in landslide areas and
embankments Inclinometer casing is typically installed in a near vertical borehole that
passes through the zone of suspected movement The bottom of the casing is anchored
in generally stable ground and serves as a reference Ground movement causes the
casing to move from its original position to a new position The rate depth and magnitude
of this displacement are calculated by comparing data from the initial survey to data
obtained from subsequent surveys The surveys are performed at intervals in time and are
conducted by lowering the measurement probe down hole to record the tilt in the casing
The measurements of casing tilt are converted to lateral deviation distances to obtain the
overall cumulative displacement of the inclinometer casing versus time

Based on the available inclinometer data and our knowledge of the site it appears that
the existing vitrified clay pipe VCP sewer lines at the project site have experienced distress
that is most likely caused by relatively shallow depth shifting of the subsurface soils The
location of the cracked shattered and collapsed pipeline segments appear to correlate with
areas observed at the ground surface that show signs of some deformation
GEOLABS INC

Based on the inclinometer data it appears that some relatively shallow depth earth
movements are occurring at the site and the movements appear to be more pronounced at
the eastern half of the project site However based on the available data we believe that it
is not yet possible to accurately define the boundaries of movement We believe that the earth
movement located in the vicinity of Boring No 4 may continue and has the potential to
increase with the passage of time Therefore the design of the rehabilitation project should
be conducted with consideration given to the potential for future earth movements to occur at
various locations across the project site
Based on the existing data the boundaries of the potentially shifting soils cannot be
accurately identified at the ground surface and the inclinometer data only provides an
indication of the depth rate and direction of movement at the specific instrument location
Currently there appears to be minimal indication of the lateral boundary of the earth
movement visible at the ground surface Indication of the earth movement boundary may be
expressed as abrupt scarps or offset of the ground surface open cracks or broader areas of
ground settlement or heaving Therefore we must assume that due to the widely scattered
location of the known cracked shattered and collapsed pipeline segments the earth
movements may be occurring throughout the entire project area The earth movement may
be composed of a single large mass of shifting soil or a series of smaller shifting soil masses
moving at varying rates with respect to each other
The collapsed segments of the existing VCP pipe are located in the general area of the
more significant ground surface and structure deformation observed in the vicinity of Kuahea
Place and extending along Kuahea Street between Kuahea Place downhill toward Helo Place

pronounced in this general location with respect to the remainder of the project site

magnitude movements presently occurring at the project site The rate of movement indicated
at Boring No I 4 may be the best available means of estimating the future rate of movement
at the project site

Pipeline Rehabilitation
In general buried utility pipelines located within a laterally extensive shifting soil mass
experience varying forces of both compression and extension as the entire soil mass tries to
move as a unit As a result the pattern of internal deformation within the slide mass may be
quite complex and the pipes may or may not experience breakage The boundaries of the
slide mass are where the greatest pipeline deformation may occur The most significant
pipeline damage usually occurs near the earth movement boundary where the soil mass
typically will experience extensional deformation at the head of the slide and compressional
deformation at the toe of the slide Utility line breaks typically are more prevalent at the
boundaries of the sliding soil mass Where a buried utility line crosses a slide boundary
repeated damage or breakage should be expected to occur
The design of the sewer line rehabilitation must take into consideration that slow earth
movements on the order of about 0 5 inches per year are occurring and likely will continue to
occur in the future Therefore the most appropriate pipeline rehabilitation method s should
incorporate the greatest pipe and joint flexibility available into the design Due to the close
proximity of the sewer lines to some existing structures and private property we believe that
trenchless technology would be appropriate for the rehabilitation of the existing pipes
Furthermore because the project site may be located in a zone of potential earth movement
it may be prudent to minimize open cut excavation work to reduce the potential for claims
associated with ground movements from adjacent property owners
Based on our review of the trenchless pipe rehabilitation alternatives it appears that
the fold and form pipe FFP technology using high density polyethylene HDPE pipe and
fused joints may provide the greatest flexural strength and would be applicable for use in a
slow earth movement environment However the FFP method may require excavation at the
lateral pipe connections to restore the lateral pipe connections Therefore the cured in place
pipe CIPP method appears to be more appropriate for the pipe rehabilitation since
excavation is not required to restore service to the lateral service pipes The intent is to
rehabilitate the existing pipes to allow for some future earth movement and to reduce the
GEOLABS INC
W O 4411 00 Page 15
frequency of future pipe repairs due to the potentially on going earth movement at the project
site
We anticipate there will be a need for some spot repair by excavation and replacement
of the collapsed and severely distressed pipe segments It is imperative to construct the pipe
repairs expeditiously and also confirm whether voids in the trench backfill or surrounding soils
have not developed as a result of infiltration and exfiltration at the collapsed pipe sections In
addition trench excavations at the site should be protected against the introduction of surface
water runoff into the subsurface
We recommend that special connectors be considered for connections between the
sewer pipes and manhole structures Due to the size and mass of the manholes these
structures may actually possess slightly higher resistance than the pipes to ground movement
Therefore the manholes may act somewhat as an anchor and move at slower rates than the
adjacent pipes Flexible connections articulated pipe joints that allow for variable extension
and compression at the manholes should be considered for use
Groundwater
Based on the available boring data the near surface soils are wet due an influx of
shallow depth water seepage from an unidentified source It is possible that the shallow depth
water seepage is being produced by leaking water supply or sewer lines located in the vicinity
of the borings However we are also aware that localized perched groundwater may exist at
varying depths and locations throughout the Kuahea Street Area Movements study area The
groundwater levels are believed to fluctuate in response to changes in rainfall and infiltration
of the surface water runoff
Due to the relatively close proximity of the steep rock slopes composing the eastern
ridgeline bounding the Waiomao neighborhood a significant volume of surface water runoff
is shed from the slopes during high rainfall events The surface water runoff is believed to
infiltrate the subsurface soils at the base of the rock slopes creating additional recharge of
groundwater into the colluvial soils This recharge gradually works its way downward and
GEOLABS INC
W O 4411 00 Page 16
toward the valley axes through the buried soil layers some of which transmit water more
effectively due to higher soil permeability As a result of this recharge process from the
adjacent rock slopes the shallow depth water seepage observed in the borings may reflect
the slow percolating movement of natural groundwater generated from the infiltration of
surface water runoff it should be noted that water seepage at the ground surface couid be
observed during and following measurable rainfall at the site The seepage of water may be
observed emanating from cracks and joints in the pavement surface and from the face of
retaining walls
In general it is known that increases in groundwater level and the resultant increase
in pore water pressure in the soil mass acts to destabilize some hillside soil deposits
increasing the risk of sudden landslide activity or slow movement under the force of gravity
Therefore subsurface drainage of soil deposits which are susceptible to slide failure assists
in lowering the soil pore water pressure and stabilizes the material against sliding To reduce
the subsurface soil pore water pressure consideration may be given to the construction of a
subdrain system along Kuahea Place The subdrain system should intercept and divert the
shallow subsurface water away from the area of significant earth movement at Kuahea Place
in an effort to reduce the driving force of the earth movement We believe the subdrain
concept is feasible due to the shallow depth of the earth movement about 6 to 8 feet deep
in the vicinity of Kuahea Place Furthermore the subdrain system could be installed at the
same time when excavations are made to repair the damaged pipeline along Kuahea Place
Utility Trenches
For the design of the new sewer line segments replacement of existing sewer line
granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about
12 inches above the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential
for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the
sides of the pipes Improper backfill material around the pipes and placement could result in
settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
GEOLABS INC
W O 4411 00 Page 17
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils or select
granular fill material The backfill should be placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and
should be mechanically compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction to reduce
the potential for future ground subsidence
If needed select granular fill materials should consist of non expansive well graded
granular materials such as crushed coral basalt or cinder sand The materials should be well
graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension In
addition the materials should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the
No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should
have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883

Field Exploration
Drilling exploration was not conducted under the scope of work for this Kuahea Place
Sewer Rehabilitation project However drilling exploration was performed by Geolabs for the

appendix for ease of reference
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos I 4 and I 5 extending to depths ranging from about
69 to 96 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the test borings
are shown on the Site Plan Plate 3 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with hollow stem augers and wireline coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials encountered in the
borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the
field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive
strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the Logs
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
