SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering consultation 
services performed for the Bridge No. 25-2 Piihonua Bridge, Hurricane Lane Damage 
Repair project planned in the District of South Hilo on the Island of Hawaii. The project 
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. This report 
summarizes the findings from our site reconnaissance and presents our geotechnical 
recommendations derived from our engineering analyses for the design of the proposed 
project. 
The recommendations provided herein are intended for the repairs of noted 
damages to the existing bridge only. The findings and recommendations presented herein 
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
Substantial damage was observed at the Piihonua Bridge after the occurrence of 
Hurricane Lane in August 2018.  We understand that this project consists of providing 
repairs and remedial measures for Piihonua Bridge due to damages sustained from 
Hurricane Lane. 
A field exploration consisting of drilling borings was not conducted for the project.  
Therefore, the design of the repairs and remedial measures were developed based on a 
site reconnaissance only.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers an area 
of approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of fives shield 
volcanoes, which include the following:  Kohala (long extinct); Mauna Kea (activity during 
recent geologic time); Hualalai (erupted in 1801), and Mauna Loa and Kilauea (both still 
active). 
The project site is on the northeastern flank of the Mauna Loa Shield Volcano, 
which had been built up by the successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and 
pyroclastic materials. The lava formation encountered at the site appears to consist of 
both a'a and pahoehoe type flows of the Kau Basalt Series, which spread and ponded as 
they approached the ocean. A’a lava is typically characterized by a porous, rough, and 
irregular flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments, including 
cobbles and boulders. Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth, rope-like or billowy 
surface and an internal structure of vesicular (porous) rock. 
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas, especially pahoehoe type 
lava. Cavities form when the lava is still in a molten state, and they represent both lava 
tubes (intra-flow cavities) and blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities). Lava tubes form 
when molten lava drains from the cooling flow, leaving a hollow tube-like structure, which 
may extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow. Blisters and pockets are 
generally smaller in horizontal extent. 
2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is in Hilo in the District of South Hilo on the Island of Hawaii.  
Piihonua Bridge is located along Waianuenue Avenue at the northwest edge of Hilo.  The 
existing two-span bridge is about 138 feet long and traverses Wailuku River.  Based on 
the topographic survey map provided, the stream water elevation is about 40 feet below 
the bridge deck elevation.

2.3 
Anticipated Subsurface Conditions 
Field exploration consisting of drilling and sampling borings was not conducted for 
the project. The anticipated subsurface conditions were evaluated by site reconnaissance 
and review of available in-house and provided information only. 
Based on our site reconnaissance, materials exposed at the existing bridge 
abutments and pier generally consist of dense basalt formation.  The basalt formation 
observed generally appeared medium hard to hard and slightly to moderately weathered. 
It should be noted that cavities and/or voids are commonly present in the basaltic lava 
flows of the vicinity. Therefore, the presence of cavities and/or voids should be expected 
during construction. 
Near the roadway elevation, silty volcanic ash soils mixed with various amounts of 
gravelly and cobbly clinker materials are exposed in some areas.   
In general, groundwater should be anticipated at near the stream water elevation.  
It should be noted that groundwater levels likely will change in response to seasonal 
precipitation, surface water runoff, storm surge conditions, and other factors.

Detailed discussions and specific design recommendations for the various repair 
areas and other geotechnical aspects of the project are presented in the following

Repair of Scoured and Undermined Areas 
Repair Nos. 1 through 4 are locations that have scoured and undermined that will 
be repaired under this project.  The undermined area at the south side of the Center Pier 
(Repair No. 3) is located under the normal stream water level.  The other four areas are 
located above the normal stream water level.

In general, we believe that the undermined areas may be repaired by forming and 
placing cast-in-place concrete to fill the voids.  Loose rocks present in the void areas 
should be removed prior to forming and placing the concrete.  The concrete placed should 
be tied with the existing foundation or rock formation using grouted dowel bars, consisting

exercise care to completely fill the voids under the structures.

undermining.  Requirements for permitting should be evaluated by others for work on the 
south side of the center pier. 
A Geolabs representative should observe the repair operations to evaluate the 
conditions of the exposed voids prior to concreting. 
3.2 
Slope Erosion Protection 
Erosion of slope at the eastern side of the North Abutment (Repair No. 5) was 
observed during the site visit.  The eroded slope face was at a near-vertical slope 
inclination.  Some vegetation was overhanging at the top of the slope.  The exposed 
materials at the face of the slope generally consisted of gravel and cobbles in a clayey 
silt matrix. 
Based on our evaluation, we believe that grouted rubble paving (GRP) may be 
used to provide protection from erosion.  The grouted rubble paving should have a 
minimum thickness of 12 inches with an inclination of one horizontal to one vertical 
(1H:1V) or flatter.  The existing slope should be flattened to an inclination of 1H:1V or 
flatter to provide long-term stability of the slope.  The top and bottom of the GRP should 
be embedded a minimum of 4 feet below grade or embedded a minimum of 1 foot into 
solid unfractured rock.

The grouted rubble paving should be well drained to reduce the potential for build-
up of hydrostatic pressures behind the paving. A typical drainage system would consist 
of 1 cubic foot of permeable material, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation), wrapped with non-woven filter fabric placed at each of the weep hole 
locations. The weep holes should be spaced no more than 6 feet apart. 
A Geolabs representative should observe the GRP placement operations to 
evaluate the conditions of the exposed slope face prior to placement of GRP. 
3.3 
Slabs-On-Grades 
We understand that new slabs-on-grade will be constructed on the western side of 
the North Abutment. In general, limits of the new slabs-on-grade should be cleared and 
grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and other unsuitable 
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site.  
The slabs-on-grade subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place 
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined 
by ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
In general, we recommend providing a minimum 6-inch thick select borrow 
subbase or aggregate base course below exterior concrete slabs. Select borrow subbase 
or aggregate base course should be moisture-conditioned and compacted to at least 
90 percent relative compaction. 
To reduce the potential for substantial shrinkage cracks in the concrete slabs, 
crack control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the slabs with 
expansion joints at right-angle intersections. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor the site grading operations for the slab-
on-grade to observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the
