In accordance with the scope of services outlined in our revised fee proposal dated 
February 24, 2021, we performed geotechnical engineering services in support of the 
design of the proposed Emergency Department Renovation/Expansion project at The 
Queen’s Medical Center – Punchbowl in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. This 
letter report summarizes our findings and presents our geotechnical recommendations 
based on our review of the available subsurface information. The recommendations 
presented herein are intended for the design of foundations, slabs-on-grade, site 
preparation, and utility trenches. The findings and recommendations presented herein 
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from 
two shield volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau. The older volcano, Waianae, is estimated to 
be of middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau is estimated to be of late Pliocene to early 
Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time erosion 
incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield, volcanic activity returned with a series of lava 
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are referred to as the 
Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
The project site is on the southern flank of Punchbowl Hill. The Punchbowl Hill 
(Puowaina) is a tuff cone near the center of Honolulu built against the end of a spur of the 
Koolau Range. The tuff is mostly brown palagonitized vitric ash and lapilli with scattered 
fragments of coral limestone and Koolau basalt.

During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. 
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that 
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic sea 
level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations.   
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
The project site is located at the existing General Services Building and Pauahi 
Tower of the Queen's Medical Center in the Punchbowl area of Honolulu on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii. The project site is approximately bounded by Miller and Punchbowl Streets 
to the north and west, respectively, and bounded by the facilities of the Queen’s Medical 
Center to the south and east.   
We understand that portions of the existing General Services Building will be 
demolished for the proposed project. The remaining of the project will include renovations 
of the General Services Building and Pauahi Tower for the expansion of the Emergency 
Department. 
The topography at the site generally slopes gently downwards from the north 
towards the south. A small retaining wall provides grade separation between the General 
Services Building and the Pauahi Tower. Based on the topographic survey map provided 
for the previous geotechnical engineering exploration performed, existing ground surface 
elevations range approximately from +22 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) on the north to 
+14 feet MSL on the south.  
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
The subsurface conditions across the project site were formulated from the review 
of selected borings previously drilled for our geotechnical engineering exploration reports 
dated April 28, 1998 and May 7, 1998. The approximate locations of the selected borings 
are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Based on the review of the available subsurface information, the project site 
generally was underlain by 15 to 16 feet of very stiff to hard silty clay/clayey silt soils below 
the existing ground surface. The very stiff to hard clayey soils were underlain by dense to 
very dense cinder sand deposits extending to a depth of about 37 feet below the existing 
ground surface. Very stiff to hard alluvial soils consisting of silty clay with some gravel 
were then encountered and extended to the maximum depth explored of approximately 
51.5 feet below the existing ground surface.

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our previous field exploration generally encountered very stiff to hard silty clay/clayey 
silt soils overlying dense to very dense cinder sand deposits and very stiff alluvial soils at 
greater depths. Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated, we believe that shallow 
foundations consisting of spread and/or continuous footings may be used to support the 
proposed renovation and expansion. The footings may be designed to bear on the stiff to 
very stiff silty clay soils encountered at shallow depths. New footings to be located adjacent to 
the existing buildings (especially buildings with basement levels) should be lowered down to 
the same depths as the footings of the adjacent buildings to avoid surcharging the existing 
foundations. 
Results from previous laboratory tests performed indicate that the near-surface 
silty clay soils exhibit a low to moderate shrink/swell potential when subjected to moisture 
fluctuations. To reduce the potential for distress to future slabs-on-grade resulting from the 
swelling of the near-surface soils, we recommend supporting concrete slabs-on-grade 
required for the project on at least 12 inches of non-expansive select granular fill 
materials. Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering design 
recommendations are presented in the following sections of this report. 
Building Foundations 
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions, we believe that the proposed 
renovation and expansion may be supported on shallow footing foundations. An allowable 
bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the design 
of the footings bearing on the stiff to hard silty clay/clayey silt soils encountered at shallow 
depths. The bearing value recommended is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased 
by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
For footings bearing on the recompacted on-site soils and/or new compacted fills, 
the bottom of the footing excavations should be recompacted to at least 95 percent relative 
compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to the placement 
of reinforcing steel and/or concrete. If soft and/or loose materials are encountered at the 
bottom of the footing excavations, they should be over-excavated to expose the underlying 
firm materials. The over-excavation may be backfilled with select granular materials, 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not 
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction, or the bottom of footing may be extended down to bear directly on the 
underlying competent materials. 
In general, the bottom of the footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches 
below the lowest adjacent finished grade. Footings located adjacent to slopes or on slopes 
should be embedded to a sufficient depth to provide a minimum horizontal setback 
distance of at least 6 feet measured from the outside edge of the bottom of the footing to 
the face of the slope. Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements should be 
embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of

If the foundations are designed and constructed as recommended herein, we 
estimate total settlements of footings supported on the recompacted on-site soils and/or 
new compacted fills to be on the order of about 1 inch or less. Differential settlements 
between adjacent footings supported on similar materials should be on the order of about 
0.5 inches or less. 
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by a combination of frictional 
resistance between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive 
earth pressure developed against the near-vertical faces of the embedded portion of the 
footings. A coefficient of friction of 0.35 may be used for footings bearing on the 
recompacted on-site soils and/or new compacted fills. Resistance due to passive earth 
pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square 
foot per foot of depth (pcf). The passive earth pressure value assumes that the concrete 
for the footings is cast neatly against the stiff/dense on-site soils or new compacted fills. 
Otherwise, the soils around the footings should be well-compacted (minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction). Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive 
pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches below the finished grade should be neglected. 
Slabs-On-Grade 
The project site is generally underlain by silty clay/clayey silt soils, which are 
considered to be moderately expansive when subjected to moisture fluctuations. Unless 
concrete slabs supported by these soils are properly designed, there is a potential for 
future distress to the lightly-loaded slab-on-grade construction resulting from the shrinking 
and swelling of these soils due to changes in moisture content. To reduce the potential for 
structural distress resulting from the swelling of the on-site soils, we recommend providing 
non-expansive select capping fill material for a minimum depth of 12 inches below the 
bottom of the slab cushion. The capping fill should also extend beyond the edges of the 
slab a minimum of 2 feet. 
The non-expansive select capping fill should be placed as soon as possible after 
the subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented in the 
"Site Preparation" section of this report. The compacted capping fill and the underlying 
subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist prior to the final placement of concrete. 
The select capping fill material should provide a capping layer of moist soil above the 
underlying potentially expansive subgrade soil, thereby reducing the loss of moisture from 
the subgrade soils. In our opinion, the reduction in potential drying and wetting of the 
subgrade soils should reduce the impact of shrink/swell effects on the slab. 
In general, we recommend placing a minimum 4-inch thick cushion layer consisting 
of No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) below the concrete slab to provide 
more uniform support of the slabs and to serve as a capillary moisture break. To reduce 
future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to the floor coverings, an impervious

Exterior concrete walkways around the building structure may be required for the

Site Preparation 
Based on the current design concept, we envision that the majority of the site 
preparation work for the proposed project will consist of minor cuts and fills to achieve the 
design finish grades. 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
thoroughly cleared and grubbed. Vegetation, debris, rubbish, and other unsuitable 
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site. Soft and yielding areas 
encountered during clearing and grubbing below any areas designated to receive fill 
should be over-excavated to expose firm natural material, and the resulting excavation 
should be backfilled with well-compacted engineered fill. The excavated soft and/or 
organic soils should be properly disposed of off-site or used in landscaping areas, if 
appropriate. 
After clearing, and grubbing, all areas beneath future slabs and existing asphalt 
pavements should be over-excavated, where necessary, to provide the minimum 12-inch 
thick capping layer of non-expansive select granular fill material below the slab subgrade 
level, i.e., bottom of gravel cushion. The select granular capping fill should extend a 
minimum of 2 feet beyond the perimeter of the proposed building slab-on-grade. If 
walkways are planned around the building structure, the capping fill should extend to the 
outside edge of the walkway slabs. 
Prior to the placement of the non-expansive select granular capping fill, the 
subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at 
least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with ASTM D 1557-91 test procedures. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

Recompaction of the slab subgrades to over 95 percent relative compaction should 
be avoided to reduce the potential for future slab distress resulting from the 
swelling/shrinkage of the moderately expansive soils. 
The subgrades should be kept moist until covered by the non-expansive select 
granular capping fill. Where shrinkage cracks are noted after preparation of the subgrade, 
we recommend moistening the soil thoroughly to close all cracks. Saturation and 
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage 
may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well-compacted 
engineered fill. Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for 
over-excavation and engineered fill placement to account for variations in the 
over-excavation quantities. 
The on-site clayey soils generated from the excavations should not be used as fill 
materials below the building areas. They should be disposed of properly off-site or used in 
landscaping areas, if appropriate. Select granular fill materials needed for the 12-inch 
capping layer should consist of imported non-expansive granular material, such as crushed 
coral or basalt. The material should be well-graded from coarse to fine with no particles 
larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent 
particles passing the No. 200 sieve.  The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 
20 or more and should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent. 
Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs for conformance with these 
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its intended use. Fill materials 
should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not 
exceeding 10 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction. 
Site grading operations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs. It is 
important that a representative from our office observe the site grading operation to 
evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation process 
and whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar to those encountered in our field 
exploration.

We anticipate that underground utility connections may be required for the project. 
In general, good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling 
of the trenches for the new utilities. The contractor should determine the method and 
equipment to be used for trench excavation, subject to practical limits and safety 
considerations. In addition, the excavations should comply with the applicable federal, 
state, and local safety requirements. The contractor should be responsible for trench

In general, we recommend providing granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) under the pipes for uniform support. 
Free-draining granular materials, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation), should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above 
the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes. It is critical to use this 
free-draining material to reduce the potential for the formation of voids below the haunches 
of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench 
backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on-site soils or select granular fill 
material. The backfill material should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content, placed in maximum 8-inch level loose lifts, and mechanically compacted 
to at least 90 percent relative compaction. In areas where trenches will be in paved areas, 
the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement finished grade should be 
compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction. Mechanical compaction 
equipment should be used to compact the backfill materials. Compaction efforts by water 
tamping, jetting, or ponding should not be allowed.

LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from the review of the available subsurface information. Variations of 
the subsurface conditions between and beyond the field data points may occur, and the 
nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until construction is 
underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the 
recommendations provided herein. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict 
the approximate boundaries between the soil and/or rock types and, as such, may denote 
a gradual transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown 
on the graphic representations and/or presented in the text herein. These data have been 
reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. However, it must be 
noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation rainfall, temperature, and other factors.
