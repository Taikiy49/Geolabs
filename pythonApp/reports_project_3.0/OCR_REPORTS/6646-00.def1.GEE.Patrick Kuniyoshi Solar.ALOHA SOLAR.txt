performed for the proposed “Patrick Kuniyoshi Solar Farm” solar power generating 
project located to the east of Maili in Lualualei Valley in the Waianae District on the

1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project involves the design and construction of a photo-voltaic solar electric 
generating facility on the northwestern side of Hakimo Road in Waianae on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii.  Based on information provided to us, the solar facility will be comprised 
of two separate subsites.  One is identified as PV System #1 which is situated to the 
north of the Maili Community Park; and, the other is PV#2 which is situated at the toe of 
the north facing slope of the eastern end of Puu O Hulu.  It is anticipated that the solar 
panels may be supported by columns supported by concrete piers. Final structural 
details were not available at the time this report was prepared.  
Our geotechnical engineering exploration focused on the subsurface ground 
conditions for foundation support of the solar panels.  A total of 12 borings were drilled 
and sampled at the site to depths of 15.9 to 17 feet below the existing ground surface 
using a truck-mounted drill rig.  Soil samples were collected using Modified California 
and standard penetration test samplers.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu is comprised largely of the weathered remnants of the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of 
the western third of the island while the younger Koolau Volcano forms the majority of 
the eastern two-thirds of the island.  It is believed that Waianae became extinct while 
Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in 
central Oahu. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation.  As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to 
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea.  
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that 
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet waters 
protected by fringing reefs.  The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in 
the sediments and reef deposits.  Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous 
materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and 
induration of the calcareous reef materials.  
2.2 
Project Site Geology 
The project site lies on the Waianae Plain. The project site is classified from a 
geological standpoint as ‘Qls’ for Quaternary Limestone (Stearns, et.al.) which are 
basically emerged coral reef formations formed during the Pleistocene Epoch 
(2-3 million years ago) during an interglacial period which caused a higher stand of sea 
level.  This elevated exposed reef is thought to have been formed during the Waimanalo 
Stand of the sea when sea level was about 25 feet higher than it is today.  The 
widespread thin brown silty clay surface layers are classified as ‘Ra’ for Recent 
Alluvium, which is soil transported by water.

2.3 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is located to the east of the town of Maili in the district of 
Waianae on the west side of the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  Based on the preliminary 
plans provided, PV System #1 will consist of roughly rectangular arrays covering 
approximately 19 acres of fallow land located to the northwest of Kaukama Road.  This 
area is relatively flat to gently sloping, with ground surface elevations ranging from 
about Elev. +32 feet in the north west corner up to Elev. +55 feet Mean Sea Level 
(MSL) in the southeast portion.  This site is sparsely to moderately vegetated with tall 
grasses, weeds and shrubs.  Several trees up to 25 feet high are present in the 
southern portion of this site.  Several dirt roads and one drainage ditch transect this 
area.  The exposed surface soils are typically light brown alluvial silts and tannish white 
coralline gravels and sands. 
PV System #2 is planned for an irregular shaped parcel approximately 16 acres 
that is located to the north of the emergency bypass road that connects Kaihoolulu Street 
and Hakimo Road.  This area is moderately overgrown with tall grasses, small shrubs and 
several small keawe trees.  This area is also gently sloping at an overall gradient of 
approximately 1% with ground surface elevations ranging from Elev. +68 feet in the 
western sector up to Elev. +88 feet MSL in the eastern portion.  Numerous piles of 
basaltic boulders are present throughout the entire area.  The near-surface soils are 
characterized by grayish brown clays overlying tan coralline gravels and sands.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered a relatively thin 
surface layer of stiff to hard silty clay over dense to very dense coralline deposits at 
shallow depths. Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of 
our field exploration. 
For this project, we recommend using cast-in-place pier/micropile foundations 
with grouted pipe posts to support the proposed panel arrays.  Based on information 
provided, we understand that vertical loads from the proposed solar panels will be 
relatively light, about 6.6 kips per post, with wind uplift anticipated on the order of about 
5.4 kips per post.  Lateral loads due to wind forces are also anticipated with the shear 
load per post being about 1,450 lbs and the moment on the order of about 5,400 ft-lb 
per post. 
The micropile should be a minimum of 12 inches in diameter and drilled to a 
minimum depth of 8 feet below the existing ground surface.  The pipe should be 
embedded to a depth of 7 feet below the surface of the grout. 
The anticipated lateral loads on the grouted post foundations will come primarily 
from wind loads acting on the solar panel transmitted through the (3 to 5 feet high) 
vertical posts that are mechanically connected to the panel array racking systems. 
Thus, the lateral load imposed on a solar panel will be resisted primarily by passive 
pressure resistance against the vertical face of the grouted post pile (or pile cap if any) 
and by shear acting along the sides of the pile.  Passive pressure resistance against the 
vertical face of the grouted post pile may be estimated using an equivalent fluid 
pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf) above groundwater level.  
A side shear resistance of up to 500 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for 
transient loads such as wind or seismic forces only.

3.1 
Foundations 
3.1.1 Solar Panel Foundations 
It is our understanding that the vertical loads for solar panel systems are minimal 
and the primary concerns are uplift and overturning due to wind loads on the panels.  
In general, there are several options available to support the solar panel arrays.  
One typical support system is H-beam or pipe posts driven about 10 feet below the 
existing ground surface.  However, the presence of competent coralline materials at 
shallow depths below the existing ground surface constrains this support system 
since the coralline materials would most likely present refusal conditions to the 
driven posts. 
In general, a micropile foundation system would consist of small diameter (typically 
less than 12 inches), drilled and grouted piles with steel reinforcing.  The micropile 
foundation is typically constructed by drilling a hole, placing reinforcing steel in the 
hole, and grouting the hole.  Micropiles are desirable because they can be readily 
installed in numerous soil types and ground conditions. In addition, micropile 
installation causes minimal disturbance relative to adjacent structures, the adjacent 
soils, and the environment. 
A variation on the micropile alternative would be to use an H-beam or pipe grouted 
into a drilled hole to support the panel arrays.  This variant would eliminate the use 
of a reinforcing cage and simplify installation.  The racking system would require use 
of a W8x beam or 5-inch pipe (5.5 inches OD).

3.1.3 Shallow Spread and Continuous Footings 
It is our understanding that some small buildings will be included in the development 
of the solar farm.  Based on our exploration, we anticipate that the project site is 
underlain by near-surface clayey soils with a moderate expansion potential when 
subjected to moisture fluctuations.  The lightly loaded building foundations should be 
designed properly due to the potential for structural distress caused by the 
expansive soil. To reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from swelling 
of the subgrade soils, we recommend properly preparing the subgrade soils prior to 
fill placement as discussed in the “Site Preparation” section. In addition, a minimum 
of 12 inches of non-expansive, structural fill (capping fill) material should be 
provided below the building foundation and slab areas, unless coral material is 
encountered at subgrade level. 
In general, we believe the new buildings may be supported on a shallow foundation 
support system bearing on the capping fill or on in-situ coral materials and/or new 
compacted fills placed to achieve the design finished grades at the site. Based on 
the anticipated grading concept and the generally stiff/dense subsurface conditions, 
we recommend using shallow spread and/or continuous footings to support the 
proposed new building construction.  An allowable bearing pressure of up to 
4,000 psf may be used for the design of shallow foundations bearing on the capping 
fill and/or new compacted fills needed to achieve the design finished grades. This 
bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for 
transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
The bottom of the footing excavations should be moisture-conditioned to a minimum 
of 2 percent over optimum moisture content and compacted to at least 95 percent 
relative compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to 
placement of reinforcing steel or concrete. Soft and/or loose materials encountered 
at the bottom of footing excavations should be over-excavated to expose the 
underlying firm materials.  The over-excavation may be backfilled with granular soils

3.1.4 Foundation Probing 
Our experience suggests that cavities and/or voids are commonly encountered in 
coral formations. To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting 
from the collapse of cavities and/or sinkholes below foundations supported on the 
native coralline materials and/or coral formations, we recommend implementing a 
program of cavity probing and grouting at the foundation locations during 
construction of buildings or equipment pads. Since no cavities were encountered at 
the time of our field exploration, it is likely that the risk of cavities under the site is 
minimal.  If the owner is willing to accept the risk, the probing operation may be 
omitted for the solar farm area. Similarly, the foundation probing and grouting 
program may also be omitted for buildings and pads supported on 5 feet or more of 
compacted fill or on clayey soils, if the level of risk is acceptable. 
For estimating purposes, we recommend drilling probe holes at each isolated 
spread footing location (one probe hole for every 50 square feet of footing area) and 
at 10 feet on-center along wall foundations or continuous strip footings. The probe 
holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet 
below the planned bottom of foundation elevation. 
If cavities and/or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operation, 
additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacing to aid in delineating the 
vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and/or void. The probe holes and cavities 
discovered should be backfilled with sand-cement grout injected (pumped) at low to 
moderate pressures.  We recommend utilizing a low strength sand-cement grout 
with a slump range of 6 to 9 inches for the grouting operations. In lieu of the sand-

In addition, the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and grouting 
operations are complete. The contractor also should be made aware that a longer 
lag time between probing/grouting operations and foundation construction might be 
required in the construction schedule. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor the probing and grouting operations to 
observe the presence of cavities and/or voids in the subsurface and to allow for 
additional recommendations to be made if excess grout take and/or changed 
conditions are observed. Observation of the probing and grouting operations for the 
building foundations should be designated as a “Special Inspection” item.

3.2.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, we believe the excavated on-site clayey soils (without deleterious 
materials and/or over-sized materials) may be reused as a source of non-structural 
fill materials. If required, imported general fill materials should consist of materials 
with particle sizes of 6 inches or less in maximum dimension and should be free of 
deleterious materials. The material should have a laboratory California Bearing 
Ratio (CBR) value of 8 or higher and a swell potential of 2 percent or less when 
tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883.  
Non-expansive, select granular fill materials (structural fill) may be required under 
building slabs, exterior flatwork, and pavement sections. The excavated on-site 
coralline materials, where encountered, may be reused as a source of non-
expansive, select granular fill materials, if they are processed to meet the above 
requirements. Geolabs should test the imported fill materials a minimum of seven 
days prior to being transported to the project site for the intended use. If required, 
imported materials, such as crushed coral or basalt, may be used for structural fill. 
The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no greater than 
3 inches in largest dimension. The material should contain between 10 and 
30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. In addition, the material should have 
a laboratory CBR value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of not more than 
1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883.

content, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The compaction 
requirements for finished subgrades subjected to vehicular traffic, such as the 
pavement subgrade, should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction. The structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts of about

Aggregate base course materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.

percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance

requirement of the on-site fills and subgrades (at least 2 percent above the optimum 
moisture) is an important requirement considering the moderately expansive nature

3.2.4 Cavities and Voids 
Native coralline materials and coral formations generally are highly variable in terms 
of hardness, weathering, and fracturing. In addition, cavities and/or voids are 
encountered commonly in coral formations within the project vicinity. In many cases, 
cavities and voids in the coral formation may be filled with loose coralline deposits 
and/or clayey alluvial soils. In areas where coralline formation is anticipated near the 
ground surface, special attention should be given to the subgrade preparation to 
reduce the potential for distress to the future improvements.

3.3 
Coral Excavation 
The coral formation in the project area appears to be moderately to highly 
weathered and soft to medium hard in nature. It is anticipated that the majority of the 
coral formation may be excavated with heavy excavation equipment, such as the large 
backhoe excavator. However, coral formations typically contain localized hard and 
crystallized zones. Therefore, we anticipate some difficult excavation conditions may 
arise in localized areas during construction. It is possible that the excavation for a 
portion of the utility line trenches will require the use of hoerams or chipping. The 
contractor must exercise care to avoid over-ripping, which would disrupt the structure of 
the coral formation, resulting in a potential loss of bearing strength for improvements in 
the vicinity. 
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the materials are based on our 
visual observation of the existing coral formation and field and laboratory data from the 
borings drilled. We recommend encouraging contractors to examine the site conditions 
and the subsurface data to make their own reasonable and prudent interpretation.

Slabs-On-Grade 
Based on our field exploration, a layer of moderately expansive clayey soils 
blankets the project site at the existing ground surface. Unless slabs-on-grade 
constructed above these expansive soils are properly designed, there is a potential for

future distress to the lightly loaded slabs-on-grade resulting from shrinking and swelling 
of the clayey soils due to changes in the moisture content. To reduce the potential for 
structural distress resulting from swelling of the subgrade soils, we recommend properly 
preparing the subgrade soils prior to fill placement. In addition, we recommend 
providing a minimum 2 feet of non-expansive, select granular fill materials below the 
building slab areas. The non-expansive, select granular fill also should extend beyond 
the perimeter of the building a minimum of 24 inches (or extend to the edge of the 
concrete sidewalk surrounding the building). 
In general, we recommend concrete slabs-on-grade be a minimum of 5 inches 
thick. We also recommend lightly reinforcing the building slabs-on-grades with No. 3 
reinforcing bars spaced at 12 inches in each direction in lieu of the standard welded 
wire fabric. This is to reduce the potential for structural distress due to differential 
movements of the slabs due to the expansive nature of the on-site soils. 
For interior building slabs (not subjected to vehicular traffic or machinery 
vibration), we recommend placing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting 
of open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) below the slabs. The open-
graded gravel cushion fill would provide uniform support of the slabs and would serve 
as a capillary moisture break. To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration 
through the slab and subsequent damage to floor coverings, we recommend placing an 
impervious moisture barrier, such as a plastic membrane, on top of the open-graded 
gravel cushion fill layer. Flexible floor coverings, such as carpet or sheet vinyl, should be 
considered because they can better mask minor slab cracking. In addition, we 
recommend designing interior walls to incorporate some flexibility in accommodating a 
small amount of possible ground movements. 
If the slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic or machinery vibration, such as 
trucks and forklifts, we recommend providing a 6-inch layer of aggregate subbase below 
the slabs in lieu of the 4-inch thick gravel cushion fill layer. The moisture barrier may be 
omitted for these slabs. The aggregate subbase should consist of crushed basaltic 
aggregates compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) 
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and 
evaluation of soil properties.  The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at 
the appropriate sample depths. 
Three Grain Size Distribution tests (ASTM C 117 & ASTM C 136) were performed 
on selected near-surface soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils 
and to aid in soil classification.  Graphic presentation of the test results are provided on 
Plate B-1. 
Two Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to measure the liquid and plastic limits of the near-surface soils and to aid in 
the soil classification and identification. It also provides an indication of the shrink and 
swell potential of the surface soils from changes in moisture content. The test results 
are presented on Plate B-2. 
Two California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D 1883) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils.  Test results were also used to evaluate the quality of the materials tested for 
use as fills.  The test results are presented on Plates B-3 and B-4. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D 3080) were performed on near-surface soil 
samples to obtain an indicative measure of the strength parameters of the near-surface 
soils (cohesion and angle of friction). The test results are presented on Plates B-5 and 
B-6. 
Two Unconfined Compressive Strength tests (ASTM D 2166) were performed on 
selected soil samples to obtain an indicative measure of the compressive strength of the 
near-surface soils. The test results are presented on Plate B-7 and B-8. 
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D 1557) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships.  The test results are presented on Plates B-9 and B-10. 
Two one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples 
to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils under a small surcharge 
pressure. The test results are summarized on Plate B-11. 
Two pH (ASTM G51) tests and one minimum resistivity (ASTM G57) test were 
performed on selected soil samples. The test results are presented on Plate B-12.
