In accordance with Specification Section 31 63 26, CSL testing will be conducted in 
general accordance with ASTM D6760.  In general, CSL testing is a non-destructive test 
method to evaluate the condition of the concrete within cast-in-place concrete drilled 
shafts.  This method involves using a piezo electric transducer (transmitter) to generate a 
signal that propagates a compression sound wave through the concrete. Another 
transducer (receiver) is used to detect the signal on the receiving end. A typical

It requires installing one CSL access tube for each foot of shaft diameter, with a 
minimum of three tubes.  The steel access tubes (2.0-inch ID) will be installed at uniform 
spacing around each shaft in all the drilled shafts to be CSL tested to permit access for the 
CSL transducers (transmitter and receiver).  The access tubes will be securely attached to 
the interior of the reinforcing cage as near to parallel to the vertical axis of the reinforcing 
cage as possible.  The access tubes will be extended from the bottom of the reinforcing 
cage to a minimum of 2 feet above either the top of the drilled shaft concrete or the 
existing ground surface, whichever is higher, for access considerations. 
The access tubes will be filled with potable water shortly after concrete placement.  
The water level in the tubes will be checked daily and filled up if needed until the CSL tests 
are conducted.  The time of CSL testing after the initial concrete placement is specified 
such that CSL testing may be performed no earlier than 5 days and no later than 
20 workdays after concrete placement. 
CSL testing will be performed using the PISA (Pile Integrity Sonic Analyzer) testing 
equipment, manufactured by Piletest.com in Herzlia, Israel.  A pair of transducers 
(transmitter and receiver) will be placed in the access tubes and lowered to the bottom of 
the tubes along a near-horizontal plane. The transmitter transducer generates a sonic 
pulse (on the order of about 10 pulses per second), which is detected by the receiver in 
the opposite tube, in depth increments of about 2 inches.  While the sonic pulses are 
generated, the two transducers are simultaneously raised within the tubes at a rate of 
about 1 foot per second until they reach the top of the drilled shaft.  This process is 
repeated for each testing tube combination.  The CSL tests will be recorded on the 
Crosshole Sonic Logging (CSL) Testing Field Worksheet, as shown on Plate 2. 
After acquisition of the field data, the data will be further reviewed and processed to 
evaluate the integrity of the cast-in-place concrete in the drilled shafts.  The results will be 
presented in a report and submitted in accordance with the submittal requirements.
