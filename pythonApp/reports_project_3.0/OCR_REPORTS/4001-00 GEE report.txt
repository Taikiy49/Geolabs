INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Magic Island Walkway Lighting project in the District of
Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project
site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations for the
proposed light poles and design of parking lot pavements only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

man made peninsula projects outward in a southerly direction and is referred to as the
Aina Moana State Recreation Area as shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 The
proposed project consists of the construction of the following
e New walkway lights
e New parking lot lights
e Possible pavement reconstruction of the parking lot entrance and exit

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central
Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of Koolau and its geomorphology
and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea
level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain
The subsurface conditions along Ala Moana Beach are dominated by fossil
Pleistocene Age coral algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of
the sea which are intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic
section in this area is Koolau Basalt
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower
relative to present sea level of Oahu About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in
sea level occurred During that rise the valleys in the project area were drowned In the
last 10 000 years or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and
marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal
environments resulting in thick deposits of soft loose sediments along the coast in the
areas which were formerly valleys and drainageways
In the early part of this century man made fills were placed to create the existing
Aina Moana Magic Island Peninsula The fill was placed upon deposits consisting of
silty sands with coral fragments and coral formations

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The project is located within Ala Moana Regional Park which is located between
Kewalo Basin and the Ala Wai Boat Harbor and south of Ala Moana Boulevard in the
District of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The Aina Moana State Recreation
Area Magic Island is located at the eastern end of the Ala Moana Regional Park
The overall site is generally flat with elevations ranging from about 4 to 14 feet
above MSL The site contains retaining walls of up to 10 feet in height abutting the ocean
on the eastern and western sides of the peninsula At the tip of the peninsula a
man made beach exists with a rock break water surrounding the beach A retaining wall
separates the park from the man made beach
The proposed project site contained asphalt concrete A C paved parking lots
and driveways three comfort stations concrete walkways and A C paved walkways
The park is vegetated with lawn grass bushes and trees throughout the site

Light Pole Torsion Resistance
In general pole foundations will be subjected to torsional moments resulting in
torsion on the shaft foundations Torsion may be resisted by the side shear along
the shaft surface and adjacent soils In our foundation analyses we have
evaluated the shafts subjected to the torsional moments computed using the
lateral force acting upon the light fixture Based on our analyses we believe that
the 24 inch square shafts and 24 inch diameter shafts extending to the lengths
recommended should be able to resist the torsional moments without significant
movements We wish to emphasize that the sides of the holes should be relatively
free of loose materials and the concrete for the shaft should have close contact to
the in situ soils after the shaft has been constructed to provide the torsional
resistance required

Light Pole Construction Considerations
The performance of the shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor s
method of construction and construction procedures As a result we recommend
that a representative from Geolabs Hawaii be present to observe the installation of
excavated and drilled shafts during construction In our opinion the following may
have a significant impact on the effectiveness and cost of the shaft foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we anticipate that drilling
through loose soils near the water table elevation will be required during
construction of the majority of the drilled shaft foundations in the parking lot and
some of the drilled shaft foundations in the park To reduce the potential for
caving in of the drilled holes temporary casing of the drilled holes may be needed
during construction Installation of the casing may be by vibration driving or
twisting to advance the casing In addition care should be exercised during
removal of the temporary casing to reduce the potential for necking of the drilled
shaft Therefore a minimum of 5 foot head of concrete should be maintained
above the bottom of the casing and or groundwater level during removal of the
casing
The load carrying capacity of shafts depends to a large extent on the contact
between the shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper construction
techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in drilling or
excavating the shaft holes and in placing concrete in the holes The bottom of all
shafts should be cleaned of loose or disturbed material prior to constructing the
foundation The bottom should also be fairly level The concrete should be
poured neat against the excavation sidewalls Backfill against the shaft
foundations should not be allowed
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids is generally not recommended
Groundwater conditions are anticipated for the majority of the parking lot drilled

engineering services during construction of the proposed Magic Island Walkway Lighting
project The items of construction monitoring that are critical which require Special
Inspection include observation of shaft foundation excavations placement of concrete in
shafts and pavement construction Other aspects of earthwork construction should also
be observed by a representative from Geolabs Hawaii This is to observe compliance
with the intent of the design concepts specifications or recommendations and to
expedite suggestions for design changes that may be required in the event that
subsurface conditions differ from those considered in this report The recommendations
provided in this report are contingent upon such observations
If actual exposed subsurface conditions encountered during construction are
different from those assumed or considered in this report then appropriate modifications
to the design should be made

become evident until construction is underway If variations then appear evident it will be
necessary to reevaluate the recommendations provided in this report

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling six borings to depths ranging from about 16 5 to 39 5 feet below the existing
ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The
borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch O D Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

samples of the near surface soil to evaluate the strength characteristics for pavement
subgrade support Results of the CBR tests are presented on Plates B 2 and B 3
