INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Proposed Aloha Tower Marketplace in the district of Honolulu on the
island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our preliminary findings from our field exploration and
laboratory testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived
from our analyses for the proposed project These preliminary recommendations are
intended for design input only and may not be appropriate to form the basis for
construction cost estimates nor for construction bidding purposes Some of the footing
recommendations may need to be revised subject to the additional finding from the
construction phase field probing work and field observation

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
of the middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be of the late Pliocene
to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which erosion
incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series
The project site is on the coastal plain of southern Oahu The coastal plain was
built on the eroded flanks of Koolau Volcano which forms the eastern two thirds 3 of
Oahu The coastal plain was built by extensive accumulation of alluvium derived from
erosion of the volcano interbedded with coral reefs and associated deposits
During the Pleistocene Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles
of continental glaciation Most of the coastal plains developed during the Pleistocene
Epoch when the sea levels fluctuated significantly As the glaciers grew and advanced
less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the
present stands of the sea level When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of
water became available such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
GEO ARS_HAWAII
W O 3164 10 Page 7
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
SITE HISTORY
The site used to be a shallow reef area prior to 1800 The original shoreline
generally traced the present alignment of Nimitz Highway from about Pier 14 to near Fort
Street The shoreline then extended westward to the existing waterfront between Piers 5
and 9 The proposed Aloha Tower Marketplace will be located on Piers 8 and 9
The area where Piers 8 through 11 are located was reclaimed by placement of
several generations of fill A coral block seawall was constructed in 1857 using the coral
blocks of the historic Honolulu Fort The area behind the seawall was reclaimed by filling
with dredged soils from Honolulu Harbor The fill materials are highly variable consisting
generally of a mixture of silty sands gravelly sands clayey silts and silty clays with shell
fragments
The existing pile supported concrete wharves and bulkheads were constructed
between 1919 and 1928 outboard of the coral block seawall The area between the coral
block seawall and the bulkhead wall was filled to above the sea level and a pile
supported pier deck was constructed above the filled area The relative thickness of fill
in this area may be on the order of 30 feet
The existing Aloha Tower structure was constructed between 1924 and 1926
Aloha Tower is a 10 story structure supported by a shallow foundation system bearing
on the coral formation underlying the site The coral formation at the Aloha Tower
W O 3164 10 Page 8
location may be on the order of 10 to 15 feet below the surface Aloha Tower is a
historical structure and will remain in place within the Aloha Tower Marketplace
development
SITE DESCRIPTION
The proposed Aloha Tower Marketplace development will be located on Piers 8
and 9 of the Honolulu Harbor Existing 1 story and 2 story office buildings warehouses
and the pier structures currently occupy the site We understand that the existing office
buildings and warehouses are supported on shallow foundations with the pier structures
supported on pile foundations Associated with the pier structures are the bulkhead walls
which also are supported on pile foundations The proposed development is bounded
by Ala Moana Boulevard to the northeast and by Piers 8 9 and 10 to the southeast
west and northwest respectively A pile supported concrete deck presently surrounds
the development Aloha Tower is located to the northwest of the proposed development
Construction of the proposed development will require demolition of the existing
office building and warehouse structures However the on grade concrete slab and
asphalt concrete pavements will remain in place provided that they are not in conflict with
the proposed improvement elevations The existing pier structures and the bulkhead
walls will also remain The historic Alona Tower will be refurbished and incorporated into
the development

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the results of our field exploration and laboratory testing programs the
proposed development is underlain by variable soil conditions ranging from loose sandy
and soft clayey fill soils to hard coral formation The quality and uniformity of the existing
fills previously placed at the site should be expected to be variable Therefore the use
of a shallow foundation system bearing on these potentially variable quality fills for support
of the proposed structures involves a higher level of risk due to the higher potential for
differential settlements
In general the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings consist of a near
surface dense fill crust of approximately 3 to 5 feet thick overlying medium dense to
dense sandy soils with occasional loose sandy pockets In the area of the proposed
Building 1 soft clayey fills were encountered below the near surface granular fill crust
Based on this information the principal geotechnical engineering considerations for this
project include the following

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling 14
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 14 at the approximate locations shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2 The depths of our borings range from about 22 to 30 feet below
the existing ground surface The borings were drilled with a Diedrich D 25 skid mounted
drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and advancing rotary coring tools as
appropriate
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A of Appendix A Graphic presentation of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 14
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on selected soil
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
One Atterberg Limits test was performed on a selected sample of the soils to
evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of the test are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths and on Plate B 1
Four sieve analyses were performed on selected samples of the soils to evaluate
the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification The test results
are also Summarized on Plate B 1
Three direct shear tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the
shear strength of the material Results of the direct shear tests are presented on Plates
B 2 through B 4
Two consolidation tests with time rates were performed on the potentially
compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the materials
encountered Results of the consolidation tests are presented on Plates B 5 and B 6
