SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the site is generally underlain by a thin (6 inch) layer 
of silty sand over fill, consisting of clay with sand and gravel to about 6.5 feet.  The clay is 
generally medium stiff to very stiff near the top of the layer and grades to soft near the 
water table. Below the fill, lagoonal deposits consisting of very soft organic clays were 
encountered to the maximum depth explored of about 26.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface in Boring No. 2.  It should be noted that older alluvium consisting of medium stiff 
clayey silt was encountered at a depth of about 23 feet below the soft lagoonal deposits in 
Boring No. 1. We encountered groundwater at about 8 feet below the ground surface at 
the time of our field exploration. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, a shallow foundation system 
consisting of wall footings may be used to support the wing walls, provided that total 
settlement on the order of 2 inches is acceptable. We recommend placing a minimum 
24-inch thick layer of select granular fill material below the foundations to provide a firm 
and unyielding bearing layer. Prior to placing the select granular fill material, a geotextile 
fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) should be provided below and along the sides of the 
over-excavation.  Placement of the geotextile fabric at the bottom and along the sides of 
the over-excavation would reduce the potential for penetration of the granular fill material 
into the in-situ moist clayey soils. 
If the estimated settlement of the shallow foundations is not acceptable, a deep 
foundation system consisting of micropiles should be used to support the wing walls. Since 
the scope of our geotechnical investigation is limited to shallow foundations only, 
additional geotechnical exploration and engineering analyses would be required for the 
design of the deep foundation support system, if deemed appropriate. The text of this 
report should be referred to for detailed discussion and recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Golf Cart Bridge Repair project at Ted Makalena Golf Course in 
Waipahu, on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are 
shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering 
analyses. These recommendations are only intended for the design of the replacement 
wing walls adjacent to the golf cart bridge at Hole 17. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project involves reconstruction of cart paths and bridge repair within Ted 
Makalena Golf Course. Planned improvements involve repair/improvements for two cart 
path bridges at Holes 14 and 17.  For the cart path bridge at Hole 17, the existing 8-inch 
CMU wing walls which are distressed will be replaced with 10-inch CMU or 10-inch 
concrete wing walls. It is believed the existing CMU wing walls are supported on shallow 
foundations. 
We understand that the existing bridge is supported on precast concrete piles. 
Ideally, the wing walls should also be supported on pile foundations; however, to reduce 
costs, a shallow foundation system is desired for the new wing walls with the 
acceptance of future settlement. As such, the scope of our geotechnical investigation is 
limited to shallow foundations only.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late 
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during 
which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield, volcanic activity returned 
with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are 
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of 
the sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available 
such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacioeustatic 
sea level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations.  
The project site is located on the Ewa Plain, which is a gently sloping land 
surface formed by the long-term accumulation of alluvial sediments consisting mainly of 
terrigenous clays and silts with some embedded cobbles and boulders derived from the 
weathering and decomposition of basaltic rock residing further upslope. Some pockets 
of marine sediments and coralline detritus materials were intercalated within the alluvial 
sediments as a result of past sea-level fluctuations. 
The Ewa Plain is dissected by various drainage gullies, which emanate in the 
upland areas and discharge into Pearl Harbor. With the passage of time, some of the

2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is within the Ted Makalena Golf Course in Waipahu on the Island 
of Oahu, Hawaii. The replacement wing walls will be located adjacent to the golf cart 
bridge at Hole 17 as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, the golf cart bridge spans an unlined drainage canal that empties 
120 yards downstream into the Middle Loch of Pearl Harbor. The top of bank elevation 
for the northern and southern shores of the stream are roughly +8 and +7 feet Mean 
Sea Level (MSL), respectively. Above the top of bank, the ground surface is relatively 
level and grassy on both shores of the canal. Below the top of bank (above the water 
surface), the western side of the bridge is generally heavily vegetated with trees and 
shrubs; however, the eastern side of the bridge is relatively less vegetated.  
According to project plans dated September 2009, the existing 8-inch CMU wing 
walls vary in height from 2.5 to 5.7 feet, and in length from 6.0 to 7.9 feet. Distress is 
visible between the joints of the individual CMU blocks.

2.4 
Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the International Building Code, 2006 Edition (IBC 2006) as amended, 
the project site may be subject to seismic activity and seismic design considerations will 
need to be addressed. The following subsections provide discussions on the seismicity 
and soil profile type for seismic design. 
2.4.1 Earthquake and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic 
plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic 
activity; therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground 
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The 
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so 
small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of 
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate 
damage.

In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on 
the Island of Hawaii. Because majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii 
(over 90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and 
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of 
Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than 
Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of 
Hawaii. 
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes 
greater than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have 
occurred in the County of Maui. The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Islands 
of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu. For example, small 
landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of 
1938 (M6.8). Some houses on the Island of Oahu were reportedly damaged as a 
result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 (M7+). 
In the last 150 years of recorded history, we are not aware of earthquakes greater 
than Magnitude 6 that have occurred on the Island of Oahu. An earthquake of 
Magnitude 4.8 to 5.0 occurred along the Diamond Head Fault in 1948 on the Island 
of Oahu. The moderate tremor resulted in broken store windows, ruptured building 
walls, and broken underground water mains.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, a shallow foundation system 
consisting of wall footings may be used to support the replacement wing walls provided 
that a total long-term settlement on the order of 2 inches is acceptable. We recommend 
placing a minimum 24-inch thick stabilization layer consisting of No. 3B Fine gravel 
(ASTM C33 No. 67) wrapped with a geotextile fabric such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent 
below the foundations to provide a firm bearing layer. Placement of the geotextile fabric 
at the bottom and along the sides of the over-excavation would reduce the potential for 
penetration of the granular fill material into the in-situ moist clayey soils.   
If the estimated total and differential settlements of the shallow foundations are 
not acceptable, a deep foundation system consisting of micropiles should be used to 
support the wing walls. Since the scope of our geotechnical investigation is limited to 
shallow foundations only, additional geotechnical engineering exploration and 
engineering analyses would be required for the deep foundation support system 
alternative, if deemed appropriate.  
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering design 
recommendations are presented in the following sections herein.  
3.1 
Wing Walls 
The proposed wing walls are essentially retaining structures and need to be 
designed in a similar fashion. Based on the subsurface conditions, the following general 
guidelines may be used for design of wing walls. 
3.1.1 Wing Wall Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, a shallow foundation system 
consisting of wall footings may be used to support the wing walls. We 
recommend placing a minimum 24-inch thick stabilization layer below the 
foundations to provide a firm bearing layer. The stabilization layer should consist 
of No. 3B Fine gravel wrapped in a geotextile fabric, such as Mirafi 180N or 
equivalent.

An allowable bearing pressure of 2,500 pounds per square foot (psf) may be 
used in the design of footings bearing on the 24-inch thick stabilization layer. This 
bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) 
when considering transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic 
forces.  
We recommend embedding the foundations a minimum of 18 inches below the 
lowest adjacent finished grade. Foundations next to utility trenches or easements 
should be embedded below an imaginary 45-degree plane extending upward 
from the bottom edge of the utility trench, or the footings should extend to a 
depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This requirement is necessary to 
avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with additional structural 
loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement. 
The original wing walls have settled, causing cracks in the CMU blocks. The 
settlement is primarily due to consolidation of the very soft lagoonal deposits 
caused by the weight of the bridge approach fills and wall loads. Due to the age 
of the existing bridge, it is likely that the settlement due to primary consolidation 
is nearly completed. However, long term secondary consolidation will probably 
continue for the life of the structure. It is very difficult to determine the magnitude 
of secondary consolidation since it is affected by minute soil structure 
characteristics and other properties. We estimate the additional long-term 
settlements of footings to be on the order of about 2 inches. Since the bridge is 
supported on piles and should not settle, the differential settlements between the 
wing walls and bridge foundations may be on the order of about 1 inch. 
Lateral loads acting on the wall may be resisted by friction developed between 
the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure 
acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A coefficient of 
friction of 0.40 may be used. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be 
estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per 
foot of depth (pcf). This assumes that the soils around the footings are well

3.1.5 Drainage 
The wing walls should be well drained to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic 
pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide zone of 
permeable material, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), 
wrapped with geotextile fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) placed directly around a 
perforated pipe (perforations down) at the base of the retaining structure 
discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes. As an alternative, a 
prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used 
in lieu of the drainage material. The prefabricated drainage product also should be 
hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall. 
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone should consist of the select granular 
fills compacted to between 90 and 95 percent relative compaction as mentioned 
above.  Unless covered by concrete slabs, the upper 12 inches of backfill should 
consist of relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential for appreciable 
water infiltration behind the retaining structures.

3.2.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off-site. Soft and 
yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to 
receive fill should be over-excavated to expose firm material and the resulting 
excavation should be backfilled with engineered fill. The excavated soft and/or 
organic soils should be disposed properly off-site or used in landscaping areas, if 
appropriate.  Foundations of the existing wing walls to be demolished should be 
completely removed. Over-excavations resulting from the demolition operations 
should be prepared and backfilled as described below. 
After clearing, grubbing, and demolition, the bottom of the over-excavation should 
be proof-rolled and compacted to obtain a firm and unyielding surface, if possible. 
Due to the high moisture contents of the existing on-site soils, scarification of the 
subgrade soils is not recommended.   
3.2.2 Fill Materials 
The excavated on-site soils may be re-used as a source of general fill materials, 
provided that they are free of vegetation, organic materials, deleterious materials, 
and clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension. 
Based on our field exploration and laboratory test results, it appears that the on-site 
soils have high in-situ moisture contents. Therefore, drying or aerating the 
excavated materials may be necessary prior to their use as general fill. It should be 
noted that the fill requirements presented herein are intended as guidelines only 
and may be modified based on Geolabs additional laboratory testing and field 
observations on the available fill materials.

3.2.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Because the on-site soils have high in-situ moisture contents, drying or aerating the 
excavated materials may be necessary prior to their use as general fill materials.  
Aeration to lower the soil moisture and more compaction effort along with thinner fill 
lifts to achieve the specified compaction may be necessary and generally would 
reduce the rate of fill placement for this project. Fill materials consisting of the 
on-site soils should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the 
optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, 
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  
The imported select granular fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above 
the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling two borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, to a depth of about 26.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. We used 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 6-inch hollow stem augers. 
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operations on a near-continuous 
basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by laboratory testing. Soils 
were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System, as 
shown on Plate A. Graphic representations of the materials encountered are provided on 
the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to 
drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on 
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
