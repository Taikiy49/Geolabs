2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in
age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae Volcano
became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank was partially
buried below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank These banked or ponded
lavas formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau
The Waianae Mountain Range is composed of layered basaltic lava flows and
pyroclastic materials which are grouped and classified as the Waianae Volcanic Series
The Waianae Volcanic Series is divided into the lower middle and upper volcanic
members The lower member comprises lava flows and associated pyroclastic rocks that
built the main mass of the Waianae Shield Volcano The middle member consists of rock
that accumulated and gradually filled the vast volcanic caldera The upper member is a
relatively thin capping layer that covered the entire top of the shield volcano late in its
history of evolution
Once the Waianae Shield Volcano formed a long period of deep erosion
sedimentation and subsidence of the Island of Oahu occurred which produced the large
valleys of the western side of the shield volcano These erosional valleys were gradually
filed with enormous accumulations of alluvium and colluvium deposited as a combined
result of stream erosion base level rise sea level rise and subsidence of the island
mass Over time and as a result of sea level fluctuation some of the older alluvial and
colluvial deposits were buried and through consolidation and cementation formed
relatively dense deposits However the current surface soil deposits above these older
deposits are typically unconsolidated

3 1 Shrinkage Cracks
Based on field observations we noted that substantial shrinkage cracks are
present at the existing ground surface of the project site In general shrinkage cracks will
start to develop in the ground due to the lack of maintenance of the soil moisture content
as the soils start to dry out under the sun and dry weather conditions The development
of shrinkage cracks in the ground was apparently intensified by the prolonged dry climatic
conditions in the Nanakuli area resulting in large fluctuations of the soil moisture content
Due to the high plasticity of the on site clayey soils shrinking of the near surface
soils will occur as soil moisture evaporates from the soil system under the prolonged dry
climatic conditions characteristic of the Nanakuli area These shrinkage cracks may close
up to a certain degree upon wetting as the soils expand during periods of wet weather or
heavy rains However shrinkage of the on site soils and subsequent cracking of the
ground surface will recur when the dry weather returns Therefore the development of
shrinkage cracks will recur when the on site clayey soils are subjected to prolonged dry
climatic conditions which causes large fluctuations in the moisture content unless some

In addition if the lots are graded to the finished pad elevations and are allowed to

residential dwellings Detailed discussions of the shrink swell behavior of the on site soils
and the mechanism of shrinkage crack development are presented below
Based on the laboratory test results performed for the subject project the on site
clayey soils generally have a high shrink swell potential when subjected to
fluctuations in the moisture content and may be classified as expansive soils In
addition it will likely lose substantial strength when saturated and will shrink
appreciably when subjected to drying resulting in shrinkage cracks In general the
mechanism of shrink swell in expansive soils is a complex phenomenon and is
influenced by a number of factors including soil characteristics and environmental
factors
Expansive soil characteristics can generally be measured by engineering properties
such as soil plasticity and density to evaluate the shrink swell potential of soils In
general the soil plasticity reflects the capacity of the clay minerals in the soil to
retain large amounts of water while maintaining a coherent structure Therefore a
highly plastic soil such as those found at the subject site will have a greater
potential for volume change shrink and swell due to the loss or absorption of
moisture under cycles of drying and wetting In addition increased soil densities
through compaction or natural depositional history such as desiccation generally
have greater shrink swell potential
Environmental factors comprise of the initial moisture content of the soil and
moisture variations over time The initial moisture content generally impact the
potential of the soil to absorb or expel water from the soil system In general a dry

Shrinkage Crack Development Mechanism
We believe that the moisture content of the on site soils was moist and relatively
constant during the mass grading phase about 10 years ago As a result the
development of large shrinkage cracks might not be evident during the site grading
operations Due to the high plasticity of the on site clayey soils shrinking of the
near surface soils started to develop as soil moisture evaporated from the soil system
under the prolonged dry climatic conditions characteristic of the Nanakuli area These
shrinkage cracks may close up to a certain degree upon wetting as the soils expand
during periods of wet weather or heavy rains However shrinkage of the on site soils
and subsequent cracking of the ground surface will recur when the dry weather
returns Therefore the development of shrinkage cracks will recur when the on site
clayey soils are subjected to prolonged dry climatic conditions which causes large
fluctuations in the moisture content unless some measures are taken to maintain the
soil moisture content at a relatively constant level
In areas where ponding water occurs the wet ground will have a higher potential
for shrinkage cracks to develop since the wet soils will lose water more readily upon
exposure to dry conditions and will shrink more Therefore it should be recognized
that this phenomenon will recur and continue unless measures are taken to
maintain the soil moisture content relatively constant in order to reduce the
shrink swell behavior of the on site clayey soils due to moisture fluctuations

Long Term Maintenance Measures
As discussed above the phenomenon of shrinkage crack development will recur
depending on climatic conditions unless measures are taken to maintain the soil
moisture content relatively constant thereby reducing the shrink swell behavior of
the on site clayey soils due to moisture content fluctuations These long term
solutions may include landscaping installation of concrete walkways around the
house structure placement of non expansive fills in the yard areas and or
provision of an irrigation system to maintain the moisture contents of the
near surface soils in a relatively narrow range
Based on our previous experience with similar soils house lots without landscaping
have experienced varying degrees of soil erosion during heavy rains It is our
opinion that landscaping of the house lots should be given serious consideration by
the homeowners since it would generally achieve the following objectives
1 Landscaping along with proper irrigation will serve to maintain the soil
moisture content relatively constant due to controlled regular irrigation which
reduces the shrink swell behavior of the soils and consequently reduces the
potential for development of shrinkage cracks
2 Development of a root system from landscaping would reduce the potential
for erosion and for altering the drainage patterns
However the future homeowners should be advised or cautioned about the following
conditions which may occur or would be potentially detrimental to their property

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
ten test borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 10 extending to depths of about
19 to 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface The test borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our personnel who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 10
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample
depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket
penetrometer tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
