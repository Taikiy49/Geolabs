INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our soils and foundation
engineering investigation conducted for the proposed water
storage tanks at the U S Naval Submarine Base Pearl Harbor
Oahu Hawaii The general location of the site and its
vicinity is shown on Plate 1 Project Location Map
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of the subsurface investigation was to explore
the soil conditions at the site to provide data for use in
the design of the foundations of the proposed structures
The scope of our investigation included field reconnaissance
the drilling of five exploratory borings and laboratory
testing of selected soil samples
Results of our field exploration and laboratory tests which
were used in the engineering analysis that formulated our
conclusions and recommendations are presented in the appen
dices of this report

SITE DESCRIPTION
The site is located along Magazine Loch in the U S Naval
Submarine Base at Pearl Harbor on the Island of Oahu Hawaii
It is bounded to the northwest by Pier S 14 to the southwest
by Building 1232 the southeast by Pierce Street and the
northeast by Building 696 The general site conditions are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The ground surface is generally level and covered by asphaltic
concrete pavement with elevations approximately 8 feet above
sea level or 108 feet U S Navy Datum
The site is currently being used as temporary trailer storage
by the Radiation Control Division

Along the southwest boundary is an existing 69 foot long blast
wall that separates the site from Building 1237 Parallel to
the northeast boundary and 8 5 feet from Building 696 is a
buried 15 inch diameter drainline
Other buried utilities include two electrical duct corridors
generally having a north to south alignment and passing through
the site at about its midpoint
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The geologic condition underlying the site are the result of a
complex history of deposition and erosion during the Pleisto
cene and Recent epochs Most of this complex history is the
result of glacioeustatic sea level changes during the Pleisto
cene
During the early Pleistocene following the cessation of acti
vity of the Koolau Volcano material eroded from the main body
of the volcano was transported by streams to the area of what
is now Pearl Harbor Alluvial material was deposited when
those streams reached the ocean in a deltaic or estuarine
environment
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1151 00 Page 5
Following that sequence of deposition the sea level fell to a
lower level and the streams eroded a channel through their
Older deposits Sea level then rose again to the Makai Range
stand at 40 to 60 feet below the existing sea level
Concurrent with that rise in sea level eruption of the
Makalapa member of the Honolulu Volcanic Series mantled the
older alluvium and stream channels with volcanic ash which
consolidated to form volcanic tuff locally known as mud
rock
During the Recent epoch as sea level rose to its current
stand the tuff below the site was mantled with two sequences
of soft lagoonal deposits Those deposits were subsequently
covered by man made fill placed during the middle portion of
this century for land reclamation projects during the construc
tion of the existing Naval Submarine Base
A simplified sketch of such a profile interpreted from our
boring information is presented on Plate 3 Idealized Subsur
face Cross Section A A to aid in the understanding of the sub
surface conditions as described in the paragraphs below

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The field exploration disclosed that the site is underlain by 8
to 10 feet of medium to dense granular fill over soft and loose
soils to depths of 48 to 61 feet A relatively thin layer of
tuff over very stiff silty clay was encountered below the loose
soils The type of foundations recommended for the proposed
structures will depend on the weight of the structures and
their sensitivity to differential settlement Shallow footing
foundations may be used for light structures while pile founda
tions may be required for heavier structures sensitive to
settlement
Equipment Building Foundations
For the proposed light one story Structure spread or continuous
Footing foundation may be used
Footings should be placed a minimum of 18 inches below the
lowest adjacent grade
GEOLABS HAWAII
An allowable bearing value of 2000 p s f may be used for
foundations on the dense sand and gravel fill The bearing
Capacity value is for total dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third for short duration wind and or seismic
loading
Minimum footing width of 18 inches for spread and continuous
footings are recommended
The bottom of all footings should be compacted to a minimum of
95 of its maximum dry density as established by ASTM D 1557
70 and inspected by the soils engineer prior to concrete
placement
If a footing is situated next to a utility trench it should be
extended to the bottom of the trench or the trench backfill
must be well compacted to prevent footing settlement due to
settlement of the trench backfill
Estimated settlements are anticipated to be on the order of
less than one half inch total and one quarter inch differential
For the anticipated static floor loads a 4 inch slab over 4
inches of well graded aggregate base course is recommended
GEOLABS HAWAII

A plastic vapor barrier over 4 inches of 3 fine gravel may be
substituted for the base course should a capillary break be
deemed necessary to protect the floor covering A thin layer
of sand should be placed over the plastic barrier to aid in the
concrete curing and minimize shrinkage cracks

recommended

Fiberglass Water Tanks Foundation
Conventional metal water tanks could be supported on shallow
foundation However special fiberglass tanks are required to
store the pure water These tanks are sensitive to settlement
due to the relatively weak seams in the structure Therefore
it is our understanding that the tanks must be placed in an
enclosed structure and surrounded with sand backfill to provide
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1151 00 Page 11
additional support This backfill will increase the total
weight of the structures and foundation settlement thus will
limit the releveling capability of the structure
We have considered a bouyancy or floating raft foundation
system where the tanks could be buried in the ground a suffi
cient depth so that the weight of the excavated soil would be
equal to the weight of the tanks thereby minimizing settle
ments However this method would require a pump system for
the tanks and possible contamination of the pure water due to
subsurface water infiltration would also have to be considered
The following foundation Systems may be considered to provide
Support for the proposed fiberglass water tanks

The structure settlements can be reduced by sur

estimate that a waiting period of about 3 to 6

when construction can commence

Pile Foundations
se A em ae em mee ee
If the settlements for a mat foundation are not
tolerable pile foundations may be utilized to
reduce the settlements due to the consolidation of
the upper loose and soft soils by transferring the
structure loads to the underlying tuff layer

stressed concrete piles are recommended The piles
should extend to the tuff formation at about 45 to
60 feet below the existing ground surface
Since the tuff layer is relatively thin the piles
should be driven with a hammer delivering not more
than about 24 000 foot pounds of energy The piles
should be driven to a resistance of 5 blows per inch
for the last 2 inches
For the above recommendations an allowable pile
capacity of 80 tons per pile may be used Pile
settlements on the order of one half of an inch are
anticipated
We recommend that a minimum of 2 test piles be
installed to evaluate the in situ pile settlement
behavior under loading actual pile driving behavior
and to determine the length of piles to be ordered
Following installation of the test piles one of the
piles should be load tested to two times the design
load to examine the load settlement relationship of
the pile The pile load test should be performed in
accordance with ASTM Test Procedure D 1143 74

The actual lateral pile capacities will vary accord
ing to the actual installation procedure loading
conditions locations and the relationship with
other structural elements Therefore no specific
recommendation is presented at this time
In general passive earth pressures of 300 p c f
can be developed on pile caps and grade beams to
resist lateral forces To gain maximum Support from
adjacent soils it should be specified that a well
compacted granular backfill should be placed around
pile caps and grade beams
Site Grading
Because the site is underlain by soft compressible materials
any new heavy fill would induce settlements It is our under
standing that site grades will remain as is Any additional
fill placement over the sites should be avoided Should any
fill be contemplated subsequent to the issuance of this report
the soils engineer should first be consulted so that he can
re evaluate potential ground settlement and modify the above
recommended pile carrying capacities due to subsequent downdrag
effect

Construction Monitoring
Should our recommendations presented herein be accepted we
recommend that the foundation plans and specifications be
reviewed by us to examine if the intent of our recommenda
tions has been properly included In addition a test pile
driving and pile load test program should be undertaken
under the direction of a soils engineer
It is recommended that the filling footing excavation and
backfilling operations be observed by the soils engineer
during construction This is to see that adequate compac
tion has been obtained and the subsurface conditions are
consistent with the findings of our subsurface investigation
The recommendations given in this report are contingent upon
the above inspections

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by
drilling 5 to 86 5 feet below the existing ground
surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 A truck mounted equipment was used to drill the
borings
The soils encountered in the borings were classified
by visual and textural examination in the field by our engineer
who continously monitored the drilling operations The classi
fications were further reviewed by visual inspection and
testing in the laboratory The soils were classified in
general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System A graphic presentation of the materials encountered is
presented on the Log of Borings Plates A 1 through A 13
Soil samples were obtained from the boring by driving
a standard penetration sampler or a 2 4 inch I D split barrel
sampler with a 140 pound hammer free falling at a height of 30
inches The blow counts to drive the sampler the last 12
inches are shown on the log of borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Rock core samples were obtained by using a rotary wash
NXM series core barrel with a diamond bit The core recovery
and rock quality designation RQD are presented on the log of
borings

Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected samples to aid in the classification and evaluation
of soil properties The results of these tests are presented
on the log of borings at the appropriate sample depths
Six sieve analyses were performed to determine the gradation
characteristics of the site materials The test results are
summarized on Plates B 1 through B 3
Five consolidation tests were performed on undisturbed samples
of the underlying soils to evaluate their settlement character
istics The test results are plotted on Plates B 5 through B 8
