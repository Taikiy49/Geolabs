SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration along the proposed pavement rehabilitation project indicated 
that the existing pavement structure is underlain by variable fill materials consisting of stiff 
sandy/gravelly silts as well as dense silty sands and gravel.  The upper fill materials are 
generally underlain by very dense clinkers over hard basalt rock formation extending to the 
maximum depth explored of approximately 6.5 feet below the existing pavement surface.  
We did not encounter groundwater in the shallow borings drilled at the time of our field 
exploration. 
In general, the type and severity of the distresses exhibited in the pavement areas 
would determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction of the existing pavements should 
be performed. Existing pavements showing minor distresses and severity are generally 
rehabilitated with cold-planing and an AC overlay. Reconstruction of the existing 
pavements is usually performed for those areas where the observed distresses are likely 
caused by failures in the underlying base, subbase, and/or subgrade materials. 
Our pavement condition survey at the project site indicated that some portions of 
the pavements under consideration for rehabilitation appear to exist in fair to good 
condition. Based on our engineering evaluation and construction considerations, we 
recommend rehabilitating these areas with a regular AC overlay consisting of cold-planing 
3 inches of the existing AC surface and replacing with 3 inches of new AC overlay. 
Since the areas requiring reconstruction due to structural distress amount to more 
than 15 percent of the total pavement area proposed for rehabilitation, the pavement 
reconstruction should be designed based on a pavement life of 30 years in accordance 
with the guidelines presented in the pavement design manual prepared by the State of 
Hawaii - Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Material Testing and Research 
Branch. In general, the existing pavements for Wailea Alanui Drive may be reconstructed 
with a new pavement structural section consisting of 3 inches of AC on 5 inches of ACB 
over 6 inches of AB.  The existing pavements for Wailea Ike Drive may be reconstructed 
with a new pavement structural section consisting of 3 inches of AC on 4.5 inches of ACB 
over 6 inches of AB.  It should be noted that CBR tests and/or field observations should be 
performed on the actual subgrade soils during construction to confirm that the above 
pavement design sections are adequate. 
We recommend placing a layer of reinforcing geogrid (such as Tensar TriAx Grid, 
TX7 or equivalent) on a layer of permeable separator (Mirafi 140N or equivalent) over the 
recompacted existing base/subbase/subgrade below the new pavement structural section.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes, the older West Maui 
(Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala (Pleistocene 
Epoch). The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain located between these 
two volcanoes. The project site is located at the northern area of this gently sloping 
plain. 
The Isthmus of Maui was created by lava flows from East Maui (Haleakala) 
ponding on West Maui. It is comprised of alluvium washed from the slopes of West Maui 
and Haleakala. The erosional processes are dominated by the detachment of soil and 
rock masses from the mountain walls, and the soil materials are transported downslope 
toward the Isthmus primarily by gravity as colluvium. Once these materials reach the 
stream in the central portion of a valley, alluvial processes become dominant, and the 
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium. In general, stream flows in Hawaii 
are intermittent and flashy, such that the stream flows transmit large volumes of water 
for very short duration. Because of this, the transport of sediments is intermittent, and 
the bulk of the stream's hydraulic load consists of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders, 
cobbles, gravel, sands, and fines. When the erosional base levels change, these 
sediment loads are left as deposits. 
When deposits are left in-place for long periods of time, chemical processes 
begin to alter the materials, simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the 
materials. Chemical processes also cause induration, or cementation, of the 
coarse-grained portion of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or 
conglomerate. Simultaneously, erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors 
and upstream in headwaters. This continued erosion generates material, which is 
transported downslope covering the older alluvial deposits. Depending on the local base 
level and rate of transport, these newer sediments are generally transient in terms of 
geologic time. In addition, their consistency and density are generally less than those of 
the older, partially consolidated deposits.

Underlying the alluvial deposits are overlapping lavas from the West Maui and 
East Maui volcanoes. The bulk of the Haleakala Shield was built during the late 
Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows of the 
Honomanu Volcanic Series. During the Pleistocene Epoch, the characteristics of the 
lavas changed to very hard, thickly bedded flows of andesitic composition. These lavas 
have been grouped as the Kula Volcanic Series. Typically, the basalt consists of thinly 
to thickly bedded a’a and pahoehoe type flows. 
2.2 
Existing Site/Pavement Conditions 
The existing Wailea Alanui Drive within the project limits is generally a four-lane 
divided roadway between Okolani Street and Wailea Iki Drive except for the additional 
turn lanes and at the intersection areas. In addition, the existing Wailea Iki Drive within 
the project limits is generally a four-lane divided roadway between Wailea Alanui Drive 
and Piilani Highway except for the additional turn lanes. The approximate limits of the 
Wailea Alanui Drive and Wailea Iki Drive roadways are shown on the General Site Plan, 
Plate 2. 
The shoulder areas beyond the edge of pavement generally consist of short-cut 
grass, sidewalks, and traffic signs. The surface soils along the shoulder areas below the 
vegetation generally consist of brown sandy silts as topsoil overlying tan coralline sands 
with silt and gravel on Wailea Alanui Drive and brown clayey silts with sand along 
Wailea Iki Drive. The existing Wailea Alanui Drive roadway alignment generally slopes 
down from north to south between the limits of Okolani Street to about Station 14+00 of 
the project limits. The roadway alignment then generally slopes upward from about 
Station 14+00 to Wailea Iki Drive.  Wailea Iki Drive generally slopes down from 
Piilani Highway to Wailea Alanui Drive. 
Our office conducted a site reconnaissance in August 2013 to collect general 
information for the existing pavement conditions along the portion of the roadways 
under consideration. Detailed descriptions of our field surveys are presented in the 
following subsections. It should be noted that the term “Structural Defects” refers to 
areas where the pavement deterioration are believed to be caused by failures in the

2.2.1 Areas with Minor to Moderate Distresses and Severity 
Based on our site reconnaissance, the majority of Wailea Ike Drive as well as the 
southbound lanes (makai lanes) of Wailea Alanui Drive within the existing 
pavements under consideration falls into this category and appear to generally be 
in fair condition with localized moderate signs of structural defects observed. 
Pavement deterioration is restricted to light alligator cracking of low to moderate 
severity, localized subgrade fatigue of low to moderate severity, as well as a few 
rough AC patches along the outside edge tire lanes. The ride characteristic of the 
pavement was generally fair.  
2.2.2 Areas with Base/Subgrade Failures 
The majority of the northbound lanes (mauka lanes) of Wailea Alanui Drive falls 
under this category and was observed to be in poor condition with moderately to 
highly distressed areas. In addition, some of the existing asphaltic concrete 
pavements in localized areas along Wailea Ike Drive and the southbound lanes 
(makai lanes) of Wailea Alanui Drive also fall into this category.  
Alligator and alligator block cracking were observed and found to be generally 
moderate to high in severity. The poor areas consist of elongated, severely 
distressed zones of alligator block cracking with depressions of up to 1.5 inches 
and rutting of the pavement within the tire lanes. Longitudinal cracking along paving 
joints were also observed with gaps up to ½” wide. Potholes patched with AC 
material were observed at various locations along the alignment, especially in the 
tire lane fatigued areas.  
Longitudinal cracking along paving joints and perpendicular to the pavement joints 
were observed with gaps up to ½” wide from about Station 19+00 to 32+00 of 
Wailea Ike Drive. In addition, pavement distress cracking extending in random 
directions from manhole cover and water valves were observed. The ride 
characteristics of the roadways were observed to be generally irregular and bumpy

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling ten borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 10, at selected locations along the roadway 
rehabilitation/reconstruction project extending to depths ranging from about 2.5 to 
6.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. In addition, three bulk samples of the 
subgrade soils, designated as Bulk-1 through Bulk-3, were collected for laboratory 
Resistance (R) Value tests.  The approximate boring locations are shown on the 
Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.7. 
In general, our borings drilled for the roadway rehabilitation project encountered 
a pavement section consisting of approximately 3.5 to 9 inches of AC over 2.5 to 
6 inches of base course materials.  Below the existing pavement section, the borings 
generally encountered variable fill materials consisting of stiff to very stiff sandy silts and 
gravelly silts as well as medium dense to very dense silty sands and silty gravel.  The 
upper fill materials are generally underlain by very dense clinkers overlying hard to very 
hard basalt rock formation extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 
6.5 feet below the existing pavement surface.   
We did not encounter groundwater in the shallow drilled borings at the time of our 
field exploration.  It should be noted that water level may vary with seasonal rainfall, 
time of the year, and other factors. 
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered in the borings are presented 
on the Logs of Borings in Appendix A. Results of the laboratory tests performed on 
selected soil samples are presented in Appendix B.

3.4 
Rehabilitation of Existing Pavements 
In general, the type and severity of the distresses exhibited in the pavement 
areas would determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction of the existing 
pavements should be performed.  Existing pavements with minor distresses are 
generally rehabilitated with an AC overlay with cold-planing.  Based on our evaluation, it

with 3 inches of new AC overlay. 
In general, the contractor should vacuum-sweep the pavement areas 
immediately after cold-planing to remove gravel-sized materials and dust not picked up

Aggregate base course materials required for the project should consist of

relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with AASHTO T-180 or ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

aggregate, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and 
compacted to no less than 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity 
determined in accordance with AASHTO T-209 or ASTM D2041. Asphaltic concrete 
(AC) material should be constructed in accordance with Section 401 Asphalt Concrete

to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway alignments by drilling 
and sampling ten borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 10. The borings extended 
to depths of about 2.5 to 6.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. The approximate 
boring locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.7. The borings were 
drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Rock 
materials were described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as 
shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2. The Rock Description System is based on 
the publication “Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in 
Rock Masses” by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977).  Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-10. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.
