Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is comprised largely of the weathered remnants of the
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger Koolau Volcano forms the majority of
the eastern two thirds of the island
After the main shield building phase of Koolau there was a long period of erosion
followed by a renewal of volcanism by the Honolulu Volcanic Series This series
consisted of several generally single event eruptions in southeastern Oahu including
Diamond Head Punchbowl Tantalus Round Top Sugarloaf and the Koko Head
complex The project site is situated on the southern flank of Koolau between the
Punchbowl and Tantalus vents
2 2
Existing Site Conditions
Based on the grading plans provided the subdivision was developed on a hillside
with the upslope lots in cut and most of the downslope lots in fill The lots involved in
this consultation are located on the downslope side and are underlain by fill materials
No grading records were available for the preparation of this report however
visual observations of the exposed fill soils at the site indicate that the material was
granular material with a maximum particle size of about 3 inches or less This material
would be relatively permeable and capable of readily transmitting water

Exposed fill materials appear to be very granular in nature and

LOT NO 23
Design team site inspection was conducted on
along with Mr Pat Young of DHHL and Mr Allen Bird the owner
The primary problem at this residence is that during periods of
heavy rainfall water percolates up under the structure adjacent to
one of the column footings The interior of the structure also shows
minor distresses that appear to be the result of structural settlement
The exfiltration of water has the potential to undermine the footing
Site Inspection
A video taken by Mr Bird was viewed It showed significant
amounts of water percolating from under the house
The original plans show that subdrains were to be installed
behind retaining walls
Daylight points for subdrains were observed on the property
No surface source for infiltrating water was located It has been
surmised that the water is coming from under the road fronting
the residence either from the subpavement base and subbase
from the fill under the road section or a combination of the two
Exposed fill materials appear to be very granular in nature and
would likely be able to transmit significant amounts of water
Observations
The geometry of the site and the location of the exfiltrating water
suggest that the water is moving along the fill native soil interface
Conclusion
The origin of the water is upslope of the residence It may be
assumed to be moving on the interface between the fill and the
native soil
Recommendations
Install Additional Subdrains
We recommend installing additional subdrains under the house
Generally these subdrains should be located between column
lines and run parallel to the contour of the slope The drains should
be routed to daylight below the house The drains should be
installed at a depth of about 4 feet below the existing ground
surface however care must be taken so that the excavation does
not encroach into an imaginary 1H 1V plane extending down from
the lower edge of the footing

LOT NO 33
Design team site inspection was conducted on
along with Mr Pat Young of DHHL and Glenn Nagasako the
owner
Site Inspection
The primary problem at this residence is the apparent structural
settlement that has resulted in interior distresses and required
repeated adjustment of doors and windows
Some buckling was noted on the exterior of the building
Drywall in the interior was distressed with some joints in
compression
Site grading plans show that this residence is underlain by about
4 to 6 feet of fill
The CMU columns remain vertical however some cracking of the
glulam beams was noted
Observations
It was noted that there appeared to be a lack of maintenance on
the building e g dry rot on the entry porch and detached rain
gutters
It appears that the building has undergone some settlement It
appears that the settlement is strictly vertical as there has been
no apparent rotation of the columns as would be the case in
bearing failure or slope creep
Conclusion
It is believed that the settlement is the result of compression of
the fill under the house
Underpinning
A possible solution would be to underpin the existing foundations
using micropiles or resistance piers The building may be
re leveled by shimming at the tops of the columns
Recommendations
Grout Stabilization
It is also possible that the building could be stabilized by injecting
a polymer grout into the fill material under the foundations This
would serve to stiffen the material and add to its support
characteristics
