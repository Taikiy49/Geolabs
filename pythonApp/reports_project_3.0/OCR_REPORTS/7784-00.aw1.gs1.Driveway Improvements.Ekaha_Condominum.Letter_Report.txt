This letter report summarizes our findings and presents our geotechnical

are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located in the upper Kalihiwai area at 2540 Halaulani Road on 
the Island of Kaua’i, Hawai’i. The project consists of the installation of approximately 
640 linear feet of Portland cement concrete (PCC) driveway pavement to provide access 
to the Ekaha Condominium. Based on the information provided, the planned driveway will 
range from about 12 to 16 feet wide with 4-foot wide grassed shoulders on both sides of 
the driveway. Gradients up to about 21 percent are anticipated along the center line of the 
new driveway. 
Based on the profile plan provided, we envision cuts up to about 2 feet deep and 
fills up to about 4 feet thick will be required to achieve the design finished grades of the 
project. We understand underground utilities are not planned for this driveway 
improvements project.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the proposed driveway by 
excavating and sampling two test pits, designated as Test Pit Nos. 1 and 2, extending to 
a test depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface.  The test pits generally 
encountered clayey silt with sand extending to 5 feet deep.  For Test Pit No. 1, silty clay 
with organics was encountered at 3 feet deep. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the shallow test pits at the time of our field 
exploration. It should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to 
rainfall, seasonal precipitation, surface runoff, and other factors. 
Descriptions and graphic representation of the material encountered in the test pits 
are provided on the Logs of Test Pits, Plates 4 and 5.  Results of the laboratory tests 
performed on selected soil samples are presented on Plates 6 through 11. 
PAVEMENT DESIGN 
We understand that a PCC pavement section is desired for the planned driveway. 
In general, we anticipate that the vehicle loading for these roadways will be light and 
consists primarily of passenger vehicles, light pick-up trucks, and occasional heavy 
vehicular traffic, such as delivery and garbage collection trucks. 
We have assumed that the pavement subgrade will consist of the on-site clayey 
silt soils encountered in our test pits conducted at the project site. Based on the site 
conditions encountered, laboratory test results and the above assumptions, we 
recommend using the following pavement section: 
Rigid (PCC) Pavements 
6.0-Inch Portland Cement Concrete 
 8.0-Inch Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction) 
14.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness on a layer of Reinforcing Geogrid 
(such as Tensar TriAx Grid TX7 or equivalent) over 
permeable separator (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) on  
Recompacted Existing Subgrade 
Based on the subgrade conditions, we recommend incorporating a layer of geogrid 
into the pavement section to reduce the thickness of aggregate base course in the 
pavement section. In general, the geogrids will interlock with the aggregate base course, 
resulting in two benefits consisting of increasing the modulus of the aggregate base 
course by providing lateral confinement and enhancing the subgrade bearing capacity. 
The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a minimum depth of about 
8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture 
content and compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction. Due to the high

natural water content of the on-site soils, we anticipate proper moisture-conditioning and 
compaction of the on-site soils would be difficult and costly.  Therefore, the required 
compaction for the subgrade was reduced from 95 percent relative compaction. Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. 
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density. Some drying of the subgrade soils should be anticipated for the 
project. 
The subgrade soils should be thoroughly moistened and kept moist until covered 
by the pavement structural section. We recommend performing California Bearing Ratio 
(CBR) and density tests on the actual subgrade soils encountered during pavement 
construction to confirm the adequacy of the above section. The recommended section 
also assumes that adequate drainage will be provided for the pavement. 
Aggregate base course should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative 
compaction and should meet the requirements of Section 31 of the County of Kauai, 
Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction (September 1986). 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the base, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. It should be noted that the 
clayey subgrade soils are susceptible to softening and loss of strength upon saturation. 
Therefore, special attention should be given to the surface drainage of the pavements.  
It is essential to slope the pavement surface adequately and to maintain positive 
drainage gradients at the pavement surface such that surface water may be carried off 
the pavement quickly for proper discharge. Surface water ponding should not be 
allowed on-site during or after construction. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor the pavement subgrade preparation to 
observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and 
scarification process and to confirm whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar 
to those encountered in our field exploration.

Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and 
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site. 
Soft and/or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill should be over-excavated to expose firm materials, and 
the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fills. The 
excavated soft and/or organic soils should be disposed of properly off-site and/or 
used in landscape areas, where appropriate. 
After clearing and grubbing, the exposed subgrades should be scarified to a 
minimum depth of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the 
optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction. 
Where shrinkage cracks develop in the subgrade soil after compaction, we 
recommend preparing the subgrade soil again as recommended above. 
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement 
weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and 
replacement with well-compacted fill. The need for over-excavation due to soft 
subgrade soil conditions should be evaluated in the field by a Geolabs 
representative. 
Fills and Backfills 
Based on the preliminary drawings, new fills up to about 4 feet thick will be 
required to raise the existing ground surface to the design finished road grades. 
In general, on-site soils may be used as a source of general backfill materials, 
provided that deleterious materials such as vegetation are removed and 
over-sized materials greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension are screened. 
Organic soils are not to be used as backfill materials. In the event that imported 
fill materials are needed for the project construction, imported fill materials should 
consist of non-expansive select granular materials, such as crushed coral or 
basalt. The materials should be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no 
larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 
30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. In addition, the materials should 
have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 
1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. 
Geolabs should test imported fill materials for conformance with these 
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use.

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Fill materials consisting of the in-situ soils and imported fill materials should be 
placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative 
compaction.  Aggregate base course materials should be moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches 
in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction. Compaction should be accomplished by using sheepsfoot rollers, 
vibratory rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction equipment. 
As an additional check for stability and uniform compaction, we recommend 
proof-rolling the finished subgrade prior to placing the aggregate base course 
material using a pneumatic-tired vehicle with a gross vehicle weight of at least 
30,000 pounds, such as a fully-loaded water truck.  The equipment used for 
proof-rolling should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute and 
make at least two passes over each area designated for proof-rolling.  Proof-
rolling should also be performed on successive lifts of aggregate base course 
material.  Areas with excessive rutting and/or pumping should be over-excavated 
to expose firm material, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
either well-compacted fill or aggregate base course material. 
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the clayey subgrade soils are 
critical elements of the earthwork, Geolabs should perform observations and soil 
density tests during the site grading operations to assist the contractor in 
obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper moisture content on 
the subgrade soils. Where compaction is less than required, additional 
compactive effort should be applied with adjustment in the moisture content as 
necessary, to obtain the specified compaction. 
Fill Slopes 
Permanent fill slopes within the shoulder area constructed using on-site fill 
materials may be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to one 
vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be 
keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill 
against sliding. The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue 
up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill 
placement recommendations. Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and 
cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face.   
Appropriate slope planting or other permanent erosion control measures to 
reduce the potential for significant erosion of the exposed slopes should be 
implemented as soon as possible after the finished slope faces are completed.

SUBSURFACE DRAINAGE 
The new roadway is located at the base of a hillside.  Due to the potential for 
seepage water flowing through the subsoils and wetting and softening the clayey subgrade 
soils, we recommend installing a perimeter subdrain in the shoulder area between the new 
pavement and the base of the hillside.  A transverse subdrain should be installed across 
the lowest point of the roadway and connect to the existing drainage culvert. We 
recommend using a 6-inch diameter perforated polyvinyl chloride (PVC) pipe for the 
subdrain system.  The 6-inch diameter PVC pipes should be installed with a minimum 
slope of 0.5 percent.  A typical subdrain detail is shown on Plate 3.
