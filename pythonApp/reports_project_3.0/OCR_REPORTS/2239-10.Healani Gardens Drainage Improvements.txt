HEALANI GARDENS DRAINAGE IMPROVEMENTS

This report summarizes recommended drainage improvements at the building sites
based on our observations at the site and discussions with other team members
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project consists of eight separate two and three story wooden residential buildings
Seven of the buildings are situated on up slope terrain with the garage on the first floor and two
living levels above A retaining structure about 6 to 7 feet high is situated at the interior end
of the garage to provide grade separation
The mass grading work was completed in late 1990 with building construction
commencing shortly after
During a heavy rainstorm several years after project completion run off accumulated
behind some of the buildings and seeped under the building and behind the retaining wall
This resulted in some movements of the retaining wall due to the increased lateral load caused
by the water pressure softening of the subgrade soils under the garage pavement and other
water related damages
DRAINAGE IMPROVEMENTS
The recommended drainage improvements include installation of swales for surface
drainage subdrains for subsurface drainage and repair of small shallow sinkholes

Surface Drainage improvements
The existing grades behind the buildings are generally level therefore in order to
increase the flow of surface run off around the buildings swales are recommended to divert
the water to the paved areas in the front of the buildings In order to maintain the grades of
the swales they should be lined with a minimum of 4 inches of concrete To minimize the
percolation of surface water into the soils adjacent to the buildings the concrete paved swales
should extend to the rear wall of the building The layout of the swales is shown on the
attached Site Plans Plates 2 1 and 2 2
The subgrade soils under the swales should be kept moist and compacted to a
minimum of 85 percent of the maximum dry density ASTM D 1557 prior to concrete
placement
Subsurface Drainage Improvements
The installation of subdrains behind the buildings will help to intercept possible
subsurface seepage The subdrains are recommended behind Building
Building Nos 3 and 4 were constructed with a reinforced earth fill instead of a CMU retaining
wall The reinforced earth fill construction includes a subsurface drainage system consisting
of a geotextile drainage layer connected to a subdrain pipe therefore a new subdrain system
is not required for these buildings
To reduce the possibility of undermining the existing building foundation the subdrain
should be installed a minimum of 3 feet away from the building wall The subdrains should be
a minimum of 18 inches wide and about 4 feet deep A non woven geotextile should be
placed against the upslope wall of the subdrain trench to act as a filter while the downslope
wall and bottom of the trench should be lined with an impervious liner to inhibit water
infiltration The trench should be filled with clean permeable gravel such as No 3B Fine with
a 4 inch diameter perforated drainpipe at the bottom of the trench daylighted to drain to a
suitable outlet A detail of the subdrain is illustrated on Plate 3
Sinkhole Repair
Shallow sinkholes ranging from a few inches to a foot in size were noted in a few areas
behind some buildings The sinkholes were excavated by hand to determine the depths and
possible cause s of the erosion We discovered that the sinkholes were underlain at shallow
depths by perforated PVC pipes believed to be the subdrain pipes behind the short rear
retaining wall as shown on the building drawings It appeared that water from the roof ponded
behind the building and seeped into the perforated pipe carrying some of the soil with it
resulting in a sinkhole at the ground surface
