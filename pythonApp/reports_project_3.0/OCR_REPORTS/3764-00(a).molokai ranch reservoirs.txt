INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Molokai Ranch Reservoirs project in the District of
Kaunakakai on the island of Molokai Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for design of the proposed reservoirs
only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations
noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed reservoir project is located within Molokai Ranch in the District of
Kaunakakai on the island of Molokai Hawaii It is desired to construct two lined reservoir
lakes for water impoundment Each of the two proposed reservoirs will have an
estimated capacity of about 16 million gallons MG The proposed grading plan and
approximate locations of the two reservoirs are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2

have a bottom elevation of approximately 1 865 feet MSL and maximum water level of

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Molokai was built during the early to middle Pleistocene Epoch Ice
Age by basaltic and andesitic lava flows from two shield volcanoes East Molokai
Mountain and West Molokai Mountain The project site is located on the western side of
the East Molokai Mountain Prior to weathering and erosion the East Molokai Mountain
was a typical elongated basaltic andesitic shield shaped dome It was built over the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3764 00 A Page 5
northwest and east trending rifts with a steep slope on the north side where the lava
flows plunged into deep water and a gentle slope on the west side where the lava flows
banked against the West Molokai dome
During the Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to fill
the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels rose
because more water was available The land mass of Molokai remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio
eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower
relative to the present sea level on Molokai Chemical and physical weathering have
produced a residual lateritic soil cover on the saprolite soils and highly weathered
basalt andesite formation underlying the site

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling seven borings designated
as Boring Nos 1 through 7 at the proposed reservoir sites extending to depths ranging
from about 11 5 to 51 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate
locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our field exploration encountered a surficial residual soil layer
consisting of stiff silty clays of up to about 5 feet thick The near surface residual soil
layer was underlain by stiff to very stiff saprolite soils followed by highly weathered
basalt andesite formation extending to the maximum depth explored of about 51 5 feet
below the existing ground surface The highly weathered basalt andesite materials
encountered were generally friable to medium hard
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings during our field
exploration However it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change
due to seasonal precipitation storm surge conditions and other factors

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered relatively competent subsoil conditions
consisting of stiff residual soils overlying stiff to very stiff saprolite soils and highly
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3764 00 A Page 7
weathered basalt andesite formation extending to the maximum depth explored of about
51 5 feet below the existing ground surface
Based on the existing topography and the proposed finish grades at the bottom of
the proposed reservoirs excavations on the order of up to about 45 feet below the
existing ground surface will be required for the proposed reservoir construction
Excavation for the two proposed reservoirs will encounter the stiff to very stiff saprolite
soils and the friable to medium hard highly weathered basalt andesite formation It
should be noted that excavation into the saprolite soils and highly weathered
basalt andesite formation may encounter localized hard zones and large unweathered
boulders embedded in the formation Therefore hard zones and unweathered
basalt andesite boulders should be anticipated at localized areas during excavation for
the proposed reservoirs
Due to the generally stiff soils and highly weathered basalt andesite materials
anticipated during the excavation for the proposed reservoirs we believe that relatively
steep side slopes on the order of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V may be used for
design of the reservoir side slopes Due to the potential for presence of weathered rock
with sharp edges and points exposed at the finish subgrade level we recommend that the
side slopes of the reservoirs be proof rolled with a smooth drum roller for a minimum of
4 passes to provide a relatively smooth surface for placement of the geotextile underlining
and impervious liner In addition we recommend that a 6 inch layer of granular fill such
as a select borrow subbase be provided at the reservoir bottom as a cushion layer prior
to placement of the underlining and impervious liner Because a granular cushion fill
layer is not required for the side slopes of the reservoirs provisions for over excavating
some portions of the reservoir side slopes due to rocky conditions and replacement with
select borrow subbase fill or an approved granular cushion fill should be incorporated into
the contract documents as a contingent item

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site or at
designated areas on site Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and
grubbing below any areas designated to receive fill should be over excavated to
expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with
well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft and or organic soils should be
properly disposed of off site or at designated areas on site
The excavated subgrades or areas designated to receive fills should be scarified
to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3764 00 A Page 10
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade including
finished subgrades we recommend that the soil be re scarified moisture
conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction If the shrinkage
cracks are minor less than inch wide the subgrade may be thoroughly
moistened to close all cracks Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed
subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over
excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted engineered fill
at no additional cost to the Owner
Prior to construction of the reservoir fill embankment a keyway should be
excavated at the toe of the reservoir embankment to provide for stability of the
embankment fill against sliding The bottom of the keyway should extend into the
underlying stiff saprolite soils or at least 2 feet below the existing ground surface
whichever is greater A typical section of the recommended keyway for the
reservoir embankment fill is provided on Plate 3 The keyway should be backfilled
with compacted engineered fill
As previously indicated the finished subgrades of the reservoir side slopes should
be proof rolled with a smooth drum roller a minimum of 4 passes to provide a
relatively smooth surface for placement of the geotextile underlining and
impervious liner for the proposed reservoir Due to the potential for presence of
weathered rock with sharp edges exposed at the finished subgrade level we
recommend that provisions be made in the contract documents for over

Cut and Fill Slopes
Cut slopes exposing the residual and saprolite soils and the highly weathered
basalt andesite formation materials encountered in the borings may be designed
with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fill slopes constructed of the excavated
materials encountered in the borings may also be designed with a slope ratio of
2H 1V or flatter
Cut and fill slopes less than 25 feet in vertical height will not require benches
Benches should be provided for cut and fill slopes with vertical heights greater than
25 feet In general an 8 foot wide bench should be provided every 25 vertical feet
or less of cut and fill slope height The benches should be sloped to drain and the
drainage swales on the bench may require some form of erosion protection such
as concrete lining Benches will not be required for the reservoir side slopes since
the interior side slopes are protected by the impervious lining system
Fill Materials
Based on our field exploration the excavated saprolite soils and highly weathered
basalt andesite materials encountered in the borings may be re used as a source
of fill material for construction of the reservoir embankment provided that the fill
materials are screened to remove over sized materials materials greater than
6 inches in largest dimension placed and compacted as recommended herein In
general fill materials for the reservoir embankment and the keyway should be
placed in lifts of approximately 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent

We understand that the excess excavated material will be taken to designated
stockpile areas Because a relatively large volume and substantial fill heights of up

should be properly placed and compacted We understand that no future

Impervious Liner and Underlining
An impervious liner will be provided for the reservoirs to reduce water infiltration
through the underlying natural material In general the material type performance
installation and protection details of the impervious liner should be designed in
accordance with the recommendations by the liner manufacturer It is critical that the liner
maintain its integrity to prevent saturation and build up of seepage water pressures within
the excavation Since the bottom of the reservoirs are sloped down toward the reservoir
embankment a subdrain starting at the low spot within the reservoir and continuing
through the reservoir embankment is recommended to relieve the potential for build up of
seepage water below the impervious liner system The subdrain should discharge to an
appropriate outlet The recommended subdrain may be placed in the same trench for the
reservoir outlet drain line A typical section of the subdrain is provided on Plate 4
Based on our experience with similar liner installations there is a tendency for the
liners to become detached at the top of the water impoundment It appears that this may
be the result of inadequate anchoring of the top of the liner and insufficient flexibility of the
liner material This is of particular importance with the steeper design side slopes of
2H 1V recommended for this project Therefore it is our recommendation that the liner
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3764 00 A Page 14
manufacturer be consulted regarding proper construction detailing and installation of the
liner with particular attention to proper anchoring to reduce the potential of liner failure
Effects of fluctuating water levels on the performance of the impervious liner near the top
anchoring should also be considered
Technical representatives of the manufacturer of the impervious liner should be
required to be on site at all times during the installation process to assure that proper
construction procedures and precautions are followed A sales representative of the
supplier s may not have the technical expertise to adequately perform this function
We recommend that a geotextile underlining consisting of a heavy duty geotextile
fabric beneath the impervious liner be provided to reduce the potential for puncture of the
impervious liner The use of a geotextile fabric is recommended in lieu of a sand cushion
due to the ease of installation and the increased puncture resistance of the impervious
liner when combined with a layer of geotextile underlining In addition a sand cushion is
not recommended because of the steeper side slopes of 2H 1V recommended for the
proposed reservoirs
Access Road Design
We understand that the existing dirt road will be re routed and will be used as an
access road to service the two new reservoirs Based on our field exploration and
laboratory testing the on site surficial clayey residual soils appear to be fairly competent
subgrade materials Based on the above assumptions and considering the light traffic
anticipated for the proposed project we believe that a minimum of 6 inches of base
course compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction and subsequently applied
with a bituminous seal coat may be used for the preliminary design of the access road
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3764 00 A Page 15
The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction prior to placement of base
course The base course should consist of crushed aggregate compacted to not less
than 95 percent relative compaction CBR and density tests should be performed on the
actual subgrade soils encountered during construction to confirm the adequacy of the
above section
If asphalt concrete pavement is desired for the access roads a pavement
section consisting of 2 inches of asphalt concrete over 6 inches of aggregate base course
may be used for preliminary design purposes
As an alternative design for the access roads consideration may be given to a soil
stabilized pavement surface using a stabilizing agent such as TerraStone or equivalent
This type of pavement design involves application of a liquid soil stabilizer diluted in water
to the scarified subgrade and subsequently compacting the subgrade soil mixed with the
stabilizing agent In general the stabilized subgrade soil should be compacted at
moisture contents above the optimum moisture to at least 90 percent relative compaction
The solution contains oxidants solvents and dispersants which can cause the soil grains
to bond together to form a stronger more durable pavement than ordinary dirt road The
specific requirements of the mixture preparation and application details of the soil
stabilized pavement and compaction will depend on the product type and should be
designed in accordance with the recommendations by the manufacturer
The roads should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all surface
water off the road Surface water ponding should not be allowed anywhere on the site
during or after construction

Headwall Structures
Based on the information provided to us we understand that headwall structures
may be necessary Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our exploration
we believe that a shallow foundation system may be used to support the headwall
structures An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot p s f
may be used for the design of foundations bearing on the stiff in situ soils and or
compacted engineered fill 90 percent minimum relative compaction This allowable
bearing pressure is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third for
transient loads such as temporary wind and or seismic forces
For earth retention a lateral earth pressure of 40 pounds per square foot per foot
of depth p c f in the form of equivalent fluid pressure may be used for design of the
headwall structures in nearly level areas 5H 1V or flatter For headwall structures
retaining a sloping condition with a gradient of 2H 1V or flatter an increased active lateral
earth pressure of 55 p c f equivalent fluid pressure may be used
Lateral loads acting on the headwall structure may be resisted by frictional
resistance developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing materials A
coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used for foundations bearing on the recompacted
residual and saprolite soils encountered at shallow depths Additional lateral load
resistance may be provided by passive earth pressure acting against the near vertical
faces of the foundation Additional resistance due to passive earth pressure may be
estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 p c f Resistance due to passive
earth pressure should be neglected for headwall foundations located on or near slopes
with a gradient steeper than 3H 1V These values assume that the soil around
foundations is well compacted or undisturbed Unless covered by pavements or slabs
the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected

Utilities
We envision that new utility lines may be required for the project A granular
bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is
recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine
gravel should also be used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes
to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort of the
backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is critical to use this free
draining material to reduce the potential of formation of voids below the haunches of
pipes which could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes and to
provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of on site soils or select granular
fill material The backfill should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 2 feet
of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to a minimum
of 95 percent relative compaction For trenches in the designated stockpile areas
non structural areas the compaction requirement for the trench backfill may be reduced
to a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction
