PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is situated in the town of Waipahu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii
The site is currently utilized as the Waikele Shopping Center Parking Lot The site
consists of two contiguous parcels totaling about 6 78 acres of land area The site is
generally located south of the H 1 Highway west of and paralleling Paiwa Street and
north of the Mill Town Center commercial development The area is primarily former
agricultural lands which have been developed for residential use in the past 50 years
The project site is in a ravine bounded by H 1 Highway to the north Paiwa Street
to the west Koaki Street to the south and Kahaulua Street to the west It is believed
that this ravine is the remnant of a cane haul road to the former Waipahu Sugar Mill that
was just southwest of the site

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to
early Pleistocene in age As volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano ceased lava flows
from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau

now known as the Schofield Plateau The project site is located on the southwestern flank
of the Waianae Volcano
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of Schofield Plateau
generated sediments which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project
site and to the south these sediments accumulated and are interbedded with marine
sediments and coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge The thickness of
the sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1 Highway
to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the
confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea
levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposited lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet
waters protected by fringing reefs Along the base of the Schofield Plateau a series of
broad alluvial terraces formed The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in
the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the calcareous
reef materials Former sugar cane plantation activities and more recent suburban
development of the area within the last 100 years have brought the project area to its
present form
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
As mentioned previously field exploration consisting of drilling and sampling
borings was not conducted for this due diligence study Instead research and review of
available in house soil and boring data in the vicinity of the project site was performed in
order to evaluate the subsurface conditions at the proposed development site Based
on available information and our experience in the area the native soils at the project
site generally consist primarily of alluvial soils in one of the terraces as the base of the
Schofield Plateau Some residual soils or Koolau Basalt may be present at shallow depths
in the northern most limits of the site The general geologic classifications are described
as follows

Residual Soils
Residual soils are derived from the weathering of the basalt lava flows These
soils are generally competent and consist of silty clay to clayey silt and transition
to weathered basalt rock with depth The residual soils if present would be
limited to the northernmost areas of the site near the H 1 Highway
Older Alluvium
This soil unit generally consists of terrigenous sediments which have been
transported by fluvial action from the upper reaches of the Schofield Plateau
The materials consist mainly of very stiff to hard brown clayey silts and silty
clays Portions of the Older Alluvium unit contain some weathered basalt
boulders and cobbles with occasional sand and gravel interbeds These soils are
generally 20 or more feet in thickness and overlie residual soils or basalt bedrock
There is a potential for encountering indurated zones during excavation
This material generally exhibits high strength with good bearing capacity
However the material often have moderate to high shrink swell potential In their
undisturbed state they have good bearing capacity but the shrink swell potential
limits their use as backfill and for foundation or pavement support This is the
predominant material type within the project
Groundwater is not anticipated at shallow depths below the existing ground
surface However groundwater levels can vary significantly depending on seasonal
rainfall groundwater withdrawal and or injection and other factors

Steep Existing Slopes
As previously described the project site is situated in a ravine that is believed to
be the location of a former cane haul road The existing side slopes of this ravine are
relatively steep approximately one and one half horizontal to one vertical In
some portions of the site these slopes exceed vertical heights of 20 feet without
midpoint benches In general the existing slopes appear to be cut however there may
be undisclosed fills along portions of the slopes
Past experience with former agricultural sites indicates that many plantation
facilities were constructed without what is now considered to be adequate engineering
The existing slopes are slightly steeper than what is currently considered to be
appropriate for the type of soils exposed in the slopes The overall slope stability of the
slopes appear adequate however some stabilization or retaining walls may be required
in localized areas
Further alluvial soils are rarely homogeneous and the sand or gravel interbeds
which may be present have a potential for slumping when landscaped and irrigated on a
regular basis Therefore some form of slope protection may be required
Further exploration and analyses are required to evaluate the slopes in greater
detail
Expansive Soil Conditions
Based on our experience in the vicinity of the project site the on site alluvial soils
may exhibit moderate to high shrink swell potential when subjected to moisture
fluctuations
The actual expansive potential of the alluvial soils should be evaluated during the
design phase Over excavation of the alluvial soils may be necessary under foundations
slabs on grade and pavements We anticipate that over excavation and replacement of
the expansive alluvial soils will be required to provide adequate support for foundations
slabs and pavements At present we estimate that over excavation depth on the order of
about 24 inches may be required The resulting over excavation would need to be
backfilled with non expansive select granular fill
Alternatively consideration could be given to raising the grade in the base of the
ravine to separate the future development from the expansive soils This could also serve
to ameliorate some of the considerations associated with the existing steep side slopes
such as the need to install retaining walls to increase level lot area and could reduce the
overall height of the slopes in some portions of the site

Foundations
We understand that the planned development will consist of lightly loaded
residential structures Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the project site
we believe that the proposed structures may be supported on a shallow foundation system
consisting of spread and or continuous footings provided that several provisions are
followed
Due to the potentially expansive nature of the alluvial soils over excavation of
these materials may be required during construction Additionally house foundations may
require deeper perimeter footings and reinforcing to help resist expansive forces from the
soils
The potential need for over excavation under the structure foundations should be
further evaluated based on the actual subsurface conditions at the structure locations
In addition the limits and extent of the surface fill should be delineated as much as
possible Therefore we recommend that the subsurface conditions be evaluated by
conducting a field exploration program consisting of drilling and sampling borings when
the type and distribution of the proposed structures are better defined
Again if the base of the ravine is raised in grade control in the nature of the fill
material could reduce these constraints on foundation design
Undocumented Fills
Based on previous experience former agricultural areas are often underlain by
undocumented fills and disturbance from crop planting harvesting may extend to
significant depths
In general we recommend that the existing embankment or undocumented fills
disclosed during additional exploration and or site grading be over excavated where
necessary to expose the underlying firm ground The resulting over excavation should be
backfilled with compacted fill The areal extent and depth of over excavation should be
determined in the field during construction by the project geotechnical engineer The
bottom of the over excavated level should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction prior to the placement of fill materials
Additional Exploration
A field exploration program should be conducted for the design of the mass
grading and infrastructure installation for the development In addition a detailed field
exploration and geotechnical engineering analysis should be conducted for the design
of the fine grading and structure foundations during the individual lot development
