In accordance with our fee proposal dated and the Contract for

Project Considerations
The Palolo Homes project site is on the lower slope at the western side of
Palolo Valley on the Island of Oahu Hawaii as shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1 The existing public housing development consists of a row of 11 multi family
housing units adjacent to an undeveloped hillside containing potential rockfall hazards In
an effort to improve safety and reduce the risk for dangerous rockfall encroachment at the
homes rockfall mitigation consisting of a 280 foot long segment of capacity
rockfall impact barrier was constructed upslope from the homes
This report addresses the Phase construction of about 280 linear feet of rockfall
impact barrier fencing on the hillside above Palolo Homes Building Nos 122 through 124
The approximate location of the rockfall impact barrier and wire cable ground anchors
installed for the Phase project is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Future phases of rockfall impact barrier construction and some boulder stabilization
work are anticipated to complete the rockfall mitigation system for the remainder of the
housing development
This report describes our field observations of the rockfall impact barrier
construction and provides a summary of the wire cable ground anchor installations In
addition records of the wire cable anchor proof testing which was performed by the
contractor is presented in Appendix A

Post Foundations
During the initial field layout and vegetation clearing for the rockfall impact barrier
alignment an area of adverse ground surface topography was identified The adverse
topography consisted of a localized ground surface depression spanning the barrier
alignment where a post foundation Foundation P 5 was originally planned To avoid
setting the barrier post within the ground surface depression which would have critically
reduced the effective capture height of the barrier the northerly section of the barrier was
realigned between and By realigning the northerly end section of
the barrier 10 feet in the upslope direction the barrier circumvented the depression and
remained on adjacent higher elevation terrain In general the realignment consisted of
moving the barrier turning point from Foundation P 4 to Foundation P 5 and shifting
as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The eight post foundations identified as P 1 through P 8 on the Site Plan were
excavated using a Caterpillar 312 C track mounted backhoe The subsurface conditions
generally consisted of stiff to very stiff dark brown silty clay with appreciable embedded
hard basaltic cobbles and boulders representing alluvial and colluvial materials The 4 inch
diameter micropile boreholes were drilled using a pneumatic drill attachment mounted on
the backhoe The micropiles were drilled and grouted from the floor of each of the eight
foundation excavations prior to pouring the concrete block foundations The contractor
requested and was granted approval for a substitution to use epoxy coated solid threaded
No 10 bars from Williams Form Engineering instead of the galvanized bars specified in
the contract documents The galvanized bars were not readily available and the delay in
the production and delivery of the galvanized bars was undesirable In addition the
contractor requested and was granted approval to use No 10 solid threaded bar couplings
from Williams Form Engineering to attain the minimum 15 foot bar length for the
micropiles as specified in the contract documents The coupled joint was deployed in the
lower portion of the grouted micropile borehole using 5 foot and 13 foot lengths of bar
connected by the epoxy coated coupling The micropiles were grouted in the drilled
boreholes using Quikrete Non Shrink Precision Grout No 1585 00

Wire Cable Ground Anchors
The contract documents specified a minimum 6 inch diameter borehole with a
minimum borehole depth of 25 feet below the ground surface for the installation of the
single 1 0 inch diameter wire cable ground anchors The contractor was unable to provide
the specified borehole due to drilling equipment limitations Therefore an exception was
granted allowing the contractor to install double wire cable anchor units in adjacent 4 inch
diameter boreholes each having a minimum depth of 15 feet below the ground surface
The exception was granted provided that the installed anchor units meet the criteria for
acceptable load capacity performance as verified by the required anchor proof testing
As a result of the doubling of the wire cable anchor quantity to create the anchor
units additional 1 0 inch wire cable anchors of 16 foot minimum length were needed on
short notice to complete the anchor installations The additional anchors were obtained
locally but could not be colored with black powder coating therefore the exposed anchor
heads were painted black after installation to match the barrier system coloration The wire
cable anchors were grouted in the drilled boreholes using Quikrete Non Shrink Precision
Grout No 1585 00

Barrier Maintenance Considerations
Rockfall impact barriers require routine non technical visual inspection to ensure
the desired performance of the system In addition periodic maintenance work to repair or
replace broken or worn barrier system components following large rock impacts may be
necessary The routine visual inspection should be performed about once per year
minimum or following known rockfall impact s The visual inspection serves to identify
potential rock debris accumulations that may require removal and to inspect for possible
changes in the system condition or effects of environmental corrosion that may reduce the
barrier performance
Barrier maintenance such as the repair or replacement of worn broken
components i e the braking elements may be required following a larger rockfall impact
The frequency of barrier maintenance typically depends on the size and frequency of
rockfall events Rockfall impact barriers are designed to require minimal routine
maintenance Where a low incidence of rockfall activity is experienced more thorough
inspection of the system components by qualified persons should be conducted at
scheduled intervals of about 5 to 10 years In corrosive environments and where rockfall
activity is relatively moderate qualified persons should perform the thorough inspections at
intervals of about 5 years
Due to the often difficult access condition of some barrier installations owners
typically establish a contract with a local rockfall specialty contractor to perform the routine
inspections large volume rock debris removal and other system maintenance when
needed In some cases where the barrier is clearly visible from readily accessible
locations such as a roadway or the base of a low height slope the owner may elect to
perform the routine visual inspections themselves

Furthermore where barriers are located at readily accessible locations the owner
may perform the removal of rock debris using construction machinery However a rockfall
specialty contractor who maintains the proper equipment and trained personnel to conduct
work on difficult access slopes should be contracted to perform the removal of large
rockfall debris and to perform barrier system repair The rockfall mitigation specialty
contractor typically utilizes portable equipment which is transported onto the slope to
perform the potentially hazardous work Furthermore the rockfall specialty contractor
would typically employ temporary safety controls to protect down slope persons and
property during the performance of the work where needed
It is important to note the need for maintenance on rockfall impact barriers should
be minimal because the barriers are designed to withstand multiple rock impacts
Therefore it is not necessary to clean the barrier if there is only a light accumulation of
rocks in the bottom of the nets The ring nets could be temporarily detached to provide
access to the rocks from the front of the barrier
Finally we suggest periodically trimming the tall wild grass and brush surrounding
the rockfall impact barrier to reduce the potential for damage resulting from possible brush
fires on the hillside Fires have occurred in the past at the site although mainly during the
dry summer months High temperatures from a brush fire adjacent to the barrier could
affect the PVC coating on the wire mesh panels resulting in melting and a loss of coating
for the wire mesh Other steel components of the rockfall barrier may be adversely
affected by fire depending on the temperature
