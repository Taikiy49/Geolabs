INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Waterfront Manor Offsite Sewer project in the Waipio area on the
island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical engineering recommenda
tions resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses
The recommendations presented herein are intended for design input and may not be
appropriate to form the basis for preparation of final construction cost estimates nor for
construction bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to install a 15 inch diameter vitrified clay sewer pipe from the
existing sewer manhole located along the Ewa Branch Trunk Sewer near
lineal feet The approximate alignment of the proposed sewer line is shown on the Site
Plan Plate 2
Based on the plan and profile provided the manhole inverts for the proposed
sewer line will be at about 3 8 to 6 1 feet MSL corresponding to about 19 to 31 feet
below the existing ground surface elevation Site surface elevations range from about
12 feet MSL at the property line of the Waterfront Manor development to about 36
feet MSL at the Ewa Branch Trunk Sewer end
In general approximately 1 033 lineal feet of trench excavation sewer pipe
installation and trench backfill will be required for this project Based on the current

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated
to be of the middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be of the late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age When the building of the Waianae and Koolau
volcanoes came to an end only a slight embayment existed on the southern coast of
Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of
the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available
such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3365 00 Page 5
The processes of erosion and deposition caused the top of the basaltic lava
flows to be highly weathered and mantled by residual soils During the Pleistocene
Epoch the Kaena stand of the sea level at approximately 90 feet MSL a delta of silt
sand and gravel built out into the embayment near the project site Agricultural land
development and reclamation projects within the last century have brought the project
site to its present form

the following

2 bedding requirements for support of the vitrified clay sewer pipe

Trench Excavation
Based on the profile plan of the proposed sewer alignment the sewer manholes
will typically be about 19 to 31 feet below the existing ground surface Therefore
excavations on the order of 32 feet below the surface are anticipated for the proposed
construction
Our field exploration disclosed that the site is generally underlain by stiff to very
hard silty and clayey soils extending to the proposed inverts of the sewer manhole at
approximately 3 8 to 6 1 feet MSL It is anticipated that conventional excavating
equipment may be used to excavate the trenches
The method and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by
the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations The excavated soils
should be stockpiled no closer than a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3365 00 Page 8
excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential of adjacent ground
movement or subsidence
Trench Support
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we believe that continuous
interlocking steel sheetpile walls horizontally braced with cross struts may be used to
support the sewer trench excavations to reduce the potential of adjacent ground loss
or movement and to reduce the amount of water to be dewatered The sheetpiles
should be maintained until completion of the trench backfilling
The steel sheeting should be driven with a suitable sheetpile hammer to
sufficient depths to improve the stability of the trench bottom and to reduce the
possibility of bottom heaving or piping quick conditions which may be created in
the subsoils by upward groundwater seepage pressure during dewatering
It should be noted that the stiff to very hard clayey and silty soils will generate
significant friction resistance during sheetpile driving Therefore hard driving conditions
during installation of the sheetpile should be anticipated It should also be noted that
the sheetpile may encounter refusal conditions in the basalt gravel stratum which was
encountered within 5 feet of the proposed pipe inverts
For estimating the earth loads acting on the horizontal trench bracing or struts
the apparent pressure diagrams presented on Plate 3 may be used for design
guidelines The pressure distributions presented in the pressure diagram do not include
a factor of safety Appropriate loads due to surcharge traffic or sloping ground
conditions adjacent to the trench excavations should be added to the earth pressure
distributions for design

Pipe Bedding
Based on our field exploration the site is generally underlain by stiff to very hard
silty and clayey soils extending to the inverts of the proposed sewer manhole at
approximately 3 8 to 6 1 feet MSL Dense basalt gravels were generally encountered
at about 7 to 13 feet MSL Therefore excavations into the basalt stratum are not
anticipated
Bedding Material
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the
load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the pipe bedding is an important
design consideration For pipes bearing on the stiff to very hard silty and clayey
soils a minimum of 6 inches of compacted granular bedding material such as
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be provided below
the pipe Although soft loose soils were not encountered in the borings drilled
if soft loose soils are encountered at the proposed pipe inverts an additional
12 inches or more of No 3B Fine gravel wrapped in a filter fabric Mirafi 140N
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3365 00 Page 10
or equivalent should be provided below the bedding layer for more uniform
support
Before the placement of bedding material the excavated trench bottom should
be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii to confirm that firm materials
are exposed at the bottom of the trench or if the bedding should be extended
deeper as recommended above
Project Impact
Based on the current design concept the vitrified clay sewer pipe will bear on
approximately 6 inches of bedding materiai consisting of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation Considering the excavation depths and the
brittleness of the vitrified clay sewer pipe we recommend that the sewer pipes
be encased in a concrete jacket as required by Class C Bedding shown in the
City and County of Honolulu Standard Details Since the sewer pipe will be
encased ina concrete jacket the first zone of backfill extending from the bedding
material to at least 6 inches above the top of the pipes will consist of portland
cement concrete Above this zone excavated on site impermeable clayey silty
soils will be used as backfill materials
In addition it is our understanding that portland cement concrete will be placed
at regular intervals along the proposed sewer line alignment extending from the
bottom of the bedding zone to the area around the sewer pipe to form an
impermeable cut off wall The cut off wall and the impermeable backfill materials
should encase the permeable cushion materials to reduce the potential for
transmission of groundwater along the trench alignment Therefore from the
geotechnical engineering point of view we believe that these design

considerations for the bedding materials are reasonable in reducing the influence
of the proposed construction on the existing artesian water conditions along the

Backfill
In general the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the Standard
Specifications for Public Works Construction September 1986 Due to the significant
excavation depth the first zone of backfill extending from the bedding material to at
least 6 inches above the top of the pipes should consist of portland cement concrete
to reduce the load distribution on the vitrified clay sewer pipe
The trench backfill above the concrete jacket from 6 inches above the top of the
pipes to the existing ground surface may consist of the excavated on site soils The
backfill should be placed in 8 inch lifts with each lift mechanically compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
In general we believe that the excavated on site material may be re used as
trench backfill material However it should be noted that the on site soils encountered
consist of clayey silts silty clays with high in situ moisture contents Therefore there
may be some difficulty in attaining the required compaction when using the on site
materials for backfill As a result we recommend that the excavated on site materials
be aerated to attain a moisture content near the optimum moisture which would
facilitate compaction to the specified requirements
Where the sewer pipe traverses existing utility lines we envision difficulty in
achieving the compaction requirement in the space between the pipes Therefore we
recommend that lean concrete be considered as backfill material in these areas

Earth Pressure Loads on Pipe
Loads on buried conduits are influenced by the width of the trench the size of
the pipes the unit weight of backfill material and the frictional resistance between the
backfill material and the trench walls To calculate the vertical loads on the buried
vitrified clay sewer pipes and the concrete jacket average unit weights of 110 and
48 pounds per cubic foot p c f for backfill material above and below groundwater
level respectively and a coefficient of friction of 0 35 between the backfill and trench
sidewall may be used
For rigid pipe earth pressure forces upon the pipe and concrete jacket increase
rapidly with increased width of the trench and therefore the width of the sewer line
trench should be kept to a minimum In general the Contractor should adhere to the
guidelines presented in Section 11 Trench Excavation and Backfill of the Standard
Specifications for Public Works Construction City and County of Honolulu
Settlements
Primary settlement of the sewer line construction is normally caused by the
difference in the unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier
compacted backfill material placed over the sewer pipes The net increase in loading
will cause settlement of the underlying subsoils below the trench invert Based on our
calculations primary settlements of the sewer pipe and the manhole on the order of
inch are anticipated for the project
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good
workmanship will be engaged during construction Additional settlement could occur
if improper trench support dewatering procedures and backfilling methods are used
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3365 00 Page 13
Therefore we believe that the sewer line construction should be designed with
sufficient flexibility to accommodate possible settlements
Manhole Structures
In general we believe that the bottom of manhole structures will bear on the stiff
to very hard silty and clayey soils encountered within 30 feet of the ground surface in
our borings To provide more uniform support for the manhole structures we
recommend that the manhole structures be supported on a 6 inch layer of cushion fill
consisting of No 3B Fine gravel For these foundation conditions an allowable bearing
pressure of up to 4 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be used for design
Settlements on the order of less than inch are anticipated for these structures
The lateral earth pressures acting on the proposed underground manhole struc
tures will depend on the type of backfill used the extent of backfill and the compactive
effort on the backfill material around the structures We recommend that the new
manhole structures be designed with an equivalent fluid pressure of 50 p c f above the
groundwater level and 85 p c f below the groundwater level The above values
assume that the backfill around the manhole structures is properly placed and
compacted
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges traffic loads line loads and point
loads within a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the structure should be
considered in the design For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the manhole
structure a rectangular distribution with uniform pressure equal to 50 percent of the
vertical surcharge pressure acting over the entire depth of the structure should be used
in design Additional analyses during design may be needed to evaluate the surcharge
effects of point loads and line loads

Permeability Testing
A falling head permeability test was conducted in Boring No 2 Results of the
permeability test are presented on Plate C 1 of Appendix C of this report An average
hydraulic conductivity of about 6 3x10 feet per minute was obtained from the
permeability test It should be noted that higher permeability may be anticipated if sand
lenses granular materials and basalt stratum such as those encountered near the
bottom of Boring Nos 1 and 3 are encountered during the excavation
Dewatering
Based on the field exploration the groundwater level is about 10 to 16 feet
above the sewer manhole inverts Therefore significant amount of dewatering during
the trench excavation will be necessary to install the sewer pipes in a dry condition
In general we envision that a temporary siltation basin will be constructed to
recharge pumped water into the ground during construction The contractor should be
aware that discharge of groundwater into state waters and storm sewer drainage
system will require a National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System NPDES permit
The contractor should consult their independent consultant or the Hawaii State
Department of Health for the latest regulations and information pertaining to the permit
application if needed
Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the contractor The selection
of equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor and he she
should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during
construction depending on the conditions encountered The dewatering method
selected should have minimal impact on the groundwater level surrounding the
proposed excavation Dewatering operations should be performed such that only clear
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3365 00 Page 15
water is pumped Excessive pumping which removes soil fines may result in a
blowing or heaving of the excavation bottom or sides
We suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in selection of
a suitable method of dewatering
1 The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or damage
to existing facilities utilities and the environment
2 The dewatering method should maintain stability of and also provide safe
and dry working conditions in the excavation
3 The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow
modifications to accommodate various ground conditions
In general it appears that sump pumping inside trenches may be used if steel
sheeting is driven sufficiently deep to prevent piping and or heaving at the trench
bottom
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to dewatering
It is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be retained by the contractor
to design and evaluate the dewatering system used
The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His qualified representative who should be required
to be continuously present on site during dewatering activities will have the best
opportunity to promptly observe the effects of dewatering during construction and to
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3365 00 Page 16
implement as soon as possible necessary precautionary or remedial measures
including but not limited to stopping or slowing down the dewatering operations
Where encountered at the bottom of excavations permeable granular subsoils
may be susceptible to piping and quick conditions The dewatering operations should
be carried out without creating a quick condition or softening at the excavation
bottoms Therefore the project dewatering operations should be done without pumping
out soil fines pumping clear water only and should be coordinated with shoring
installation such that the excavation stability is not affected
Special caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering utility trenches
connected to excavations If this occurs the granular bedding and or backfill in the
utility trenches could act as subdrains and cause significant areal groundwater
drawdown resulting in settlements and potential damage to utility lines and or other
existing adjacent structures

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory tests were performed on selected soil samples obtained during our
field exploration A laboratory testing program was developed to schedule appropriate
tests to aid in evaluating pertinent engineering properties of the soil materials sampled
Moisture Content ASTM Test Method D 2216 80 and Unit Weight Determina
tions are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid in evaluating certain properties
such as relative saturation The moisture content expressed as a percentage of the
dry weight of the tested sample and dry unit weight are shown at the appropriate
sample depths on the Logs of Borings
Two Unconfined Compression tests ASTM Test Method D 2166 85 were
performed on selected cohesive soil samples Results of these tests are presented on
Plate B 1 and at the appropriate sample depths on the Logs of Borings
One Sieve Analysis was performed on a selected sample to evaluate the
gradation characteristics of the soils The test results are presented on Plate B 1
Two Atterberg Limits Tests ASTM Test Method D 4318 84 were performed to
evaluate the plasticity of the fine grained portion of the soils The Liquid Limit and
Plasticity Index of the material are used to classify the soil in accordance with the
Unified Soil Classification System The results of these tests are shown at the
appropriate sample depths on the logs of boring A summary of the test results is
presented on Plate B 2
Two Direct Shear tests were performed on selected samples to evaluate the
shear strength parameters The test results are presented on Plates B 3 and B 4
