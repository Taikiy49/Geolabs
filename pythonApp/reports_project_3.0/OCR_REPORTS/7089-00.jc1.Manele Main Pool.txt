Dear Ms. Lileikis: 
This letter report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Manele Main Pool project site on the Island of Lanai, Hawaii.  The 
project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This letter report summarizes our geotechnical engineering recommendations 
resulting from our field exploration, site reconnaissance, review of the available as-built 
drawings, and previous experience in the vicinity.  These recommendations are pertaining 
for the foundations and site grading only.  The findings and recommendations presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report. 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located at the Four Seasons Hotel (Manele Resort) and involves 
reconfiguration of the hotel main pool facility including re-grading and re-shaping the 
landscape areas.  Based on the information provided, we understand that grading 
operation may consist of approximately 4 to 12 feet of cut, to achieve the design finished 
grade.  Retaining walls will be constructed for grade separation. 
Details of the grading and structural design were not available at the time this report 
was prepared.  We envision that conventional retaining walls and/or soil nail retaining walls 
may be required, to accommodate existing terraced ground conditions and existing 
adjacent building structures.  Special attention should be given to the construction means 
and methods, to allow construction access and minimize risk of undermining adjacent 
existing building structures.  
Based on the as-built drawings provided, we understand that the existing hotel 
buildings are supported on the shallow foundation systems.  Based on the review of the 
original grading plans, the southeast and the southwest portions of the hotel were filled 
with 12-inch minus coral fill about 15 to 20 feet thick.

Regional Geology 
The Island of Lanai is a shield volcano built by eruptions at the summit and along 
three rift zones more than 1.20 to 1.46 million years ago.  The principal rift zone trends 
northwestward as a broad ridge and is responsible for the conspicuous elongation of the 
island in that direction.  A less conspicuous bulge on the southern side of the island is the 
result of eruptions along the southwest rift zone.  The summit of the shield collapsed to 
form a caldera from which a shallow graben, bordered by an echelon of step faults, 
extends south-southeastward toward Manele Bay.  Numerous dikes exposed in the sea 
cliff indicate that this Manele Graben lies along another rift zone. 
The caldera was largely, but not completely, filled by lava flows and the present 
Palawai Basin is a remnant of the caldera. Just to the west of it, Miki Basin (with an 
average diameter of about 0.9 miles) is a nearly filled pit crater.  The top of the ridge 
between these basins is about 140 feet above the floor of the Palawai Basin.  On the 
south, the floor of Palawai Basin merges with the floor of the Manele Graben, where the 
most recent lava flows in the caldera overflowed onto the outer slope of the volcano. 
The project site is located within the Manele Graben. 
Site Description 
The project is in the southern portion of Lanai, makai of the Four Seasons Resort in 
Manele.  The Manele Resort was constructed on a cut slope terrace of high ground on the 
north and a fill embankment of low ground on the south.  The existing pool facility is 
fronting the beach and the Pacific Ocean, surrounded by the existing hotel main lobby, 
eastern and western hotel wings on a terraced ground condition. 
Based on the topographic map provided, the ground elevations at the site ranged 
from approximately +70 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) on the north to +40 feet MSL on the 
south. 
Subsurface Condition 
We explored the subsurface conditions across the project site by drilling and 
sampling five borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, extending to depths of 
about 8 to 27 feet below the existing ground surface.  The approximate boring locations 
are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Based on our field exploration, the project site generally is underlain by 
approximately 5 to 9 feet of surface fills placed over weathered basalt rock formation.  In 
general, the surface fill is on the order of 2 to 23 feet thick and comprised of sandy and

Discussion and Recommendations 
Based on the boulder fills encountered in the borings, it may not be feasible to 
construct the retaining walls using a soil nail system.  We recommend construction of 
conventional retaining walls to support the planned grading separation. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we envision using 
shallow spread and/or continuous footings to support the proposed conventional concrete 
retaining walls.  An allowable bearing pressure of up to 4,000 pounds per square foot (psf) 
may be used for the design of shallow foundation system bearing on rock fill or rock 
formation. 
Detailed discussions and recommendations for foundation design and site grading 
are presented in the following sections. 
Shallow Foundations 
We recommend using shallow foundations to support the proposed retaining 
structures at the project site.  An allowable bearing pressure of up to 4,000 psf may be 
used for the design of shallow foundations bearing on rock fill or rock formation.  This 
bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one third for transient 
loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces.  A modulus of subgrade 
reaction of 150 kips per square foot per inch of deflection (pci) may be used for the 
compacted subgrade. 
Where loose a’a basalt (clinker) or cinder are encountered at the foundation 
subgrade elevation, we recommend over-excavating the loose cinder material and/or 
loose a’a basalt below the footing footprint to expose the underlying dense basalt 
formation to a maximum depth of 24 inches.  The subgrade over-excavation should

extend at least 12 inches beyond the edge of the footings.  The over-excavation should 
be backfilled with compacted structural fill materials. 
In general, the shallow foundation should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches 
below the lowest adjacent finished grade.  Footings constructed near tops of slopes or 
on sloping ground should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal 
setback distance of 6 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the face of 
the slope. 
The structural fill materials should consist of well-graded granular materials 
generally less than 6 inches in maximum dimension with sufficient fines to prevent the 
occurrence of voids in the compacted mass.  The structural fill materials should be 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction in accordance with 
ASTM D1557.  Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed 
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with the 
recommendations presented herein, total settlement of foundations is estimated to be 
on the order of less than 1 inch with differential settlements on the order of about 
0.5 inches. 
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance 
between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth 
pressure developed against the near-vertical faces of the embedded portion of 
foundations.  A coefficient of friction of 0.5 may be used for foundations bearing on the 
compacted fill or rock formation.  Resistance to lateral loads due to passive earth 
pressure may be calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 400 pounds per square 
foot per foot of depth (pcf) for foundations embedded in the compacted structural fill 
materials or rock formation.  These values assume that the soils around the foundations 
are well compacted.  Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in 
the upper 12 inches below the finished grade should be neglected. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor footing excavations prior to the 
placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation bearing conditions 
and the required embedment depths. 
Retaining Structures 
In general, we believe that retaining wall foundations may be designed in 
accordance with the parameters presented in the “Shallow Foundations” section herein.  
Wall foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded a minimum depth of 
24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade.  For sloping ground conditions, the 
footing should extend deeper to obtain a minimum 6-foot setback distance measured 
horizontally from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope.  Wall footings

Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges, traffic loads, line loads, and point 
loads within a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the wall should be considered in 
the design.  For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the wall, a 
rectangular distribution with uniform pressure equal to 28 percent of the vertical surcharge 
pressure acting on the entire height of the wall, which is free to deflect (cantilever), may be 
used in design.  For walls that are restrained, a rectangular distribution equal to 44 percent 
of the vertical surcharge pressure acting over the entire height of the wall may be used for 
design.  Additional analyses during design may be needed to evaluate the surcharge 
effects of point loads and line loads. 
In general, retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build-up of 
hydrostatic pressures.  A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide zone of 
permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), placed 
directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at the base of the walls 
discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes.  As an alternative, a prefabricated 
drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used instead of the drainage 
material.  The prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a 
perforated pipe at the base of the wall. 
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of the on-site granular 
soils or select granular fill material.  Unless covered by concrete slabs, the upper 
12 inches of backfill should consist of low expansion, impervious soils to reduce the 
potential for water infiltration behind the walls. 
Site Preparation 
We anticipate that site grading will involve up to about 12 feet of cuts and minor fills, 
to achieve the finished grade. In general, a Geolabs representative should monitor the site 
preparation to confirm whether the exposed soil/rock conditions during excavation are 
similar to those anticipated and/or encountered in our field exploration.  The following 
recommendations are intended to provide guidelines for the design of site preparation 
work. 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
thoroughly cleared and grubbed.  Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site to reduce the 
potential for contamination of the excavated materials to be used as embankment fill 
materials. 
In general, the on-site materials generated from the excavations may be re-used as 
a source of general fill and backfill materials, provided that the materials are screened of 
over-sized materials (greater than 6 inches in maximum dimension) and are free of 
deleterious materials.  Where used as fill and backfill materials, the on-site soils should be 
properly moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts

Excavation 
Based on our field exploration and previous borings in the vicinity, the project site is 
generally underlain by dense basalt formations ranging from hard to very hard with 
localized beach conglomerate deposits.  It is anticipated that the basalt formation near the 
ground surface may be readily excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment, such 
as excavators and ripping with large bulldozers.  However, localized hard zones should be 
anticipated and will require the use of hoerams or chipping for excavation.  Our laboratory 
tests indicated that the unconfined compressive strength of the basalt rock may be up to 
11,000 pounds per square inch (psi) or higher. 
Special attention should be given to excavation adjacent to the existing buildings.  
Based on the available as-built drawings, we understand that portions of the existing 
eastern and western hotel buildings were constructed on embankment fills.  The 
embankment fills consist of 12 inches minus coral fills.  We encountered 18 feet of basaltic 
boulders and cobbles fills in one of the borings drilled.  Based on the as-built drawings, the 
footings supported the existing hotel buildings were installed on about 3 feet of the 
structural fills that was placed on top of the embankment fills. 
We envision that the excavation for the planned retaining wall construction may 
expose the coralline/basaltic boulder fills, placed underneath of the hotel buildings.  To 
reduce risk of undermining the existing footings, we recommend a minimum horizontal 
distance of 8 feet between outside edge of the existing footing and the face of the 
temporary cut slope.  This temporary cut slope may be constructed with a slope inclination 
of up to half horizontal to one vertical (0.5H:1V), provided shotcrete about 4 inches thick to 
be placed on the face of the slope, to reduce risk of the embankment fill raveling.  The 
shotcrete facing should be placed as soon as the boulder fills are exposed. 
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based 
on our visual observation of the existing basalt formation and the laboratory testing results 
conducted on the selected rock samples from our field exploration.  We recommend 
encouraging contractors to examine the site conditions and the subsurface data to make 
their own interpretation. 
Utility Trenches 
We envision that the utilities for the improvements may primarily consist of water, 
sewer, and drain lines.  In general, good construction practices should be utilized for the 
installation and backfilling of the trenches for the new utilities.  In addition, the excavations 
should comply with the applicable federal, state, and local safety requirements.  The 
contractor should be responsible for trench shoring design and installation.

In general, we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) for support under the pipes.  
Free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation), should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above 
the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes.  It is critical to use this 
free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of 
pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes.  Improper trench backfill 
could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level of 12 inches above the pipes to 
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of on-site granular soils (with a 
maximum particle size of 3 inches) or select granular fill material.  The backfill should be 
placed in maximum 8-inch loose level lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 
90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground 
subsidence.  Where trenches are located below pavement areas, the upper 3 feet of the 
trench backfill below the pavement finished grade should be compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction.
