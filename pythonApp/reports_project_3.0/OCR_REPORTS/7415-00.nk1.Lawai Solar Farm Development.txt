SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered a thin layer of surface fill about 1 to 
2  feet thick overlying residual and/or saprolite clayey soils, extending to the maximum 
depth drilled of approximately 21.5 feet below the ground surface. We did not encounter 
groundwater in the borings drilled at the time of our field exploration. However, it should 
be noted that water levels may vary with seasonal rainfall, time of year, and other 
environmental factors. 
Based on the anticipated light structural loads and the generally stiff subsurface 
conditions, we recommend using shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings to 
support the new buildings.  An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per 
square foot (psf) may be used for the design of shallow foundations bearing on the 
recompacted on-site soils and/or new compacted fills needed to achieve the design 
finished grades. For footings bearing on the recompacted on-site soils and/or new 
compacted fills, the bottom of the footing excavations should be recompacted to at least 
90 percent relative compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface 
prior to the placement of reinforcing steel and/or concrete. 
To provide sufficient uplift and lateral load resistances, we recommend supporting 
the solar panel array structures on a deep foundation system consisting of cast-in-place 
concrete drilled shafts. Based on the anticipated column loads and the subsurface 
conditions encountered, we believe that a 12-inch diameter cast-in-place concrete 
drilled shaft may be used and extended to depths of about 6 to 8 feet below the ground 
surface for the foundation support of the new solar arrays.

recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
conducted for the Lawai Solar Farm Development project in the Lawai area on the 
Island of Kauai, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project. 
These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of 
foundations, slabs-on-grade, retaining structures, site grading, and pavements only. The 
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at 
the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located in the Lawai area on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. The 
general vicinity of the Lawai Solar Farm Development is presented on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. Based on the information provided, the project involves design 
and construction of a new photovoltaic (PV) power generating facility encompassing 
approximately 100 acres.  
Based on the information provided, we understand that the current exploration is 
for the planning purpose, providing general subsurface conditions and foundation 
design recommendations. Additional borings will be required for the detailed design 
phase after the facility layout plans are available, to delineate the subsurface conditions 
and verify the foundation design parameters predicted in the current planning phase.  
Detailed information pertaining to the structural loads and project layout were not 
available at the time this report was prepared. However, we envision that structures will 
consist mainly of elevated solar panel arrays and small support structures one to 
two-stories in height.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single dissected basaltic shield volcano 
built by the extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series beginning about 
5 to 6 million years ago. The eruption of the Waimea Volcanic Series ended about 
2½ million years ago and was followed by a long period of erosion. Following the 
cessation of this main volcano shield-building phase, about 1½ million years ago, there 
was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of the post-erosional 
Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the thick alluvial sediments of 
the Palikea Formation. 
Rock formations of the Koloa Volcanic Series are generally characterized as 
thick lava flows composed of dense basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in 
north-south trends at various locales. Associated with the Koloa Volcanic Series lava 
flows are some deposits of pyroclastic materials (volcanic ash and cinders), which 
usually form cinder cones surrounding the vent. Rock formations of the Koloa Volcanic 
Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of Kauai, including the project site. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, many sea level changes occurred as a result of 
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great continental 
glaciers accumulated, ocean levels decreased. Conversely, as the glaciers receded or 
melted, global sea levels increased. The landmass of Kauai remained essentially stable 
during these changes, and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. These 
glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea, which were both higher and 
lower relative to the present sea level of the Island of Kauai. 
The project area is generally composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of 
lava from the Koloa Volcanic Series. The basalt rock formation observed in the Koloa 
area appear to be representative of the pahoehoe lava flow type which spread and 
ponded as it approached the ocean from inland areas. Pahoehoe lavas are typically 
characterized by a smoother, billowy surface and an internal structure of 
vesicular (porous) rock with limited clinker materials.

At the project site, in-situ chemical weathering of the Koloa lava flows has formed 
a mantle of residual saprolitic soils. In general, saprolite is composed mainly of silty 
material while residual soils are more clayey. Both residual and saprolitic soils are 
typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks. The residual and saprolitic soils 
grade to basaltic rock formation with depth. 
2.2 
Site Description 
The project site is located on a vacant parcel of land in the Lawai area on the 
Island of Kauai, Hawaii. It is approximately bounded by Koloa Road to the north, the 
Kukui’ula Development to the west, and Aepoeha Reservoir to the east as shown on the 
Project Location Map (Plate 1) and Site Plan (Plate 2).  
Based on our field observations, the parcel of land consists of gentle hills and 
generally decreases in elevation in a southerly direction. Generally, the project area is 
covered by shoulder to head-high California grass with trees scattered sparsely 
throughout. An unpaved access road runs down the center of the project limits.

Subsurface Conditions 
The subsurface conditions were explored by drilling and sampling five borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 105, extending to depths of approximately 16.5 
to 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, one bulk sample of the 
near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1, was collected for laboratory California Bearing 
Ratio (CBR) and thermal resistivity testing to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics and heat transfer characteristics, respectively, of the near-surface soils. 
The approximate boring locations drilled and bulk sample collected are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, our field exploration encountered a thin layer of surface fill material 
about 1 to 2 feet thick over residual and/or saprolitic soils. The fill material generally 
consisted of stiff silty clay soils with a little sand. Below the fill materials, we 
encountered stiff clayey residual and/or saprolitic soils extending to the maximum depth 
explored of approximately 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered a thin surface fill layer about 1 to 
2 feet thick over residual and/or saprolitic soils. The fill material generally consisted of 
stiff silty clay soils with a little sand. Below the fill materials, we encountered stiff clayey 
residual and/or saprolite soils, extending to the maximum depth drilled of approximately 
21.5 feet below the ground surface. We did not encounter groundwater in the borings 
drilled at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on the anticipated light structural loads and competent subsurface 
conditions encountered, we recommend using shallow spread and/or continuous strip 
footings to support the building structures.  An allowable bearing pressure of up to 
3,000 psf may be used for the design of shallow foundations bearing on the 
recompacted on-site soils and/or new compacted fills needed to achieve the design 
finished grades. For footings bearing on the recompacted on-site soils and/or new 
compacted fills, the bottom of the footing excavations should be recompacted to at least 
90 percent relative compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface 
prior to the placement of reinforcing steel and/or concrete. 
We recommend that the solar panel array structures may be supported on 
cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts, to provide sufficient uplift and lateral load resistance. 
The drilled shaft foundation may consist of 12-inch diameter cast-in-place concrete piers 
extending to depths of about 6 to 8 feet below the finished grade, depending on the design 
uplift load demand. 
The design of metallic substructures, such as metallic piping, should consider the 
effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure. On the basis of the field resistivity 
survey results, the subsurface soils at the project site have resistivity values ranging from 
about 8,026 to 15,358 ohm-cm, corresponding to corrosion ratings of 4 to 5, respectively. It 
should be noted that the resistivity values of the subsurface soils varied substantially with 
depth and location across the project area; therefore, special attention should be given by 
the corrosion engineer when using the resistivity results provided in this report.

3.1 
Building Foundations 
We understand that a new 100-acre photovoltaic (PV) power generating facility is 
planned at the Lawai site. Details of the PV facility layout plans and structural loading 
information were not available at the time this report was prepared. We envision that the 
new buildings may consist of one to two stories with reinforced concrete or concrete 
masonry unit (CMU) walls. 
Based on the anticipated light structural loads and the competent subsurface 
conditions encountered, we believe that the new buildings may be supported on shallow 
foundation system, consisting of spread and/or continuous strip footings. An allowable 
bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for the design of the footings bearing on 
the recompacted on-site soils and/or new compacted fills needed to achieve the design 
finished grades. The bearing value recommended is for dead-plus-live loads and may be 
increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic 
forces. 
Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of footing excavations should 
be over-excavated until stiff/dense materials or to a maximum of 2 feet deep in the footing 
excavation. The over-excavation may be backfilled with select granular materials 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction in accordance with ASTM D1557. Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. 
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density. 
In general, the bottom of footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches 
below the lowest adjacent finished grade. Footings located adjacent to slopes or on slopes 
should be embedded to a sufficient depth to provide a minimum horizontal setback

soils around the footings should be well compacted (minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction). Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive pressure resistance in 
the upper 12 inches below the finished grade should be neglected. 
3.2 
Slabs-On-Grade 
We anticipate that the subsurface clayey and/or silty soil may exhibit a slight to 
moderate potential for shrinking and swelling when subjected to moisture fluctuations. 
To reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slab-on-grade resulting 
from shrinking and swelling of the near-surface clayey and/or silty soils, we recommend 
embedding the thickened-edge footings a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest 
adjacent grade.

We recommend properly preparing the slab-on-grade subgrades by scarifying to 
a depth of about 8 inches, moisture conditioning to at least 2 percent above the 
optimum moisture, and compacting to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade, we recommend 
thoroughly moistening the soils to close the cracks or preparing the subgrades again. 
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather 
and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with 
well-compacted fill. 
For interior building slab (not subjected to vehicular traffic or machinery 
vibration), we recommend placing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting 
of open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) below the slabs. The open-graded 
gravel cushion fill would provide uniform support of the slabs and would serve as a 
capillary moisture break. To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration through 
the slab and subsequent damage to floor coverings, an impervious moisture barrier is 
recommended on top of the gravel cushion fill layer. Flexible floor coverings, such as 
carpet or sheet vinyl, should be considered because they can better mask minor slab 
cracking. 
Where the slabs-on-grade will be subjected to equipment vibration and/or 
vehicular traffic, such as forklifts, we recommend placing the floor slab over 6 inches of 
aggregate subbase in lieu of the 4-inch thick cushion fill layer. The aggregate subbase 
should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction. Where slabs are intended to function as rigid pavements, a 
minimum slab thickness of 6 inches may be used for preliminary design purposes. 
Provisions should be made for proper load transfer across the slab joints that will be 
subject to vehicular traffic.  
Exterior flatwork required for the project should be underlain by a minimum 
6-inch thick layer of non-expansive, select granular material or aggregate subbase. The 
subgrade soils below the flatwork should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content, and 
recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. The non-expansive,

Solar Array Foundations

Lateral Load Resistance 
Lateral loads imposed on the pre-engineered steel frame structures may be 
resisted by a combination of the lateral load capacity of the drilled shafts and the 
passive pressure acting against the vertical faces of the pile caps. Passive earth 
pressure against the vertical faces of the pile caps may be estimated using an 
equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pcf. 
The lateral load resistance of drilled shafts is a function of the stiffness of the 
surrounding soil, the stiffness of the shafts, allowable deflection at the top of the 
shafts, and the induced moment in the shafts. The lateral load analyses were 
performed using the program LPILE 2013 for Windows, a microcomputer

Foundation Settlements 
Settlement of the drilled shaft foundation will result from elastic compression of 
the shaft and subgrade response of the foundation embedded in the subsurface 
soils. Total settlements of the drilled shafts are estimated to be on the order of 
less than 0.5 inches. Differential settlements between drilled shafts may be 
approximately half of the total settlements. We believe that a significant portion of 
the settlement is elastic and should occur as the loads are applied.

frictional resistance between the shaft and the surrounding soils. Therefore, proper 
construction techniques, especially during the drilling operations, are important. The 
contractor should exercise care in drilling the shaft hole and in placing concrete into 
the drilled hole. 
The subsurface materials generally consist of residual and saprolitic material, 
which may contain cobbles and boulders. Therefore, some difficult drilling 
conditions may be encountered and should be expected in these soils. In 
addition, although we did not encounter basalt formation in our borings, very hard 
drilling conditions into basalt formation may be encountered at the project site 
and should be expected. The drilled shaft contractor will need to have the 
appropriate equipment and tools to drill through the cobbles, boulders, and hard 
basalt formation. 
Based on our field exploration and the estimated length of the drilled shafts, 
groundwater is generally not expected in the drilled holes during the shaft 
installation work. Due to the relatively short length of the drilled shafts, concrete 
placement using the free fall method should be acceptable. In the event of 
seasonal rainfall and perched groundwater, water may be encountered in the 
drilled holes and concrete placement by tremie method would be required under 
such conditions.  
A low-shrink concrete mix with high slump (7 to 9-inch slump range) should be 
used to provide close contact between the drilled shaft and the surrounding soils. 
In addition, the concrete should be placed promptly after drilling (within 24 hours 
after drilling of the holes) to reduce the potential for softening of the side-walls of 
the drilled hole. 
It is imperative that a Geolabs representative is present at the project site to 
observe the drilling and installation of the drilled shafts during construction. 
Although the drilled shaft is designed based primarily on skin friction, the bottom 
of the drilled hole should be relatively free of loose materials prior to placement of

3.5.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared thoroughly. Surface vegetation and other unsuitable materials should be 
removed and disposed of properly off-site. Soft and yielding areas encountered 
during clearing below areas designated to receive fill and/or future improvements 
should be over-excavated to expose firm material and the resulting excavation 
should be backfilled with well-compacted fill. The excavated soft soils should be 
properly disposed of off-site and/or used in landscape areas, where appropriate. 
After clearing, the exposed subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 
8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture, 
and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. The compaction 
requirements for finished subgrades subjected to vehicular traffic, such as the 
pavement subgrades, should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed 
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage 
by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

In general, the excavated on-site soils should be suitable for use as general fill 
materials provided that the maximum particle size is less than 3 inches in largest 
dimension. The on-site materials generated from the excavations may be used as a 
source of general fill or backfill materials provided that they are screened of the 
over-sized materials and/or processed to meet the above gradation requirements 
(less than 3 inches in largest dimension). 
Imported materials required for site filling should consist of select granular fill 
material, such as crushed coraline and/or basaltic materials. The materials should 
be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no greater than 3 inches in largest 
dimension. In addition, the materials also should contain between 10 and 
30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The materials should have a CBR 
value of 20 or higher, and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested in 
accordance with ASTM D1883. Geolabs should test imported fill materials a 
minimum of seven days prior to being transported to the project site for the 
intended use.

3.5.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Fill materials consisting of the on-site soils should be moisture-conditioned to at 
least 2 percent above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 
8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed 
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage 
by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Imported fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content, placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 or 95 percent relative compaction, as appropriate. 
Aggregate base course and subbase materials should be moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in 
loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers or other types of 
acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or ponding should not 
be allowed to compact the fills.  Where compaction is less than required, additional 
compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture content, as 
necessary, to obtain the specified compaction.

contractor for the project should be cautioned that these hard cobble/boulders 
and/or basalt rock zones could be encountered in the excavations and may 
require chipping and/or the use of hoerams to excavate the materials.

3.8 
Corrosion Potential 
Design of metallic substructures, such as electrical conduits or other metallic 
piping, should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure.  
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
regarding the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the 
resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of 
metallic objects. 
A total of eight field resistivity surveys were conducted at a maximum of about 
20-foot intervals at two locations, designated as R-1 and R-2, using the in-situ Wenner 
SECTION 3. DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
W.O. 7415-00 
GEOLABS, INC. 
Page 23 
Hawaii • California 
4-Pin Method and a Nilsson Model 400 resistance meter manufactured by Nilsson 
Electrical Laboratory, Inc. The method utilizes four surface probes or pins (electrodes) 
to evaluate the average resistivity (an indication of corrosivity) of the underlying soil 
mass. The field resistivity survey was used to evaluate the average resistivity values of 
the soil mass at the approximate survey locations. Results of the field resistivity surveys 
are presented in Appendix C. It should be noted that the field resistivity survey may be 
influenced by temperature, weather conditions, soil moisture, existing underground 
utilities and other factors. 
Based on the results of our field resistivity survey, the subsurface soils at the 
project site have resistivity values ranging from about 8,026 to 15,358 ohm-cm, 
corresponding to corrosion ratings of 4 to 5, respectively. It should be noted that the 
resistivity values of the subsurface soils varied substantially with depth and location 
across the project area. Therefore, special attention should be given by the corrosion 
engineer when using the resistivity results provided in this report. 
One set of corrosivity testing, including Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and 
Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0) was performed by TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on a 
selected soil sample obtained from our field exploration. Results of the corrosivity tests 
are presented in Appendix D. 
Based on the relatively low value of the chloride and sulfate content tested on the 
in-situ clayey soil materials, we believe that either Type I or Type II (Type I/II) cement 
may be used for the concrete in contact with the ground planned. 
We envision that electrical ductlines will be installed underground. Geotherm 
USA, our subcontractor, conducted laboratory soil thermal resistivity testing to evaluate 
the thermal characteristics of the in-situ clayey soils encountered at the site. Detailed 
discussions and test results are provided in the reports entitled “Thermal Analysis of 
Native Soil, Lawai Solar Farm Development – Kauai, HI” dated December 1, 2016, as 
included in Appendix D.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
five boreholes, designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 105, extending to depths between 
about 16.5 and 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a

textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1.

in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials

Field Resistivity Survey 
Geophysical survey equipment (i.e. resistance meter) was mobilized to the project 
site on October 25, 2016 for the field resistivity survey.  Eight soil resistivity surveys were 
conducted at two locations, designated as R-1 and R-2, using the in-situ Wenner 4-Pin 
Method and a Nilsson Model 400 resistance meter manufactured by Nilsson Electrical 
Laboratory, Inc. The method utilizes four surface probes or pins (electrodes) to evaluate 
the average resistivity of the underlying soil mass. The average resistivity values of the soil 
mass, extending from the existing ground surface to the test depth, was assessed by 
using this method at the approximate survey locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The soil resistivity surveys were performed along the existing ground surface at the 
approximate survey locations shown on the site plan. The average soil resistance along 
the proposed alignment was evaluated at each survey location with the electrodes aligned 
at equal spacing of generally 2.5, 5, 10, and 20 feet. The depth of penetration of a 
resistivity sounding is a function of the electrode spacing. 
The resistance meter (Nilsson Model 400) provides a voltage between the outer 
electrodes, causing an electrical current to flow between them. The voltage drop between 
the inner electrodes is then measured by a sensitive, built-in voltmeter. As a result, the soil 
resistance (in ohms) is obtained from the voltmeter dial reading. The soil resistivity 
(in ohm-cm), which is directly related to both resistance and spacing, can then be 
computed. The evaluated soil resistances and computed resistivity values are presented 
on Plate C-1. In addition, the computed resistivity values with pin spacing at the test 
locations along each pipe line alignment are presented in the graphs on Plate C-2. At the 
time of resistivity surveying, the temperature was about 75º to 80º F. The existing ground 
surface was damp to moist. It should be noted that the soil resistivity may be influenced by 
temperature, weather conditions, existing underground utilities and other factors.
