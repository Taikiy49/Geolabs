PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located along the coastline adjacent to the Lihue Airport in the
Hanamaulu Ahukini area of Lihue on the Island of Kauai Hawaii The project site is a
former County landfill located easterly of the airport between Runway 17 and the cliffs
fronting the shoreline We understand that the former County landfill was closed in the
1970 s after having received municipal waste products since about the 1940 s By
executive order of the County the landfill site was terminated The site now resides
under the ownership of the State of Hawaii Department of Transportation The former
landfill is located on a flattened bluff top adjacent to some steep exposed coastal slopes
that extend down toward the sea The former landfill site is approximately 1 000 feet in
length and about 200 feet in width as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Over the years the easterly facing coastal slopes have reportedly experienced
surface erosion which has resulted in the exposure of the landfill materials along the
slopes comprising the eastern limit of the former landfill site The surface erosion has
resulted in the deposition of exposed landfill materials on the shoreline area at the base
of the slopes The landfill materials appear to consist mainly of household trash
construction debris and some automobile parts We understand that it is desired to

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single dissected basaltic shield volcano
built by the extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series beginning about
5 to 6 million years ago The eruption of the Waimea Volcanic Series ended about
2 million years ago and was followed by a long period of erosion Following the
cessation of this main volcano shield building phase about 1 million years ago there
was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of the post erosional
Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the alluvial sediments of the
Palikea Formation
Most of the near surface soils in the project area consist of residual and saprolitic
soils completely weathered rock derived from the in situ weathering of the Koloa
Volcanic Series igneous rocks In general the residual and saprolitic soils typically
grade to highly and moderately weathered basalt rock formation with increasing depth
Therefore basaltic rock boulders may be locally embedded in the deeply weathered
soils Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series are generally characterized as thick lava
flows of dense basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in
various locales Associated with the Koloa Volcanic Series lava flows are some
deposits of pyroclastic materials volcanic ash and cinders which usually form cinder
cones surrounding the vent Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the
eastern half of the Island of Kauai including the project site
During the Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean feil since there was less water available to
fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The landmass of Kauai remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These
giacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and
tower relative to the present sea level of the Island of Kauai

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The project site mainly encompasses relatively flat to gently sloping terrain and
some steep coastal hillslopes located between the existing Lihue Airport runway and the
ocean in the Hanamaulu Ahukini area of Lihue on the island of Kauai Hawaii The
former Lihue landfill footprint is approximately rectangular in shape measuring
approximately 960 lineal feet LF long in the north south direction and about 200 to
300 LF wide in the east west direction An existing dirt access road borders the western
side of the study area The ground surface elevations range between about 83 feet
Mean Sea Level MSL at the western edge of the site near the existing access road to
about 70 feet MSL along the top of the coastal bluff above the ocean A steep hilislope
leading down toward the shoreline exists along the eastern boundary of the project site
The hillslope slopes down toward the east at variable inclinations generally ranging
between about one and a half horizontal to one vertical on the upper flatter
portion of the slope to about 1H 4V on the lower steeper portion of the slope The Site
Plan Plate 2 depicts the existing biuff top topography up to about the top edge of the
steep coastal slope The Site Plan also depicts the approximate mapped limits of the
former landfill based on our field exploration and our review of the available historical
aerial photographs of the site
The ground surfaces on top of the bluff generally consist of stiff reddish brown
clayey and silty soils that are believed to represent the landfill capping soil cover in the
disturbed areas of the project site and the native residual soils in the undisturbed areas
of the project site The exposed ground surfaces were observed to be generally dry and
firm although some topographic depressions and soil mounds were locally observed
Most of the ground surfaces were observed covered with vegetation consisting of low
shrubbery and grasses Some dirt vehicle trails were observed traversing the site as
the adjacent shoreline area is open to the public for recreational use Only limited
landfill debris was observed exposed at the ground surface on the bluff top However
significant quantities of landfill debris were observed exposed on the ground surfaces in
the vicinity of the top edge of the coastal bluff and on the hillslope leading down to the
shoreline The visible debris appeared to consist of glass plastics concrete debris and
some larger metallic debris were believed to be automobile parts
Based on our observations some surface erosional features gullies and earth
slumps were observed located along the top edge of the bluff and on the steep coastal
hillslopes The erosional features generally consist of shallow incised gullies which
indicate that surface storm water runoff may have flowed over the sloping ground
surfaces Other erosional features appear to represent old landslide scars and surface
breakouts of landfill waste An elongated earth berm composed of loose mounds of dirt
was observed traversing north to south across the middle potion of the former landfill
site Another loose soil berm and drainage diversion ditch was encountered along the
top of the coastal hillslope The berm and ditch features appear to have been
constructed in an effort to intercept surface water runoff for erosion protection

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by the excavation and
backfilling of twenty three 23 test pit excavations at selected locations across the former
landfill site The test pits are identified as Test Pit Nos 1 through 23 The locations of the
test pits were selected based on the available interpretive maps and aerial photographs of
the project site depicting the possible limits of the landfill The purpose for the test pits was
to explore the depth and composition of buried landfill waste across the site The
approximate locations of the test pits are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In addition four test borings were drilled at selected locations located easterly of
the test pits along the top of the coastal hillslope The borings are identified as
Boring Nos 1 through 4 as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The purpose for the borings
was to obtain additional subsurface information closer to the top edge of the coastal slope
in an effort to locate the underlying native soils and basalt rock formation which is
believed to underlie the landfill materials The borings were advanced with 6 inch
diameter hollow stem augers to depths ranging from about 25 to 35 feet below the existing
ground surface
The test pits were excavated with a Komatsu backhoe excavator capable of
reaching the maximum trench depths of about 16 to 17 feet below the ground surface
The test pits were logged by a field engineer from our office to record a general description
of the materials encountered and the depth where native residual saprolitic soils were
encountered in the pits
It was anticipated that brackish or saline groundwater conditions could be
encountered at a depth of about 70 to 80 feet below the ground surface Therefore it was
desired to install a groundwater monitoring well at the site A suitable location was
selected within the footprint of a backfilled test pit in an effort to reduce the possibility of
encountering landfill waste which could potentially retard auger penetration However
several attempts were made at various locations to auger the test boring for the installation
of the monitoring well The test boring attempts repeatedly met refusal on hard basalt
boulders or basalt rock formation at depths ranging between about 20 to 30 feet below the
ground surface in the vicinity of Test Pit No 8 Therefore the monitoring well installation
was aborted due to the hard subsurface conditions which require rock coring or air
percussion hammer drilling methods Based on the test boring attempts the top of the
native soil materials appears to have been encountered at a depth of about 18 feet below
the existing ground surface in the vicinity of the Test Pit No 8 location
In general our test pit field exploration encountered approximately 2 to 6 feet of stiff
clayey capping fill soils overlying the buried landfill waste Some test pits encountered only
limited capping soil cover of less than about 1 foot thickness Test Pit 15 and
17 Based on the test pit exploration highly variable loose and soft to medium stiff landfill
wastes were encountered extending to depths of about 5 feet to greater than about 16 feet
below the existing ground surface Test Pit 12 13 and 17 did not encounter
native residual soils below the landfill waste due to the 16 foot limitation of the backhoe

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our review of the geophysical exploration report prepared by others for
the project we concur with the findings pertaining to the general location of the higher
conductivity levels associated with potential buried metallic wastes however the thickness
of the landfill waste may be underestimated in some areas especially along the eastern
boundary of the site where deeper landfill waste profiles extending to depths of about
18 to 24 feet were encountered by the borings located near the crest of the coastal slope
Furthermore our observations of the exposed coastal slopes indicate that in situ landfill
deposits extend to the bottom of the hillslope located at the shoreline elevation Thus it is
believed that the landfill wastes represent a thickened wedge of fill material which overlies
a pre existing native coastal hillslope composed of saprolitic soils and weathered basalt
rock materials Based on our observation of the surrounding similar natural coastal
hillslopes located toward the south of the project site it is estimated that the native

Based on our preliminary evaluation of the desired remedial slopé stabilization
with respect to the surface and subsurface conditions encountered we believe that a
potentially viable remedial stabilization solution may consist of the removal of the landfill
waste along the coastal hillslope to expose the underlying stable ground encompassed
by the native geologic materials Furthermore the remaining 15 to 25 foot thick top
portion of landfill waste on the bluff may either be flattened to a stabilized inclination of
about 3H 1V or a buttress fill may be constructed along the top of the slope to stabilize
the remaining landfill waste
Other alternatives such as the hardening of the shoreline construction of slope
retaining structures or installation of surface slope coverings on the existing slopes may
not offer cost effective long term performance suitable for this project Following is a
brief discussion of the conceptual remedial alternative proposed for this project It
should be noted that the recommended remedial improvements do not encompass
landfill closure requirements or other regulatory requirements
Removal of Landfill Waste
We envision that the thickened wedge of landfill waste on the coastal slope could
be excavated to expose the underlying native coastal hillslope believed to be composed
of saprolitic soils and weathered basalt rock materials The excavation of the landfill
materials using heavy construction equipment may be difficult due to the potential
instability of the landfill deposits on the steep coastal terrain
By removing the unstable landfill deposits the shoreline slope could be stabilized
and the potential risk of a large slope failure depositing landfill debris on the shoreline and
in the coastal waters is significantly reduced
Slope Flattening Alternative
Once the landfill wastes have been removed from the coastal hillslope it is
envisioned that a lower cost remedial alternative to stabilize the remaining landfill
deposits would be to develop a slope bench founded on the native residual soils
underlying the landfill waste From the new bench elevation assumed to be typically
about 20 to 25 feet below the top of the bluff the existing landfill embankment could be
cut back to a flatter inclination of about 3H 1V and the new slope surface could be
capped with impervious clayey soils The flattened slope with a clay soil capping layer
should increase the resistance against potential sliding and surface erosion of the
landfill slope A slope stability analysis should be performed to evaluate the factor of
safety against sliding based on the anticipated highly variable landfill soil properties

Due to the sloping conditions and softer landfill materials below compaction of
the capping fill may be limited to about 85 percent An erosion mat may be considered
over the sloping capping fill to resist erosion and promote vegetation A conceptual
drawing of the slope flattening alternative is provided on Plate 4 for reference
Slope Buttress Alternative
As an alternative to flattening the remaining landfill waste slope it is envisioned
that a fill buttress could be constructed along the edge of the landfill slope This
remedial alternative is considered to be a more desirable solution that should greatly
increase the stability of the remaining landfill waste slope and prevent future surface
erosion from uncovering the buried waste
The remedial stabilization alternative would consist of excavating the landfill
waste to construct a buttress founded on the native soils The buttress would be
constructed in horizontal lifts to achieve the desired compaction of the buttress fill The
alternative is anticipated to involve the excavation of a significant quantity of landfill
waste to construct the buttress A conceptual drawing of the slope buttress alternative
is provided on Plate 5 for reference
Other Considerations
Other drainage improvements such as a lined surface interceptor ditch could be
constructed to divert surface water away from the top of the slope face Additionally
new fills could be placed to mound the entire landfill footprint to reduce the potential for
ponding of surface water thus reducing potential leachate generation
We understand that the ultimate remedial slope stabilization solution is subject to
regulatory review and other considerations and therefore additional subsurface
exploration may be needed to obtain required soil material parameters to be used in a
slope stability analysis to justify the recommended stabilization improvements
A detailed topographic survey of the existing coastal slope condition should be
performed to provide topographic information in support of more detailed analysis of the
conceptual alternatives presented in this report
