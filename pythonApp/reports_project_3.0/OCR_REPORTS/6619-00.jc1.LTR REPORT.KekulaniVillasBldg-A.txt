PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
We understand that settlement was noticed at the corner of the Building “A” of the 
Kekuilani Villas and reported in early 2010. The settlement caused some structural 
distress in the corner units of A101 (down-stairs) and A105 (up-stairs), especially on the 
lanai slab. A structural engineer was engaged for a structural assessment and 
recommended installing a micropile foundation system to support the settling slab. 
It is desired to evaluate the settlement and determine effective repairs. Based on 
the information provided, we understand that the existing building is a wood-framed 
townhouse structure supported on a concrete slab-on-grade foundation system. The 
building was constructed in early 1990’s. 
It appears that the lanai slab may be supported on the thickened-edge footing, 
independent from the building slab-on-grade foundation. An up-stairs lanai corner post is 
supported directly on the down-stairs lanai slab. Gaps up to 1.5 inches wide were 
observed between the lanai slab and the building perimeter footing.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island.  The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age and forms the 
majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  Waianae became extinct while Koolau 
was still active; thus, Waianae’s eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in the 
central Oahu region. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation.  As the worldwide glaciers grew and advanced, less water 
was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands 
of the sea.  When the glaciers melted and receded, a larger volume of water became 
available such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level. Thus, the island 
shorelines have transgressed and regressed in response to the fluctuations.  The 
processes of terrestrial erosion and the associated depositional environments were 
affected by these glacio-eustatic sea level fluctuations.  When the sea level was low, the 
erosional base level was correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths 
below the present sea level.  As a result, terrestrial sediments were carried further 
seaward and deposited in what had been marine environments.  When the sea level 
was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments accumulated at 
higher elevations.  In addition, it allowed for the deposition of marine deposits such as 
coral and limestone in environments that had been covered with terrestrial soils and 
rock. 
The project site is situated on the Ewa Plain to the southeast of the Waianae 
Mountains. The Ewa Plain is a gently sloping alluvial plain formed by the deposition of 
alluvial clays and silts derived from the weathering of basalt rock further up-slope. The 
alluvial deposits accumulated and the materials interbedded with marine sediments and 
coral/algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge. The thickness of the 
sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H-1 Highway to 
over 1,000 feet at Ewa Beach. This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the 
confining formation, or “caprock,” over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu. 
Basalt rock formation resides below the alluvial and marine deposits. 
The project site is situated over the alluvium and coral reef formation overlain by 
the surface fill. The surface fills are present due to subdivision development in the area.

FIELD EXPLORATION 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
one boring, designated as Boring No. 1. The boring extended to about 26 feet below the 
existing ground surface using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous-flight 
solid-stem augers. The boring location is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by 
testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D 2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1 of Appendix A. 
Graphic representations of the materials encountered and other observations, such as 
groundwater levels (if encountered), are presented on the Log of Boring, Plate A-1 of 
Appendix A. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop.  The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on 
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
LABORATORY TESTS 
Samples recovered from our field exploration were visually examined and 
classified in our Honolulu laboratory. Selected soil samples were tested to evaluate the 
physical characteristics and engineering properties. The laboratory testing program was 
formulated with the emphasis on evaluating the moisture, density, and expansion 
potential of the soils encountered. 
Moisture Content and Unit Weight tests were performed on selected soil samples to 
aid in defining the interface between the various soil layers encountered. The moisture 
content tests were performed in general accordance with ASTM D 2216. The unit weight 
tests were performed in general accordance with ASTM D 2937. The test results are 
presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths in Appendix A.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling one boring 
extending to a depth of approximately 26 feet below the existing ground surface. The 
approximate boring location is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, we encountered about 16 feet of fills and about 9 feet of alluvium within 
the boring depth. The fill consisted of medium stiff to very stiff clayey soil. The alluvium 
was comprised of soft to stiff clay. Groundwater was not encountered within the drilled 
depth. It should be noted that groundwater levels are expected to fluctuate with variation in 
rainfall, surface water runoff, groundwater withdrawal, and other factors. 
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered from our field exploration are 
presented on the Log of Boring, Plate A-1 of Appendix A. 
Floor level survey was conducted in three units (A101, A105 and A106) to 
determine the interior slab settling pattern. It should be noted that A101 (1st floor) and 
A105 (2nd floor) were adjacent to the settled lanai. A106 was next to A105, further away 
from the settling lanai. 
Differential settlement up to about 4.5 inches was measured in the lanai area of 
A105 and about 3 inches in the lanai area of A101, as shown on the Level Survey Plans, 
Plates 3.1 and 3.2. Nominal differential settlement (less than 1 inch) was measured in 
other areas of A101 and A105, as well as A106 (as shown on Plate 3.3). It should be 
noted that the carpet was present in the interior of these three units. Therefore, the 
differential interior settlement up to about 1 inch is considered to be measurement error 
and should be ignored. 
In addition, CCTV inspection was conducted in the existing 24-inch reinforced 
concrete pipe (RCP) drain line, adjacent to Building “A”. There was a concern that poor 
condition of the existing RCP drain line may have an influence on the lanai settlement. 
The inspection was performed through a catch basin located at the edge of the driveway 
adjacent to the dumpster. The CCTV camera recorded via remote control device, walking 
along the drain line until a 5-foot by 5-foot metal grate inlet, and terminated at Kekuilani 
Loop, a total of about 150 feet distance. 
The CCTV indicates that the existing 24-inch RCP drain line is currently in sound 
condition with minor maintenance deficiency caused by debris encountered during the 
inspection. Based on the results of the CCTV inspection, it appears that the lanai 
settlement observed at the corner of the Building “A” may be not related to the existing

DISCUSSION OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the results of the field exploration, we believe that the near-surface 
clayey soil under the existing lanai may have been softened by the run-off water from the 
roof gutter and surface runoff. The softened clayey soil appears to be the cause of the 
settlement. 
Based on the results of the floor level survey and our observation, it appeared that 
the settlement was isolated to the lanai area only. This is consistent with the finding that 
the lanai slab was not structurally connected to the building thicken-edge footing. The wall 
cracks may be caused by the dragging movement from the lanai, where it was adjacent to 
the building walls. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the boring and limited 
construction access, we recommend using a deep foundation system consisting of 
micropiles for support of the existing lanai structural loads. The lanai slab may be 
re-leveled after the micropile installation and structurally connected to the existing building 
thicken-edge footing. In addition, the run-off water from the roof gutter should be diverted 
away from the slab footing. 
The capacity of the micropile foundation should be derived primarily from skin 
friction between the micropile shaft and the stiff clayey soils encountered in the borings. 
We recommend installing the micropiles at each corner of the lanai. 
A micropile consists of a small diameter (usually less than 12 inches) drilled and 
grouted pile with steel reinforcing.  The micropile foundation typically is constructed by 
drilling a borehole, placing reinforcing steel in the hole, and grouting the borehole. 
Micropiles are desirable because they can be installed readily in access restrictive 
environments and in numerous soil types and ground conditions.  In addition, installation of 
the micropiles generally causes minimal disturbance to adjacent structures, the adjacent 
soils, and the environment. 
Based on the availability of local material inventory, we envision that a micropile 
system with a minimum grout bulb diameter of 5.5 inches may be used.  We recommend 
designing each micropile based on an allowable compressive load capacity of 20 kips for 
5.5-inch bulb diameter.  The allowable compressive load capacity for the piles is for 
dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as 
wind or seismic forces.  Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we 
recommend extending the micropiles to 20 feet below the bottom of the lanai slab. 
To facilitate the micropile drilling and ensure the quality of the grouting, we 
recommend advancing steel casing to the bottom of the micropile during the drilling

It should be noted that the bond stress between the grout bulb and the soil is highly 
dependent on the drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed by the 
contractor to install the micropile.  Therefore, the bond stress between the grout bulb and 
the soil may vary considerably between different contractors and micropile foundation 
systems. 
A specialty contractor experienced in the construction of a micropile foundation 
system (minimum five projects) using casing method should install the micropiles. Due to 
the specialized nature of the micropile foundation construction, observation of the 
micropile foundation system should be designated a “Special Inspection” item.  Therefore, 
a Geolabs representative (Special Inspector) should be present to observe the 
geotechnical aspects of the micropile foundation construction. 
It should be noted that the micropile foundation system provides low lateral load 
resistance due to the relatively small diameter of the micropile.  Therefore, the lateral load 
resistance contributed by the micropiles should be neglected. 
As an alternative, Helical Pier may be installed in lieu of the micropile at each 
corner of the lanai. The Helical Pier consists of a solid shaft (1.5 inches in square) with 
typical double helix (8 inches diameter) near tip of the shaft. The Helical Pier system is an 
axially loaded end-bearing deep foundation. The pile can be installed without a predrilled 
hole and no drilling spoils. The installation of the Helical Pier may be terminated by 
measuring a rotational torque as resistance for computing the pile capacity. 
We envision that a minimum of 4,000 foot-pound rotational torque or a minimum of 
25-foot depth may be required, for an allowable compressive load capacity of 20 kips with 
a factor of safety of 2.0. If the actual size of the Helical Pier is different from the above, 
Geolabs should be consulted for the minimum rotational torque requirements.

LIMITATIONS

upon information obtained from our review of the available information and our field boring 
conducted for the building settlement evaluation project. Variations of subsurface
