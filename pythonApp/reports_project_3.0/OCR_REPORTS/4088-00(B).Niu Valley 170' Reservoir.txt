INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering reconnaissance
performed for the slope evaluation and stabilization project at the Niu Valley
170 Reservoir site in Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1

This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field reconnaissance and engineering analyses
These recommendations are intended to assist in the review and selection of possible
remedial measures for erosion control and slope stabilization The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Niu Valley 170 Reservoir project site is located within Board of Water Supply
BWS property in Niu Valley on the southeastern portion of the island of Oahu Hawaii
The site may be accessed via Mahimahi Street in Niu Valley The site is bounded to the
west by the residential subdivision along Panio Street and to the east by Kuliouou ridge
where the water supply reservoir is located
The subject hillslope erosion is occurring on the western portion of the
BWS property bordering the residences on the eastern side of Panio Street The
backyards of three residential properties abut the BWS property An existing chain link
fence separates the BWS property from the residential properties
A history of soil and rock erosion occurring along the existing chain link fence has
been documented The ground surface erosion has resulted in deposits of loose soil and
rock accumulating within the bordering private properties On a few occasions small
boulders have come loose from the hillslope and rolled into the fence and backyard
areas As a result of the ongoing erosion and expressed concern from the neighboring
residents it is desired to evaluate the existing slope conditions and provide the BWS with
remedial alternatives to reduce the effects of slope erosion

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt lava from two separate
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger volcano Koolau is estimated to be late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity and deep
valley erosion following the eruption of Waianae and Koolau basalt volcanic activity
GEOLABS HAWAII
4088 00 B Page 4
returned to the island of Oahu as a series of lava flows followed by cinder and ash
eruptions These most recent volcanic eruptions are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic
Series For the past 1 2 million years weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred
creating the valley and ridge topography visible today on the southern flank of the Koolau
mountain range
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of the Koolau mountain range
and is generally underlain by basalt rock formation built by the extrusion of lavas of the
Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized as layered flows of
fractured vesicular basalt with interbedded clinker layers Mantling the rock formation is a
relatively thin layer of soil and rock fragments represented by eroded and transported
material generated from the weathering of the basalt rock The soil and rock fragments
are known geologically as alluvial and colluvial deposits
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
mass wasting which is essentially the detachment of soil and rock masses from valley
wails or hillsides and transportation downslope primarily by gravity to be deposited as
colluvium Once these colluvial materials become affected by streamflow alluvial
processes become dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as
alluvium
The project site is located on the western lower hillside flank of the ridgeline
separating Niu Valley from the adjacent Kuliouou Valley located to the east The ridgeline
extends northerly to merge with the backbone of the Koolau mountain range As a result
of the project site located at the base of the ridgeline the ground surface is composed of
colluvial deposits overlying basalt rock formation at variable depths in the subsurface It
is these surficial colluvial deposits that are subject to erosion when exposed at the ground
surface or existing as exposed over steepened scarp faces

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
An existing chain link fence of approximately 190 feet in length traverses the
slope at the property boundary between BWS land and the two private residences
Fong and Hatanaka properties In general the fence traverses slightly undulating
terrain of about equal elevation with a slight rise toward the northern end The fence is
located about 2 feet upslope of the surveyed BWS property boundary At the time of
our site visit a string line had been set to define the approximate location of the
property line between surveyed corner stakes A photograph of the existing fence line
and slope condition is provided on Plate 5 of this report
The existing chain link fence has been modified in an attempt to reduce the
passage of loose soil and stones under the fence The modification essentially consists
of a second layer of chain link mesh attached to the lower portion of the fence and
ground surface located upslope of the fence line A photograph of the fence
modification is included as Plate 6 of this report
The additional chain link layer appears to reduce some erosion however fine
soil and stones smaller than 2 inches in diameter continue to pass through the chain
link mesh The chain link reinforcing mesh does not extend into the subsurface below
the fence line therefore soil and stones also continue to pass beneath the fence
Hillslope
In general the subject hillslope stands at an average of about one horizontal to
one vertical 1H 1V extending from about 10 feet easterly of the chain link fence to the
paved access road located midway toward the ridgetop Included in this slope segment
are sporadic outcroppings of layered basalt rock generally less than about 10 to 15 feet
in height and standing near vertical The hillslope at the fenceline and extending
downslope in the private backyard areas generally exists at about 2H 1V Several level
terraces separated by low rock walls have been constructed in the backyard of the
Fong residence

It should be noted that some eroded vertical scarps of about 2 to 4 feet in vertical
height have developed immediately downslope of the fence but generally within the
BWS property These scarp exposures were observed contributing to the loose soil
and cobble deposition observed within the Fong property The scarp exposures do not
extend across the entire property boundary In general the scarps exist across a
lateral distance of about 30 to 40 feet at the central portion of the Fong BWS property
boundary The scarps were observed to expose a brown conglomerate of cobbles and
gravel in a matrix of sandy clay representing weakly cemented older colluvial deposits
A photograph of a scarp exposure is included as Plate 7 of this report
In general the backyard of the Hatanaka property exists at about 2H 1V as a
relatively continuous uniform slope extending down to intersect a concrete swale
located between the house and the backyard at the bottom of the slope The slope is
planted and is generally uniform in gradient with no slope scarps or irregular features
visible
Soil Conditions
Based on our observation of the ground surface the following is a generalized
description of the near surface soil rock conditions at the site The soil profile appears
to consist of a surface layer of dark brown silty clay with loose gravel and cobbles
representing topsoil colluvial deposits This layer appears to extend to depths of less
than about 2 feet below ground level Below this layer lies a brown weakly cemented
older colluvial deposit of gravel and cobbles supported in a clayey sand matrix
extending to unknown depth The older colluvial deposits appear stiff medium dense
but may be broken apart by a pick hammer with only slight resistance
Rock formation was not observed at the ground surface along the fence line
except for a localized outcrop situated along the Fong BWS property Other ground
surface rock outcrops may exist hidden by vegetation along the fence line particularly
along the Hatanaka BWS boundary The depth to rock in the subsurface is not known
GEOLABS HAWAII
4088 00 B Page 7
but is anticipated to be relatively shallow on the order of less than 10 feet below ground
level
Erosion Problem
It is our understanding the erosion problem has been occurring for some time
and has not changed in magnitude except for periodic greater influx of eroded soil and
stones during wet weather The soil erosion and resultant deposits appear to be
generated from two sources
1 loose surface soil and stones passing through and beneath the chain link
fence generated from areas upslope of the fence and
2 erosion of exposed scarps consisting of older colluvial deposits just below
the fence line and within BWS property
The soil erosion does not appear to significantly affect the private properties and
may be considered more of a nuisance event requiring periodic clearing of garden
walkways of the loose stones and soil It was reported by the Hatanaka s that over a
period of about 25 years only one boulder had dislodged from the upper hillslope and
rolled into the fence causing some damage to the fence The Fong s have reported that
on occasion a boulder has rolled into their backyard garden apparently as a result of
previously mentioned scarp erosion or erosion occurring along the bottom of the fence
allowing larger rocks to pass beneath In general rocks greater than about 12 to
18 inches in diameter were not observed downslope of the fence line within BWS
property Therefore hazard associated with potential rock slide is considered to be of
low risk Based on the property owner s comments and our observation of the rock
outcroppings on the hillslope significant rock fall hazards generated from upslope
areas do not appear to exist at this time However the soil erosion problem will
continue to occur if slope conditions remain as they are without some remedial
intervention

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The surface soil erosion observed at the site is part of ongoing natural geomorphic
weathering and depositional processes acting on the hillslope Should the erosional
products be undesirable remedial measures may be taken to reduce the effects of the
natural hillslope erosion processes The remedial measures include the installation of
various barrier structures to catch eroded soil and rocks generated from upslope areas
In addition the remedial measure selected should address the erosion prone slope
scarps located along the property boundary
The current erosion of the exposed scarp surfaces appear to be the result of a
previous slope alteration that created the scarps In order to reduce the weathering and
erosion from the scarp faces some slope grading work should be accomplished to
achieve a more uniform slope gradient devoid of the irregular scarps that are prone to
deterioration Plate Nos 2 and 3 provide generalized sketches of the existing slope
condition and recommended slope grading
It is our opinion that the soil erosion and deposition processes could be reduced by
implementing some limited slope grading and installation of a barrier structure along the
property line

presented for consideration Each of the alternatives support advantages and

Concrete or Rock Wall Barrier
This method may be viewed as the best solution in terms of long term
performance and reliability in the success of reducing the influx of soil rock deposits
from upslope areas This method would include the removal of the existing fence and
grading of a portion of the slope along the property boundary to remove the existing
slope scarps and other irregularities to provide for a uniform compacted slope surface
The wall may be designed for about 6 feet in height with about 3 feet embedded in the
subsurface and about 3 feet of exposure above ground level A chain link fence could
be installed on the wall surface to provide for security and serve as additional
catchment for potential rock fall of boulders Drainage of stormwater should be
provided by adequate weepholes in the wall to allow passage of water
The disadvantages include the relatively high cost and difficult access for
construction equipment A temporary trail may be necessary to provide for access of
excavation and concrete delivery equipment Should an access trail be necessary the
temporary cut trail should be restored to the natural slope gradient and compacted
upon completion Due to the slightly undulating topography along the property line a
wall of variable height may be necessary to achieve a uniform top of wall elevation
Also the construction of a wall across the undulating topography would require
additional consideration for site drainage
New Chain Link Fence Combined with Rock Wall and Slope Grading
This method is a lower cost alternative to wall construction across the entire
property boundary and should reduce most of the heavy equipment access concern
This alternative would involve the removal of the existing fence and grading of the
immediate area surrounding the property boundary to provide for a uniform and
compacted slope surface devoid of slope scarps and including the provision of a level
bench for the new fence installation and maintenance
GEOLABS HAWAITI

This alternative would consist of the construction of a new reinforced chain link
fence combined with a limited length approximately 45 lineal feet of rock wall to
support the existing relatively short length segment of slope scarp located at the BWS
and Fong property boundary
A new chain link fence should be installed along the property line similar to the
existing design but with deeper footings and more closely spaced support poles
Additionally the fence design should include provision for the installation of an
additional layer of galvanized wire cloth mesh on the lower up slope fence face The
wire cloth mesh also known as hardware cloth should consist of a maximum mesh
size of inch square The mesh is available in 100 foot length rolls with widths ranging
from 2 to 4 feet Wire mesh is considered to be the most appropriate material for use in
comparison to other mesh types such as plastic or synthetic fabric materials that may
not withstand the prolonged exposure to sunlight and impact of debris containment
We recommend the use of a minimum 4 foot width to allow for a minimum of about
2 to 3 feet of above ground fence coverage and a minimum of about 1 to 2 feet of
embedment at the ground surface
The wire cloth mesh would act as a sieve for retention of loose gravel and stones
that would otherwise pass through the chain link mesh The wire cloth mesh should be
attached to the chain link fence using additional horizontal pipe rails for securing the
mesh to the fence In addition vertically oriented pipe rails placed midway between the
chain link fence support posts should be utilized to provide for firmer support of the wire
cloth mesh Stormwater would be allowed to pass through the fence and wire mesh
materials A sketch of the chain link fence with wire mesh reinforcing as described is
provided as Plate 4 1 of this report
A system of lateral braces should be included in the design to resist forces from
the potential accumulation of debris along the fence during extreme rainfall events as
well as protect against potential boulder impact The braces would be similar to those

installed along the segment of fence existing on the southern side of the reservoir
entrance driveway It should be noted that excavation for the lateral brace foundations
could encounter rock formation in the shallow subsurface
The 45 lineal feet of rock wall may be constructed to retain the existing relatively
short length segment of slope scarp at the BWS and Fong property boundary The wall
should be constructed to retain the approximately 4 feet high existing slope scarp
Weepholes should be provided to drain stormwater runoff from behind the wall A
schematic sketch of the proposed wall structure is provided as Plate 4 2 of this report
Although this alternative has many advantages in terms of ease of construction
and cost it would require some periodic annual inspection for signs of wear and removal
of accumulated debris from the fence line following the rainy season Furthermore the
wire mesh may require replacement in the event the 19 gauge galvanized wire develops
rust corrosion however we believe the wire mesh would need the least maintenance
effort and exceed the lifespan of other available geotextile fabrics used for erosion
control
Based on our review of the available geotextile fabrics used for erosion control of
finer soil materials such as sand and clay the available geotextile fabrics do not have a
long term life expectancy due to degradation effects from ultraviolet rays The fabric
tends to weaken in a relatively short period of time and becomes prone to tears and
perforations by impacting material Therefore we have included the use of woven
geotextile filter fabric as an optional consideration because of the relatively high
maintenance and frequent replacement requirement of the filter fabric material We
estimate that the filter fabric may require replacement annually and the effort to remove
and install new fabric with the required subsurface embedment would be costly

However should the passage of fine soil materials through the fence be deemed
unacceptable a layer of erosion control silt fence material such as woven geotextile
fabric could be attached and embedded below ground on the down slope lower face of
the fence The fabric could be attached to the chain link fence by vertically weaving a wire
thread through the fabric and chain link mesh The fabric should be securely embedded
below ground to reduce the potential for the fabric to move and allow soil to pass
beneath In this way the course granular materials would be collected by the wire mesh
and chain link fence and the finer soils would be intercepted by the geotextile fabric The
risk of perforation of the fabric by granular materials would be lessened due to the
collection of rock material by the wire mesh and chain link mesh It must be stressed that
if it is desired to utilize the geotextile fabric replacement of the fabric will likely be required
on an annual basis It is our opinion that the optional woven geotextile fabric may not be
necessarly

Grading of Slope
This method would involve the cutting filling and compaction of portions of the
slope along the existing fence line to remove the erosion prone scarps and irregularities
to provide for a more uniform slope surface In this way surface runoff would flow over
a smoother surface without enhancing the erosion and continued uphill migration of the
scarps In addition the slope could be swept of loose surface deposits such as gravel
GEOLABS HAWAIl
4088 00 B Page 15
cobbles and boulders within the immediate area of the fence and extending about
20 feet upslope
The disadvantages include the requirement that the existing fence be replaced
due to the grade modification that would likely undermine the existing fence posts and
chain link mesh currently acting as erosion containment A slope sweep to clear loose
deposits would only serve as a temporary measure allowing additional debris to
accumulate over time Furthermore debris and runoff emanating from upslope areas
would continue to have the potential to pass through the chain link barrier and deposit
within the private property
No Action
This alternative consists of leaving the slope and existing fence in an as is
condition By allowing the surface erosion to continue it appears that the existing fence
would be at risk of collapse due to undermining of the fence posts and the erosional
retreat of the existing scarps leading to eventual undercutting of the fence line
Furthermore soil and rock deposits would continue to pass through the fence with the
erosion eventually allowing larger cobbles and boulders to roll beneath the increasing gap
at the fence bottom
Preferred Alternative
From a geotechnical engineering viewpoint to provide for long term relatively low
maintenance erosion control the primary preferred alternative would be the construction
of a concrete wall or rock wall in combination with some slope grading work to alleviate
the scarp conditions Rock wall construction would utilize less concrete than a concrete
wall therefore reducing the need for concrete truck access or concrete pumping to the
site
GEOLABS HAWAII
4088 00 B Page 16
The secondary preferred alternative would be to install a new reinforced chain link
fence with wire cloth mesh combined with a limited length approximately 45 lineal feet of
wall to support the slope scarp area at the BWS and Fong property boundary The slope
grading would be limited to the removal of localized scarp conditions and the provision for
a level pad to facilitate the fence and wall installation and future maintenance
The tertiary preferred alternative from a geotechnical engineering viewpoint would
be to install a new reinforced chain link fence with wire cloth mesh and some slope
grading to provide for a level pad to facilitate the construction of the chain link fence and
provide for future maintenance access
The ultimate selection should balance the long term project needs construction
cost maintenance and liability issues The primary preferred alternative will have lower
maintenance needs but higher construction costs especially if rock formation is
encountered in the wall footing excavations
Additionally it may be prudent to implement the chain link fence alternative and
defer the additional installation of geotextile filter fabric until soil erosion conditions are
observed that warrant the use
