Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration and
engineering analyses performed for the proposed Old Kalanianaole Road Culvert
Replacement Project in Kailua on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project location and
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations based
on our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses performed The
recommendations presented herein are intended for design of foundations retaining
structures and site grading only The findings and recommendations presented herein
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

Project Considerations
The Old Kalanianaole Road Culvert Replacement project site is located along
Old Kalanianaole Road to the southwest of Kalanianaole Highway in Kailua on the
Island of Oahu Hawaii There is an existing pipe culvert crossing Old Kalanianaole
Road draining from southwest to northeast
The project consists of replacing or upgrading the existing culvert and providing
new headwall structures

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be
late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during
which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These
series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The caldera of the Koolau Volcano extends from Kaneohe to Waimanalo and
from the Koolau Pali to seaward beyond Lanikai The project site is within the Koolau
caldera complex The original rocks in the complex generally consisted of nearly level
bedded caldera filling basalts Much of the rock was intruded by dikes during volcanic
activity and portions were chemically altered by hydrothermal activity within the caldera
complex
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of
the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available
such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these

dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the
stream flows transmit large volumes of water for very short durations Because of this
the transport of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load
consists of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When
the erosional base levels change these sediment loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes
begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the
material Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the
coarse grained portion of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or
conglomerate Simultaneously erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors
and upstream in headwaters This continued erosion generates material that is
transported downslope covering the older alluvial deposits
Depending on the local base level and rate of transport these newer sediments
are generally transient in terms of geologic time In addition their consistency and
density are generally less than those of the older partially consolidated deposits The
deposits of low permeability soil horizons at the base of the Koolau Range have created
a confined aquifer condition At the Kaneohe area these confined aquifers are supplied
by groundwater from dikes within the Koolau Range Since water within a confined
aquifer is under pressure boreholes that penetrate these aquifers will encounter a
piezometric or artesian head of groundwater

Subsurface Conditions
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling two borings
Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths of approximately 25 1 and 25 3 feet below the
existing ground surface Boring No 1 was drilled on the downstream side of the existing
drainage structure on the road pavement Boring No 2 was drilled on the upstream side
on the shoulder of the road The boring locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general under the surface pavement section or fill our borings encountered a
layer of orange brown silty clay alluvium which graded from about 8 to 10 feet below
the ground surface to a yellow or orange brown sandy silt alluvium underlain by
cobbles and boulders alluvium or weathered rock at depths of about 16 to 24 feet
below the ground surface The alluvial clays and silts were generally medium stiff to
stiff in consistency and the underlying cobbles and boulders were tightly packed to a
very dense state We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of
our exploration However it is anticipated that transient groundwater conditions will
most likely occur during times of stream flow at the site It should be noted that these
groundwater levels may fluctuate depending on rainfall seasonal precipitation and
other factors
The field exploration methodology is described in Appendix A herein
Descriptions and graphic representations of the materials encountered in the borings
are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2 of Appendix A The results of
the laboratory tests performed on selected soil samples are presented in Appendix B

Foundations
As previously mentioned our borings encountered stiff to very stiff alluvial deposits
overlying boulders or weathered basalt formation at a depth of 16 to 24 feet below
the existing ground surface We did not encounter groundwater at the time of our
exploration However transient groundwater conditions are anticipated at the site
during periods of stream flow
Based on the relatively competent subsurface conditions encountered and the
schematic design information provided we believe that the new drainage structures
may be supported on shallow foundation systems An allowable bearing pressure
of up to may be used for design of drainage structure foundations bearing
on the compacted on site clayey soils This bearing value is for dead plus live loads
and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused
by seismic forces
Unless covered by concrete channel lining the bottoms of foundations at the
upstream and downstream ends of the culvert replacement should be embedded a
minimum of 4 feet below the lowest adjacent grade to aid in resisting scour and to
mobilize lateral resistance as discussed below If the foundations are covered by
concrete channel lining the embedment depth may be reduced to 2 feet
It should be recognized that it is common for localized soft soil pockets to occur
within alluvial deposits In general the bottom of foundation excavations should be
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior to placement of
reinforcing steel and concrete or CRM material Soft and or loose materials that

over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation to
remove the soft and or loose materials should be backfilled with select granular fill
materials compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the
foundation may extend deeper to bear directly on the underlying competent
material The select granular material should consist of crushed coral or basaltic
gravel The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no

Drainage
The drainage channel and headwall structures should be well drained to reduce the
build up of hydrostatic pressures A typical drainage system should consist of a
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
placed near the bottom and along the length of the wall discharging to an
appropriate outlet or weepholes The weepholes should be spaced no more than
6 feet apart and be set at the base of the gravel drain but above the level of the
structural backfill

Other Considerations
Based on the boring information we believe that temporary cut slopes on the order
of about 1H 1V or flatter may be used for open cut excavations in the medium stiff
to stiff clayey soils anticipated downstream Excavated soils should not be
stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the excavation
from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential for excessive ground
movement
If the new drainage structures will be located close to residential houses these
excavations will need to be adequately shored as open cut excavations are not
practical The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with
applicable safety requirements The contractor should be solely responsible for the
adequacy and safety of the shoring installation His her representative who should
be required continuously present on site during excavation and construction work
will have the best opportunity to promptly observe changing conditions during
construction such as unforeseen subsurface conditions unexpectedly high
groundwater table inappropriate construction sequence or techniques etc which
may affect the shoring stability
Shoring
We anticipate that excavation depths up to about 18 feet below the ground surface
may be required for the installation of the replacement culvert structures Due to the
crossing of the existing road shoring of the excavation will be a critical item The
excavation may be supported by bracing or installing sheet piles for the new
drainage structures We envision that boulders may be encountered during sheet
pile installation and may limit penetration The contractor s structural engineer
should design the shoring system with proper bracing during excavation to provide
adequate lateral support for the excavation
It is important to install adequate sheeting prior to the excavation and to maintain it
tight against the excavation walls with proper bracing during excavation The

cleared and grubbed thoroughly Vegetation concrete debris deleterious materials and
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off site to reduce
the potential for contamination of the excavated materials
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas
designated to receive fill should be over excavated to expose firm natural material and
the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted general fill The
excavated soft soils should be properly disposed off site

Qualified technical personnel should monitor site grading and site preparation

in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage
by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density Compaction should be
accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable
compaction equipment Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed to
compact the fills

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 to depths of approximately 25 1 and
25 3 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring locations are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight augers
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operations on a continuous
basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in the
laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered during drilling are provided on the Logs of
Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler or a 3 inch OD split barrel sampler with a 140 pound
hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and
third 6 inches a total of 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings
at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
