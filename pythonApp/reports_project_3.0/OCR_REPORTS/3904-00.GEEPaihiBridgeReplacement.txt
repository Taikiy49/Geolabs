INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Paihi Bridge Replacement project in the District of Hana on
the island of Maui Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and presents our geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations retaining
structures and site grading only The findings and recommendations presented herein
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

The project site is located along Hana Highway at Milepost 44 95 in the District of
Hana on the Island of Maui Hawaii Based on the information provided we understand
that the existing Paihi Bridge which spans across Paihi Stream will be replaced with a
new wider single span concrete bridge structure The new bridge structure will be
approximately 60 feet in length and 30 feet in width and will be situated generally in the
same location as the existing bridge as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The western

finished deck elevation of the new structure will be at about 210 feet Mean Sea

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Maui is the second largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago with
an area of approximately 728 square miles This island was formed by the activity of two
volcanoes the older West Maui Tertiary Epoch and the more recent East Maui also
known as Haleakala Pleistocene Epoch The Isthmus of Maui is the narrow gently
sloping plain located between these two volcanoes
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3904 00 Page 5
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of the Haleakala shield
volcano in the District of Hana Haleakala was generally built in three distinguishable
series of lava flows The lower unit the Honomanu Volcanic Series late Pliocene early
Pleistocene Epoch consists of thinly bedded tholeiitic basaltic pahoehoe and a a lava
flows These lavas tend to be very permeable and carry basal water Overlying this unit
is the Kula Volcanic Series late Pleistocene Epoch composed primarily of thicker alkalic
a a flows which may contain many inter stratified thin ash soil layers A long quiescent
period with few eruptions followed during which time canyons were carved in the
volcano due to eustatic changes higher and lower stands of sea level At the end of the
resting period copious flows erupted along the southwest and east rifts This is the third
and upper rock unit known as the Hana Volcanic Series Holocene Epoch The lavas
from this volcanic series range in composition from ultra basic olivine porphyries to
non porphyritic alkalic basalts

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration at the bridge replacement site generally encountered
moderately to highly weathered severely fractured basalt rock formation and or clinker at
depths ranging from about 2 to 3 5 feet below the existing ground surface or roadway
grade A slightly harder less fractured basalt formation was encountered at depths of
about 13 5 to 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface or pavement grade
The basalt formations encountered in the borings are closely to severely fractured
resulting in a low Rock Quality Designation RQD Since the RQD of the basalt
formation is less than 50 percent the basalt formation at the site may be considered to be
erodible and will be subject to stream scour In order to reduce the effects of stream
scour on the foundations of the bridge we believe that counter measures will need to be
implemented at the abutment and stream banks to protect the bridge foundations from
the effects of scour
Provided that counter measures are implemented at the bridge location to reduce
the effects of scour on the bridge foundations we believe that a shallow foundation
system bearing on the gray medium hard to hard basalt rock formation may be used to
support the proposed new abutments and wing walls Based on our field exploration we
anticipate that the bottom of footings will need to be located at a depth of at least 17 feet
below the existing roadway grade or extended to the depth of the existing abutment
foundations whichever is deeper in order to bear on the medium hard to hard basalt
formation To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse
of cavities below a footing we recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting
be implemented at the foundations for the abutments and wing walls during the project
construction

Transportation the Paihi Bridge Replacement project will need to be designed based on

Scour Potential
Scour is the result of erosive action of flowing water excavating and carrying
away material from the bed and banks of the stream Total scour over a period of time
generally consists of three components 1 Aggregation and Degradation
2 Contraction Scour and 3 Local Scour The rates of scour depend on a number of
factors such as the shape and dimensions of a pier or abutment depth of flow velocity
of approach flow size and gradation of stream bed material and bed configuration
One of the factors affecting the scour depth is the grain size characteristic of the
stream bed material The median diameter of the stream bed material D in
conjunction with the depth of flow and flow velocity is used to calculate the fall velocity
of the stream bed materials in scour depth analysis Based on the materials
encountered in the two borings drilled for the project we anticipate that the closely to
severely fractured basalt formation and or clinker with a relatively low RQD will be
encountered at the foundation level Due to the close to severe fracturing and low RQD
RQD less than 50 percent the basalt formation at the site may be considered an
erodible material and will be subject to stream scour

in order to evaluate the magnitude of stream scour at the site a median particle
diameter D of may be used for the stream bed material in the scour analyses
to determine the scour depth As a result of the relatively poor quality basalt formation
and or clinker in terms of resistance to scour encountered at the site and the relatively
deep scour depths anticipated counter measures to reduce the scour potential will be
required for the replacement bridge foundations Therefore the foundation design

Bridge Foundations
Based on the design criteria provided the replacement bridge foundations will be
designed based on the Load and Resistance Factor Design LRFD method Based on
the subsurface conditions encountered we recommend that a shallow foundation system
bearing on the gray medium hard to hard closely fractured basalt rock formation be
used for support of the planned bridge abutment and wing wall structures
Based on LRFD methods an ultimate bearing pressure of up to 20 000 pounds
per square foot psf may be used to evaluate the extreme event limit state of the footings
bearing on the gray medium hard to hard basalt formation To evaluate the strength limit
state of the foundations a bearing value of may be used
In general footings should be embedded deep enough to bear directly on the
gray medium hard to hard closely fractured basalt rock formation or extended to the
depth of the existing bridge foundations whichever is deeper Based on our
exploratory borings we anticipate that the bottom of footings will need to be located at
least 17 feet below the existing pavement grade in order to bear directly on the medium
hard to hard basalt rock surface If soft and or loose materials or less competent basalt
formation are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations they should be over
excavated to expose the underlying medium hard to hard basalt rock formation The
over excavation should be backfilled with concrete or lean concrete or the bottom of
footing may be extended deeper to bear on the basalt rock surface

Foundation Probing
Based on our experience in the area voids and or cavities may exist in the basalt
rock formation encountered at the project site To reduce the potential for loss of
foundation support resulting from the collapse of cavities below a footing we recommend
that a program of cavity probing and grouting be implemented during construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that a probe hole be drilled for every
100 square feet of foundation area or at 10 feet on center along the abutment and wing
wall foundations The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should
extend at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of footing elevation If cavities and or
voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operation additional probes
should be drilled at closer spacing to aid in delineating the vertical and lateral extent of
the cavity and or void The probe holes and cavities discovered should be backfilled with
sand cement grout injected at low to moderate pressures We recommend that a low
strength sand cement grout mixture with a slump range of 6 to 9 inches be utilized for the
grouting operations
Because of the potential for encountering large cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that the probe drill be available on site until the probing and grouting
operations are complete It should be noted that a longer lag time between
probing grouting operations and foundation construction might be required in the
construction schedule

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the potential for build up of
hydrostatic pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide
zone of permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation immediately adjacent to the wall with a perforated pipe perforations
facing down at the base of the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or
weepholes As an alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as
MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used instead of the drainage material The
prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a
perforated pipe at the base of the wall
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone should consist of Type A Structure
Backfill Material conforming to Section 703 20 of the HSS Unless covered by
concrete slabs or pavements the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of
relatively impervious material to reduce the potential for water infiltration behind the
walls

basalt formation and or clinker may be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal
to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter Where cut slopes expose dense gray basalt formation
the cut slope may be steepened to a slope inclination as steep as 1H 1V if desired
Cavities that may be exposed on the cut slope face should be backfilled and grouted We
recommend that cut slopes exposing the surface fill and or severely fractured moderately
to highly weathered basalt formation and or clinker be immediately protected by
appropriate slope planting or other means to reduce the potential for erosion of the
exposed soils
Permanent fill slopes constructed with either the general fill materials or imported
fill materials may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fills placed on
slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to
provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations should start at the
lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the
above fill placement recommendations Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling
and cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face Water
should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and slope planting should be provided
as soon as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths of about
51 and 63 5 feet respectively below the existing ground surface using truck mounted
and portable drill rigs equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools The
approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 1 through
A 2 2
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties

sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the
soils The sample was remolded to near the optimum moisture content of the soil

of 18 inches The sample was compacted in 5 equal layers with each layer receiving
10 blows of energy The sample was soaked for 4 days prior to the penetration test The
results of the test are presented on Plate B 2
