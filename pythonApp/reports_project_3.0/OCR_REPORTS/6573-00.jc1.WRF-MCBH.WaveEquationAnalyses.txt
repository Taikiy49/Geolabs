In general accordance with the Contract Documents we performed wave equation
analyses to evaluate whether the concrete piles may be driven to the specified pile tip
elevation with the proposed equipment selected for the above project The wave equation
analyses were conducted based on the pile driving equipment information provided by
your office and the subsurface conditions contained in the referenced construction
drawings
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is at Marine Corps Base Kaneohe on the Island of Oahu Hawaii
We understand it is planned to demolish and replace existing low voltage distribution
systems and incidental related works The new generator building and transformer fuel
tank will be supported on 16 5 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles
Based on the information provided a total of 37 piles are specified including 7 test
piles and 30 production piles RFP request for proposal documents requires that test
piles and production piles should be cast 10 and 5 feet longer than estimated pile length
respectively The estimated pile tip elevation is 40 feet with cut off elevation of about
5 feet Therefore the pile lengths for test piles and production piles are 55 and 50 feet
respectively

The pile locations will be predrilled to the elevation 10 feet specified in the
contract documents prior to driving
Hammer and Driving System
We understand that it is proposed to use a single acting diesel
hammer for the pile driving operations Based on the pile driving equipment data forms
provided the hammer has a ram weight of 4 190 pounds and a maximum
at the maximum pump setting The contract documents stipulate the use of an efficiency of
72 percent for diesel hammers in a wave equation analysis
A pile cushion consisting of 9 inches of plywood cushion block is proposed for the
pile driving operations The plywood cushion block generally has a cross section area of
approximately 306 square inches for the 16 5 inch octagonal concrete piles Schematic
details of the driving system are presented on the Pile and Driving Equipment Data Forms
in the Appendix Page 1
Pile Details
We understand the piles will be cast from concrete with a minimum 28 day
unconfined compressive strength of 6 500 pounds per square inch psi for the 16 5 inch
octagonal concrete piles The minimum of working force after all losses in the prestressed
steel is 167 kips as shown on Construction Drawing Sheet No S 501 The precast shop
drawings propose using 175 kips of working force after all losses The effective prestress
after all losses is computed to be about 774 psi
Therefore the maximum allowable stress compression in the 16 5 inch octagonal
precast prestressed concrete pile during pile driving is about 4 750 psi while the
maximum allowable stress tension is approximately 1 000 psi

proposed and the idealized subsurface conditions interpreted from the boring logs
presented in the construction drawings The wave equation analyses were performed for

Driving Stresses
Graphs showing the computed maximum compression and tensile stresses during
driving are provided on Plates 2 1 and 2 2 for the test pile and production pile analyzed
respectively Please note that the graphs were prepared based on kips per square inch
ksi which is equivalent to 1 000 psi
Based on our analyses the compressive stresses during the pile driving for the test
piles and production piles are less than the allowable compressive stress of 4 750 psi for
the 16 5 inch octagonal concrete piles The compression stresses developed during pile
driving did not exceed the allowable compressive stresses for the full range of driving
resistance at Pump Settings 1 through 4
The tensile stresses developed during the pile driving estimated for the test piles
and production piles also are less than the allowable tensile stress of 1 000 psi for the
16 5 inch square concrete piles for the full range of driving resistance at Pump Settings 1
through 4
Special attention should be given to the pump settings in order to control the energy
during pile driving within the predrilling depth The pile should be removed from the

ABOUT THE WAVE EQUATION ANALYSIS RESULTS
The GRLWEAP program simulates the behavior of a preformed pile driven by
either an impact hammer or a vibratory hammer The program is based on
mathematical models which describe motion and forces of hammer driving
system pile and soil under the hammer action Under certain conditions
the models only crudely approximate often complex dynamic situations
A wave equation analysis generally relies on input data which represents
normal situations In particular the hammer data file supplied with the
program assumes that the hammer is in good working order All of the input
data selected by the user may be the best available information at the time
when the analysis is performed However input data and therefore results
may significantly differ from actual field conditions
Therefore the program authors recommend prudent use of the GRLWEAP results
Soil response and hammer performance should be verified by static and or
dynamic testing and measurements Estimates of bending or other local
non axial stresses and prestress effects must also be accounted for by the
user
The calculated capacity blow count relationship i e the bearing graph
should be used in conjunction with observed blow counts for the capacity
assessment of a driven pile Soil setup occurring after pile installation
may produce bearing capacity values that differ substantially from those
expected from a wave equation analysis due to soil setup or relaxation This
is particularly true for pile driven with vibratory hammers The GRLWEAP user
must estimate such effects and should also use proper care when applying blow
counts from restrike because of the variability of hammer energy soil
resistance and blow count during early restriking
Finally the GRLWEAP capacities are ultimate values They MUST be reduced by
means of an appropriate factor of safety to yield a design or working load
The selection of a factor of safety should consider the quality of the
construction control the variability of the site conditions uncertainties
in the loads the importance of building and other factors
