INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Puukapu Farm Lots project near the town of Waimea in the
District of South Kohala on the island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of
the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design
input only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report

The proposed project site is located to the south of Kahilu Road near the town

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The project site is underlain by basaltic lava flows of the Hamakua Volcanic
Series of Mauna Kea which were deposited during the Pleistocene Epoch Stearns and
Macdonald 1946 The Hamakua Volcanic Series is typically composed of basaltic
lava flows which are mantled by a surface layer of palagonitized ash locally referred
to as Pahala Ash The ash soil at the project site differs from the Pahala Ash found in
other locations on the island of Hawaii in that the in situ moisture content is generally
lower and the soil exists in a friable state
Based on previous experience in the Kamuela area the lava flows underlying the
site are of the a a form which is characterized by a rough spiny or rubbly surface of
fragmental clinker A dense core of massive basalt which frequently has a thinner less
continuous layer of clinker below the massive core underlies the clinker The clinker
surface of the lavas at the site have been mantled by the Pahala Ash as discussed
above

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling ten borings along
the existing dirt roads designated as Boring Nos 1 through 10 to depths ranging from
about 8 to 11 feet below the existing ground surface Two additional borings
designated as Boring Nos 11 and 12 were drilled to a depth of about 50 feet below
the existing ground surface for percolation testing Six bulk samples designated as
through 6 were obtained for the near surface soils to evaluate its
pavement support characteristics The approximate locations of the borings and near
surface bulk samples are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our field exploration generally encountered a surficial deposit of volcanic ash
consisting of medium dense silty sands and stiff silty soils in a relatively dry and friable
state varying from about 2 to 7 feet thick This was underlain by approximately 2 to 8
feet of highly weathered basalt materials saprolites consisting of very stiff silts and

The surficial volcanic ash encountered at the site has relatively low moisture

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered a relatively thin mantle of volcanic
ash deposit over competent saprolitic materials and basalt rock formation across the

project site The surficial volcanic ash deposit generally consist of medium dense silty
sands and stiff silty soils in a relatively dry and friable state varying from about 2 to 7
feet thick In general we believe that the near surface soils generated from cut areas
may be reused as a source of general fill material provided that they are properly
processed and placed on prepared subgrades as recommended in the Site Grading

Due to the sensitive nature of the ash soils to moisture fluctuations special care

responsibility of each individual lessee Based on the gently sloping terrain at the
subject site and the road profile plans provided we anticipate that site grading work for
the proposed roadway construction will generally consist of cuts and fills less than

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site or in
a designated area to reduce potential contamination of the excavated materials
The near surface ash soils encountered in cut areas may be stockpiled and
reused as a source of general fill material provided that the recommendations
presented in the Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements subsection are
followed
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3529 00 Page 10
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below any
areas designated to receive fill should be over excavated to expose firm natural
material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted
engineered fill The excavated soft soils should be properly disposed of off site
After clearing and grubbing all areas beneath future pavements should be over
excavated to a minimum depth of 6 inches below the bottom of the base course
layer Where necessary over excavation of up to 18 inches below the bottom
of the base course layer may be required depending on the soil conditions
encountered This over excavation is to provide for the select borrow subbase
course required below the pavement section If zones of soft loose or saturated
soils are encountered at the over excavated subgrade level deeper over
excavation may be required to expose the underlying firm material The
resulting over excavation should be backfilled with compacted engineered fill
The over excavated subgrade should be moisture conditioned to about the
optimum moisture content and proof rolled to about 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Due to the
generally thixotropic nature of the near surface ash soils use of the vibratory
action of the compactor should be carefully controlled to avoid disturbing the ash
soils In addition moisture conditioning of the ash soils should be carefully
controlled to avoid over wetting of the soils It should be noted that the near
surface ash soils may be sensitive and difficult to achieve the specified
compaction Therefore additional compaction effort with proper moisture control
may be necessary to meet the specified compaction criteria
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3529 00 Page 11
If the subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade preparation the subgrade
should be proof rolled and a filter fabric such as Mirafi 500X or equivalent
should be placed on the subgrade prior to placement of fill material Where
shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend
that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close all cracks or the subgrade should
be scarified moisture conditioned and recompacted Saturation and subsequent
yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage
may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well
compacted engineered fill
In areas where the cut subgrades expose basalt rock formation the subgrades
should be proof rolled with a minimum 20 ton vibratory roller or a Caterpillar D 9
bulldozer or similar heavy equipment for a minimum of 6 passes to help detect
and collapse near surface cavities Any loose areas or cavities disclosed during
clearing and proof rolling operations should be backfilled with well compacted
engineered fill materials The proof rolling operation should also be performed
at the finish subgrades of cut areas in basalt rock formation
Due to the potential presence of soft and or wet soils contract documents should
include unit prices for over excavation and engineered fill placement to account
for variations in the over excavation and backfill quantities Our laboratory test
results indicate that the near surface soils have variable CBR values ranging
from about 1 to over 30 In addition dense saprolites and basalt rock formation
are encountered at shallow depths in localized areas Therefore it is our opinion
that the contract documents should include unit prices for 6 12 and 18 inches
of over excavation and select borrow subbase course placement to account for
variations in the over excavation and select borrow subbase course backfill
quantities due to the variable subsoil conditions at the site

Fills and Backfills
The fill and backfill materials to be used as general fill material up to the bottom
of the select borrow subbase should consist of well graded materials less than
6 inches in largest dimension In general the near surface ash soils mixed with
cinder sands encountered during our field exploration should be suitable for use
as general fill materials provided that they are moisture conditioned to about the
optimum moisture and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction
The on site cut materials generated from excavations into the underlying basalt
formation may be used as general fill or backfill materials provided that they are
screened of the over sized materials greater than 6 inches in largest dimension
and processed to meet the above gradation requirements This requirement is
to facilitate future utility trench excavations Over sized rock fragments may be
disposed of off site or broken down to smaller sized materials and incorporated
into the fill material If the excavated materials do not contain sufficient fines to
produce a well graded material off site borrow or on site rock crushing of large
sized rock fragments or boulders should be considered to provide the required
gradation and particle sizes to develop a well graded material
If imported material is required the material should be non expansive granular
material such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material
should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches
in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles
passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value
of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell value of 1 percent or less
Imported fill materials should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for conformance with
these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its intended use

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
General fills and backfills up to 2 feet below the finish grade of the pavement
areas should be moisture conditioned to about the optimum moisture placed in
level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to at least
90 percent relative compaction Fill select borrow and base course materials
within 2 feet of the finish grade of pavement areas should be compacted to at
least 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the
same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
Optimum moisture is the water content corresponding to the maximum dry
density Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory
rollers multiple wheel pneumatic tired rollers or other types of acceptable
compaction equipment
Due to the dry and sometimes windy site conditions moisture conditioning of fill
materials will be a critical aspect during fill placement In some areas the in situ
soils appear to be dry relative to the optimum moisture content Therefore
water trucks should be available in sufficient number to provide adequate water
for moisture conditioning during compaction The State and Federal
governments have enacted stringent regulations pertaining to the release of dust
from construction sites Therefore it is also advisable to institute a dust control
program during grading operations to reduce the amount of airborne dust due
to the nature of the on site soils

Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings it appears that
most of the cut slopes would expose the surficial ash soils consisting of stiff silty
soils and medium dense silty sands In general we believe that cut slopes
composed of the in situ volcanic ash soils or the underlying dense saprolitic
materials may be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to one
vertical 2H 1V or flatter Where the cut slopes expose dense basalt formation
at the slope face the cut slope may be steepened to a slope inclination of
1H 1V Cavities that may be exposed on the cut slope face should be backfilled
and or grouted if necessary We recommend that cut slopes exposing ash soils
be immediately protected by appropriate slope planting or other means to reduce
the potential for erosion of the exposed soils
Permanent fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or
flatter For cut and fill slopes steeper than 4H 1V with vertical heights greater
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3529 00 Page 15
than 20 feet we recommend that a minimum 8 foot wide bench be provided at
the mid point of the slope to reduce the potential for erosion due to surface run
off water We also recommend that V ditches or earth berms be provided at
the top and bottom of slopes In addition we also recommend placement of
geotextile fabrics on the slope to reduce the potential for erosion
Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the
existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling
operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal
compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design
slope ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face In the event that over cutting
of a slope occurs keying and benching requirements should be implemented
instead of backfilling the slope to the design grade with sliver fills Alternatively
cut slopes may be buttressed with fill slopes constructed of non expansive fills
Water should be diverted away from the top of slopes and slope planting should
be provided as soon as possible to reduce potential erosion of the finished
slopes A sketch of typical keying and benching requirements is shown on

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
ten borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 10 to depths ranging from about 8
to 11 feet below the existing ground surface using a truck mounted drill rig equipped
with continuous flight augers Two additional borings designated as Boring Nos 11
and 12 were drilled to a depth of about 50 feet below the existing ground surface for
percolation testing The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site
Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field geologist who monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the
materials encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left
in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of

APPENDIX C
Field Percolation Testing
The permeability of the in situ soils were estimated by performing six constant
head percolation tests in Boring Nos 11 and 12 at depths of approximately 20 30 and
50 feet below the existing ground surface The test results are presented on Plates C 1
through C 6
The constant head test was performed by pumping water into the borehole until
the water surface has stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a sufficient period of
time The water height and the rate of pumping were then measured to provide data
upon which to base calculation of the subsoil permeability
