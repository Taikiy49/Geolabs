This report presents the results of our Phase I Rockfall Potential Hazard 
Assessment performed for the proposed Makena Mauka Off-Site Retention Basin 
project in Makena on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project location and general 
vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. This report summarizes the 
findings from our evaluation and analysis and presents our preliminary geotechnical 
findings and recommendations for the proposed project. 
The preliminary findings and recommendations provided herein are intended to 
provide an assessment of the potential hazard associated with possible falling rock from 
the basin cut slopes only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are 
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
Project Considerations 
The Makena Residences & Beach Club project is in the District of Makawao on 
the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project generally consists of renovating the existing 
hotel development into a luxury condominium complex. As part of the project, an off-site 
retention basin will be constructed on existing undeveloped land underlain by basaltic 
rock formation. 
The proposed retention basin will have a floor elevation of about +200 feet Mean 
Sea Level (MSL).  Rock cut slopes at the mauka side of the basin are planned at one 
horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V) with approximate 8-foot wide benches at about 20-foot 
height intervals on the slope. Based on the preliminary plans, cuts at the proposed 
1H:1V slopes will extend 20 to 40 feet below the existing ground surface, exposing

Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui is comprised of two major volcanoes, the older Tertiary Epoch 
(West Maui) and the more recent Pleistocene Epoch (East Maui), also known as 
Haleakala (Pleistocene Epoch). The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain 
located between these two volcanoes. The Isthmus of Maui was created by lava flows 
from Haleakala Volcano ponding against West Maui Volcano. The gently sloping plains 
subsequently were filled with alluvium washed down from the slopes of West Maui and 
East Maui (Haleakala) Volcanoes. The erosional processes are dominated by the 
detachment of soil and rock masses from the mountain walls, and the soil materials are 
transported down slope toward the isthmus primarily by gravity as colluvium. Once 
these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley, alluvial processes 
become dominant, and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium. 
The project site is on the western flank of the East Maui Volcano, which forms 
the east side of the Island of Maui. The East Maui Mountain is primarily composed of 
lavas from the Kula and Hana Volcanic Series. The basalt formations at the project site 
may be part of the Kula Volcanic Series, which consist of a thick series of alkalic a`a 
lava flows. 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is about ½-mile mauka (easterly) of the existing Makena-Alanui 
Road in Makena on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. An unpaved access road leads to the 
site from an existing water storage tank.  The perimeter of an existing golf course is 
down slope and about 500 feet towards the west from the project site. Undeveloped 
vacant land bounds the project site towards the north, south, and east. The approximate 
location of the project site and vicinity is shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
The project site currently exists as vacant and undeveloped land.  Based on our 
observations, native vegetation consists of scattered large Kiawe trees, shrubbery, and 
dryland grasses.  The existing ground surface conditions generally consist of thin rocky 
soils with scattered basaltic boulders overlying basalt rock formation. Based on our 
observations, the existing surface boulders generally range up to about 4 feet in 
dimension. The climate in the area is generally arid with a lower than normal frequency 
of rainfall. Based on the available topographic survey map, the existing ground surface 
elevations range from about +200 to +250 feet MSL within the gently sloping terrain 
encompassing the proposed retention basin. The Site Plan for the proposed retention 
basin project is presented on Plate 2.

Anticipated Basin Surface Conditions 
We anticipate that the proposed basin cut slope surfaces may be composed of 
hard bare rock surfaces with limited vegetation. Furthermore, the proposed basin floor 
may be composed of a bare rock surface with some minor soft sediment deposition and 
natural vegetation growth.  
Therefore, our rockfall simulation model was developed using appropriate 
program input parameters that would reflect the anticipated cut slope and basin floor 
ground surface conditions. 
Computer Rockfall Simulation 
We performed statistical computer modeling and simulation of potential falling 
rock conditions using the Colorado Rockfall Simulation Program (CRSP) Version 4 to 
analyze whether possible falling rock emanating from the higher elevation basin cut 
slopes could roll and breach the basin at the lower elevation region. Because the 
retention basin is proposed and not yet constructed, input parameters to run the 
program (including probable rock size, shape, and numerical parameters approximating 
the slope surface and vegetation conditions) were estimated based on the boring log 
information and our experience with similar rock slope settings.  We used the available 
preliminary grading plan by Austin Tsutsumi & Associates dated March 2016 to develop 
the idealized basin slope profile for our analysis. 
The CRSP is a computer program that is a widely accepted engineering tool 
used by experienced analysts to estimate potential rockfall behavior by simulating 
rockfall activity based on input parameters assigned on a site-specific basis. The 
rockfall simulation program provides a statistical evaluation of potential rockfall behavior

Each rockfall simulation run consisted of releasing 1,000 source rocks from the 
cut slope region to obtain a broad statistical distribution of the computed rock roll-out 
distance, velocity and bounce height at three selected statistical analysis points along 
the basin floor. Simulated source rock consisted of spherical basaltic boulders ranging

slope profile. The basin rock source and deposition areas as well as the three 
analysis points, identified as Analysis Point Nos. 1 through 3, are shown on the 
Slope Profile A-A’, Plate 3.  
Where rockfall hazard mitigation is warranted, the generally accepted standard of 
practice is to achieve a minimum 90% retention/control of potential falling rock hazards. 
Complete 100% control of potential falling rock hazards is typically impractical and not 
cost effective to achieve.  Rockfall is affected by many external factors including 
erosional decay, extreme weather and other factors that generally cannot be controlled. 
However, through prudent study and application of available technology combined with 
high quality construction practice, the construction of rockfall mitigation improvements 
can effectively reduce the risk to an acceptable level.

Discussion and Recommendations 
Based on our review of the available subsurface information pertinent to the 
Makena Mauka Off-Site Retention Basin project, the drilled borings generally 
encountered fill materials consisting of dense gravel and cobbles from the ground 
surface extending down to about 1 to 5 feet below the ground surface. The near-surface 
fill materials are generally underlain by interbedded layers of a’a clinker and dense 
basalt formation extending down to the maximum depth explored of about 42 feet below 
the ground surface.  The basalt formation encountered was generally moderately 
weathered and hard. The clinker materials generally consisted of weakly-consolidated, 
medium dense cobbles and gravel. 
Based on the available grading plan, the majority of the retention basin will be 
constructed in cut condition.  In general, we envision that most of the cut slopes and basin 
floor may expose dense basaltic rock formation. Berms on the down slope side of the 
basin may be constructed of fill material. 
Based on the results of our rockfall simulation analyses using the available 
schematic grading plan for the model slope and basin geometry, the potential for falling 
rock emanating from the basin cut slopes to pass across the basin floor and breach the 
opposing basin slope is essentially nil. Based on our Phase I assessment, it appears 
that potential rockfall emanating from the basin cut slopes should be completely 
retained within the basin and should not impart a potential rockfall hazard on the area 
surrounding the exterior of the proposed retention basin. Therefore, we believe that no 
special rockfall mitigation controls or structures are necessary to protect persons and 
infrastructure located outside of the retention basin from rockfall occurring inside of the 
basin. 
CRSP Results 
We modeled two possible scenarios of potential rockfall identified as Case Nos. 1 
and 2. The Case No. 1 model assumed natural slope surface conditions (i.e. some 
sedimentation and vegetation growth) after a period of basin use following construction. 
The Case No. 2 model assumed a worst-case scenario using very hard, barren rock 
surfaces devoid of sedimentation, vegetation growth, and rock surface irregularity 
(roughness). We ran Case No. 2 to show that even by specifying a very smooth and 
very hard slope surface (conservative simulation model) potential rockfall from the basin 
cut slopes should not breach the basin interior. 
For both Case studies, spherical basaltic boulders ranging from 2 to 6 feet in 
dimension were modeled as potential rockfall released from the basin cut slope. 
Engineering analysis points were established along the fall-line profile to evaluate the 
simulated rockfall behavior at progressive distances away from the source rock slope. 
Analysis Point No. 1 is near the cut slope toe and Analysis Point No. 3 is near the berm 
sidewall at the opposite end of the basin.

Limitations 
The findings and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from visual site observations, assumed site conditions, and 
computer simulation and statistical analysis of potential rockfall activity only. Variation in 
the surface and subsurface conditions between our observations and analysis points 
may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until 
additional field exploration or construction is underway. If variations then appear 
evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the findings and recommendations provided 
herein. 
It should be noted that slopes composed of rock materials (rock slopes) 
deteriorate with the passage of time due to natural weathering processes, wet-dry and 
hot-cold cycles, and erosion conditions.  Due to the inherent deterioration of rock slopes 
resulting from natural processes (weathering, wet-dry and hot-cold cycles, and erosion 
conditions), the potential for rockfall hazard at any site changes with the passage of 
time.  Therefore, the findings and recommendations contained herein may be used only 
within a reasonable time from the date of issuance of this letter report. Land use, site 
conditions, and/or other factors may change with the passage of time. Therefore,
