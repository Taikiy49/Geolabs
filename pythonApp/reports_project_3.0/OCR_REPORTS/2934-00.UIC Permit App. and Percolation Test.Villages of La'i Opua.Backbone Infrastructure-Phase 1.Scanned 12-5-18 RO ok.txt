INTRODUCTION
In accordance with the request of Isemoto Contracting Company we have made
visual observations upon completion of construction of twenty one 21 new drywells
at the project site In addition we have tested six 6 of the new drywells This report
has been prepared in support of Underground Injection Control permit application
No UH 1747
The approximate location of the project site and its vicinity is shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1 Our work was performed in general accordance with our proposal
to Isemoto Contracting Company dated
This report summarizes findings resulting from our field observations and testing
of the new drywells at the project site
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located approximately two and a half miles north of Kailua Kona
and is mauka of the Honokohau Harbor in Kealakehe District of North Kona on the
island of Hawaii It is our understanding that the County of Hawaii requires that new
development create no net increase in discharge of surface or storm water runoff The
general practice on the isiand of Hawaii is to utilize drywells as a method of disposal of
surface runoff

The project consists of a new parkway approximately 4 400 feet in length which
will provide access to the Villages of La i Opua development It was proposed to install
twenty one 21 drywells to provide on site disposal of storm water run off along the
roadway alignment

PHYSICAL CHARACTERISTICS
The site is located in the District of North Kona on the island of Hawaii The
climate of the area is mild with an average annual temperature of about 74 F and annual
rainfall ranges from about 20 to about 40 inches per year
The site is situated on the western flank of Mount Hualalai The topography is
relatively gentle to moderate sloping down towards the west to the ocean from the
central portion of the island
Formerly the site had been undeveloped land At the time of the drywell testing

Regional Geology
The island of Hawaii is composed of five 5 basalt shield volcanoes built by the
extrusion of the lavas during the Pleistocene and the Holocene Epochs The site is
located on the western flank of Mount Hualalai a now dormant volcano which last
erupted between 1801 1803 This mountain is thought to be of an intermediate age
between Mauna Kea and Mauna Loa its larger neighboring mountains Hualalai appears
to have entered the late stage of the eruptive cycle Rocks of Hualalai Volcano cover a
portion of the western side of the island of Hawaii Most of its lavas are alkalic olivine
basalt and physically are composed of both Pahoehoe and A a flow types
Earthquake Conditions
The island of Hawaii is designated in the 1985 Uniform Building Code as Seismic
Zone lll The drywells were constructed in a relatively competent rock formation and they
generally consist of simple excavated pits lined with perforated precast concrete rings and
catch basin inlets at the ground surface Therefore seismic conditions are not
considered to be significant
Flood Problems
Site grading to provide positive drainage of the site has been designed and is
under construction In addition the site is located inland of and at a higher elevation
than the expected tsunami inundation zone for this portion of the island of Hawaii
Therefore it is anticipated that flooding should not affect the operation of the drywells
Land Use
Most of the site is situated below the Underground Injection Control Line for this
portion of the island of Hawaii and is in a suburban area zoned for mixed commercial and
residential development Construction and use of drywells at the site is considered to be
consistent with this use

SYSTEM OPERATION
It is proposed to use the new drywells for the disposal of storm water runoff flowing
from the paved roadway Use of the drywells will be intermittent and will be dependent
upon weather conditions The influent will not be treated prior to disposal
The complete drywell system will consist of twenty one 21 drywells along the
roadway alignment All twenty one 21 drywells are located down gradient of the U I C
Line
Injection will be under gravity pressure i e the head pressure that builds up within
the individual drywells
As built sketches of the excavated portions of the drywells are shown on Plates A 1
through A 7 These sketches show in section the construction of the drywell sidewalls
and supporting structures
Since the new drywells are intended for intermittent disposal of storm water runoff
only there is no additional standby system nor are monitoring wells planned
GEOHYDROLOGIC CONSIDERATIONS
The site is located at Latitude North and Longitude West
The ground surface elevations at the site range from approximately 48 feet to 245 feet
above Mean Sea Level MSL
The regional characteristics of groundwater in the area is fresh to brackish water
in a basal volcanic aquifer of relatively high permeability Published data indicates that

WELL CONSTRUCTION
Generally the drywells encountered interbedded layers of Pahoehoe and A a
basalts of the Hualalai Volcanic Series
11 16 and 17 were constructed as follows
The bottom four feet 4 of the drywells are protected by a precast perforated
concrete ring to support the sidewalls of the excavation and to allow for infiltration into the
surrounding strata The remaining side wall of the excavated drywells consist of basalt
boulder masonry Prior to placing the precast perforated ring the dimensions of the
excavated pits measured approximately 12 to 14 feet in diameter On site excavated
materials were used as backfill behind the precast perforated ring and masonry work
and 21 were constructed with
perforated concrete rings placed at varying depths The attached as built drywell sections
provide a graphic depiction of these drywells
In general the excavations for the drywells encountered layered medium dense
clinker and dense to very dense basalt rock For most of the drywells the excavation
work was performed by blasting the rock formation and excavating the loose materials
generated by the blasting and 15 were excavated by using
the auger drilling method Therefore neither perforated concrete rings nor boulder
masonry was placed for sidewall support except for masonry curbing in the upper two 2

PERMEABILITY TESTING
The subsurface permeability conditions at the site were tested by the injection of
clear fresh water from a water tank truck with a capacity of approximately 6 000 gallons
Injection rates were measured by use of an in line flow sensor During testing clean
water was injected at about 100 to 300 gallons per minute for a period of about one half
hour to one hour depending on the flow rate for each test During the injection of water
the variations of the water level in the drywells were recorded with respect to time and
rate of injection These tests indicated that the formation has an approximate coefficient
of permeability k with a range on the order of approximately 500 to 1400 feet per day
Groundwater was not observed in any of the excavated drywells prior to the
beginning of the testing
The results of the tests are presented in Appendix B
