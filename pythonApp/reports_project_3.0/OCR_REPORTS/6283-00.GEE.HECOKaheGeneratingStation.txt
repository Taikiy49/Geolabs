performed for the Welding and Valve Repair Shop project at the existing HECO Kahe
Generating Station in the Nanakuli area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting
from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses The
recommendations presented herein are intended for the design of foundations
slabs on grade retaining structures and the individual wastewater system only The
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at
the end of this report

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The
younger shield Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae
Volcano became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank was
partially buried below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank These banked or
ponded lavas formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau
The Waianae Mountain Range is composed of layered basaltic lava flows and
pyroclastic materials which are grouped and classified as the Waianae Volcanic Series
The Waianae Volcanic Series is divided into the lower middle and upper volcanic
members The lower member comprises lava flows and associated pyroclastic rocks
that built the main mass of the Waianae Shield Volcano The middle member consists
of rock that accumulated and gradually filled the vast volcanic caldera The upper
member is a relatively thin capping layer that covered the entire top of the shield
volcano late in its history of evolution
Once the Waianae Shield Volcano formed a long period of deep erosion
sedimentation and subsidence of the Island of Oahu occurred which produced the
large valleys of the western side of the shield volcano These erosional valleys were
gradually filled with enormous accumulations of alluvium and colluvium deposited as a
combined result of stream erosion base level rise sea level rise and subsidence of
the island mass Over time and as a result of sea level fluctuation some of the older
alluvial and colluvial deposits were buried and through consolidation and cementation
formed relatively dense deposits

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic
plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic
activity therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during
volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most are so
small that they can be detected only by sensitive instruments However some of
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to moderate
damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii Because majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii
over 90 percent are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic activity and

Based on the anticipated grading concept and the generally stiff dense subsurface

loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation

be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall

covered by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches of backfill should also consist of
low expansion relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential for excessive
water infiltration behind the walls

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly Vegetation debris deleterious materials and
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off site to
reduce the potential for contaminating the excavated materials to be used as fill

operations should be backfilled with compacted granular fill material Existing
underground utilities to be discontinued should be removed and the resulting
excavation should be backfilled properly with the excavated on site materials The
on site materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content placed in 6 to 8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction Utilities to be abandoned in place under the
proposed structure should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete or CLSM

Fill Materials
In general the excavated on site granular materials may be used as a source of fill
materials provided that deleterious materials such as vegetation and or organic
matter are removed and over sized materials greater than 3 inches in maximum
dimension are screened Imported fill materials required for the project construction
should consist of non expansive select granular material such as crushed coral or
basalt The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no
larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and
30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a
laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent
or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D 18838
Aggregate subbase materials required for the project should consist of crushed
basaltic aggregates and should conform to Section 31 of the City and County of
Honolulu Standard Specifications should test the
imported fill materials for conformance with these recommendations prior to
delivery to the project site for the intended use
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Select granular fill and aggregate subbase materials should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts of
about 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to at least 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or
other types of acceptable compaction equipment

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 The borings extended to depths of

Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous full time basis These classifications were further reviewed visually
and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with
ASTM D 2487 Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes

representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 6

California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some samples
were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D 1586
Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive

APPENDIX C
Field Percolation Tests
Field percolation tests were performed in general accordance with the State of
Systems and Chapter 10 of the Ten States Standards to evaluate the permeability of
the subsurface soils for wastewater disposal
Two percolation tests designated as P 1 and P 2 were performed by drilling a
4 0 inch diameter borehole to depths of about 4 and 7 feet below the existing ground
surface The approximate locations of the field percolation tests are shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2
The borehole was presoaked for a minimum of 4 hours prior to the percolation
testing Approximately one inch of gravel was then placed at the bottom of the borehole
to protect the bottom of the hole from scouring and sediments During testing the hole
was Carefully filled with water to a minimum depth of 12 inches above the gravel pack
The time intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which
calculation of the percolation tests were based Results of the individual percolation
tests are shown on Plates C 1 and C 2
