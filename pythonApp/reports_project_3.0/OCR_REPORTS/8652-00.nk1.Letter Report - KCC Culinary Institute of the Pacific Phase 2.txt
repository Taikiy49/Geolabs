PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located on the southern end of the KCC Culinary Institute on 
Diamond Head Road on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The Culinary Institute is bounded by 
Diamond Head Road to the north, Diamond Head Crater to the south, and Wauke Street 
to the northwest. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location 
Map, Plate 1. 
We understand that loose rocks were observed on a small section of slope on the 
south side of the project site. The ongoing improvements project involves the construction 
of new retaining walls in this area. Based on the information provided, we understand that 
recent inclement weather is suspected to have caused loose rock material and potential 
rockfall hazards in this area. The initial area of concern is marked on the Site Plan,

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
The area of concern is located along the southern boundary of the KCC Culinary 
Institute Phase 2 project that is currently under construction. Loose rocks were noted on 
the existing cut slope behind the southern retaining wall after recent inclement weather.  
A visual site reconnaissance was conducted by our geologist and senior engineer 
on March 21, 2023. The purpose of our field reconnaissance was to observe the existing 
slope conditions and evaluate potential rockfall hazards and/or areas of possible slope 
instability. Site reconnaissance photographs depicting the observed conditions at the 
project site are presented on Plates 3.1 through 3.7. 
The area of concern consists of a cut slope into the in-situ volcanic tuff formation 
on the northern edge of Diamond Head. Two loose blocks of fractured tuff approximately 
12 inches in diameter were noted in this area (Photograph No. 1, Plate 3.1). It appears

that a weakly cemented mantle of colluvial material consisting of weathered tuff and 
coralline gravel is overlying the volcanic tuff formation (Photographs 2 and 3, Plates 3.1

observed from a profile view of this area. The interface between the two materials is 
inclined at about a 30 to 40-degree angle from horizontal (Photograph No. 4, Plate 3.2). 
After observing the initial area of concern, we were asked by the on-site University 
of Hawaii and HDR, Inc. representatives to observe additional slope areas to the west of 
the initial area of concern. Note that the following areas were outside of our scope of work 
for this project. 
Immediately west of the initial area of concern, the existing cut slope consists of a 
near-vertical face of the volcanic tuff formation. The slope area was observed to have a 
moderate amount of vegetation on the top-of-slope and minor slope vegetation on the 
slope face (Photograph Nos. 4 through 6, Plates 3.2 and 3.3). A potentially loose tabular 
boulder at the top of the slope was observed, as well as other small, loose rock pieces 
(Photograph No. 7, Plate 3.4). A small undermined section was observed that created a 
small overhang, as well as additional loose pieces as we moved west along the slope 
(Photograph Nos. 8 through 10, Plates 3.4 and 3.5).  
Further west, the existing volcanic tuff cut face forms an amphitheater-shaped 
section that bowls outward towards Diamond Head. On the left side (east side) of the 
bowl, a large willow tree was observed on the slope face. The roots of the tree were 
observed growing through the fractures in the tuff formation up to about 30 feet away from 
the main tree. Remnants of a concrete slab and underlying imported fill material were 
observed against the slope face (Photograph No. 12, Plate 3.6). On the right side of the 
amphitheater-shaped section, the excavated volcanic tuff slope face was generally 
observed to be near-vertical (Photograph No. 13, Plate 3.7). Minimal loose rock material 
was observed in the amphitheater-shaped section of the slope. However, the exposed 
tuff was generally soft and relatively friable (broken easily with a geologist hammer).  
The western-most slope section that we were asked to observe generally 
consisted of an excavated, near-vertical volcanic tuff face approximately 5 feet behind a 
newly constructed retaining wall (Photograph No. 14, Plate 3.7). The exposed face was 
relatively friable. Minimal loose rock debris was observed on the slope face. 
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field observations, we believe there are minor potential rockfall 
hazards present at the initial area of concern. However, based on our on-site 
conversations, we understand that an additional retaining wall will be constructed from 
the back of the existing wall to the face of the existing slope. This would help to contain 
future soil and/or rock material from falling into open areas planned to be used by people. 
The approximate location of the future retaining wall is illustrated below.

Based on the above, we recommend scaling and sloping back the colluvial soil 
material overlying the in-situ volcanic tuff formation. The excavated slope face should 
have an inclination of two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. Loose surface rocks 
and debris should be removed by manual methods, such as pry bars or other hand tools.  
Based on our field observations of the slope areas outside our scope of work for 
this project, we believe there are potential rockfall hazards that should be addressed. We 
understand that the areas at the base of these slopes will be frequently used by people. 
Therefore, since pedestrians will be in close proximity to the exposed slope faces, we 
believe there is a moderate risk of injury if rockfall hazard mitigation measures are not 
implemented. We recommend performing a Phase I rockfall hazard evaluation program 
on these slope areas to determine appropriate rockfall hazard mitigation measures. 
Possible rockfall hazard mitigation measures at the project site may consist of, but are 
not limited to, options and/or combinations of options such as slope scaling, rockfall 
catchment ditches, draped wire mesh, and shotcrete slope protection.  
Please contact our office if you would like to coordinate a Phase I rockfall hazard 
evaluation program for the subject slopes that are outside our scope of work for this 
project.
