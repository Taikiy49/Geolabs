PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site encompasses a section of trail along the Kapaloa Falls hiking trail
near Pololu Valley in North Kohala on the Island of Hawaii The section of trail includes
some steep cliffs bounding a narrow valley and waterfall The trail was developed by
manually cutting into the rock cliffs during the early development of the regional water
tunnel system
As a result of the October 2006 Big Island earthquakes the trail was damaged and
an emergency contract to rehabilitate the trail was undertaken During rehabilitation work
on the trail the contractor uncovered a prominent ground crack trending along the trail
alignment for a distance of about 70 linear feet Reference marker stakes were set by the
DLNR across the crack and measurements over the past year indicate little if any ground
movement However the nature of the crack appears to represent existing severe ground

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii is the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers an
area of approximately 4 030 square miles The island was formed by the coalescence
of five primary shield volcanoes Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea activity during
recent geologic time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both
still active Due to the coalescence of the primary volcano flanks the oldest lava

comprising the lower volcano flanks have been overlapped and deeply buried by
younger lavas thus the evolution of the island shape visible today
The geologic history of the Island of Hawaii began in the early Pleistocene with
rapid building of separate volcanic island domes which eventually coalesced to form a
single island Volcanic activity temporarily ceased at Mauna Kea and Kohala as a period
of deep terrestrial erosion and marine erosion took place which formed a broad shelf
now located offshore from Mauna Kea and Kohala at a depth of about 1 200 feet
relative to today s sea level Eruptions continued at Kea and
Hualalai and new eruptions began at Kilauea Terrestrial canyons up to 3 000 feet deep
and tall coastal sea cliffs were carved into the older Kohala and Mauna Loa mountain
flanks followed by substantial submergence of the whole island mass The island
submergence drowned the mouths of the deeper canyons causing the deposition of
thick alluvial deposits on the canyon floors especially at Kohala Following the early
island submergence a rapid re emergence of the island occurred with renewed stream
incision as volcanic activity finally ceased at Kohala Various sea level fluctuations
submergence and emergence followed with alternating episodes of renewed stream
incision and alluvial deposition occurring
By late Pleistocene the regional surface deposition of Pahala Ash occurred on
all five volcano slopes that were not being overflowed with new lava Also renewed
volcanism filled in some large volcano calderas and the younger lavas spilled into some
of the eroded canyons For a period of time about 250 000 years ago there was some
ice age glaciations at the Mauna Kea summit Subsequent eruptions at Mauna Kea
resulted in glacier melting and large scale debris floods The last glaciers at Mauna Kea
melted during the Holocene
Due to the presence of on going volcanic activity and the subsurface movement of
magma at Mauna Loa and Kilauea the Island of Hawaii is within a zone of active seismic
influence The Island of Hawaii experiences frequent light to moderate earthquakes
associated with the movement of underground magma and shifting of the earth s crust
under the weight of the growing island mass Occasional strong and damaging
earthquakes have been recorded both historically and in recent geologic time The
occurrence of strong earthquakes and ground shaking may involve geologic fault rupture
and some lateral ground surface offset
The project site is on the western side of Pololu Valley within a tributary gulch
containing Kapaloa Stream as shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 The project
site is in an area of high rainfall and high topographic relief Pololu Valley is incised
through thick sequential lava flows belonging to the Pleistocene Pololu Volcanic Series
Based on the available geological mapping information the individual lava flows are
believed to be on the order of about 5 to 15 feet thick In general Pololu Valley evolved by
uninterrupted stream erosion which produced a deep and steeply sloped canyon The
steep canyon side slopes were initially produced by stream down cutting and erosion
which continues today in conjunction with other geological mass wasting processes Mass

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
A site reconnaissance was conducted on August 18 2011 under fair weather
conditions by Steven Carr and John Chen Geolabs Roger Matsuoka and Steve Bergfeld
DLNR Rick Gordon Kohala Surety and Clem Na Ala Hele Trails The purpose of the
ground reconnaissance was to observe the existing condition of the fissure ground crack
near Kapaloa Falls
The Kapaloa Falls project site is approximately 1 mile from the trailhead gate as
shown on Plate 1 The hiking trail is currently posted as closed to the public The hiking
trail traverses the western side of Pololu Valley before reaching the V shaped tributary
gulch containing Kapaloa Falls and Stream We observed evidence of very recent
rockslides that had impacted the trail leaving large boulder debris on the trail at several
locations The 5 to 6 foot wide hiking trail traverses on contour along the steep northern
and southern walls of Kapaloa Gulch The trail passes behind Kapaloa Falls at the axis of
the gulch Kapaloa Falls drops sub vertically several hundred feet in length toward the
gulch floor at the gulch axis The gulch floor is covered and partially filled with colluvial
boulders A sketch showing the typical gulch profile is provided on Plate 2
The side slopes of the geologically young V shaped gulch are generally steeper
than about one quarter horizontal to one vertical and are vertical to slightly
overhanging in places The hiking trail was manually carved into the gulch side slopes
during the early development of a regional network of foot trails and water supply tunnels
developed to support agriculture irrigation A sketch showing the typical trail condition and
slope profile carved into the gulch is provided on Plate 3 The gulch side slopes appear to
be composed of sequential flows of a a lava including interbedded rubbly clinker and
dense hard rock materials Because the foot trail was carved into the slope a rock
overhang of several feet is present above the trail The roof overhang provides some
limited shelter from possible upslope falling rock
Based on our observations the ground crack spans approximately 70 linear feet in
the vicinity of two existing White Moho trees identified as Tree Nos 1 and 2 on Plate 4
The existing trees are growing along the outside edge of the trail Because the ground
crack approaches Tree No 2 the young tree was cut near the base by DLNR to reduce
the potential for additional ground levering and slope failure around the base of the tree
We observed the trail floor being overgrown with grass due to the presence of a thin near
surface soil horizon The crack alignment and associated narrow ground depression

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The Island of Hawaii is within an active seismic zone In general earthquakes
that occur throughout the world are caused by shifting tectonic plates In contrast
earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic activity Earthquakes in
Hawaii also result from the underground movement of magma that comes close to the
surface but does not erupt The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes
each year but most are so small that they can only be detected by sensitive seismic
monitoring instruments However some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt
and a few cause structural damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on
and near the Island of Hawaii Earthquakes directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes
Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii are related to the movement of
underground magma the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground shaking
diminishes with increased distance away from the active volcanoes at the southern and
eastern portions of the Island of Hawaii
Based on a review of available United States Geological Survey USGS
geologic map information there are multiple mapped fault traces associated with the
Kohala Volcano summit caldera southeast of the project vicinity The USGS data
maintained by the Hawaii Volcano Observatory HVO show occasional light to

Rockslide Hazard
Falling rocks are a special category of natural hazard within the large family of
gravitationally driven phenomena called landslides Rockfall events may involve various
modes of detachment and movement including detachment from the parent rock outcrop
individual boulder movement raveling of loose rock material from steep slope faces
toppling of rock outcrops and rock slides Rockfall is the fastest type of landslide and is
primarily caused by the loss of support and detachment of individual components of the
rock mass under the influence of gravity Complex interactions between the physical
parameters of both the rock and the slope may cause the falling rocks to move down the
slope in a high velocity seemingly random and erratic manner Rockfall is a natural and
on going process that occurs in steep terrain Rockfall source areas may be defined by
topographically high regions where the rock mass may contain discontinuities such as
open fractures undercuts or inclined bedding surfaces capable of producing sliding
action In addition source areas may include loose surface boulder deposits where
reactivation of movement occurs due to loss of support on steep slopes or the impartation
of an external force to initiate movement

Construction Considerations
Based on the location and geometry of the canyon project site access and staging
for construction equipment is difficult and would likely be costly as indicated by the sketch
on Plate 2 The floor of the canyon should not be used for construction staging due to the
high potential for flash flooding and dangerous falling rock conditions There is some
slightly wider trail terrain and less severe slopes that could be used for construction
staging at the entrance to the canyon on the northern slope
In addition construction work along the edge of the narrow trail in the canyon is
dangerous and requires experienced personnel and specialized tools and equipment to
perform the work Workers would also be exposed to potential falling rock conditions and
rapidly changing weather and slope runoff conditions
Conceptual Repair Options
To accommodate the very difficult construction access and limited available
construction staging area we envision that construction equipment and materials would
likely be mobilized and demobilized by helicopter Based on our visual observation and
schematic engineering analysis we believe that the failure wedge and observed fissure on
the trail floor may extend to depths ranging between about 10 and 15 feet below the
ground surface Based on our schematic engineering analysis we believe that an
anchored wire mesh system may be viable to restrain the potential failure wedge against
the steep V shape canyon wall Other repair alternatives such as a tieback anchor
retaining wall may be considered but may not be as cost effective as the anchored wire
mesh system
The anchored wire mesh system is an active slope stabilization system where an
alloyed high tensile steel wire mesh is anchored into the slope face by applying a defined
torque force over the soil rock anchors and associated spike plates The soil rock anchors
consist of commercially available rock anchor bars that are drilled and grouted in a pattern
array on the slope face
The slope to be anchored with the steel wire mesh should be trimmed of thick
vegetation or trees and scaled of loose materials prior to the installation of the anchored
wire mesh system The primary intent of this stabilization system is to closely pin the

flexible steel wire mesh to the slope face as a retention system Thus the anchored wire
mesh installation stabilizes the outer most horizon of earth materials which are typically
responsible for most shallow seated and thin skinned type slope failures
Once the steel wire mesh is fastened to the slope by the rock anchors the mesh is
pre stressed with a defined force against the soil rock anchors utilizing special spike
plates Reacting to the tension of the anchors the steel wire mesh presses on the slope
surface and acts to reduce the potential for appreciable slope deformation and slippage
preventing breakouts from occurring
A detailed anchored wire mesh system design is not available at this conceptual
study stage The anchored wire mesh system should be designed with topographic
surveying support to determine lateral extents in the longitudinal and vertical directions A
preliminary estimated construction cost was performed for the described anchored wire
mesh system The preliminary estimated construction cost is on the order of about
