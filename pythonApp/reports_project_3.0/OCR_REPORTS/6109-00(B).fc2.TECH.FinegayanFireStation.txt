SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling nine borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 9 extending to depths ranging from about 1 5 to
7 9 meters below the existing ground surface Two falling head field percolation tests were
also conducted in the future detention pond area
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site generally is underlain by
limestone formations Where surface soil fill was encountered the material generally
consisted of medium dense to dense silty limestone gravel about 0 15 meter thick
The surface soil fill was underlain by soft in terms of rock hardness description to
medium hard limestone formations extending to the maximum depths explored of about
7 9 meters below the ground surface The limestone formations generally appeared to be
fractured and slightly weathered to relatively unweathered It should be noted that the
strength characteristics of limestone formations are highly variable The depositional
nature of the formation creates localized areas of hard crystalline limestones inter mixed
with soft severely fractured detrital limestones The limestone formations are frequently
cavernous or contain pockets of voids
We did not encounter groundwater in any of the borings drilled for this study
However groundwater levels would be subject to change due to seasonal precipitation
surface water runoff and other factors Additionally it should be noted that water seepage
is commonly encountered in limestone formations due to the infiltration of surface water

Earthquakes and Seismic Hazards
Guam is situated adjacent to the subduction zone that forms the Marianas Trench
The island is tectonically active and has experienced approximately 20 earthquakes
exceeding Magnitude 7 0 since 1902 Of these events fifteen occurred prior to 1950 and
five have occurred subsequent to that date Several large faults and fault zones that
traverse across the Island of Guam include the following Pago Bay Adelup Point Fault
River Valley Fault Zone Tamuning Barrigada Fault Pugua Fault
and Mount Santa Rosa horst block
The proximity of the project site to faults and fault zones is a major consideration
when evaluating geologic and seismic hazards Of the major faults and fault zones
mentioned above the trace of the Pugua Fault lies closest to the project site at
approximately 8 kilometers km to the west of the project site The Pugua Fault stretches
from off shore near Uruno Point and extends south to about Taguac Several minor faults
located about 3 km south of the project site are also present
Based on our evaluation of the project site we do not believe that significant
geologic and seismic hazards would impact the proposed fire station development With
the exception of moderate to strong ground shaking we believe that the potential for
surface fault rupture ground failure liquefaction lateral spreading and ground lurching
seiches and seismically induced flooding are considered to be low at this project site

Liquefaction Potential
Liquefaction is a phenomenon during which loose saturated cohesionless soils
such as sand and silt temporarily lose their shear strengths during ground shaking
induced by severe earthquakes Significant factors known to affect the liquefaction
potential of soils are the characteristics of the materials such as grain size distribution
and relative density the plasticity of the fine grained soils the degree of saturation the
initial stresses overburden pressures acting on the soils and the characteristics of the
earthquake such as the intensity and duration of ground shaking
As previously discussed we expect that the proposed fire station site would
experience moderate to strong ground shaking from one of the nearby earthquake faults
during the design life of the project We did not encounter groundwater in the borings
drilled for this study In addition relatively intact limestone was found at relatively shallow
depths The limestone formations below the groundwater level were expected to be
dense therefore we believe that the phenomenon of soil liquefaction is not a design
consideration for this project site The risk for potential liquefaction is considered extremely
low at this project site based on the subsurface conditions encountered

Where surface soil fill was encountered the thickness of the fill material was less than
about 0 15 meters Considering the medium hard to hard limestone formations
encountered at the project site it is our opinion that a shallow foundation system

New Building Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we recommend
utilizing shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous strip footings to
support the new building structures For design of footings we recommend utilizing an
allowable bearing pressure of up to for foundations bearing on new
engineered fills or bearing directly on the underlying limestone material The bearing
pressure provided above is for supporting dead plus live loads and may be increased by
up to 50 percent for supporting transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces
Footings should have a minimum width of 500 mm and the bottom of footings
should extend a minimum of 500 mm below the lowest adjacent finished grade For
footings bearing on the underlying limestone formation the footing bottom should be
relatively level and clear of sharp points that may result in concentrated loading of the
foundations
Footings adjacent to planned retaining walls or other below grade structures should
be embedded deep enough to avoid surcharging the retaining wall foundations or
below grade structure Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should extend
below a 45 degree imaginary plane projecting upward from the bottom edge of the utility
trench or the footing should extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines
This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation
settlement
If the footings are designed and constructed in strict accordance with the
recommendations presented herein we estimate total settlements of footings to be on the
order of about 13 mm or less with differential settlements between adjacent footings
supported on similar materials to be on the order of about 6 mm
Due to the potential for presence of cavities as commonly encountered in limestone
deposits we recommend implementing a program of probing and grouting of the
foundation locations during construction Probing and grouting requirements are discussed
further in the following Foundation Probing section

Foundation Probing
Because of the potential for presence of solution cavities within the limestone
formation at the project site we recommend implementing a program of probing and
grouting the foundations during construction For estimating purposes we recommend
drilling probe holes at 3 meters on centers for the continuous strip footings In addition
probe holes should be drilled at each isolated spread footing location one probe per
4 5 square meters The probe holes should be at least 75 mm in diameter and should
extend to a depth of at least 3 meters below the planned bottom of footing elevation
If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operations
additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacing to help delineate the vertical and
lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes and cavities discovered should be
backfilled with Controlled Low Strength Material CLSM with a slump of about 150 mm to
225 mm The CLSM should be injected pumped at low to moderate pump pressures
As an alternate to CLSM sand cement grout with a slump of about 150 mm to 225 mm
may also be used for the grouting operations
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
qualified geotechnical engineer If excess grout take presence of cavities or voids and
changed conditions are observed additional recommendations may be required to modify
the foundation design
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend obtaining unit prices for additional probing and grouting during bidding In
addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and grouting
operation is complete The contractor should be made aware that a longer lag time
between probing grouting operations and foundation construction might be required in the
construction schedule

low to low swelling potential Therefore we anticipate that the presence of expansive soil
is likely not a design consideration for this project site
In general we recommend scarifying the subgrade soils if exposed at the
subgrade level to a minimum depth of about 200 mm moisture conditioning to above the
optimum moisture content and recompacting to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction The slab subgrades should be kept moist until the final placement of cushion
fill and slab concrete Where limestone material is exposed and scarification of the
subgrades is not practical the subgrade soils should be proof rolled with a minimum
9 metric ton vibratory drum roller for a minimum of eight passes to help detect and
collapse near surface cavities and or voids
For interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic or machinery vibration
we recommend placing a minimum 100 mm thick layer of cushion fill consisting of
open graded gravel ASTM C 33 Size No 67 below the slabs The open graded gravel
cushion fill would provide uniform support of the slabs and would serve as a capillary
moisture break To reduce the potential for excessive future moisture infiltration through
the slab and subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture barrier 15 mil
puncture resistant membrane should be provided on top of the gravel cushion fill layer
Interior walls should be designed to incorporate some flexibility to accommodate a small
amount of possible ground movements
Where the slabs will be subjected to machinery vibration we recommend providing
a 150 mm layer of aggregate subbase below the slabs in lieu of the 100 mm thick gravel
cushion fill layer The aggregate subbase should consist of crushed aggregates
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Exterior flatwork such as the walkways around the facility should consist of at least
100 mm thick concrete supported on 100 mm of cushion fill consisting of open graded
gravel These exterior flatwork slabs should have no routine vehicular traffic traversing the
walkway section except for occasional emergency vehicles The thickened edges of slabs
adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at least 300 mm below the lowest
adjacent grade

After clearing and grubbing areas at grade or areas designated to receive fills

Imported fill materials required for site filling should consist of select granular fill
materials such as crushed coral and basaltic gravel The material should be well graded
from coarse to fine with particles no greater than 75 mm in largest dimension The
material should have a California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 25 or higher and a swell
value of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883 The material
should also contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve
Fill and backfill materials required for the project construction should be placed in
level lifts not exceeding 200 mm in loose thickness moisture conditioned to within
2 percent of the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction Compaction requirements for the subgrades of improvements
subjected to vehicular traffic such as the access road driveway and parking lot areas
should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Based on the current site grading plan some rock excavations would be required
especially for utility installation The results of the field exploration program indicated that
the limestone formations were generally soft to medium hard near the ground surface and
moderately weathered and fractured It is our opinion that mass excavations into this
limestone material could be accomplished by regular earthmoving equipment and
excavators It is possible that localized fresh hard rock areas may require ripping tool
such as a Caterpillar D 9 tractor with a single tooth ripper or jack hammering
