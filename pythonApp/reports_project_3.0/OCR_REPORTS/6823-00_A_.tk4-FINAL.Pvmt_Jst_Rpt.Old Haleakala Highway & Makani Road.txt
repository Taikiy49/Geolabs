SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration along the majority of the proposed roadway reconstruction 
project alignments generally encountered very stiff to hard clayey silts extending to the 
maximum depth explored of approximately 6.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the shallow borings drilled at the time of our field 
exploration. 
Based on the poor conditions of the existing pavements, we recommend 
reconstructing the entire Old Haleakala Highway and Makani Road within the limits under 
consideration for this project with a new pavement section.  Because the project will 
involve the reconstruction of the existing roadways, we have performed a detailed 
economic comparison involving both flexible and rigid pavement sections in accordance 
with the latest guidelines provided by the State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation, 
Highways Division, Material Testing and Research Branch. 
Based on our field exploration program and the current design concept, we envision 
that the new pavements will be constructed over the in-situ clayey silt soils in the shallow 
subsurface.  Based on the anticipated subgrade conditions, the traffic data provided for our 
pavement analyses, the economic analysis performed, and construction considerations, it 
is our opinion that new pavements for the roadway reconstruction project may consist of 
(a) 3 inches of AC over 8 inches of ACB on 6 inches of AB for Lower Old Haleakala 
Highway extending from Aeloa Road to Pukalani Street; (b) 3 inches of AC over 6 inches 
of ACB on 6 inches of AB for Upper Old Haleakala Highway extending from Pukalani 
Street to Kula Highway; and (c) 2.5 inches of AC over 4 inches of ACB on 6 inches of AB 
for Makani Road extending from Old Haleakala Highway to Haleakala Highway Bypass. It 
should be noted that CBR tests and/or field observations should be performed on the 
actual subgrade soils during construction to confirm that the above pavement design 
sections are adequate. 
We recommend placing a layer of reinforcing geogrid (such as Tensar TriAx Grid, 
TX7 or equivalent) on a layer of permeable separator (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) below the 
new pavement structural section.  The reinforcing geogrid would serve to enhance the 
R-value of the subgrade materials by providing stabilization and reinforcement to the 
underlying base/subbase/subgrade materials. 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to 
saturation of the base, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the pavement surface

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This pavement justification report presents the results of our geotechnical 
engineering analyses performed for the Old Haleakala Highway and Makani Road 
Pavement Reconstruction project in Makawao on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project 
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the design of new pavements only. The findings and recommendations presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The roadway reconstruction project is along Old Haleakala Highway from 
Aeloa Road to Kula Highway and along Makani Road from Old Haleakala Highway to 
Haleakala Highway Bypass for a total distance of approximately 2.12 miles. The 
approximate limits of the roadway reconstruction project are shown on the General Site 
Plan, Plate 2. 
We understand that the proposed pavement project will be federally funded. 
Therefore, a pavement justification report conforming to the requirements and 
guidelines of the State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation will be required. The 
new pavements will be designed based on the methodology described in the Pavement 
Design Manual dated March 2002 prepared by the State of Hawaii - Department of 
Transportation, Highways Division, Materials Testing and Research Branch. 
Special attention was given to the potential presence of near-surface soft and/or 
loose soils, which may be a contributing factor to the pavement failures observed along 
the roadways under consideration. The findings obtained from our field exploration and 
laboratory testing programs will be used as a basis to formulate geotechnical 
engineering recommendations for the pavement reconstruction for the project 
roadways.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes, the older West Maui 
(Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala (Pleistocene 
Epoch).  The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain located between these 
two volcanoes.  The project site is situated on the isthmus connecting the two volcanic 
domes. 
The isthmus was built by the deposition of sediments over the underlying basalts 
of the two volcanoes.  During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fell to stands lower than 
present sea level exposing large areas of coralline sand on what was the ocean bottom. 
Wind action transported these coralline sands inland forming extensive areas of sand 
dunes.  In some areas, these dunes have been mantled by man-made fill which was 
apparently placed during the period that the area was used for the cultivation of sugar 
cane. 
2.2 
Existing Site/Pavement Conditions 
The existing Old Haleakala Highway and Makani Road are generally a two-lane 
undivided roadway within the project limits of Old Haleakala Highway from the 
intersection of Aeloa Road to Kula Highway; as well as Makani Road from the 
intersection of Old Haleakala Highway to the new Haleakala Highway Bypass. The 
approximate project limits of the roadway are shown on the General Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The shoulder areas beyond the edge of the pavement generally consist of short 
cut grass, sidewalks in some areas, traffic signs and utility posts. The surface soils 
along the shoulder areas below the vegetation generally consist of brown clayey silts 
and patches of highly weathered rock formation exposed in the roadway cuts. The 
existing Old Haleakala Highway roadway alignment slopes downward from south to 
north from the intersection with Kula Highway to the intersection with Aeloa Road. The 
Makani Road roadway alignment generally slopes downward from east to west from the 
intersection with the new Haleakala Highway Bypass to the intersection with 
Old Haleakala Highway. Our office conducted a site reconnaissance in January 2014 to

3.1 
Methodology of Pavement Design 
The pavement structural section presented herein was generally determined 
based on the methodology described in Chapter 3 of the Pavement Design Manual 
dated March 2002. The Pavement Design Manual was prepared by the State of Hawaii 
- Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Materials Testing and Research 
Branch. The pavement design methodology is based on the Hveem Stabilometer 
method developed and used by the California Department of Transportation (Caltrans). 
The design procedures for rigid pavements are based on the design procedures of the 
Portland Cement Association “Thickness Design for Concrete Highway and Street 
Pavements.”

Our field exploration and laboratory testing indicate that the roadway alignments 
under consideration for this project are underlain by clayey silt soils.  We understand 
that placement of asphalt concrete base (ACB) or Portland Cement Concrete (PCC) 
directly on the clayey silt subgrade is not recommended.  Therefore, the pavement 
section should consist of an aggregate subbase (ASB) layer between the upper ACB or 
PCC and the underlying clayey silt subgrade soils. 
Due to the substantial number of roadway intersections and the long length of the 
roadway alignments, we envision that new pavements for the project would need to be 
constructed in phases.  Because of the poorly graded nature of the permeable base 
material, we envision that the material could not be compacted into a dense and stable 
mass.  Our experience suggested that the permeable base material would tend to 
unravel or spill out of the completed pavement section when excavation is made for the 
adjacent section. Therefore, incorporation of a permeable base layer for drainage 
purpose would not be practical from a construction standpoint and was not included in 
our pavement analyses.

we have determined the following flexible and rigid pavement design sections for the 
proposed Old Haleakala Highway and Makani Road Pavement Reconstruction project.

The exposed clayey silt subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of about

scarification process and to confirm whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar

Aggregate base course materials required for the project should consist of 
crushed basaltic aggregates with a 1.5-inch maximum nominal size and should conform 
to Subsection 703.06 of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for Road and 
Bridge Construction (2005).  Aggregate base material should be moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with AASHTO T-180 or ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Asphalt concrete base (ACB) material should consist of asphalt-treated basalt 
aggregate, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and 
compacted to no less than 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity 
determined in accordance with AASHTO T-209 or ASTM D2041. Asphaltic concrete 
(AC) material should be constructed in accordance with Section 401 Asphalt Concrete 
Pavement of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge 
Construction (2005) and its amendments.

3.5 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development 
of good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage 
structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration 
or premature failure of the pavements.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway alignments by drilling 
and sampling twelve borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 12. The borings 
extended to depths of about 4.6 to 6.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.5. The 
borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Rock 
materials were described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as 
shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2. The Rock Description System is based on 
the publication “Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in 
Rock Masses” by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977).  Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-12. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.
