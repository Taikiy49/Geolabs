2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Maui is the second largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago with
an area of approximately 728 square miles This island was formed by the activity of
two volcanoes the older West Maui Volcano Tertiary Epoch and the more recent East
Maui Volcano also known as Haleakala Pleistocene Epoch The Isthmus of Maui is
the narrow gently sloping plain located between these two volcanoes
The project site is on the southeastern flank of the Haleakala Shield Volcano in
the District of Hana Haleakala Volcano generally was built in three distinguishable
series of lava flows The lower unit the Honomanu Volcanic Series late Pliocene early
Pleistocene Epoch consists of thinly bedded tholeiitic basaltic pahoehoe and a a lava
flows These lavas tend to be very permeable and carry basal water Overlying this unit
is the Kula Volcanic Series late Pleistocene Epoch composed primarily of thicker
alkalic a a flows which may contain many inter stratified thin ash soil layers
A long quiescent period with few eruptions followed during which time canyons
were carved in the volcano due to eustatic changes higher and lower stands of the sea
level At the end of the resting period copious flows erupted along the southwest and
east rifts This is the third and upper rock unit known as the Hana Volcanic Series
Holocene Epoch The lavas from this volcanic series range in composition from
ultra basic olivine porphyries to non porphyritic alkalic basalts
2 2 Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in
the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or
during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The Island of
Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most are so small that

2 3 Existing Site Conditions
The project site is at Milepost 44 95 along Hana Highway in the District of Hana
on the Island of Maui Hawaii The existing bridge which spans across Paihi Stream is
a one lane single span concrete structure paved with asphaltic concrete The bridge is
approximately 43 feet in length and about 18 feet in width The bridge abutments and
wing walls are of cement rubble masonry CRM and concrete construction
The cut slopes along Hana Highway in the project vicinity and below the existing
bridge abutments exposed weathered basalt formation and clinker Slight to moderate
vegetation covered portions of the stream banks toward the south Paihi Stream in the
vicinity of the existing bridge includes a waterfall and plunge pool
Based on the topographic survey prepared by ControlPoint Surveying Inc the
existing ground surface at the roadway level ranges from about 208 to 210 feet MSL
and the streambed varies from about 15 to 60 feet below the existing roadway grade
Based on measurements performed by ControlPoint Surveying Inc on
May 15 2007 the north abutment structure was bearing on a basalt rock ledge
estimated to be approximately 19 feet thick top elevation is approximately 201 feet
MSL and bottom elevation is approximately 182 feet MSL Clinker material cemented
basaltic cobbles gravel and sand in a medium dense to dense condition was observed
below the basalt ledge extending down to the existing streambed which is at an
elevation of approximately 153 feet MSL
The clinker material below the basalt rock ledge has scoured creating an
overhang condition of the basalt rock ledge The clinker material has scoured a
maximum of about 17 feet laterally from the face of the basalt rock ledge The overhang
condition extends upstream by more than half of the bridge width measured from the
downstream edge of the bridge
The existing bridge has been closed to the public by the County of Maui due to
the potential instability of the basalt rock ledge supporting the north abutment structure

3 3 Stream Channel Material Scour Analysis
One of the most common causes of bridge failure stems from scouring of bridge
foundations from flood or other water flow damage Therefore the foundation design of
the bridge abutments will need to take into consideration the potential for
stream channel scour
Scour is the result of erosive action of flowing water excavating and carrying
away material from the bed and banks of streams and or channels Total scour over a
period of time generally consists of three components 1 Aggradation and
Degradation 2 Contraction Scour and 3 Local Scour The rates of scour depend on a
number of factors such as the shape and dimensions of a pier or abutment depth of
flow velocity of approach flow size and gradation of stream channel bed material and
bed configuration

stream channel below the abutment The vertical face of the clinker extended to the

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the potential for build up of
hydrostatic pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide
zone of permeable material such as drain rock AASHTO M43 Size No 67

The subsurface conditions at the proposed replacement bridge site were
explored by drilling and sampling three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3
extending to depths ranging from about 52 1 to 102 1 feet below the existing ground
surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with

on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1
through A 3
Soil samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description

quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Five Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on selected
rock core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the rock
formation encountered The unconfined compression test results are presented on
Plate B 1
Four Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on
Plate B 2
