REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is situated on the southwestern flank of Kohala Mountain which is the
oldest of the five shield volcanoes forming the Island of Hawaii Kohala Mountain was built
during the Pleistocene Epoch by basaltic lava flows from the Polou and Hawi Volcanic
Series Typically the lava flows of the Polou Volcanic Series are composed of theolitic
olivine basaltic lava flows grading to oceanite In general such flows are characterized as
thinly bedded with a high degree of porosity
Basalt is considered to be relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted through
the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and along
clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they serve as
the major water transmission features
Coral formation embedded with lagoonal sediments and beach sand deposits typically
overlie the basalt formations at the project site A surficial layer of man made fill was placed
over these deposits for the development of Kawaihae Harbor within the last century The
filling activities have brought the land area around Kawaihae Harbor to its present form

FIELD EXPLORATION
The subsurface conditions on land at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths of
approximately 20 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers The subsurface conditions in
the area of the new floating pier were explored by drilling and sampling two off shore borings
designated as Boring Nos 3 and 4 extending to depths of about 40 feet below the sea water
level The borings were drilled with portable drilling equipment equipped with coring tools
using the rotary wash drilling method The approximate locations of the borings drilled are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on the Boring Log Legend in Appendix A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 4 of Appendix A
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock or coral materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D
2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method
which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow
core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description

Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration indicates that the project site is generally underlain by loose to
medium dense coralline deposits embedded with lagoonal and beach sand deposits
throughout the depths explored Based on the subsurface soil conditions encountered at the
project site and lateral loading anticipated we believe that 12 inch square or 16 5 inch
octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles may be used to support the new floating pier
The piles should be embedded a minimum of 25 feet into the mud line and supported on
medium dense coralline deposits

Lateral Load Resistance
Lateral load resistance for driven piles is a function of the stiffness of the surrounding
soil the stiffness of the pile allowable deflection at the top of the pile and the induced
moment in the pile Based on the subsurface information and the lateral load anticipated
foundation analyses using the computer program LPILE PLUS 3 0 for Windows was
performed to evaluate the top of pile deflections
The program LPILE PLUS 3 0 for Windows is a microcomputer adaptation of a finite
difference laterally loaded deep foundation program originally developed at the University
of Texas at Austin The program solves for deflection and bending moment along a deep
foundation under lateral loads as a function of depth The analysis was carried out with the
use of non linear p y curves to represent soil moduli at various depths The analyses were
performed for a laterally loaded 12 inch square and 16 5 inch octagonal precast prestressed
concrete pile with a free head boundary condition at the top of the pile Based on the lateral
load anticipated of 3 to 6 kips per pile the results of our lateral load analyses are presented

Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a rated energy of
about 30 000 foot pounds of energy per blow The hammer should be equipped with an
energy control level such that driving within the loose soft soils may be controlled at a lower
energy to reduce tensile stress development within the piles which could damage or cause
cracking of the piles
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the matching of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be driven
This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy to the top of the
pile and also reducing damage to the piles Geolabs should review the pile driving
equipment proposed to evaluate whether the equipment is capable of installing the piles to
the required tip elevations The Contractor should forward pile and driving equipment data

Construction Considerations
The piles should be driven to the depth where piles have adequate embedment below
the mud line in order to achieve adequate lateral resistance The driving criteria should be
determined after selection of the pile driving equipment
Each pile should be driven continuously without interruption A pile may be rejected
or its lateral load capacity may be reduced when the driving resistance is interrupted for more
than 4 hours and the pile cannot be driven to the required depth During soft driving such
as when the pile tip is within soft loose soils the hammer stroke or energy should be
carefully controlled such that significant tensile stresses do not develop within the pile
Considering the relatively few number of piles for this project a test pile program may
not be performed
Retaining Structure Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions at the project site we believe that shallow
continuous footings may be used to support the retaining walls planned at the site In
general foundations may be designed based on an allowable bearing pressure of up to
3 000 pounds per square foot psf for footings bearing on the dense existing fill This
bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third for transient
loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
Foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded a minimum depth of
24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade For sloping ground conditions the
footing should be embedded deeper to obtain a minimum 6 foot setback distance measured
horizontally from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope Footings oriented
parallel to the direction of the slope should be constructed in stepped footings
Foundations located next to utility ttenches or easements should be embedded below
a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or
as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging
adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential
for appreciable foundation settlement
lf soft and or loose materials are encountered at the footing subgrades the materials
they should be over excavated and replaced with compacted structural fill The
over excavation should be extended until the underlying dense materials are exposed The
over excavation should be backfilled with structural fill materials moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative

If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our

Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance

per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soils around the footings are
well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper
12 inches of soil should be neglected

Dynamic Lateral Earth Pressures
Since the project site is located on the Island of Hawaii seismically active dynamic
lateral earth pressures should be considered in the design of retaining structures Dynamic
lateral forces due to seismic loading may be estimated by 10H pounds per
lineal foot of wall for level backfill where H is the height of the wall in feet It should be noted
that the dynamic lateral forces provided assume that the wall will be allowed to move
laterally by up to about 2 to 4 inches in the event of an earthquake If this amount of lateral
movement is not acceptable the retaining wall should be designed with significantly higher
dynamic lateral forces for a restrained condition
The dynamic lateral earth pressures presented above are in addition to the static
lateral earth pressures An appropriately reduced factor of safety may be used when
dynamic lateral earth pressures are accounted for in the design of the retaining structure
Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic pressures
A typical drainage system consists of 1 cubic foot of permeable material such as
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation wrapped with filter fabric at each of the
weephole locations The weepholes should be spaced not more than 6 feet apart Unless
covered by concrete or asphaltic concrete the upper 12 inches of the wall backfill should
consist of relatively impervious materials such as the on site materials to reduce water
infiltration behind the walls
