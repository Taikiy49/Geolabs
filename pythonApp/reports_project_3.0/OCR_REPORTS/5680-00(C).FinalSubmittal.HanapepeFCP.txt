The following sections of this geotechnical analysis report present the basis of the
geotechnical engineering evaluation conducted on the I Wall type flood walls at the Hanapepe
Flood Control Project FCP located in Hanapepe on the Island of Kauai Hawaii The
geotechnical engineering evaluation conducted was performed in accordance with the Phase II
Interim Guidance for Evaluating Existing I Walls prepared by the U S Army Corps of Engineers
USACE dated
1
Existing In Situ Soil Conditions and Parameters
The location and general vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1 The approximate project limits are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 A field
exploration program was conducted at the site to evaluate the existing subsurface
conditions and to determine the probable depths of the existing I Walls Our field
exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling two borings designated as Boring
Nos 1 and 2 behind the existing I Wall extending to depths of about 53 feet below the
existing ground surface The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2
In general the project site land side of the I Wall is underlain by approximately 6 feet of
very stiff fill materials consisting of clayey silts overlying dense river deposits consisting of
silty gravel extending to about 10 feet Soft to medium stiff estuarine deposits were
encountered below the river deposits extending to depths of about 15 to 17 feet Below the
estuarine deposits loose to medium dense silty sands and soft to medium stiff organic silts
were encountered extending to about 53 feet below the existing ground surface
Groundwater was encountered at depths of approximately 10 to 12 feet below the existing
ground surface during our field exploration The groundwater levels encountered
correspond to about Elevations 0 5 to 2 5 feet MSL Considering the proximity of the
project site to Hanapepe River and the Pacific Ocean groundwater levels likely will
fluctuate depending on factors such as tidal influences seasonal rainfall and precipitation
storm surge conditions and other factors
The field exploration methodology is described in Appendix A of this report Descriptions
and graphic representations of the materials encountered in the borings drilled are
presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2 Results of the laboratory tests
conducted on selected soil samples are presented in Appendix B
Following drilling of the two borings we installed 3 inch diameter PVC pipes and grouted
the annular space between the PVC pipes and bored holes for non destructive testing
The non destructive test selected was the parallel seismic testing to determine the
probable depths of the existing I Walls The parallel seismic testing was performed 7 days
after completion of the borings Based on the results of the parallel seismic testing the
probable depth of the sheet piles comprising the I Walls extends to about 20 feet MSL
Based on the subsurface information and laboratory test results the following idealized
soil profile and soil parameters were used in our analyses of the I Walls It should be noted
that we estimated the coefficient of permeability from the sieve analysis test results the

Existing I Wall Geometry and Dimensions
The existing I Wall is located along the southern bank of the existing Hanapepe River
flowing toward the Pacific Ocean The existing I Wall is about 183 feet in length and is
trapezoidal in shape when looking from plan view as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on a search for construction drawings we were able to obtain some drawings
related to the design of the I Walls from the County of Kauai Based on the available
drawings we understand the existing I Wall was constructed circa 1959 The original top
elevation of the I Wall was at about 16 feet MSL with the land side elevation at about
6 5 feet MSL Additional work was conducted on the existing I Wall in 1965 by adding a
2 foot concrete section on top of the original I Wall In addition a 6 foot thick embankment
was placed behind the I Wall at a slope
In addition two borings designated as Boring 1 and Boring 7F 7 shown on the available
construction drawings were drilled near the existing I Wall for the original construction in
1959 For ease of reference logs for Boring 1 and Boring 7F 7 are included as Plate 3
From the construction drawings the tip of the I Wall was shown at about 10 feet MSL
However the tip of the I Wall was estimated at about 20 feet MSL based on the parallel

I Wall Site Classification
Some construction drawings were available for our review but the information contains
limited or no loading history The subsurface conditions are fairly well defined based on the
two borings drilled and the laboratory tests conducted in support of this study Therefore
the project site may be classified as an Ordinary Site and the minimum factors of safety
for the Hanapepe FCP I Walls are based on the Ordinary Site classification
5
Depth of Crack
The existing I Wall was extended through Layer 1 dumped rock fill and Layer 2 organic
clayey silts into Layer 3 silty sands on the levee side river side Based on the Phase II
Interim Guidance for Evaluating Existing I Walls prepared by the USACE dated October
25 2006 the depth of crack was simplified and assumed to extend to the bottom of
Layer 2 organic clayey silts due to the undrained characteristics of the layer Therefore a
crack extending to the bottom of Layer 2 was assumed in our analyses for this site
6
Preliminary Geotechnical Evaluation
We performed 16 analysis cases from Case 1 through Case 16 considering various
water levels on the river side ranging from 9 5 feet MSL top of levee to 18 feet MSL
top of I Wall for two different I Wall tip elevations 10 and 20 feet MSL and two
different bottom of Hanapepe River elevations 4 5 and 12 5 feet MSL

Seepage Piping and Uplift
The evaluation for seepage is in general accordance with the applicable portions of
Design Guidance of Levees Design Guidance
for Levee Underseepage May 01 2005 and EM Seepage Analysis
and Control for Dams April 1993
The seepage analyses were performed using the computer program Phase2
version 6 from RocScience Inc The model for the seepage analyses was
established using the finite element method to simulate water seepage in a
saturated steady state flow condition under higher water levels on the levee side
river side
Because relatively impervious organic clayey silts undrained soils were
encountered over the relatively pervious silty sand free draining materials a gap

I Wall Sheet Piling Tip Penetration
The minimum sheet piling tip penetration requirements are summarized as follows
2 5 times the exposed height of the floodwall on the land side of the I Wall
10 feet below the lower ground elevation
Based on the existing levee and I Wall geometry and dimensions the existing
I Wall with a 10 feet or 20 feet MSL tip elevation met the two minimum tip
penetration requirements
The computer program SPW911 from Pile Buck Inc was used to assess the I Wall
rotational and flexural failure potential In general the I Wall external stability
analysis was conducted in accordance with EM Design of Sheet Pile
Walls March 1994
As mentioned previously a 5 to 15 foot wide and 6 foot thick earth embankment
with slope was constructed and placed over the original ground surface in
1965 Due to limitations of the computer program this earth embankment was
modified to a 3 foot thick 4H 1V sloping ground condition without the 5 to15 foot
wide crest in our external stability analysis model This model would yield more
conservative results lower than the actual safety factors than the actual
conditions Results of the I Wall penetration analyses also are summarized on
Plate 5 for reference Results of the individual I Wall tip penetration analysis are
presented in Appendix D

Global Slope Stability
Global slope stability of the existing levee combined with the I Wall was evaluated
using the computer program ReSSA from ADAMA Engineering Inc The global
slope stability analyses take into consideration the additional water pressure
applied on the levee side to evaluate the rotational slope failure mechanism
Based on the results of the global slope stability analyses the existing levee
combined with the I Wall has a minimum factor of safety of 6 5 which is greater
than the required factor of safety of 1 6 for the Ordinary Site classification for
global slope stability Results of the global slope stability analyses are summarized
on Plate 5 for reference
It should be noted that the global slope stability analyses assessed two possible
failure circles 1 one potential failure circle exiting the slope on the levee side and
2 one potential failure circle exiting the slope on the land side because both sides
of the I Wall have sloping ground conditions The toe of the critical slip circle exits
the levee side when the water level on the levee side is low However the toe of
the critical slip circle exits the land side embankment when the water level on the
levee side is higher Details of the critical slip circles for the 16 cases analyzed are
presented in Appendix E
6 4
Wall Height and Cantilever Height Verification
Based on the information from the available construction drawings and our
observations and field measurements during our site reconnaissance we do not
believe that the existing I Wall and the embankment have experienced appreciable
differential settlement Wall heights and cantilever heights measured were in
general agreement with the information shown on the available construction
drawings of 1959 and 1965 Please refer to the photographs of the I Wall and levee
in Appendix F for reference
Several near vertical cracks spaced at about 10 to 15 feet apart were observed in
the original concrete cap portion of the I Wall as shown in the photographs in
Appendix F It is believed that these cracks likely were caused by concrete
shrinkage because construction joints were not observed in the original concrete
portion of the I Wall The 2 foot high concrete wall extension constructed on top of
the original I Wall was formed with construction joints extending above the cracks
6 5

Conclusions
Due to relatively thick blanket layer present on top of the free draining materials within the
existing I Wall penetration depth excessive seepage piping and heaving may occur in a
flood event when the water level on the levee side reaches 12 5 feet MSL or higher
8
Limitations of Analyses
The analyses and conclusions submitted herein are based in part upon information
obtained from the subsurface conditions shown in the construction documents as listed in
the references and the two borings drilled for this study Variations of the subsurface
conditions between and beyond the field borings may occur and the nature and extent of
these variations may not become evident until further investigation is underway If
variations then appear evident it will be necessary to re evaluate the analyses and
conclusions provided in this report

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling two
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 The borings were drilled with a
truck mounted drill rig at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered during drilling are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 1
through A 2 2
Soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general
accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches a total of 12 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer and torvane shear tests were performed on selected
cohesive soil samples retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an
indication of the unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample The torvane shear
test provided a quick estimate of the undrained shear strength of the soil sample
Results of the pocket penetrometer tests and the torvane shear tests are presented on
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight determinations ASTM D 2937 were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Three Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples of the
soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1 The test results also are summarized
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Six sieve analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 were performed on selected samples
retrieved from the borings to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on Plates B 2
and B 3
Two Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the in situ soils The test results are presented
on Plates B 4 and B 5
Three unconsolidated undrained triaxial compression TXUU tests ASTM Test
Designation D 2850 were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear
strength of the silty and clayey soils encountered The approximate in situ effective overburden
pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the relatively undisturbed soil
sample The test results and the stress strain curves are presented on Plates B 6 through B 8

This report summarizes the results of Non Destructive Testing NDT integrity testing
services performed at the above referenced site on May 31 2007 The integrity testing was
required to assess the depth of flood walls a k a I walls for the Hanapepe River Flood Control
Project The integrity testing was conducted using the parallel seismic PS method to estimate
the depth to the tip of the sheeting
A 3 component geophone down hole package BHG 3 manufactured by Geostuff and
an exploration seismograph RAS 24 manufactured by Seistronix were used to carry out the
PS integrity testing The RAS 24 records digitizes and processes seismic signals for further
evaluation in the field and or office refer to Appendix A for literature and details on the PS
integrity testing method
Background and Site Description
The site is located along the Hanapepe River in Kauai Hawaii The overall project
involves the analysis of the stability of the I wall system Two boreholes designated as B 1
and B 2 were completed along the South side of the I Wall The PS integrity testing was
performed in both boreholes to estimate the length of the steel sheeting portion of the I wall at
these two specific locations
I Wall Construction Details
We understand that the I wall system was constructed using the following typical
procedure The I wall sheeting was driven into the ground to approximately elevation 10 feet
and capped with a concrete wall tapering to approximately elevation 18 feet A compacted fill
berm approximately 6 feet in depth was put in place on the South side of the I wall system
above the original ground surface

Instrumentation and Testing Procedure
The I wall concrete surface was utilized for impact points for using a 10 lb hammer
with a trigger switch Each test consisted of striking the concrete surface several consecutive
times until consistent blows were recorded These signals were then averaged and stored in the
RAS 24 After testing was completed the data was reviewed in the field and permanently
saved for further analysis The seismic signals record with channel numbers a k a
Reflectogram for the PS analyses are presented in Appendix B
Analysis and Conclusions
The seismic signal records were magnified and filtering was applied to the signals in
order to enhance the data interpretation Our major findings and conclusions are summarized
below as follows refer to the Appendix A for definitions and meanings of the PS interpretation
and Appendix B for the reflectograms
1 We reviewed the data for responses that 1 may be a result of the reflection from the tip
of the I wall or 2 occurred prior to these anticipated lengths
2 The test results for B 1 were fairly conclusive and we were able to determine the
estimated depth of the I wall We were not able to obtain conclusive test results for B 2
due to cultural interference and or change in soil conditions adjacent to this particular
borehole
3 The estimated depth to the tip of the I Wall appears to be approximately 30 to 32 5
from the existing ground surface at the elevation 10 feet mark The PS signal
responses for B 1 appeared to be consistent with the as built drawings
The PS integrity testing method is based on low strain stress wave propagation through
a sound section of the superstructure that is in good contact with the foundation element
homogeneous soil matrix adjacent to the borehole and a significant change in the rate of the
seismic signal below the foundation element Although defects i e large voids and bulges can
sometimes be identified along the edge of the foundation element the primary use for the PS
integrity testing method is to assess the depth of a foundation element
The PS testing results should not be used as the sole judgment for assessing the stability
of the I wall system Other field observations should be evaluated in conjunction with PS test
results including construction procedures methods observations and any other pertinent
information pertaining the construction of the I wall system All available information should
be used to comprehensively evaluate the stability of the I wall system

Appendix A Parallel Seismic Method
Introduction
The Parallel Seismic PS method allows for the determination of the length of deep
foundations when not accessible due to access constraints with an existing
superstructure above the foundation
Procedure
A sensor i e Hydrophone or triaxial geophone package is lowered to the bottom of a
borehole adjacent to the foundation and the superstructure is struck with a 10 lb
hammer with a trigger switch attached A seismic signal transmits through the
superstructure and into the foundation element The signal is recorded into a data
acquisition system to evaluate and store the arrival times The sensor is raised from
the bottom of the borehole at a pre determined interval until a significant change in the
rate of the seismic signal The output of the test is a stacked graph of time against
depth a k a reflectogram as shown below
