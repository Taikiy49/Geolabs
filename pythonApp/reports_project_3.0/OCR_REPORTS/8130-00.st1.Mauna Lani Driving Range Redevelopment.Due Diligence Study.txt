REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers an 
area of approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of the 
following five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea (activity during the recent 
geologic time), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 – 1803), and Mauna Loa and Kilauea (both 
still active). 
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of Mauna Loa Mountain. Based 
on available geologic information and geomorphological interpretation, the project site 
appears to be underlain by basaltic lava flows of the Kau Volcanic Series of Mauna Loa 
Volcano, which were deposited during the Holocene and Pleistocene Epoch (USGS 2005). 
The lava formation underlying the site generally consists of a’a basalt type flows at 
the ground surface, which spread and ponded as they flowed toward the ocean. A'a lava 
typically is characterized by a porous, rough, and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged 
accumulation of rock fragments, including cobbles and boulders. Typically, a denser and 
more layered lava rock material is contained within the lava flow core.  
Pahoehoe lava is anticipated at deeper depths. Pahoehoe lava is characterized by a 
smooth, rope-like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular (porous) rock. 
Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lavas. Cavities form when lavas are still 
in a molten state and represent both lava tubes (intra-flow cavities) and blisters and pockets 
(interflow cavities). Lava tubes form when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving 
a hollow tube-like structure, which may extend for a large longitudinal distance along the 
flow. Blisters and pockets (interflow cavities) are generally smaller in horizontal extent. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Our preliminary geotechnical engineering exploration consisted of excavating a 
total of 12 test pits, designated as Test Pit Nos. 1 through 12, extending to depths of about 
3.5 to 9.7 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate test pit locations are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Based on our test pits excavated, the subsurface conditions at the site generally 
consist of fill materials underlain by clinker materials. The thickness of the fill materials 
generally ranged from about 4 to 16 inches at the test pit locations. In Test Pit No. 1, fill 
materials extended down to about 3.3 feet below the existing ground surface. Fill 
materials generally consisted of stiff sandy silts and dense silty sands. 
Fill materials were generally underlain by clinker materials consisting of medium

Fill Materials 
We understand that it is desired to balance the cut and fill earthwork quantities for 
the development of the project. Based on the preliminary plans provided, excavations of 
up to about 15 feet in height may be required for the project. In general, the majority of 
the planned excavation, especially in deeper cuts, will likely occur at the existing mounds 
of the driving range. Some excavations are also planned along the center portions of the 
existing driving range. 
In general, we recommend capping the fill areas of the project site with a minimum 
2-foot thick layer of structural fill materials. Structural fill materials should consist of 
well-graded granular materials generally less than 6 inches in maximum dimension with 
sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass. In addition, 
structural fill materials should contain less than 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 
sieve by weight.

Fill and backfill materials below the minimum 2-foot thick structural fill layer may 
consist of general fill materials. General fill and backfill materials should consist of 
well-graded granular materials, the majority of which are less than 12 inches with an 
absolute maximum dimension of 18 inches. Materials between 12 and 18 inches in 
particle size should be limited to about 15 percent or less of the total volume of general 
fill materials. 
Based on the test pits excavated, we envision that the majority of excavated cut 
materials would consist of clinker materials. In general, the clinker materials are suitable 
as a source of structural and general fill materials. Some boulders were also present in 
the clinker materials. In general, we believe that the boulders may be used as a source 
of general fill (with a maximum dimension of 18 inches) or may be reduced in size to be 
used as structural fill (with a maximum dimension of 6 inches). 
Near-surface fill materials generally consist of silty and sandy soils of less than 
about 16 inches in thickness. In general, we believe that the fill materials may be mixed 
with clinker materials and used as a source of general and structural fill. 
We envision that the development of the project will require some materials smaller 
than 6 inches in size, such as 3B Fine gravel under slabs-on-grades and base course 
under roadways and utility line trenches. Therefore, some imported materials may be 
required for the project development. As an alternative, the on-site clinker materials and 
boulders may be processed with a crusher to generate the base course and 3B Fine 
gravel required for the project. 
Site Preparation 
As mentioned above, the project site is generally underlain by clinker materials. 
Clinker materials generally contain substantial amount of void spaces in their natural 
condition. Therefore, special attention should be given to site preparation prior to the 
commencement of fill placement and/or construction of improvements at the project site. 
In general, subgrade preparation should consist of proof-rolling finished grades in 
cuts and areas to be filled with a minimum 20-ton (static weight) vibratory drum roller for a 
minimum of eight passes. It should be noted that these subgrade preparation procedures 
are consistent with the standard of practice for similar type projects and similar subsurface 
conditions along the Kohala Coast on the Island of Hawaii.

Lava Tubes, Cavities and Voids 
Lava tubes and cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas on the Island 
of Hawaii, especially pahoehoe type lavas. Lava tubes and cavities may vary significantly in 
size from small blisters to large lava tubes of 20 feet in diameter or more. In general, the 
majority of small cavities are limited in lateral extent.  However, potential for long lava tubes 
that may traverse across the entire project site exists. 
Based on our test pits excavated, the near-surface materials at the project site 
generally consist of clinker materials from a’a lavas. We envision that basalt formation 
consisting of pahoehoe lavas may be encountered at deeper depths.  
In general, site preparation requirements are designed to reduce the risk of potential 
future collapse of lava tubes, cavities and voids. To further reduce the risk, implementation 
of foundation probing and grouting may be considered during the design of the project. The 
need for foundation probing and grouting should consider the structural column loads of the 
buildings, foundation bearing materials after mass grading, and prevalence of potential lava 
tubes and cavities under the footings. Therefore, we recommend that the subsurface 
conditions be evaluated by conducting a field exploration program consisting of drilling and 
sampling borings during the design phase of this project when the type and distribution of 
the proposed structures are better defined. 
Site Drainage 
We envision that development of the proposed project will require construction of a 
stormwater disposal system. In general, we believe that the subsurface conditions 
consisting of clinker materials can transmit water quite readily in both the horizontal and 
vertical directions. Therefore, shallow seepage pits or similar structures should be adequate 
to dispose stormwater runoff. We recommend performing permeability tests of subsurface 
materials during the design phase of this project when the scope of the project and 
stormwater disposal systems are better defined.

The following terms describe the resistance of a rock to indentation or scratching:
Specimen breaks with difficulty after several "pinging" hammer blows.
Example: Dense, fine grain volcanic rock
Specimen breaks with some difficulty after several hammer blows.
Example: Vesicular, vugular, coarse-grained rock
Specimen can be broked by one hammer blow. Cannot be scraped by knife. SPT may penetrate by
~25 blows per inch with bounce.
Example: Porous rock such as clinker, cinder, and coral reef
Can be indented by one hammer blow. Can be scraped or peeled by knife. SPT can penetrate by
~100 blows per foot.
Example: Weathered rock, chalk-like coral reef
Crumbles under hammer blow. Can be peeled and carved by knife. Can be indented by finger
pressure.
