INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Puunana Water Treatment Plant Reservoir in the Hoolehua
area on the island of Molokai Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site
are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and _ geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses The recommendations presented herein are intended for design of
foundations site grading and trench excavation and backfill only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located near the existing Mauna Loa Reservoir on top of Puu
Nana in the Hoolehua area on the island of Molokai Hawaii Based on the information
provided to us at the time of our report preparation we understand that the proposed
project consists of the following
1 A 300 000 gallon reservoir The proposed water treatment plant reservoir
will be a steel tank structure of approximately 48 feet in diameter with finish
floor elevation at 1369 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
2 A control building approximately 32 foot by 60 foot with finish floor
elevation at 1367 feet MSL
3 A lined containment pond approximately 30 foot by 30 foot by 10 foot
deep
We understand that the proposed steel tank structure will be constructed near
existing grades Therefore we anticipate that grading at the proposed water tank site
would generally consist of minor cuts and fills on the order of about 1 foot or less to
achieve a level pad for the new water tank

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Molokai was built during the early to middle Pleistocene Epoch Ice
Age by basaltic and andesitic lava flows from two shield volcanoes East Molokai
Mountain and West Molokai Mountain The project site is located on the eastern side of
the West Molokai Mountain Prior to weathering and erosion the West Molokai Mountain
was a typical elongated basaltic shield shaped dome It was built over the northwest and
east trending rifts with a steep slope on the north side where the lava flows plunged into
deep water and a gentle slope on the east side where the lava flows banked against the
East Molokai dome and formed the Hoolehua Plain
During the Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to fill
the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels rose
because more water was available The land mass of Molokai remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio
eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower
relative to the present sea level on Molokai Chemical and physical weathering have
produced a residual lateritic soil cover on the saprolite soils and basalt formation
underlying the site

The proposed project site is located adjacent to the existing telecommunication
facilities on the top of Puu Nana The site is currently accessible via an existing dirt road
At the time of our field exploration the project site was occupied with two severely
deteriorated metal tanks with collapsed roof and concrete foundation
The proposed water tank location is generally level with an existing ground
elevation at about 1370 feet Mean Sea Level MSL The proposed control building
location is also generally level with existing ground elevations at about 1367 to
1368 feet MSL while the proposed washout pond location is sloping gently downwards

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the proposed water treatment plant reservoir site
were explored by excavating and sampling four test pits to depths ranging from about 5 to
8 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the test pits are

anticipated below the excavated depth
Groundwater was not encountered in the test pits at the time of our field

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration indicated that the proposed water tank site is underlain by
residual soils consisting of stiff clayey silts Based on our evaluation of the subsurface
conditions with respect to the proposed water tank construction the principal geotechnical
engineering considerations for the project include the following
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3922 00 Page 6
1 foundation support for the proposed water tank structure
2 subgrade preparation below the tank structure and
3 trench excavation and backfill of utility lines
Based on the results of our field exploration we believe that a shallow foundation
system bearing on the stiff residual soils encountered at shallow depths or bearing on a
compacted subgrade may be used to support the water tank structure
Detailed discussion and recommendations for design of foundations site
grading trench excavation and backfill and other geotechnical aspects of the project are
presented in the following sections of this report
Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we
recommend that a shallow foundation system bearing on the stiff residual soils or a
prepared subgrade be used to support the proposed water tank structure and control
building The walls of the new tank structure may be supported on a perimeter ring
footing embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent grade Spread
and or continuous wall footings for the control building should also be embedded a
minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent grade An allowable bearing pressure
of up to 3 500 p s f may be used for design of footing foundations bearing on the stiff
residual soils and or prepared subgrade This allowable bearing pressure recommended
is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third for transient loads such as
temporary wind and or seismic forces
We anticipate that the bottom plate of the tank structure will be supported on a
bedding layer of sand or similar material to provide more uniform support of the steel tank
structure We recommend that the sand bedding layer consist of a uniformly graded sand
of about 6 inches in thickness The sand bedding should be compacted using a vibratory
plate for a minimum of 4 passes in order to provide a firm base for the bottom plate of the
tank structure
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3922 00 Page 7
Where foundations are located adjacent to new or existing utilities the foundations
should extend to a depth below an imaginary 45 degree plane projected upward from the
bottom of the invert of the utility pipe This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging
adjacent utilities with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement
If footings are designed in accordance with the recommendations presented
herein total settlement of footings is estimated to be on the order of less than 1 inch with
differential settlements on the order of about inch
Lateral loads acting on the tank or building structure may be resisted by frictional
resistance between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive
earth pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of
footings A coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used for footings bearing on the stiff
residual soils and or compacted subgrade Resistance to lateral loads due to passive
pressure may be calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square
foot per foot of depth p c f for footings embedded in the residual soils and or compacted
fills This value assumes that the soil around the footings is relatively undisturbed or
well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the
upper 12 inches below finished grade should be neglected
If soft and or loose soils are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavation
they should be over excavated to expose competent native material and replaced with
select granular material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM
D 1557 91 test procedures
It is recommended that a representative from Geolabs Hawaii observe the
foundation excavations prior to placement of reinforcing steel to evaluate the competency
of the bearing materials and the embedment depths

Site Grading
Based on the information provided we anticipate that grading at the proposed site
will consist of minor cuts and fills on the order of 1 foot or less Site grading operations
should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii to observe whether the
exposed soil conditions are similar to those encountered in our exploration The following
recommendations are intended to provide guidelines for the design of site grading work
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and unsuitable materials
including concrete foundations of the existing deteriorated water tanks should be
removed and disposed of properly off site to reduce the potential for contamination of the
excavated materials
Following clearing and grubbing subgrades to receive fill should be scarified to a
depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction If subgrades are residual soils they should be proof rolled with a
vibratory drum roller 15 ton minimum or similar heavy equipment a minimum of 6 passes
prior to fill placement Any soft loose material disclosed during clearing operations should
be over excavated and backfilled with engineered fill compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established
in accordance with test procedures
Generally the on site residual soils may be used as a source of fill and backfill
materials Where used as fill and or backfill material the on site soils should be placed in
level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction
Any imported material should be tested and approved by Geolabs Hawaii for its
intended use prior to delivery to the project site Where needed imported granular
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3922 00 Page 9
materials should consist of crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material
should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles greater than 3 inches in
largest dimension The material should have a laboratory CBR value of at least 20 anda
swell value of less than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883 test
procedures The imported material should also be moisture conditioned and compacted
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Containment Pond
It is our understanding that a lined containment pond approximately 30 foot by
30 foot by 10 foot deep is included in the proposed development In general the
material type performance installation and protection details of the impervious liner
should be designed in accordance with the recommendations by the liner manufacturer
It is critical that the liner maintains its integrity to prevent saturation and build up of
seepage water pressures within the excavation A subdrain is recommended to relieve
the potential for build up of seepage water below the impervious liner system The
subdrain should discharge to an appropriate outlet
Based on our experience with similar liner installations there is a tendency for the
liners to become detached at the top of the water impoundment It appears that this may
be the result of inadequate anchoring of the top portion of the liner and insufficient
flexibility of the liner material This is of particular importance with the design side slopes
of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V Therefore it is our recommendation that the
liner manufacturer be consulted regarding proper construction detailing and installation of
the liner with particular attention to proper anchoring to reduce the potential of liner failure
Effects of fluctuating water levels on the performance of the impervious liner near the top
anchoring should also be considered
Technical representatives of the manufacturer of the impervious liner should be
required to be on site at all times during the installation process to assure that proper
construction procedures and precautions are followed A sales representative of the
supplier s may not have the technical expertise to adequately perform this function
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3922 00 Page 10
We recommend that a geotextile underlining consisting of a heavy duty geotextile
fabric beneath the impervious liner be provided to reduce the potential for puncture of the
impervious liner The use of a geotextile fabric is recommended in lieu of a sand cushion
due to the ease of installation and the increased puncture resistance of the impervious
liner when combined with a layer of geotextile underlining In addition a sand cushion is
not recommended because of the steep side slopes of 2H 1V
Access Road Design
We understand that the existing dirt road will be re routed and will be used as an
access road to the proposed reservoir Based on our field exploration and laboratory
testing the on site surficial clayey silt residual soils appear to be fairly competent
subgrade materials Based on the above assumptions and considering the light traffic
anticipated for the proposed project we believe that a minimum of 6 inches of base
course compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction and subsequently applied
with a bituminous seal coat may be used for the preliminary design of the access road
The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction prior to placement of base
course The base course should consist of crushed aggregate compacted to not less
than 95 percent relative compaction CBR and density tests should be performed on the
actual subgrade soils encountered during construction to confirm the adequacy of the
above section
If asphalt concrete pavement is desired for the access roads a pavement
section consisting of 2 inches of asphalt concrete over 6 inches of aggregate base course
may be used for preliminary design purposes
As an alternative design for the access roads consideration may be given to a soil
stabilized pavement surface using a stabilizing agent such as TerraStone or equivalent
This type of pavement design involves application of a liquid soil stabilizer diluted in water
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3922 00 Page 11
to the scarified subgrade and subsequently compacting the subgrade soil mixed with the
stabilizing agent In general the stabilized subgrade soil should be compacted at
moisture contents above the optimum moisture to at least 90 percent relative compaction
The solution contains oxidants solvents and dispersants which can cause the soil grains
to bond together to form a stronger more durable pavement than an ordinary dirt road
The specific requirements of the mixture preparation and application details of the soil
stabilized pavement and compaction will depend on the product type and should be
designed in accordance with the recommendations by the manufacturer
The roads should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all surface
water off the road Surface water ponding should not be allowed anywhere on the site
during or after construction
Utilities
We envision that new utility lines and or piping network will be required for the
proposed new water tank Based on the stiff residual soils encountered in the test pits
relatively easy digging with conventional equipment is anticipated for utility trench
excavation For pipes bearing on the residual soils a poorly graded granular bedding
material with little fines such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
S4C or equivalent is recommended below the pipes as bedding material Other alternate
pipe bedding material should consist of granular material containing minimal fines with
particles less than 1 inch in maximum dimension Free draining granular materials such
as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be used for the trench
backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes This should provide adequate support
around the pipes and to reduce compaction of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of
damaging the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipe to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of the excavated on site residual
soils containing particles less than 6 inches in largest dimension Trench backfill should
be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in 8 inch maximum level
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3922 00 Page 12
loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to
reduce the potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are located below the
water tank structure and pavement areas the upper 2 feet of trench backfill below the
finish subgrade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain
surface run off away from the foundations The finish grades outside the proposed water
tank should be sloped to shed water away from foundations and to reduce the potential
for ponding Excessive landscape watering near the foundations should also be avoided
Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to
reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
