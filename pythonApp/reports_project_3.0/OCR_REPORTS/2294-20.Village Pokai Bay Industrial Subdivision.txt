INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration for the
proposed Village Pokai Bay Industrial Subdivision development located in Waianae on
the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses These recommendations are intended for design input only and may not be
appropriate to form the basis for construction cost estimates or for use in construction
bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Village Pokai Bay development as planned includes three phases The
northern portion of the development is Phase which is basically a residential area and
is currently under construction The proposed Phase II is located at the southeast portion
of the total development and will consist of mass grading for the future construction of
121 single family residential units and an industrial subdivision area Plans for Phase III
were not available at the time this report was prepared but it is anticipated to include the
remaining areas in the south and southeast portions of the development
The planned industrial subdivision will be located west of the residential area in the
Phase Il development It is anticipated that materials excavated from the industrial
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2294 20 Page 3
subdivision area will be used as fill for residential areas of Phases I and Ill The mass
balance for the grading operation should be considered in the grading design of this
project
Present plans call for the construction of cut slopes up to about 60 feet high
upslope of the industrial subdivision portion of the development
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our geotechnical exploration was to obtain an overview of the
subsurface conditions at the project site and to develop an idealized subsurface data set
for the formulation of geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the design
of the mass grading for the proposed industrial subdivision development
Our work was done in general accordance with our proposal to RYM Waianae
Partners Inc dated Our scope of work consisted of the following
1 Reconnaissance of the project site and its vicinity to visually observe the
surface conditions
2 Mobilization and demobilization of a truck mounted drill rig a water truck
and operators to and from the project site
3 Drilling and sampling of twelve 12 borings at the project site to depths
ranging between 16 and 48 feet below the existing ground surface
4 Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the borings by a field
geologist from our firm

GENERAL SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located at the northern base of Puu Mailiilii in the Waianae
district on the leeward coast of the island of Oahu Hawaii Part of the site was formerly
a coral limestone quarry Most of the site is now covered with dryland vegetation such
as kiawe trees haole koa and grass
The site is flanked on the south by the lower slopes of Puu Mailiilii and part of the
property is located on the mountain Approximately 1 acres of the site just southwest
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2294 20 Page 5
of the center is relatively flat with surface slopes of about 10H 1V sloping upwards
towards the south This area is at an elevation of about 20 feet higher than the
surrounding areas to the east and north
The northwest portion of the site is relatively level and is currently used as a
junkyard The eastern part of the site is relatively level sloping upwards toward the south
at a slope generally ranging from 15H 1V in the lower areas to 10H 1V in the upper areas
The lower slopes of the Puu Mailiilii form the south side of the site with a slope generally
of about 2H 1V The existing ground elevations range from about 38 to 150 feet
Mean Sea Level MSL
Much of the slopes of the Puu Mailiilii within the project boundaries are mantled
with dark expansive adobe clays with gravel Previous experience with similar areas in
the Waianae district indicate that some of these adobe slopes may have adverse mass
Stability characteristics with a potential for downslope movement known as creep
Creeping soils in combination with the abundance of boulders on the mountain indicate
a risk of boulders rolling down the slope

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Our field exploration disclosed that the majority of the industrial subdivision site
higher western lower northeastern and central portions are generally underlain by
competent coralline soils In the upper slope areas in the southern portion of the site
surficial layers of alluvium and colluvium with boulders about 10 to 40 feet in thickness
are generally underlain by basalt and or the coral formation In general the soils
encountered on site have good slope stability characteristics with the exception of the
adobe soils found generally in the upper three 3 to four feet 4 of parts of the site
These sloping clay deposits have high shrink swell potential and a strong tendency for
downhill creep movement
If thick deposits of adobe soils are encountered during grading we recommend
removal of all such soils above a 10H 1V slope line and burial of the over excavated soils
deep in fills elsewhere on the site Adobe soils should be capped with a minimum of
three feet 3 of non expansive material The on site coralline soils are considered to be
suitable for this capping material
The planned cut slopes on the south side of the site will be constructed by
exposing colluvial and alluvial materials coral formation materials and basalt rock The
allowable design cut slope ratio will differ for each material exposed during construction
The recommendations herein should be considered general guidelines for the field
operations during which the actual sequences and qualities of materials encountered may
vary from the general descriptions provided in this report

Site Grading
Fills up to seven feet 7 high and cuts up to about 60 feet deep are planned for
the industrial subdivision development
All surficial adobe clay soils should be removed of above a 10H 1V slope line and
buried deep in fills elsewhere on the site Adobe soils should be Capped with a minimum
of three feet 3 of non expansive material The on site coralline soils are considered to
be suitable for this capping material
A three foot 3 thick layer of expansive silty clay was encountered in
Boring No 104 at approximately the planned grade elevation The depth and extent of
this material is unknown beyond the boring but it is likely that more such material would
be encountered during grading When exposed all such materials should be treated in
the same manner as the adobe soil discussed above
For the level areas where the finished grade exposes this expansive silty clay layer
the grade should be over excavated by a minimum of three feet 3 and backfilled with
the on site coralline material placed in ten inch 10 thick loose level lifts compacted to
90 percent of its maximum dry density as determined in accordance with ASTM Test
Method D 1557 91
Standard earthwork and grading specifications are provided in Appendix C
Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
Cleared of trees and other vegetation debris rubbish and other deleterious
materials These materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2294 20 Page 9
In areas to receive fill the existing ground should be scarified to a depth of six
inches 6 moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent of its maximum dry density as deter
mined in accordance with ASTM Test Method D 1557 91 Soft or yielding areas
should be over excavated and stabilized by backfilling with select material placed
in ten inch 10 thick loose level lifts compacted to 90 percent of its maximum dry
density as determined in accordance with ASTM Test Method D 1557 91
Fill Material
Materials used for fills placed within the upper three feet 3 of the finish subgrade
level should be select non expansive material generally less than three inches 3
in maximum dimension with a plasticity index Pl not exceeding 20 as determined
in accordance with ASTM Test Method D 4318 84 The on site coralline soils are
considered suitable for this purpose
If additional off site borrow soil is required it should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii
to evaluate its suitability as fill for the project prior to its delivery to the project site
Boulder Fills
Many surface boulders were noted on the upslope areas of the site In addition
a large quantity of boulders will be generated from excavation operations in the
colluvium materials Boulders at the surface and in the excavations may be used
in the deeper fills provided that the following recommendations are followed
1 Boulders must not be nested together They should be placed so that
compaction equipment is able to compact around them
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2294 20 Page 10
2 Large boulders should not be used within three feet 3 of the finish grade
elevation
3 Care must be exercised to avoid placement of boulders within the locations
and depths of the proposed utility alignments to reduce potential difficulties
in the later excavation of the utility trenches
Fill Material Placement and Compaction
Fills should be placed in maximum ten inch 10 thick loose level lifts moisture
conditioned to at least two percent 2 above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent of its maximum dry density as determined
in accordance with ASTM Test Method D 1557 91 Each layer should be spread
uniformly and blade mixed to attain uniformity of the material and moisture content
Additional fill material should not be placed on any fill layer which has not been
properly compacted
Mass Balance
It is our understanding that the cut materials in the industrial subdivision areas will
be used as fill materials in the residential areas of Phases II and III Our laboratory
tests indicate that the coralline materials in the western portion of the site are very
porous with dry densities ranging between about 72 and 107 pounds per cubic
foot p c f and with porosity ranging between about 36 and 74 percent The
relatively high porosity should be taken into account during mass balance
computations as it would likely reduce the production of coralline materials as fill
from cutting
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2294 20 Page 11
Our field exploration also indicated that an approximately 15 foot thick layer of
expansive silty clay was encountered in the planned cut at the location of Boring
No 108 Because of the high swell potential of this material it should not be used
within the upper three feet 3 of fill Because similar materials were not
encountered in nearby borings the extent of this material is unknown In
computing the mass balance the presence of this material should be considered
Based on the current plans only minor fills on the order of less than several feet
are planned for the Industrial Subdivision area of Village Pokai Bay If fill slopes
are planned the fill slope should be designed using a slope inclination of two
horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter This is based on the assumption that
the fill slope will be constructed of select material
In general the design cut slopes will expose a sequence of materials consisting
of colluvial or alluvial deposits overlying coralline deposits which overlie basalt
The approximate relationship between the materials encountered and the planned
cut slopes are shown on the Generalized Cross Sections Plates 3 through 5 The
approximate locations of the cross sections are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The excavation of the colluvial materials may present difficulties due to the
abundance of rocks cobbles and boulders The hard basalt rock mass may be
expected to be highly resistant to excavation
Based on the results of our field exploration and laboratory testing program we
believe that the following slope ratios may be used for the design of cut slopes in
different materials
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2294 20 Page 12
1 Cut slopes in colluvium material may be designed with a slope ratio of
2H 1V or flatter
2 Cut slopes in coralline deposits may be designed with a slope ratio of
1 H 1V or flatter The coralline deposits encountered in our borings were
jointed and fractured
3 Cut slopes in basalt may be designed with a slope ratio of 1H 1V or flatter
Cut slopes in basalt may be steepened to 14H 1V depending on the field
conditions provided that provisions for flattening the slopes are incorporated
into the design and construction
An engineer or geologist of Geolabs Hawaii should be present to observe the cut
slopes during construction to evaluate if the exposed soil conditions are consistent
with our assumptions made in the design
It is our understanding that the boundary of the Conservation Use Area is fixed
Therefore some portions of the cut slopes may extend past the planned toe of
slope in order to allow sufficient space for the construction of the designed slopes
For cut and fill slopes greater than 20 feet in height a minimum eight foot 8 wide
drainage bench should be placed at the mid height of the slope
Water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and slope planting should
be utilized to reduce erosion

Foundations
It is our opinion that shallow spread or strip footings bearing on the on site
coralline soils or compacted fill may be used to support the proposed industrial
subdivision structures Preliminary recommendations for foundation design are as follows
1
Footings on level areas should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches
below the lowest adjacent finished grade
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot p s f
may be used for footings bearing on the in situ coralline deposits or on
properly compacted fill
The bottom of all foundation excavations should be cleared of loose material
and bear on firm material The foundation excavations should be observed
by a geotechnical engineer to observe that an adequate bearing surface
has been exposed
The ground surface should be graded to direct water away from the
foundations
If thickened edged slabs on grade are used for support the thickened edge
should extend a minimum of eight inches 8 beiow the lowest adjacent
exterior grade The slab should be a minimum of four inches 4 thick and
should be supported by a four inch 4 thick cushion of No 3B Fine gravel
or ASTM C 33 No 67 gravel A moisture barrier membrane should be
incorporated between the slab and cushion to reduce the potential for
moisture infiltration It is recommended that isolation joints be provided
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2294 20 Page 14
between the slabs on grade and the wall foundations to allow for some
relative movement between the slabs and walls
Boulder Protection
Properties adjacent to the upslope areas may be subject to boulders rolling down
the hillside The falling boulders are a general phenomenon occurring on all ridge
Slopes on Oahu Various catchment methods and boulder barriers can be used to
reduce the hazard of falling boulders Some methods that may be considered include
but are not necessarily limited to the following
1 A boulder catchment area about 10 to 15 feet wide with or without a fence
or wall could be utilized near the property boundary to catch rolling
boulders An interceptor ditch along the upper boundaries of the site could
be used in this capacity
2 Boulder sweeps along the upper boundaries of the site could be performed
periodically to identify and remove loose boulders that appear likely to roll
down the slopes
3 The natural trees and vegetation along the upper portions of the site should
be retained to act as a natural barrier against movement of the boulders
and to reduce erosion
4 Where practical the structures should be setback away from the toe of
hillside slopes to create an additional boulder catchment area
It must be pointed out that whatever methods are used an inherent risk of falling
boulders could still exist for the lots adjacent to the cut slopes
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2294 20 Page 15
The state of the art of soil engineering practice has not advanced to a point that
a solution is available for this area It is our opinion that no matter what one does there
is always the risk of boulders rolling down the hill if not from areas near the proposed
industrial subdivision then from areas higher up on the slopes Whatever measure is
chosen it only reduces the risk It should be pointed out that no guarantee against future
falling boulders can be made due to changes in soil and rock conditions with time
weathering erosion earthquake and construction vibration and improvements and
construction activities at and around the site
The owners of the planned industrial subdivision lots should be made aware of the
aforementioned limitations and be cautioned against future improvements in the upper
areas of the lots which may disturb the stability of the existing boulders

Utilities
In order to reduce accumulation of water around the utility lines from seepage
leakage etc which could saturate and weaken the surrounding soils we recommend
that clean gravel be used as cushion material below the pipes The trench and gravel
cushion layer should be shaped to drain water into catch basins and manholes
