In accordance with your request, we have performed a preliminary geotechnical 
engineering exploration in support of the Kapolei West Development, Phase 1 Mass 
Grading project for Parcel LA-1 in the Kapolei area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  This 
report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our

to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The proposed Kapolei West development encompasses approximately 500-acre 
undeveloped property in the Kapolei/Honouliuli area in the District of Ewa on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii. It is generally bounded by Farrington Highway and Honokai Hale/Nanakai 
Gardens Subdivisions toward the north, Kalaeloa Harbor towards the south, Kapolei 
Commons towards the east, and the existing Ko Olina Resort towards the west. 
The Parcel LA-1 area is located near the eastern portion of the development, as 
shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. Based on the information provided, the 
Parcel LA-1 development encompasses approximately 11 acres of area, consisting of 
about 150 multi-family units and a hotel. We understand that the proposed mass grading 
construction will require site grading of about 5 to 25 feet of fills and cuts to achieve the 
design finished grades. 
In general, the existing site was partially occupied by the coralline material 
stockpiles, that is planned to be removed as part of the mass grading operation. Based on 
our field observation, the surface terrain at the project site was uneven with ground surface 
elevations ranging from about +51 to +83 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
As part of the preliminary geotechnical exploration, the subsurface conditions at 
the project site were explored by drilling and sampling two borings, designated as Boring 
Nos. 101 and 102, extending to the maximum depth explored of about 16.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface. In addition, one borehole, designated as Boring No. I-101, was 
drilled to a depth of about 5 feet below the ground surface for the infiltration testing access. 
To examine the native subsurface conditions, the above boreholes were located near 
the toe of the stockpiles. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by stiff to hard 
clayey/silty alluvial soils extending to the maximum depth explored of about 16.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings drilled at the time of our field 
exploration. It should be noted that groundwater levels can fluctuate depending on tidal 
fluctuations, storm surge conditions, seasonal precipitation, groundwater withdrawal and/or 
injection, and other factors at the project site. 
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration methodology and the Logs of Borings 
are presented in Appendix A. Results of the field infiltration testing performed at the 
selected location are presented in Appendix B.

Site Preparation 
At the onset of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and 
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site to 
reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials. 
Prior to replacing the fill material, the subgrade should be scarified to a depth of 
at least 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to about 2 to 4 percent above the 
optimum moisture content, and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil 
established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Soft and/or yielding areas encountered at the existing ground surface below 
areas designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over-excavated 
to expose stiff and/or dense materials. The resulting excavation should be 
backfilled with well-compacted general fill materials. The excavated soft and/or 
organic soils should be disposed of properly off-site or used in future landscaped 
areas, if appropriate. Contract documents should include additive and deductive 
unit prices for over-excavation and compacted fill placement to account for 
variations in the over-excavation quantities. 
After the soil is moisture-conditioned and recompacted, it should be protected to 
retain the moisture by keeping it moist or immediately capped with new fill. If the 
recompacted soil dries out and shrinkage cracks develop, it should be scarified, 
moisture-conditioned, and compacted again. 
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement 
weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and 
replacement with well-compacted fill. A Geolabs representative should evaluate 
the need for over-excavation in the field.

Ewa/Kapolei area during prolonged dry weather conditions. Since shrinkage 
cracks tend to be a conduit for water infiltration into the anticipated subsurface

soils, we recommend implementing special site preparation procedures 
consisting of installing a cut-off trench along the perimeter of the grading limits at 
the project site. We recommend a cut-off trench at least 3 feet deep and 5 feet

Fills and Backfills 
Generally, we anticipate the earthwork would likely involve two primary types of 
materials: select granular fill and general fill. Select granular fill should consist of 
non-expansive granular material, such as crushed coralline and/or basaltic 
materials. The material should be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles 
no larger than 3 inches in the largest dimension and should contain between 10 
and 30 percent of particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have 
a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 
1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. 
General fill may consist of the on-site soils with a maximum particle size of 
6 inches and should be free of deleterious material. In general, the excavated 
on-site soils may be reused as a source of general fill material, provided they are 
free of vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other unsuitable materials. 
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
General fills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to about 2 to 4 percent above the optimum 
moisture content, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The 
non-expansive select granular fill materials should be placed in level lifts of about 
8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture 
content, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction (or 95 percent 
compaction for finished subgrades subjected to vehicular traffic). 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content 
(percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
We wish to emphasize that moisture conditioning of the fill materials is an integral 
and critical part of the earthwork recommendations provided herein. Compaction

Excavations 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by stiff to hard 
clayey/silty soils, which may be excavated with normal heavy excavation 
equipment, such as excavators, and ripped with large bulldozers. However, based 
on our experience in the project vicinity, it is not uncommon that isolated coralline 
detritus/formation and cobbles/boulders could be encountered. Therefore, deeper 
excavations may encounter hard/dense coral and/or cobbles/boulders, which may 
require the use of hoerams. 
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the subsurface materials are 
based on our field exploration data from the borings drilled and experience in the 
project vicinity. We recommend encouraging contractors to examine the site 
conditions and the subsurface data to make their own interpretations. 
Erosion Control Measures (Landscape) 
In general, shrinkage cracks may develop in the ground due to the lack of 
maintenance of the soil moisture content as the soils start to dry out under the sun 
and dry weather conditions. The development of shrinkage cracks in the ground 
was apparently intensified by the prolonged dry climatic conditions in the 
Kapolei/Ewa area resulting in large fluctuations in the soil moisture content. 
We recommend grassing the graded surfaces to reduce the potential for significant 
soil erosion soon after the completion of the site grading. The relatively dry climatic 
conditions of the Kapolei/Ewa area likely will require controlled irrigation of the 
graded surfaces to maintain the moisture contents and to keep graded surfaces 
free of large shrinkage cracks. However, over-watering of the graded surfaces 
should be avoided to reduce the potential for saturation and softening of the surface 
soils.

Retaining Structure Foundations 
In general, retaining structure foundations may be supported on a shalloe 
foundation system with an allowable bearing pressure of up to about 3,000 pounds 
per square foot (psf) bearing on recompacted in-situ soils or general fills placed in 
accordance with the recommendations presented herein. This bearing value is for 
dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such 
as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
Retaining wall footings should be at least 18 inches wide and should be embedded 
a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grades. For sloping 
ground conditions, the footing should extend deeper to obtain a minimum 6-foot 
setback distance measured horizontally from the outside edge of the footing to the 
face of the slope. Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should 
be constructed in stepped footings. 
Bottom of the footing excavations should be recompacted to at least 90 percent 
relative compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to 
the placement of reinforcing steel and concrete. Soft and/or loose materials 
encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations should be over-excavated to 
expose the underlying firm materials. The over-excavation should be backfilled with 
select granular fill materials compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction, or the bottom of footing may be extended down to bear on the 
underlying competent materials. 
Foundations that are located next to utility trenches or easements should be 
embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom 
edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This 
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with 
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation 
settlement. 
Lateral loads acting on the retaining structure may be resisted by friction between 
the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and/or by passive earth pressure 
acting against the near-vertical faces of the footing. A coefficient of friction of 0.3 
may be used for footings bearing on the stiff on-site soils or new compacted fills. 
Resistance to lateral loads due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using 
an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf). 
This value assumes that the soils around the footings are well compacted. Unless 
covered by pavements or slabs, the passive pressure resistance in the upper 
12 inches of soil should be neglected. 
A Geolabs representative should observe the footing excavations and subgrade 
preparation prior to the placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the 
foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depths.

Drainage 
The retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the potential for build-up 
of hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide 
zone of permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation), placed directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at 
the base of the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes. As an 
alternative, a prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may 
be used instead of the drainage material. The prefabricated drainage product also 
should be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall. 
The backfill from the bottom of the wall to the bottom of the perforated pipe or 
weephole should consist of relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential 
for significant water infiltration into the subsurface. In addition, the upper 12 inches 
of the retaining structure backfill should consist of relatively impervious materials to 
reduce the potential for significant water infiltration behind the retaining structure 
unless covered by concrete slabs at the surface. 
Utility Trenches 
We understand that the utilities for the new development project will consist of 
primarily electrical, water, drain, and sewer lines. In general, good construction practices 
should be utilized for the installation and backfilling of the trenches for the proposed 
utilities. The contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used for 
excavation, subject to practical limits and safety considerations. The excavation should 
comply with all applicable local, state, and federal safety requirements. The contractor 
should be responsible for trench shoring design and installation. Trench shoring and 
bracing should conform to the appropriate health and safety requirements. 
Based on the results of our field exploration, it appears that conventional 
excavation methods may generally be utilized for utility line excavation. The upper 
clayey/silty soils encountered are generally stiff to hard. However, some of the utility trench 
excavations will likely encounter dense coralline detritus/formation and/or cobble/boulder 
materials, which may pose some excavation difficulties during the trench excavation. 
In general, we recommend providing granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No. 
3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) under the pipes for support. Free-draining 
granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), also should 
be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide 
adequate support around the pipes. It is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce 
the potential for the formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide 
adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill could result in 
settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes.

Field Infiltration Testing 
We conducted a falling head infiltration test at a selected location to evaluate the 
infiltration characteristics of the subsurface materials encountered. The test was performed 
in general accordance with the procedures in Appendix D of the State of Maryland, 
Department of the Environment “Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I and II” (rev. 2009). 
These procedures are consistent with other states’ procedures and may generally be 
considered an industry standard. The results of the infiltration test performed at the 
selected location are presented in Appendix B. 
The field infiltration test was performed by drilling a borehole, utilizing a 4-inch 
solid-stem auger to a selected test depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Upon reaching the test depth, a 4-inch diameter PVC solid casing was set to the bottom of 
the drilled borehole to allow infiltration only through the soil exposed at the bottom of the 
borehole. A falling head infiltration test was performed to determine the average infiltration 
rate of the underlying subsurface materials. The test consisted of four trials of filling the 
casing with 24 inches of water and taking periodic readings until the water drained out or 
up to one hour. The infiltration rate was then calculated based on the results of the fourth 
and/or last trials for the test location. The calculated infiltration rate at the test location is 
summarized in the table below.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring No. 101 and 102, extending to the maximum depth 
explored of about 16.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, a borehole, 
designated as I-101, was drilled to a depth of about 5 feet below the ground surface for the 
infiltration testing access. The approximate boring location is shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. The boring was drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous 
flight augers. 
Our geologists classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM 
D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.  Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in 
general accordance with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test, and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.
