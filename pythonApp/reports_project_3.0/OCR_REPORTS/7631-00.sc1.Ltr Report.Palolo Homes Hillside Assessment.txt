In accordance with our fee proposal dated October 27, 2017, this letter report 
presents the results of our geotechnical engineering due diligence assessment 
conducted for the Palolo Homes - New Jersey Hillside Erosion and Rockslide project in 
Palolo Valley on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity 
are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes our field observations pertaining to the existing baseline 
erosion and potential hazard conditions at the project site and presents our 
recommendations for mitigation design. The findings and recommendations presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The Palolo Homes project site is on sloping ground comprising the lower hillside 
at the northern side of Palolo Valley on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. There are 
11 existing residential duplex buildings at the project site that are along the hillside and 
above Jersey Avenue. Erosion occurred on the upslope side of the 11 buildings, 
identified as Building 114 through 125, excluding Building 116.  
The existing Palolo Homes public housing development was originally 
constructed between about 1950 and 1955 and consists of a series of multi-story units 
constructed across the slope by cutting into the hillside to create the building pads for 
the upslope row of housing units. As a result, there are existing Concrete Rubble

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
We conducted a general reconnaissance at the rear of the 11 housing unit 
buildings and the surrounding slopes to document the existing site conditions with 
respect to the reported upslope erosion, including potential rockfall hazards and the 
condition of the existing retaining and drainage structures at the rear of the buildings.  
Our reconnaissance was conducted on November 17, 2017 by our geologist and 
engineer under fair weather and dry site conditions. Selected photographs showing the 
existing site conditions and other noteworthy features observed during our 
reconnaissance are presented on Plates 3 through 13 for reference. 
General Existing Slope Conditions 
The purpose of our site reconnaissance was to observe the reported slope 
erosion conditions upslope from the row of the 11 housing buildings. The subject slope 
spans between the row of the housing units and an unlined drainage interception swale 
that is higher on the slope but within the housing development property limits. The 
existing slope area is approximately 1,100 feet in length across the slope and ranges 
between about 40 and 80 feet in width spanning from the buildings towards the upslope 
drainage swale.  
The slope above the housing units is retained by existing CRM walls that range 
between about 3 and 8 feet in height. Some of the CRM walls have a concrete swale 
just behind the top of the wall while other walls have no top of wall swale. In addition, 
some CRM walls have a narrow concrete drainage trough constructed along the toe of 
the wall. Discharge from the wall swale and trough is either toward the north or toward 
the south depending on the location of an appropriate discharge outlet for the swale.

We observed the existing CRM retaining walls and swales at the rear of the 
buildings terminate at the ends of the buildings without proper returning structures; 
therefore, there are generally short-length slope segments between the buildings that 
have no existing slope retention or drainage interception structure to hold back debris 
and collect runoff from the slope. We observed that some attempts have been made to 
place stacked rocks and concrete rubble to slow the erosion and encroachment of slope 
debris at the rear of the buildings. 
The typical upslope inclination is approximately two horizontal to one vertical 
(2H:1V) with some localized sections of steeper and flatter slope inclinations ranging 
between about 1H:1V and about 3H:1V. The upslope area is generally not maintained 
and is densely vegetated with mature Haole Koa shrubbery, tall grass and scattered 
trees. The existing dense shrubbery vegetation and ground cover vegetation obscured 
some areas of the slope from view.  
However, we observed scattered areas of exposed ground surface indicating the 
slope may be generally composed of stiff to very stiff dark brown silty and clayey soil 
containing hard basaltic cobbles and boulders ranging between about 1 and 5 feet in 
larger dimension. Most of the existing surface cobbles and boulders that we observed 
from the rear of the buildings appear to be partly embedded to non-embedded at the 
ground surface. Larger size boulders of about 3 to 5 feet in larger dimension appear in 
generally stable embedded or nested ground settings further up on the slope towards 
the unlined drainage interception swale. 
We observed that some attempts to reduce the erosion immediately adjacent to 
the existing retaining walls have been made by clearing the unmaintained shrubbery 
vegetation and excavating to shallow depths to create some limited flatter ground 
bordered by various make-shift retention features composed of dry-stacked cobbles, 
and small boulders and assorted concrete rubble. Attempts have also been made to 
slightly raise the height of the existing CRM retaining wall by placing dry-stacked rocks 
and rubble along the top of the wall.

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our reconnaissance conducted at the rear of the 11 housing buildings, 
it appears the reported slope erosion and resulting deposition of slope debris may be 
due to a combination of possible factors including the following: 
1. 
The existing unimproved condition of the upslope ground surfaces 
contains regions of bare soil exposure and abundant loose surface 
cobbles and boulders that are prone to erosion and down slope 
movement, especially during heavy slope runoff conditions. The erosion 
potential is greater on slopes steeper than about 2H:1V. 
2. 
The root systems of the existing dense Haole Koa shrubbery and larger 
trees (Christmas Berry, Mango, and Ficus trees) on the slope do help to 
retain some loose soil and rocks but the canopy growth appears to inhibit 
the growth of a stabilizing ground cover such as grass or other dense 
foliage ground cover. 
3. 
The existing Haole Koa shrubbery growing in close proximity to the 
buildings appears to produce an appreciable amount of organic debris that 
accumulates in the existing low gradient swales thereby potentially 
reducing the effectiveness and discharge capacity of the various swales. 
4. 
The advanced age and original retaining wall and swale construction in 
expansive adobe clay soil has produced some mainly localized structural 
distress of the existing walls and swales as a result of long-term soil creep 
and the expansive soil condition which is typically exasperated by soil 
wetting and drying cycles. 
5. 
The presence of feral chickens on the slope cause additional scour and 
erosion of the surface soils by their foraging activity on the sloping ground. 
6. 
Tenants appear to have made some adjustments to the original slope 
grades and retaining wall dimensions adjacent to the CRM wall by 
deploying make-shift slope/debris retention devices that should be 
considered temporary. 
Based on our preliminary assessment of the existing site conditions, we 
recommend the following design elements for mitigation of the erosion and potential 
rockfall hazard conditions be included in the next phase of detailed evaluation. 
1. 
Slope surface stabilization and erosion control; 
2. 
Rehabilitation of the existing distressed retaining wall and swale 
structures; 
3. 
Reconstruction of the existing structurally deficient retaining wall and 
swale structures; 
4. 
Construction of additional new retaining wall segments, return walls and 
drainage swale structures where the need exists.

LIMITATIONS 
The findings and recommendations submitted herein are based upon information 
obtained from visual site reconnaissance only. Variation in the surface and subsurface 
conditions beyond our observation may occur, and the nature and extent of these 
variations may not become evident until additional field exploration or construction is 
underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the findings 
and recommendations provided herein. 
It should be noted that slopes composed of rock materials (rock slopes) deteriorate 
with the passage of time due to natural weathering processes, wet-dry and hot-cold 
cycles, and erosion conditions.  Due to the inherent deterioration of rock slopes resulting 
from natural processes (weathering, wet-dry and hot-cold cycles, and erosion conditions), 
the potential for rockfall hazard at any site changes with the passage of time.  Therefore, 
the findings and recommendations contained herein may be used only within a reasonable 
time from the date of issuance of this letter report.  Land use, site conditions, and/or other 
factors may change with the passage of time.  Therefore, additional work to evaluate the 
applicability of the findings and preliminary recommendations contained in this letter report 
due to changes with the passage of time will be required.   
Finally, there are no guarantees in the professional engineering and architectural 
design fields with respect to potential rockfall hazards due in large part to the 
unpredictable nature of rockfall activity, which is affected by many external variables 
including natural and man-induced causes. This report has been prepared for the 
exclusive use of Palolo Valley Homes, L.P., Mutual Housing Association of Hawaii, and
