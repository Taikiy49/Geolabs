In accordance with our fee proposal dated May 19, 2006 and subsequent
discussions on the project scope of work, we performed a geotechnical engineering
exploration for the Kohala Mountain Road Pavement Repairs, Waiaka Junction toward
Hawi project in the District of South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii. This report
summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing, and presents
our findings and recommendations for the project design. The findings and
recommendations are intended for design input only and are subject to the limitations
noted at the end of this report.
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is at the existing Kohala Mountain Road (Hawaii State Route 250)
in the District of South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii. The project location and general
vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
Based on the information provided, the project will involve pavement repair and
guardrail improvement to the existing Kohala Mountain Road from Mile Post 2.54
(Sta. 772+50) to Mile Post 6.44 (Sta. 570+00) for a distance of approximately 20,250 lineal
feet. The approximate limits of the pavement repair and guardrail improvement project are
shown on the General Site Plan, Plate 2.
We understand that the original roadway design consisted of a 10-foot wide
travelway with a 4-foot wide shoulder area on each side. The original 10-foot wide
travelway was subsequently widened to 18 feet to accommodate two 9-foot wide travel
lanes by converting the shoulder areas into part of the travel lanes. Since the shoulder
lane conversion was not properly constructed, we understand that it is badly rutted with
signs of base failure. Therefore, it is proposed to reconstruct the 4-foot wide shoulder lane
conversion along both sides of the original 10-foot wide travelway with new pavement
section. In addition, localized areas within the original 10-foot wide travelway that exhibit

PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering exploration was to obtain an overview
of the surface and subsurface conditions to formulate geotechnical engineering
recommendations for the design of pavements and site preparation only. To accomplish
this, we conducted an exploration program consisting of the following tasks and work
efforts:
1. Coordination with the State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation,
Highways Division to obtain the necessary excavation permit and with the
various utility companies for toning of the boring locations.
2. Provision of traffic control and safety devices at the boring locations during
our field exploration program.
3. Mobilization and demobilization of a truck-mounted drill rig and
two operators to and from the project site.
4. Drilling and sampling of nine borings extending to depths of about 5.3 to
12.5 feet below the existing pavement surface for a total of approximately
58 lineal feet of field exploration.
5. Performance of two in-situ falling head permeability tests at selected
boring locations to evaluate the infiltration characteristics of the
subsurface materials.
6. Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the borings by our
geologist.
7. Laboratory testing of selected soil samples obtained during the field
exploration as an aid in classifying the materials and evaluating their
engineering properties.
8. Analyses of the field and laboratory data to formulate geotechnical
engineering recommendations for the design of pavements and site
preparation.
9. Preparation of this report summarizing our work on the project and
presenting our findings and recommendations.

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is at the existing Kohala Mountain Road (Hawaii State
Route 250) in the District of South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii. The project limits
extend from Mile Post 2.54 (Sta. 772+50) to Mile Post 6.44 (Sta. 570+00) for a distance
of approximately 20,250 lineal feet. We understand that the original roadway consisted
of a 10-foot wide travelway with a 4-foot wide shoulder area on each side. The original
10-foot wide travelway was subsequently widened to 18 feet to accommodate two 9-foot
wide travel lanes by converting the shoulder areas into part of the travel lanes. At the
time of our field exploration, we observed that the existing pavements within the
shoulder lane conversion were generally in poor condition with significant rutting and
alligator cracking.
The terrain along Kohala Mountain Road within the project limits slopes up
gradually from the Waiaka Junction (Mile Post 2.54) towards Hawi (Mile Post 6.44) with
elevations ranging from about +2,400 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) at about Sta. 772+50
(east end of project) to about +3,180 feet MSL at about Sta. 570+00 (west end of
project).

borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1A, 1B, 2A, 2B, 2C, 3A, 3B, 4A, and 4B, extending

methodology described in Chapter 3 of the Pavement Design Manual dated
March 2002 prepared by the State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation,

used by the California Department of Transportation (Caltrans).

Design Subgrade Conditions
Based on our field exploration results, we anticipate that the pavement subgrade
soils will likely consist of the near-surface clayey soils for the proposed pavement
repair project. Laboratory testing performed on the near-surface clayey soils

design R-value of 8 was adopted in our pavement analyses for the subgrade
materials. If site grading exposes soils other than those assumed, additional tests
should be performed to confirm and/or revise the recommended pavement section
for actual field conditions.

Pavement Subgrade Preparation
The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches,
moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content, and
recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers
to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil established in accordance with AASHTO T 180 or ASTM D 1557.
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the
maximum dry density.
If pumping conditions are encountered during compaction of the subgrade soils, the
subgrade soils may be stabilized by cement treatment to reduce the potential for pumping
subgrade conditions. As a guide, one sack of cement may be used for every 50 square
feet of subgrade area. This preliminary mix should be adjusted in the field to suit the
exposed soil conditions. Alternatively, the subgrade may be proof-rolled and a woven
geotextile fabric, such as Mirafi 500X or equivalent, should be placed on the subgrade
prior to placement of the aggregate subbase layer.
Where shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade, we
recommend scarifying the soils and preparing again as recommended above. Saturation
and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor
drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well-compacted fill. Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices
for over-excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the
over-excavation quantities.
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due
to saturation of the base course, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the
pavement surface should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to
carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. Surface water
ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after construction.

Site Preparation
Based on the information provided, we understand that minor cuts and fills will be
required to accommodate the installation of new guardrails along selected roadway
locations. In general, the area within the contract grading limits should be cleared and
grubbed thoroughly at the on-set of earthwork. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material
and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off-site. Soft
and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to receive fill or
future improvements should be over-excavated to expose firm and/or stiff material, and
the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fill. The excavated
soft and/or organic soils should be disposed properly off-site.
After clearing and grubbing, the existing ground or over-excavated subgrades
should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least
2 percent above the optimum moisture content, and recompacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction. The compaction requirement should be increased to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction in areas subjected to vehicular traffic.
Permanent cut and fill slopes to accommodate the guardrail installation may be
designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. Fills
placed on slopes with inclinations steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched
into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding. The filling
operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal
compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations. Fill
slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio to
obtain a well-compacted slope face. In the event that over-cutting of a slope occurs,
keying and benching requirements should be implemented instead of backfilling the
slope to the design grade with sliver fills. In addition, water should be diverted away
from the tops of slopes (cut and fill slopes), and slope planting should be provided as
soon as possible to reduce the potential for significant erosion of the finished slopes.
Aggregate subbase (ASB) materials required for the new pavements should
conform to the requirements stipulated in the Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road
and Bridge Construction (2005). The aggregate subbase should be
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in 8-inch level
loose lifts, and compacted to no less than 95 percent of the maximum density according
to Subsection 203.03(C)(2) — Relative Compaction Test of the Hawaii Standard
Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction.

Imported fill materials, if required, should consist of non-expansive select
granular material, such as crushed coral or basalt. The material should be well graded
from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension. In
addition, the material should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the
No. 200 sieve. The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and
should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with
ASTM D 1883. The materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content, placed in 8-inch level loose lifts, and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction. Geolabs should test imported fill materials for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the
intended use.

Field Permeability Testing
Field permeability tests were conducted at selected boring locations to evaluate the
infiltration characteristics of the subsurface materials along the roadway alignment. The
permeability tests were performed in Boring Nos. 1B and 4A as shown on the Site Plans,
Plates 3.1 through 3.4.
The permeability tests were performed at depths of about 4 and 4.5 feet below the
existing pavement surface. Falling head permeability tests were performed to determine
the average hydraulic conductivity of the underlying subsurface materials. Based on our
field exploration, the subsurface materials encountered at the site generally consist of silty
clay and clayey silt materials. The test results indicate that the subsurface materials
exhibit a hydraulic conductivity, k, of about 1.4 x 107 and 1.8 x 10* feet per minute. The
permeability test results are presented in Appendix C.

LIMITATIONS
The findings and discussions submitted herein are based, in part, upon information
obtained from the field borings and bulk samples. Variations of subsurface conditions
between and beyond the borings and bulk samples may occur, and the nature and extent
of these variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations
then appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the findings and discussions
provided herein.
The field boring locations indicated herein are approximate, having been
estimated by taping and pacing from the features shown on the Topographic Survey
Maps transmitted by Wesley R. Segawa & Associates, Inc. on October 17, 2007.
Elevations of the borings were estimated by interpolation from the spot elevations and
contour lines shown on the same plans. The physical locations and elevations of the
borings should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used.
The stratification lines shown on graphic representations of the borings depict the
approximate boundaries between soil types and, as such, may denote a gradual transition.
As previously mentioned, we did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of
our exploration. However, it must be noted that groundwater level changes may occur due
to seasonal precipitation, surface runoff, and other factors.

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling nine shallow
borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1A, 1B, 2A, 2B, 2C, 3A, 3B, 4A, and 4B, extending
to depths of about 5.3 to 12.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. We drilled the
borings using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers.
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System, as shown on the Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings,
Plates A-1 through A-9.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving
a 3-inch outside diameter (OD) Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer
falling 30 inches. In addition, we obtained some samples from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of
Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and
drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an
18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths.
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample. Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937)
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths.
Three Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations of the test
results are provided on Plate B-1.
Two Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 and C 136) were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on
Plate B-2.
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D 1883) were performed on
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-3 and B-4.

APPENDIX C
Field Permeability Testing
APPENDIX C
Field Permeability Testing
The permeability of the in-situ materials along the roadway was evaluated by
performing two falling head tests in Boring Nos. 1B and 4A. The tests were performed at
depths of about 4.5 and 4 feet below the existing pavement surface, respectively. The
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.4.
During the falling head testing, the boreholes were carefully filled with water to the
top. The time intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which to
base calculation of the permeability values of the subsoils. The test results of the field
permeability tests are presented on Plates C-1 and C-2.
