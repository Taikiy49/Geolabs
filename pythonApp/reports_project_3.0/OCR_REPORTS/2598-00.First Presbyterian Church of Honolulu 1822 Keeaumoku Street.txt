INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed improvements to First Presbyterian Church Of Honolulu on
Keeaumoku Street in Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location of
the project site is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings of our field exploration and our geotechnical
engineering recommendations derived from our analyses based on the data obtained for
the subject project These recommendations are intended for design input and are not
intended for construction bidding or estimation purposes
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the subsurface
conditions at the church addition site in order to develop a generalized soil rock data set
for the formulation of geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the site
grading and foundation design of the proposed structures
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2598 00 Page 3
The work was performed in accordance with our fee proposal to KOP Hawaii
dated The scope of work consisted of the following
1 Review of available soil and geologic information pertinent to the project site
and its vicinity
2 Reconnaissance of the project site and its vicinity to make visual observa
tions of the surface soil conditions
3 Drilling and sampling eight 8 borings to depths of about 13 5 to 31 5 feet
below the existing grades at the site
4 Laboratory testing of selected samples obtained during the field exploration
to determine the classification and engineering properties of the materials
encountered
5 Engineering analyses of the field and laboratory data for the formulation of
our geotechnical design recommendations
6 Coordination and overall project management of our work and design team
consultation by a Principal Engineer of our firm
7 Preparation of this report presenting the findings and the design recommen
dations
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration and laboratory testing are presented
in the appendices of this report

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to construct a two level parking structure adjacent to the existing
church building over an existing open parking lot The new parking structure will occupy
basically the entire existing parking lot and consist of a semi basement type structure
Asmaller one story parish hall addition approximately 110 feet by 120 feet in plan
will be built above the upper level of the new parking structure and connected with the
existing church sanctuary A one story chapel addition connecting to the existing
Fellowship Hall of the church will also be built on this level
Up to about 10 feet of mass excavation and retaining walls will be needed for the
new basement parking construction except at the southern corner where the entrance
to the lower level is planned Up to about 5 feet of fill may also be needed to grade the
portion of the site adjacent to Keeaumoku Street
GENERAL SITE CONDITIONS
The existing Presbyterian Church of Honolulu is located at the northwest corner
of the intersection of Keeaumoku Street and Nehoa Street in the Makiki area of Honolulu
The new structures will be constructed over the open parking area west of the existing
sanctuary of the church Adjacent properties to the church site are generally located
away from the common property lines with the exception of a small wooden building north
of the church site
The open parking area is fairly level near the existing church structure approxi
mately at an elevation of 116 0 feet above Mean Sea Level MSL The existing ground
surface at the southern half of the parking area then slopes down at an approximate
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2598 00 Page 5
gradient of 8 to 12 in a southwesterly direction to an approximate elevation of 106 feet
MSL at the southern corner
The open parking area is currently paved with asphalt concrete Surrounding the
northern and western sides of the parking area are planted areas and a plant nursery
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsoil conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling a total of
eight 8 borings to depths of 13 5 to 31 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Subsoil Conditions
The borings encountered cinder sand volcanic ash deposits to the maximum
drilled depths of 31 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the open parking area
The upper cinder is highly weathered and consists of brown to dark brown silty
sand which generally increases in density with depth from loose to medium dense in the
top 5 to 13 feet to medium dense to dense between the depths of 5 and 23 feet below
the existing ground surface
Grayish black cinder sand which is generally unweathered or slightly weathered
was encountered below the upper brown silty cinder sand This lower layer of cinder also
increases in density generally from medium dense to dense with depth
A localized cemented seam or zone was encountered approximately between the
depths of 22 5 and 24 0 feet below the ground surface in Boring No 6 It appears that
this cemented zone is localized However localized cementation is typical in cinder
deposits and similar cemented zones may also be encountered elsewhere at the site
during construction

Groundwater
A groundwater level was not encountered in the borings during our field
exploration However moist or wet zones were encountered between approximately 17
to 20 feet below the existing ground surface in Boring and 6 indicating the
probable presence of transient seepage zones
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
A relatively deep basement excavation of approximately 10 feet below the existing
ground surface is anticipated for the proposed construction We believe that the
proposed basement excavation will entail removal of the loose to medium dense upper
silty cinder Therefore spread footing foundations may be used to support the proposed
building additions by bearing directly on the underlying medium dense to dense cinder
sand
A groundwater level was not encountered in the borings within the anticipated
excavation depth The moist to wet zones encountered appear to be localized transient
seepage zones well below the excavation bottom As a result continuous excavation
dewatering is not anticipated
Adequate shoring support must be provided to the vertical excavation faces of the
basement excavation particularly adjacent to the sanctuary of the existing church
Various shoring systems each with its own cost and risks involved may be considered
Since more space seems to be available adjacent to Keeaumoku Street at the site
the excavation face adjacent to the street may be sloped back to a one and one half
horizontal to one vertical stable slope during construction

Site Grading
Relatively deep excavation of approximately 10 feet is anticipated for the proposed
basement construction Substantial backfilling behind the basement wall is also
anticipated where the excavation face is sloped to a 2H 1V slope
At the on set of earthwork the area within the project grading limits should be
grubbed and cleared of all vegetation debris and existing structures to reduce the
potential of contaminating the excavated material from the proposed basement
excavation
After the completion of basement excavation the underlying cinder subgrade
should be scarified to a minimum depth of six 6 inches below the finish floor level and
recompacted to a minimum of 90 of its maximum dry density as established by ASTM
Test Method D 1557 78
Any local soft or loose spots and or unsuitable materials encountered at the
excavated level should be removed and replaced with properly compacted select fill
material or the on site cinder sand The backfill should be placed in maximum 8 inch
loose lifts and compacted to obtain a minimum of 90 of its maximum dry density as
established by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
Where an excavation face behind a basement wall is to be sloped back to avoid
the use of shoring support we believe that a maximum slope ratio of one and one half
horizontal to one vertical may be used for the temporary cut slope during
construction
The wall backfill material should be placed in maximum 10 inch loose lifts and
compacted to obtain a minimum of 90 of its maximum dry density as established by
ASTM Test Method D 1557 78

Footing Foundation
Shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used to support the proposed
structures at the basement parking level The footing foundations may bear directly on
the underlying cinder sand subsoil below the basement parking
The footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade The excavated footing subgrade should be properly recom
pacted to a minimum of 95 of its maximum dry density as established by ASTM Test
Method D 1557 78 to provide a uniform support
We recommend that an allowable bearing capacity of 4 000 p s f be used to
design the footings bearing on the recompacted subgrade The design bearing value
may be increased by up to one third 1 3 for seismic wind and or other short term
loading conditions
It is estimated that footing settlements under the design bearing pressure will be
differential
Resistance to lateral loads acting on the proposed building structures may be
obtained by passive earth pressure and frictional resistance developed against the
footings We recommend that an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 p s f per foot of depth
be used to determine the passive earth pressure developed against the vertical faces of
the embedded footings A frictional coefficient of 0 45 may be used to compute the
frictional resistance developed between the concrete footings and the recompacted
footing subgrade

Excavation
Excavation up to 10 feet deep below the existing surface is anticipated for
construction of the basement level of the proposed building additions The basement
excavation must be properly supported with adequate shoring at its vertical excavation
faces especially adjacent to the existing church building
The choice of a shoring system depends on many considerations including the
cost and the risks involved The owner is urged to retain a specialty contractor to
perform further evaluation and the actual shoring design The following discussion only
provides a cursory evaluation for conceptual design input and construction cost
estimation purposes
Where available space allows the excavation face may also be sloped to a one
and one half horizontal to one vertical slope as discussed in the preceding Site
Grading section
Excavation Method
Based on the subsoil conditions we believe that the excavation operations should
not be difficult utilizing conventional mechanical earth moving equipment
In general the method and equipment to be used should be left to the choice of
the contractor within practical limits and safety considerations
Excavation Support
It appears that the vertical excavation faces in the cinder sand subsoils may be
supported with continuous steel sheeting or a soldier beam and lagging system
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2598 00 Page 10
If steel sheeting is used the sheetpiles must be properly interlocked during
installation prior to the basement excavation and be maintained tight against the
excavation faces during excavation The sheet piles should be maintained until
completion of the proposed basement parking The steel sheeting should be
driven properly with a suitable sheetpile hammer to achieve sufficient embedment
depth
A soldier beam and lagging system may be installed by driving steel H piles at
predetermined spacing along the anticipated excavation faces After the vertical
H pile supports have been driven to sufficient embedment depth below the
basement excavation timber lagging of sufficient thickness should be installed
progressively with the excavation and fit horizontally between the flanges of
adjacent H piles to retain the subsoil behind
It must be noted that some movement of the adjacent ground supported by the
shoring system might still occur because of changes in earth stresses which will
normally occur during excavation To reduce the potential for and magnitude of
adjacent ground movement additional lateral support should be provided to the
vertical members of the shoring system such as the sheeting or the H piles
A specialized lateral anchor system such as tiebacks may be used to maintain the
steel sheeting or the steel H piles tighter against the excavation faces at various
depths In general we believe that the tieback anchors may be particularly
effective in providing lateral support at locations near adjacent building footing
foundations Permits for extending the tiebacks below adjacent properties must
be obtained prior to construction
Alternately inclined rakers may be installed progressively with the basement
excavation at various levels to support the steel sheeting or H piles However we
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2598 00 Page 11
believe that the raker support may generally be expected to allow more adjacent
ground movement than the tieback system
Construction Precaution
The shoring system used must comply with all applicable safety requirements
established by all pertaining authorities The adequacy and safety of the design
and installation of the shoring system used should be made the sole responsibility
of the contractor His qualified representative who should be required to be
continuously present on site will have the best opportunity to promptly observe
any changing conditions during construction such as unforseen subsurface
conditions cemented layers unexpected high groundwater table unsuitable
construction sequence or techniques etc which might affect the excavation
shoring stability
Even with good construction procedures some movement of the shoring system
might still occur because of changes in earth stresses which will normally occur
during excavation The magnitude of movement will depend greatly on workman
ship for example how quickly and tightly the excavation support is installed It
also depends on the types of subsoils the depth and width of excavation and also
the progress rate of excavation
In view of the complexity of the problem it is difficult to accurately predict
movement of a shoring system and its adjacent ground Therefore it is important
to realize that excavation support should be installed properly and as early as
practicable and that the adjacent ground be continuously monitored for cracks
dips and or other indications of movements such as excavation face sloughing

Parking Pavement
In general we believe that the anticipated cinder sand subgrade below the
proposed basement parking can provide competent bearing support for parking
pavement if properly recompacted to at least 95 of its maximum dry density as
established by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
If concrete slab on grade is used for the parking pavement the following
preliminary design section may be used
6 Inch Portland Cement Concrete
6 Inch Base Course 95 Compaction
12 Inch Total Pavement Thickness
If asphalt concrete parking pavement is elected the following preliminary design
section may be used
2 Inch Asphalt Concrete
6 Inch Base Course 95 Compaction
8 Inch Total Pavement Thickness
Laboratory CBR tests should be performed on the actual subgrade soil samples
during construction and the above design sections may be revised if necessary The
recommended pavement sections assume light to medium passenger traffic and good
drainage provided for areas adjacent to the pavements

Design Review
Plans and specifications for the proposed construction should be forwarded to the
project geotechnical engineer for review and written comments prior to construction This
review is needed to determine adherence to the recommendations given herein If this
review is not made the geotechnical engineer cannot assume responsibility for
misinterpretation of the recommendations
Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the project geotechnical engineer be retained to provide
geotechnical engineering services during construction of the foundation phases of the
work This is to observe compliance with the design concepts specifications or
recommendations and to allow design changes in the event that subsurface conditions
differ from that anticipated prior to start of construction The recommendations given in
this report are contingent upon such observations
If actual exposed rock soil conditions encountered during construction are different
from those assumed or considered in this report then appropriate modifications to the
design should be made
LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based upon
information obtained from the borings Variations of conditions between the borings may
occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until
construction is underway If variations then appear evident it will be necessary to
reevaluate the recommendations given in this report
The locations of the borings were determined by taping from existing structures
and or objects indicated on the topographic survey of the site Elevations of the borings
were approximately determined by interpolation from elevation points on the topographic
GEOLABS HAWAII

survey map provided by KOP Hawaii on The physical locations and
elevations of the borings should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the
method used
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the boring points depict
the approximate boundaries between soil types and as such may denote a gradual
transition
Water level data from the borings were collected at the times given on the graphic
representations and or in the text of this report These data have been reviewed and
interpretations made in the formulation of this report However it must be noted that
fluctuation may occur due to variations in rainfall tides temperature and other factors
