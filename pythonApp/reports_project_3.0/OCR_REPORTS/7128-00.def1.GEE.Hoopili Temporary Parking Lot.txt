1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project involves the development of a temporary parking lot to serve 
passengers using the Honolulu Rail Transit system in the Ewa – Kapolei area.  It is our 
understanding that, subject to funding levels, portions of the parking lot will be surfaced

potential to be paved in the future.  Detailed plans of the parking area and related 
infrastructure were not available for this exploration.  However, it is anticipated that the 
infrastructure will include drainage, electrical and lighting service and irrigation.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age. As the volcanic activity in Waianae Volcano ceased, lava flows 
from Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau, 
now known as the Schofield Plateau. The project site is located on the southwestern 
flank of the Waianae Volcano. 
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of Schofield Plateau 
generated sediments, which were transported to the coast. In the project vicinity and to 
the south, these sediments have accumulated and are interbedded with marine 
sediments and coral/algal reef formations, forming a sedimentary wedge. The 
thickness of the sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate 
Route H-1 Highway to over 1,000 feet at Ewa Beach. This wedge forms the Ewa Plain 
and serves as the confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of 
Southern Oahu. Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time 
through the present.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to 
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. 
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that 
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and

Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is on the northern portion of the Ewa - Kapolei area on the Island 
of Oahu, Hawaii; and, is a portion of the Ho`opili development area, which is roughly 
bounded by the H-1 Interstate Freeway to the north, Fort Weaver Road to the east, the 
new North – South Road (Kualakai Parkway) to the west and the new East – West 
Road (Keahumoa Parkway) to the south.  The temporary rail parking lot site is situated 
in agricultural fields to the southeast of Farrington Highway and to the southwest of the 
existing Board of Water Supply pumping station. 
At the time of our field exploration, the site was generally being used for 
commercial farming of various vegetable and herb crops. Based on the topographic 
plan provided, the project site generally slopes down from the northeastern corner 
(approximately elevation +175 feet MSL) toward the central southeastern boundary 
(approximately elevation +156 feet MSL).

3.2.1 Site Preparation 
Most of the site is currently under cultivation. The soil in the portions that are not 
being cultivated is generally dry and friable with shrinkage cracks up to 1 to 2 feet 
deep. Because of the observed ground conditions and previous experience in the 
vicinity, we recommend implementing special site preparation procedures during 
the earthwork construction for the project.  
In general, the areas within the contract grading limits should be cleared and 
grubbed thoroughly at the on-set of earthwork. Vegetation, debris, deleterious 
materials, and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly 
off-site or disposed in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of 
the excavated materials. The upper 12 inches of the soils below the existing ground 
surface should be removed as part of the clearing and grubbing operations, and the 
resulting spoils should be disposed properly off-site (or used in landscaped areas, if 
appropriate) because they contain an appreciable amount of organic materials. The 
upper 12 inches of the soils below the ground surface that will be removed as part 
of the clearing and grubbing operations may be classified as “unsuitable” materials 
and should not be used as fill for this project.

3.2.2 Excavation 
Based on the anticipated grading and on our field exploration, excavation will

The above discussion regarding the rippability of the surface materials is based on

3.2.3 Fills and Backfills 
General fills may consist of on-site clayey soils placed at a minimum of 2 percent 
above optimum moisture content.  If required, imported general fill materials should 
consist of soil and rock materials less than 6 inches in size with a CBR value of 8 or 
more and with a maximum swell of less than 2 percent when tested in accordance 
with ASTM D1883. 
If required, structural fill material should be used for the top 2 to 3 feet of fill below 
structures. The structural fill should consist of non-expansive granular material, 
such as crushed coral, basalt or cinder sand.  The material should be well-graded 
from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and 
should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The 
material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a 
maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested in accordance with 
ASTM D1883.  Geolabs should test imported fill materials for conformance with 
these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its intended use. 
Geolabs should observe and/or test imported fill materials for their suitability prior to 
being transported to the site for the intended use. 
Base course and select borrow subbase material for road pavement construction 
should consist of crushed basalt aggregate and should conform to the City and 
County of Honolulu, Department of Public Works, “Standard Specifications for 
Public Works Construction,” dated September 1986.

3.2.4 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements  
Fill, borrow fill and backfill, should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Compaction should be accomplished with sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, 
multiple-wheel pneumatic-tired rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction 
equipment. 
Moisture-conditioning and compaction of the fill materials, especially with clayey 
soils, are critical elements of earthwork; therefore Geolabs should conduct 
observations and soil density tests during site grading to assist the contractor in 
obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper moisture content. 
Where compaction is less than required, additional compactive effort should be 
applied with adjustment of moisture content, as necessary, to obtain the specified 
compaction. It should be noted that the moisture requirement of the fills and 
subgrades (about 2 percent above the optimum moisture) is an important 
requirement particularly when the on-site clayey soils are used. 
3.2.5 Cut and Fill Slopes 
In general, we believe that cut slopes into the soil material may be cut at an 
inclination of two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. 
Fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. Fills 
should be keyed and benched into existing slopes to provide stability of the new fill 
against sliding.  The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue 
up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement 
recommendations.

Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope

3.3 
Pavement Design 
For this project, we anticipate vehicle loading will consist primarily of passenger 
vehicles, light pick-up trucks, and vans.  We have assumed that the pavement subgrade 
soils will be similar to the alluvial materials generally encountered during our field 
exploration.  
It is also our understanding that the future parking area will be partially paved 
with asphaltic concrete (AC) pavement and the remainder will be gravel surfaced 
parking area with an option to pave with AC subject for future available funding.  
Because of this option, our pavement design incorporates the subbase course and base 
course that would be required for an AC pavement under the gravel surfacing. 
For the gravel surface parking areas, we offer two options.  The first option would 
be to surface the area with an additional 2 inches of base course that could be removed 
in the event that it is desired to pave with AC. 
The second option would be to use one of the commercially available gravel 
paving systems that uses clean gravel and has synthetic material cells to contain the 
gravel and stabilize the traffic and parking surfaces.  We reviewed available information 
on several of these systems and found that they appear to be generally equivalent in 
function and installation. 
Many of the systems incorporate a filter fabric between the surface gravel and 
the underlying base layer.  We recommend the using a geotextile separator to facilitate 
removal of the gravel layer and preparation of the base course for placement of an AC 
surface course if desired.

In addition, paved areas should be sloped, and drainage gradients should be

around the pipes and to reduce compaction of the backfill, thus reducing the possibility

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon 
information obtained from our field exploration and review of available soils data. 
Variations of conditions may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may 
not become evident until design or construction is underway. If variations then appear 
evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the discussions presented herein. 
The boring sample locations are approximate, having been spot located on 
Google Earth™ to obtain waypoint coordinates for the selected locations and using a 
hand-held Garmin™ Oregon 450T to navigate to the boring locations for staking in the 
field. Elevations of the borings were estimated from the waypoint location properties in 
Google Earth™. The physical locations and elevations of the field borings should be 
considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification breaks shown on graphic representations of the borings depict 
the approximate boundaries between soil types and, as such, may denote a gradual 
transition. We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it must be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by the drilling and sampling of 
three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, to depths of about 20 to 21.1 feet 
below the existing ground surface using a truck-mounted drilling rig with continuous 
flight augers. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified 
Soil Classification System, as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-3. 
Soil samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler or a 3-inch OD Modified California Sampler with liners 
using a 140 pound hammer with a 30-inch drop. The blow counts needed to drive the 
sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected surface soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and as an aid in classifying the surface 
soils.  Graphic presentations of the test results are provided on Plate B-1. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM C117 & C136) was performed on a selected soil 
sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the disturbed near-surface materials 
and to aid in soil classification. A graphic presentation of the test results is provided on 
Plate B-2. 
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-3 through B-5. 
Two one-inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the on-site soils. The test results 
are summarized on Plate B-6.
