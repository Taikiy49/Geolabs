INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed MRI building addition at the North Hawaii Community Hospital in the
Kamuela area on the Island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site
are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
derived from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses for the
proposed project The recommendations presented herein are intended for the design of
building foundations slab on grade support and site preparation only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located within the existing North Hawaii Community Hospital
complex in the Kamuela area on the Island of Hawaii Based on the information provided
it is proposed to construct an addition with a footprint of approximately 4 000 square feet on
the southeast side of the existing hospital We understand that the addition will be a
one story structure and will abut the existing hospital building at the southeast corner as
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Structural and grading details were not available at the time this report was prepared
In general we anticipate the structural loads for the building to be relatively light Based on
our field observations we envision that only minor grading consisting of cuts and fills on the
order of approximately 2 feet or less would be required to achieve the design finished grades
at the site

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which had activity during
recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea
which are both still active
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield
Volcano The project site is underlain by basaltic lava flows of the Hamakua Volcanic Series
of Mauna Kea which were deposited during the Pleistocene Epoch Steams and Macdonald
1946 The Hamakua Volcanic Series is typically composed of basaltic lava flows which are
mantled by a surface layer of palagonitized ash locally referred to as Pahala Ash The
Pahala Ash is generally highly sensitive and thixotropic in nature The ash soil at the project
site includes some Pahala Ash and some ash where the in situ moisture content is generally
lower In addition the volcanic ash soil at the site exists in a friable state
Based on previous experience in the Waimea Kamuela area the lava flows underlying
the site are of the a a form which is characterized by a rough spiny or rubbly surface of
fragmental clinker A dense core of massive basalt which frequently has thinner less
continuous layers of clinker below the massive core underlies the clinker The clinker
surface of the lavas at the site have been mantled by the Pahala Ash as discussed above

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the MRI building addition site were explored by drilling
and sampling two test borings extending to depths of about 20 feet below the existing ground
surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our field exploration encountered volcanic ash consisting of stiff to very stiff
sandy silts from the ground surface to about 8 to 9 5 feet below the existing ground surface

quantity with depth The volcanic ash layer was underlain by slightly fractured to massive
slightly weathered to relatively unweathered basalt rock formation In addition our borings
encountered small cavities of about 0 5 feet in height and a thin clinker layer of about 1 5 feet
in height The clinker layer appeared to be mixed with volcanic ash
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings at the time of our field exploration
However it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to seasonal
precipitation storm surge conditions and other factors

Building Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe that
a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be used
to support the new building addition Our borings encountered volcanic ash soils near the
ground surface The volcanic ash soils are generally light and susceptible to loss of strength
upon saturation Therefore we recommend that the soils at the footing subgrade level be
over excavated a minimum of 24 inches below the bottom of footing elevation The resulting
over excavation should be backfilled with compacted select granular fill materials such as
select borrow subbase aggregate subbase In addition the over excavation and backfill
below the footings should extend a minimum of 12 inches beyond the sides of the footing
The volcanic ash soil subgrade should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to at least 90 percent
relative compaction The select granular fill materials should be moisture conditioned and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to
the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the
same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum

moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot psf may be
used for the design of footings bearing on the 24 inch compacted select granular fills This
bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient
loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces The footings should be embedded
a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade Care should be exercised
when over excavating below the footings for the new building footing located adjacent to the
existing building to avoid undermining the existing building foundation Therefore it may be
necessary to excavate and backfill the 24 inch thick compacted select granular fill below the
footings in increments of about 10 foot long sections
Where foundations are located next to the existing foundations retaining structures
utility trenches and or easements they should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary
plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the surrounding foundations utility trench
or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to reduce the
potential for surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and
to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate that total settlements of footings bearing on the 24 inch
compacted select granular fill may be on the order of about 1 inch We estimate that
differential settlements between adjacent footings supported on the 24 inch compacted
select granular fill to be on the order of about 0 5 inches
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 40 may be used for footings bearing on the compacted select granular fills
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid

Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the new building addition
Due to the presence of volcanic ash soils we recommend that a minimum 6 inch layer of
select granular fill be placed below the slab for more uniform support Prior to placement of
the select granular fill the ground surface or subgrade soils should be scarified to a minimum
depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction The select granular fill should be
placed as soon as possible after preparation of the slab subgrade to reduce the potential for
drying out of the subgrade soils
In general we recommend that a minimum 4 inch layer of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM
C 33 No 67 gradation be incorporated below the concrete slab as a cushion layer above
the 6 inch thick select granular fill To reduce the potential for moisture infiltration a plastic
vapor barrier may be considered below the slab A 2 inch thick moist fine sand layer may
be considered above the plastic vapor barrier to aid in the curing of the concrete Provision
of the 2 inches of fine sand above the vapor barrier will be left up to the discretion of the
project structural engineer Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be
considered since they can better mask minor slab cracking
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project Due to the
presence of volcanic ash soils at the surface we recommend that the walkways be
supported on a minimum 6 inch thick layer of select granular fill compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction Concrete for the exterior walkways may be placed directly
GEOLABS INC
W O 4484 00 Page 9
on the 6 inch thick select granular fill Crack control joints should be provided in the concrete
walkway at intervals equal to the width of the walkways and at right angle intersections
In general the thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be
embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade It should be noted
that areas adjacent to the slab should be backfilled tightly against the edges of the slab with
on site soil materials In addition areas adjacent to the slabs should be graded to divert
water away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the slabs and
foundations
Site Preparation
We envision that site grading work consisting of minor cuts and fills on the order of
approximately 2 feet or less will be required for the proposed project construction In general
we recommend that the following guidelines be used for the minor site grading anticipated
at the site At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials
within the grading limits should be removed and disposed of properly off site
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas
designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over excavated to expose firm
or dense material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill
The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site Contract
documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and
compacted fill placement to account for variation in the over excavation quantities
Existing underground utilities to be abandoned at the site should be removed The
resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with the on site materials
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in 6 to 8 inch loose lifts
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of
the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum

moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
After clearing and grubbing areas beneath the future building area should be
excavated to the bottom of the select granular fill recommended below the building slab
and or foundation The volcanic ash soil subgrade should be scarified to a depth of about
8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction In addition the subgrade soils should be
wetted and kept moist until placement of the select granular fills
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend
that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or scarified and prepared again as
recommended above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to
inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and
replacement with well compacted fill at no additional cost to the owner
The select granular fills and imported fill materials if required should consist of
non expansive granular material such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand
The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches
in largest dimension In addition it should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles
passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more
and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less Imported fill material should be
tested by Geolabs for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the
project site for the intended use
Site preparation should be observed by a representative from Geolabs It is important

Utility Trenches
We envision that some new utility lines and utility connections will likely be needed for
the proposed project In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining
granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also
be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide
adequate support around the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce
the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate
support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of the
backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils The backfill
materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in
about 8 inch loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground subsidence Where
trenches are located below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the
pavement grade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
It should be noted that the on site volcanic ash soils are susceptible to loss of strength
when saturated Therefore the finished grades outside the building addition should be
sloped to shed water away from the existing and new structure foundations to reduce the
potential for ponding Excessive landscape watering near the foundation areas should also
be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and
drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
building foundations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be properly
backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths of about 20 feet below
the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the drilled borings are shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as
the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses
a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit
is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
One one inch ring swell test was performed on a relatively undisturbed sample to
evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results of the test are
summarized on Plate B 1
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of the
soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The results of the
test are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic
representation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
