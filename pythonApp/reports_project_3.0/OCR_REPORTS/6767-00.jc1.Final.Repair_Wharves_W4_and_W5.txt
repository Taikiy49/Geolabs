Dear Mr. Lock: 
This letter report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the proposed Repair Wharves W4 and W5 project at the Joint Base Pearl 
Harbor-Hickam facility on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general 
vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This letter report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations 
derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the 
project. The geotechnical recommendations presented herein are intended for the design 
of foundations only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to 
the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The design-build project consists of repairing the existing W4 and W5 wharves, 
including replacing some of the existing 18-inch square concrete fender piles, installing 
new 24-inch square concrete fender piles, and repairing the concrete deck and other 
related works. The project scope generally includes demolition and removal of the 
existing deteriorated and/or damaged concrete pile fender system and replacement with 
a new fender system consisting of precast, prestressed concrete fender pile clusters 
with plastic walers, blocks and chocks. 
We understand the new concrete fender pile system consists of eighty-eight 
24-inch and twelve 18-inch square precast, prestressed concrete piles. Based on the 
information provided in the RFP contract documents, the concrete fender piles will need 
to extend from the top of pile at approximately Elevation +109 feet to a tip elevation of 
about Elevation +39 feet. It should be noted that all elevations in this report are 
referenced to U.S. Navy Datum (+100 feet USND = Mean Lower Low Water).

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. Waianae Volcano became extinct 
while Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below 
Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank. The Koolau Volcano reached the end of its 
main shield-building phase about 2 million years ago. 
After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which erosion cut valleys deep into 
the Koolau Shield Volcano, volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by 
cinder and tuff cone formation. These series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic 
Series. 
The forces of weathering and erosion attacked the Schofield Plateau, generating 
sediments, which were transported to the coast. In the vicinity of the project site and 
further south, these alluvial sediments were interbedded with marine sediments, 
coral-algal reef formations, and volcanic deposits of the Honolulu Volcanic Series to form a

designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 106, extending to depths of about 75 to 95 feet

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
We understand it is proposed to install a new fender system consisting of 24-inch 
square precast, prestressed concrete fender piles, for berthing of the specified submarines 
and/or surface vessels. In addition, twelve existing 18-inch square concrete fender piles 
are planned to be replaced with the same size precast, prestressed concrete piles, in 
support of the specified surface vessels only. In general, the concrete fender pile system 
will absorb the energy imparted by the berthing vessels. Therefore, the lateral load 
capacity of the precast, prestressed concrete fender piles is the main design consideration 
for the fender pile system. 
Based on the information provided, we understand two 24-inch square concrete 
fender piles will be considered to absorb 100 percent of the berthing loads generated from 
the submarines, while four 24-inch square concrete fender piles are designed to support 
the surface vessels at each fender location. 
Based on the information provided, we understand the maximum lateral load from 
the submarines imposed on a single 24-inch square concrete fender pile is approximately 
13 kips, acting between about Elevation +103 and Elevation +83 feet via a deep draft 
Camel fender. For modeling purposes, we used a distributed load of about 650 pounds 
per lineal foot applied between about 6 and 26 feet below the top of the fender pile in our 
lateral load analyses of the fender piles. Please note that the top of fender pile is at about 
Elevation +109 feet. 
For the surface vessels, the maximum lateral load imposed on the single 24-inch 
square concrete fender pile is approximately 26 kips, acting between about Elevation +104 
and Elevation +102 feet via a Yokohama pneumatic fender. A distributed load of about 
13,000 pounds per lineal foot was modeled in our lateral load analyses, applied between 
about 5 and 7 feet below the top of the fender pile. 
We understand that the maximum lateral load imposed on a single 18-inch square 
concrete fender pile is approximately 43 kips, acting between about Elevation +104 and 
Elevation +102 feet. A distributed load of about 21,500 pounds per lineal foot was modeled 
in our lateral load analysis, applied between about 5 and 7 feet below the top of the fender 
pile. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site and the above 
structural loading provided, we believe two different pile tip elevations likely will be required 
to provide sufficient lateral support and bearing resistance for the self-supporting fender 
piles. Based on our engineering analyses, we recommend extending the fender piles to 
the minimum tip elevations presented in the following table. For ease of reference, we 
included the pile tip elevations specified in the RFP documents and the new 
recommended pile tip elevations for comparison purposes.

We also recommend pre-drilling the 24-inch pile locations to near the new pile tip 
elevations to clear the pile driving path from obstructions and to reduce the need for hard 
driving conditions to install the fender piles to the design tip elevations. Due to the 
presence of dense coralline formation within the pile depth, we recommend predrilling by a 
34-inch diameter auger in Pile Zones A and C. Due to the presence of soft to medium stiff 
soil conditions along the pile shaft between Bent 25 and Bent 54, we recommend 
predrilling by a maximum 20-inch diameter auger in Pile Zone B. The primary purpose of 
utilizing a maximum 20-inch diameter auger for predrilling at these pile locations is to 
preserve some of the friction between the pre-drilled hole and concrete pile shaft to 
support the weight of the pile. 
We recommend extending the new 18-inch square concrete fender piles to the 
same tip elevation presented in the above table. The 18-inch fender pile locations should 
be predrilled by a maximum 26-inch diameter auger to near the new pile tip elevations. 
Based on our review of the RFP documents, we understand the existing 18-inch square 
concrete fender piles in Wharf W4 were installed to Elevation +45 feet. As-built information 
in Wharf W5 was not available at the time of this report was prepared. Therefore, the new 
18-inch fender piles likely will be extended about 6 or 11 feet deeper than the existing 
18-inch fender piles. 
The lateral load analyses were performed using the program LPILE-plus for 
Windows, which is a microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference, laterally loaded pile 
program originally developed at the University of Texas at Austin. The program solves for 
deflection and bending moment along a pile under lateral loads as a function of depth. The 
analysis was carried out with the use of non-linear “p-y” curves to represent soil moduli. 
The lateral deflection was then computed using the appropriate soil moduli at various 
depths. 
In general, we assumed the boundary condition near the top of the fender pile (at 
about Elevation +109 feet) is pinned (free to rotate with zero lateral deflection). In addition, 
we used a mudline elevation of about +65 feet resulting in a free-standing pile length of 
about 44 feet. The following table summarizes the results of our lateral load analyses for

report. However, it must be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in tides,

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling six borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 106, extending to depths of about 75 to 95 feet 
below the water level. The approximate boring locations are shown on the General Site 
Plan, Plate 2. We drilled the borings using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with rotary 
wash drilling/coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operations on a near-continuous 
(full-time) basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil 
Classification System, as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-6. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings in general 
accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
we obtained some samples from the borings in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of rock and/or hard materials encountered at the site were obtained 
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The material left in the hollow core of the bit 
is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were described in 
general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log 
Legend, Plate A-0.2. 
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from 
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 4 feet of material is 
recovered from a 5-foot core run, the recovery would be 80 percent and would be shown 
on the Logs of Borings as REC = 80%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound 
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any 
drilling-induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 
5-foot core run, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of 
Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) 
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and 
evaluation of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at 
the appropriate sample depths. 
Eight Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 & C 136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on 
Plates B-1 and B-2. 
Four Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth. Graphic presentation of test results is 
provided on Plate B-3. 
Three strained-controlled, consolidated-drained direct shear tests were 
performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the 
in-situ soils. The direct shear tests were performed in accordance with ASTM D 3080, 
and the test results are presented on Plates B-4 through B-6. 
Three 
Unconsolidated 
Undrained 
Triaxial

(TXUU)

(ASTM D 2850) were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained 
shear strength of the silty and clayey soils encountered. The approximate in-situ 
effective overburden pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the 
relatively “undisturbed” soil sample. The test results and the stress-strain curves are 
presented on Plates B-7 through B-9.
