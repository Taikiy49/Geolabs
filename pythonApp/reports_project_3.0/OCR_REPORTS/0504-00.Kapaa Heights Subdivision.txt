FIELD INVESTIGATIONS

Three 3 inch diameter borings were drilled with a
portable auger drilling machine to depths of 15 to 20
feet below the existing ground surface Three additional
holes were drilled with a 4 inch hand auger to depths of
5 feet below the existing ground surface to check the
percolation rate in the upper soils
The material encountered in all borings was a reddish
brown fine sandy clayey silt that classified as ML in
accordance with the Unified Soil Classification system
Based on our knowledge of the local geology it appears
that the site soils are residual in nature The basalt
rock was subjected to chemical weathering that altered
the rock to soil Because of the uneven weathering rate
the soil will vary with depth and horizontal position
This type of soil structure will cause varying rates of
percolation
The percolation tests were performed by filling the borings
with water and allowing the soils to become saturated No
casing was required in any of the borings The holes were
then refilled with water and the drop of water level was
recorded Table 1 presents the results of our percolation
tests

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The soils encountered in our borings consist of residual
stiff clayey silt The upper portion of the site soils
contain more clay and hence the percolation rates are low
The soil becomes less clayey with an increase in depth
The high variability of the site soils were reflected in
the percolation rates which varied from 0 1 to 1 0 gallons
per square foot per hour
It is our opinion that an average rate of percolation
should be assumed for the site soils This will preclude
