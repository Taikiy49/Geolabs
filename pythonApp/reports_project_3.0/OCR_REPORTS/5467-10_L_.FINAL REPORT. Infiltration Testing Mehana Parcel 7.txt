SECTION 1 GENERAL
1 1
Introduction
This report presents the results of our infiltration tests performed for the Mehana
at Kapolei Parcel 7 project in Kapolei on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
A previous geotechnical engineering exploration report dated was
issued for the project This report supplements our previous report and summarizes the
results of our infiltration testing and engineering analyses for the storm water runoff
drainage system for the project The recommendations presented herein are intended
for the design of the detention and infiltration system for the subsurface disposal of
storm water runoff The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to
the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2
Project Considerations
The project involves the development of drainage improvements by means of
subsurface storm water detention infiltration system for the Mehana at Kapolei Parcel 7
development in Kapolei Oahu Hawaii Based on preliminary information we understand
that underground stormwater chambers are planned at four different locations within the
development to impound surface runoff and allow it to infiltrate into the ground
In order to provide information for the design of the drainage improvements we
conducted a geotechnical engineering exploration by excavating five 7 to 9 feet deep
test pits at the proposed infiltration chamber locations In addition five double ring
infiltrometer tests were performed at the bottom of these test pits representing the
anticipated installation depths of the underground chambers
1 3

The purpose of our work was to obtain infiltration data of the in situ soils to assist
in the stormwater disposal design In order to accomplish this we performed an
exploration program consisting of the following tasks and work efforts

Site Description
The project site is on the southern outlying area of the City of Kapolei on the
Island of Oahu Hawaii Parcel 7 is situated on the southwest central portion of the
Mehana at Kapolei Development and is bounded by the proposed Road A
Kakala Street to the north Proposed Road K Kama aha Boulevard to the west
Road W Wakea Street to the east and Renton Road to the south
At the time of our field exploration Parcel 7 site was largely undeveloped with a
light to heavy cover of dry land vegetation such as Keawe Haole Koa and Kamatsile
trees with an undergrowth of dry grass The project site is relatively flat with elevations
ranging from about 56 feet MSL near the northwest project limits to about 52 feet MSL
near the southeast corner The only visible improvement is the trapezoidal road prism
along Road E that cuts across Parcel 7 from north to south
2 2
Subsurface Conditions
We explored the subsurface conditions at the proposed underground infiltration
chambers by excavating five test pits designated as Test Pit Nos 1 through 5
extending to depths from 7 0 to 9 0 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate test pit locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our field exploration encountered a thin layer of alluvial soils consisting of clayey
silt overlying coral formation at very shallow depths below the existing ground surface
Some solution cavities were encountered in the excavated test pits however these
cavities were generally in filled with alluvial clay
In general the upper 3 to 4 feet of coral formation encountered at the test pit
excavation sites are relatively hard and massive necessitating the use of larger more
powerful equipment to excavate The underlying coral formation generally below 5 feet
is more friable and readily breaks into sandy gravel with fines during excavation
We did not encounter groundwater in the test pits at the time of our field
exploration However it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change

Infiltration Testing
The infiltration rate of water into the subsurface soils were characterized by
performing infiltration tests using a double ring infiltrometer at the bottom of each of the
five excavated test pits in accordance with ASTM D3385 Standards
The tests were performed by driving two open concentric cylinders into the
ground partially filling the rings with water and maintaining a constant water level The
volume of liquid added to the inner ring to maintain a constant water level is the
measure of the volume of water that infiltrates the soil The incremental volumes
infiltrated during timed intervals were converted into infiltration velocities expressed in
centimeters per hour cm hr and plotted versus elapsed time as shown in our
infiltration test data Appendix B

dominated by a surface layer of alluvial soils consisting of silty clay generally ranging in
thickness from less than a foot to as thick as 2 feet in some localized areas overlying
hard coral formation near the surface becoming more friable at approximately 5 feet and
below

our field exploration However it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to
change due to rainfall time of year seasonal precipitation surface water runoff and
other factors

Fills and Backfills
We envision an underground infiltration system consisting of a series of
prefabricated stormwater retention and dispensing chambers In order to facilitate
installation and provide for more uniform bearing support we recommend using a
gravel substrate or bedding layer consisting of crusher run free draining
materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C33 No 67 gradation or similar
with a minimum thickness of at least 6 inches spread over the base of the
excavation The chamber elements may be backfilled with similar materials of
crusher run rock or coarse gravel up to 1 foot above the top and covered with a
suitable filter fabric such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent prior to backfilling Backfills
may consist of the excavated coralline soil that is not contaminated with on site
clay materials

Detention Infiltration System Design
Based on the subsurface conditions explored at the site we anticipate that the
hard near surface coralline layer encountered at the site would provide a
semi impermeable barrier that would inhibit the rapid percolation of stormwater into the
substrata during a storm event Therefore it is our opinion that a properly designed
stormwater management system that would channel runoff into subsurface detention
chambers located in the underlying more permeable horizon is a viable alternative for
this project
The infiltration rates measured in our study indicates a range that generally fall
under a Hydrologic Soil Group HSG category of A and B corresponding to infiltration
