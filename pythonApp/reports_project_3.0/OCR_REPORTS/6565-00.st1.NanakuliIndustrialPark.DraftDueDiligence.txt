Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau Shield Volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and

forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae Volcano became extinct
while Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below
Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed a
broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau
The Waianae Mountain Range is composed of layered basaltic lava flows and
pyroclastic materials which are grouped and classified as the Waianae Volcanic Series
The Waianae Volcanic Series is divided into the lower middle and upper volcanic
members The lower member comprises lava flows and associated pyroclastic rocks that
built the main mass of the Waianae Shield Volcano The middle member consists of rock
that accumulated and gradually filled the vast volcanic caldera The upper member is a
relatively thin capping layer that covered the entire top of the shield volcano late in its
history of evolution
Once the Waianae Shield Volcano formed a long period of deep erosion
sedimentation and subsidence of the Island of Oahu occurred which produced the large
valleys of the western side of the shield volcano These erosional valleys were filled
gradually with enormous accumulations of alluvium and colluvium deposited as a
combined result of stream erosion base level rise sea level rise and subsidence of the
island mass Over time and as a result of sea level fluctuation some of the older alluvial
and colluvial deposits were buried and through consolidation and cementation formed
relatively dense deposits However the current surface soil deposits above these older
deposits typically are found to be unconsolidated
The Island of Oahu is no longer sinking beneath the sea however the effects of
the early whole island subsidence combined with the added effect of major Pleistocene
Age sea level fluctuations worked to create drowned river valley systems with the
development of coral reefs The coral reefs and lithified sand dunes may be observed
exposed subaerially on Oahu and buried in submarine deposits well offshore of the island
The coral reefs and sand dune deposits were formed in shallow offshore seas and
coastlines during periods of higher and lower sea levels associated with the effects of the
advance and retreat of glaciers which controlled the volume of water contained in the
seas Today some of the shallow sea coralline deposits are visible on the flanks of
mountains or buried beneath terrestrial sediments evidence of the higher and lower
stands of the sea in prehistoric time

Subsurface Conditions
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project by drilling and sampling
seven borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 7 at the approximate locations shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were advanced to depths of about 20 1 to 26 5 feet
below the existing ground surface
Based on our field exploration and the geologic setting the subsurface materials at
the site generally consist of materials deposited from further up slope along Pu u
Heleakala alluvium and colluvium At the lower elevations of the site along Lualualei
Naval Road some deposits of coralline detritus mixed with alluvium and colluvium may be
anticipated at greater depths
Our borings generally encountered adobe clay soils and colluvium from the ground
surface extending down to the maximum depth explored of about 26 5 feet below the
ground surface In general adobe clay soils are highly expansive and consist of very stiff
clays The colluvium encountered generally consists of stiff to hard silty clays with varying
amounts of sands gravel cobbles and boulders Along the areas with lower elevations
the colluvium was mixed with coralline detritus consisting of dense sands and gravel
Groundwater was not encountered in our borings at the time of our field exploration
However groundwater levels can fluctuate depending on seasonal precipitation
groundwater withdrawal and or injection and other factors

Foundations
We understand that details of the proposed structures and layout have not been
determined at the time this letter report was prepared However we understand that the
photovoltaic panels generally will be relatively light weight structures supported on a post
column Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe
that the photovoltaic panels may be supported on a micropile or similar small diameter
drilled foundation system
We envision that the photovoltaic panels may be supported on 3 to 5 inch diameter
micropiles extending down to about 6 to 10 feet below the ground surface The size and
depth of micropiles should be evaluated during the design phase based on the required
structural load demand It should be noted that presence of boulders and cobbles in the
colluvium may cause some difficult drilling conditions for the micropile foundation
installation The presence of boulders and cobbles in the colluvium also would pose
appreciable difficulties for foundations that are driven into the ground
We envision that support facilities consisting of single story structures may be
required for the project In general we recommend utilizing shallow spread and or
continuous footings to support the new buildings and appurtenant structures
Our limited field exploration encountered varying subsurface conditions across the
project site Therefore we recommend the subsurface conditions be evaluated further by
conducting a design level field exploration program consisting of drilling and sampling
additional borings during the design phase of the project when the type and distribution of
the proposed structures are better defined

As mentioned previously relatively thick deposits of adobe clays were encountered
in our borings Based on our experience the on site adobe clays soils may exhibit high
shrink swell potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations

We envision the photovoltaic panels generally will be supported on relatively short
micropile foundations Depths of the micropile foundations should be evaluated further
during the design phase to mitigate the effects of potentially expansive soil conditions
In addition we envision that support facilities consisting of slab on grade structures
flatworks and access roadways may be required for the project Unless slabs on grade
constructed above the expansive soils are properly designed there is a potential for future
distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of the
clayey soils due to changes in the moisture content
Therefore over excavation of the adobe clay soils likely will be necessary under
below the foundations slabs on grade and pavements may be on the order of about
36 inches in areas underlain by the highly expansive soils The resulting over excavation
would need to be backfilled with non expansive select granular fill Select granular fills
generally consist of well graded granular soils with a maximum dimension of 3 inches or
less Due to the expansive nature of the on site materials select granular fill materials
would need to be imported to the site
Rockfall
As mentioned previously many boulders scattered around the site were observed
during our field exploration The sizes of the boulders were typically about 2 to 4 feet in
largest dimension with some boulders of up to about 8 feet in largest dimension The
presence of these boulders indicates that potential rockfall hazards exist at the project site
In general the rockfall hazard decreases further down slope in the flatter areas of the
project site along Lualualei Naval Road However many boulders were still present at
areas along Lualualei Naval Road
Based on the topography of the site rockfall hazard and its impact to the proposed
development should be considered and studied during the design phase It should be
noted that an engineering slope hazard evaluation may be required by the City and County
of Honolulu Department of Planning and Permitting for the development of the proposed
project due to the proximity of the project site to the adjacent slopes Section of
the Revised Ordinances of Honolulu
Slope Stability
Based on the available maps the ground surface elevations at the project site
range from about 65 to 150 feet MSL within the proposed Nanakuli Industrial Park In
general this corresponds to slope inclinations of about 10 percent or less In areas up
slope of the proposed Nanakuli Industrial Park limits the slope inclination increases
substantially to over 20 percent
The project site generally is underlain by adobe clay soils and colluvium Based on
our experience adobe clay soils and colluvium have a tendency to creep and move

Site Grading
Grading plans were not available at the time this letter report was prepared Based
on the sloping condition of the existing terrain significant effort may be required where
level pads for the panels are desired Based on the sloping ground conditions placement
of new fills will require keying and benching into the existing slope to provide stability of the
new fill against sliding For fill slopes with a vertical height greater than 10 feet we
recommend that a key be provided at the toe of the fill slope to provide stability of the new
embankment fill against sliding In general the key should be embedded at least 2 feet
below the lowest adjacent grade and should have a minimum base width of 10 feet
In general due to the presence of the highly expansive adobe clay soils imported
select granular fill likely will be required in fill areas of the project site As mentioned
previously over excavation of the subgrades under structures and roadways may be
necessary based on the expansive nature and distribution of the on site clayey soils
Because large boulders are present at and near the existing ground surface
excavations in cut areas and utility line trenches may encounter some difficult excavation
conditions Excavated boulders and cobbles may be reused as fill materials if reduced in
size and or used as rock fill in deeper fill areas
In general rock fills may be utilized provided that the top of the rock fill layer is
placed at least 4 feet below the finished subgrades In addition boulders greater than
18 inches in largest dimension should not be exposed at the fill slope face Boulder
placement should be set back a lateral distance of at least 6 feet measured from the edge
of the fill slope face

Field Exploration
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling seven borings designated
as Boring Nos 1 through 7 extending to depths of about 20 1 to 26 5 feet below the
existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings drilled at the site are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using track mounted drilling
equipment equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 7
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

A P P E N D I X B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Four one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples
to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The test results are
summarized on Plate B 1
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits for soil classification The test results are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Two Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on
Plate B 3
Two sets of corrosivity tests consisting of and minimum resistivity
tests were performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field
exploration The test results are presented on Plate B 4
