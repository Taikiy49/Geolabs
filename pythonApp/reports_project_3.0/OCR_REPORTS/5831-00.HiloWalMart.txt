Project Considerations
It is planned to relocate the existing Wal Mart Store facility in Hilo The existing
store is within the Waiakea Center along Kanoelehua Avenue north of East
Makaala Street The new store will be east of the current site along Railroad Avenue The
site is currently occupied by several warehouse and commercial structures
The new store will include about 156 622 square feet and 784 parking spaces as
well as retaining walls and utilities water sewer and electrical lines The building
structure will be a combination of concrete masonry shear walls and steel columns
supporting roof loads by means of steel joist girders and steel bar joists We understand
that the perimeter walls will be tied to the building slab with dowels
Details of the preliminary grading plan or other design concepts were not
available at the time this report was prepared Based on our experience in the project
vicinity we anticipate that the subsurface conditions at the property would likely consist
of basalt rock at shallow depths

Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes which include the following Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea activity during
recent geologic time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both
still active
The site is situated on the northeastern flank of Mauna Loa on rocks of the
Prehistoric Member of the Kau Volcanic Series In the site area these rocks are typified by
thick a a basalt lava flows with a surface of coarse spiny fragmental clinker generally
overlain by a thin mantle of mucky organic soil At the site previous grading work has
removed the topsoil layer

The lava formation appears to be of both a a and pahoehoe type flows which
spread and ponded as they approached the ocean A a lava is typically characterized by a
porous rough and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock
fragments including cobbles and boulders Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth
rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock
Cavities form when lavas are in a molten state and represent both lava tubes
intra flow cavities and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes form when molten lava
drains from the cooling flow leaving a hallow tube like structure which may extend for a
longitudinal distance along the flow Blisters and pockets are generally smaller in
horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted
through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and
along clinker layers Clinker and cavities serve as primary water transmission features
because of their high permeability
Existing Site Conditions
The site is comprised of a single parcel encompassing 15 574 acres of land area
The site is bounded by East Makaala Street to the south Railroad Avenue to the west
industrial development to the north and the existing Hilo Wal Mart store to the east
The site at present is occupied by six buildings in various states of repair The
buildings are located within the central portion Only the central portion has been
developed the remainder of the site is vacant and generally overgrown by tall grasses
The site was previously graded forming 3 or 4 indistinct terraces stepping down from the
high point near the intersection of Makaala Street and Railroad Avenue to the low point
along the northern boundary
Roadways on the site are a mixture of gravel surface and small areas of asphaltic
concrete Other infrastructure such as water lines and electrical poles with lines are on
the site
Anticipated Subsurface Conditions
Four borings were drilled in the vicinity of the proposed building Based on the
boring data we anticipate the project site may be underlain by 5 to 10 feet of fill over hard
basalt rock formation The fill consisted of basalt cobbles and boulders with gravel and
appeared to be in a medium dense to dense condition The basalt rock formation is
believed to consist mostly of a a lava rock with the potential for cavities In Boring No 3
we encountered a 3 foot layer of soft organic gravelly silt between the surface fill and
underlying basalt rock We believe that this layer represents the original ground condition
prior to filling

Building Foundation
Based on the subsurface information we anticipate that spread footings may be
used to support the new building The footing foundations would bear on the rock fill
or natural basalt formation
Existing Rock Fill
The existing rock fill appears to be in a medium dense to dense condition however
since it will provide the primary support of the new building we recommend
over excavating and recompacting the rock fill within 3 feet below the building
foundations This will help to provide a more uniform bearing layer for the new
building The soft layer encountered in Boring No 3 appears to be below the
primary zone of influence of the new building foundations however we recommend
delineating the extent of this layer during the design stage to determine if special
measures are required
Hard Rock Excavation
It is anticipated that the rock fills and basalt rock materials may be excavated with
normal heavy excavation equipment such as excavators and ripped with large
bulldozers However some of the deeper excavations into the dense lava may
require the use of hoerams or chipping The excavation of the basalt rock
formation is anticipated to generate boulder sized rock materials In order to
produce the desired gradation for rock fills we anticipate that on site crushing and
processing of the excavated materials may be necessary

Subgrade Preparation
In an effort to reduce the potential for collapse of near surface cavities and or voids
within the property we recommend employing subgrade preparation procedures
within the limits of grading at the commencement of the mass grading operations
The intent of the subgrade preparation procedure is to assist in detecting and
collapsing near surface cavities and or voids contained in the near surface rock
formations The subgrade preparation procedure recommended is consistent with
the standard of practice for similar type projects and similar subsurface rock
conditions throughout the State of Hawaii
In general the subgrade in cut areas should be proof rolled with a Caterpillar 825
sheepsfoot roller or similar compactor minimum 20 tons for about eight passes to
help detect and collapse near surface cavities and or voids Where cavities and or
voids are encountered during the proof rolling operations the cavities and or voids
should generally be exposed and backfilled in near level lifts
Cavity Probing and Grouting
To reduce the possibility for collapse of deeper buried cavities which may
adversely affect the building foundation performance it may be desired to perform
a program of cavity probing and grouting along the major foundation systems
Probing and grouting essentially consists of drilling an array of probe holes to
depths of about 10 feet below the finish grade in cut areas Cement grout is
pumped at low pressure in the drilled probe holes in an effort to stabilize the buried
voids encountered by the probe holes against potential collapse
Geologic Hazards
There are geological hazards present with a potential to have adverse impacts
on the project These hazards include but are not necessarily limited to the
following
e Lava flow encroachment and pyroclastic fallout ash deposition from
possible future volcanic eruptions
e Seismic activity
The US Geological Survey has divided the Island of Hawaii into nine lava flow
hazard zones with the highest risk assigned the designation of Zone 1 and the
lowest risk assigned Zone 9 The project site is situated in an area designated as
Zone 3 In this zone lava flows younger than the year 1800 cover between about 1
to 5 percent of the zone s surface About 15 to 75 percent of the land surface in the
zone is less than 750 years old Lava inundation risk in Zone 3 is less than Zones 1
and 2 due to the distance from active rift and vent areas Since the entire city of Hilo

For pyroclastic fallout it should be noted that Hawaiian volcanic eruptions are
generally quiescent in nature with very little explosive activity that would eject
pyroclastic material over widespread areas
Most volcanic eruptions produce fragments of lava that are airborne for at least a
short time before being deposited on the ground These fragments are called
tephra and include ash cinders and Pele s hair In Hawaii tephra is usually
ejected by lava fountains and poses a serious hazard only in the immediate vicinity
of an erupting vent The ash soils generally identified as Pahala Ash appear to be
Pleistocene or earlier in age Therefore it is most likely that pyroclastic fall is a very
minor hazard for the site
With respect to seismic activity it should be recognized that since the Island of
Hawaii is the site of three active volcanoes there is an appreciable level of seismic
activity associated with the volcanism Since the earliest records in 1868 there
have been at least 15 seismic events on and around the Island of Hawaii that
exceeded Magnitude 6 on the Richter scale The Uniform Building Code UBC
designates the entire island as Seismic Zone 4 Therefore the level of seismic
activity associated with development of the site should be adequately addressed in
the design of structural elements for the project
As previously discussed the project site appears to be situated along the
northeastern rift of Mauna Loa Localized faulting and ground movements are
common along active rift zones during volcanic activity
Although present the hazards associated with volcanic activity are currently
impossible to estimate due to limited amount of historic activity on Mauna Loa All of
the identified hazards except for those directly associated with volcanic activity
i e ground movements lava flows and pyroclastic fallout can be addressed
through proper project design However the magnitude of these hazards may
present economic constraints on development of the site

the subsurface conditions for design of the proposed development The exploration
could be better defined when the footprint of the proposed structures and site grading
criteria are known
