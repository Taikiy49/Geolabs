1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the water system improvements project along Kamehameha IV Road and
Liliha Street in the District of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location
and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations and site
preparation only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The project site is located along Kamehameha IV Road and Liliha Street in the
District of Honolulu area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii It is proposed to install water
mains and appurtenances along Kamehameha IV Road and Liliha Street The water line
along Kamehameha IV Road will be 36 inches in diameter and 1 500 feet in length from
North School Street to Kini Place The water line along Liliha Street from Kuakini to Judd
Streets will be 24 inch diameter and approximately 1 800 feet long The approximate
project limits are shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 1 and 2 2

lines Based on the information provided we understand that the proposed structures
may be installed approximately 10 feet below the ground surface Details for the
structures were not available at the time this report was prepared

pressure parameters for the design of the valve vault structures

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in central Oahu
The Koolau volcanic shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by
thinly bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities ceased approximately
2 2 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicates that the Island of Oahu has
subsided by as much as 1 200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic activity
Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of the
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed behind
the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the lagoons
Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global
sea levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature
These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher
and lower relative to the present sea level of Oahu

The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the
sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of the
upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high
energy conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal
sediments During these stable periods the growth of fringing coral algal reefs formed
distinct ledges of reef limestone at elevations roughly conforming to the level of the sea
during that period About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred
During that rise the valleys in the project area were submerged In the last 10 000 years
or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine sediments
have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting
in thick deposits of soft loose sediments along the coast in areas that were formerly valleys
and drainageways
The geologic history of the project area has been further complicated by the
deposition of recent pyroclastic materials and lava flows from eruptions of the vents of
members of the Honolulu Volcanic Series including Aliamanu Salt Lake Makalapa
Kamanaiki Punchbowl and other minor vents These
post erosional i e following the cessation of shield building events were
contemporaneous with the Pleistocene sea level fluctuations
Many of the eruptions were rather explosive in nature due to the interaction of
groundwater with the rising magma resulting in the expulsion of steam under extremely
high pressure together with large quantities of pyroclastic material predominantly ash

Based on our field exploration the Kamehameha IV Road site is underlain by a clay
deposit while the Kini Place site is underlain by clay soil overlying weathered cinder sand
material The Liliha Street site is underlain by clay material overlying colluvium deposits
extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 17 5 feet below the existing

underlain by clay soil overlying saprolite material Groundwater was not encountered in

3 1 Foundations
Based on the information provided we understand that the invert of the valve vault
boxes may be located at approximately 10 feet below the ground surface Based on the
subsurface conditions encountered in our exploration the bottom of the valve vaults near
Boring No 1 will be located on very stiff to hard clay soils and the valve vaults near
Boring No 2 will be located on medium dense cinder sand materials The bottom of the
valve vaults near Boring Nos 3 and 4 will be located near the top of the colluvial boulder
layer and the valve vaults near Boring No 5 will be located on medium stiff clayey silt
materials
Based on the generally competent subsoil conditions encountered at the sites we
believe that spread footings may be used for support of the new valve vault structures An
allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for
design of foundations bearing on a 12 inch cushion layer consisting of No 3B Fine Gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation to provide a uniform support This bearing value is for

underlying competent material

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling five
borings extending to depths of about 15 to 17 5 feet below the existing ground surface
The five borings were drilled at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plans
Plates 2 1 and 2 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight solid stem augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 5
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation
of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
One sieve analysis test ASTM C 136 and ASTM D 1140 was performed on a
selected soil sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in
soil classification Graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plate B 1
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification
Results of the test are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depth Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Three direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the shear strength parameters The test results are presented on
Plates B 3 through B 5
