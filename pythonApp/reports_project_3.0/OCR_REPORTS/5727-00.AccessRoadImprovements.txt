1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our preliminary geotechnical engineering
assessment performed for the Laupahoehoe Gulch Access Road Improvements project
in the Laupahoehoe area of North Hilo on the Island of Hawaii The project location and
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations derived
from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses for the project
The findings presented herein are intended as a preliminary assessment of the existing
potential natural hazards and structural deficiencies which may require remediation
prior to the re opening of the road to the public The recommendations are intended to
present conceptual design level improvements for rockfall and landslide remediation
roadway widening retaining structures and drainage improvements only The
preliminary findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The project includes a preliminary geological and geotechnical engineering
assessment of the existing closed and abandoned County roadway on the eastern side
of Laupahoehoe Gulch It is desired to evaluate whether the closed roadway could be
feasibly rehabilitated to serve as a secondary public access link between the existing
Laupahoehoe Point community and the existing Hawaii State Route 19 Belt Highway
The closed roadway alignment is approximately 3 600 lineal feet in length and traverses
steep coastal down slopes and high sub vertically inclined rock cut slopes
The roadway corridor has experienced a history of rockfalls landslides and other
slope instability In addition heavy groundwater seepage conditions occur along a
lengthy segment of the tall cut slopes composed of weathered basaltic rock The closed
roadway corridor has deteriorated with the passage of time and limited maintenance
attention The existing pavements guardrails shoulder barriers and some retaining

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii is the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers an
area of approximately 4 030 square miles The island was formed by the coalescence
of five primary shield volcanoes Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea activity during
recent geologic time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both
still active Due to the coalescence of the primary volcano flanks the oldest lower
flanks have been overlapped and buried deeply by more recent volcanic eruption of
lava thus the evolution of the broad dome shape of the Island of Hawaii that is visible
today
As an illustration of the current volcanic activity on the Island of Hawaii the
Mauna Loa Volcano has experienced a summit or flank eruption at irregular intervals
spanning about 5 to 25 years apart over the past 150 years or so The Kilauea Volcano
is experiencing an active eruption cycle which has spanned continuously since 1983
Therefore the southern portion of the island remains volcanically active and is building
out in a seaward direction along the southeast facing coastline in response to the
current activity of the Kilauea Volcano In contrast volcanic activity at the northern and
central portion of the Island of Hawaii has waned with no eruptive activity in recent
geologic time A sixth volcano Loihi which is presently an active submarine volcano
lies offshore from the southeast coastline of the Island of Hawaii and may emerge
above sea level some hundreds of thousands of years in the future
Due to the presence of on going volcanic activity at the Kilauea and Loihi
Volcanoes the Island of Hawaii is within a zone of active seismic influence The Island
of Hawaii experiences frequent light to moderate earthquakes associated with the
movement of underground magma and shifting of the earth s crust under the weight of
the growing island mass Occasional strong and damaging earthquakes have been
recorded both historically and in recent geologic time The occurrence of strong

2 2 Project Site and Vicinity
The Laupahoehoe Access Road project site is on the lower northeasterly facing
flank of Mauna Kea along the Hamakua coastline of the District of North Hilo on the
Island of Hawaii The project site is bounded by the Hawaii Belt Road State Route 19
toward the south and the Pacific Ocean toward the north as shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1 The summit of Mauna Kea located about 19 miles toward the
southwest of the project site rises approximately 13 800 feet above sea level The
project site and vicinity is typified by regional moderately inclined northeasterly facing
mountain slopes that abruptly terminate at the Hamakua coastline The truncated
mountain slopes have created tall steeply sloping cliffs composed of weathered basaltic
rock formation extending approximately 100 to 300 feet in height above the ocean
In the project vicinity the near surface mountain slopes of Mauna Kea Volcano
typically are covered with a surface veneer of volcanic ash Pahala Ash that overlies
layered lava rock belonging to the Hamakua Volcanic Series Based on our review of
available geologic information the project site traverses the near surface volcanic rock
and weathered by products of the Pleistocene age Hamakua Volcanic Series which
erupted during the shield forming stage of Mauna Kea Volcano The Hamakua Volcanic
Series consists of basaltic lava flows and some interbedded volcanic tephra ash and
cinder deposits The lava flows consist of the a a and pahoehoe types that erupted
from multiple source vents which were located on the subaerial slopes of Mauna Kea
Volcano The lava flows generally are gray colored where fresh and brownish colored
on exposed and weathered surfaces Aeolian wind blown sedimentary materials
tephra fall cinder and ash and colluvial deposits discontinuously mantle the lava
flows Potassium Argon age dating indicates an approximate range in age of the
Hamakua Volcanic Series extending from about 65 000 to about 250 000 years ago
Lava flows of the younger Laupahoehoe Volcanic Series Pleistocene to
Holocene age are mainly restricted to the upper slopes of Mauna Kea Volcano located

peak ground acceleration of about 0 34g In general earthquakes that occur throughout
the world are caused by shifting tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in
Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic activity associated with volcanic eruptions The
earthquakes may also result from the underground movement of magma that comes

In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on

2 4 Groundwater Considerations
We anticipate that the basal groundwater surface at the project site may
correspond to adjacent stream water surface elevations or mean sea level depending
on location Based on our observations the steep rock cut slopes at the eastern end of
the access road alignment exhibit discharges of continuous measurable groundwater
Hawaii California

flow The discharges from the slope are characterized as relatively widespread
non point natural groundwater seepage related to perched groundwater conditions
Perched groundwater conditions appear to exist locally within the predominantly
unsaturated ground comprising the project site slopes Perched groundwater should be
anticipated due to the regional high annual rainfall and the presence of confining
geologic layers composed of low permeable rock and soils embedded within the
generally highly permeable stratified lavas The presence of highly permeable volcanic
strata and other buried groundwater conduits such as basalt clinker and lava tubes
accelerate the percolation of groundwater from regions of groundwater recharge higher
mountain slopes toward regions of groundwater discharge within streams or at the
ocean
The presence of buried groundwater confining strata such as low permeability
volcanic ash clayey soil seams or other fine grained low permeability rock may retard
the downward percolation of groundwater and cause the localized buildup of pore water
pressure in the geologic formation The increased pore water pressure could result in
localized saturated ground conditions Where permeable geologic formations are
exposed on the steep rock cut slopes seepage of groundwater may occur
It is important to note that the buildup of groundwater pressure in weak ground
conditions may lower the factor of safety against sliding and initiate slope instability
such as rockfall and landslide activity
2 5 Climatic Considerations
As mentioned previously the project site is located along the north northeasterly
facing Hamakua Coast of the Island of Hawaii The climatic conditions may be
summarized as being coastal humid tropical The Hamakua Coast is exposed to the
prevalent moisture carrying trade winds which generally blow from a northeasterly
direction As a result of the orientation of the coastal terrain with respect to the incoming
trade winds portions of the Hamakua Coast receive an abundance of annual rainfall
The Hamakua Coast also may be referred to as the windward coast when the trade

wind weather pattern is established Rainfall carried by the trade wind flow is
accentuated along the windward coast by orographic lifting which is created by the
rising and cooling oceanic air mass as it encounters the higher mountain slopes located
inland from the coastline
Based on a review of available precipitation data obtained from the National
Climatic Data Center NCDC for the Laupahoehoe area Station 133 1 between the
period of 1993 and 2001 the average annual rainfall appears to be on the order of
about 120 inches For the same period an annual rainfall extreme of about 222 inches
was recorded in 1994 and an annual rainfall low of about 68 inches was recorded in
1998 In general the monthly total rainfall ranged from about 1 inch to about 26 inches
with the higher monthly totals generally occurring between the months of October
through April The extreme recorded monthly rainfalls ranged from a monthly rainfall low
of less than about 0 25 inches to a monthly rainfall high of about 35 inches
Persistently wet ground conditions often accelerate the chemical weathering of
volcanic rock and may form deeper weathering profiles in the rock slopes The deeper
weathered rock and wetter slope surface conditions may contribute to elevated rockfall
and landslide activity

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The slopes along the Laupahoehoe Access Road are exhibiting active slope
erosion and cliff evolution The cut slopes exposing weathered and highly fractured
basaltic rock formation are producing falling rock ranging from stones to very large
boulders Based on our observation we believe that the following potential natural
hazards are present along the roadway
Rockfall and landslide
Groundwater seepage and surface runoff
Unstable trees
Debris flow
The structural deficiencies consisting of roadway width constrictions damaged
and missing roadside barriers ineffective control of surface drainage and some
distressed and offset retaining wall structures were also observed
Based on the results of the field exploration and reconnaissance it is our opinion
that the currently closed existing Laupahoehoe Gulch Access Road should not be
re opened to vehicular traffic or hiking trial without extensive rockfall landslide mitigation

3 3 Rockfall Impact Barrier
In general the rockfall impact barrier is a specialized heavy duty containment
barrier designed to intercept and retain large boulder impacts The barrier is designed to
absorb the energy of a rock impact and dissipate the energy through the use of flexible
nets which act as a braking element The rockfall impact barrier would thereby retain
rocks as opposed to bouncing or striking with enough force to topple the retention
system
The impact barrier typically consists of support posts or steel beams with lateral
cable supports and flexible braking elements Special cable ring nets and wire mesh
materials span between the support posts or steel beams The posts or beams may be
fastened to the ground plate with a hinge pin The hinge pin acts as predetermined
breaking point and protects the anchorage from destruction in case the post or beam is
directly impacted from a rockfall The impact barrier may be designed for varying load
impacts that are estimated based on the existing site conditions such as boulder size
slope inclination and composition computed rolling velocities and possible boulder
bounce heights from rockfall impacts analyzed using the Colorado Rockfall Simulation
Program CRSP computer software
In general the CRSP computer program simulates various size boulders falling
from specified slope geometries to provide statistical information for the anticipated
rockfall velocity rock bounce height and rolling energy Our rockfall simulation analysis
was performed by constructing slope cross sections using ground topographic contours
from USGS with modifications based on our field observation It should be noted that
the actual topographic map is not included in the current conceptual design study
Therefore it may be necessary to perform additional evaluation to verify the following

Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling two borings designated
as Boring Nos 1 and 2 each extending to a depth of about 40 feet below the existing
ground surface The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plans Plate
3 2 and 3 3 The borings were drilled using truck mounted drill equipment equipped with
rotary coring tools
Materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual
observation and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left
in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core
run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 determinations were performed on selected
soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The test
results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D4318 was performed on a selected soil
sample to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Graphic
presentations of the test results are provided on Plate B 1
One Sieve Analysis test ASTM C 117 C 136 was performed on a selected
soil sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on
Plate B 2
