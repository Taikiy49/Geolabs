REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which
time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These younger volcanic
series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
worldwide cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water
was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of
the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available
such that the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations and within the previously carved depressions
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii and in the heads of valleys the erosional
processes are dominated by the detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls
and the transportation of the colluvial materials down slope toward the axis of a valley
primarily by gravity mass transfer Once these colluvial materials reach the stream in the
central portion of a valley alluvial processes become dominant and the water carried
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
The project site is on the lower hillside slopes above the floor of Kapakahi Gulch in
the Waialae Nui area of Honolulu as shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
Kapakahi Gulch is a relatively narrow erosional valley carved into the Koolau Shield by

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is in the northerly direction from the end of Luinakoa Street on the
west side of Kapakahi Gulch in the Waialae Nui area on the Island of Oahu The project
site is bounded by Kapakahi Stream toward the east undeveloped rocky mountain slopes
toward the north and west and the existing Luinakoa Street residential subdivision toward
the south Based on the information provided the proposed 20 lot development option
extends approximately 300 feet northerly and parallel to Kapakahi Stream from the end of
Luinakoa Street Furthermore the proposed 50 lot development option would extend
about 1 100 feet northerly from the end of Luinakoa Street
The project site slopes down toward the east at variable inclinations A noticeable
change in the overall slope gradient is apparent trending across the mid elevation portion
of the project site Typical slope gradients of about 5 to 20 percent were encountered on
the lower elevation eastern portion of the site and steeper slope gradients of about 40 to
75 percent were encountered on the rocky higher elevation western portion of the site

between the upslope surface rock outcroppings and the down slope thicker alluvial and
colluvial soil deposits Based on our reconnaissance the approximate location of the

The detachment of boulders from parent rock outcroppings is a result of natural
chemical weathering physical rock decomposition and gravitational erosion processes
acting on a rock mass containing open fractures or other rock outcrop discontinuities The
timing of the detachment and fall of rocks cannot be accurately predicted because of the
many and varied factors involved in hill slope evolution processes Furthermore rockfall
events are varied and may be triggered by heavy rainfall high wind heavy surface water
runoff slope disturbance by human or construction related activity and earthquakes
Open rock fractures typically develop from the natural rock jointing inherent to
extrusive lava flow formations Where the rock joints intersect the lava bedding surfaces or
other changes discontinuities in the rock mass character a separation may occur that
can progressively widen and ultimately result in the detachment of a portion of the rock
mass to create a loose boulder Should the detached boulder become mobilized typically
by the application of external force s such as earthquake activity heavy surface water
runoff or elevated pore water pressure in the slope gravitational forces could overcome
friction and cause the boulder to begin falling If the fall is not stopped by an adjacent
obstruction or other energy absorbing feature a short distance from initial mobilization
such as a large tree soft soil deposit flat or depressed terrain or other heavy rock
outcrop obstruction the rock could rapidly accelerate down hill on the sloping terrain
despite the presence of thick vegetation Boulder falls that have gained appreciable rolling
and bouncing momentum are dangerous and difficult to stop by rigid structures especially
at high velocity
It should be noted that potential rockfall emanating from the steep rock slopes on
the east side of the gulch should not affect the project site because of the buffer provided
by the existing development and drainage channel at the valley axis

The primary geotechnical issues relevant to site development pertain to potential
earth movements associated with the existing adobe clay soils and the presence of
potential rockfall hazards upslope from the site
Poor Quality Soils Adobe Clay
Our study disclosed that a large portion of the project site is underlain by a
significant thickness of adobe clay soils having high expansion potential and low shear
strength when wet Based on a review of available subsurface information the problematic
adobe clay soils may extend at least 6 to 10 feet below the existing ground surface before
transitioning to older soil deposits consisting of reddish and brownish colored silts and
clays Based on our reconnaissance it appears that the areal coverage of the surface clay
soils broadens in the northerly direction Therefore development that encroaches further
into the valley may encounter greater surface coverage by the adobe clay soil deposits
The presence of thick adobe clay deposits on sloping terrain poses potential problems
related to long term slope stability and foundation performance Adobe clay soils could
become very weak when they are affected by an increase in soil moisture content by rain
water infiltration and seepage and are subject to slope creep or landslide movement
during extended wet ground conditions
Traditional grading such as the complete removal of the adobe clay or the partial
removal of the adobe clays and replacement with non expansive select granular fills may
not be cost effective due to the anticipated thickness and extent of the native valley fill soil
deposits In addition cuts into the natural adobe clay soils should be avoided due to the
difficulty in stabilizing the mass of adobe clay soils above the developed cut slope
Additional extensive grading may be required to remove or reduce the amount of adobe
clay deposits beyond the proposed cut limits to reduce the potential for slope instability
above the cuts
To reduce the potential for earth movement at the project site we recommend
consideration of the following generalized concepts for site development
e Avoid large cuts into the adobe hillside by constructing a fill buttress on the
low side of the site adjacent to the stream and raising the grade by the
placement of non expansive select granular fills
e The roadway and associated utility corridor primarily water sewer and
drain lines also should be constructed in a stabilized buttress fill composed
of non expansive select granular fills to protect the critical infrastructure
from possible earth movements and utility line leakage rupture which could
elevate the soil moisture content in the subsurface and result in slope
instability

Potential Rockfall Hazards
Our study disclosed that the project site is exposed to potential rockfall hazards
from the adjacent east facing mountain slopes containing surface rock outcrops and
scattered surface boulders Based on our preliminary rockfall hazard analysis using the
Colorado Rockfall Simulation Program CRSP falling boulders emanating from the steep
slopes composed of rock outcrops have the potential to encroach on the upslope property
boundary with appreciable impact energy In addition the rock slopes in their present
unaltered condition within the upslope side of the project site have some potential for
producing lower risk rockfall hazards Potential rockfall from these lower elevation slopes
should be of lower risk due to the anticipated lower impact energy as a result of the limited
slope length within the project limits
To reduce the potential for hazardous rockfall encroachment emanating from the
upper slopes above the development and the smaller portion of existing rock slopes within
the development we recommend the following rockfall mitigation concepts for
consideration
e Construction of an appropriate capacity rockfall impact barrier along the
upslope side of the development to intercept and retain fallen rock material
The flexible steel rockfall impact barrier should be constructed within a
designated easement minimum 35 to 40 feet wide to facilitate future
maintenance rock removal and or repair of the rockfall catchment system
Ideally a graded slope bench may be constructed along the barrier
alignment during mass grading of the site for new development where slope
access is relatively unencumbered The purpose for the slope bench is to
provide a more uniform and graded ground surface along the barrier
alignment to facilitate the barrier construction and future maintenance needs

Slope Surface Drainage
Based on our observations following a significant winter rainfall event we observed
evidence of fresh surficial soil erosion and concentrated slope water runoff that had
entrained and mobilized localized areas of loose surface soils and organic debris These
conditions were observed on the steeper rock slopes within the proposed development
and the rock slopes above the proposed development Because the proposed

rockfall impact barrier along the upslope side of the development

surface rock outcrops mixed with some surficial soils We anticipate that grading along the
upper half of the site may encounter the rock formation at the existing ground surface The
rock generally may be characterized as slightly to moderately weathered vesicular
medium hard to hard pahoehoe type lava with interbedded clinker
