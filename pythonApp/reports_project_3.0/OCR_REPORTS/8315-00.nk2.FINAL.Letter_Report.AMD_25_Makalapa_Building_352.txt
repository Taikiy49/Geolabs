REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age 
and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. Waianae became extinct 
while Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau 
lava banking against its eastern flank.  
After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time erosion incised deep 
valleys into the Koolau Shield Volcano along with the accumulation of the terrigenous 
and lagoonal deposits along the coastal areas, volcanic activity returned to portions of 
the Island of Oahu as a series of localized lava flows followed by explosive cinder and 
tuff cone formations. These late eruptions belong to the Honolulu Volcanic Series and 
are believed to have occurred between about 30,000 and 800,000 years ago.  
The scattered eruptions emanated from about 37 vents distributed mainly over 
the south and east sides of the older Koolau Shield Volcano (Gramlich, Lewis, & 
Naughton, 1971). Multiple cinder and tuff cone vents belonging to the Honolulu Volcanic 
Series erupted easterly of the Pearl Harbor East Loch in the Salt Lake area. The more 
prominent Salt Lake area volcanic vents still retain some visible remnant crater structure

as represented by the subaerial topography of the Aliamanu and Makalapa Craters. 
These post-erosional (i.e., following the cessation of shield building) events were 
contemporaneous with some of the late Pleistocene sea level fluctuations. 
These late eruptions were explosive in character due to the rising magma's 
interaction with groundwater that resulted in violent steam explosions that expelled large 
quantities of pyroclastic materials generally composed of volcanic ash and cinder with 
some embedded blocks of basaltic rock. These deposits consolidated to form volcanic 
tuff, locally referred to as “mudrock.” 
The oldest of the eruptions (at Aliamanu Crater) caused the initial large diversion 
of Halawa Stream to the north of its original location into what are now the Southeast 
and Magazine Lochs of Pearl Harbor during the Kaena (+95 feet) high stand of the sea. 
The Aliamanu tuff is a water-laid pyroclastic deposit resulting from the higher stand of 
the sea at the time of deposition.  
Following the deposition of the Aliamanu tuff, stream erosion and the deposition 
of alluvial soils continued prior to the next phase of pyroclastic eruptions at Salt Lake. 
The later Salt Lake area eruptions were essentially simultaneous and have been 
assigned to the stand of the sea that was between about 40 and 60 feet below the 
present sea level. The last Salt Lake area pyroclastic eruptions deposited a mantle of 
volcanic tuff over much of the Makalapa-Salt Lake area. 
In summary, the project site is situated along the northwestern rim of Makalapa 
Crater. In general, the subsurface materials underlying the site consist of man-made fills 
overlying volcanic tuff and older alluvium with increasing depth.  
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, extending to depths ranging from 
about 41 to 42.5 feet below the existing ground surface. We also performed two 
Dynamic Cone Penetration (DCP) tests extending to depths of about 5.9 to 8.1 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring and DCP test locations are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our borings generally encountered a pavement section consisting of about 
3 inches of AC pavement underlain by about 0.5 to 4.75 feet of basaltic fill material

depths of about 11 to 20 feet. Below the weathered volcanic tuff, a relatively thick layer 
of volcanic tuff rock was encountered to depths of about 34 to 41 feet, followed by older 
alluvium extending to the maximum depth explored of about 42.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. The volcanic tuff was severely to closely fractured, highly to moderately

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration program, the western half of the existing slope is 
composed of approximately 11 to 12 feet of relatively stiff/dense fill and weathered 
volcanic tuff material overlying volcanic tuff formation to the maximum depth explored of 
about 42.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The eastern half of the slope is 
composed of approximately 12 feet of very loose to medium dense fill overlying about 
10 feet of relatively stiff/dense weathered volcanic tuff material. Underlying these layers, 
hard volcanic tuff and very stiff older alluvium were encountered, extending to the 
maximum depth explored of about 41.5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Groundwater was not encountered in our borings at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, our site reconnaissance 
program, and our engineering analyses, we do not believe that the existing slope 
presents global slope stability issues. Ongoing detachment of soil and rock debris from 
the slope face appears to be primarily caused by erosion due to water flowing over the

face of the slope and by the large trees at the top-of-slope. One of the most frequent 
causes of raveling is invasive tree roots prying on the existing cracks located within rock 
formations. Tree roots (especially of the larger trees) frequently develop and grow larger 
in the discontinuities of rock materials. The added force of wind action on tree limbs and 
branches may create leveraged forces that are transferred to the roots lodged within the 
discontinuity. Over time, the repetitive leveraging of the discontinuity can loosen rock 
and cause it to fall. Because of the numerous large trees at the project site, we believe 
that tree root pry is a significant mechanism for creating rock slope instability because of 
the hardy growth of trees near the top-of-slope.  
The following discussions are presented to aid the owner in selecting an erosion 
control/slope stabilization system. The key design elements to be considered are 
drainage improvements at the top of the slope, vegetation clearing, rock slope scaling, 
and slope protection. Discussions of each of these elements are presented in the 
following sections. 
Due to the proximity of Buildings S275 and 352 to the toe of the slope, adequate 
temporary rockfall barriers should be installed near the base of the slope prior to the 
start of construction to protect the structures. The temporary barriers should be able to 
resist the anticipated kinetic energy of the falling boulders and be constructed to an 
adequate height to catch boulders that may bounce at an appreciable height. 
Erosion Control and Slope Stabilization Design Alternatives 
Based on our field exploration program, site reconnaissance, and engineering 
analyses, we believe that erosion is the primary mechanism for soil/rock detachment 
and possible shallow slope failures on the existing slope face. Therefore, priority should 
be given to constructing drainage improvements at the top-of-slope and protecting the 
slope face from future erosion due to stormwater and large tree roots.  
Based on our evaluation, we believe that there are five potentially viable 
long-term erosion control and slope stabilization alternatives for the project site. Each 
solution has advantages and disadvantages in terms of effectiveness, longevity, and 
cost. These aspects are discussed in the following sections. The five potential 
alternatives are: 
1. 
Installing drainage improvements at the top-of-slope, slope vegetation 
removal (including the large trees at the top-of-slope), and major scaling of 
loose rocks and soil debris from the slope face.  
2. 
All the improvements from Alternative No. 1 plus a conventional retaining 
wall at the bottom-of-slope to retain future soil and rock debris that will fall 
down the face of the slope due to ongoing erosion. 
3. 
All the improvements from Alternative No. 1 plus the installation of a 
draped wire mesh system on the prepared slope surface.

Alternative No. 1 
Alternative No. 1 would reduce the amount of water flowing over the face of the 
slope and remove the large trees at the top-of-slope that are acting as one of the 
primary mechanisms for erosion. Rock slope scaling would remove existing loose soil 
and rock debris that could pose hazards to the adjacent buildings. However, future 
slope scaling and debris clearing would be necessary due to future erosion of the 
exposed slope face. This is the cheapest design alternative but only has an estimated 
useful life of less than about 5 to 6 years. 
The aesthetics of the slope would be similar to the existing conditions. We 
envision that low-lying vegetation would regrow on the slope face over time. A 
noticeable accumulation of soil and/or rock debris may develop by the end of the 
estimated useful life.  
Alternative No. 2 
Alternative No. 2 would add a conventional retaining wall to the base of the slope 
to retain soil and rock debris that detach off the slope face over time due to erosion. 
Regular maintenance to remove the accumulation of debris behind the retaining wall 
would be necessary. In addition, because the base of the slope abuts existing structures 
(Building S275 and the existing walkway), substantial cuts into the existing toe of the 
slope would be necessary to construct the retaining wall footing. Cutting into the toe of 
the slope could cause instability. Excavations into the volcanic tuff formation would also 
be time-consuming and costly.  
The aesthetics of the slope face would be similar to the existing conditions. 
However, the conventional retaining wall may be unsightly, especially when soil and 
rock material accumulate behind the wall.

draped wire mesh based on the corrosive environment at the project site). 
The installation of a draped wire mesh system on the slope face would serve to control

Alternative No. 4 
Alternative No. 4 would add an anchored wire mesh system over the face of the 
cleared and scaled slope face. Like the draped wire mesh system, the useful life of the 
improvements would be about 10 to 15 years (or the life of the anchored wire mesh 
based on the corrosive environment at the project site). The anchored wire mesh would 
serve to control the release of loose rock that may develop on the slope face and help 
to stabilize sections of the existing slope prone to shallow slope failures. Erosion control 
matting (ECM) is placed directly against the slope face and treated with Hydromulch 
before placement of the wire mesh. Therefore, the ECM protects the face of the slope 
from future erosion. Because the wire mesh is pinned tightly to the slope by the drilled 
anchors, loose soil and rock debris are retained against the slope face instead of falling 
to the bottom of the slope. Therefore, a setback distance at the toe of the slope is not 
necessary with the anchored wire mesh system. As previously mentioned, based on our 
engineering analyses, we do not believe that there are potential slope instability issues 
at the project site. Therefore, the anchors would serve only to pin the wire mesh tightly 
to the slope and would not contribute to global slope stability. Difficult drilling conditions 
and slow production rates may be encountered in the hard volcanic tuff formation 
encountered at the project site. Therefore, considerable construction delays could occur 
during the anchor installation work.  
The ECM and Hydromulch encourage the growth of vegetation on the slope face 
that could hide the appearance of the black wire mesh. However, sections of the mesh 
could still be visible, especially if regular maintenance of the slope vegetation 
(i.e., watering) is not performed. Examples of anchored wire mesh systems are

Recommended Design Alternatives 
Based on the above considerations, we believe that the best long-term erosion 
control/slope stabilization systems for the project (in order of effectiveness) are 
Alternative Nos. 5 and 4. We believe that Alternative No. 5 may be the most 
aesthetically pleasing, have the longest useful life, and be more cost-effective than 
Alternative No. 4. Alternative No. 4 has the advantage of providing a natural, vegetated

Slope Vegetation 
The existing ground cover vegetation should be removed to completely expose 
the slope surface in preparation for slope scaling and the installation of slope 
improvements. The four large shower trees at the slope crest should be 
considered for removal to eliminate future erosion due to large tree roots.  
Slope Scaling 
Slope scaling consists of experienced crews employing manual labor and hand 
tools to remove loose soil, rocks, and precariously positioned rock fragments 
from the slope face. The scaling crews performing the slope scaling work should 
be experienced in the performance of slope scaling by rappelling and/or working 
from crane baskets. Slope scaling work typically proceeds by starting from the 
top of the slope and working downward in a controlled and orderly manner in 
conjunction with the combined progress of all the scaling crews working on the 
slope face.

Shotcrete Slope Protection 
Following vegetation removal and slope scaling, we recommend installing 
fiber-reinforced shotcrete over the face of the existing slope. The exposed face of 
the shotcrete should be sculpted and stained to match the characteristics of the 
surrounding volcanic tuff formation. 
Shotcrete placement should be performed by an experienced nozzleman. Prior to 
production shotcreting, it is recommended that unreinforced test panels of 
shotcrete be constructed for inspection.  
The shotcrete slope protection should be well-drained to reduce the potential 
build-up of hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system for the shotcrete 
slope protection would consist of 2-foot-wide strips of a prefabricated drainage 
product (such as Miradrain, Enkadrain, or an equivalent product) placed on the 
face of the slope at a maximum horizontal spacing of 5 feet on-center. The 
prefabricated drainage product should extend from the top to the base of the 
slope and should be hydraulically connected to a weephole near the toe of the 
slope.  
Anchored Wire Mesh System 
An anchored wire mesh system is a slope treatment that involves the installation 
of steel wire mesh panels anchored onto the slope face with soil/rock nails in an 
effort to prevent soil and rocks from moving/falling under the long-term slope 
protection system. The primary intent of this stabilization system is to closely pin 
the wire mesh to the slope face as a retention system. A typical detail of the 
anchored wire mesh system is provided on Plate 4. 
The following provides a brief outline of the anchored wire mesh installation 
procedures. 
1. 
Trim slope face of vegetation, loose materials, and protruding rock outcrops. 
2. 
Stakeout grouted soil/rock anchors in general accordance with the project 
spacing requirements considering the low spots. 
3. 
Excavate the dell for pre-tensioning of the anchored wire mesh before drilling 
the grouted soil/rock anchors.

Based on our evaluation of the slope, we recommend that the grouted soil/rock 
anchor be a minimum of 14 feet long, spaced at 8-foot on-center in a staggered 
pattern. The drilled hole for the grouted soil/rock anchor should be a minimum of 
6 inches in diameter. The grout should be non-shrink grout and should have a 
minimum unconfined compressive strength of 5,000 psi or greater. The grouted 
soil/rock anchors should have an ultimate pullout capacity of 33 kips. 
The grouted soil/rock anchors should be set in low spots, where possible, to pull 
the anchored wire mesh into the depressions and against the ground. This will 
avoid major hollow spaces and reduce the quantity of vegetation material 
required during the greening of the slope face. If the anchor pattern does not 
permit setting grouted soil/rock anchors in the low spots, supplemental (short) 
anchors may be installed to achieve this purpose. The supplemental grouted 
soil/rock anchors should consist of a 1.375-inch diameter solid threaded bar 
(Grade 75) with a minimum embedment length of 8 feet. 
Due to Hawaii's climate, which is moderately to highly corrosive, the useful life of 
the anchored wire mesh system could be highly variable, depending on the 
quality of the steel wire mesh and hardware, corrosion protection, and 
workmanship employed during the anchored wire mesh installation. 
A minimum of 10 percent of the grouted soil/rock anchors should be proof tested. 
Proof tests should be performed by incrementally loading the anchor to a 
maximum test load of 150 percent of the design pullout capacity. An electronic 
load cell should be used to measure the applied load to the anchor. The anchor 
head movement during the test should be measured and recorded.

Concrete Sidewalk Repair 
Based on the results of our field exploration, the near-surface soils generally consist 
of sandy silt, silty sand, and silty gravel with localized thin layers of clayey silt. As 
previously mentioned, we did not conduct swell potential laboratory testing for the project. 
Therefore, to reduce the potential for sidewalk distress resulting from potential swelling of 
the near-surface soils, we recommend over-excavating the in-situ material a minimum of 
6 inches and replacing it with select granular fill material compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction. In addition, we recommend that the subgrade soils be kept 
within about 2 percent above the optimum moisture content prior to the final placement of 
concrete.  
The subgrade soils below the slabs-on-grade should be scarified to a depth of about 
10 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content, 
and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. The non-expansive, 
select granular material should be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction. To reduce the potential for substantial shrinkage cracks developing in the slabs, 
crack control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with 
expansion joints provided at right-angle intersections.  
It should be emphasized that the areas adjacent to the slab edges should be 
backfilled tightly against the edges of the slabs with relatively impervious soils. These 
areas should also be graded to divert water away from the slabs and to reduce the 
potential for water ponding around the slabs.

CORROSION POTENTIAL 
Laboratory corrosion tests, including pH, minimum resistivity, chloride content, 
and sulfate content, were performed on selected samples obtained during our field 
exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of the near-surface soils at the project site. The 
test results are summarized and presented in Appendix C. Detailed results of the 
Chloride Content (EPA 300.0) and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0) tests performed by 
Eurofins TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. are presented in Appendix E. 
Design of metallic substructures, such as electrical conduits or other metallic 
piping, should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure. 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
regarding the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the 
resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of 
metallic objects. 
Based on the results of corrosivity testing, the subsurface soils at the project site 
exhibit a minimum resistivity value of approximately 400 ohm-cm, indicating that the 
subsurface soils are Extremely Corrosive (Corrosion Rating of 1 based on the Board of 
Water Supply, City & County of Honolulu Water System External Corrosion Control 
Standards dated 1991) to buried metallic structures. Therefore, we recommend properly 
designing metallic substructures (such as piping) for protection against the potential for 
corrosion. 
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent on the pH, chloride content, and sulfate content found in the 
soil. In general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm), 
sulfate content of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH of greater than 5.0 may be considered 
“non-corrosive” to underground concrete pipelines and structures based on the Board of 
Water Supply, City & County of Honolulu Water System External Corrosion Control 
Standards dated 1991. It should be noted that the pH values of the soils tested were 
greater than 5.0 and that chloride and sulfate contents were not detected within 
reporting limits. However, it may be appropriate to consult with a professional corrosion 
engineer to review the test results and provide detailed recommendations for corrosion 
protection.

Receipt

Receipt Exceptions
The following sample(s) was received at the laboratory outside the required temperature criteria: B-2 RS-1 1-2.5ft (320-82237-1). The 
sample was received out of temp at 15.1C. There was no cooling agent present in the cooler.

(320-82237-1). Container label uses an apostrophe in place of "ft" in the sample IDs. Logged and labeled according to the COC.
General Chemistry

the sample matrix: B-2 RS-1 1-2.5ft (320-82237-1), (320-82237-A-1-B MS) and (320-82237-A-1-C MSD). The samples were turbid and 
extremely difficult to filter. Elevated reporting limits (RLs) are provided. Because of this dilution, the surrogate spike and matrix spike 
concentration in the sample was reduced to a level where the recovery calculation does not provide useful information.
No additional analytical or quality issues were noted, other than those described above or in the Definitions/Glossary page.
