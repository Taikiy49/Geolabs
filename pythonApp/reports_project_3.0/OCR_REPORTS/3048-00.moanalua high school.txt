performed for the proposed additions and upgrades within the existing Moanalua High
School in the Salt Lake District on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our design level geotechnical engineering recommendations derived

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The project site is located near the southeastern flank of the Aliamanu vent and on
the northeastern flank of the Salt Lake vent complex of the Honolulu Volcanic Series
As the molten magma ascended through these vents it reacted with the
groundwater generating steam This steam caused explosive eruptions which deposited
low cones of ash material that have consolidated to form tuff locally referred to as
mudrock
The Aliamanu tuff is an older water laid ash deposit that was laid down when the
sea level was higher than the present The Aliamanu eruptive event deposited its material
over alluvium originating from Halawa Stream
After this phase of volcanic activity ceased sedimentary deposits accumulated over
the Aliamanu tuff before the next phase began Then the Salt Lake eruptions began
when the sea was at about 40 to 60 feet below its present level The Salt Lake eruptive
event deposited its material over earlier tuffs from the older Aliamanu events
The water that accumulated in the crater was believed to originate from
depressional springs and rain water During periods of little rainfall the shallow ponded
water was reduced by evaporation and over the years the mineral salt content of the
ponded water increased

Recently man has developed the crater floor by filling and grading the area for
residential and golf course purposes

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on the field exploration the project site generally appears to be underlain
by a surficial fill layer over cemented tuff deposits mudrock at shallow depths It is our
opinion that the proposed upgrades and additions are feasible from a geotechnical
engineering point of view provided that the recommendations presented in this report are
followed
We believe that the dense surficial fill materials and the shallow cemented tuff
deposits encountered would provide adequate foundation support for the new
construction It appears that shallow footings and slab on grade foundations may be
used to support most of the new facilities However deep foundations such as drilled
piers are required to support the multi level bridge walkway and the elevator shaft
installation Detailed geotechnical engineering recommendations pertaining to the
foundation design of the proposed facilities are further discussed in the following sections
of this report

Elevator Mechanical Pits
Since the slab on grade for the elevator mechanical pits may be subjected to

Retaining Walls
Low retaining walls approximately three feet 3 in height are planned at various
locations across the project site for grade separation and wheelchair protection General
guidelines for the design of the retaining wall structures are as follows
1 Wall foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded a minimum
depth of twelve inches 12 below the lowest adjacent finish grade For
sloping ground the footing should extend deeper to obtain a minimum six
foot set back distance from the outside edge of the footing measured out
horizontally to the face of the slope If well cemented tuff deposits are
encountered the above set back distance may be reduced by embedding
the footings a minimum of eighteen inches 18 into the well cemented tuff
deposits
2 An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 p s f may be used for the
design of wall footing foundations bearing on the in situ dense stiff soils
3 For conventional retaining wall an active earth pressure of 35 p s f
equivalent fluid pressure per foot of depth is recommended For at rest
earth pressures a value of 45 p s f equivalent fluid pressure per foot of
depth may be used

W O 3048 00 Page 16
4 For lateral resistance design a passive earth resistance of 300 p s f per foot
of depth may be used A base friction factor of 0 35 may also be used to
determine the resistance against sliding of the walls
5 Backfill directly behind the retaining walls should consist of select well
draining self compacting granular material with sizes generally between
three fourths inch 4 and one fourth inch such as No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation to minimize excessive compaction effort
required The upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of non expansive
impervious material to reduce water infiltration behind the walls
6 A subsurface drainage system subdrains or weepholes should be utilized
behind the walls to protect against build up of hydrostatic pressure
Subdrains should be properly constructed to drain water away from the
wall

Minimal site grading work is anticipated for the project construction
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of off site

Slabs and pavements that are to be demolished should be completely removed
Any over excavation resulting from the demolition should be backfilled with compacted
select granular fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the

Excavation
Shallow excavations on the order of about five feet 5 below the existing ground
surface will be required to construct the new elevator mechanical pits Based on our field
exploration the project site is generally underlain by dense gravelly sand over tuff
formation at shallow depths We believe that shallow excavations in the dense gravelly
sand and well cemented tuff deposits should be able to stand unsupported at relatively
steep angles for temporary construction purposes However due to the granular nature
of the near surface soils some degree of unravelling of the subsoils at the excavation
faces may be anticipated
It is also our opinion that excavation in the well cemented tuff deposits may require
hard ripping or hoerams Care must be exercised to avoid over ripping which would
disrupt the structure of the rock formation resulting in a potential loss of bearing strength

Drainage
The finished grade outside the rescue assistance structures and the elevator pits
should be sloped to shed water away from foundations and to reduce the potential for
ponding Also it is suggested that gutter systems be installed around the rescue
structures and that the discharge be diverted away from the foundation and pavement
areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be
avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and
drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be
properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce water infiltration
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be
maintained to drain all surface run off away from slabs and footings

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling of ten
10 borings to depths ranging between 10 to 23 5 feet below the existing ground surface
at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled
using both portable and truck mounted equipment
The material encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System A graphic presentation of the materials encountered are presented
on the Boring Logs Plates A 1 through A 10
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the
Boring Logs at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of four inches 4 in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0
foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring Logs as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
