This memorandum summarizes our understanding of the project and presents 
our preliminary geotechnical engineering recommendations for the design of the 
proposed Kilauea Avenue Safe Routes to School project planned in the Hilo area on the 
Island of Hawaii.  
It should be noted that our preliminary recommendations presented herein are 
based on our recently performed limited test borings. Laboratory testing on selected soil 
samples are in progress, and the remaining test borings are scheduled to be completed 
at a later date. It should be noted that our preliminary recommendations may change 
after the field exploration has been completed. A detailed summary of our findings and 
recommendations will be contained in our geotechnical engineering report. The final 
report should be consulted when it becomes available. 
Project Considerations 
The project is located along Kilauea Avenue from Ohea Street to Haihai Street in 
Hilo on the Island of Hawaii. The project consists of installing sidewalks, bike lanes, and 
drainage improvements. Additionally, we understand that new retaining walls and 
remediation of steep slope conditions may be required at about 14 locations along the 
roadway. Based on the information provided, the drainage improvements consist of 
installing ten dry wells along the project alignment. In addition, an extension of the culvert 
headwalls will be required at four locations. 
Subsurface Conditions 
The subsurface conditions of the project site in the memorandum are based on 
nine recently completed test borings performed at the east end of the project site. In 
general, our borings encountered an asphaltic concrete surface layer about 3 to 6 inches 
thick underlain by fill, alluvium, and basalt formation extending to the maximum depth 
explored of 30 feet below the existing ground surface. 
The fill material generally consisted of medium dense to very dense silty/gravelly 
sand and loose to very dense sandy gravel. The alluvial soils were generally composed of 
medium stiff to hard sandy/silty clay and medium dense to very dense sandy/clayey gravel 
and silty sand. We encountered groundwater in two of the nine borings at a depth of about 
15 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration. It should be

Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the International Building Code, 2018 Edition (IBC 2018), the project site 
may be subject to seismic activity, and seismic design considerations will need to be 
addressed. The following sections provide discussions on the seismicity, soil profile type 
for seismic design, and the potential for liquefaction at the project site. 
Earthquakes and Seismicity 
Generally, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in 
the tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to 
volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before 
or during volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the 
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not 
erupt. The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but 
most are so small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. 
However, some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause 
minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on 
the Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement 
of magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa 
Volcanoes on the Island of Hawaii. Because majority of the earthquakes in 
Hawaii (over 90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic 
activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from 
the active volcanoes located in the southern portion of the Island of Hawaii. 
The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than 
Magnitude 6 (M6+), including the recent May 4, 2018 earthquake. Based on 
information obtained from the United States Geological Survey (USGS) Bulletin 
2006, the following is a list of some destructive earthquakes that occurred on the 
Island of Hawaii since 1868.

Footing subgrades should be recompacted to at least 90 percent relative 
compaction to provide a relatively firm and unyielding surface prior to the placement of 
reinforcing steel or concrete. Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of 
footing excavations should be over-excavated to expose the underlying firm materials. The 
over-excavation should be backfilled with general fill materials compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction, or the footing may be deepened to bear on the underlying 
firm materials.  
In general, the bottom of the footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches 
below the lowest adjacent finished grade. Footings constructed near the tops of slopes or 
on the sloping ground should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal 
set-back distance of 6 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the slope 
face. 
Footings adjacent to existing retaining walls should be embedded deep enough to 
avoid surcharging the retaining wall foundations. Foundations next to utility trenches or 
easements should be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward 
from the bottom edge of the utility trench or the footings should be embedded to a depth 
as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This requirement is necessary to avoid 
surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce 
the potential for appreciable foundation settlement. 
If foundations are designed and constructed in strict accordance with the 
recommendations presented herein, we estimate total settlements of the foundations to be 
less than 1 inch. Differential settlements between adjacent footings supported on similar 
materials may be on the order of about 0.5 inches or less. 
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction between the base 
of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth pressure developed 
against the near-vertical faces of the embedded portion of foundations. A coefficient of 
friction of 0.4 may be used for footings bearing directly on new compacted fills or the 
recompacted on-site sandy/gravelly soils. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may 
be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of 
depth (pcf), assuming that the soils around the footings are well-compacted. Unless 
covered by pavements or slabs, the passive pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches 
below the finished grade should be neglected. For footings adjacent to sloping ground, the 
passive resistance should be reduced or neglected.

Slabs-On-Grade 
We anticipate concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project. The 
concrete walkways may be supported on the on-site grading materials or select granular 
fill materials compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Control joints should 
be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at 
right-angle intersections. The bottom of thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved 
areas should be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade. 
It should be emphasized that the areas adjacent to the slab edges should be 
backfilled tightly against the edges of the slabs with relatively impervious soils. These 
areas should also be graded to divert water away from the slabs and to reduce the 
potential for water ponding around the slabs. 
Retaining Structures 
Retaining structures, such as retaining walls of up to about 15 feet in height and 
culvert headwall extensions, may be required for the project. The following general 
guidelines may be used in the design of retaining structures planned at the project site. 
Retaining Structure Foundations 
In general, we believe retaining structure foundations may be designed in 
accordance with the recommendations and parameters presented in the 
"Foundations" section herein. The retaining structure footings should have a 
minimum width of 18 inches. In addition, the bottom of wall foundations on relatively 
flat areas should be embedded at a minimum depth of 24 inches below the lowest 
adjacent finished grade. A Geolabs representative should observe the footing 
excavations during construction to evaluate the exposed conditions. 
For sloping ground conditions, the bottom of the footing should extend deeper to 
obtain a minimum 6-foot setback distance measured horizontally from the outside 
edge of the footing to the slope face. Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction 
of the slope should be constructed in stepped footings.

Drainage 
In general, retaining structures should be well-drained to reduce the potential for 
build-up of hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of a 
12-inch wide zone of permeable material, such as open-graded gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), placed directly around a perforated pipe 
(perforations down) at the base of the retaining wall and along the length of the 
wall. The perforated pipe should discharge to an appropriate outlet or weep 
holes. 
As an alternative, a prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or 
EnkaDrain, may be used instead of the permeable drainage material. The 
prefabricated drainage product also should be hydraulically connected to a 
perforated pipe at the base of the retaining wall. Unless covered by concrete or 
asphaltic concrete, the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively 
impervious materials, such as well-graded soils, to reduce the potential for 
significant water infiltration behind the walls.

Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, old 
foundations and pavements, and other unsuitable materials should be removed 
and disposed of properly off-site. 
Loose and/or soft soils, where encountered, should be over-excavated to expose 
firm and/or dense materials, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled 
with well-compacted fills. The excavated loose and/or soft soils should be 
disposed of properly off-site and/or used in landscape areas, where appropriate. 
After clearing and grubbing, the cut subgrades and areas to be filled should be 
scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction. The compaction requirement should be increased to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction under areas subjected to vehicular traffic. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D1557. 
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) corresponding to 
the maximum dry density. 
Prior to fill placement and in areas where the cut subgrades expose basalt 
formation, the subgrades should be proof-rolled with a minimum of 20-ton 
vibratory roller or a Caterpillar D-9 bulldozer or similar heavy equipment for a 
minimum of six passes to help detect and collapse near-surface cavities. Loose 
areas or cavities disclosed during clearing and proof-rolling operations should be 
opened, cleaned of debris, and backfilled with properly compacted fill or 
concrete.

Imported fill materials should consist of non-expansive select granular material, 
such as crushed basalt or coral. The materials should be well-graded from 
coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in the largest dimension and 
should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. 
The material should have a California Bearing Ratio value of 20 or more and 
should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested in accordance 
with ASTM D1883. Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs for 
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for 
the intended use. 
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted 
to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the 
in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry 
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum 
moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density.  
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or 
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or 
ponding should not be allowed to compact the fills. Where compaction is less 
than required, additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of 
moisture content as necessary to obtain the specific compaction. 
Excavation 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by fill 
materials over alluvium and basalt formation. It is anticipated that the surface fill 
materials may be excavated readily with normal heavy excavation equipment, 
such as excavators. However, deeper excavations may encounter cobbles and 
boulders in the alluvium and dense basalt formation and will likely require the use 
of hoerams or chipping. 
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the subsurface materials are 
based on our visual observation of the field data from the borings. Contractors 
should be encouraged to examine the site conditions and the subsurface data to 
make their own reasonable and prudent interpretation.
