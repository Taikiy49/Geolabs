INTRODUCTION
The Unit 1 portion of the North Halawa Highway extends from Station 376 00
northwestern side of Halawa Quarry to Station 480 00 approximately two miles into the
valley The Phase 1B portion of the project extends from Station 428 00 to Station
480 00 approximately one mile in length A report dated was prepared
and issued by Geolabs Hawaii for this section
A portion of the Phase 1B section of the highway was realigned to avoid an
archeological site
This report presents the results of our additional geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the realigned portion from approximately Station 450 to
Station 467 of the proposed H 3 North Halawa Highway Unit 1 Phase 1B in Halawa
Valley on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location of the highway realignment
is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings of our additional field exploration and our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses based on the data
obtained These recommendations are intended for design input and are not intended
for construction bidding or estimation purposes

The purpose of our additional exploration was to obtain an overview of the
subsurface conditions along the realigned portion of the route in order to develop a
generalized soil rock data set for the formulation of geotechnical engineering
recommendations pertinent to the site preparation and grading requirements of this
section of the proposed H 3 highway

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
General
The project site is located on the island of Oahu the third largest of the eight major
islands of the Hawaiian Archipelago Oahu was formed by the volcanic activity of two
large shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau North Halawa Valley is an erosional feature
which has been deeply incised into the Koolau dome
Koolau was formed during the late Pliocene Epoch and early Pleistocene Epoch
by the accumulation of thinly bedded basaltic lava flows extruded from vents along a
fissure line over thirty miles in length which roughly paralleled the trend of what is now the
Koolau Range The main shield building activity ended in the early Pleistocene Epoch
approximately 2 5 million years ago
After the end of the main shield building activity the forces of erosion began to cut
deep valleys into the volcanic domes During the middle to late Pleistocene Epoch the
Ice Age sea levels fluctuated eustatically in response to the cycles of continental
glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill the ocean
basins so sea levels fell to stands below those currently existing When the glaciers
melted and retreated more water became available so sea levels rose to elevations above
those existing at present
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacioeustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 30 Page 6
correspondingly low and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised so that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
mass transfer i e masses of soil and or rock become detached from the steep valley
walls and are transported downslope toward the axis of the valley primarily by gravity as
Colluvium Once this material reaches the stream in the central portion of the valley
alluvial processes become dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited
as Alluvium
in general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashing i e the flows
transmit large volumes of water for very short durations of time Because of this
transport of sediment is intermittent and the bulk of a stream s hydraulic load consists of
a poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sand and fines When the erosional
base levels rise these sediment loads are left as deposits at higher elevations
When deposits are left in place long enough chemical processes begin to alter the
materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the material and causing
an induration or cementation of the coarse portion of the sediment into a poorly
consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate Simultaneously erosion continues in
the areas above the valley floors and upstream in the headwaters This continued erosion
generates material which is transported downslope or downstream covering the older
alluvial deposits
Depending upon the local erosional base level and the rate of transport these
newer sediments are generally transient in terms of geological time In addition their
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 30 Page 7
consistency and density are generally less than that of the older partially consolidated
deposits
In general our exploration encountered several distinct types of geological units
The following are descriptions of the material types
Koolau Basalt
Koolau Basaits consist of thinly bedded basaltic lava flows with an average flow
unit thickness of 10 feet or less The flows occur both in the a a and pahoehoe
forms with transitions between the two forms The lavas are generally vesicular
with jointing resulting from contraction during cooling and solidification of the
molten rock
The Koolau Basalts grade with depth from extremely weathered saprolites to
relatively unweathered materials The basalts generally have good strength
characteristics and may be considered to be excellent bearing formations
Residual Materials
Residual materials are actually a subset of Koolau Basalts since they are soil
materials derived from the in situ chemical weathering of the parent basaltic rocks
These soils range from structureless residual clayey silts or silty clays to saprolites
extremely weathered basalt which are soils that retain the relict structure of the
parent rock e g joints vesicles and or flow structures
These soils generaily grade from soft to hard with depth and continue to grade to
saprolite and to the underlying basalt The residual materials generally exhibit
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2254 30 Page 8
good strength characteristics and are good bearing materials for lightly to
moderately ioaded structures
Older Sediments
These materials were laid down during the later Pleistocene Epoch and are poorly
sorted bouldery alluvial and colluvial deposits In situ weathering and induration
have altered the materials into a highly weathered poorly consolidated conglomer
ate Core samples of these older sediments are typified as highly weathered
cobbles and gravel in a matrix of cemented sandy silt
These materials generally have good to excelient strength characteristics and
provide good bearing for light to moderate foundation loads
Recent Sediments
These materials are differentiated into two units Alluvium and Colluvium
The Alluvium is composed of terrestrial sediments deposited by the movement of
water and is composed mostly of silts and clays with gravel cobbles and boulders
The deposits are poorly sorted and highly variable in consistency and density
They are deposited during periods of active flow of streams and are encountered
in and adjacent to the stream channels
The Colluvium is detrital rock and soil which has been transported from its original
position by gravity This material is generally bouldery Adobe clay and typically
has poor stability and foundation support characteristics

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The field exploration disclosed that most of the highway alignment along the valley
floor is underlain by recent alluvial deposits consisting of cobbles and boulders in a matrix
of river gravel and silty sand The alluvial deposits appear to be adequate to support the
proposed high embankments
The sidehill areas are underlain by stiff alluvial and colluvial soils over dense basalt
rock formation Between Stations 455 50 and 463 00 where up to 20 to 40 feet of fill
is planned for the Emergency Escape Ramp proper site grading procedures
recommended in this report should be closely followed to improve the Stability of the
embankments
Site Grading
The proposed site grading involves primarily embankments up to about 20 to 40
feet high along the valley floor to meet the design grades along the realignment For

Ground Preparation
The site should be cleared of all ground surface vegetation and debris and
existing structures The resulting materials should be removed and properly
disposed of off site
Any local soft spots and unsuitable soft materials encountered within the
construction area shouid be removed and replaced with compacted fill
Following the clearing and grubbing all areas designated to receive fill should be
scarified to a depth of six 6 inches and recompacted to a minimum of 80 of its
maximum dry density as determined by AASHTO Test Method T 180 The
moisture content of the soil should be near the optimum moisture content
Fill Materials
It is anticipated that most of the potential fill material will be generated from the
hillside cuts into the colluvium The matrix of this material is predominantly clay
with moderate to high swelling potential and containing a significant percentage of
bouiders
Also the in situ material generally has a moisture content in excess of 40 which
is about 10 above the optimum moisture content Therefore although use of the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 30 Page 12
natural on site material would result in a significant savings on material costs there
are definite disadvantages to the use of these materials as described below
1 Wet Environment The excavated soil would need to be dried out to near
optimum moisture content prior to placement in the embankment In light
of the relatively high rainfall normally experienced in North Halawa Valley
the drying of the soil may not be feasible during a significant portion of the
year thereby slowing construction of the embankments
2 Over Size Particles The boulders contained in the potential fill materials
would need to be separated from the finer portions of the material to
exclude them from the embankments This additional processing of the soil
would further slow the filling operations and increase costs
3 Expansive Nature and Weak Subgrade Strength Due to the expansive
potential of the clayey soil the on site material should not be used within
the upper three 3 feet of the embankments
4 Embankment Stability Between Stations 455 50 and 463 00 where the
Emergency Escape Ramp is planned up to 40 feet of fill is required on
steep hillside areas which have natural slope ratios ranging from
high embankment it is recommended that imported select granular
materials such as base course or select borrow be used to form the bottom
three 3 feet of fill between Stations 455 50 and 463 00 where the
Emergency Escape Ramp is planned

In light of the above limitations consideration should be given to the use of

Imported borrow should consist of non expansive granular material generally less

A limited amount of excavated basalt rock is anticipated along the alignment of the

material if the particles larger than 6 inches in size are first removed

Mechanically Stabilized Earth Retaining Walls MSERW
Based on the cross sections along the realignment as provided to us by ParEn
Inc terraced Mechanically Stabilized Earth Retaining Walls MSERW are planned to
support the roadway embankments and the Emergency Escape Ramp
MSERW s are composite systems of soil and reinforcing members comprising
layers of welded wire mesh fabrics grids or steel strips within the backfill to improve the
shear and compressive strength of the soil
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2254 30 Page 16
The reinforcing systems are placed in horizontal layers as the fill is placed and are
connected to the precast wall facing units The outer wall facings usually consist of
precast concrete units which are founded on level concrete pads These pads should be
embedded a minimum of about one tenth of the height of the MSERW s or about four 4
feet
The bulk of the material comprising the MSERW s along the valley floor is
anticipated to bear on the recent Alluvium consisting mainly of cobbles and boulders
The MSERW s supporting the Emergency Escape Ramp will bear on compacted fill placed
over the steep hillside areas
The external stability of the structure is analyzed as a gravity retaining wall for the
following modes of failure 1 bearing capacity 2 base sliding 3 overturning 4 deep
seated slips Based on the current wall configurations provided to us our analysis
indicates that these external stability criteria can be satisfied by the proposed MSERW
structures
Select granular material is required in order to develop the required frictional
resistance between the backfill and reinforcing members The minimum requirements for
the backfill material are as follows
a Not more than 15 by weight finer than a No 200 sieve
b not more than 25 by weight coarser than 3 inches
C a maximum particle size of less than 6 inches and
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 30 Page 17
d the corrosion potential must also be limited to reduce deterioration of the
metal reinforcing members if they are used
The internal stability analysis which primarily determines the length spacing and
type of reinforcement used and design of the remaining internal details of the MSERW
systems will depend on the particular system used and therefore must be accomplished
by the specialty contractor selected for the construction
Steep temporary cuts will be required during construction in order to allow
installation of the MSERW It appears that the lower half of the deep wall excavation will
encounter basalt rock or conglomerate which may be cut at a slope ratio of one fourth
horizontal to one vertical on a short term basis The upper half of the
excavation will be into stiff soil Ideally these soils should be sloped at about one
horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V or flatter However due to the steep existing ground
surface very large cuts would result and would require a tremendous amount of backfill
In light of the above it appears that steep temporary slope ratios of one half horizontal
to one vertical may be adopted for construction of the MSERW s Some
sloughing of the soil slopes may occur depending on the weather conditions and length
of time that the cuts are exposed Additionally temporary shoring of the steep cuts in soil
may be required
