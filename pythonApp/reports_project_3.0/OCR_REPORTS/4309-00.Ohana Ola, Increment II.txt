INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Ohana Ola Increment II located in the Waianae District on the Island of
Oahu Hawaii The site is located at the northwest corner of the intersection of Lualualei
Homestead Road and Kuwale Road The general location and vicinity of the project site are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses These
recommendations are intended to assist in the design of the mass grading building structure
foundations retaining walls slabs pavements utilities and on site sewage disposal only The
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end
of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger shield
Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age and forms the majority
of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active
and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank
These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau
The Waianae Range is composed of layered basaltic lava flows and pyroclastic
material which are grouped and classified as the Waianae Volcanic Series The Waianae
Volcanic Series is divided into lower middle and upper volcanic members The lower
member comprises lava flows and associated pyroclastic rocks that built the main mass of the
Waianae Shield Volcano The middle member consists of rock that accumulated and
gradually filled the vast volcanic caldera The upper member is a relatively thin capping layer
which covered the entire top of the shield volcano late in its history of evolution
Once the Waianae Shield Volcano formed a long period of deep erosion
sedimentation and subsidence of Oahu occurred which produced the large valleys of the
western side of the shield volcano These erosional valleys were gradually filled with
enormous accumulations of alluvium and colluvium deposited as a combined result of stream
erosion base level rise sea level rise and subsidence of the island mass Over time and
as a result of sea level fluctuation some of the older alluvial and colluvial deposits were buried
and through consolidation and cementation formed relatively dense deposits However the
current surficial deposits above these older deposits are typically found to be
unconsolidated

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is a 12 382 acre lot located at the northwest corner of the intersection
of Lualualei Homestead Road and Kuwale Road in the Waianae District on the Island of
Oahu Hawaii The site is bounded by seven multi family townhouse structures with a central
community center building and a 20 stall parking lot to the east The site is also bounded by
agricultural lots to the north south and west and Kuwale Road to the east The site is a flag
lot off of Lualualei Homestead Road however access to the site is also possible from Kuwale
Road
The topography is slightly sloping down toward the existing ditch which traverses the
site generally in an east west orientation Ground elevations at the site range from about

southeast and southwest corners of the project site The existing ditch was observed to be
about 4 to 8 feet in depth with banks at two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V slope
inclinations The project site was covered with dry vegetation kiawe trees bushes boulders
and bare soil areas throughout the majority of the project site

PERCOLATION TESTING
Six percolation tests were performed in six separate shallow borings The testing
was performed in general accordance with the State of Hawaii Department of Health s
Administrative Rules Chapter 2 Wastewater Systems and Chapter 10 of the
Ten States Standards to evaluate the infiltration characteristics of the subsurface materials
for wastewater disposal septic tanks or leach fields Our four percolation tests were
performed by drilling 4 inch diameter boreholes to depths of about 4 8 to 5 feet below the
existing ground surface using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with auger equipment The
two percolation tests performed previously by Dames Moore were performed by drilling
4 inch diameter boreholes to depths of about 3 9 to 4 2 feet below the existing ground
surface The approximate locations of the percolation test borings are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2
Approximately one inch of gravel was then placed at the bottom of the boreholes to
protect the bottom of the holes from scouring and sediments During testing the holes were
carefully filled with water to a minimum depth of 12 inches above the gravel pack The time
intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which calculation of the
percolation rates were based The percolation tests performed by Dames Moore indicated
percolation rates of about 8 minutes per inch at Boring No DMP 1A and about 83 minutes
per inch at Boring No DMP 2 The results of our percolation tests are presented in
Appendix C of this report
GEOLABS INC

Based on the percolation tests conducted the percolation rates generally range from
about 8 to 100 minutes per inch We anticipate that it will be difficult to assess the appropriate
percolation rate for each location due to the highly variable soil profile and the high clayey
nature of the on site soils at the project site Therefore to increase the probability of
intersecting the more permeable layers and since the more permeable layers appear to be
below a depth of about 5 feet below grade we recommend that the leach fields be lined at the
bottom with at least 3 feet of open graded gravel material With the implementation of the
above recommendations we recommend that an average percolation rate of 50 minutes per
inch be used in the design of the individual septic tanks and leach fields Note that 60 minutes
per inch is the slowest percolation rate allowed by the State of Hawaii Department of Health
During construction we recommend that percolation tests be performed to obtain approximate
percolation rates to confirm the absorption capacity of the disposal system
Due to the high expansion and shrinkage potential of the on site clayey materials the
septic tanks and leach fields should be located at least 10 feet from structures with slab on
grade type construction

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials
should be removed and disposed of properly off site or stockpiled in a designated area
to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials Within the limits
of the slabs on grade the on site soil should be excavated to provide the
recommended non expansive fill thickness
Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the resulting excavation
should be properly backfilled with fill material placed in 6 to 8 inch level loose lifts and
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Utilities to be abandoned

in place under the proposed structures should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete

Soft and yielding areas encountered should be over excavated to expose firm natural
material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted
engineered fill
Finished subgrades and areas designated to receive fills should be scarified to a depth
of 8 inches moisture conditioned to a minimum of 2 percent above the optimum
moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend
that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close all cracks or prepared again as
recommended above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade
due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft
areas and replacement with well compacted engineered fill
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage

Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by
dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Fills and Backfills
Non structural fill should consist of on site soils free of vegetation and deleterious
materials with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension Note that
non structural areas are defined as areas beyond a lateral distance of 3 feet from
structures and pavements
Structural fill and select granular fill should consist of imported non expansive
granular fill material such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The
material should be free of vegetation and deleterious materials The material should
GEOLABS INC

be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest
dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the
No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and
should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1883 The on site soils are not suitable for structural fill
material or select granular fill material Note that structural areas are defined as areas
within a lateral distance of 3 feet from structures and pavements
Aggregate base course and select borrow subbase materials should meet the material
requirements for Base Course and Subbase Course as specified in Sections 30 and
31 respectively of the Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction

recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its intended use
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM
D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers
multiple wheel pneumatic tired rollers or other types of acceptable compaction
equipment

Cut and Fill Slopes
Cut and fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fills
placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the existing
slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations should
start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in
accordance with the above fill placement recommendations
Fill slopes in excess of 5 feet in height should be constructed by overfilling and cutting
back to the design slope ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face Track rolling of
slopes in excess of 5 feet in height should not be accepted In the event that
over cutting of a slope occurs keying and benching requirements should be
implemented instead of backfilling the slope to the design grade with sliver fills Water
should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and slope planting should be provided
as soon as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes

Pier Type Foundations
Pier type foundations should bear on stiff dense on site soil or properly compacted
select granular fill material The foundations should have a minimum embedment
depth of 36 inches Alternatively if 24 inches of properly compacted select granular
fill is provided beneath the foundations the embedment depth may be reduced to
12 inches However the select granular fill should extend at least 12 inches laterally
from the edge of the foundations In addition properly compacted on site soils
should be placed above the select granular fill to reduce water infiltration below the
foundations

from the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the structures and that the discharge be diverted away from the
foundation and pavement areas We recommend that the discharge be at least 3 feet away
from the building Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should be
avoided Planters next to foundations should also be avoided or have concrete bottoms and
drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils Gravel strips adjacent to
the building should be avoided
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry surface
water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after
construction Due to the fairly level grade of the area construction of drainage inlets to a
subsurface drainage system may be necessary to accommodate proper drainage of surface

foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent loss of strength of the near surface soils The foundation excavations should be

should be maintained to drain surface run off away from walls and foundations

APPENDIX C
Field Percolation Testing
Four percolation tests were performed in the four shallow borings designated as
Boring Nos P 1 to P 4 The testing was performed in general accordance with the State
Systems and Chapter 10 of the Ten States Standards to evaluate the infiltration
characteristics of the subsurface materials for wastewater disposal septic tanks or leach
fields Our percolation tests were performed by drilling 4 inch diameter boreholes to
depths of about 4 8 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface using a truck mounted drill
rig equipped with auger equipment The approximate locations of the percolation test borings
are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Approximately one inch of gravel was then placed at the bottom of the boreholes to
protect the bottom of the holes from scouring and sediments During testing the holes
were carefully filled with water to a minimum depth of 12 inches above the gravel pack
The time intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which
calculation of the percolation rates were based The results of our percolation tests are
presented on the Site Evaluation Percolation Test Data Plates C 1 through C 4
