SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic
lavas from two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano
Waianae is estimated to be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is
estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age As volcanic
activity in Waianae ended lava flows from Koolau banked against its
eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as the Schofield
Plateau or Central Oahu
The forces of weathering and erosion attacked this plateau generating
sediments which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the
project site and further south these sediments accumulated along with and
interbedded with marine sediments and coral algal reef formations to form
a sedimentary wedge ranging in thickness from zero feet in the area of H 1
Freeway to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Fwa Plain
and serves as the confining formation or caprock over the artesian
basal aquifers of southern Oahu Deposition of sediments has continued
from earlier geologic time through the present
During the Pleistocene or Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in
response to the advance and retreat of the continental glaciers As the
glaciers grew sea levels fell below the present level Conversely as
the glaciers melted the sea rose to levels higher than the present
These fluctuations in sea level resulted in fluctuations in sedimentary
deposition as the base level changed During higher stands of the sea
sediments accumulated at elevations above present sea level During lower
stands these sediments were exposed and eroded or hardened by exposure
These Pleistocene sediments are referred to in this report and our
previous report as Older Alluvium
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 1889 10 Page 6
Following the Pleistocene sea level stabilized at its current
elevation and sediments began to accumulate in areas which had been eroded
during the lower stands of the sea Most of these sediments have been
deposited in estuaries marshes and similar low energy environments
Therefore they are less consolidated than the previously exposed
Pleistocene sediments These younger sediments are referred to in this
report and our previous report as Recent Alluvium

DISCUSSTON AND RECOMMENDATIONS
In general it is our opinion that the subject residential and golf
course development projects are feasible from a geotechnical engineering
point of view provided that our recommendations are followed
It is our current understanding that the proposed golf course area is
inadequately drained and subject to flooding from Honouliuli Stream This
flooding is partially due to the relatively flat grades and low relief
found in the makai portion of the proposed golf course area
Consideration had been given to constructing a drainage channel through
the area to alleviate the potential flooding However the soft ground
and high groundwater conditions encountered during our field
reconnaissance and boring exploration limited the feasibility of such a
channel Therefore it was determined that raising the grades of the
makai area to create a more restricted drainage course would channelize
the flows to allow better control of the high stages of Honouliuli Stream
The fill embankment type of mass grading at the makai area of the
proposed golf course presents two major requirements for grading design
and construction First there must be a ready source of fill material
for the site filling type of mass grading effort Second the soft wet
ground conditions encountered in the lower makai area may present
settlement and stability problems unless special design considerations and
construction techniques are utilized
In our opinion the most readily available source of on site fill
material is from the proposed Increment I housing area Preliminary
estimates indicate that lowering the grades in Increment I an average of
three 3 feet would yield sufficient fill material for the proposed golf
course
The underlying soft compressible soil conditions will result in
earth settlements which will require special consideration in the
earthwork design and allowance for additional quantities of fill material
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 1889 10 Page 9
to account for the settlements Additionally the soft wet soils may
become unstable when influenced by loads imposed by fills This potential
instability will affect the grading design by limiting the steepness
and or height of fill slopes
As previously discussed the soils encountered in the Increment I
housing area and the proposed golf club house area may be described as
Older Alluvium and generally consist of clays with a low to moderately
high swelling expansion potential As discussed elsewhere in this report
it is not readily possible to distinguish between the low expansion
potential soils and the moderately high expansion potential soils
Therefore it is preferable to design for the higher expansion potential
and give special consideration to the preparation of the subgrade and the
construction of the building foundations
Special considerations must also be given for the utilities
irrigation system and structures which will be built in the portions of
the proposed golf course area underlain by compressible subsoils

Foundations
Our exploration has indicated that most of the proposed Increment I
housing area is underlain by Older Alluvium soils These soils were
deposited in a complex depositional environment The complexities of the
environment have resulted in a wide range of variation in soil types and
characteristics in both the horizontal and vertical dimensions our
laboratory testing indicates that the soils are primarily clays but range
from low to high plasticity Visually it is not readily possible to
distinguish between the low and high plasticity clays Such critical
differentiation may be verified by further laboratory testing
In general low plasticity CL soils have low shrink swell potential
and high plasticity CH soils have high potential Laboratory swell
testing performed for the Increment I housing development generally
confirms that this situation exists at the project site Laboratory swell
test values on one inch thick air dried soil samples range from negligible
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1889 10 Page 14
to in excess of 10 when saturated under a surcharge load of 55 p s f
loading conditions which are equivalent to a conventional slab on grade
residence The difference between the soils is not apparent without
laboratory testing
Unless it is possible to test a sufficient mmber of soil samples in
the subgrade of each lot during construction so that individualized

Irrigation System
It is anticipated that most of the irrigation system will be
installed in areas underlain by the soft wet Recent Alluviun
soils The placement of fill for golf course grading will induce
long term settlements in these soils which could cause damage to the
irrigation piping Therefore it is recommended that flexible pipe
with joints which will compensate for ground pipe movement be
utilized The pipe should be bedded and cushioned in a granular
material such as S4C or coarse sand
One possible type of pipe to use is PVC irrigation pipe with
bell and spigot O ring sealed joints The pipe is somewhat
flexible and the joints would allow some compensation for movement
Excessive movement could result in uncoupling of the joint However
this could be tolerated as a maintenance condition with the
realization that some damages to the grounds and or any adjacent
structures or pavements could occur when the joint separates and
allows water to come in contact with the subsoils
An alternative which could be considered is the use of a highly
flexible pipe or tubing such as polybutylene or high density
polyethylene These pipes could be laid in a sinuous manner in a
loose sand cushion in a trench which has been excavated somewhat
wider than normal This would allow the irrigation line to elongate

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
tn nn it em i
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the
Boring Logs at the appropriate sample depths
Ten 10 Atterberg Limit tests were performed to aid in the
Classification of the soils The test results are summarized on
Plate B 1
Twenty eight 28 one inch ring swell tests were performed on
undisturbed and remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential
of the soils in both natural and compacted conditions The test
Three 3 sieve analyses tests were performed on selected
samples of the soils to evaluate their grain size distribution The
test results are presented on Plate B 3
Four 4 direct shear tests were performed on selected samples
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the soils The
test results are shown on Plates B 4 through B 7
Nine 9 consolidation tests were performed on selected samples
to evaluate the potential settlement characteristics of the soils
The test results are shown on Plates B 8 through B 16
One 1 triaxial shear test was performed on a selected sample
to evaluate the strength characteristics of the soil The test
result is shown on Plate B 17
Three 3 Proctor tests were performed to determine the moisture
content verses dry density relationship of the compacted soil The
test results are summarized on Plate B 18
Three 3 California Bearing Ratio CBR tests were performed on
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate their suitability for
road subgrade support The test results are summarized on Plate
B 18
