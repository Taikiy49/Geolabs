INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our supplemental geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the proposed Maui Sam s Wholesale Club Wal Mart located
at the intersection of Haleakala Highway and Dairy Road near the Kahului Airport on the
island of Maui Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the site are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our supplemental findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our supplemental field exploration laboratory testing and

input for foundation and site grading and may not be appropriate to form a basis for the
preparation of construction cost estimates nor for construction bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed project consists of the construction of a new wholesale warehouse
facility in Kahului on the island of Maui Hawaii We understand the proposed warehouse
facility will include construction of an approximately 128 000 square foot building with a
loading dock area for trucks and a paved parking lot with 1007 stalls as shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2
The proposed building superstructure will consist of a combination of load bearing
concrete block walls and steel columns supporting the roof with steel joist girders and bar
joists The finished floor elevation is planned at 14 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
The truck loading ramp area is anticipated to be a below grade ramp The height
of retaining wall associated with this structure is not known at this time We understand
that standard duty flexible pavements are planned for the parking lot areas and special
heavy duty flexible and or rigid pavements are planned for the truck drive areas
Based on information provided by Wal Mart maximum column loads on the order
of 145 kips for dead plus live loads are anticipated for the proposed warehouse
structure Maximum column uplift loads are about 30 kips The maximum uniform load
on continuous footings is 6 kips per lineal foot The estimated maximum uniform floor
slab load is 250 pounds per square foot p s f with a maximum concentrated floor slab
load of 16 kips

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The borings drilled and test pits excavated for this supplemental exploration
generally encountered dry to moist sandy and clayey silt fill soils extending to depths of
GEOLABS HAWAII

about 3 to 12 feet below the surface within the proposed building pad area These fill
soils generally consist of a matrix of clayey silt or sandy silt containing abundant gravel
cobbles boulders and debris The consistency of the silts ranged from loose and dry
near the surface to very stiff and moist with increasing depths We believe that past uses
for the site included cultivation of sugar cane Cane fields are commonly ripped and
turned to depths of up to 5 feet this typically results in a near surface soil profile that may
appear to be fill The engineering properties of such disturbed native soils are those of
undocumented fill
The surficial fill soils are generally underlain by alluvial sandy soils residual silty and
clayey soils and basalt in a moderately to highly weathered state The alluvial sands are
generally medium dense to very dense containing abundant gravel at greater depth
Boulders were encountered below the fill soils in Boring No 7 from a depth of about 13
to 25 feet below the ground surface The residual silts and clays are derived from the
underlying basalt formation and grade from medium stiff to hard The basalt formation
was encountered in the three supplemental borings consistently at a depth of about 27
to 30 feet Although slightly to moderately weathered the basalt formation encountered
by our exploration should be able to provide good foundation support characteristics
Groundwater was encountered in the borings drilled at the time of our
supplemental field exploration from depths ranging from about 10 to 11 feet below the
existing ground surface corresponding to elevations of 3 to 5 feet MSL which falls
within the range of 1 to 7 feet MSL encountered during our initial exploration We
encountered groundwater in only one of the 13 test pits excavated for this supplemental
exploration TP 32 at a depth of about 10 feet below the ground surface which falls
within the range of 61 to 12 feet below the ground surface encountered in our earlier
exploration It should be noted that groundwater elevations are subject to change due
to rainfall time of year and tidal fluctuations

Predrilling
It should be noted that a high frequency of cobbles and boulders were

Pile Driving Criteria
Each pile should be driven continuously without interruption with the exception of
splicing if needed A pile may be rejected or its design capacity may be reduced
when the driving resistance is interrupted for more than four 4 hours and the pile
cannot be driven to the required depth
Because local variations in bedrock weathering and strength characteristics are
likely to exist in the bearing basalt the supporting capacity of each pile must be
confirmed in the field by our office during driving This may be accomplished by
using a driving resistance procedure such as the Engineering News Record ENR
formula or the more sophisticated Wave Equation Analysis to evaluate pile bearing
capacity
For preliminary design purposes driving may be terminated when the whole pile
length is driven down to the finished tip elevations or when aggregated blow
counts are 40 blows for the last foot Appropriate driving criteria will be developed
after selection of the pile driving equipment

approved by Geolabs Hawaii prior to delivery to the job site Imported soils having
low expansion potential should be brought to above the optimum moisture content
and compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction All surfaces
should be finished to create smooth unyielding subgrades and should be kept
moist until covered by concrete slabs or pavements
Excavation for Utility Lines
It is anticipated that underground utility trenches of up to 10 feet deep will be
required during construction associated with the warehouse facility Based on our
borings we anticipate that conventional excavation techniques may be used for utility
trench excavation with the possible exception where boulders and concrete may be
encountered Because of the variable quality and composition of the fill soils on site we
anticipate that utility trenches greater than about 5 feet in depth below the existing ground
surface would need to be shored Because groundwater was encountered at depths
ranging from about 71 to 15 feet below the ground surface dewatering would also likely
be required Determination of the method and equipment used for excavation should be
left to the contractor within practical limits and safety considerations
Excavation Support
Because of the variable subsoil conditions and shallow to moderate groundwater
levels encountered in our exploration it is our opinion that continuous excavation support
will be required for utility excavations deeper than about five feet 5 In general we
believe that the trenches may be sloped back in conformance with OSHA requirements
If the sequence of construction prevents sloping back of the upper sides of the trench
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3045 10 Page 22
then standard temporary shoring consisting of plywood sheeting and hydraulic jacks in
conformance with OSHA requirements may be used
The shoring and bracing system used to support the trenches must comply with
all applicable safety requirements The adequacy and safety of the shoring and bracing
installation operations should be made the responsibility of the contractor His
representative who should be required to continually monitor the excavation and
dewatering work at the site would therefore have the opportunity for observing changing
conditions which might affect the excavation stability during construction
Appropriate loads due to surcharge equipment traffic or sloping ground conditions
adjacent to the excavations should be added to the recommended earth pressures for
design purposes
Even if good construction procedures are followed some movement of the
excavation support bracing system may be anticipated because of changes in earth
stresses during excavation Movements depend greatly on workmanship e g how
quickly and tightly the support is installed It also depends on the types of subsoils the
depth and width of excavation and the type of support In view of the complexity of the
problem it is difficult to accurately estimate the amount of movement to be anticipated
of a bracing system and the adjacent ground Therefore it is important to realize that the
excavation support should be installed as early as practicable and that measurements of
vertical and horizontal movements are required especially in areas where ground
movements may result in damage to existing adjacent structures
The ground adjacent to excavations should be continuously monitored for cracks
or dips and other indications of movements and possible sloughing of the excavation
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3045 10 Page 23
walls Such monitoring is particularly critical in areas adjacent to existing structures and
utilities
Excavation Dewatering
Dewatering of the utility excavations may be necessary since the groundwater
levels encountered in our field exploration ranged up to only about 71 feet below the
ground surface Dewatering for construction is the sole responsibility of the contractor
The dewatering operations should be coordinated with the trench shoring support so that
the stability of the excavations is not jeopardized The operations should be carried out
without softening the bottom of the excavations
It is our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents should
be written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural groundwater table
and to exclude the water from the excavations to permit construction of underground
utilities under safe and dry conditions Therefore these works or systems should include
but not be limited to pumping
Dewatering Method
The method and equipment to be used for the excavation dewatering should be
determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations
We suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in selecting a
suitable method of dewatering
1 The method should result in the least disturbance or damage to the
adjacent existing structures
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3045 10 Page 24
2 The method should maintain stability of and also provide safe and
dry working conditions in the excavations
3 The method should be sufficiently flexible to allow modifications to
accommodate various ground conditions
Dewatering by means of a well point system along the outside of the excavations
is generally not recommended The resultant areal depression of the natural
groundwater table could induce consolidation of subsoils resulting in potential
ground settlements
Construction Precautions
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to
dewatering It is also recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be
retained by the contractor to design and evaluate the dewatering system used
The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His qualified representative who should be
required to be continuously present at the site will have the best opportunity to
promptly observe the effects of dewatering during construction and to implement
as soon as possible necessary precautionary or remedial measures including but
not limited to stopping or slowing down the dewatering operations
Where encountered at the excavation bottoms permeable granular subsoils may
be susceptible to piping and quick conditions Therefore the project dewatering
operations should be done without pumping out soil fines pumping clear water
GEOLABS HAWAII

operations would likely be discharged to a County storm drain system A permit
