Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes which include the following Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which
has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and
Mauna Loa and Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of Hualalai Mountain which
makes up the west central portion of the island The lava formation observed at the
project site prior to site grading activities appears to consist of both a a and pahoehoe
type lava flows which spread and ponded as they approached the ocean Ava lava
typically is characterized by a porous rough and irregular flow surface resembling a
jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders Pahoehoe lava is
characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of
vesicular porous rock
Cavities are encountered commonly in basaltic lavas especially the pahoehoe type
lava These cavities are formed when the lava is still in a molten state and they represent
both lava tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava
tubes are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like
structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Blisters and
pockets are generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted
through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and
along clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they
serve as the primary waiter transmission features
Subsurface Conditions
As mentioned previously the Maniniowali Development project site is located in
the Kaupulehu area in the District of North Kona on the Island of Hawaii In general the
original topography of the project site prior to site grading activities consisted of irregular
or hummocky lava flow terrain with frequent topographic depressions separated by
adjacent higher relief mounds and low ridge features
Based on observations during the mass grading operations the project site
generally is underlain by medium hard to hard basalt formation and clinker materials It
should be noted that lava tubes cavities and blisters are encountered commonly in
basaltic lava flows in the vicinity of the project site In addition numerous lava tube
features were observed at the project site during the mass grading operations It should
be noted that near surface lava tubes detected within the limits of grading for the mass

Discussion and Recommendations
Based on the grading plan for the project the original ground surface elevations
ranged from about 128 to 140 feet Mean Sea Level MSL at the majority of Lot No 6
Lot No 6 generally was graded to a finished pad elevation of about 138 feet MSL Mass
grading at Lot No 6 generally consisted of excavations of up to about 2 feet deep and
placement of up to about 10 feet of new fills to achieve the design grades
During the mass grading operations extensive subgrade preparation procedures
were employed within the limits of grading at the commencement of the mass grading
operations for the project to reduce the potential for collapse of near surface cavities
and or voids within the lots The intent of the subgrade preparation procedures is to assist
in detecting and collapsing near surface cavities and or voids contained in the porous
near surface materials It should be noted that the subgrade preparation procedures
employed for this project are consistent with the standard of practice for similar type
projects and similar subsurface conditions along the Kohala Coast on the Island of Hawaii
In general the subgrade preparation in fill areas consisted of ripping the original
ground to a depth of about 3 feet or until refusal using a ripper tooth of a large bulldozer to
assist in detecting and collapsing near surface cavities and or voids contained in the
porous near surface materials After the fill surfaces have been ripped the ground was
generally leveled with the use of a Komatsu 375 bulldozer working the surface for about
four passes to provide a relatively level surface After leveling of the rugged surfaces with
the bulldozer the fill subgrades generally were proof rolled with a Caterpillar 825
sheepsfoot roller compactor minimum 20 tons for about eight passes to help detect and
collapse near surface cavities and or voids
Fill materials consisting of some cobbles and boulders and structural fills were
placed in the fill areas of the project site Structural fills consisted of well graded granular
materials generally smaller than 6 inches in dimension In general the fill materials within
the top 2 feet of the lots within the fill areas of the pad consisted of structural fill materials
It should be noted that large boulders of up to about 3 feet in dimension generally
were placed about 4 feet below the finished grades within the pad areas of the lots and
choked off with finer sized materials In addition boulders generally were placed about
6 feet or more horizontally from the face of the fill slopes
Subgrades in cut areas generally were proof rolled with a Caterpillar 825
sheepsfoot roller compactor minimum 20 tons for about eight passes to help detect and
collapse near surface cavities and or voids It should be noted that the mauka portions of
