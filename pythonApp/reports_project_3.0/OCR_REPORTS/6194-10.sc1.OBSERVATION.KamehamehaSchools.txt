Project Considerations
The project site extends approximately 750 linear feet along Princess Drive at the
Puna Street Gate entrance to the Kamehameha Schools Kapalama Campus in the
Kamehameha Heights Kapalama area of the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project site
encompasses a section of basalt rock cut slopes along the existing roadway and upper
flatter natural slopes which extend toward the eastern campus property boundary as
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The purpose for the rockfall mitigation project was to
reduce the potential for falling rocks to encroach on the roadway
Due to the presence of the existing Cultural Preservation Site A 2 requiring
preservation within the project limits an archaeologist was retained to aid in the
development of an appropriate construction plan in accordance with generally accepted
archaeological feature preservation guidelines Furthermore full time archaeological field
monitoring services were provided during the construction of the localized Grouted Rubble
Pavement GRP slope protection below the preservation feature
This report summarizes the construction of the rockfall mitigation improvements
shown on the project construction plans This report provides a generalized description of
our field observations conducted during the rockfall mitigation construction
Rockfall Mitigation Construction
We observed the construction of rockfall mitigation improvements at the project site
between March 22 2011 and Prometheus Construction performed the
rockfall mitigation contract work During construction full time field monitoring of the GRP
installation was provided by an archaeologist from Cultural Surveys Hawaii We observed
the following major work tasks
Vegetation cutting and ground surface exposure
Rock demolition and removal from the upper slope area
Sweep of cobbles and boulders from the upper slope area
Scaling of the lower rock cut slope
Construction of localized GRP slope facing
Construction of Concrete Rubble Masonry CRM pedestal supports
Application of bonded fiber matrix hydromulch
The following generalized descriptions of the construction activity are based on our
field observations during construction

Rock Demolition
We observed the demolition of several larger blocks of unstable rock and loose
boulders residing on the upper slope surfaces The rock demolition was necessary to
break the larger rocks into pieces that could be safely removed from the slope The rock
demolition encompassed both loose surface boulders and outcroppings of fractured and
potentially unstable rock outcrops
The rock demolition was performed by drilling small diameter holes in the target
rock and using a hydraulic rock splitter to fracture the rock into manageable pieces for
controlled removal Temporary protection measures such as wire netting and anchored
cables were used to secure the rock while demolition proceeded Some of the broken
rock fragments were used in the CRM pedestal support construction at rock outcrops B 2
and B 3
Boulder Sweep
We observed the collection and removal of existing loose surface cobbles and
boulders from the upper slope surfaces within the project limits In general rocks larger
than about 6 inches in dimension were swept from the slope to reduce the potential for
falling rock hazards Some of the rocks were also used in the CRM pedestal support
construction at rock outcrops B 2 and B 3
Rock Slope Scaling
We observed the manual scaling of loose rocks from the existing rock cut slope
face within the project limits The work was performed by a 3 man scaling crew and a
Bobcat loader The crew removed loose rock from the slope face by hand and by using a
steel pry bar We observed that most of the rock slope instability is the result of invasive
tree roots prying into the existing rock fractures Many of the penetrating roots
some ranging up to about 4 inches wide had developed slope face separations between
the loose severely broken rock and the deeper more competent rock material
Some sections of the rock cut slope face were composed of severely fractured and
decomposed rock material which required a more intensive scaling effort to develop a
stabilized slope face The approximate locations of the more intensive rock scaling efforts

Grouted Rubble Pavement GRP
The GRP slope facing was constructed below the Cultural Preservation Site A 2
under the full time observation of an archaeologist from Cultural Surveys Hawaii Based
on our observations the GRP slope facing was constructed in general conformance with
the archaeological preservation plan developed for the site
The masonry crew from Prometheus Construction performed the GRP installation
at the specified location using competent basalt stones derived from an off site source
Cement mortar was mixed onsite with an electric mixer The GRP was constructed to
closely approach but not bond to the bounding preservation stone alignment
Concrete Rubble Masonry CRM
The basalt rock CRM pedestal supports for overhanging rock outcrops B 2 and B 3
shown on the construction plans were constructed using on site competent rock materials
and Quikrete mortar mix The pre existing void space below the project rock outcrops was
completely filled to engage the roof of the void with the supporting CRM material
Hydromulch
To complete the project unseeded bonded fiber matrix hydromulch was applied to
selected areas of bare soil exposure after construction Due to the hard rocky and uneven
slope surface encountered after vegetation clearing between about and about
the bonded fiber hydromulch was used instead of erosion control matting for
better temporary erosion control The installation of erosion control mats was determined
to be largely ineffective for the rocky and rough irregular slope surface to be covered
In addition we observed a rapid re growth of natural tall grass vegetation on the
majority of the upper slope surfaces therefore the application of unseeded hydromulch in
these areas was determined by Geolabs to be unnecessary due to the natural
re establishment of the pre existing ground cover growth The bonded fiber hydromulch
was applied to selected areas along the crest of the cut slope where some localized bare
soil exposures were encountered between about and about

Slope Maintenance Considerations
A substantial amount of loose fractured rock material was removed from within the
project limits which should significantly reduce the potential for dangerous falling rock
conditions However due to natural ongoing rock weathering and erosion decomposition
processes and the close proximity of existing large trees with penetrating roots on the
upper slope we believe that some future scaling of loose and unstable rock from the cut
slope face will be necessary The rock cut slope should be periodically inspected for signs
of potential large rock instability at about 1 to 2 year intervals It may be necessary to
conduct some level of additional rock cut slope scaling at about 5 to 10 year intervals to
maintain a relatively clean and stable rock cut slope face
Based on our observations we do not envision the need for additional rockfall
mitigation on the upper flatter slope surfaces for a longer period of time due to the limited
and generally stable rock outcroppings remaining on the slope However the existing
larger upright boulder and surrounding rocks contained within the Cultural Preservation
Site A 2 were left undisturbed during the rockfall mitigation work Therefore the large
upright boulder and associated smaller rocks should be visually monitored for signs of
developing instability such as tilting or fracturing that could lead to a rockfall
To reduce the potential for developing future rock cut slope instability we
recommend the elimination of existing tree and wood stem shrubbery growth on the cut
slope face and within about 10 feet from the top of the cut slope Existing tree and shrub
growth such as ironwood and rubber tree on the cut slope face and near the crest of the
cut slope should be cut and the remaining stumps and roots poisoned to prevent
re growth The elimination of tree growth in these areas should help to reduce the potential
for root penetration within the fractured rock formation comprising the slope face Based
on our observations tree roots prying and expanding within existing rock formation
fractures and other rock discontinuities is a significant cause of the rock slope instability at
this site If landscaping is desired along the cut slope we recommend the planting of low
ground cover such as vines and ferns that do not have penetrating and expansive root
systems
Furthermore we observed two mature Eucalyptus trees growing at the top of the
cut slope bordering the new GRP construction site We observed near surface roots from
these trees traversing the existing slope and penetrating into the slope face We
recommend removal of these trees cut and leave short tree stump to reduce the potential
for inducing future slope instability and cracking of the new GRP slope facing New tree
growth within about 10 feet of the cut slope crest should be deterred to promote the
longer term stability of the cut slope face
