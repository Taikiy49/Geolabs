SECTION 3 SITE CHARACTERIZATION
3 1 Climate
The Island of Guam is in the North Pacific Ocean between 144 degrees
36 minutes and 144 degrees 58 minutes longitude and between 13 degrees 13 minutes
and 13 degrees 40 minutes latitude as shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 In
general the climate of Guam is warm and humid with little variation in temperature from
month to month Based on information obtained from the National Oceanic and
Atmospheric Administration NOAA Guam s mean annual temperature is about
27 degrees Celsius throughout the year Typically annual rainfall averages from about
2 000 to per year The rainy season is during the months of July through
November when the monthly rainfall can average over 300 mm per month
Tropical storms and typhoons often pass through or near the Island of Guam
Tropical storms are defined as closed pressure systems with wind speeds ranging from
60 to 120 kilometers per hour Typhoons are similar phenomena with wind speeds
exceeding 120 kilometers per hour During the rainy season tropical storms and
typhoons are common in the vicinity of Guam Tropical storms and typhoons bring high
intensity rainfall when their zone of influence is near Guam In extreme events in the
past a typhoon has produced winds of over 280 kilometers per hour over 3 5 meters of
inundation and 650 mm of rainfall in one day
3 2 Regional Geology
The Island of Guam is the largest and southernmost of the Mariana Island
archipelago which is an island arc parallel to the trend of the subduction zone forming
the Mariana Trench The Mariana Trench is part of a belt of trenches which extend from
Japan to the Palau Islands and include the Japan Trench Mariana Trench Yap Trench
and Palau Trench
The Island of Guam can be broadly divided into three geologic provinces
the southern mountains the central mountains and the northern plateau The
formation of the island started during the Eocene Epoch by the eruption of basaltic

rhyolite lavas The mountainous central portion of the island is composed of
Eocene volcanic rocks and conglomerates tuffs and limestones of Eocene and
Oligocene age Lava flows and limestone formations of the Miocene Epoch make up the
majority of the southern one third of the island The plateaus of the northern portion of
the island were formed while this part of the island was still beneath the sea level
During the Oligocene and Miocene Epochs a complex series of coral and algal reefs
formed an elongated platform of limestones and related deposits
Weathering and solution working of the limestone has partially re crystallized the
calcium carbonate of the limestone forming massive limestones in some areas and
poorly to well cemented detrital limestones in others Typically the detrital limestones
are highly variable in strength characteristics and are karstic or cavernous in nature
Cavities and pockets of voids are expected in the limestone formations In addition the
limestone formations are generally porous Except during heavy storms the majority of
the precipitation naturally percolates through the formation producing little or no surface
water runoff
3 3 Existing Site Conditions
The project site is at the corner of Shoreline Drive and an un named access road
across from the South Tipalao housing area on the southwest side of the U S Naval
Base Guam The southwestern side of the property is generally elevated land fronting
Shoreline Drive across from the South Tipalao housing development The other
two sides of the property are currently bordered by undeveloped land
The proposed new facility is adjacent to the existing Transportation Services
Building 365 a large warehouse structure to the northeast and South Tipalao housing
development to the southwest A siren tower is at the east corner of the site Access to
the new facility will be via an access road from Shoreline Drive or Marine Drive
The project site is presently a large open area of gently sloping grass land on the
order of approximately 22 000 square meters Existing vegetation on the site primarily
consisted of woody brush The topography of the overall project area generally slopes
from the north to the south with a gradient of approximately 3 to 4 percent The

SECTION 4 GEOTECHNICAL EXPLORATION PROGRAM
The geotechnical exploration program conducted for the Military Working Dog
Relocation design build project generally consisted of the following field and laboratory
work components A brief description of each work component is presented below
Detailed discussions findings and test results of the various components are provided
in the respective appendices herein
4 1 Test Boring Exploration
Soil sampling methods and field testing are described in more detail in the
introduction to Appendix A The following discussion provides an overview of the
methods and procedures
Typically the test borings were advanced with 200 mm diameter hollow stem
augers Sampling of the soils was performed using the modified California sampler
ASTM D 3550 and the Standard Penetration Test SPT split spoon sampler
ASTM D 1586
In situ testing of the subsurface materials in the borings included the SPT which
was performed in accordance with ASTM D 1586 SPT sampling provides a measure of
the resistance or consistency of a soil and is reported as the uncorrected N value which
is defined as the number of blows required to drive a standard split spoon sampler
300 millimeters with a 63 5 kilogram hammer after 150 millimeters of initial seating The
penetration resistance of the modified California sampler was recorded in a similar
manner and the modified California sampler blows per 300 millimeters can be
approximately related to the SPT N value through correlation
4 2 Field Permeability Testing
The permeability of the in situ materials were evaluated by performing falling
head tests at selected locations within the future detention pond to evaluate the
infiltration characteristics of the subsurface materials During the falling head test the
boreholes were filled carefully with water to the top of the boreholes The time intervals

APPENDIX A
Test Boring Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
ten borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 10 The borings extended to depths
ranging from about 7 2 to 10 2 meters below the existing ground surface using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight hollow stem augers In addition four
bulk samples of the near surface soils designated as Bulk 1 through Bulk 4 were
collected for laboratory testing The field exploration work was conducted between
March 17 and 23 2009 The approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D 2487
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1 Rock materials
were described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the
Rock Log Legend Plate A 0 2 The Rock Description System is based on the publication
Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses by
International Society for Rock Mechanics March 1977 Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 10
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 75 mm OD Modified California sampler with a 63 5 kilogram hammer falling 760 mm
In addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
50 mm OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 150 mm of a 450 mm drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample
depths

upon which to base calculation of the permeability values of the subsurface materials The
field permeability test results are presented on Plates B 1 and B 2

APPENDIX C
APPENDIX C
Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected soil
sample to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic presentations of the test
results are provided on Plate C 1
Five Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plate C 2
To evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the on site clayey soils
Unconfined Compression test was performed on a selected in situ sample in
accordance with ASTM D 2166 The stress strain curve of the unconfined compression
test is presented on Plate C 3
Four laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The test results are presented on Plates C 4 through C 7
Four Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates C 8 through C 11
