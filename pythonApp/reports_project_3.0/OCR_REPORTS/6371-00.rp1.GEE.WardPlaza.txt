REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the
Waianae and Koolau Shield Volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which
time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred
to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The project site is situated on the southern flank of the Koolau Volcano and on the
coastal plain of Southern Oahu Therefore the geomorphology and subsurface conditions
in the area are directly related to the glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea level during the
Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain The coastal
plain was built on the eroded flanks of the Koolau Volcano which forms the eastern
two thirds of the Island of Oahu The coastal plain was built by extensive accumulation of
alluvium derived from erosion of the volcano interbedded with coral reefs and associated
deposits
The subsurface conditions in the Ward Plaza area are dominated by fossil
Pleistocene Age coral algal reef deposits and related deposits which are intercalated with
terrigenous sediments laid during the higher stands of the sea Therefore the project site
is generally underlain by deposits consisting of calcareous sediments in a lagoonal
depositional environment

designated as Boring Nos 1 through 5 at selected locations within the project area The
borings generally extended to depths of about 5 feet below the existing pavement surface
The approximate borings drilled for this project are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2

PAVEMENT DESIGN
The design procedures used in determining the new flexible pavement section is
based on the Guide for Design of Pavement Structures 1993 developed by the
American Association of State Highway and Transportation Officials AASHTO In
general we recommend the pavement rehabilitation for this project consist of removing
the existing asphaltic concrete layer by milling or cold planning and replacing it with new
asphaltic concrete of about 2 inches in thickness
Traffic Determinations
Based on our field observations we expect the majority of the traffic that will
traverse the project area likely will involve passenger vehicles and some small trucks
Based on the AASHTO design guide we utilized a minimum of 50 000 equivalent single
axle loads ESALs in our design for a 20 year design period
Pavement Structural Sections
Our field observations indicated that the pavement areas under the current project
scope generally exhibit medium severity distress The current design concept involves
removing the existing asphaltic concrete and replacing with a new asphaltic concrete
surface
Based on our field exploration and our experience at the project area we anticipate
soft lagoonal deposits at relatively shallow depths as evidenced by the subsurface
conditions encountered in Boring Nos 1 and 2 Due to the presence of the soft materials
very near the surface conventional pavement reconstruction would involve removing a
minimum of 2 to 3 feet of the near surface materials and replacing it with gravel backfill
wrapped with geotextile fabrics The new flexible pavement section asphaltic concrete
and base course would then be constructed over the engineered backfill This typical
approach likely will involve a fair amount of excavation and potential for over excavation

To reduce the potential for encountering the near surface soft lagoonal deposit
materials we recommend the following approach to rehabilitate the pavement at the
project location
In general we recommend removing the existing asphaltic concrete layer to expose
the underlying base and or subbase materials We then recommend proof rolling the
exposed base materials to obtain a dense and unyielding surface A 2 inch thick layer of
new asphaltic concrete layer may be placed on the proof rolled existing base In this way
we would not excavate below the existing pavement surface any deeper than necessary to
reduce the potential for encountering the soft lagoonal deposit materials encountered in
the borings
After removal of the existing asphaltic concrete layer to accommodate the new
pavement structural section the exposed subgrades should be proof rolled with a
minimum 5 ton static weight drum roller for a minimum of eight passes to compact the
exposed subgrade The drum roller should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet per
minute
In the event that the exposed base materials are pumping during the proof rolling
operations the pumping surface may be stabilized by using cement treatment As a guide
stabilization by cement treatment may consist of one sack of cement 94 pound bag for
approximately 25 square feet of subgrade area Geolabs should be contacted to evaluate
the pumping conditions and to evaluate whether the pavement sections need to be revised
and or modified based on the exposed conditions
We understand the finished grades of the parking areas are slightly lower than the
adjacent roadway We recommend the paved areas be sloped adequately and drainage
gradients should be maintained to carry surface water off the pavements In addition
surface water ponding should not be allowed on site during or after construction
