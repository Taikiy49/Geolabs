1.1  
Project Considerations 
This data report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Kamehameha V Highway relocation along Ni’aupala Fishpond between Mile 
Post 14.62 and Mile Post 14.89 on the Island of Molokai, Hawaii. The project location is shown 
on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
Erosion is occurring along the ocean side of the highway which has caused the ocean side 
edge of the road to be undermined. The road has been significantly damaged to where the road 
is restricted to only one lane of traffic. Based on the information provided, the highway was likely 
built on the fishpond walls consisting of stacked cobbles and boulders. The approximate location 
and dimensions of the fishpond walls are unknown.  
The design team needed to determine the approximate location and dimensions of the 
fishpond walls, including the relative alignment of an existing waterline under the existing 
roadway for planning purposes. To obtain this information, a test pit exploration was proposed 
by the designers at pre-determined locations of the roadway impacted by the ongoing erosion.  
This geotechnical data report presents the results of the test pit exploration conducted 
by RNK Construction, observed by Oceanit and Geolabs representatives on September 18, 2023 
for a single test pit located approximately 37 feet east of Our Lady of Seven Sorrows Catholic 
Church driveway on the east-bound lane of Kamehameha V Highway.

SECTION 2.  
SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
The following sections provide a description of the geologic setting of the project site, the 
subsurface materials and the groundwater conditions encountered in the test pit. 
2.1  
Regional Geology 
The Island of Molokai was formed by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two primary 
shield volcanoes during the early to middle Pleistocene Epoch. The shield volcanoes are known 
as the East Molokai Mountain and the West Molokai Mountain. The West Molokai Mountain, 
comprising the western end of the island, is a low-profile volcanic dome compared to the higher 
profile volcanic dome of the East Molokai Mountain, which comprises the eastern end of the 
island of Molokai. A broad upland plain known as the Hoolehua Plain comprises the central 
portion of the island where the two shield volcanoes coalesced. It should be noted that a 
compact, younger, and short-lived volcano erupted in the late Pleistocene Epoch at Kalaupapa to 
form the Kalaupapa Peninsula lowland along the north coast of the eastern volcano. 
The older West Molokai Mountain consists almost entirely of thin-bedded a`a, and 
pahoehoe lavas with limited late-stage cinder and spatter cones. The volcanic deposits are 
referred to as the West Molokai Volcanic Series. The existing ground surfaces are generally 
covered by extensive lateritic soil development indicating a long-ago volcanic extinction while 
the East Molokai Mountain remained active. Western Molokai is generally arid with few 
water-supply resources. 
The younger East Molokai Mountain dome is mainly composed of thin-bedded tholeiitic 
a`a, and pahoehoe lavas referred to as the lower member of the East Molokai Volcanic Series. 
Most of the East Molokai dome is veneered with alkali basalts and trachyte which erupted from 
large cones and bulbous domes atop the lower member deposits. These deposits are referred to 
as the upper member of the East Molokai volcanic series. 
The tall and broad eastern volcanic dome is deeply incised by large drainage valleys that 
cut into the softer caldera filling lavas comprising the central portion of East Molokai Mountain. 
The large valleys generally drain towards the northern coastline. Faulting and erosion by

ocean-wave-energy attack is believed to have created the steep and tall cliffs bounding most of 
the northern shoreline of the island. The uplands of Eastern Molokai are generally forested and 
high enough to readily tap incoming moisture from the predominant trade wind flow; thus, 
groundwater resources in East Molokai are generally plentiful. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), a variety of sea level changes occurred as a result 
of widespread glacial advance and retreat in the worldwide continents. As the great continental 
glaciers advanced and accumulated, the level of the ocean fell due to a lower quantity of water 
contained in the oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded/melted, global sea levels 
rose due to the increase in meltwater. The landmass of the Island of Molokai remained essentially 
stable in terms of uplift and settlement during these changes; therefore, the fluctuations of sea 
level were eustatic with respect to the landmass elevation. These glacio-eustatic fluctuations 
resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower relative to the present sea level on 
Molokai. Thus, in the present time, ancient marine deposits may be exposed on the land surface 
and ancient terrestrial deposits may be encountered buried in today’s offshore depositional 
environment. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were also affected by these glacio-eustatic sea 
level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the landmass erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and deep valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosion base level was raised such that sediments accumulated 
at higher elevations.  
In the mountainous regions of the Island of Molokai, the erosion processes are dominated 
by mass wasting, which is the detachment and fall by rockfall and landslide activity, of various 
soil and rock materials from the steep-sided valley walls. These mobilized and mixed colluvial 
deposits are then transported by gravity downslope toward the axis of a valley. Once these 
materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley, alluvial transport processes become 
dominant, and the sediments are further mobilized and deposited as alluvium accumulations. 
In general, many Hawaii stream flows are intermittent and flashy, such that the discharge 
flows may transmit very large volumes of water for a very short duration. Because of this, the

transport of sediments is intermittent, and the bulk of the stream's hydraulic load consists of a 
poorly-sorted mixture of boulders, cobbles, gravel, sands, and fines. When the landmass 
erosional base level changes, these sediment loads and deposits are either scoured and 
remobilized or buried in place as alluvial and colluvial deposits. 
When alluvial and colluvial deposits are buried in place for long periods of time, chemical 
processes begin to alter the materials simultaneously, causing a breakdown or further 
weathering of the material. Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the 
sediment, resulting in poorly to moderately consolidated sedimentary rock, such as siltstone, 
sandstone, and conglomerate. Simultaneously, erosion may continue in the upland areas above 
the valley floors. This continued upland erosion generates additional unconsolidated materials, 
which are then transported downslope to further bury the older consolidated alluvial and 
colluvial deposits. 
Depending on the local base level and rate of transport, these younger alluvial and 
colluvial sediments are generally transient in terms of geologic time and space. In addition, their 
consistency/density is generally lower than those of the older, partly to mostly consolidated 
deposits. 
In the last 10,000 years or so, the sea level has adjusted to its present stand. Terrigenous 
and marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low-energy estuarine or lagoonal 
environments, resulting in deposits of soft and/or loose sediments along the coast in the areas 
that were formerly valleys and drainageways. 
The project site is located along the southern shoreline of the Island of Molokai, where 
the geology is predominantly affected by alluvial, lagoonal, and near-shore deposits. The 
placement of recent fill material has brought the project area to its present form.  
2.2 
Subsurface Stratigraphic Units 
We anticipate four stratigraphic units in the vicinity of the proposed road realignment 
limits at Ni’aupala. The stratigraphic units consist of soil and rock materials of varying character 
and engineering physical properties. These are:

 Ta (Upper member Pleistocene) - Lava flows composed chiefly of dense and clinker 
andesite and trachyte, usually nonporphyritic. The dense beds have low 
permeability, but the clinker beds are very permeable. They carry small quantities 
of perched water locally. 
 Tb (Lower member Pleistocene and Pliocene) - Lava flows composed of generally 
thin bedded moderately to highly vesicular basaltic pahoehoe and a’a of olivine 
and picrite basalt, with thin intercalated beds of vitric ash. It is highly permeable 
and freely yields water to wells and tunnels. They carry brackish water along the 
coast of central Molokai. 
 Qa - Unconsolidated and consolidated alluvium; and marine non-calcareous 
sediments. These sedimentary deposits are poorly permeable in general but yield 
fresh or brackish water along Molokai’s south shore.  
 Rs – Beach deposits of the present sea, chiefly non-calcareous on east Molokai. 
Generally loose chiefly basaltic sand. The beach sands are highly permeable and 
contain abundant water, generally salty. 
The Tertiary volcanic rocks of the East Molokai volcanic series Ta and Tb may be encountered 
at depth at the toe of the slopes adjacent to the road realignment inland. The recent 
sedimentary rocks derived from earthy alluvium Qa and beach sand Rs deposits generally 
occur along the coastal plains abutting the foothills of the east Molokai Mountain. The 
approximate delineation of the geological units at the immediate project site is shown in the 
Geologic Plan (Plate 3). 
2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the near-surface conditions by excavating and sampling a single 3-foot by 
6-foot by 5-foot-deep test pit designated as Test Pit No. 1. The approximate test pit location is 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The pavement structure consisted of 8.5 inches of asphaltic concrete underlain by 
approximately 9 inches of fill material consisting of dense, brown, silty sand with some gravel. 
The excavation encountered subgrade materials consisting of approximately 12 inches of dense 
brown silty sand with traces of gravel (fill) overlying medium dense to loose, grayish white 
fine-grained, uniformly-graded silty coralline sand (beach deposit) extending to the maximum

2.4 
Groundwater 
Groundwater was encountered at a depth of about 4.75 feet below the existing pavement 
surface at the time of our field exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater level 
is subject to change due to tidal fluctuations, rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface 
water runoff, and other factors.  
2.5 
Underground Waterline 
The existing 12-inch-diameter ductile iron pipe waterline was encountered at about 
3.25 feet below the existing pavement surface and about 7 feet from the centerline of the road. 
The pipeline runs parallel to the makai edge of the road and is about 1 foot from the vertical 
projection of the upper edge of the stone wall. The sand was used for the pipe bedding material. 
The location of the pipe relative to the road is shown on Plate 3. 
2.6 
Rock Wall 
The test pit excavation indicates that the rock wall along the shoreline that lines the south 
facing slopes of the road is about 2.4 feet wide. The bottom of the rock wall generally extends 
down to a depth of about 5 feet below the existing pavement surface.  The back side of the rock 
wall generally slopes to the south at about the same inclination as the front side of the rock wall. 
Based on our observations, the rock wall material below the existing roadway consists of rounded 
basaltic cobbles and boulders.  The rocks appear to have been placed along the slopes to protect 
the road embankment against erosion.  A presumptive sketch of the section of the road and rock 
wall is shown on Plate 3.

LIMITATIONS 
The subsurface information submitted herein is based, in part, upon limited information 
obtained from the field test pit exploration and experience in the vicinity of the proposed project 
site. It should be noted that it is not possible to observe and/or anticipate all the site conditions

subsurface conditions from those described in this report may occur, and the nature and extent

exploration is conducted at the project site or construction is underway.

approximate boundaries between soil types and, as such, may denote a gradual transition. Water

variations in tides, rainfall, temperature, and other factors.

Test Pit Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the Kamehameha V Highway Realignment 
project site at MP 14.6 by excavating the test pit designated as Test Pit No. 1.  The test pit was 
measured 3 feet by 6 feet and extended to a depth of approximately 5 feet below the existing 
pavement surface. The test pit was excavated using a PC 35 excavator hand digging tool, water 
pressure jet and a vacuum truck. The excavation and sampling work was conducted on September 
18, 2023. The test pit location is shown on Site Plan, Plate 2. Soil samples were obtained from the 
test pit at the depths indicated on the test pit log at the appropriate sample depths. 
The test pit was backfilled with well-graded gravel from the bottom up to minus 6 inches 
below the existing pavement grade. The pavement was restored with two 3-inch lifts of 
“Aquaphalt” asphaltic paving material. 
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the test pit by visual and textural 
examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice for 
Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the test pit excavation and backfilling 
operations on a near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification 
System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend (Plate A-0.1). Deviations made to the soil classification 
in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil Classification Log Key (Plate A-0.2). 
Graphic representations of the materials encountered and other observations, such as 
groundwater level, etc., are presented on the Test Pit Log, Plate A-1. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on the cohesive soil layers at selected depths 
on the walls of the test pit. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Log of the test pit at the corresponding test depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Samples recovered from our field exploration program performed for the project were 
visually examined and classified in our laboratory in Waipahu, Hawaii. Selected samples were 
tested to evaluate the physical characteristics and engineering properties of the soils 
encountered. The laboratory testing program was formulated with an emphasis on the 
classification of the soils encountered. 
The laboratory tests performed included the determination of natural moisture content, 
Atterberg Limits, and grain size distribution. Results of the tests performed are presented on 
Plates B-1 and B-2. Detailed descriptions of the various tests performed for this exploration are 
presented below. 
Moisture Content 
Moisture content tests were performed on selected soil samples to aid in defining the 
interface between the various soil layers encountered. The moisture content tests were 
performed in general accordance with ASTM D2216. The test results are presented on the Log of 
the test pit at the appropriate sample depths. 
Atterberg Limits 
To evaluate the liquid and plastic limits of the soil encountered, three Atterberg Limits 
tests were performed in accordance with ASTM D4318. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Test Pit at the appropriate sample depths, and a graphic presentation of the test results 
is provided on Plate B-1. 
Sieve Analysis 
To provide information on the particle size distribution of the soils encountered, four 
Sieve Analysis tests were performed to evaluate the gradation characteristics and to aid in soil 
classification. The Sieve Analysis tests were performed in accordance with ASTM C117 and C136. 
Graphic presentations of the test results are provided on Plate B-2.
