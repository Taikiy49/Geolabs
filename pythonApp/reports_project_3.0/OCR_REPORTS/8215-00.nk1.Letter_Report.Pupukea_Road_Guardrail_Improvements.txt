Project Considerations 
The project site is located along Pupukea Road on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  We 
understand that improvements to the guardrail on the makai side of the roadway is 
desired. Pupukea Road is a two-lane roadway with one lane of traffic in each direction. 
Based on the information provided, we understand that there is an existing steep 
slope section directly adjacent to the existing guardrail on the downslope side of Pupukea 
Road. We understand that a new retaining wall system is desired within this section. The 
new guardrail will be installed near the top-of-slope, adjacent to the existing roadway.

Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was formed by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The younger 
Koolau shield is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  Waianae became extinct while 
Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas 
banking against its eastern flank. The project site is generally located at the northern flank 
of Koolau. 
Koolau was built during the late Pliocene Epoch and early Pleistocene Epoch by 
the accumulation of thinly-bedded basaltic lava flows extruded from vents along a fissure 
line over 30 miles in length, which roughly paralleled the trend of what is now the Koolau 
Range. The main shield building activity ended in the early Pleistocene Epoch, 
approximately 2.5 million years ago. In-situ chemical weathering of the Koolau lava flows 
has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years. The weathering process has formed a mantle 
of residual and saprolitic soils. In general, saprolite is composed mainly of silty material 
and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks. The saprolite grades to basaltic 
rock formation with increased depth. 
Existing Site Conditions 
The guardrail improvement project is located at a hairpin turn along the downslope 
side of Pupukea Road in Pupukea on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The proposed 
improvements are located above the Pupukea Foodland. The general project location is 
shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.  
At the time of our field exploration, the asphaltic concrete (AC) pavement within 
the project limits appeared to be in fair to good condition. The existing guardrail was noted 
leaning outward away from the road. Undermining of the guardrail posts were observed. 
A dry-stack basalt boulder retaining wall was observed supporting the downslope side of 
Pupukea Road. Based on our field observations, the wall face inclination appeared to 
range from near-vertical to about one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V).  
The near-vertical sections of the dry-stack wall appeared largely intact. However, 
signs of erosion at the guardrail posts were observed. Portions of the sloping dry-stack 
wall were severely deteriorated; most of the cobbles and boulders had migrated 
downslope due to erosion, and large sections of soil slope were exposed.  
Based on the available topographic information, the existing roadway elevation 
ranges from about +126 to +138 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) within the project limits. The 
height of the slope below Pupukea Road ranges from about 2 to 15 feet.

Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to a depth of about 21.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. In addition, one bulk sample of the near-surface soils, 
designated as Bulk-1, was obtained to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the near-surface soils. The approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on 
the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our borings generally encountered a surface fill layer about 4.5 to 11.5 feet thick 
underlain by residual soil and basalt rock formation extending to the maximum depth 
explored of about 21.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. 
The fill material generally consisted of 7.5 to 8 inches of AC, medium dense to very 
dense silty/sandy gravel and stiff to very stiff sandy silt. The basalt rock formation was 
moderately fractured, slightly weathered, and hard. The residual soil generally consisted 
of very stiff to hard silty clay with some cobbles.  
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change 
due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration methodology and the Logs of Borings 
are presented in Appendix A. Results of the laboratory tests performed on selected soil 
samples are presented in Appendix B. Photographs of the core samples retrieved from 
our borings are presented in Appendix C.

Wall Preparation 
At the on-set of construction, surface vegetation, loose material, and deleterious 
material should be removed from the rock wall face and adjacent areas. The existing 
dry-stack boulders should not be removed from the wall face to avoid causing slope 
instability. Care should be taken in removing material so that the stability of the existing 
wall and slope is not compromised.  
Soil Nailed Wall 
A soil-nailed retaining wall system consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars 
grouted into drilled holes to stabilize an excavation or slope. A shotcrete facing is 
generally applied to the face of the wall or slope providing the appearance of a 
conventional concrete retaining wall. To provide a natural rock appearance, the shotcrete 
facing may be sculpted and stained to match the appearance of the surrounding 
environment. The reinforcing bars provide additional tensile and shear strength to the 
wall. The reinforced soil mass behaves in a manner similar to a gravity retaining wall but 
lacks a footing. 
The soil nails should be installed by drilling a minimum 5.5-inch diameter hole with 
an inclination of approximately 15 degrees from horizontal. The inclination of the nails 
may be increased to avoid existing underground structures. The soil nail bar should 
consist of an ASTM A615 Grade 75 threaded bar with a minimum bar diameter of 1 inch. 
Due to the proximity of the soil-nailed wall to the Pacific Ocean, we recommend using a 
double corrosion protection system for the nails consisting of epoxy-coated bars 
surrounded by neat cement grout or sand-cement mixture with a minimum 28-day 
compressive strength of 4,000 pounds per square inch (psi). 
Based on our soil nail analysis, we recommend using design nail lengths of 10 feet 
extending into the fill, residual soil, and basalt formation encountered in our field 
exploration. The soil nails should be spaced 4 feet on-center vertically and 6 feet 
on-center horizontally. The first nail should be installed about 2 feet below the top of the 
slope. 
Based on the above design parameters, the soil-nailed retaining wall system 
should have a factor of safety of 1.5 or greater for the combined internal and external 
stability for the static loading condition considering a surcharge load of 250 pounds per 
square foot (psf) on the roadway travel lanes. In addition, we understand that the new 
retaining wall system will need to withstand a 13.5 kip impact load caused by vehicular 
traffic impacting the guard rail. Therefore, the soil nails were designed such that two 
adjacent nails near the top of the slope could resist the 13.5 kip traffic impact load.

Soil Nail Installation 
To facilitate the installation of the soil nails through the existing dry-stack retaining 
wall, a shotcrete flash coat should first be applied to the face of the dry-stack wall. Soil 
nail installation activities should avoid causing instability to the existing wall.  
Potentially difficult drilling conditions may be encountered during the installation of 
the soil nails due to the presence of the dry-stack retaining wall, cobbles in the residual 
soil, and basalt rock formation. Utilizing a temporary casing may be required to maintain 
an open hole for the soil nail installation. 
Soil Nail Testing 
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated behind the soil-nailed retaining 
wall, we recommend performing a minimum of one pre-production verification test on a 
sacrificial soil nail installed near the top of the slope on the southern end of the existing 
near-vertical dry-stack retaining wall. In addition, proof tests should be performed on at 
least 10 percent of the production soil nails during construction to confirm the bond 
stresses used in the design. 
The verification test should be loaded gradually to 150 percent of the design load 
(14 kips) for the creep test and subsequently to the maximum test load of about 
200 percent of the design load. In addition, the load at every loading increment should be 
held for at least 10 minutes, and creep tests should be held for at least 60 minutes. The 
proof tests should consist of subjecting the soil nail to at least 150 percent of the design 
loads and the load should be held for at least 10 minutes (until stable). The proof test 
nails may be incorporated into the permanent soil nail retaining wall provided they satisfy 
the test criteria. Pullout tests and creep tests on the soil nails are integral components of 
the design for the soil nail retaining wall system. Therefore, we recommend conducting 
pullout tests and creep tests in the presence of a Geolabs representative. 
Shotcrete Facing 
Shotcrete placement should be performed by an experienced nozzleman. Prior to 
production shotcreting, it is recommended that unreinforced test panels of shotcrete be 
constructed for inspection. We understand that the shotcrete facing may be sculptured. 
Therefore, adequate thickness of the final facing should be provided to accommodate the 
sculptured surfacing. 
Drainage 
The soil-nailed retaining wall should be well-drained to reduce the potential for the 
build-up of hydrostatic pressures. Drainage elements consisting of 2-foot wide strips of a 
prefabricated drainage composite product may be used on the face of the slope before 
application of a shotcrete flash coat used for temporary stabilization. The prefabricated 
drainage composite product should extend from the top of the slope to the base of the 
slope and hydraulically connected to a weep hole near the base of the wall.

Pavement Design 
We envision that pavement restoration may be required for the project, and that 
flexible AC pavement will be used to match the existing roadway. Traffic volume 
information was not available for Pupukea Road at the time this report was prepared. We 
understand that Pupukea Road does not experience bus traffic and that the roadway is 
primarily used to access residences.  
Based on the “Structural Design Requirements for New Asphalt Concrete 
Pavements” issued under the Engineering and Policy Memorandum No. CEB-1-06 by the 
City and County of Honolulu, pavements that are anticipated to support relatively few 
loads (less than 1 million equivalent single axle load, ESALs) over their design life and 
contain no regular bus routes are generally considered “low-volume” pavements. 
Conversely, pavements that do not meet the low-volume criteria in the memorandum are 
generally considered “high-volume” pavements supporting an appreciable amount of 
loading. Based on our understanding of Pupukea Road’s traffic demand, we believe the 
roadway may be considered a “low-volume” pavement based on the above-referenced 
memorandum.  
Based on our field exploration, the pavement subgrades beneath the existing road 
are generally underlain by medium dense to dense silty gravel. Based on the laboratory 
CBR test results, a design CBR value of 50 was used in our pavement analyses for the 
anticipated subgrade conditions along the proposed roadway alignments. 
Based on the subsurface materials encountered and the above assumptions, our 
preliminary pavement section recommendation is presented below. 
Flexible Pavement Section

  3.0-Inch Asphaltic Concrete Base

11.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade 
The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and recompacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place 
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same 
soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content 
(percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.  
The aggregate base course should meet the requirements stipulated in Section 31 
of the Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction, City and County of Honolulu 
(September 1986). Aggregate base course should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, 
and compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction. California Bearing Ratio

Limitations 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from our field borings and bulk sample. Variations of subsoil 
conditions between and beyond the field borings may occur, and the nature and extent of 
these variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then 
appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations provided herein. 
The field boring locations indicated in this report were located using a handheld 
GPS device. The elevations of the borings were estimated by using the spot elevations 
and contours shown on the Topographic Survey Map received from KSF, Inc. on 
March 23, 2021. The locations and elevations of the field borings should be considered 
accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification breaks shown on the graphic representations of the borings 
depict the approximate boundaries between soil types and, as such, may denote a 
gradual transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown 
on the graphic representations and/or presented in the text of this report. This data has 
been reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. However, it 
should be noted that groundwater levels can fluctuate depending on surface water runoff, 
storm surge conditions, seasonal precipitation, perched groundwater, and other factors.
