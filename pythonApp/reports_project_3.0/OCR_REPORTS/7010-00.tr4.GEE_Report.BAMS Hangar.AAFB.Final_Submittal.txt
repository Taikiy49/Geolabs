SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the subsurface conditions at the site generally 
consist of medium dense to very dense sand and gravel fills underlain by limestone 
(rock formation) extending to the maximum depths explored. Groundwater was not 
encountered in our borings at the time of our field exploration and is not anticipated to 
be encountered in the relatively shallow excavations for this project. 
Based on the generally competent subsurface conditions encountered at the 
hangar location, we believe a shallow foundation system consisting of spread footings 
and/or continuous strip footings bearing on the limestone formation may be used to 
support the hangar structure and associated structural components. Design of the 
shallow foundations bearing on the limestone formation may be based on an allowable 
bearing pressure of up to 300 kPa for supporting dead-plus-live loads. The allowable 
bearing pressure may be increased to 450 kPa when considering transient loads, such 
as those caused by wind or seismic forces. When using these higher bearing pressures 
for design of the footings, we recommend using a greater embedment depth of 
1.2 meters below the lowest adjacent grade.  
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the other structure locations 
for the project, we believe the existing fills, new fills, or the limestone formation 
anticipated at the foundation subgrade level would provide suitable foundation support 
for the other structures, such as the fire pump house, water tanks, retaining wall, etc. 
Therefore, we recommend using a shallow foundation system consisting of spread 
footings and/or continuous strip footings to support the other structures. An allowable 
bearing pressure of up to 200 kPa may be used for design of spread and/or continuous 
strip footings bearing on compacted fill or limestone formation. This bearing value is for 
supporting dead-plus-live loads and may be increased to 300 kPa for transient loads, 
such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. In general, footings designed with the 
lower allowable bearing pressure should be embedded a minimum of 500 mm below the 
lowest adjacent finished grade. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL

performed for the BAMS Forward Operational and Maintenance Hangar project within 
Andersen Air Force Base on the Island of Guam. The project location and general 
vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations derived 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses. The 
recommendations presented herein are intended for design of foundations, slabs-on-
grades, pavements, earthwork requirements, retaining structures, and underground 
utilities only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the 
limitations noted at the end of this report.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
The Island of Guam is the largest and southernmost of the Mariana Island 
archipelago, which is an island arc parallel to the subduction zone forming the Mariana 
Trench. The Mariana Trench is part of a belt of trenches extending from Japan to the 
Palau Islands that also includes the Japan Trench, Izu-Bonin Trench, Yap Trench, and 
Palau Trench. 
The Island of Guam can be broadly divided into three geologic provinces: 
(1) the southern mountains; (2) the central mountains; and (3) the northern plateau. The 
island formation started during the Eocene Epoch by the eruption of basaltic rhyolite 
lavas. The mountainous central portion of the island is composed of these Eocene 
volcanic rocks and conglomerates, tuffs, and limestones of the Eocene and Oligocene 
Epochs. 
Lava flows and limestone formations of the Miocene Epoch make up the majority 
of the southern one-third of the island. The plateaus of the northern portion of the island 
were formed while this part of the island was still beneath the sea level. During the 
Oligocene and Miocene Epochs, a complex series of coral and algal reefs formed an 
elongated platform of limestones and related deposits. Tectonic activity has lifted these 
coral-algal limestones above sea level to form the northern Guam Plateau. The central 
portion of the island consists of the transition from the volcanic materials of the southern 
mountains to the limestone formations of the northern plateau. The project site is 
situated in a relatively level portion of the northern plateau, which forms the main portion 
of Andersen Air Force Base.  
The local geology is dominated by the limestone plateau. Weathering and 
solution working of the limestone has partially re-crystallized the calcium carbonate of 
the limestone forming massive limestones in some areas and poorly to well-cemented 
detrital limestones in other areas. Typically, the detrital limestones are highly variable in 
strength characteristics and are karstic or cavernous in nature. In addition, the 
limestone formations are generally porous. Except during heavy storms, the majority of

is described as being very shallow and well drained. Urban land consists of areas 
covered with roads, building, parking lots, and air strips.  
2.1 
Site Description 
As indicated previously, the project site is within Andersen Air Force Base on the 
Island of Guam. The site is bounded by Taxilane ‘B’ to the north, Hangar 4 to the west, 
Hangar 6 to the east, and the flightline wall to the south. In general, the project site 
slopes downward to the west-northwest with existing ground surface elevations ranging 
from about +173 to +179 meters relative to Mean Sea Level (MSL). All elevations in this 
report are referenced to MSL, unless otherwise indicated. The location of the site is 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The site is dominated by the existing Hangar 5, which is to be deconstructed and 
replaced with the BAMS hangar. Hangar 5 sits roughly in the middle of the site. 
Immediately adjacent and to the east of Hangar 5 is a retention basin that is 
approximately 50 meters long, 15 meters wide, and 1 to 1.5 meters deep, relative to the 
surrounding ground surface. During our field exploration water was observed ponding 
on the northern portion of the retention basin. A Portland Cement Concrete (PCC) apron 
connected Hangar 5 with Taxilane ‘B.’ While older, the PCC is in relatively good

2.2 
Subsurface Conditions 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling thirteen borings 
(designated as Boring Nos. B-101 through B-113) extending to maximum depths of 
about 13.3 meters below the existing ground surface. In addition, two deep borings 
(19.8 meters deep) also were drilled to conduct the infiltration tests in support of the 
injection well design. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. 
The subsurface conditions at the site generally consist of fill materials that extend 
to depths of about 0.3 to 1.4 meters below the existing ground surface in the borings. 
The fill materials generally consist of medium dense to very dense sands and gravel 
with varying amounts of silt. The fill materials are underlain by limestone (rock 
formation) that extends to the maximum depths (19.8 meters) explored in our borings.  
Groundwater was not encountered at the time of our field exploration and is not 
anticipated to be encountered within the relatively shallow excavations for this project. It 
should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, time of 
year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of 
Borings in Appendix A. Laboratory tests were performed on selected samples obtained 
during our field exploration, and the test results are summarized on the Logs of Borings 
and are presented in Appendix B. Selected boring logs (relevant to our project) from the 
borings contained in the Request For Proposal (RFP) documents are included in 
Appendix C for ease of reference.

2.3.1 
Earthquakes and Seismicity 
Guam is situated adjacent to the subduction zone that forms the Marianas 
Trench. The island is tectonically active and has experienced approximately 
20 earthquakes exceeding Magnitude 7.0 since 1902. Of these events, fifteen 
occurred prior to 1950 and five have occurred subsequent to that date. Several 
large faults and fault zones that traverse the Island of Guam include the 
following:  
1. 
Pago Bay - Adelup Point Fault 
2. 
Santa Rita - Talofofo River Valley Fault Zone 
3. 
Tamuning - Barrigada Fault 
4. 
Pugua Fault  
5. 
Tarague Fault  
6. 
Mount Santa Rosa horst block 
The proximity of the project site to faults and fault zones is a major consideration 
when evaluating geologic and seismic hazards. Of the major faults and fault 
zones mentioned above, the trace of the Tarague Fault lies closest at roughly 
4 kilometers to the west of the project site. 
Based on our evaluation of the project site, we do not believe that significant 
geologic and seismic hazards would impact the proposed BAMS Hangar project. 
With the exception of moderate to strong ground shaking, we believe the 
potential for surface fault rupture, seismically induced flooding, liquefaction, and 
lateral spreading are considered to be low at this project site.  
2.3.2 
Soil Profile 
Based on the subsurface materials encountered at the project site and the 
geologic setting of the area, we anticipate the project site may be classified from 
a seismic analysis standpoint as a “Very Dense Soil and Soft Rock Profile.” 
Therefore, we believe the seismic design of the building structures may be 
designed based on a Site Class C soil profile based on the International Building 
Code, 2012 Edition.

3.1.4 
Permanent Cut Slopes 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings, cut slopes 
generally should expose some fills at the ground surface, weathered limestone, 
and limestone formation at the cut faces. Cut slopes may be excavated at a 
standard slope inclination of 2H:1V (two horizontal to one vertical) or flatter.

of the slope. Geolabs should be consulted to evaluate the specific cut slope 
location where the steeper cut slope inclination is desired. 
3.2 
Hangar Foundations 
Based on the generally competent subsurface conditions encountered at the 
hangar location, we believe a shallow foundation system consisting of spread footings 
and/or continuous strip footings bearing on the limestone formation encountered at 
shallow depths may be used to support the hangar structure and associated structural 
components. Due to the long span nature of the hangar structure, large compression 
and thrust loads will be generated from the structure to be resisted at the foundation 
level. 
Design of the shallow foundations bearing on the limestone formation may be 
based on an allowable bearing pressure of up to 300 kPa for supporting dead-plus-live

3.8 
Underground Utilities 
Generally, the excavated soils from the trench excavation for underground 
utilities should be stockpiled no closer than a horizontal distance equal to the depth of 
the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential for appreciable 
ground movement toward the open trench. The trench excavation should comply with 
the applicable local, territorial, and federal safety requirements. 
In general, we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 150 mm of 
open-graded gravel (such as ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) under the pipes to support 
the utility lines. Free-draining granular materials, such as open-graded gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), also should be used for the initial trench backfill up to 
about 300 mm above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes. It is 
critical to use the free-draining materials around the pipes to reduce the potential for 
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support around 
the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill around the pipe could result in backfill 
settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 300 mm above the pipes to 
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on-site soils 
having particles less than 75 mm in largest dimension. The backfill should be 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in about 200 mm loose 
lifts, and mechanically compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
Where trenches are located in paved areas, the upper 1 meter of the trench backfill 
below the pavement surface should conform to the recommendations in the “Site 
Grading” section herein.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with 
ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. Mechanical compaction equipment should 
be used to compact the backfill materials. Compaction efforts by water tamping, jetting, 
or ponding should not be allowed.

3.10 Corrosion Potential and Chemical Attack to Concrete 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil 
characteristics with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. 
Generally, the lower the resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried 
metallic structure. Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will 
contribute to corrosion to metallic objects. Based on our experience in the area, the 
subsurface materials may be classified as mildly to moderately corrosive for buried 
metallic structures. A corrosion engineer should be consulted for detailed 
recommendations on corrosion protection of metallic substructures, if appropriate. 
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent on the pH and the chloride and sulfate contents found in the 
soil. In general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm), 
sulfate content of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH of greater than 5.0 may be considered 
“non-corrosive” to underground concrete pipelines and structures. Based on our 
experience in the area, the in-situ soils may be considered “non-corrosive” to

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling 13 borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. B-101 through B-113, extending to maximum depths of 
about 13.3 meters below the existing ground surface. In addition, two deep borings 
(designated as P-101 and P-102) also were drilled up to 19.8 meters deep to conduct 
the infiltration tests in support of the injection well design. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend (Plate A-0.1). 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. 
Rock materials were described in general accordance with the Rock Description 
System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System 
is based on the publication “Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of 
Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International Society for Rock Mechanics 
(March 1977). Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on 
the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 thru A-15. 
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 51-mm 
(2-inch) OD standard penetration sampler using a 63.5-kg (140-pound) hammer falling 
460 mm (30 inches). In addition, some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were 
obtained in general accordance with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, 
by driving a 76-mm (3-inch) OD modified California sampler with the same hammer and 
drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 152 mm 
(6 inches) of a 460-mm (18-inch) drive are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on 
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration resistance 
shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows required for the specific 
sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the Standard 
Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Please note that the efficiency of the safety hammer used for this project was 
measured recently. Hammer calibration tests were performed on the Mobile B-61 
automatic trip safety hammer in March 2014 for another project. The energy data was 
analyzed, and the results indicate an energy transfer ratio of 88% (average of 87.1% 
and 89.2%) may be used for the automatic trip safety hammer assembly.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil and limestone properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at 
the appropriate sample depths.  
One Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D4318) was performed on a select soil sample 
to evaluate its liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth, and graphic 
presentation of the test results is provided on Plates B-1. 
Three Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected 
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of near-surface soils and to aid in 
soil classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on 
Plate B-2. 
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557) were performed on the 
bulk soil samples to evaluate the relationship between the moisture content and the dry 
density of the near-surface soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-3 and B-4. 
Two laboratory soaked California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were 
performed on bulk soil samples to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the 
soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-5 and B-6.
