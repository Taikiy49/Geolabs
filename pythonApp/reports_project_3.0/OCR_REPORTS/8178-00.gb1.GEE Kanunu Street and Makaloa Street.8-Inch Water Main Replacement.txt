SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the borings drilled at the site, the subsurface conditions consisted of about 
6 inches of asphaltic concrete underlain by loose and very dense silty sand fill extending to 
depths of about 1 and 4.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  The silty sand fill was 
underlain by soft to very stiff silty clay and clayey silt fill extending to depths of about 4.5 to 
8 feet below the existing ground surface.  Below the fills, the borings encountered lagoonal 
deposits consisting of very soft to stiff clayey silt and very loose to medium dense silty sand 
and gravel extending to depths of about 28 and 32 feet below the existing ground surface.  
A thin layer of coral about 2 to 3-foot thick was encountered below the lagoonal deposits.  
Coralline detritus consisting of medium dense to very dense silty sand and gravel was 
encountered below the coral layer and extended to the maximum depth explored of about 
41.5 feet below the existing ground surface. We encountered groundwater in the borings at 
the time of our field exploration at depths varying from about 5 to 7 feet below the existing 
ground surface. 
We understand that most of the new water mains will be installed using 
conventional open trench method. For pipes bearing on the on-site soils, a minimum of 
12 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel should be provided below the pipe. Where soft and/or 
loose soils are encountered at or near the invert of the pipes, such as the loose and/or 
soft lagoonal deposits, a stabilization layer consisting of 24 inches of No. 2 coarse 
aggregate wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) should be 
provided below the bedding layer for uniform support. 
The initial backfill extending from the bedding material to at least 12 inches above 
the top of the pipes (or groundwater level) should consist of No. 3B Fine gravel.  If 
groundwater is encountered, the bedding and initial trench backfill should be wrapped in a 
non-woven geotextile fabric such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent.  
The trench backfill from 12 inches above the top of the pipes (or groundwater level) 
to the finished subgrade may consist of the excavated on-site soils provided that they are 
free of deleterious materials and particles greater than 6 inches in the largest dimension.  
Select borrow subbase or Controlled Low Strength Material may also be considered for use 
as trench backfill.  The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and 
specific geotechnical recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for Kanunu Street and Makaloa Street, 8-Inch Water Main Replacement 
project located in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and 
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project. 
These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of water 
main installation using the open trench method and pavement and concrete sidewalk 
restoration only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the 
limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project involves the installation of approximately 840 linear feet of 8-inch water 
main along Kanunu Street, from Keeaumoku Street to Kaheka Street, and approximately 
1,520 linear feet of 8-inch water main along Makaloa Street between Keeaumoku Street and 
Poni Street.  In addition, approximately 144 lineal feet of water main will be installed on 
Amana Street and Keeaumoku Street. 
Based on the information provided, invert elevations for the new water mains will 
generally range from about 4 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface.  Inverts as deep 
as 12 feet below the existing ground surface are planned in areas where the water mains 
would need to extend below existing utilities.   
We understand that trenchless methods are being considered for the water mains 
crossing below an existing 3-foot by 7-foot double box culvert at the intersection of Makaloa 
Street and Kaheka Street.  The feasibility of installing the water main using trenchless 
methods will be discussed in a separate report.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late 
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which 
time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a 
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are referred 
to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
The project site is located on the southern flank of Koolau Volcano, and its 
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to the glacio-eustatic 
fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu 
Coastal Plain. The subsurface conditions in the Ala Moana area are dominated by fossil 
Pleistocene Age coral/algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of 
the sea that are intercalated with terrigenous sediments. The base of the stratigraphic 
section in this area is Koolau Basalt. 
During the later Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred 
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. These 
glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower 
relative to the present sea level on the Island of Oahu. About 15,000 years ago, a 
relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred. During that sea level rise, the valleys in the 
project area were drowned. In the last 10,000 years or so, the sea level has adjusted to 
its present stand. Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued to accumulate in 
low-energy estuarine or lagoonal environments, resulting in thick deposits of soft and/or 
loose sediments along the coast in the areas that were formerly valleys and 
drainageways. 
In the early part of this century, the Ala Moana area consisted of low, marshy 
wetlands. As the City of Honolulu grew and the Ala Moana area was urbanized, 
man-made fills were placed to reclaim the marshy areas and lagoons for development.

Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is located along Kanunu Street between Keeaumoku Street and 
Kaheka Street and along Makaloa Street between Keeaumoku Street and Poni Street in 
Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  Kanunu Street and Makaloa Street are two-lane 
roadways with parking lanes on both sides of the roadways.  The roadways are paved 
with asphaltic concrete, which appears to be in relatively fair condition. 
The existing ground surface elevations along the centerline of the water main on 
Kanunu Street range from about +6 feet to +6.7 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).  The existing 
ground surface elevations along the centerline of the water main on Makaloa Street range 
from about +4 to +7 feet MSL.  An existing 3-foot by 7-foot double box culvert generally 
runs along the middle of Kaheka Street.  The new water main will cross below this 
double-box culvert at the intersection of Makaloa Street and Kaheka Street.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the borings drilled at the site, the subsurface conditions consisted of 
about 6 inches of asphaltic concrete underlain by fills extending to depths of about 4.5 to 
8 feet below the existing ground surface.  Below the fills, the borings encountered very 
soft and very loose lagoonal deposits extending to depths of about 28 to 32 feet below 
the existing ground surface.  A thin layer of coral about 2 to 3-foot thick was encountered 
below the lagoonal deposits.  Coralline detritus consisting of medium dense to very dense 
silty sand and gravel was encountered below the coral layer and extended to the 
maximum depth explored of about 41.5 feet below the existing ground surface. We 
encountered groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration at depths 
varying from approximately 5 to 7 feet below the existing ground surface. 
We understand that most of the new water mains will be installed using the 
conventional open trench (cut-and-cover) method. We understand the invert depths of 
the new water mains will range from about 4 to 12 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Since the water main alignments are located within the existing roadways, we believe that 
temporary cut slopes for open-cut excavations are not practical. Therefore, it appears the 
trench walls would have to be cut near vertical, necessitating the use of shoring during 
construction.   
Some of the water main excavations may extend below the groundwater level; 
therefore, dewatering will be necessary in these areas. Because the project is in a 
developed area, dewatering should be conducted in such a manner that the dewatering 
will not cause areal ground subsidence, which may cause potential damage to the existing 
nearby structures and underground utilities. The contractor should consider a dewatering 
system that includes a cut-off wall and tremie concrete plug.  
For the design of thrust blocks, we recommend using a lateral bearing pressure of 
up to 1,000 pounds per square foot (psf) for thrust blocks located above elevation +1 foot 
MSL.  For thrust blocks located below elevation +1 foot MSL, the lateral bearing pressure 
should be reduced to 500 psf.  Detailed discussions and recommendations for these items 
and other geotechnical aspects of the project are presented in the following sections.

3.1 
Open Trench (Cut-and-Cover) Method 
We understand that most of the new water mains will be installed using the 
conventional open trench (cut-and-cover) method. The portion of the water main crossing 
the existing double-box culvert at the intersection of Makaloa Street and Kaheka Street will 
be installed below the double-box culvert using trenchless methods.  We understand the 
invert depths of the new water mains range from about 4 to 12 feet below the existing ground 
surface. 
 Earth Pressure Loads on Pipes 
Loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the trench, the size of the pipes, 
the unit weight of the backfill material, and the friction resistance between the backfill 
material and the trench walls. To calculate the vertical loads on the buried utility 
pipes, we recommend that an average unit weight of 135 pounds per cubic foot (pcf) 
for the backfill material and a coefficient of friction of 0.30 be used. Earth forces acting 
upon the pipe generally increase rapidly with the width of the trench. Therefore, the 
width of the trench should be kept to a minimum. Traffic loads on the buried pipes 
should also be considered for the portion of the pipes located in roadway areas. 
 Trench Excavation 
As mentioned above, we understand the inverts for the new water mains will 
generally be on the order of about 4 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface with 
some water main inverts extending down to about 12 feet below the existing ground 
surface. Based on our borings, trench excavations will likely encounter fills consisting 
of loose to very dense silty sand and soft to very stiff silty clay/clayey silt above the 
groundwater level.  Excavations below the groundwater level will encounter lagoonal 
deposits consisting of very soft clayey silt and loose to medium dense silty sand and 
gravel.  It is anticipated that most of the material may be excavated with normal heavy 
excavation equipment.

It is important to install adequate shoring and to maintain it tight against the 
excavation walls with proper bracing during construction. The properly braced 
shoring is essential to reduce the potential for appreciable lateral movements of the 
adjacent ground into the excavation, which may result in potential settlement or 
distress to adjacent structures or other improvements. 
It must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and the adjacent 
ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation. Due to 
the complexity of the stress changes, it is difficult to accurately estimate the 
magnitude of movement. The magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship, 
such as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are installed, the 
subsurface conditions, the size of the excavation, and the rate of excavation. 
Therefore, it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed

 Pipe Bedding 
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the load 
supporting capacity of the pipe. Therefore, the pipe bedding is an important design 
consideration. For pipes bearing on the on-site soils, a minimum of 12 inches of No. 
3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) should be provided below the pipe. 
Where groundwater is encountered, the bedding should be wrapped on all sides by 
non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent). Where soft and/or loose soils are 
encountered at or near the invert of the pipes, a stabilization layer should be installed 
below the pipe bedding.  The stabilization layer should consist of 24 inches of open-
graded No. 2 coarse aggregate (ASTM C33 No. 4 gradation) wrapped in a 
non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent), as shown on Plate 3. 
The granular bedding should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width of 
one-fourth (1/4) of the outside pipe diameter. Before the placement of bedding 
material, a Geolabs representative should observe the excavated trench bottom to 
confirm that firm materials are exposed at the bottom of the trench or whether the 
installation of a stabilization layer is needed.  
 Trench Backfill 
In general, the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the Water 
System Standards, State of Hawaii, 2002. The initial backfill extending from the 
bedding material to at least 12 inches above the top of the pipes (or groundwater 
level) should consist of No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation).  If 
groundwater is encountered, the initial trench backfill should be wrapped in a non-
woven geotextile fabric such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent. 
The trench backfill from 12 inches above the top of the pipes (or groundwater level) 
to the finished subgrade may consist of the excavated on-site soils provided that 
they are free of deleterious materials (vegetation) and are screened of particles

 Thrust Blocks 
We envision that thrust blocks will be required at some locations to provide 
resistance to the thrust forces due to the pressurized pipes. The thrust force acting 
on the pipes may be resisted by passive earth pressure acting against the face of 
the thrust block.  
Based on the available information, the planned inverts are about 4 to 12 feet 
below the existing ground surface. As mentioned above, we anticipate most of the 
water main inverts will likely be in the existing fills. However, some of the water 
main inverts will need to extend into the lagoonal deposits consisting of soft silt/clay 
and loose sand.  Based on the subsurface conditions encountered along the water 
main alignments, we recommend using a lateral bearing pressure of up to 1,000 
pounds per square foot (psf) for the design of thrust blocks located above elevation 
+1 foot MSL.  For thrust blocks located below Elevation +1 foot MSL, the lateral 
bearing pressure should be reduced to 500 psf.

Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges, traffic loads, line loads, and point loads 
within a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the wall should be considered in the 
design. For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the wall, a 
rectangular distribution with a uniform pressure equal to 53 percent of the vertical 
surcharge pressure acting on the entire height of the wall should be used. Additional 
analyses during design may be needed to evaluate the surcharge effects of point 
loads and line loads.

 Settlements 
The primary settlement of the new water main is normally caused by the difference 
in the unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted 
backfill material placed over the pipes. The net increase in loading will cause the 
settlement of the underlying subsoils below the trench invert. Based on our 
calculations, primary settlement on the order of less than 0.5 inches is anticipated 
for the project. 
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good 
workmanship will be engaged during construction. Additional settlement could 
occur if improper trench support is used. 
3.2 
Dewatering 
Dewatering of excavations will be necessary where the existing groundwater level is 
above the water main invert. We encountered groundwater in our drilled borings at depths 
of about 5 to 7 feet below the ground surface at the time of our field exploration. The 
groundwater levels encountered generally correspond to about Elevations -0.6 to 
+1.2 feet MSL. 
Based on the groundwater levels anticipated and the planned water main inverts, 
dewatering will be required along portions of the water main alignments. Therefore, 
dewatering provisions will need to be included in the contract documents for the proposed 
construction. Because the excavation may involve the discharge of groundwater, a National 
Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) permit will likely be required for this 
discharge. The contractor should consult their independent consultant for the latest 
regulations and information pertaining to the permit application. 
Due to the presence of loose and/or soft soils, we do not recommend dewatering by 
means of a well point system along the outside of the excavations. The resultant areal 
depression of the natural groundwater table could induce consolidation of the compressible 
subsurface soils, resulting in potential ground settlements, which could affect the adjacent 
existing structures. The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing

Dewatering for construction is the contractor’s responsibility. The selection of 
equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor, and he/she 
should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during 
construction depending on the conditions encountered. The dewatering method

We suggest considering the following three basic criteria in the selection of a suitable 
method of dewatering: 
a. 
The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or

b. 
The dewatering method should maintain the stability of, and also

c. 
The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow 
modifications to accommodate various ground conditions. 
 Dewatering Precaution and Monitoring 
The contractor must carefully evaluate the potential impact of the dewatering system 
selected on depressing the natural groundwater table prior to dewatering. The 
contractor should retain a qualified geotechnical engineer to design and evaluate the 
dewatering system used. 
The contractor should be solely responsible for the impact and safety of the 
dewatering operations. His/her qualified representative, who should be continuously 
present on-site during dewatering activities, will have the best opportunity to promptly 
observe the effects of dewatering during construction and to implement, as soon as 
possible, necessary precautionary or remedial measures including, but not limited to, 
slowing down or stopping the dewatering operations. 
Where encountered at the bottom of excavations, permeable granular soils may be 
susceptible to piping and “quick” conditions. The dewatering operations should be 
carried-out without creating a “quick” condition or softening at the excavation 
bottoms. Therefore, the project dewatering operations should be performed without 
pumping out soil fines (pumping clear water only) and should be coordinated with the

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
seven borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 7, extending to depths ranging from 
about 11.5 to 41.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations 
are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 through 2.3. The borings were drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools.  
Our geologists classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-7. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required 
for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on 
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the

Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. 
For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any 
drilling, mechanical, and handling-induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used 
to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical 
Properties of Rocks and Minerals” by Robert S. Carmichael (1989).
