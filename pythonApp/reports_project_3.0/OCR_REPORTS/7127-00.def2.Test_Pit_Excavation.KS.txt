PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
Based on the information provided, there are three undocumented fill sites 
encompassed by the study. The three fill sites are designated as HDOH Area Nos. 1, 2, 
and 3. The three sites are on the down slope (south) side of Reservoir Service Road in the 
upper portion of the Kamehameha Schools Kapalama Campus. The Reservoir Service Road 
is an asphalt paved road accessed by Waonahele Road, which provides vehicle access to 
two existing reservoir tank sites.  The project location is shown on the attached Plate 1.  
HDOH Area Nos. 1 and 2 are adjacent to fill sites situated west of the existing upper 
reservoir tank site. HDOH Area No. 3 is situated near the end of Reservoir Service Road and 
appears to be much smaller than HDOH Area Nos. 1 and 2. 
We understand that the three fill sites contain undocumented soil and rock materials 
as well as construction debris and some landscape waste. The fills were not placed under 
controlled and engineered conditions. Through this exploration, it is desired to evaluate the 
long term stability of the fills and possible mitigation measures that may be taken to reduce 
future erosion and slide movements.

For this exploration, test pits, or trenches, were excavated by an environmental 
consultant, EA Engineering, Science and Technology, Inc. (EA), to evaluate the contents and 
condition of the fills from an environmental perspective. As geotechnical engineering 
consultants, Geolabs Inc. assisted in the geotechnical engineering evaluation of the fill sites 
with respect to the potential for ground subsidence or movement during the test pit 
excavation efforts. A Geolabs representative observed the condition of the existing fills as 
exposed by the test pits. In support of the combined environmental and geotechnical 
engineering exploration, Geolabs Inc. previously prepared the Guidelines for Test Pit 
Excavation and Activity Hazard Analysis (AHA) from a geotechnical engineering perspective. 
It should be noted that Geolabs, Inc. did not collect samples of the fill materials for 
testing and our engineering evaluation has been based on visual observation of the test 
trench excavation activity and the materials exposed by the temporary excavations. 
Visual observation of the landscaping tractor that is partially buried in the southern 
slope of Area 1 indicated that the fill above the tractor is partially supported by timber cribbing 
and removal, at present, would most likely destabilize the overcast fill slope above the tractor. 
Therefore, the machine was left in place for future removal during a future remedial action. 
TEST TRENCH EXPLORATION  
Between March 9 and 27, 2015, a test trench field exploration was conducted to 
characterize the existing fill materials for possible contamination utilizing environmental 
protocol for material sampling and testing by EA.  Excavation services were provided by 
Ron’s Construction, Inc. as a subcontractor to the prime sitework contractor, Elite Pacific 
Construction.  Excavation and backfilling work was undertaken using a Hitachi 85 track 
excavator and a John Deere 410K combination loader-backhoe. 
This exploration consisted of the 13 originally planned trenches, five additional 
trenches excavated at the request of the Hawaii Department of Health (HDOH) and an 
additional seven potholes excavated at the request of HDOH.  The test trenches were 
sampled by EA for various environmental contaminants using the multi-increment sampling 
method.  The potholes were generally shallow excavations intended to obtain an overview of 
the amount of waste materials present in the subsurface at the selected locations. Samples 
were not collected from the pothole excavations; nor were the excavations formally logged. 
The locations of the trenches and potholes are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 
and 2.2.  Graphic logs of the excavated trenches are shown on the attached Plates A-1 
through A-18 of Appendix A.  Daily Field Reports of the excavation activities are attached in 
Appendix B. 
The test trenches ranged in depth from about 3 feet to about 15.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface at the time of the field exploration; and, were excavated using a 
Hitachi 85 track excavator.  A Geolabs, Inc. geotechnical engineer/geologist observed the 
excavations on a near continuous basis to evaluate the stability of the existing fill materials

The Pothole Excavation Nos. 1, 2, 3, 6 and 7 were generally conducted in the 
northerly perimeter slope areas of HDOH Area 2 to make observations of the materials 
present in these side slopes.  Visual observation of the excavation of these potholes 
generally indicated that the soils in the slopes are overcast materials with a high percentage 
of oversize fragments of basaltic rock, concrete and asphaltic concrete.  This observation is 
consistent with supposed origin of the fills and typical equipment operator methodology.  It 
was also noted that mature trees were present in the slope faces leading to the conjecture 
that the fill material had been pushed out over existing trees.  
Pothole Excavation Nos. 4 and 5 were performed to explore areas where additional 
unsuitable or oversized material was suspected to be present.  Pothole No. 4 was excavated 
adjacent to EX-6 to further explore that area.  During the excavation of that pothole, additional 
decomposing organic matter was found along with an in-situ tree trunk which had been cut 
off just below the existing ground surface level.  Attempts were made to remove the tree 
trunk; however, the available equipment could not dislodge it. 
Pothole No.5 was excavated to the north of EX-3 due to an abundance of oversized 
asphaltic concrete and other miscellaneous materials on the surface at that location.  The 
excavation indicated that there was only a minimal amount of asphalt, concrete and metal 
present in the subsoils at that location. 
Upon completion of excavation, logging and sampling, all of the test trenches were 
backfilled using excavated material.  The material was moisture conditioned to near optimum 
moisture content prior to being placed in the trenches.  The backfill was placed in lifts of 
about 2 feet in thickness and tamped using the buckets of the Hitachi 85 and the John Deere 
410K.  When the level of the backfill had been brought to near ground surface, the backfill 
material was also wheel rolled using the John Deere 410K until the trench was completely 
filled.

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, the subsurface conditions encountered by the test trench exploration 
appear to be relatively competent and do not pose an imminent threat to mass stability 
of the three fill areas nor of the surrounding area.  However, it is recommended to 
permanently restrict fill areas, if left in place, from structural use due to the potential for 
future ground settlements.  Permissible future uses would be as open space, for 
temporary open storage, cultivation of landscaping materials, temporary parking or 
similar uses. 
Voids in a fill mass can result in ground settlement as soil migrates into the voids 
over time.  We did not observe voids in the fill materials exposed during this exploration.  
Even in areas with nested boulders and oversized asphaltic concrete fragments, the 
interstitial areas between the clasts were filled with soils; and, the large fragments were 
generally tightly packed forming a clast supported framework. 
The presence of organic matter or oversized materials does not necessarily 
make a fill unsuitable from a geotechnical engineering perspective.  As long as the 
percentage of organic matter is low, e.g., less than about two to three percent of the 
mass volume, the decomposition of the organic material will not result in significant 
ground settlements unless the material is concentrated in discrete pockets.  It is 
recommended to remove the organic matter encountered in the area of EX-6 and 
Pothole No. 4 and dispose of properly at an off-site location. 
Similarly, the presence of oversized material will not significantly impact the 
performance of a non-structural fill.  The primary issues associated with oversized 
materials would be the possible presence of voids under and between clasts which 
could allow the overlying soil to migrate into the voids, possible point loading of 
structural elements and difficulty in excavating for future infrastructure installation. 
The presence of miscellaneous materials, such as metal, reinforcing bars, 
masonry, plastic pipe, etc., also does not significantly impact the performance of a fill as 
long as the percentage of such material is low, e.g., less than about five percent. 
Based on a preliminary estimate provided by Elite Pacific Construction, the prime 
sitework contractor for this project, the excavation work at the site totaled about 
1,300 bank cubic yards.  Of this total, less than about 13 cubic yards each of asphaltic 
concrete (AC) and portland cement concrete (PCC) were removed from the trenches 
and hauled off site for disposal.  This is roughly equivalent to about one percent each of

AC and PCC.  About 2 cubic feet of miscellaneous metals and other materials were also

Therefore, it may be concluded that there is not a sufficient quantity of AC and 
PCC to impact the stability of the fill materials currently present at the three fill sites; nor, 
to warrant excavating the fill soils to process them for removal of the AC and PCC 
materials. 
It should be noted, however, that the fill present at the three areas appears to be 
as much as 30 feet in maximum thickness; and, that the maximum depth excavated in 
the test trenches was about 15.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  Therefore, 
significant data gaps in the present exploration are the quality of the fill at depths 
exceeding the maximum trench depth and whether the original ground surface was 
cleared and grubbed before the fill material was placed. 
Because of these data gaps, we recommend further geotechnical engineering 
exploration at the fill sites to determine the condition of the deeper fill materials and the 
base of the fill areas. 
As previously discussed, the existing perimeter slopes of the fill areas appear to 
be overcast material.  This overcast material is generally friable and does not appear to 
have been compacted during its placement.  The average slope ratio of these perimeter 
slopes is about one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V), or 45 degrees.  This exceeds the 
normal range of the angle of repose for soil materials, which is typically about 25 to 
40 degrees for earth materials. Therefore, these slopes will need to be flattened or 
stabilized with retaining walls/buttress fills. 
In summary, we offer the following findings and recommendations: 
1. 
No evidence was observed during the current exploration that would 
preclude in-place closure of the three fill areas. 
2. 
If closed in place, the three areas should be permanently restricted from 
any future structural use.  Non-structural uses would be permissible. 
3. 
Further exploration is needed to explore the deeper fill materials and to 
evaluate the condition of the bases of the fill areas. 
4. 
A slope stabilization system may be used to support the perimeter slopes 
of the fill areas. Additional exploration would be required for detailed 
design of such a system.

LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon 
information obtained from our field exploration and review of available soils data. Variations 
of conditions may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become 
evident until design or construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be 
necessary to re-evaluate the discussions presented herein. 
The test trench and pothole locations are approximate, having been located and 
staked by ControlPoint Surveying for EA Engineering, Science and Technology, Inc.  
Elevations of the excavations were estimated from the topographic plan provided. The 
physical locations and elevations should be considered accurate only to the degree implied 
by the methods used. 
The stratification breaks shown on graphic representations of the excavations depict 
the approximate boundaries between soil types and, as such, may denote a gradual 
transition. We did not encounter groundwater in the excavations at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it must be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in 
seasonal rainfall, and other factors. These data have been reviewed and interpretations 
made in the formulation of this report.

Arrived at site at 0700.  A daly safety meeting was held.  Ron's crew worked on policing up waste materials left 
from yesterday's removal action.  Less than 2 cy of miscellaneous metal objects and a large tractor tire were loaded 
onto a Ron's flatbed and hauled from the site.
At about 0835, excavation was started on Pothole #5 in the access trail between Area 1 and Area 2.  This 
excavation encountered mostly a reddish brown clayey silt material, with a surface layer of cold plane material 
along the access trail. The in-place material was generally dry to damp and stiff in consistency with no evidence of 
instability or voids.
This pothole was excavated at the request of HDOH based on the fact that some chunks of AC were observed at 
the surface.  The excavation was concluded at about 1100 and encompassed about 75 bank cy of digging.  
Approximately 1.5 cy of AC fragments and about 1 cubic foot of miscellaneous metal materials were encountered 
during the excavation.  These materials were segregated for later removal and disposal.  The pothole was 
backfilled between about 1210 and 1345 using the JD 410K.  The spoils were moisture conditioned prior to 
placement in the excavation, tamped with the hopto bucket and then wheel rolled.
At about 1220, excavation was started on the final trench, HDOH-A1-5.  The trench was excavated using the 
Hitachi 85 and was about 32 feet in length with an average depth of about 5 feet BGS.  With the exception of a lens 
of light brown silty sand with coralline gravel extending from the northern origin of the trench to about 19 feet south 
at a depth of about a foot, the excavation encountered dense dark gray sandy gravel with occasional cobbles (cold 
plane material) for its entire length.  Excavation was completed at about 1312 and the trench was backfilled 
between about 1332 and 1418.  The material was moisture conditioned prior ot replacement, tamped using the 
excavator and track rolled.
The work areas were policed and consultant equipment was demobilized from the site.
Departed site at 1540.
