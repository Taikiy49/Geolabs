SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Keck Residence located at 5 18 and 19 in the
Waikii area of South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity
of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input
only and may not be appropriate to form the basis for construction cost estimates nor for
construction bidding purposes
1 2 Project Considerations
The project site is located at 5 18 and 19 in the Waikii area of South
Kohala on the Island of Hawaii
The subject lot is approximately rectangular in shape Based on the information
provided we understand that a two story main house a guest house an arena a stable
and a tractor barn are planned for the Keck Residence project Detailed information
pertaining to the design grades and structural loads were not available during the
preparation of this report
Based on our borings and previous experience in the area it appears that the site
is underlain by a surface deposit of volcanic ash over basalt formation at deeper depths
Special consideration was given to the effects of the volcanic ash encountered at the
project site on the future foundations and slab on grade design of the proposed residence

SECTION 2 0 SITE CHRACTERIZATION
2 1 Regional Geology
The project site is situated on the northeastern flank of Hualalai which is the third
oldest of the five shield volcanoes forming the Island of Hawaii Hualalai was built during
the Pleistocene and Holocene Epochs by basaltic lava flows from the Hualalai Volcanic
Series
Typically the lava flows of the Hualalai series are composed of alkalic olivine
basaltic lava flows grading to hawaiite These lava flows are frequently mantled by a layer
of palagonitized ash locally referred to as Pahala Ash
During the later stages of the active phase of Hualalai the Puu Waawaa vent
opened This event extruding lavas of trachytic composition built a large cinder cone
now known as Puu Waawaa On the southerly side of this vent a thick trachyte lava
broke through the cone The flow which was on the order of about 700 feet thick
turned to the south and formed the long ridge now known as Puu Anahulu Subsequent
to the Puu Waawaa eruption younger lava flows from Mauna Loa overran portions of
this ridge
Our field exploration encountered a relatively thick surface layer of ash soil mantling
the site which is believed to be the original airfall deposits from the Puu Waawaa eruption
The ash soil at the project site differs from Pahala Ash found in other portions of the Island
of Hawaii in that the in situ moisture content is relatively lower and the soil exists in a
friable state as opposed to the plastic soil found in areas such as along the Hamakua
Coast

2 3 Subsurface Conditions
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling eight borings to
depths of 10 to 20 feet below the existing ground surface Samples were taken at
selected depths from the borings The approximate locations of the borings are shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our field exploration encountered a surficial deposit of volcanic ash soils
approximately 5 to 14 feet thick consisting of silty soils with consistency ranging from
medium stiff to very stiff This was underlain by highly weathered cinder consisting of very
stiff sandy and gravelly silt material and extended to the maximum depths of the borings
Hard basalt formation was encountered in Boring No 1 at approximately 14 feet below
ground surface and extending to the maximum depth of the boring
The volcanic ash encountered at the surface has relatively low moisture contents
and exists in a friable state The soil when wetted has relatively low strength
characteristics and when in a dry state is susceptible to erosion Therefore appropriate
soil erosion protection dust control provisions and construction procedures should be
observed and implemented during the foundation construction and site preparation work
for the subject project

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration indicates that the site is underlain by a surficial deposit of
volcanic ash approximately 5 to 14 feet thick consisting of medium stiff to very stiff silty
soils The dry friable ash encountered at the surface has relatively low strength
characteristics and is susceptible to wind and water erosion Therefore special
erosion dust control provisions and construction procedures should be observed and
implemented during the foundation construction and site preparation work for the subject
project
Conventional shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings
may be used for support of the proposed single family residence structures Because of
the presence of the dry friable silty ash soils we recommend that slabs on grade bear on
a minimum of 6 inches of compacted select granular fill Detailed recommendations are
presented in the following sections of this report
3 1 House Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site we recommend that
shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the proposed
structures An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot psf
may be used for the design of shallow foundations bearing on the recompacted ash
soils or engineered fills This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces
For footings bearing on the ash or the engineered fills the bottom of footing
excavations should be recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete If soft and or loose materials are
encountered at the bottom of footing excavations the material should be
over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The resulting over excavation
should be backfilled with select granular materials compacted to a minimum of

90 percent relative compaction In lieu of backfilling the over excavation with
compacted granular fill the footing bottom may be extended down to the underlying
competent material
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive
earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A
coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted ash
or engineered fills Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using
an equivalent fluid pressure of 250 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pef This
assumes that the soil around footings is well compacted The passive pressure should
be reduced for foundations located on slopes Unless covered by pavements or slabs
the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grades Footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping ground
should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal set back distance of
8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the slope face
Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded
below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the
bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate total settlements of the foundations to be less than
1 inch Differential settlements between footings are estimated to be on the order of
0 5 inches

3 2 Slabs On Grade
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by
near surface ash soils with low expansion potential when subjected to moisture
fluctuations To reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded
slabs on grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of the silty clays due to changes in
the moisture content we recommend that the subgrade soils be properly prepared and
capped with at least 6 inches of non expansive select granular fill capping fill material
below the slab cushion
The non expansive select granular capping fill should be placed as soon as
possible after the subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations
presented in the Site Preparation section of this report The prepared capping fill and the
underlying subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist prior to the final placement of
concrete Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend that the soils be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks Saturation and
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage
may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with engineered fill
We recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No 3
Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be placed below the slabs to serve as a
capillary break and to provide more uniform support of the slabs To reduce moisture
infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture barrier is
recommended on top of the No 3 Fine gravel Two inches of moist fine sand may be
considered above the moisture barrier to provide protection of the moisture barrier and to
aid in curing of the slab concrete Provisions of the 2 inches of moist fine sand above the
moisture barrier will be left up to the discretion of the project structural engineer Flexible
floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered since they can better
mask minor slab cracking

3 3 Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and the proposed design finished floor
elevation we envision that the site grading work for the proposed project will generally
consist of minor cuts and fills
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared Debris deleterious materials and other unsuitable materials should
be removed and disposed of off site Soft and yielding areas encountered during
clearing below areas designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over
excavated to expose firm material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled
with engineered fill The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly
disposed of off site Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit
prices for over excavation and engineered fill placement to account for variations in the
over excavation quantities
After clearing and demolition the areas within the future slab on grade should be
over excavated to provide the 6 inch thick layer of non expansive select granular fill
material Areas to receive fill and finished subgrades in cut areas should be scarified to
a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The
compaction requirement should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction in areas subjected to vehicular traffic Relative compaction refers to the
in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of
the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum
dry density
The on site silty soils generated from the cut areas may be reused as a source of
general fill material provided that they are free of vegetation deleterious materials and
rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension The 6 inch non expansive
capping layer recommended below the slabs and imported fill material should consist of

3 6 Leach Field
We understand that on site sewage treatment and disposal by the leach field
method is planned for the proposed residence Therefore field percolation tests were
conducted in our field exploration to obtain data pertaining to the preliminary design of
the sewage disposal system A total of two field percolation tests were performed at the
project site
The field percolation tests were performed in general accordance with the State
Wastewater Systems and Chapter 10 of the Ten State s Standards to evaluate the
permeability of the subsurface soils for wastewater disposal The tests were performed
by drilling 4 inch diameter boreholes extending to depths of about 4 5 feet below the
existing ground surface Approximately 1 to 2 inches of gravel was then placed at the
bottom of the borehole to protect the bottom of the hole from scouring and sediments
During the test the borehole was carefully filled with water to a minimum depth of
12 inches above the gravel The time intervals and water drops were then recorded to
provide data upon which the calculation of the percolation test was based
Based on the percolation tests conducted the percolation rates at the site
generally ranged between 2 and 5 minutes per inch indicating relatively good
percolation

3 7 Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines may be required for the project A granular
bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is
recommended under the pipes Self compacting granular materials such as No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the trench backfill up to
about one foot above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to
reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the
pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level one foot above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select granular material The
backfill should be placed in maximum 9 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted
to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground
subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 2 feet of the trench
backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to 95 percent relative
compaction
3 8 Drainage
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Also it is advised that gutter systems
be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from the
foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and
slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have
concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent loss of strength The foundation excavations should be properly
backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of the concrete to
reduce water infiltration

drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an
