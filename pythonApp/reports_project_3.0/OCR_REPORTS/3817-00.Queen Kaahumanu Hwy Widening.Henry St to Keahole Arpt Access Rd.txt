This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Queen Kaahumanu Highway Widening project between Kailua Kona
and Keahole International Airport in the District of North Kona on the Island of Hawaii

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes The five shield volcanoes include the following Kohala which is long extinct
Mauna Kea which has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last
erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is situated on the western flank of Hualalai Mountain which makes
up the west central portion of the island Most of the near surface basaltic lava formation
at the site appears to be composed of the pahoehoe type lava flow which spread and
ponded as it approached the ocean Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or
billowy surface with an internal structure of vesicular porous rock In addition to the
pahoehoe type lava some land area of the project site appears to be locally covered by
a a lava flows The a a lavas are typified by the appearance of loose rubbly clinker layers
interspersed with seams of very dense massive basalt rock The dense basalt rock layers
represent the hard interior core of the individual lava flow
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas especially the more fluid
pahoehoe lava Cavities are formed when the underground molten lava flow is insulated
from cooling occurring at the ground surface Cavities in lava rock represent both laterally
extensive lava tubes intra flow cavities and isolated blisters and pockets inter flow
cavities Lava tubes are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow interior
leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally
along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally closed cavity features that are usually
smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted
through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and

2 2 Faulting and Seismicity
Based on a review of available geologic information for the region there are no
known mapped geologic faults located in the vicinity of the project site Furthermore the
absence of long parallel fissure vents normal faults and open surface cracks suggests
that volcanic rift extension and intrusion of magma that characterize the active rift zone
activity of Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes do not appear to operate on Hualalai
Volcano However it should be noted that Hualalai Volcano is not considered to be
dormant as recent age volcanic eruptions occurred in 1801 in the vicinity of Keahole
located about 11 miles northerly of Kailua Kona In addition a month long swarm of
earthquakes that occurred in 1929 at Hualalai indicates that the potential for movement of
magma below the volcano remains The potential for future eruption of Hualalai Volcano
is possible although the location and magnitude of possible future eruptions are not well
understood
It should be noted that a large normal fault system is located in the vicinity of
Kealakekua Bay This active fault system is associated with Mauna Loa Volcano The
seaward block of land associated with the fault system has been displaced in a downward
and seaward direction due to seismic activity occurring along the fault trace
Due to the location of the project site on the Island of Hawaii the site is considered
to be located in a seismic zone with moderate to high potential for recurring seismic
activity Earthquake activity and fault rupture of the ground surface are possibilities
however the location of the project site with respect to the relatively distant location of
known active faults reduces the risk for fault rupture
It should be noted that earthquake activity on the Island of Hawaii is usually
associated with the underground movement of magma beneath the active volcanoes of
Mauna Loa and Kilauea In addition adjustment of the earth s crust under the weight of
the Island of Hawaii is also responsible for earthquake activity The greatest number of

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in
loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction General fills and backfills
should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 18inches in loose thickness
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with AASHTO T 180 ASTM Test Designation
D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density Compaction should be accomplished
by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable compaction
equipment
Rock fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of the
majority of the rocks or 5 feet whichever is less Placement should be done by
haul trucks traversing previously placed lifts and dumping at the edge of the
currently placed lift Spreading of the rock fill should be done by a large bulldozer
in order to facilitate seating of the rocks The rock fill should be watered heavily
during placement by water trucks traversing in front of the current rock lift face and
spraying water continuously during placement Compaction equipment such as a
Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar heavy compaction equipment providing suitable
energy to achieve the required compaction should be utilized The number of
passes usually six to eight passes to be made to achieve proper compaction

system based on the limited space for construction of a temporary shoring system
The soil nail retaining wall system would serve both as temporary shoring of the
excavation and the permanent retaining wall at these locations A soil nail retaining
wall system consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars grouted into drilled
holes to stabilize an excavation or slope A shotcrete facing is generally applied to
the face of the reinforced excavation to provide the appearance of a conventional
concrete retaining wall The reinforcing bars provide both additional tensile and
shear strengths to the soil or rock and reinforce the side wall of the excavation
The reinforced soil or rock mass behaves in a manner similar to a gravity retaining
wall but lacks a footing In general the face of the soil nail retaining wall system is
slightly battered at a slope inclination of 1H 12V or flatter
Soil nail retaining walls are normally installed in a cut condition only Where a fill
condition exists at the proposed soil nail retaining wall we recommend that the fills
be placed first to allow construction of the soil nail retaining wall entirely in a cut
condition The fill materials to be used in the area of the soil nail retaining wall
should consist of well graded materials with some cohesion to reduce the potential
for raveling of the materials during the soil nail retaining wall installation
Design of the soil nail retaining wall system will need to consider both the internal
and external stability of the reinforced soil or rock mass The design of the
internal stability includes establishing the size spacing orientation and length of
the grouted reinforcing bars The external stability includes overall slope stability of
the reinforced excavation

APPENDIX A

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
20 borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 20 extending to depths of about 20 to
25 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted
drill rig equipped with rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 20
Core samples of the rock formation and or boulder materials encountered at the
site were obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core
drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation
The material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination
and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and would
be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
