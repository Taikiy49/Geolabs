SITE DESCRIPTION
The proposed project site is located at Building 1805 within the Hickam Air Force
Base on the island of Oahu Hawaii The building is bounded by McCord Street to the
east and Moffett Street to the south
The existing dormitory buildings are three story structures with concrete masonry
walls and slab on grade concrete floors It is our understanding that the structures are
supported on pile foundations A terrace and planter structure are situated near the
center of the existing building with several concrete walkways traversing the entire project
site
The existing ground surface at the site slopes gently downwards to the north at a
gradient of about one 1 percent The grading plan prepared for the existing housing
development indicated that the existing ground elevations range from approximately 11
to 14 feet above Mean Sea Level MSL
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our exploration program was conducted between October 26 and 30 1992 The
exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling six 6 borings to depths ranging
between 34 5 and below the existing ground surface The approximate
locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our explorations encountered a surficial layer of fill material
approximately two 2 to three feet 3 thick consisting of very stiff silty clay material
overlying an alluvial deposit of medium stiff clayey silt to a depth of approximately seven
feet 7 below the existing ground surface This was underlain by compressible layers
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2985 00 Page 6
of loose sands and soft organics clay to depths of approximately 20 to 35 feet below the
existing ground surface
Competent deposits of coral reef formation were generally encountered below the
overlying compressible layers at the northern portion of the project site At the southern
side of the project site the competent bearing stratum of coral reef formation and volcanic
tuff deposit were not encountered until a depth of approximately 35 feet below the ground
surface
Groundwater was encountered in the drilled borings at depths of approximately 9
to 15 feet below the existing ground surface during the time of our field exploration
These depths generally correspond to elevations of approximately 0 to 6 feet above MSL
Due to the proximity of the shoreline and the Pacific Ocean it is likely that the
groundwater levels measured in the borings during our field exploration may reflect
neither the highest nor the lowest possible levels at the locations measured The
groundwater levels at the project site may be influenced by tidal fluctuations seasonal
precipitation and storm surge conditions
Strong petroleum odor was encountered from the samples and soil cuttings
obtained at a depth between 7 5 and 10 5 feet below the ground surface in Boring No 2

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
On the basis of our evaluation we believe that the most significant geotechnical
engineering consideration for the proposed dormitory construction is the potential ground
settlements due to the presence of potentially compressible subsoils We understand that
structural connections between the existing structures and the proposed new additions
are not planned As a result differential settlements between the existing and the
proposed structures can be of significance We anticipate that there will be some
differential settlements between the existing pile supported structures and the proposed
new additions if a shallow foundation system is used for support of the additions Based
on our evaluation we estimate total settlements on the order of one inch 1 for the new
additions supported on shallow foundation systems Since the existing structure is
supported on piles differential settlements between the existing structures and proposed
additions should also be about one inch 1 If the estimated differential settlements are
not acceptable then we recommend a deep foundation system consisting of micro pile
foundation system We estimate settlements of proposed additions founded on a micro
pile foundation system to be less than one half inch 4 with small post construction
settlements
Conventional Pile Foundation
Since the existing dormitory building is supported on concrete pile foundations a
similar driven pile foundation system was initially considered for the new lavatory facilities
However due to the proximity of the new additions to the existing structure vibration
generated during the pile driving operation may cause distress to the existing dormitory
structure
GEOLABS HAWAIl
W O 2985 00 Page 8
In addition existing structures such as the terrace planters and concrete walkways
may limit the accessibility of the pile driving rig and boom crane to some of the proposed
pile locations
In addition the mobilization cost of the pile driving equipment would be relatively
high in comparison with the scope of work involved for the subject project Based on
these drawbacks it is our opinion that the use of a similar driven concrete pile foundation
system would not be practical and economical for the proposed additions
Drilled piers were considered in lieu of piles however installation of drilled piers
will require casing and dewaiering due to the presence of loose granular soils and high
groundwater tabie
Shallow Foundations
Cur field exploration indicate that tne site is underlain by a compressible layer of
loose silty sand and soft organic clay below the near surface crust Based on the subsoil
conditions and the anticipated structural ioadings we estimate that total settiements would
be on the order of about three quarter of an inch 3 4 for a conventional spread footing
foundation and about one inch 1 for a mat foundation Due to the organic nature of
the underlying soft clay deposits a significant portion of the above settlements will be
long term with continued settlements after cornpletion of construction
Therefore it is our opinion that if the estimated long term settlements are
acceptable for the subject project shallow foundations may be used to support the new
lavatory facilities An allowable bearing value of 2000 p s f may be used and the footings
should be embedded about 18 inches below the ground surface

Micro Pile Foundations
Due to the stringent requirements for the allowable settlements of the new facilities
micro pile foundations consisting of small diameter steel pipe piles with closed ends may
be used to support the proposed lavatory facilities The pipe piles will be filled with
structural concrete after completion of the pile driving operations These micro piles
would provide foundation support with relatively small settlements and can be installed
with a small pile driving hammer or vibratory hammer
The steel pipe piles should have a minimum diameter of eight inches 8 A
design capacity of 10 kips per pile may be used for piles bearing on the coral formation
or the volcanic tuff deposits encountered at a deeper depth Based on our field
exploration pile length varying between 20 to 35 feet may be anticipated for the project
construction However variations in the embedment length across the site should be
expected A minimum pile spacing of three feet 3 center to center is recommended
An allowable lateral load capacity of five 5 kips per pile with a maximum induced
moment of nine 9 kips feet occurring at a depth of three feet 3 below the top of the
pile may be used to resist lateral loads An allowable uplift capacity of four 4 kips per
pile may also be used for the design of the pile foundation
Pile settlements are estimated to be on the order of less than one half inch 1
Settlements should be elastic and should occur as the loads are applied
The pipe piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a minimum
rated energy of about 15 000 foot pounds Because local variations in soil strength
characteristics are likely to exist in the bearing soils the supporting capacity of each pile
must be confirmed in the field during driving This will be accomplished by using a driving
resistance procedure such as the Engineering News Record ENR formula or the more
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2985 00 Page 10
sophisticated Wave Equation Analysis to evaluate pile bearing capacity The driving
criteria will be developed after the selection of the pile driving equipment
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the matching of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be driven
This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy to the top of
the pile and also reducing damage to the pile Geolabs should review the pile driving
equipment proposed to evaluate if the equipment is capable of installing the piles to the
recommended capacity Due to possible variation in the length of the piles unit prices
should be obtained during bidding for add ons shorter piles additional welds if used at
the connection etc
Predrilling may be required to penetrate through the upper hard fill layer to facilitate
the pile driving operation
Site Grading
We anticipate minimal site grading for the proposed dormitory renovation project
Due to the moderately high swelling potential of the near surface soils at the project site
special considerations and procedures should be observed and implemented for the site
grading
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of off site
After clearing and grubbing the areas to be occupied by the proposed additions
should be overexcavated to a minimum depth of 18 inches below the future slab
subgrade level i e bottom of slab cushion Any loose or soft spots encountered at the
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2985 00 Page 11
overexcavated subgrade level should be removed and replaced with compacted non
expansive select granular material The on site clayey material resulting from the over
excavation should be removed disposed of off site and replaced with select non
expansive granular borrow fill material
The overexcavated subgrade surface should be moisture conditioned to above
optimum moisture content and recompacted to 90 percent of its maximum dry density as
determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 Select off site granular material should be
placed in level lifts not exceeding ten inches 10 in loose thickness and compacted to
a minimum of 90 percent of its maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test
Method D 1557 78
The select granular material should be non expansive well graded from coarse to
fine with no particles larger than three inches 3 in maximum size and with less than 20
percent passing the No 200 sieve Any imported fill material should be tested and
approved by the geotechnical engineer prior to delivery to the project site
Site grading should be designed to divert surface water away from the proposed
additions
Concrete Floor Slab
A pile cap serving as the ground floor slab may be utilized for the proposed
renovations To reduce future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor
coverings an impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane on top of
a four inch 4 gravel cushion layer is recommended One inch 1 of moist fine sand
may be placed above the plastic membrane to help concrete curing

New Sewerlines
New sewerlines are anticipated for the proposed project A granular bedding
consisting of six inches 6 of select borrow or ASTM C 33 No 67 is recommended
under the pipes
Self compacting granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel should be used
for the trench backfill up to about one foot above the pipes to provide adequate support
around the pipes and to reduce the possibility of damaging the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level one foot 1 above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select granular material and
placed in maximum ten inch 10 horizontal loose lifts mechanically compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent of its maximum dry density as determined in accordance with
procedures described in ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 to reduce the potential for future
ground subsidence
For those pipes with inverts near the underlying loose silty sand and soft clay the
granular bedding should be increased to a minimum of 18 inches wrapped around by a
filter fabric for more uniform support of the pipe
Drainage
The finished grades outside the proposed renovations should be sloped to shed
water away from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Also it is advised
that gutter systems be installed around the improvements and that the discharge be
diverted away from the foundation areas Excessive landscape watering near the
foundations and slabs should also be avoided
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2985 00 Page 13
Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and
drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be
properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce water infiltration
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain
all surface run off away from the slab and footing foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling of six
6 borings to depths ranging between 34 5 and 101 5 feet below the existing ground
surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were
drilled using truck mounted equipment
The material encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who continuously monitored the drilling
operations These classifications were further reviewed by visual inspection and testing
in the laboratory All soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System A graphic presentation of the materials encountered are presented
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a standard penetration sampier or 3 O D Modified California sampler with a
140 pound hammer free falling 30 inches The blow counts to drive the sampler the
last 12 inches are shown on the Boring Logs at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of four inches 4 in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0
foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring Logs as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

Laboratory Testing

samples an as aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the Boring Logs at the appropriate sample depths
Two 2 one inch 1 ring swell tests were performed on selected undisturbed
samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the subsoils The test results are
summarized on Plate B 1
Two 2 direct shear tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the
shear strength parameters The test results are presented on Plates B 2 and B 3
