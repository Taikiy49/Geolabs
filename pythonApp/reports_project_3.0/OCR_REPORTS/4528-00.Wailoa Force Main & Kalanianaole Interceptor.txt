SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Wailoa Force Main Replacement project in the Hilo area on the Island of
Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our preliminary geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the project These preliminary recommendations are intended for design
input only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The proposed Wailoa Force Main project is a portion of the Hilo Sewerage System
located in the vicinity of Wailoa River area in Hilo on the Island of Hawaii The project
involves the installation of approximately 4 470 lineal feet of 30 inch force main and
1 060 lineal feet of 36 inch gravity sewer
The force main alignment begins at the pump station along Manono Street then
extends along Manono Street to Kuawa Street The line continues along the southern side
of Kuawa Street to Railroad Avenue heading northeast to Kamehameha Avenue The
alignment continues along Kamehameha Avenue and ends near Keaa Street The gravity
sewer alignment extends along Keaa Street from Kamehameha Avenue to Kalanianaole
Street
The proposed depths of the sewers generally vary from about 6 to 9 feet below the
existing ground surface The exceptions are the two locations where the new sewer will
cross under existing large drainpipes Trench depths in these areas may vary from about
11 to 14 feet below the existing ground surface

2 1 Regional Geology
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes which include the following Kohala which has long been extinct Mauna Kea
which has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801
and Mauna Loa and Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield
Volcano which has been built up by successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and
pyroclastic materials The near surface lava flows are from the Mauna Loa Volcano
overlying the Mauna Kea Shield The lava flows of the Mauna Kea Shield are believed to
be Pliocene to Pleistocene in age and belong to the upper member of the Hamakua
Volcanic Series The near surface lava flows of the Mauna Loa Shield may be on the
order of only 750 to 1 500 years old
The lava formation at the site appears to be pahoehoe flow which is characterized
by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous
rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lava These cavities are formed
when the lava is still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities
and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes are formed when molten lava
drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a
large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in
horizontal extent

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration it appears
that the proposed force main sewer line profile is underlain by hard basalt rock formation
at shallow depths
Detailed discussions of the geotechnical aspects of the project and our preliminary
geotechnical engineering recommendations for design are presented in the following
sections of this report
3 1 Open Trench Cut and Cover Installation
We anticipate that the force main and gravity sewer will be constructed by the
conventional open trench method Based on the design invert elevations anticipated for
the system we believe that the pipes will be underlain by basalt rock formation
Additionally most of the invert elevations along the alignment will be above the
groundwater table However at two locations the new pipe will cross under existing drain
lines and extend below the groundwater
Earth Pressure Loads on Pipes and Thrust Blocks
Loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the trench the size of the
pipes the unit weight of backfill material and the frictional resistance between the
backfill material and the trench walls To calculate the vertical loads on the buried
sewer pipes an average unit weight of 130 pounds per cubic foot pcf for backfill
material and a coefficient of friction of 0 30 are recommended Earth forces upon
the pipe generally increase rapidly with the width of the trench Therefore the width
of the trench should be kept to a minimum Traffic loads on the buried pipes if
applicable should also be considered for the portions of the pipes located in
roadway areas
We envision that thrust blocks may be required at some locations to provide
resistance to the thrust forces due to the pressurized pipes for the force main
The thrust force acting on the pipes may be resisted by passive earth pressure

acting against the face of the thrust block Resistance due to passive pressure may
be calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per
foot of depth pcf for the upper fill and trench backfill material For thrust blocks
cast against the natural rock formation an allowable lateral bearing value of 3 000
psf may be used To estimate the frictional resistance between the thrust block and
surrounding soils a coefficient of friction of 0 30 may be used These values
assume that the soils around the thrust block are well compacted and that the top
of the thrust block is embedded a minimum of 3 feet below the ground surface
Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a great effect upon the load
supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore pipe bedding is an important design
consideration In general granular bedding consisting of 10 inches of No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation or similar free draining material is
recommended under the pipes and manhole structures
Backfill
In general trench backfill should be performed in accordance with the Hawaii
Standard Specifications for Road Bridge and Public Works Construction 1994
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above
the pipes or 12 inches above the groundwater level to provide adequate support
around the pipes and to reduce compaction of the backfill thus reducing the
possibility of damaging the pipes Compaction by water ponding or jetting should
not be allowed for this project Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of
the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on site granular soils or
select granular fill material The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture content placed in maximum 8 inch horizontal loose lifts and
mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce

Based on our exploration minimal settlements are anticipated due to the competent
bearing conditions below the pipe invert
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good

Construction Considerations
The above settlement estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and
good workmanship will be consistently practiced by the contractor during
construction Based on our knowledge and experience with similar soils significant
settlement of the pipes may be attributed to the construction methodology and
equipment used by the contractor Therefore special attention should be given to
the installation procedure for the proposed sewer lines to reduce the potential for
settlement distress in the future

Based on the information provided for the proposed sewer project the pipeline
inverts will be about 6 to 9 feet below the existing ground surface and will be constructed
by open trench excavation However some localized deeper inverts will be required in
order to cross under the existing utilities

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration disclosed that the site is generally underlain at shallow depths
by hard basalt formation Therefore hard excavation utilizing hoe rams and chipping
should be anticipated
Excavation Method
It is anticipated that conventional earthmoving equipment may be used to excavate
the upper portions of the sewer line trenches based on the subsurface conditions
encountered However the excavation in the rock formation will be difficult and
probably require the use of hoe rams and chipping The method and equipment to
be used for excavation should be determined by the contractor subject to practical
limits and safety considerations
The excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance
equal to the depth of the excavation measured from the edge of the excavation in
order to reduce the potential for appreciable ground movement In addition the
excavated soils should be stockpiled at least 15 feet away from underground
utilities to reduce the potential for appreciable ground movement or subsidence
which may damage the underground utility lines
Excavation Support
Based on the depth of excavation and the upper gravel fill encountered we believe
that shoring of the sides of the excavations will be necessary to protect personnel
working in the excavations Because a majority of the sewer lines will be running
along the existing roadway sloping of the sides for an open cut excavation will not
be possible
It is important that adequate sheeting be installed prior to excavation and
maintained tight against the excavation walls with proper bracing during excavation
The properly braced sheeting is essential to reduce appreciable lateral movements
of the adjacent ground into the excavation which may result in potential
settlements or distress to adjacent structures or other improvements such as the
roads or utilities

The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His her representative who should be required to
be continuously present on site during excavation and construction work will have
the best opportunity to promptly observe changing conditions during construction
such as unforeseen subsoils unexpectedly high groundwater table inappropriate
construction sequence or techniques etc which may affect shoring stability
However it must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and
the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during
excavation Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to accurately
estimate the magnitude of movement The magnitude also depends greatly upon
workmanship such as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are
installed on the subsoil conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of
excavation
Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed
properly and as early as practical if necessary and that the adjacent ground
should be continuously monitored for cracks dips and or other indications of
movements with instruments
Dewatering of excavations will be necessary where the existing groundwater level
is above the bottom of the proposed excavation These conditions will occur at two
locations where the force main crosses under existing drain lines and at a low area near
Railroad Avenue Therefore dewatering provisions will need to be included in the
contract documents for the proposed construction A layer oil was encountered at the
water level in Boring No 2 The proposed invert level in this area is above groundwater
level and therefore consiruction should not be affected by the contamination However
the limits of the contaminated area are unknown and sustained pumping in this vicinity
may cause some migration of the oil

Subsoil Permeability
The in situ permeability of the underlying rock formation is generally high due to the
fractures in the formation The actual subsoil permeability at a particular location
may range broadly and also vary locally in terms of orders of magnitude
Dewatering Method
Based on the anticipated invert elevations of the proposed sewer lines and the
hardness of the rock formation dewatering with cut off walls such as interlocking
sheet piles is not feasible It is anticipated that dewatering through sump pumping
will be required
Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the contractor The selection of
equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor and
he she should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be
required during construction depending on the conditions encountered The
dewatering method selected should have minimal impact on the groundwater level
surrounding the proposed excavation The dewatering operations should be
coordinated with the trench shoring support such that the stability of the
excavations would not be jeopardized The operations should be carried out
without softening the bottom of the excavations
It is our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents should
be written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural groundwater
table and or to exclude the water from the excavations to allow construction of the
proposed structures under safe and dry conditions These works or systems may
include but are not limited to pumping
Dewatering Considerations
We suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in selection of a
suitable method of dewatering
a The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or
damage to existing buildings roads and environment
W O INC Page 11
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION RECOMMENDATIONS
b The dewatering method should maintain stability of and also provide
safe and dry working conditions in the excavation
C The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow
modifications to accommodate various ground conditions
Dewatering Precaution and Monitoring
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to dewatering
lt is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be retained by the
contractor to design and evaluate the dewatering system used
The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His her qualified representative who should be
continuously present on site during dewatering activities will have the best
opportunity to promptly observe the effects of dewatering during construction and to
implement as soon as possible necessary precautionary or remedial measures
including but not limited to stopping or slowing down the dewatering operations
Where encountered at the bottom of excavations permeable granular subsoils may
be susceptible to piping and quick conditions The dewatering operations should
be carried out without creating a quick condition or softening at the excavation
bottoms Therefore the project dewatering operations should be done without
pumping out soil fines pumping clear water only and should be coordinated with
shoring installation such that the excavation stability is not adversely affected
Excessive pumping which removes soil fines may result in a blowing or heaving
of the excavation bottom or sides
Groundwater drawdown outside the excavation may cause additional settlement
resulting from consolidation of the loose soft compressible soils Therefore the use
of a deep well system outside the excavations to draw down the groundwater level
should not be allowed

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 extending to depths ranging from
about 11 5 to 51 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of
the borings are shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 1 thru 2 5 The borings were drilled
using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with solid stem and hollow stem augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer and or geologist who monitored the drilling
operations on a near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with
the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of
the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 thru A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition samples were also obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
