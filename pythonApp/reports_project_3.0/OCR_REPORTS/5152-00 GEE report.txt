2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae Volcano forms
the bulk of the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau Volcano
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae
Volcano became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank is
partially buried below Koolau lava in Central Oahu
The project site is located in an area dominated by recent stream alluvial and
lagoonal deposits overlying formations of volcanic origin During the Pleistocene Epoch
Ice Age sea levels fluctuated and reached as much as 300 feet below the present sea
level Stream transported sediments consisting mainly of boulders cobbles and
gravel were deposited at a lower sea level As the sea level began to rise the capacity
of the streams was reduced and finer sands and silts were subsequently deposited At
some point the accumulation of sediments could not keep up with the rising sea level
and the area became submerged or flooded
As a result very fine grained sediments were then deposited in the marine or
estuarine environments Eventually the sea level stabilized while the sedimentation
process of fine grained sediments and shell beds continued Sediments with shell beds
are characteristic of near shore deposits Subsequently organic sediments which are
peaty and contain plant fibers and fragments accumulated and are indicative of marshy
conditions
Land development within the last 100 years has brought the site to its present
form Many of the resulting fills over the alluvial and marsh deposits are of poor quality
in terms of supporting heavy structural loads

Fabric Retaining Wall
In order to provide a greater usable area for the new skateboard facility we
understand that it is desired to provide a near vertical slope inclination along the outside
edges of the fill embankment In general we recommend that a flexible retaining
structure such as a geotextile fabric retaining wall be implemented to provide a near
vertical slope face We understand that the height of the new geotextile fabric retaining
walls will be limited to about 5 feet
The geotextile fabric retaining wall system is a composite wall system which
utilizes woven geotextile fabric to reinforce the backfill zone and to improve the shear
strength of the reinforced soil zone This composite system essentially forms a gravity
wall structure with an ability to carry appreciable structural loads In addition geotextile
fabric retaining walls are desirable due to the flexibility of the wall ease of construction
high load carrying capacity and economy
In general the reinforced zone of the geotextile fabric retaining wall should
extend laterally behind the wall face to a distance of approximately 70 to 75 percent of
the wall height or a minimum of 6 feet which ever is greater The vertical spacing of the
geotextile fabric will need to be determined by the design engineer As a minimum the
geotextile fabric should be placed at a maximum vertical spacing of 18 inches The
front face of the geotextile fabric should be wrapped at least 3 feet from the face of the
wall
The backfill may consist of the excavated on site materials compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction A unit weight of 125 pounds per cubic foot
pcf may be used for the compacted on site materials for design purposes The

moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in about 8 inch
loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction
to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground subsidence Where trenches are
located below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the
pavement grade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling two borings to depths of approximately 40 and 41 5 feet below the existing
ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
rotary wash equipment at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and
Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration
Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
