performed for the proposed County of Hawaii Transfer Station Replacements, Volcano

limitations noted at the end of this report.

SECTION 2.0 - SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2.1. Regional Geology
Island of Hawaii is the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers an area of
approximately 10,360 square kilometers (4,000 square miles). The island was formed
by the activity of the following five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea
(activity during recent geologic times), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 - 1803), and
Mauna Loa and Kilauea (both still active).
The site is situated near the summit of Kilauea on rocks of the Puna Volcanic
Series. These rocks are typified by relatively thin-bedded basalt lava flows in both the
pahoehoe and a‘a forms.
The U.S. Department of Agriculture classified the surface soils in the area as
Keei extremely stony muck (rKGD) on 6 to 20 percent slopes. This soil is generally
found at intermediate elevations on Mauna Loa and Mauna Kea; and, consists of thin
organic soils overlying pahoehoe basalt. Typically, 25 to 50 percent of the ground
surface is rock outcrops.
Typically, the surface soil layer is very dark brown organic material and averages
only a few inches to about a foot thick. The soil is extremely permeable and the
underlying rock formation has much lower permeability.

most of the grading consisted of leveling the existing ground to create a yard area for
the facility. There is an existing timber retaining wall on the chute side of the trailer
holding area. Existing ground surface elevations are on the order of about 3,200 feet

SECTION 3.0 - DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration indicated that the project site is generally blanketed by thin
surface layers of man-made fills, of less than about 1 foot thick, overlying in-situ
volcanic rock formation in the borings to the maximum depths explored. We did not
encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration.
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered during our field exploration, we
envision that the buildings and structures for the proposed replacement transfer station
facility may be supported on a shallow foundation system consisting of spread or
continuous footings.
A falling head percolation test indicated that the shallow subsurface permeability
at the site is extremely rapid (i.e., the time required for one inch of water to percolate
from the boring was considerably less than one minute). Generally, this rate is
considered to be too rapid and unsuitable for a leach field. However, if the bottom of the
leach field trench is over-excavated and replaced with 3 feet of soil, such as medium to
fine sand, wastewater effluent could be applied at about 2 gallons per day per square
foot of leach field trench bottom.
Our geotechnical engineering recommendations for the design of foundations,
slabs-on-grade, retaining structures, site grading, wastewater systems, drainage and
pavements are further discussed in the following sections herein.
3.1. Building Foundations
Based on the conditions encountered in our exploration, we believe that shallow
spread and/or continuous footings may be used to support new building structures at
the site. An allowable bearing pressure of 4,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be
used for the design of foundations bearing on compacted subgrade prepared in
accordance with recommendations presented in this report.
The bottom of footing excavations (except in rock formation) should be
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction prior to the placement of

reinforcing steel or concrete. Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of
the footing excavations should be over-excavated to expose the underlying firm
materials. The over-excavated area may be backfilled with new fill material compacted
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction or the bottom of footing may be
extended down to bear on competent materials at a greater depth.
Footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grades or 12 inches into rock formation. Footings constructed near the
tops of slopes, or on sloping ground, should be embedded deep enough to provide a
minimum horizontal setback distance of 8 feet measured from the outside edge of the
footings to the face of the slope. Footings adjacent to planned (or existing) retaining
walls should be embedded deep enough to avoid surcharging the retaining wall
foundations, or the planned retaining walls should be designed to resist the additional
structural loads.
Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a
45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench
or embedded at least as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This requirement is
necessary to avoid surcharging the adjacent below-grade utility lines or structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation
settlement.
Lateral loads acting on building structures may be resisted by friction developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A coefficient of
friction of 0.4 may be used for footings bearing on the dense on-site soils or new
compacted fills. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf). This
assumes that the soils around the footings are well compacted. Unless covered by
pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be
neglected. In addition, the passive earth pressure for foundations located on slopes
should be reduced.

3.2. Foundation Probing
Small voids and/or cavities were encountered only in Boring No. 1V during our
exploration. However, based on our experience in the area, there is a potential for
presence of lava tubes, blisters and/or cavities in the basalt formations. In order to
reduce the potential for structural damage due to collapse of voids/cavities, a probing
and grouting program is often conducted below structures. However, due to the
relatively light structures anticipated for this project, we believe that the potential for loss
of foundation support resulting form collapse of the cavities below the foundation is
relatively small and the probing and grouting program may be omitted if this small risk is
tolerable. However, if it is desired to further reduce the potential for loss of foundation
support, then a program of cavity probing and grouting at the foundation locations
should be conducted during construction.
3.3 Site Grading
Details of the proposed site grading for the Volcano Transfer Station replacement
project were not available at the time this report was prepared. The following
discussions of grading are general in nature and are intended to provide guidance for
design of grading work that may be required.
3.3.1 Site Preparation
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off-site. Soft and/or
yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to
receive fill should be over-excavated to expose firm material, and the resulting
excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fills. The excavated soft and/or
organic soils should be disposed properly off-site and/or used in landscape areas,
where appropriate. Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit

prices for over-excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in
the over-excavation quantities.
After clearing and grubbing, subgrades, finished subgrades, and areas designated
to receive fills should then be scarified to a minimum depth of 12 inches,
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the
in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture
is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry
density.
3.3.2 Fill Materials
Fill materials required to raise the existing ground surface to the proposed finished
subgrades may consist of the excavated on-site soils, provided that they are free of
vegetation, deleterious materials, and rock fragments greater than 6 inches in
maximum dimension. Imported materials should consist of granular materials with
particle sizes of 6 inches or less in maximum dimension and should be free of
deleterious materials, and have a minimum CBR of 20.
Aggregate base course materials required for the flexible and rigid pavement
sections should consist of crushed basalt aggregate and should conform to
Sections 703.06 and 703.17 of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for
Road and Bridge Construction, 2005 (HSS). Imported fill materials should be
tested for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project
site for the intended use. Geolabs should either perform the testing or review
testing results submitted by the contractor or supplier.
3.3.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture,
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the
in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density

of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture
is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry
density.
Aggregate base course materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.
Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during the site grading

3.3.4 Excavation
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by shallow
surface fills with basalt formation encountered at depths ranging from about 1.0 foot
to about 5.5 feet below the existing ground surface. We anticipate that the
near-surface materials may be excavated readily with normal heavy excavation
equipment, such as backhoes, and ripped with large bulldozers. However,
excavations encountering basalt formation at greater depths may require the use of
hoerams, chipping, or blasting, if permitted.
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based
on our visual observation of the existing materials encountered and field data from
the borings drilled and sampled at the site. Contractors should be encouraged to
examine the site conditions and the boring data to make their own interpretation.
3.3.5 Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration, we believe
that cut slopes for this project will expose competent granular materials. In general,
the cut slopes planned may be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to
one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter.

Permanent fill slopes constructed using the on-site materials (or imported fill
materials) may also be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. Fills
placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the
existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding. The filling operations

3.4 Slab-On-Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs-on-grade will be used for new structures on
this project. For interior building slabs that will not be subjected to vehicular traffic, we
recommend placing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No. 3 Fine
gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) below the slabs. The open-graded gravel cushion
fill would serve as a capillary moisture break and would provide for uniform support of
the slabs. To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent
damage to the floor coverings, an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top
of the gravel cushion layer.
Where the slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic, such as trucks and/or
forklifts, we recommend providing a 6-inch layer of aggregate subbase below the slabs
in lieu of the 4-inch cushion fill layer. The aggregate subbase should consist of crushed
basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.
For slabs that will be subjected to heavy equipment traffic, the structural engineer
should design the concrete slab for the intended loads. For design of structural slabs, a
modulus of subgrade reaction of 200 pounds per cubic inch (pci) may be used for the
compacted base course. Provisions should be made for proper load transfer across the
slab joints that are subjected to vehicular traffic.

Because the project site is on the seismically active Island of Hawaii
(Seismic Zone 4 in accordance with the 1997 Uniform Building Code), forces due to
dynamic lateral earth pressures will need to be considered in the design of retaining
structures. The force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures (Amax=0.40g) may be
estimated using 16H* pounds per foot of wall length for level backfill conditions,
where H is the height of the wall in feet. For a maximum 3H:1V sloping backfill
condition, dynamic lateral earth forces due to seismic loading may be estimated
using 28H? pounds per lineal foot of wall.

3.5.4 Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the potential for build-up of
hydrostatic pressures.
For site retaining walls, the typical drainage system may consist of 1 cubic foot of
permeable material, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation),
wrapped with non-woven filter fabric placed at each of the weephole locations. The
weepholes should be spaced no more than 6 feet apart. As an alternative, a
prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used
instead of the permeable drainage material. The prefabricated drainage product
should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the
retaining wall.

3.7 Wastewater Disposal
We understand that on-site sewage treatment and disposal by septic tank and
leach field method are planned. Therefore, we have conducted a field percolation test to
obtain data for the preliminary design of the sewage disposal system at the proposed
site.
Field percolation testing was performed in general accordance with the State of
Hawaii, Department of Health’s Administrative Rules, Chapter 11-62-31.2, “Wastewater
Systems” and Chapter 10 of the Ten States Standards to evaluate the permeability of the
subsurface soils for wastewater disposal. The percolation test was performed by drilling a
4.0-inch diameter borehole to a depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface.
The percolation test was performed within the near-surface rock formation generally
encountered in the project area.

Approximately one inch of gravel was then placed at the bottom of the borehole

field trench bottom.
3.8 Drainage
As previously indicated, proper surface drainage is critical to the performance of
foundations and slabs. Therefore, the finished grades outside the structure should be
sloped to shed water away from the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding.
It is also advised to install gutter systems around the building and to divert the discharge
away from the foundation and pavement areas. Excessive landscape watering near the
foundations and slabs should also be avoided.
The foundation excavations should be backfilled properly against the walls or
slab edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for water
infiltration. In addition, drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and
should be maintained to drain surface water runoff away from foundations and slabs.

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling
three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1V through 3V, extending to depths of
approximately 11.0 to 15.0 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate
boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. We used a truck-mounted drill rig
equipped with rotary coring tools.
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field. He monitored the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3.
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113, Diamond
Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method, which uses a
hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of the
bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description.
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. If 3.7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5.0-foot core run, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock, based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals."
