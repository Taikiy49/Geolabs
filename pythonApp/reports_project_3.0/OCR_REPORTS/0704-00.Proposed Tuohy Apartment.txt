SUMMARY
The site is underlain by a thick boulderly
and cobble clay layer In order to provide
uniform support with minimum differential
settlement for the proposed structure a
5 foot thick layer of compacted gravel is
recommended under all the footing founda
tions Allowable bearing capacity up to
6 000 pounds per square foot can therefore
be used
The on site soils are of low expansion
potential and slab on grade construction
can be utilized
INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of a foundation investi
gation for the Tuohy Apartment a 10 story apartment develop
ment located on Nuuanu Avenue Honolulu Hawaii
The purpose of the subsurface investigation is to explore
the existing soil conditions at the site and to provide
soil data for use in the design and construction of the
building foundation

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located in the lower Nuuanu Valley
area on the southeastern side of Nuuanu Avenue below
Judd Street Honolulu Oahu
The total area of the site is about 17 000 square feet and
its average dimensions are about 230 feet deep by 75 feet
wide The deepest length of the site is along the northern
side about 255 feet
The site is bounded on the northwest by Nuuanu Avenue and
on the northeast and southwest by two high rise condominiums
The southeastern side is bounded by a slope of about 20 per
cent dropping down about 20 feet to an adjacent lower level
area beyond which lies Nuuanu Stream A rock wall runs
along the northeastern property line and the rear portion
of the southwest property line
Access into the site is by way of an existing paved drive
way entering from Nuuanu Avenue leading to a small parking
and garage area in the center of the site
At the time of our soil investigation 4 single story wood
framed dwellings occupied the site

The site generally slopes down in a southeast direction
at about 0 to 10 percent The front half of the site is
relatively level while the rear half has a slope that is
more discernable Lava rock boulders are located at the
surface along the northeastern boundary midway through
the site The surface drainage of the area is in a south
east direction but no visible signs of drainageways are
present The ground surface elevations vary from about
135 feet in the northwest to about 107 feet in the south
east
The site vegetation consists of shrubs and several large
mango avocado coconut and monkey pod trees
PROJECT CONSIDERATION
It is presently planned to construct a 10 story reinforced
concrete structure approximately 90 by 55 feet in plan at
the rear portion of the site A 2 level parking structure
will occupy the remaining front portion of the site area
For the proposed project the site grading will mainly
require cut into the existing grade The maximum cut is
about 5 feet deep for the parking structure and about 10
feet deep for the main tower structure
The maximum wall load for the main building will be about
32 kips per lineal foot

Geology
During a volcanic quiescent period of at least 2 million
years after Oahu s major island building lava eruptions
ceased numerous deep depressions in the Koolau range were
cut primarily by streams eroding and gouging material out
from the range leaving back well formed mature valleys such
as Nuuanu Valley The quiescent period then ended with the
occurrence of volcanic activity on the southeastern end of
the island and covered many eroded areas with fresh lava
ash cinders and spatter This new set of eruptions con
stituted the Honolulu Volcanic Series with popular eruptive
landmarks such as Diamond Head Koko Head Punchbowl Salt
Lake and others Among these famous eruptive landmarks
were many others that occurred but are not as well known
or noticeable because of their indiscriminate nature
In particular are the Makuku and Luakaha cones which occurred
in the upper area of Nuuanu Valley The Makuku cone is located
near the west side of the valley a mile from the Pali gap
The Luakaha cone is located on the east side of the valley
near the upper end of the upper reservoir The eruption of
these two cones poured lava down Nuuanu Valley covering and
flattening the valley floor while pushing Nuuanu Stream out
of the valley s axis to the eastern base of the valley wall

It is this lava flow which comes down Nuuanu Valley or
the rocky material from it that is encountered at the pro
ject site beneath a relatively shallow layer of soil Con
stant weathering and erosion from the time of its occurrence
has produced many in place and transported boulders and
fractured rock layers derived from this lava flow Deep
beneath this lava flow probably lies the eroded remnants
of the former valley floor along with other ancient geologic
formations

Soil Conditions
The soil profile of the project site consists of a brown
to gray brown clayey soil with some gravel from the surface
down to about 5 feet The soil becomes more rocky between
the depths of 5 to 10 feet and consits of cobbles and
boulders intermixed with the clayey soils and with clay
pockets occurring at various depths From 10 feet down to
the bottom of the borings the underlying material becomes
primarily rock and consists of a boulderly material in various
stages of decomposition and hardness with the exception of
boring no 4 located in the center of the project site
In boring no 4 dense lava rock was encountered at about
17 feet depth down to the end of the boring at 29 5 feet
In boring no 5 next to the existing front house a fill
layer of black sand was noted at about 2 to 6 feet depths
A similar layer of dark brown to black sand occurs in
boring no 6 behind the front house at a depth of 3 5 to
5 feet

What appears to be a group of very large boulders occurs
midway through the site along the mauka boundary as men
tioned in the site description Their size is at least
5 feet in diameter and they extend above the ground surface
intersecting part of a rock wall that runs along that boun
dary
No groundwater level was noted in the drilled holes at the
time of our field exploration
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The site area is underlain by a relatively thick clayey
boulderly layer Continuous wall footing foundation as
planned for the main tower structure is highly recommended
for the boulderly soils encountered at the proposed site
For the proposed structure to be constructed over the
boulderly soil the two major concerns from a foundation
view point are how to minimize the differential settlement
and how to provide uniform foundation support for the struc
ture To overcome the above problems it is recommended
that a compacted gravelly layer be provided under all the
footing foundation which will create a relatively uniform
layer within the high intensity stress zone directly beneath
the footing

Foundations
The bottom of the spread and wall footing should be over
excavated to allow for placing a minimum 5 foot thick
layer of compacted gravel The gravel material can be crushed
rock with less than 15 fines The gravel backfill should
be placed in 9 inch loose lifts and compacted to 95 of
the laboratory maximum density in accordance with ASTM 1557
The spread and wall footing can then be designed for an
allowable bearing pressure of 6 000 pounds per square foot
Settlement predictions in this type of soil material are
empirical judgement can only be based on previous experience
in similar soil types The estimated total settlement should
be less than 1 inch with 75 of this settlement occurring
during construction It is anticipated that differential
settlement between the parking garage and the high rise
will be on the order of one half of an inch
Where existing or abandoned utility lines are located under
the foundation the footing should either be stepped down
or be designed to bridge over the loose backfill areas
During construction if any abandoned cesspool or loose
utility backfill is located below the footing foundation
the sludge and soft material should be removed down to firm
ground and replaced with lean concrete up to the bottom of
the gravel layer under the footing
GEOLABS HAWAII
TUOHY APARTMENT 77
From our previous experience of the adjacent high rise
project perched and trapped water may be encountered
in the boulderly layer It is not anticipated that
excessive hydrostatic pressure would be developed due
to the proposed construction It should be pointed
out that gravel backfill will be necessary to provide
adequate drainage beneath and around the proposed footings
in the lower elevations
Slab On Grade
Low expansive soils were encountered during the subsurface
explorations The laboratory test on the near surface clay
soil indicated a CBR value of 18 with an expansion of less
than 1
A four inch base course layer is recommended below the con
crete slab at the lower parking level and the basement of
the high rise All backfill for the footing excavation at
the slab area should be placed in 9 inch layers and compacted
to a minimum of 90 of its maximum dry density as specified
above Prior to placing the base course layer the subgrade
soil should be wetted to above its optimum moisture content
and compacted to a minimum of 90 compaction

Retaining Wall Design
Retaining walls will be utilized at the lower level parking
and the basement of the high rise structure The foundations
for retaining walls should be designed in accordance with the
recommendations for the continuous wall footing foundations
For the retaining wall not directly under the main tower
structure the footing foundation can bear on the undis
turbed in situ soil using an allowable bearing pressure of
2 500 pounds per square foot For the retaining wall bearing
directly on the stiff in situ soil it is recommended that
it should be constructed in short sections to reduce differen
tial settlements
It is recommended that a subsurface drainage system be pro
vided to prevent the build up of hydrostatic pressure behind
all the retaining walls Granular rock backfill should be
utilized as backfill behind the retaining wall This will
permit the wall to be designed for an equivalent fluid
pressure of 40 pounds per cubic foot for a free standing
retaining wall For the retaining wall with support at
the top 60 pounds per cubic foot equivalent fluid pressure
should be used
A frictional factor of 0 4 should be utilized to determine
the sliding resistance of the retaining wall foundation

Earthwork Site Grading
Site grading involves cuts up to 10 feet to construct
the lower parking level and basement Miscellaneous
fill materials abandoned utility lines and storm drains
may be encountered within a few feet of the surface during
the grading operation
During excavation the on site gravelly soils can be
stockpiled separately The soil can be used as gravel
backfill provided that no rocks in excess of 6 inch in
diameter are incorporated into the fills and that the
percentage of soil passing the 200 seive does not exceed
15
Sections of shallow trench excavation into the stiff in
situ soil formations may be left open for short term con
struction period without sloughing the vertical walls
However the excavation should be supported to keep the
ground movement to a minimum
CONSTRUCTION MONITORING
It is recommended that the foundation excavation for the
footing construction be inspected by the soils engineer
prior to the placement of the gravel layer under the foun
dation to insure that adequate bearing has been obtained
and that the subsurface conditions are consistent with the
findings of our subsurface investigation
