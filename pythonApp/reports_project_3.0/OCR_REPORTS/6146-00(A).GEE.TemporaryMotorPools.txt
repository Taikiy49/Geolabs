2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau shield is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a 
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are 
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
Schofield Plateau was formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded 
against the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene 
Epoch. The dips of the lava beds generally range from 3 to 5 degrees (near horizontal). 
The lava flows on the plateau have weathered to depths of 15.2 to 30.5 meters (50 to 
100 feet) and are characterized by the red colors of the soil. The deep narrow canyons, 
such as Waikakalaua, Kipapa, and Kaukonahua, are a result of this weathering. The 
brown and reddish brown soils generally consist of ancient alluvial deposits and residual 
soils overlying the weathered rock.

2.4.1 Earthquake and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic 
plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic 
activity; therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground 
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The 
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are 
so small that only sensitive instruments can detect them. However, some of the 
earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate 
damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on 
the Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement 
of magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa 
Volcanoes on the Island of Hawaii. Because majority of the earthquakes in 
Hawaii (over 90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic 
activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from 
the Island of Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous 
earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not 
confined only to the Island of Hawaii. 
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes 
greater than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have 
occurred in the County of Maui. The effects of earthquakes occurring on the 
Islands of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu. For example, small 
landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of 
1938 (M6.8). Some houses on the Island of Oahu were reportedly damaged as a 
result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 (M7+).

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend 
supporting the temporary structures on shallow spread footings bearing on the in-situ 
clayey soils. The footings should extend a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest 
adjacent finish grade.  The dead weight of the structures and the foundations may be 
used to provide the required uplift load capacity.  In addition, the weight of the backfill 
material placed above the foundations may be used to provide resistance to uplift loads. 
In lieu of shallow spread footings, soil anchors may be used to support the 
temporary structures in compression and tension.  In general, uplift loads may be 
resisted by shear along the anchor surface and the adjacent soils. The project structural 
engineer should check the structural capacity of the soil anchors in tension.  All soil 
anchors installed for the project should be proof tested up to 200 percent of the design 
load. 
The design vehicle for the aggregate-surfaced and asphaltic concrete pavements 
is AASHTO WB-50 semi-trailer.  Based on the design vehicle and a design CBR value 
of 12, we recommend using a 6-inch thick aggregate surfacing layer for 
aggregate-surfaced pavements.  There is a high potential for raveling and rutting of the 
aggregate surface layer.  Therefore, the aggregate-surfaced pavements should be 
periodically maintained.  Maintenance of the aggregate-surfaced roads would likely 
consist of grading to remove ruts and potholes caused by the environment and traffic.  
In addition, the surface layer may have to be scarified and additional surface course 
material would need to be placed to increase the thickness back to the required 
thickness. 
We recommend using 3.0 inches of asphaltic concrete underlain by 6.0 inches of 
aggregate base course as the pavement section for asphaltic concrete pavements.  The 
aggregate base course should be compacted to a minimum of 100 percent relative 
compaction.

3.1 
Temporary Structure Foundation  
Based on the generally competent subsoil conditions encountered at the site, we 
believe that the temporary structures may be supported on a shallow foundation system 
consisting of spread footings.  An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per 
square foot (psf) may be used for design of spread footings bearing on the in-situ soils.  
This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for 
transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces.   
The bottom of the footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below 
the lowest adjacent finish grade.  Deeper embedment may be required to provide 
resistance to uplift and moment reactions. The dead weight of the structures and the 
foundations may be used to provide the required uplift load capacity.  Additionally, the 
weight of the backfill material placed above the foundations may be used to provide 
resistance to uplift loads, if applicable.  A unit weight of 110 pounds per cubic foot (pcf) 
may be used for the on-site compacted clayey soils. 
The bottom of foundation excavation should be firm and unyielding prior to 
placement of reinforcing steel or concrete.  Soft and/or loose materials encountered at 
the bottom of the foundation excavation, they should be over-excavated to expose the 
underlying firm materials.  Well-compacted on-site soils should be used to backfill the 
resulting over-excavation.  The bottom of the foundations may be extended down to the 
underlying competent material in-lieu of backfilling the over-excavation. 
Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a 
one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V) imaginary plane extending upward from the 
bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. 
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate the total settlements of the foundations to be less than

3.2 
Site Preparation 
Based on the information provided, we understand that minimal site grading work 
will be required at the project site.  At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the 
contract grading limits should be cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, 
deleterious material, and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed 
off-site. Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be 
over-excavated to expose firm material. The resulting excavation should be backfilled 
with engineered fill. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be properly 
disposed of off-site. Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit 
prices for over-excavation and engineered fill placement to account for variations in the 
over-excavation quantities. 
After clearing and grubbing, areas to receive fills and finished subgrades in cut 
areas should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 
2 percent above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction. The areas subjected to vehicular traffic should be 
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers 
to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry 
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum 
moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum 
dry density.  
Where shrinkage cracks are observed after the subgrade compaction, we 
recommend preparing the subgrade soil again as recommended above. Saturation and 
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor 
drainage may require over-excavating the soft areas and replacing these areas with 
engineered fill. A Geolabs representative in the field should evaluate the need for 
over-excavating due to soft subgrade soil conditions.

The on-site soils generated from the cut areas may be reused as a source of

Excavation 
Based our field exploration, the soils encountered at the site are generally stiff to 
very stiff.  Therefore, we believe that conventional excavation methods may generally 
be utilized for excavations.  
However, we believe there is a high potential for encountering hard basalt rock 
(in the form of relatively unweathered cobbles and large boulders) within the residual 
and saprolitic soil layers at Site G-H. In addition, there is a high potential for 
encountering relatively hard and unweathered cobbles and large boulders within the

Utility Line Trenches 
In general, for utility line support, we recommend using a granular bedding, 
consisting of 6 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), under the 
pipes. Free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, 
No. 67 gradation), should also be used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches 
above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the 
compaction effort of the backfill, thus reducing the possibility of pipe damage. It is 
critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids 
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. 
Improper trench backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes 
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on-site soils 
with a maximum particle size of 6 inches. The backfill should be moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture, placed in maximum 8-inch level loose lifts, and 
mechanically compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the 
potential for future ground subsidence. Where trenches are below pavement areas, the 
upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement finish grades should be 
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction.

3.5.1 Aggregate-Surfaced Pavements 
A 4-inch thick layer of aggregate was calculated for aggregate-surfaced pavements.  
Due to the high potential for raveling and rutting of the aggregate surface and the 
high potential for encountering variable subgrade conditions at the site, we

W.O. 6146-00(A) 
Page 15 
recommend increasing the thickness of the aggregate surface layer to 6 inches.  
The aggregate-surfaced pavements should consist of gravel material with a 
maximum particle size of 2 inches and particles passing the No. 200 Sieve should 
be between 8 and 15 percent.  In addition, the liquid limit should not be greater than 
35 and the plasticity index should be between 4 and 9.  Recommended gradations 
for the aggregate surfacing material may be found in UFC 3-250-09FA.  The 
aggregate surface layer should be compacted to a minimum of 100 percent relative 
compaction. 
The subgrade soils under the aggregate surface layer should be scarified to a 
depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the 
optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction. Soft and/or loose soils encountered at the pavement subgrade should 
be over-excavated to the underlying firm or dense materials. The resulting 
over-excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fill.  Relative compaction 
refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum 
dry density as determined by ASTM  D 1557.   
Adequate drainage should be provided to allow removal of the surface water and 
reduce infiltration and saturation of the aggregate surface layer and subgrade, 
which could lead to softening of the subgrade causing rapid deterioration of the 
aggregate-surfaced pavement.  It should be noted that there is a higher potential for 
raveling and rutting of the aggregate surface layer.  Therefore, periodic 
maintenance will be required for the aggregate-surfaced pavement.  As mentioned 
in UFC 3-250-09FA, the majority of the maintenance will consist of periodic grading 
to remove the ruts and potholes caused by the environment and traffic and to 
replace fines.  In addition, the surface layer may have to be scarified, provide 
additional surface course material to increase the thickness back to the required 
thickness, and compacted to the specified density.

3.5.2 Asphaltic Concrete Pavements 
Based on our engineering analyses, the following pavement section was calculated 
for the temporary maintenance structure pavements consisting of asphaltic 
concrete: 
Temporary Maintenance Structures (Calculated Section) 
  2.5-Inch Asphaltic Concrete 
  5.0-Inch Aggregate Base Course (100 Percent Relative Compaction)  
  7.5-Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Subgrade 
As previously mentioned, we believe that there is a high potential for encountering 
variable subgrade conditions at the site.  Therefore, we recommend using the 
following pavement section for preliminary design purposes:   
Temporary Maintenance Structures (Recommended Section) 
  3.0-Inch Asphaltic Concrete 
  6.0-Inch Aggregate Base Course (100 Percent Relative Compaction)  
  9.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Subgrade 
The subgrade soils under the pavement areas should be scarified to a depth of 
about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum 
moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
Soft and/or loose soils encountered at the pavement subgrade should be 
over-excavated to the underlying firm or dense materials. The resulting 
over-excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fill. 
Aggregate base course should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no 
larger than 2 inches in largest dimension and should contain less than 10 percent 
particles passing the No. 200 Sieve.  The aggregate base course should be 
compacted to a minimum of 100 percent relative compaction. CBR tests and/or 
field observations should be performed on the actual subgrade soils during 
construction to confirm whether the above design section is adequate. The 
recommended pavement section assumes good drainage will be provided for areas 
adjacent to the pavements.

3.6 
Drainage 
The finished grades outside the structures should be sloped to shed water away 
from foundations and pavements and to reduce the potential for ponding. It is also 
advised to install gutter systems around the structures and to divert discharge away 
from the foundation and pavement areas. Excessive landscape watering near the 
foundations and pavements should also be avoided. Planters next to foundations should 
be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water 
infiltration into the subsoils. Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible 
and maintained to drain surface run-off away from the pavements and foundations. 
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the 
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil 
saturation and subsequent loss of strength and/or heaving of the near-surface clayey 
soils.

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling twelve borings extending to depths of about 12.5 to 16.5 feet below the
existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan,
Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a track or truck-mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight augers.
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System, as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings,
Plates A-1 through A-12.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving
a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In
addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of a 18-inch drive are
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths.
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

APPENDIX  B 
APPENDIXB
Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937)
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths.
Six Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth.
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1.
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D 3080) was performed on a selected soil sample
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics. The Direct Shear test results are
presented on Plate B-2.
One Unconfined Compression tests (ASTM D 2166) were performed on selected
samples of the soils to evaluate the undrained shear strength characteristics. The test
results along with the stress-strain curves are presented on Plate B-3.
Four laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) tests (ASTM D 1883) were
performed on the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-4 through B-7.
