This technical memorandum provides our geotechnical recommendations for the
design of the HART CCUR retaining wall along Honolulu Community College (HCC). Our
recommendations are based on the information provided, our experience in the area, field
observations, a review of geologic maps, and available subsurface information from
previous field exploration programs conducted for the Honolulu Authority for Rapid
Transportation (HART) Transit Elevated Guideway project.
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
A site reconnaissance of the project site was conducted by our project engineer on
August 23, 2022 to evaluate the existing site conditions at the new retaining wall site. The
general project location is depicted on the Project Location Plan, Plate 1. Our field
observations during our site visit are presented below. Site reconnaissance photographs
depicting the observed conditions at the project site are presented on Plates 3.1 through 3.8.
The existing retaining wall is located along Dillingham Boulevard, bordering the
southeast corner of HCC. To the west of the retaining wall, the existing ground surface
was primarily composed of brown silty soil with vegetation debris. A small outcrop of
coralline limestone was observed, similar to what was observed at the wall location
(Photograph No. 1). At the western end of the wall, the retaining wall primarily consisted
of the in-situ limestone. Sections of cement rubble masonry (CRM) wall appear to have
been constructed on top of the limestone to extend the wall to the desired height
(Photograph No. 2). Moving east along the wall, sections of CRM were frequently
observed filling in the gaps of the in-situ limestone to complete the retaining wall
(Photograph Nos. 3 through 10). It should be noted that a HECO electric pole guy wire
anchor was observed to be installed in the existing limestone near the center of the
retaining wall (Photograph No. 7). In general, the height of the exposed limestone
formation decreased towards the eastern end of the wall. Approximately 50 feet from the
eastern end of the wall, the limestone formation suddenly dips down in elevation
(Photograph No. 11). Beyond this point, only small outcrops of limestone were observed
at the base of the wall (Photograph Nos. 12 and 13). Based on our observations, it
appears that the retaining wall backfill consists of soil and rubble (Photograph Nos. 14
through 16). In addition, limestone formation was not observed at the existing ground
surface in the areas above the retaining wall.

ANTICIPATED SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Based on available geologic maps, the existing retaining wall is located near the
southwestern limit of a Known region of near-surface limestone (symbol Qls). The
neighboring mapped deposit consists of recent fill (symbol Rf). The boundary between these
two regions generally cuts through HCC and extends just to the south of Dillingham
Boulevard near the proposed retaining wall location. A Geology Map of the project area is
presented on Plate 2.
Based on the information provided, the proposed retaining wall is closest to
Boring Nos. B-695 and B-696 from the HART Transit City Center Elevated Guideway
project. Details of the project, boring locations, boring logs, and laboratory tests are
presented in the report entitled “Geotechnical Data Report, Honolulu Rail Transit Project,
City Center Section Utilities and Guideway, Middle Street to Ala Moana Center, Honolulu,
Oahu, Hawaii, Volume 2 — Bent 690 to Bent 755,” prepared by Yogi Kwong Engineers, LLC,
dated June 18, 2015. The location of Boring Nos. B-695 and B-696 as well as the Logs of
Borings are presented on Figure 3.1 and Plates A-6.1 through A-7.4, respectively, appended
to this memorandum for ease of reference. For the purposes of the new retaining wall, only
the upper 17 to 20 feet of materiasl encountered in the drilled borings are discussed in the
following discussion.
Boring No. B-695 generally encountered a pavement structure consisting of
approximately 4 inches of asphaltic concrete (AC) overlying about 8 inches of the granular
base material. Beneath the pavement section, fill material consisting of relatively stiff
clays/silts and medium dense sandy gravel were encountered to about 5 feet. Medium
dense coralline detritus consisting of sand and gravel was encountered to about 12.5 feet,
followed by a coral formation extending to about 20 feet below the existing ground surface.
The coral formation graded to severely to closely fractured and medium hard.
Boring No. B-696 generally encountered a pavement structure consisting of
approximately 8 inches of asphaltic concrete (AC) overlying about 16 inches of fill material
consisting of very dense silty gravel. Beneath the pavement section, coralline detritus
(consisting of medium dense sands and gravels) was encountered to about 10 feet, followed
by an approximately 7-foot-thick layer of closely fractured, soft to medium hard coral
formation to a depth of about 17 feet below the existing ground surface.
Groundwater was encountered at depths of about 5.5 to 7.3 feet below the existing
ground surface at the time of our field exploration, corresponding to elevations of about
+0.1 to -1.3 feet MSL. However, it should be noted that water levels may vary with tidal
fluctuations, seasonal rainfall, time of year, and other environmental factors.

may be subject to seismic activity, and seismic design considerations will need to be

addressed. The following subsections provide discussions on the seismicity and the
potential for liquefaction at the project site.
The subsurface conditions at the project site generally consist of coralline detritus,
coral formation, and basalt rock formation at depths of about 35 to 36 feet extending to
depths of about 88 to 110 feet below the existing ground surface. Boring No. B-696
encountered hard older alluvium beneath the basalt rock formation extending to a depth of
about 110 feet. The coralline detritus encountered generally consists of granular materials
with weakly to strongly cemented zones and pockets of loose sandy and silty soils. The
loose sandy and silty soils encountered in the coralline detritus generally occur in pockets
and generally do not appear as continuously layered materials. Therefore, we believe the
coralline detritus with zones of loose sandy and silty soils are not susceptible to liquefaction
due to the confined nature of the coralline detritus deposits.
Based on the subsurface materials encountered and the geologic setting of the
area, we believe that the project site may be classified from a seismic analysis standpoint
as being a “Very Dense Soil and Soft Rock” site corresponding to a Site Class C soil
profile based on the LRFD Bridge Design Specifications, 9th Edition (2020).
RETAINING WALL FOUNDATIONS
Based on our field observations, it appears that in-situ coralline limestone
formation is present at or near the ground surface along the existing retaining wall
alignment. We understand that the new retaining wall will be set back to the north, farther
away from Dillingham Boulevard. Based on the available boring logs, coral formation was
encountered at depths of about 10 to 12 feet below the existing ground surface. However,
based on our experience in the area, we understand that there are many underground
utilities within Dillingham Boulevard, many of which are below the groundwater table.
Therefore, we believe that the near-surface coral formation and/or limestone was
previously excavated to install the underground utilities along Dillingham Boulevard, and
the near-surface “coralline detritus” encountered in the borings may actually consist of
backfill material. This may explain why coralline rock formation was observed at the
ground surface at the existing wall location but encountered deeper in the drilled borings.
Therefore, we believe the new retaining wall foundations may bear on in-situ coralline
limestone formation.
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions along the new retaining wall
alignment, we believe that shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings bearing on the
in-situ coral limestone formation may be used to support the retaining wall. The following
values may be used for the design of the retaining walls bearing on soil material based
on LRFD methods. ,

If soft and/or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing
excavations, they should be over-excavated to expose the underlying coral limestone
formation. The footing excavation may be backfilled with lean concrete or flowable fill from
the coral formation up to the design bottom of the footing elevation.
In general, the bottom of the footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches
below the lowest adjacent finished grade. Footings should have a minimum width of
18 inches. Footings located adjacent to slopes or on slopes should be embedded to a
sufficient depth to provide a minimum horizontal setback distance of at least 6 feet
measured from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope.
Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded
below a one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V) imaginary plane extending upward from
the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation
settlement.
Due to the potential for the presence of cavities and/or voids as commonly
encountered in the coral formations in the area, we recommend proof-rolling all the exposed
wall foundation subgrades. The foundation subgrade should be proof-rolled utilizing a
minimum 10-ton vibratory drum roller a minimum of eight passes to detect the presence of
near-surface cavities and/or loose zones. Proof-rolling operations should be conducted
under the observation of a Geolabs representative. Where soft/loose soils are encountered

The values provided above assume that select granular fill will be used to backfill
behind the retaining structure. It is assumed that the backfill behind the retaining structure
will be compacted to between 90 and 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction
refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557 (AASHTO T180) test
procedures. Over-compaction of the retaining wall backfill should be avoided.
Imported materials to be used as select granular fill should consist of non-expansive
granular material, such as crushed coral or basalt. The select granular fill should be
well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension.
The material should also contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200
sieve. The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a

In general, an active condition may be used for gravity retaining walls and retaining
structures that are free to deflect by as much as 0.5 percent of the structure height. If the
tops of the structures are not free to deflect beyond this degree or are restrained, the
retaining structures should be designed for the at-rest condition. These lateral earth
pressures do not include hydrostatic pressures that might be caused by groundwater
trapped behind the structures.
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges, line loads, and point loads within a
horizontal distance equal to the depth of the wall should be considered in the design. For
uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the structure, a rectangular
distribution with a uniform pressure equal to 33 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure
acting on the entire height of the wall, which is free to deflect (cantilever), may be used in
the design. For walls that are restrained, a rectangular distribution equal to 50 percent of
the vertical surcharge pressure acting over the entire height of the structure may be used
for design. Additional analyses during design may be needed to evaluate the surcharge
effects of point loads and line loads.
For non-restrained conditions, dynamic lateral earth forces due to seismic loading
(a = 0.209g) may be estimated by using 4H? pounds per linear foot of wall length for level
backfill conditions, where H is the height of the wall in feet. It should be noted that the
dynamic lateral earth force provided assumes that the wall will be allowed to move
laterally by up to about 1 inch in the event of an earthquake. For restrained conditions,
dynamic lateral earth forces due to seismic loading may be estimated by using
6.8H* pounds per linear foot of wall length for level backfill conditions. The resultant force
should be assumed to act through the mid-height of the wall. The dynamic lateral earth
forces are in addition to the static lateral earth pressures provided above. An appropriately
reduced factor of safety may be used when dynamic lateral earth forces are accounted
for in the design of the retaining structures. )
In general, retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build-up of
hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch-wide zone of
permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), placed
directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at the base of the walls
discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes. As an alternative, a prefabricated
drainage product, such as MiraDrain, may be used instead of the drainage material. The
prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated
pipe at the base of the wall.
The backfill from the bottom of the wall to the bottom of the weephole should
consist of relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential for significant water
infiltration into the subsurface. In addition, the upper 12 inches of the retaining wall backfill
should consist of relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential for significant
