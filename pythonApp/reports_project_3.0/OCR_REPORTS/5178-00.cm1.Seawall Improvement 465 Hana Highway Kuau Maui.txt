PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed project consists of improving an existing seawall at 465 Hana
Highway 09 021 in Kuau on the Island of Maui Hawaii The residential lot is a
flag lot with an area of 13 079 square feet The lot is roughly rectangular in shape with
access from a driveway off Hana Highway The northern end of tne lot faces the ocean
and the ground surface is about 14 feet above sea level The lot is relatively flat but the
ground surface slopes downward from the backyard to the ocean with relatively steep
gradients of about one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V to near vertical in localized
places The eastern portion of the slope is covered with dumped rip rap consisting of 1 to
3 foot sized basalt boulders The western portion of the slope was covered with grouted
boulders however the westernmost portion had been undermined and failed It appears
that the grouted boulder slope protection had been placed on a shelf of residual soil
approximately 6 feet above sea level
It is desired to evaluate various options to repair the failed portion of the slope
protection and to improve the entire seawall

The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on the Boring Log Legend Plate A
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the site were obtained by using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 21138 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD also is a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core
run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50
In general our field exploration encountered a thin layer of fill topsoil consisting of
dark brown sandy silt to a depth of about 2 to 4 feet The fill was underlain by residual soil
consisting of stiff reddish brown silty clay with remnant boulders or thin basalt ledges to a
depth of about 13 to 20 feet The residual soil was underlain by extremely weathered
basalt consisting of medium dense gray silty sand and gravel to depths of about 20 to
29 feet over highly weathered basalt The highly weathered basalt was underlain by hard
basalt rock at depths of about 25 to 33 feet to the full depth of the borings
Groundwater was encountered at about 14 6 to 15 feet below the existing ground surface
at the time of our field exploration It should be noted that groundwater levels are subject
to change due to tides seasonal precipitation and other factors

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The repair or improvement of the existing seawall is complicated by several
conditions at the site First observations of the damage to the existing seawall and
discussion with others indicate that the area is subjected to relatively large waves A study
of the severity of the waves and forces is not included in our scope of work but may be
important to the design
Second the subsurface exploration indicates that a hard erosion resistant layer is
not present at shallow depths below the site The borings encountered hard basalt
formation at depths of 25 to 43 feet below the backyard elevation or about 11 to 29 feet
below sea level Therefore construction of a conventional wall system on the basalt
formation is not practical and other more complicated and costly alternatives may need to
be considered
Third equipment access to the site is severely limited The width of the access
driveway and space along the sides of the house are too small for conventional
construction equipment necessary to construct retaining walls It would be desirable to
obtain construction easements along the beach area however we do not know if beach
access is feasible
General guidelines for short term and long term alternatives for the seawall
improvement are presented below
Retaining Wall System
A conventional retaining wall system could be constructed along the property line
Ideally the wall should bear on hard erosion resistant formations to resist undermining by
wave action however hard layers were not encountered within practical depths below the
site Therefore this wall system would be considered only a temporary solution as wave
action would eventually undermine the wall stability In order to provide some resistance
the wall foundations should be embedded as deep as practicable Methods to increase
the resistance of the wall to undermining are available but are generally costly and not
appropriate for the subsurface conditions at the site Cement or chemical grouting are
sometimes used to solidify specific soil conditions however they are generally ineffective
in the residual soils and extremely weathered rock formation at the site Permanent sheet
piles were also considered to resist the erosion however the remnant boulders and thin
basalt ledges in the residual soils and extremely weathered basalt would prevent proper
penetration of the sheet piles

Retaining wall foundations may be designed with an allowable bearing pressure of
up to 3 500 pounds per square foot psf on the residual soil or extremely weathered
basalt This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third
1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
The retaining wall should be designed to resist the lateral earth pressures due to
the adjacent soils and surcharge effects The recommended lateral earth pressures for
design of retaining walls with level backfill conditions expressed in equivalent fluid
pressures are presented below
Above Water 40 pounds per cubic foot pcf
Below Water 85 pounds per cubic foot pcf
These values provided above assume that the wall is free to deflect and that
granular fill will be used to backfill behind the wall It is assumed that the backfill behind
the retaining wall will be compacted to between 90 and 95 percent relative compaction
Over compaction of the retaining wall backfill should be avoided
In general an active condition may be used for walls that are free to deflect by as
much as 0 5 percent of the wall height Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges line
loads and point loads within a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the wall should be
considered in the design Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by
frictional resistance between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials A
coefficient of friction of 0 4 may be used for footings bearing on the extremely weathered
basalt
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures above the water level A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 foot
wide zone of permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM D 33 No 67
gradation immediately around a perforated pipe perforations down at an elevation
above the water level discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes Backfill behind
the permeable drainage zone should consist of granular fill materials less than 3 inches in
maximum dimension Unless covered by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches of backfill
should consist of non expansive impervious material to reduce water infiltration behind the
walls
Construction of the retaining wall is expected to be slow and costly due to the very
limited accessibility for larger construction equipment and the relatively deep excavation
depths Also underpinning of the existing house will probably be required prior to
excavation for the wall construction to prevent foundation settlement

protection It appears that this portion of the slope has performed better than the western
portion with grouted boulders However it is unclear whether this is due to particular site

conditions such as the angle of the wave impacts or other site factors In any case a short
term stabilization method could include placement of rip rap boulders on the western
slope The rip rap should extend down to below the water level to protect against erosion
of the residual soil currently exposed below the grouted boulders
The size of the boulders necessary and anticipated life of the slope protection

The feasibility of this method would also depend on the actual location of the

Drilled Shaft Wall
The remnant boulders and thin hard layers in the residual soil and extremely
weathered basalt would prevent proper penetration of a sheet pile wall system
Additionally the proximity of the existing house would prevent installation of an anchorage
system to provide the necessary lateral resistance for the wall These limitations may be
overcome by a drilled shaft wall system
The construction of a drilled shaft consists of drilling a hole usually 2 to 5 feet in
diameter to the desired embedment depth and placement of reinforcing steel and
concrete in the hole The drilled shaft can be placed through most natural formations The
diameter of the shaft would be selected to be able to resist the lateral soil loading If the
drilled shafts are placed adjacent to each other in a row a retaining wall system is formed
Conceptually a drilled shaft wall system could provide a long term seawall at the
site The major limitations would be the accessibility for the drilling equipment and cranes
for lifting of the reinforcing steel and concrete placement Additionally the costs are
anticipated to be high due to the difficult access and relatively hard drilling conditions
