This report summarizes our earthwork observation and testing services performed 
during the site grading operations, foundation excavation, pavement construction, and 
utility installation for the Medline at Kapolei Business Park project from July 8, 2016 
through May 5, 2017 in accordance with our fee proposal dated July 5, 2016. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located at Lot 14 within the Kapolei Business Park, Phase 2 
development in the Kapolei area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project involves the 
construction of a warehouse building along the east side of the property and 16 sunken 
loading docks that can accommodate semi-trailer trucks with 45-foot long containers along 
the west side of the property. An asphalt concrete paved parking lot is also planned along 
the southeastern corner of the property to serve the new warehouse facility.  A layout of 
the new facility is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 1. 
The new warehouse will be a single-story structure with a gross building area of 
approximately 82,000 square feet. The new building will measure approximately 513 feet

EARTHWORK OPERATIONS 
The general contractor for the project is Swinerton Builders.  Jayar Construction, 
Inc. performed the site grading operations, foundation excavation, pavement construction, 
and utility installation. The site grading consisted of clearing and grubbing, footing 
excavation, subgrade preparation, placement and compaction of fills and backfills, 
pavement construction, and utility trench excavation and backfill. 
Following the clearing and grubbing operations, the existing ground was generally 
over-excavated (where necessary) to the subgrade elevations within the building and 
pavement limits. The coral formation exposed at the over-excavated subgrades was then 
proof-rolled with a minimum 10-ton sheepsfoot and/or vibratory drum rollers to detect the 
presence of potential near-surface cavities, sinkholes, or loose zones.  New fills required 
for the project generally consisted of the excavated in-situ coralline materials or imported 
non-expansive select granular materials.  The excavated in-situ coralline materials were 
generally screened of the over-sized materials and/or processed to a maximum particle 
size of 3 inches or less. 
Our field observations indicated that coralline materials mixed with clayey soils 
were encountered below the building slab and pavement areas at the project site.  Based 
on our laboratory test results performed on the materials, it appears that the clayey soils 
are slightly to highly expansive when subjected to moisture fluctuations.  Based on our 
evaluation of the field conditions and laboratory test results, we recommended 
over-excavating the expansive clayey soils, where encountered, and replacing with 6 to 
12 inches of non-expansive select granular material (such as the on-site coral or Grace 
Pacific 3-inch minus material) below the building footprint.  For the driveway, parking, and 
loading dock area, 6 inches of aggregate subbase were added below the base course 
layer under the AC pavements and the aggregate subbase below the PCC pavements 
was increased from 6 to 12 inches.

The non-expansive select granular fill materials were generally placed in near-level 
lifts, brought to above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent of the maximum dry density determined in accordance with ASTM D1557 
(Modified Proctor). The base course and aggregate subbase under the AC and PCC 
pavements were generally placed in near-level lifts, brought to above the optimum 
moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent of the maximum dry density 
determined in accordance with ASTM D1557 (Modified Proctor).  During the foundation 
excavation and preparation, the excavated footing bottom was generally recompacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
New utility connections were installed to serve the new development. In general, a 
bedding layer consisting of 6 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel was provided below the piping 
and manholes for uniform support. Free-draining granular materials consisting of 
No. 3B Fine gravel was also used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the 
pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort 
on the initial trench backfill. The trench backfill was placed in 8-inch level loose lifts and 
mechanically compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction. Where trenches 
are below pavement areas, the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement 
grade was compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
A cavity was encountered in the parking lot area during the excavation of the 
24-inch drain line. The bottom of the cavity extended to a depth of about 10 feet below the 
design finish grades.  Based on our field observations and evaluation, we recommend 
removing the loose materials at the bottom of the cavity and backfilling the cavity with 
flowable fill consisting of Controlled Low Strength Material (CLSM).  Approximately 
50 cubic yards of CLSM was used to fill the cavity. 
During the site grading operations, samples of the on-site and imported materials 
utilized as fill and backfill materials were obtained and transported to our office for testing. 
The maximum dry densities and optimum moisture contents were established in our 
laboratory in general accordance with ASTM D1557 (Modified Proctor), and the test 
results are summarized on Plate 2 of this report. 
Field density tests were performed during the fill and backfill operations on an 
as-need basis to confirm our field observations as variations in construction procedures 
and soil conditions were noted. A total of 147 field density tests, including retests of failed 
areas, were performed using the nuclear probe method in accordance with ASTM D6938. 
Results of the compaction tests performed are summarized on Plates 3.1 through 3.9 of 
this report. These tests were performed on the prepared ground surfaces and on 
constructed fills and backfills at the site. Test locations and elevations were estimated by 
pacing and interpolating from on-site survey stakes. These locations and elevations should 
be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. Areas assessed 
as unsatisfactory were reworked until the specified minimum compaction was obtained. 
Based on the observations of our field representatives and the results of the 
referenced tests, it is our opinion that the earthwork operations during the site grading,
