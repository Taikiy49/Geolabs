PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is on the hillside of the Inner Diamond Head Crater. A section of 
the shotcrete along the near-vertical cut slope adjacent to the tunnel exit has been 
observed cracking and spalling off, resulting in a safety hazard. The section of the 
cracking shotcrete is about 65 feet long. 
Based on the information provided, we understand that the tunnel was constructed 
during World War II. The tunnel is currently used as a primary vehicular/pedestrian access 
into the inner crater ring for the park recreation activities and the military facilities. The 
tunnel entrance was shotcreted to prevent fractured rock reveling. 
It is desired to repair the deteriorated/cracked shotcrete that pose a safety hazard 
to the vehicular and pedestrian users. We understand that the as-built drawings for the 
tunnel shotcrete were not available at the time of this report.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of the western 
third of the island while the younger Koolau Volcano forms the majority of the eastern two-
thirds of the islands. It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active, 
and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu. 
Volcanic activity from Koolau Volcano ceased for nearly 2 million years. Volcanic 
activity once again resumed from mid-Pleistocene Epoch and has lasted through recent 
geologic time. These volcanic events, known as the Honolulu Volcanic Series, have formed 
cinder, spatter, tuff and ash cones throughout the southern portion of the Island of Oahu. 
One of the most prominent vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series is Diamond Head. 
Diamond Head is a tuff cone and was generally formed by hydromagmatic explosions. 
Materials within the tuff cone consist of magmatic debris, blocks of Koolau basalt, and 
fragments of coral limestone torn from the underlying reef. The project site is on the eastern 
slope along the inner ring of Diamond Head Crater. The subsurface conditions of the project 
site generally consist of fallen materials from the upper slope overlying weathered volcanic 
tuff materials and volcanic tuff formation at greater depths. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITION 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling two 
borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 22.6 and 21.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface, respectively. The approximate boring locations are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  
Our field exploration suggests that the project site is generally underlain by colluvium 
over weathered volcanic tuff and volcanic tuff formation at greater depths. In general, our 
drilled borings encountered colluvium consisting of sandy clay to a depth of about 1 foot 
below the existing ground surface. Beneath the colluvium, our drilled borings encountered 
weathered volcanic tuff generally consisting of very stiff sandy clay and dense clayey sand 
extending to depths between about 13 and 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Underlying the weathered volcanic tuff, Boring No. 1 encountered medium hard to hard 
volcanic tuff formation extending to the maximum depth explored of about 22.6 feet below 
the existing ground surface. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the project site at the time 
of our field exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to 
change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, seepage, 
and other factors.

OBSERVATION OF EXISTING SHOTCRETE 
The project site is along the Diamond Head Crater Road outside of the west portal of 
the Kahala Tunnel. Cracking and spalling were observed in the northern shotcrete wall at 
approximately Diamond Head Crater Road Sta. 21+00 to Sta. 21+65. The damaged portion 
of the shotcrete wall is approximately 12 to 22 feet in height and is near vertical to about 
one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V) inclination. 
The project site is generally underlain by weathered volcanic tuff consisting of very 
stiff sandy clay and dense clayey sand which grades to hard volcanic tuff formation with 
depth. The fractured near-vertical slope is covered by concrete shotcrete. Based on our 
observation, the existing shotcrete facing is about ½-inch thick with no visible wire-mesh 
reinforcing. Based on our experience with concrete shotcrete walls, we anticipate areas of 
the concrete shotcrete may be up to 4 inches thick. 
Our site reconnaissance consisted of visual observation in spall/crack areas to 
determine approximate limits of the emergency shotcrete repairs identified on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. The shotcrete distresses observed may be categorized as follows: 
Aging and/or shrinkage cracks; 
Excessive seepage water; and 
Vegetation lifting. 
The height of the shotcrete wall generally matches the approximate 5H:1V sloping 
grade behind it. It should be noted that ground surface above and orthogonal to the shotcrete 
wall is relatively level and without drainage structures. The absence of a drainage swale 
may have resulted in water infiltration behind the shotcrete facing, which may have 
introduced excessive hydrostatic pressure. 
We also observed over-grown vegetation at the top of the wall. Vegetation grew 
through the shotcrete layer resulting cracks and spalls. 
SHOTCRETE REPAIR RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our observations, it appeared that an imminent falling object hazard may 
occur at the north shotcrete wall outside of the west portal of the Kahala Tunnel. This 
assessment was based on the sizes of spall/crack, locations of potential falling objects, 
and dimensions of the wall. Potential falling objects have been temporarily restrained by 
anchoring a chain-link mesh to the wall face. It is our opinion that the emergency shotcrete 
repairs should be implemented at the northern shotcrete wall at approximately Diamond 
Head Crater Road Sta. 21+00 to Sta. 21+65.

As previously mentioned, reinforcement was not observed in the existing ½-inch 
thick shotcrete facing. We recommend using fiber reinforced shotcrete with a minimum 
7-day unconfined compressive strength of 3,000 pounds per square inch (psi) for the 
shotcrete repairs. The fiber reinforcement should consist of synthetic macro fiber 
reinforcement in accordance with ASTM C1116-03, Strux 85/50, or accepted equal. The 
shotcrete should contain 128 ounces per cubic yard of shrinkage reducing admixture and 
the water to cement ratio should be limited to 0.45. We recommend a thorough cure of 
the shotcrete by use of a curing agent. The repaired shotcrete thickness should be a 
minimum 2 inches thick and maximum 3 inches thick with the exterior surface matching 
the existing adjacent shotcrete facing.  
Based on our observation of the existing shotcrete, we identified the section to be 
included for repair as part of the emergency procurement on the Site Plan, Plate 2. It 
should be noted that the identified repair section is approximate. The extent of the 
shotcrete distress/failure will not be visible until clearing and removal operations expose 
the actual conditions behind the shotcrete facing. Therefore, a sufficient contingency fund 
should be set aside. 
We believe that over-grown vegetation may have, in part, contributed to the 
spalling and cracking observed on the existing shotcrete facing. We recommend 
thoroughly clearing and grubbing vegetation above the shotcrete wall within 5 feet from 
the top edge of the wall. We also believe that the drainage swale (concrete ditch) is an 
effective means to reduce water infiltration behind the shotcrete facing. We recommend 
constructing a new concrete ditch along the top of the shotcrete wall to prevent water 
infiltration behind the shotcrete facing. Detailed construction requirements are presented 
in the following subsections. 
Pre-Construction 
Prior to starting the repair construction, the contractor should verify and delineate 
the repair sections by visually examining and sounding the concrete surface with a 
hammer or by other approved method. The repair sections should be clearly marked on 
the shotcrete facing with spray paint for the engineer’s inspection and concurrence. 
The contractor should remove concrete in the identified repair sections using 
shallow saw cut, pneumatic hammer, and/or chisels, as appropriate. The exposed 
concrete surface and/or fractured rock surface should be abrasive blasted to remove 
loose or weakened material/debris that could prevent or reduce shotcrete bond, prior to 
applying shotcrete. The edges of the patch should be square to the thickness of the patch 
with no feathering of edges. Long, narrow patching areas or sections of a patch should 
be avoided. The repair areas should be saturated with clean water the day before applying 
shotcrete and kept moist until the shotcrete placement. 
The time between removal of shotcrete section and applying shotcrete should not 
exceed 5 days. If the time allowance exceeds 5 days, the contractor should prepare the 
surface as the above, before applying shotcrete.

The shotcrete contractor’s nozzleman should have a minimum of 3 years shotcrete 
experience on similar size and type projects. The nozzleman should be certified by the 
American Concrete Institute (ACI). The experience description and certification should be 
submitted, including the name, address and telephone number of referenced projects, for 
the engineer’s review and approval. The nozzleman should maintain certification at all 
times while work is being performed for the emergency shotcrete repairs. 
The contractor should submit the concrete mix design with fiber reinforcement, 
associated with compressive strength testing results meeting the specified strength, for 
the engineer’s review and approval. 
Shotcrete 
The shotcrete should be placed from the lower part of the repair area upwards to 
prevent accumulation of rebound.  The nozzleman should maintain a position to orient 
the nozzle at a distance and approximately perpendicular to the working face, so that 
rebound will be minimal and compaction will be maximized. The rebound should be 
removed by blowing and raking off, to avoid sand pockets in the shotcrete. 
The nozzleman should maintain the water amount at a practicable minimum, so 
the mix properly adheres to the repair area. Water content should not become high 
enough to cause the mix to sag or fall from vertical or inclined surfaces, or to separate in 
horizontal layers. 
The shotcrete placement should be suspended in the event of high winds or heavy 
rain that may prevent proper application or cause cement washing out or mortar 
sloughing. 
The surface finish of the repair section should be applied similar to the existing 
adjacent shotcrete. The joint between the repair section and the existing shotcrete should 
be finished with proper floats and steel trowels. The contractor should check for sags, 
bridging, and other deficiencies as soon as the shotcrete is placed. 
Quality Control and Testing 
A test panel with dimensions of 18” x 18” x 6” should be prepared prior to 
production shotcrete placement for the engineer’s review and acceptance. The test panel 
should be prepared in a similar position as the emergency shotcrete repairs, to 
demonstrate the shotcrete is being applied properly and matching the existing shotcrete 
facing. The test panel should be stored on-site for reference. 
Three core samples with 3-inch diameter should be retrieved from the test panel 
7 days after placement. The cores should be inspected for delamination, sand pockets, 
and tested for unconfined compressive strength. If the unconfined compressive strength 
is less than 3,000 psi, the contractor should revise the concrete mix design and prepare 
another test panel for approval and testing at no additional cost to the State.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
two exploratory borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 
22.6 and 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface, respectively. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled with a track-mounted 
drill rig equipped with 4-inch OD solid-stem augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock materials were described in general accordance 
with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The 
Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods for the 
Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International Society 
for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the Standard 
Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.
