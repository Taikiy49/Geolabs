INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the slopes along Mokapu Saddle Road located between Mikiola Drive and
400 feet east of the centerline of Kahinani Way in the District of Kaneohe on the island
of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design associated with slope
stabilization which includes the design of retaining wall foundations GRP slopes
excavation and backfill placement only The findings and recommendations presented
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

Mikiola Drive to 400 feet east of the centerline of Kahinani Way The approximate slope
lengths to receive remedial work listed from west to east are 370 feet 290 feet and
400 feet respectively The slopes are generally at about a three fourths horizontal to
one vertical 4H 1V to 1H 1V slope inclination with slope heights ranging from 0 to

MSL at the slope on the west end to about 97 feet MSL at the slope on the east end

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age and forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lava
During the later Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles
of continental glaciation As the continental glaciers advanced less water was available
to fill the oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell to below its present stand When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea
levels rose to above its present level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and
fans of terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the
sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of the
upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high
energy conditions
In some of the mountainous regions of Hawaii chemical weathering has altered
the basaltic flows pyroclastic ejecta to a soil like condition Erosional processes
GEOLABS HAWAII
consisting of detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and transport
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium have also altered
the natural condition of the volcanic flows Once these materials reach the stream in the
central portion of valley alluvial processes become dominant and the sediments are
transported and deposited as alluvium
When alluvial colluvial deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical
processes begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or
weathering of the material Erosion continually deposits newer material covering the
older alluvial colluvial deposits Depending on the rate of transport the newer
sediments are generally transient in terms of geologic time
The project area is generally underlain by these older and more recent alluvial
deposits or by the soil like weathered basaltic flows pyroclastic ejecta Urban
development has since altered the natural formation of the area

Slope Stabilization Alternatives
A GRP lining on the slope face and up against the cutoff ditch was considered
However there was a concern that water may still undermine the cutoff ditch and
therefore pop out failures within the GRP slopes may occur Also due to the steep
slope water infiltration may lead to deeper slope failures
Other stabilization methods such as a Keystone retaining wall gravity retaining
wail or conventional cantilever retaining wall would require large excavation into the
steep slope which could further destabilize the slopes and the houses above the slope
Other alternatives such as placing a buttress or filling or cutting the slope to create a
GEOLABS HAWAII
flatter slope are not possible due to the area constraints Based on the above a
cantilever retaining wall with a reverse footing is recommended
Due to the existence of two catch basins which would conflict with the retaining
wall footings at Slope No 3 transitions to a GRP grouted rubble pavement also known
as a grouted rip rap is recommended at the catch basins Since the areas proposed for
the GRP are at the ends of this slope are of limited length and are currently in fair to good
condition we feel that construction of a GRP is a favorable solution
Due to the need for transitions from GRP to retaining wall use of veneer rock
facings on the retaining wall may be considered for aesthetic reasons It should be noted
that the thickness of the veneer facing will be about 2 to 2 inches
Retaining Wall Recommendations
The retaining walls should extend to the ends of the slope In the case of the
Slope No 3 the retaining wall should reside between the two catch basin structures
The eastern GRP should be extended to about 400 feet east of the centerline of
Kahinani Way Note that at 400 feet east of the centerline of Kahinani Way the bottom
of the cut slope exposes weathered rock material
An ultimate bearing pressure of up to 12 000 pounds per square foot psf may
be utilized to evaluate the extreme event limit state for the design of retaining wall
foundations bearing on the underlying stiff clayey silt material To evaluate the strength
limit state of foundations a resistance factor of 0 6 or a bearing pressure of up to
may be utilized
An allowable bearing pressure of 4 000 pounds per square foot psf may be
utilized to evaluate the service limit state for the design of retaining wall foundations
bearing on the underlying stiff clayey silt material This bearing value is for dead plus live
loads and may be increased by one third for transient loads such as those caused by

wind or seismic forces The tops of footings should be embedded a minimum of 4 feet
below the existing grade to allow for future development of the shoulder into an additional
travel lane
To minimize excavation into the slope the heel of the wall should not extend past
the stem by more than 2 feet However for instances where the slope height dictates
walls with toe lengths in excess of 8 feet beyond the stem the heel of the wall may be
increased to 4 feet beyond the stem Slope stability due to the additional cut is not
significantly reduced since the additional cut is limited in width
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements it should be
embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the
utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to
avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for foundation settlement
We estimate that footing settlements bearing on the underlying stiff clayey silt
material as recommended herein to be less than one half inch total Differential
settlement over a 20 foot length of wall is estimated to be less than one fourth inch If
soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of footing excavations the
footings should be deepened to expose the underlying firm materials
The service and strength limit states of the retaining wall should be designed for
the situation where the material over the toe of the wall is removed during construction of
the wall for an additional travel lane Therefore the wall should be designed for the
retaining wall condition for the full height of the wall The extreme event limit state of the
retaining wall may be designed for the fully constructed roadway condition

Retaining Wail Backfill
Backfill behind the retaining wali should consist of on site soils or select granular
fill material compacted to a minimum 90 percent relative compaction ASTM D 1557 91
Over compaction of the backfill should be avoided
Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the
existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations
should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in
accordance with the above fill placement recommendations
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope
ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face In the event that over cutting of a slope
occurs keying and benching requirements should be implemented instead of backfilling
the slope to the design grade with sliver fills Water should be diverted away from the
tops of slopes and slope planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the
potential for erosion of the finished slopes

Imported fill should consist of non expansive select granular material such as

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring and 4 to a depth of about 21 feet below
the existing ground elevation at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with auger
equipment
The subsurface conditions at the site were also explored by hand augering and
sampling one boring designated at Boring No 2 to a depth of about 3 4 feet below the
existing ground elevation at the appropriate location shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch O D Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of
an 18 to 24 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
One Atterberg Limits Test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of
this test is summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
representations of the test results are provided on Plate B 71
Two 1 inch ring swell tests were performed on remolded samples to evaluate the
swelling potential of the soils under surcharge loads These tests were run on air dried
samples Results of these swell tests are presented on Plate B 2
One Triaxial Unconsolidated Unconfined Compression test TXUU was performed
on a relatively undisturbed sample to evaluate the strength characteristics for retaining
wall design parameters Results of this TXUU test are presented on Plate B 3
Two direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate their peak and residual shear strength characteristics The test results and
shear stress versus normal stress plots are presented on Plates B 4 and B 5
