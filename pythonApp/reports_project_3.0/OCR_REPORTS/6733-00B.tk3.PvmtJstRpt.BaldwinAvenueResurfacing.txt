SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration along the proposed Baldwin Avenue Resurfacing project 
generally encountered hard to very hard clayey silts extending to a depth of approximately 
5.5 feet below the existing pavement surface.  We did not encounter groundwater in the 
shallow borings at the time of our field exploration. 
In general, the type and severity of the distresses exhibited in the pavement areas 
would determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction of the existing pavements should 
be performed. Existing pavements showing minor distresses and severity are generally 
rehabilitated with cold-planing and an AC overlay. Reconstruction of the existing 
pavements is usually performed for those areas where the observed distresses are likely 
caused by failures in the underlying base, subbase, and/or subgrade materials. 
Our pavement condition survey at the project site indicated that the majority of the 
pavements under consideration for resurfacing appear to exist in fair to good condition. 
Based on our engineering evaluation and construction considerations, we recommend 
rehabilitating these areas with a regular AC overlay consisting of cold-planing 2.5 inches of 
the existing AC surface and replacing with 2.5 inches of new AC overlay. 
Since the areas requiring reconstruction due to structural distress amount to more 
than 15 percent of the total pavement area proposed for rehabilitation, the pavement 
reconstruction should be designed based on a pavement life of 20 years in accordance 
with the guidelines presented in the pavement design manual prepared by the State of 
Hawaii - Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Material Testing and Research 
Branch. In general, the existing pavements may be reconstructed with a new pavement 
structural section consisting of 2.5 inches of AC on 4 inches of ACB over 6 inches of ASB.  
It should be noted that CBR tests and/or field observations should be performed on the 
actual subgrade soils during construction to confirm that the above pavement design 
section is adequate. 
We recommend placing a layer of reinforcing geogrid (such as Tensar TriAx Grid, 
TX140 or TX5 or equivalent) over the recompacted existing base/subbase/subgrade below 
the new pavement structural section. The reinforcing geogrid would serve to provide 
stabilization and reinforcement to the underlying base/subbase/subgrade materials.  We 
also recommend allocating a contingency fund for additional distressed areas requiring 
reconstruction that may develop between the time the construction plans are prepared and 
the actual commencement of the project construction.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This pavement justification report presents the results of our geotechnical 
engineering analyses performed for the Baldwin Avenue Resurfacing (Makawao Avenue 
to Haliimaile Road) project in the Makawao area on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The 
project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the rehabilitation and reconstruction of the existing pavements only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The roadway resurfacing project is along Baldwin Avenue between about 
Makawao Avenue and Haliimaile Road in the Makawao area on the Island of Maui, 
Hawaii. The approximate limits of the roadway resurfacing project are shown on the 
General Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The existing roadway within the project limits is a two-lane undivided roadway 
measuring approximately 2 miles.  Based on the information provided, we understand 
that it is desired to resurface the existing pavements with a new asphalt concrete 
overlay. However, a pavement condition survey conducted by our office indicated that 
some locations along the roadway alignment are in poor condition. These areas are 
experiencing pavement distresses consisting of rutting, depressions, and cracking. 
Therefore, it is our opinion that these areas will need to be reconstructed with a new 
pavement structural section. 
Based on the pavement design manual prepared by the State of Hawaii – 
Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Material Testing and Research 
Branch, new pavements for the localized or spot repair requiring reconstruction should 
be designed based on a design life of 10 years when the areas requiring repair because 
of structural distress amounts to less than 15 percent of the total pavement area

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes, the older West Maui 
(Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala (Pleistocene 
Epoch).  The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain located between these 
two volcanoes.  The project site is situated on the isthmus connecting the two volcanic 
domes. 
The isthmus was built by the deposition of sediments over the underlying basalts 
of the two volcanoes.  During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fell to stands lower than 
present sea level exposing large areas of coralline sand on what was the ocean bottom.  
Wind action transported these coralline sands inland forming extensive areas of sand 
dunes.  In some areas, these dunes have been mantled by man-made fill which was 
apparently placed during the period that the area was used for the cultivation of sugar 
cane. 
2.2 
Existing Site/Pavement Conditions 
The existing Baldwin Avenue within the project limits is generally a two-lane 
undivided roadway between Makawao Avenue and Haliimaile Road. The approximate 
limits of the roadway under consideration are shown on the General Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The shoulder areas beyond the edge of pavement generally consist of short cut 
grass, sidewalks at some locations in Makawao town, traffic signs and utility posts. The 
surface soils along the shoulder areas below the vegetation generally consist of brown 
clayey silts with some sand. The existing Baldwin Avenue roadway alignment generally 
slopes down from south to north throughout the project limits.  
Our office conducted a site reconnaissance in December 2012 to collect general 
information for the existing pavement conditions along the portion of the roadway under 
consideration. Detailed descriptions of our field surveys are presented in the following

3.4 
Rehabilitation of Existing Pavements 
In general, the type and severity of the distresses exhibited in the pavement 
areas would determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction of the existing 
pavements should be performed.  Existing pavements with minor distresses are 
generally rehabilitated with an AC overlay with cold-planing.  Based on our evaluation, it 
is our opinion that pavement areas with minor distresses may be rehabilitated with an 
AC overlay consisting of cold-planing 2.5 inches of the existing AC surface and 
replacing with 2.5 inches of new AC overlay. 
In general, the contractor should vacuum-sweep the pavement areas 
immediately after cold-planing to remove gravel-sized materials and dust not picked up 
by the cold-planing operation.  It should be noted that the vacuum-sweep should not 
preclude the dust control operation during cold-planing. In general, the contractor 
should begin paving no later than three working days after cold-planing begins.  Finally, 
vacuum sweeping of cold-planed surfaces should also be performed immediately prior 
to paving.

Design Pavement Section 
As previously discussed in the “Existing Site/Pavement Conditions” section 
herein, some of the observed distresses along portions of the roadway are likely caused 
by failures in the underlying base, subbase, and/or subgrade materials. In general, 
these areas should be reconstructed with a new pavement section in lieu of an 
AC overlay section. In addition, these distressed areas should be repaired prior to the 
AC overlay work for the entire roadway under consideration. Since the majority of the 
pavement areas in poor condition are confined to a certain stretch of Baldwin Avenue, it 
is our opinion that reconstruction of the entire Baldwin Avenue within the limits under 
consideration for this project is not necessary.

we recommend using the following flexible pavement design section for the proposed 
reconstruction areas as shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.5. Detailed 
calculations and analyses of the pavement design are presented on Plates C-3.1 and

the pavement subgrade preparation. Due to the relatively high moisture content of the

Aggregate subbase materials required for the project should consist of crushed 
basaltic aggregates and should conform to Subsection 703.17 of the State of Hawaii, 
Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction (2005).  Aggregate subbase 
material should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers 
to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry 
density of the same soil established in accordance with AASHTO T-180 or 
ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Asphalt concrete base (ACB) material should consist of asphalt-treated basalt 
aggregate, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and 
compacted to no less than 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity 
determined in accordance with AASHTO T-209 or ASTM D 2041. Asphaltic concrete 
(AC) material should be constructed in accordance with Section 401 Asphalt Concrete 
Pavement of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge 
Construction (2005) and its amendments. 
It should be noted that additional areas requiring structural repairs with new 
pavement sections may develop between the time the construction plans are prepared 
and the actual commencement of the project construction. Therefore, the road should 
be re-surveyed to evaluate whether new distressed areas have developed that may 
require reconstruction with new pavement sections. In addition, a contingency fund 
should be allocated in the construction budget for these possible extra costs.

3.6 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development 
of good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage 
structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration 
or premature failure of the pavements.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway alignment by drilling and 
sampling twelve borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 12. The borings extended 
to depths of about 4.5 to 5.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. The approximate 
boring locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.5. The borings were 
drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-12. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample.  Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the logs of borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Four Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits of the near-surface fine-grained soils. 
The test results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample 
depth. Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D 1557) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships.  The test results are presented on Plates B-2 and B-3. 
Two laboratory Resistance (R) Value tests (ASTM D 2844) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-4 and B-5.
