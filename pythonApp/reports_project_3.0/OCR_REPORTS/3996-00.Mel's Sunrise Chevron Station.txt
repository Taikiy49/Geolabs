INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed reconstruction of Mel s Sunrise Chevron Station located in
the Kapahulu area on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations site
grading and pavement sections only The findings and recommendations presented
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two primary
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island
including the leeward coast The younger shield volcano Koolau is estimated to be late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age Ice Age and forms the majority of the eastern two
thirds of the island including the area of Honolulu and the windward side of Oahu The
Waianae volcano became extinct while Koolau remained active therefore the eastern
flank of Waianae was partially buried beneath Koolau lavas as they coalesced and
banked against the Waianae Range
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels rose and fell in fluctuating
response to the cycles of worldwide continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and
advanced less free water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels
responded by falling below the present stands of the sea When the glaciers melted and
receded an excess of free water became available such that the sea levels responded by
rising to stands that were above the present sea level
During the evolution and erosion of Oahu river deltas and alluvial fans composed
of accumulations of terrigenous sediments were formed During elevated stands of the
sea these sedimentary accumulations were deposited at the elevated interfaces of
historic shorelines and bays In addition the high sea level stands created accumulations
of coral reef deposits at correspondingly high elevations and also provided for the
deposition of shallow lagoonal and marine sediments in newly formed quiet water
environments protected by outlying fringing reefs In contrast the lower sea level stands
caused streams to carve channels and valleys in the aforementioned sediments and reef
deposits Furthermore subaerial exposure of the marine sediments and calcareous
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3996 00 Page 5
materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic and lagoonal materials and induration of
the calcareous reef materials to occur
The processes discussed above have resulted in the occurrence of coral reef and
marine deposits located at subaerial elevations that are above the present day sea level
stand Likewise terrigenous sediments are also found today buried in off shore marine
environments As a result the stratigraphical interpretation of Oahu s coastal geology
can be complex due to the historic intermixing of marine and terrigenous materials in
environments where they would not otherwise be found Furthermore recent and on
going land development and reclamation continue to bring the island of Oahu to its
present form

Foundations
The proposed construction will consist of a new convenience store pump islands
with canopies and two underground fuel storage tanks Based on the subsurface
conditions encountered in our exploration we believe that a shallow foundation system
consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be used to support the proposed
structures An allowable bearing pressure of up to may be used for the design
of footings bearing on the compacted fill soils This allowable bearing pressure is for
dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as
temporary wind and or seismic forces Footings should be embedded a minimum of
18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade We estimate that total footing
settlements under the anticipated design loads to be on the order of less than 1 inch with
differential settlements of approximately 0 5 inches
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive
earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings
A coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used between the base of the footings and the
clayey soils encountered in our borings Resistance due to passive pressure may be
calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of
depth pcf This assumes that the soil around footings is well compacted Unless
covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below
finished grade should be neglected
We anticipate that the pump island canopies may be subjected to uplift forces
created by wind conditions Uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the
following 1 dead weight of the structure 2 weight of backfill material above the footing
where applicable and 3 side shear between the sides of the footings and surrounding
soils Contribution of dead weight from the backfill may be estimated using a unit weight
of 110 pcf For side shear between the sides of the footings and the adjacent soils a
shear value of 150 psf may be used
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3996 00 Page 10
Where footings are located adjacent to or on slopes the footings should be
embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal set back distance of at least
8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope Where
footings are located adjacent to below grade structures or utilities the footings should
extend to a depth below an imaginary 45 degree plane projected upward from the bottom
of the below grade structure or utility This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging
adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the
potential for foundation settlement
Footing excavations should not be allowed to dry before placing concrete If
shrinkage cracks appear in the footing excavations the soil should be thoroughly
moistened to close all cracks and recompacted to provide a firm base prior to concrete
placement
Footing excavations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii
prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to evaluate the competency of the
bearing materials and embedment depths If unsuitable materials are encountered in the
footing excavations these materials should be over excavated to expose firm soils and
replaced with compacted engineered fill
Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed
structures such as the convenience store pump islands and concrete pad overlying the
underground storage tanks The near surface fills encountered at the proposed site
consist of fill soils which exhibit slight to moderate expansion potential when subjected to
moisture fluctuations Therefore to reduce the potential for structural distress to lightly
loaded slabs we recommend that the subgrade soils be properly prepared and a
minimum of 6 inches of non expansive capping fill material be provided below concrete
slabs on grade The non expansive capping fill should extend a minimum of 2 feet
laterally beyond the edge of the slab
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3996 00 Page 11
The subgrades for slabs on grades should be scarified to a depth of at least
8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to
the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of
the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
The non expansive capping fill recommended below the floor slabs should consist
of imported granular material such as crushed coral basalt gravel or cinder sand The
material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in
largest dimension The material should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR
value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 It should contain between 10 and 30 percent
particles passing the No 200 sieve All select granular fill materials should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to at least
90 percent relative compaction The slab subgrade should be kept moist until covered by
cushion fill or base course
To provide more uniform support of interior slabs not subject to vehicular traffic
such as the convenience store we recommend that the concrete slabs on grade be
supported on a minimum 4 inch thick cushion fill above the capping fill layer The
cushion fill layer should consist of open graded gravels such as No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation which will also serve as a capillary moisture break The
slab cushion fill should be placed as soon as possible after the placement and
compaction of the capping fill as recommended above
For slabs on grade with moisture sensitive surfacing we recommend that an
impermeable membrane be placed over the cushion fill to reduce the potential for
migration of moisture vapor through the concrete slab In order to promote more uniform
GFO I ARS HAWAH
W O 3996 00 Page 12
curing of the slab concrete and to provide protection of the vapor membrane placement
of a 2 inch layer of moist fine sand above the membrane prior to placing the slab concrete
may be considered If the sand layer is desired the sand should be moistened slightly
prior to placing concrete Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should
be considered for the convenience store since they can better mask minor slab
cracking
Where concrete slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as the pump
islands and underground storage tank pads we recommend that the slabs be supported
on a 6 inch layer of select borrow subbase in lieu of the 4 inch cushion fill layer vapor
barrier and 2 inch sand The base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
For design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per
cubic inch pci may be used for the compacted base course The thickened edges of
slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at least 12 inches below the
lowest adjacent grade

consist primarily of passenger vehicles light pick up trucks and occasional heavy trucks
We have made our preliminary pavement design assuming the pavement subgrade soil
will be similar to the stiff to very stiff fill soils encountered in the borings Based on the
above assumptions we recommend the following pavement sections be used for
preliminary design purposes
Asphaltic Concrete Pavement
2 0 Inch Asphalt Concrete
6 0 Inch Basalt Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
6 0 Inch Basalt Subbase Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction

Portland Cement Concrete Pavement
6 0 Inch Portland Cement Concrete
6 0 Inch Select Borrow Subbase Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
12 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on a Moist Compacted Subgrade
The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction The subbase and base
course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction
Laboratory CBR tests should be performed on samples of the actual subgrade
soils encountered during construction to confirm the above recommended design section
The recommended pavement section assumes that good drainage will be provided for
areas adjacent to the pavements

Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry surface
water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after
construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the
pavement areas we recommend that the curbs extend a minimum of 2 inches into the
subgrade below the base and or subbase course to reduce the potential for migration of
landscape water into the pavement section Alternatively a subdrain system could be
constructed to collect excessive water from landscaping irrigation For long term
performance we recommend a subdrain system be constructed adjacent to paved and

Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines may be required for the proposed project In
general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding consisting
of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under the
pipes The backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of free draining
materials such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes
from compaction of the backfill
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finish grade should consist of the on site soils less than 6 inches in
maximum dimension or select granular material The backfill should be moisture
conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in maximum 8 inch level
loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to
reduce the potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement
areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction

Drainage
The finished grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations
and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent loss of strength and or heaving of the near surface silty and
clayey soils The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the
footings immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for water
infiltration
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain
surface run off away from the slabs and foundations
