SECTION 1.  
INTRODUCTION 
As part of the City Center Section of the Honolulu Rail Transit Project (HRTP), existing 
utilities along the proposed 4-mile-long guideway need to be relocated. We understand that the 
existing overhead 138 kV lines along Dillingham Boulevard will be relocated underground. At 
ten locations, the lines will transition from underground to overhead in which two of the ten poles 
will be standard poles. The different locations and poles are summarized below. 
1. 
Oahu Community Correctional Center – 1 Standard and 1 Riser Pole 
2. 
Mokauea Street – 1 Riser Pole 
3. 
Kalihi Street – 1 Riser Pole 
4. 
Kokea Street – 1 Standard and 1 Riser Pole 
5. 
Iwilei Substation – 4 Riser Poles 
We understand that the design of the pole structures will be performed by Hawaiian 
Electric Company (HECO). The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. 
The objective of the geotechnical exploration program is to collect information to 
characterize the geologic and hydrogeologic conditions near the planned HECO pole locations. The 
collected data will be used to support the design of the new pole structures.  
This Geotechnical Investigation Work Plan (GIWP) serves as a general plan for our field 
explorations, including drilling, sample storage and handling, and geotechnical laboratory testing 
conforming to the design approach presented in the Geotechnical Planning Report (GPR). Soil and 
rock samples will be obtained, and both field and laboratory tests will be performed. We 
understand that two of the borings maybe started at this time. The proposed boring locations are 
presented on the Proposed Boring Location Plan, Plate 2.  
Depending upon site access considerations and the presence of existing utilities, the 
exploratory borings generally will be accomplished within the limits of the proposed foundation 
footprint. In areas where this cannot readily be accomplished, Geolabs will request permission to

FIELD EXPLORATIONS 
The proposed exploration locations are presented on the Proposed Boring Location Plan, 
Plate 2. The explorations will be observed by field representatives from Geolabs. Each 
representative, who will generally be an engineer or geologist, will log the explorations and collect 
geotechnical soil and rock samples in a consistent manner. 
Explorations for the new HECO Steel Pole structures generally will meet or exceed the 
minimum requirements stipulated in Section 9.6.3 of the Compendium of Design Criteria. 
Depending upon site access issues and traffic control requirements, the schedule of completing 
the explorations will vary.  
2.1 
Summary of Explorations and Field Testing 
Based on the available geotechnical information for the project prepared by HART and our 
experience, the subsurface conditions at the proposed utility pole locations generally consist of the 
following deposits, extending to the maximum explored depth of approximately 301.5 feet below 
the existing ground surface.  
1. 
Fill materials consisting of medium dense to dense silty sands and gravel, and very 
stiff to hard silty clays and clayey silts. 
2. 
Recent alluvium consisting of medium stiff silty clays and medium dense clayey 
sands. 
3. 
Older alluvium consisting of medium stiff to very hard sandy clays and clayey silts, 
and medium dense to very dense silty to clayey sands with gravel. 
4. 
Marsh deposits consisting of very soft to soft organic clayey silts.  
5. 
Lagoonal deposits consisting of very soft sandy clays and silts, and very loose to 
medium dense silty sands. 
6. 
Coralline detritus consisting of loose to very dense silty sands, and silty to clayey 
gravel with cemented cobbles and boulders. 
7. 
Coralline sandstone that is severely to slightly fractured, soft to very hard.

2.2 
Drilling and Exploration Methods and Considerations 
The drilling equipment for the project would generally consist of a skid, track, or 
truck-mounted drill rig as well as portable drilling equipment, a flatbed support vehicle for supplies 
and equipment, a small-sized water truck (as necessary), and a pick-up truck for the 
engineer/geologist. The type of drilling method(s) used at each boring location will be selected 
based on the anticipated subsurface conditions from our knowledge and experience in the vicinity 
and will be adjusted/modified during the actual drilling operation.   
Borings will be advanced with a combination of 4-inch continuous-flight solid-stem augers 
and continued with PQ wireline coring methods to advance the PQ-size casing. In general, auger 
drilling will be performed in areas where stiff to hard cohesive soils (older alluvium) exist and in 
locations where rock coring is not anticipated and will be limited to above the water table. In areas 
where the soft recent alluvium and high groundwater levels are present and in locations where 
rock coring is anticipated, wash boring drilling techniques will be used.  
Drilling will be performed by Geolabs using equipment and personnel detailed in the 
previously provided Drilling Contractor Qualifications (January 26, 2022) prepared for this project. 
The efficiency of the hammer-rod-energy transfer for the various drill rigs used will be established 
through Pile Driving Analyzer (PDA) testing during the drilling and Standard Penetration Test (SPT) 
sampling operations using full length drill strings. Drill rig specific calibrations will be completed 
within one year of performing soil sampling.

2.2.1 Solid-Stem Auger 
Geolabs will use solid-stem auger (SSA) if auger drilling is accomplished. Solid-stem auger 
drilling incorporates 4-inch diameter continuous-flight augers that mechanically cut and 
remove soil from the borehole. Solid-stem auger produces a moderate amount of easily 
obtained cuttings, and little or no fluid is required in the drilling process. This type of 
drilling is usually limited to use above the water table and to about 100 feet, especially in 
dense or stiff soils. Split-barrel soil sampling operations are accomplished after the augers 
are removed from the hole. Drilling will switch over from SSA to PQ-size Wireline Coring at 
about the anticipated water table.  
2.2.2 PQ-Size Wireline Coring 
Borings accomplished by Geolabs typically will be advanced with a combination of 4-inch 
diameter continuous-flight solid-stem augers and continued with PQ-size wireline coring 
methods to advance the PQ-size casing. Sampling of the soils will be performed using the 
modified California spilt-barrel sampler (ASTM D3550), and the split-spoon sampler 
(ASTM D1586). In the softer soils, “relatively undisturbed” and “undisturbed” samples will 
be obtained for testing by driving a ring-lined modified California split-barrel sampler 
(ASTM D3550) and pushing a 3-inch outside diameter (OD) thin-walled steel Shelby tube 
(ASTM D1587) into the soil formation. 
Where the borehole will be advanced utilizing the PQ core barrel in the soil formations, 
samples of the soils recovered from the PQ core barrel also will be obtained and retained 
in core boxes for visual observation. In some cases, the borehole will be advanced utilizing 
the PQ casing only as a rotary wash drilling method; therefore, there will be no soil samples 
retrieved between the soil sampling points (soil sampling by the modified California 
split-barrel sampler or the split-spoon sampler for the Standard Penetration Test at 
maximum 10-foot intervals). The borings will be advanced into the rock formations with PQ 
wireline coring methods using a double tube/triple tube PQ rock core barrel. The PQ 
wireline coring methods will produce rock core samples that are 3.35 inches in diameter.

2.2.3 Pavement Coring and Patching 
For borings located in existing paved areas, the pavement will be cored prior to the drilling 
and sampling operations. The coring and patching will be accomplished in accordance with 
the permitting agencies’ requirements. The coring operations may be accomplished by the 
driller or by a specialty coring contractor, depending upon the type and thickness of the 
pavement. Upon completion of the coring and prior to the borehole drilling, the cored 
pavement will be covered with a secured steel plate to enable normal vehicle traffic to 
traverse the area. At the end of each workday and prior to the borehole completion, a 
steel casing about the size of the borehole with an oversized round steel plate welded at 
the top will be inserted into the unfinished borehole to allow vehicle traffic to traverse the 
area. Upon completion of the borehole drilling and sampling operations, the pavement will 
be patched in accordance with the requirements outlined in the street-use and excavation 
permits. 
2.2.4 Hand Excavation 
Due to the potential for utilities in the vicinity of each new utility pole location, the upper 
portion of each borehole may be hand excavated prior to drilling (to a maximum depth of 
about 5 to 7 feet) to confirm the absence of utilities prior to drilling the borings. At a 
minimum, hand excavation or potholing will be accomplished in accordance with the 
requirements of Article 3.05.B of Section 02 32 00 when the boring location is located 
within 3 feet of an (active or inactive) underground utility line. 
Hand excavation or potholing generally will not be performed in areas where toning and 
One-Call Center utility clearance verify that utilities do not exist, or if the utility owners 
indicate an absence of their utilities. If native soils are encountered in a hand excavation at 
less than 5 to 7 feet deep, drilling may commence provided that there are no adjacent 
utility locating paint marks that indicate the presence of directionally drilled or tunneled 
utilities. At selected locations, grab soil samples may be collected from the hand excavation 
hole using hand tools.

2.3 
Soil Sampling 
Soil sampling will be accomplished in the boreholes at 5-foot depth intervals. The sampling 
technique will consist of driving split-spoon samplers every 5-foot depth along the borehole. 
Thin-walled steel tube samples also may be retrieved instead of the disturbed driven samples. 
Depending upon the nature and consistency of the soils, particularly for the older alluvium, the 
thin-walled tube samples may be replaced with a modified California sampler fitted with internal 
rings or brass liners driven with a 140-pound hammer. Pocket penetrometer readings generally 
will be obtained and recorded by the field representative on retrieved cohesive soil samples where 
appropriate. Geolabs field representatives will observe the drilling, prepare a field log of the 
borings, collect soils samples, and transport the soil and/or rock samples to the sample storage 
facility. A duplicate sample for laboratory analysis may be collected and provided to HART, or their 
representative in the field when contamination is encountered or suspected. 
2.3.1 Grab Sampling 
Grab samples may be obtained from the soil cuttings and will be stored in plastic bags or 
jars, depending on the proposed geotechnical testing. 
2.3.2 Spilt-Spoon Sampling 
Split-spoon soil samples will be collected from each boring. The sample generally will be 
collected in association with the SPT. Soil samples also may be collected with a lined 
modified California split-barrel sampler using penetration test methods. 
Geotechnical soil samples will be collected from the split spoons and placed into plastic 
bags or jars. Multiple sample containers may be used for each sample, depending upon the 
nature and volume of the soil collected. The geotechnical samples will be stored in boxes 
or containers and transported to the sample storage facility. 
At a minimum, the samples will be transferred to the sample storage facility at the 
completion of each boring. In some instances, the rock core and soil samples will be 
transferred at the end of each drill shift.

2.3.2.a 
Standard Penetration Test Sampler 
Disturbed samples will be collected in association with the SPT using a 2-inch OD 
split-spoon sampler driven with a 140-pound hammer, free falling 30 inches. The 
sampler will be driven 18 inches with blows recorded for each 6 inches of 
penetration. If refusal is encountered, defined as 50 blows for 6 inches or less of 
penetration, a total of 100 blows are obtained, or 10 successive blows produce 
no advancement, the sampling operation will stop without advancing the full 
18 inches. At a minimum, as indicated in Article 3.07 of Section 02 32 00, SPT 
testing will be accomplished at not greater than 10-foot depth intervals along 
the borehole. 
2.3.2.b Modified California Sampler 
In granular soils that contain numerous gravels and cobbles, a modified 
California split sampler may be used to improve sample recovery. In addition to 
the coarse-grained soils, the modified California split sampler may be fitted with 
internal rings or brass liners and used in the hard, cohesive soils to collect soil 
samples for laboratory strength and consolidation testing. This generally would 
be accomplished if the cohesive material is hard and/or contains gravel such that 
using thin-walled tube sampling is not practical. 
The modified California sampler (3-inch OD, 2.42-inch inside diameter [ID]) will 
be driven with a 140-pound hammer, free falling 30 inches. The sampler will be 
driven 18 inches with blows recorded for each 6 inches of penetration. If refusal 
is encountered, defined as 75 blows for 6 inches or less of penetration, a total of 
150 blows are obtained, or 10 successive blows produce no advancement, the 
sampling operation will stop without advancing the full 18 inches.

Relatively undisturbed samples will be collected within the soft soil formation. Collection of 
Shelby tube samples (3-inch OD, 2.87-inch ID) will be attempted in the soft silt and clay 
recent alluvium, if encountered. In the very dense or hard older alluvium, samples will be

2.3.4 Borehole Designations and Sample Numbering 
We have established a borehole designation and a soil sample numbering system for 
consistency in the project. The borings will generally be designated with an alphanumeric 
label such as “B-X” where: 
B – designates the exploration as a boring 
X – designates the boring number (1- to 3-digits) 
Drive samples will be numbered in sequential order for each boring, from top down. When 
multiple samples are collected from a split spoon, the containers will be numbered with 
the sample number and then the container number (e.g., Sample SS-6, 1 of 3, 2 of 3, and 
3 of 3). If a geologic discontinuity is noted in a split spoon, then the different soil types will 
be collected in separate containers and labeled with “A” and “B” (e.g., Samples SS-6A and 
SS-6B). The following designations will be used and recorded on the field log depending 
upon the sample type retrieved.

2.4.2 Health and Safety 
Geolabs has prepared an in-house Site-Specific Health and Safety Plan (SSHSP) for use for 
the HRTP HART CCUR HECO Steel Pole Foundations project. All field personnel will read 
and sign the SSHSP prior to the start of the field exploration program. The drilling and 
excavation contractors will have their own health and safety standard operating 
procedures that will govern their work.

drill helpers, field engineers, and geologists. The purpose of this meeting will be to identify 
potential site-specific hazards and to remind personnel to be aware of their surroundings. 
Refer to the SSHSP for more information.

2.4.3 Potential Environmental Contamination

2.4.5 Noise Control 
We do not anticipate that the proposed fieldwork will exceed the noise levels established 
by the Honolulu Noise Control Ordinance (NCO). For boreholes located in residential areas, 
the driller will use noise insulating panels to mitigate noise levels. In addition, HART has 
applied for a noise variance to accomplish this work.  If noise complaints are received from 
the public during the field activities, any or all of the following measures may be used in an 
attempt to further control noise: 
Reduce drill rig motor revolutions per minute (RPM). 
Place additional portable plywood sound walls around the drill rig, where feasible. 
Insulate the portable sound wall with noise blankets. 
If complaints continue to be received after implementing the above measures, the field 
coordinator will develop additional mitigation techniques. Noise monitoring, if performed, 
will use the dB(A) weighted range to evaluate the effectiveness of the noise control 
measures. 
2.4.6 Drill Spoil Disposal 
Drill spoils generated by this work generally will consist of drill cuttings and drilling mud. 
Spoils generated from explorations will be placed in drums (or receptacles) and 
transported to a storage area for future disposal by Geolabs at an appropriate disposal 
facility. HART will be apprised of the final disposition of the drill spoils. Suspected or known 
contaminated material from field and laboratory screenings or as may become evident 
during the recycling/disposal process will be tested prior to disposal, based on disposal 
facility requirements. Geolabs will provide documentation of the final disposition of the 
drill spoils.

Soil and rock samples will be obtained from the explorations for geotechnical testing

3.1 
Geotechnical Testing 
Soil and rock samples collected for geotechnical testing will be stored in bags, jars, or 
tubes, and in core boxes, in the project storage facilities and at the testing laboratories. The goals 
of the laboratory testing program include the following: 
Evaluate, characterize, and assess the subsurface conditions relative to the 
foundation design of the project. 
Based on the results of the index testing performed on samples retrieved from 
explorations accomplished along the project corridor, develop a database of 
material properties that can be related to the results of the consolidation and 
strength tests. 
The laboratory testing program for the explorations completed at the drilled shaft 
locations will generally include the following: 
1. 
Water content determinations on selected soil samples retrieved, except wood or 
gravel soils, to provide index parameters useful for correlating soil behavior. 
2. 
Grain size analyses on selected granular samples to evaluate soil gradation and 
provide index parameters useful for correlating soil behavior. 
3. 
Atterberg limits determinations on selected clayey samples retrieved from the 
explorations. The tests will be performed to provide index parameters useful for 
correlating soil behavior. 
4. 
Corrosion tests on selected soil samples. This information is useful for design of 
utilities and other subsurface components. 
5. 
Unit weight and moisture determinations on selected tube and rock core samples. 
This information is an index parameter used for geotechnical characterization and 
design.

6. 
Swell and expansion tests on clay soils retrieved near the existing ground surface. 
These tests will provide information for evaluating the swell characteristics of the 
near-surface soils. 
7. 
Point load tests may be performed on selected rock cores retrieved from the 
explorations. These index test results will be useful for evaluating potential rock 
strengths that may be encountered in the drilled shaft construction. 
8. 
Uniaxial compressive strength (UCS) tests will be performed on selected rock core 
specimens. This information will be useful for evaluating rock strengths and unit 
weights for the design and construction of the drilled shafts. 
9. 
One-dimensional consolidation tests with time measurements will be performed 
on relatively undisturbed soil samples (preferably Shelby tube samples) to 
determine compressibility. 
10. 
Direct shear tests will be performed on selected relatively undisturbed samples to 
characterize the soil strength properties. 
11. 
Triaxial Consolidated-Undrained (TXCU) compression tests may be conducted on 
select relatively undisturbed and remolded samples to evaluate the strength and 
stress-strain relationships of the selected soil samples under consolidated 
undrained conditions. No TXCU tests are intended unless the deposit is thick 
enough to apply SHANSEP to determine shear strength with depth profile. 
Specimens will first be subjected to a confining fluid pressure in a triaxial chamber 
to achieve consolidated conditions. The specimen will then be sheared in 
compression at a constant rate of axial deformation (strain controlled). No drainage 
will be permitted during the shearing phase of the test yielding an undrained 
loading condition. Pore pressure measurements will be taken to evaluate the total 
and effective strength of the soil. 
12. 
Triaxial Unconsolidated-Undrained (TXUU) compression tests will be conducted on 
select relatively undisturbed and remolded samples to evaluate the strength and 
stress-strain relationships of the selected soil samples under unconsolidated 
undrained conditions. Specimens will first be subjected to confining fluid pressure 
in a triaxial chamber under unconsolidated-undrained (UU) conditions. Confining 
pressure will be calculated based on the effective overburden pressure above the 
soil specimen. The specimen will then be sheared in compression at a constant rate 
of axial deformation (strain controlled). No drainage will be permitted during the 
shearing phase of the test; instead, pore pressure measurements will be taken.  
Actual locations and test quantities will vary depending on the sample quantity and quality 
and soil/rock conditions encountered in each field exploration point. Tested soil samples will be 
disposed of based on normal soil testing laboratory protocol.

General Geologic Unit Description 
Fill

Fill placed by humans, frequently without proper compaction control. The fill 
material generally consists of clayey silts and sandy silts with stiff to hard

Ra 
Soil generally deposited by sedimentation in a very low energy environment such 
as an estuary or bay. Frequently, soils assigned to this unit accumulated very

Recent Alluvium is predominately fine-grained with organics, soft to very soft in

Detritus and

Formation 
Qls 
The coralline deposits unit is composed of fossil remnants of coral-algal reefs 
and their related detrital debris deposits. Detritus is the loose fragments or 
grains that have been broken away from the coral-algal reef. The coralline 
detritus deposits are weakly to strongly cemented coralline debris consisting of 
gravel, sand and silt-sized clastic fragments. Some of the coralline fragments 
were dissolved by fresh water flow and the solution cavities filled with cemented 
and/or uncemented fine sands and silts. These dissolution zones sometimes 
form “coral tubes”, which are local zones of high permeability or grout take. 
Older Alluvium 
Qa 
Older alluvium deposits were deposited in alluvial environments, buried over 
long periods of time by more recent deposits or desiccated to become 
overconsolidated, and then subsequently uncovered at the ground surface by 
erosion. They generally consist of sandy and silty clay, and clayey and sandy 
silt. Some of the streams which deposited these materials were high-energy 
streams and therefore large particles, ranging from gravel to cobbles to 
boulders, are encountered in the older alluvium. These coarse-grained parts of 
the unit may contain artesian groundwater conditions.

Honolulu 
Tuff 
Qht 
Volcanic tuff deposits associated with the cones of the Aliamanu and Makalapa 
craters of the Honolulu volcanic series. Tuff is a pyroclastic deposit, composed 
of consolidated volcanic ash. This tuff resembles siltstone or mudstone (mud 
rock) and is typically a competent rock, except where it has been weathered to 
saprolite. 
Koolau Basalt 
Tkb 
The Ko’olau Basalt consists of pahoehoe flows of basaltic lavas and may have 
a mantle of cobbles and boulders or residual soils overlying the top of the basalt 
rock formation. In addition, various types of volcanic rock sub-components, such 
as clinker layers and lava tubes, may also be encountered within the rock 
formation. As is characteristic of a tropical environment, the basalt is weathered 
in place to varying degrees. Subunits of the Ko’olau Basalt are residual soil, 
saprolite, and clinker. In addition, Ko'olau Basalt is separated into weathered 
(Tkb1) and unweatered (Tbk2) rock. 
Tkb1 
Weathered Tkb: Brown, red-brown, and yellow-brown, weak, highly to 
completely weathered, highly vesicular basalt. Core recoveries are generally 
less than 75 percent. 
Tkb2 
Unweathered Tkb: Brown, gray to light-gray, strong to very strong, fresh to 
moderately weathered, slightly vesicular to massive. Core recoveries are 
generally greater than 75 percent.
