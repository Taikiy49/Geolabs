SITE CONDITIONS
Surface
As shown by the enclosed Vicinity Map Plate 2 the proposed site
is located at the northeast corner of the Barbers Point Naval Air
Station The ground surface is essentially level varying between
elevations 32 in the southeast to elevation 38 in the northwest
Several abandoned roadways sidewalks and concrete slabs exist
throughout the area The eastern edge of the proposed site is
presently covered by sugar cane The remaining surface is gener
ally weed and brush covered with numerous Keawe trees
Numerous surface outcroppings of coral are present along with sev
eral surface depressions apparently resulting from the collapse of
near surface voids
Geology
The Ewa Plain encompassing Barbers Point Naval Air Station was
formed during a late stage Pleistocene Epoch of Oahu s Geologic
history The plain was formed by the combined constructional sedi
mentary processes of coral algal reef and alluvial plain formations
Stream sediments were washed into the Pearl Harbor embayment from
the Waianae and Koolau Volcanic Ranges as the region sank The
W Page 4
embayment may never have been very deep and it probably filled as
it sank The shallow water origin of the area is evidenced by the
presence of shallow water coral algal reefs similar to those form
ing in Kaneohe Bay now These reefs apparently grew during the war
mer interglacial stages of the Ice Age when eustatic sea level rose
above its present height During the glacial stages sea level de
clined due to the accumulation of water in the glaciers
A late stage relative lowering of the sea level has left the reef
exposed This exposured the reef to rainwater mixed with rotting
vegetation which formed slightly acid groundwater This acid
groundwater is probably responsible for the voids or pukas and
openwork a lattice like appearance of the reef formations by
enlargement of natural reef cavities
Subsurface Explorations
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored in two phases
The initial phase consisted of preliminary geologic reconnaissance
and a subsurface explorations program of seven borings each appro
ximately 45 feet deep During the second phase a more detailed
subsurface investigation was undertaken and consisted of 21 borings
with depths varying from 15 to 50 feet
The drilling was accomplished using truck mounted rotary drilling
equipment with continuous flight augers and NX core barrel with dia
mond bits The borings were continuously inspected by an engineer
or geologist from our firm The boring locations for both phases
are shown on the Site Plan and Boring Locations Plate 1
W 0 Page 1975
In all borings either a standard penetration D ring
samples or rock cores were taken at selected intervals The core
barrel was used when the drilling became too hard for the conven
tional augers
The standard penetration test consisted of driving a 2 inch 0 D
split spoon sampler 18 inches with a 140 pound hammer free falling
at a distance of 30 inches The number of blows required to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches is termed the standard penetration
resistance N and is an approximate measure of the relative den
sity or consistency of the soils The N values are shown at the
appropriate locations on the boring logs
The soil samples obtained from the subsurface explorations were
Classified in the field by visual and textural examinations The
samples were then returned to our laboratory for additional test
ing and confirmation of the visual classifications All soils
were classified in accordance with the Unified Soil Classification
System A graphic representation of the soils and rocks encountered
is presented on the Boring Logs Plates A 1 thru A 27
Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface explorations disclosed that the site is generally
underlain by hard to medium hard coralline algae coral with lightly
cemented coral sands and shells to 50 feet the limit of our explora
tions Some thin layers of soil generally less than two feet in
thickness were encountered near the surfaces This soil consist of
brown clayey silt which varied from medium stiff to stiff in con
sistency
W Page 1975
As previously discussed the coral beneath the site was part of a
reef formation Because of the variability of the reef environment
the coral is not a uniform deposit The near surface reef materials
are generally relatively hard because of solution and redeposition
of calcium carbonate by evaporating capillary water The exposure
of the reef to the slightly acid rainwater from rotting vegetation
can produce a lattice like appearance to the reef by dissolving and
removing portions of the calcium from the reef
In addition to the weathering of the feef formation the basic growth
pattern is somewhat erratic and produces numerous natural voids
which are often filled with sands and reef debris These materials
may or may not be cemented

CONCLUSIONS RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The predominate foundation material at the site is the coral reef
formation which will provide adequate support for the proposed
structures Where the coral contains voids the bearing capacity
is essentially reduced to zero since the collapse of even a 3 inch
void could produce a corresponding 3 inch settlement of the foun
dation
Due to the erratic nature of the voids their position and extent
cannot economically be defined by a subsurface exploration program
W O 415 10 Page 1975
It is therefore necessary to include within the construction pro
cedures methods for assuring that these voids do not occur at cri
tical locations or within minimum depths beneath the foundations
Included within this report are recommended procedures for locating
and filling the voids Once these voids are eliminated from beneath
the foundations a high bearing capacity can be utilized The recom
mendations given in the following paragraphs are based upon con
struction procedures which will fill the voids located in critical
areas
Foundations
It is recommended that spread and line foundations be designed for
an allowable bearing capacity of bearing upon the natural
soils Where fill will be placed beneath the structures it is re
commended that the foundations be extended through the fill to the
nature soils or be designed for maximum contact bearing pressures
of Based upon these pressures the estimated maximum
settlements are 0 7 inch total and 0 4 inch differential
To utilize these bearing pressures it will be necessary to under
take a combined probing and filling program to eliminate voids be
neath the foundations The recommended procedures are given in
Appendix D Suggested Specification for Locating and Filling of
Subsurface Voids
W O 415 10 Page 1975
The probing would utilize small diameter holes at five foot centers
to locate voids beneath the foundations The probes should extend
a minimum of five feet below the foundation subgrade for foundations
designed for Should foundations designed for
be utilized the probe depth can be reduced to four feet
Voids located by the probing should be filled with lean concrete
or cement grout utilizing 4 inch diameter injection holes Voids
uncovered by the excavations should be filled with compacted gra
nular materials
Where mat foundations will be utilized for foundation support the
mats should be designed to span a five foot diameter area where
loss of support may occur
Floor Slabs Paving
To provide even support for slabs on grade or pavement the existing
surface should be scarified to a depth of 6 inches moistened if
necessary and compacted to 90 of the maximum dry density established
by American Society for Testing and Materials ASTM Test D1557 72
The coral at the site with a CBR value of 25 will provide excellent
subgrade support for the proposed pavements when compacted to a mini
mum of 90 of the maximum density The pavement should consist of
2 inches of asphalt and 6 inches of base course In areas subject
to heavy traffic or frequent truck loadings the asphalt pavement
W O 415 10 Page 1975
thickness should be increased to 24 inches In non coral areas the
same pavement thickness can be utilized if twelve inches of sub base
material such as coral is used
Lateral Earth Pressures
Basement and retaining walls with horizontal backfill should be de
signed for equivalent fluid pressures of 35 pef providing granular
backfill is utilized The granular backfill should not be compacted
in excess of 95 of the maximum density to avoid developing wedging
forces A frictional factor of 0 55 can be utilized to evaluate
the sliding resistance of the foundations
Surcharges due to sloping backfill adajacent footings hydrostatic
pressure construction equipment etc must be considered in the
design of the retaining structures
Stability of Slopes
The on site coral reef materials can be cut at nearly vertical slopes
The coral does contain localized pockets of sand and uncemented mate
rial which will not stand at these vertical cuts It will be neces
sary to remove these materials during the excavation operations
They should be of limited extent and will not materially affect the

Slopes of 1 horizontal to 1 vertical 1 to 1 Permanent cut
slopes into the cemented materials can be cut at slopes of 1 to l

Excavation Problems
The coral contains zones of hard ledge coral which may be difficult
to excavate in confined areas such as utility trenches or foundation
excavations While the material may be ripped in large excavations
it is anticipated that special excavation methods such as jack ham
mers or blasting will be required in confined excavations
Site Preparation
In all areas to receive fill or structures the surface vegetation
and cohesive soil should be removed and wasted The exposed coral
surface should then be thoroughly ripped with a D 9 dozer or equi
Ce a
valent to uncover near surface voids The surface material
should then be moisten as required and recompacted to 90 of the
maximum dry density Due to the limited amount of on site cohesive
soils and the difficulty of compacting them when wet it is recom
mended that they not be utilized as fill

Inspection During Construction
In order to utilize the recommended bearing pressures of 6 000 pounds
per square foot it is essential that the near surface voids and voids
at critical locations be located and corrected The probing and f ill
ing operations should be inspected on a full time basis by an engi
neer from our firm who is familar with the subsurface conditions at
the site
Where the fill will be utilized for structural or pavement support the
fill placement should be monitored on a full time basis to assure
that the recommended degree of compaction is consistently achieved
