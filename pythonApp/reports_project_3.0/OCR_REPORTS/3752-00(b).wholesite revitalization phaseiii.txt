INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Wholesite Revitalization Phase III project located within the
Pearl City Peninsula Naval Housing facility on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general
location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations site
preparation and pavements only The findings and recommendations presented herein
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield volcano Koolau forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct
while Koolau was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in
central Oahu
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3752 00 B Page 5
The project site is located on the southern side of Pearl City Peninsula in Pearl
Harbor on the south central coastal plain of Oahu The geologic conditions underlying
the Pearl Harbor lochs were the result of a complex series of depositional and erosional
events due to glacio eustatic sea level changes and post erosional volcanic activities
during the Pleistocene Epoch
The Koolau volcanic shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly
bedded basaltic lava flows Following the cessation of activity of the Koolau Volcano
alluvial materials were deposited in an estuarine and deltaic environment in the area of
Pearl Harbor The sea level fluctuated several times receding perhaps 100 meters below
and rising about 30 meters above the present sea level As Oahu sank coral reef grew
around the shorelines keeping pace with the subsidence of the island
During the Recent Epoch as sea level rose to its current stand the Middle Loch
area was covered with additional sediments consisting primarily of cohesive soils with
localized granular soils over the underlying alluvium Land development and reclamation
projects in the past 40 to 60 years have added man made fills along the shoreline
bringing the site to its present configuration

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling twelve borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 12 extending to a depth of
about 3 5 meters below the existing ground surface Two bulk samples designated as
Bulk 1 and Bulk 2 of the near surface soils were obtained to evaluate their physical and
pavement support characteristics The approximate locations of the borings and
near surface bulk samples are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general the majority of our borings throughout the project site encountered
surficial fills and alluvial deposits consisting of very stiff to hard silty clays extending to a
depth of approximately 3 5 meters below the existing ground surface Toward the
southwestern portion of the project site our test borings encountered a thin surficial fill
layer of clayey soils approximately 0 3 to 0 8 meters thick overlying medium dense to
dense coralline deposit of sandy gravel and silty sands

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by very
stiff to hard clayey soils and medium dense to dense coralline deposits at shallow depths
Our laboratory tests indicated that the near surface clayey soils generally exhibit
moderate to high shrink swell expansion when subjected to fluctuations in the soil
moisture contents Based on the generally stiff subsoil conditions encountered at the
project site we envision that slab on grade foundations with thickened edge footings may
be used to support the proposed new additions anticipated for the subject project
However in order to reduce the potential for structural distress to the new slabs
resulting from swelling of the on site expansive soils we recommend that non expansive
select granular fill material be provided for a minimum depth of 300 millimeters below the
slab cushion The perimeter thickened edges of the new slabs should be embedded a
minimum of 500 millimeters below the lowest adjacent exterior grade In addition special
attention should be given to the recommended procedures for subgrade preparation of
slabs and pavements to reduce the potential shrink swell effects of the near surface soils
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering design
recommendations are presented in the following sections of this report

Slab On Grade Foundations
In general we envision that slab on grade foundations with thickened edge
footings may be used to support the proposed new additions anticipated for the subject
project As previously indicated the project site is generally underlain by moderately to
highly expansive clayey soils Unless slabs on grade supported by these expansive soils
are properly designed there is a potential for future distress to the residential structures
resulting from wetting and subsequent swelling of the expansive clayey soils
To reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from swelling of the on site
expansive soils we recommend that non expansive select granular fill material be
provided for a minimum depth of 300 millimeters below the slab cushion The perimeter
thickened edges of the slabs should be embedded a minimum of 500 millimeters below
the lowest adjacent exterior grade The base of the thickened edge should have a
minimum width of 300 millimeters Graphic illustration of the recommended foundation
type is presented on Plate 3 Slab On Grade on Expansive Soil of this report
An allowable bearing pressure of up to may be used for the
design of shallow foundations bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or engineered fill
material This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third
for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces Lateral loads acting
on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance developed between the bottom
of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure acting against the
near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of friction of 0 3 may be used
for footings bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or engineered fill materials
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 50 kilonewtons per square meter per meter of depth kN cu m This
assumes that the soil around footings is well compacted or undisturbed Unless covered
by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 300 millimeters of soil should
be neglected
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3752 00 B Page 9
It is recommended that the bottom of all footings bear on the stiff or dense
near surface soils The bottom of the footing excavations should be recompacted to at
least 90 percent relative compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing
surface prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete If soft and or loose materials
are encountered at the bottom of footing excavations they should be over excavated to
expose firm materials The over excavation should be backfilled with granular material
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom should
be extended down to the underlying competent material Relative compaction refers to
the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of
the same soil established in accordance with procedures described in ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement If foundations are designed in accordance with our recommendations we
estimate total settlements to be less than 25 millimeters with differential settlements on
the order of 15 millimeters
Prior to placement of the select granular fill the clayey subgrades should be
scarified to a minimum depth of 200 millimeters moisture conditioned to at least
2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction The select granular fill material should provide a capping layer of
moist soil above the underlying potentially expansive subgrade soil thereby reducing the
loss of moisture from the clayey subgrade soils In our opinion the reduction of drying
and subsequent wetting of the subgrade soils should reduce the impact of shrink swell
effects on the slab caused by potential heaving of the underlying expansive clayey soils
when wetted The select granular fill should be moisture conditioned placed in level
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3752 00 B Page 10
loose lifts not exceeding 200 mm and compacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction
In general all new interior slabs should be supported on a minimum 100 mm thick
layer of cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation or
Basaltic Termite Barrier BTB To reduce moisture infiltration a plastic vapor barrier
should be placed above the cushion fill A 50 mm thick moist fine sand layer may be
considered above the plastic sheet to aid in the curing of the concrete Flexible floor
coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered since they can better
mask any minor slab cracking It is also recommended that interior walls be designed to
incorporate some flexibility to accommodate some amount of possible ground
movements
Where the slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as the carport slabs a
150 mm layer of base course is recommended in lieu of the 100 mm cushion fill layer
vapor barrier and 50 mm sand layer The base course should consist of crushed basalt
aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction The 300 mm
select granular fill layer recommended for slab on grade construction should also be
provided for the carport slabs
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project We
recommend that a minimum 300 mm cushion layer consisting of the select granular fill
material be provided below the concrete slab The select granular fill cushion layer
should be compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction Construction joints
should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at
right angle intersections
Areas adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly against the slab with
non expansive impervious soil and graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce
the potential for water ponding around the foundations

Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and the proposed finish grades minimal site
grading work will be required for the construction of new improvements at the subject site
In general the area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and
grubbed at the on set of earthwork All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of off site
Slabs and foundations of the existing structures that are to be demolished should
be completely removed Any over excavation resulting from the demolition should be
backfilled with compacted granular fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned should
be removed and the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with select
granular fill material moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in
200 mm loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Utilities that are to be abandoned in place under the proposed structures should be
backfilled by pumping lean concrete under low pressure
After demolition clearing and grubbing all areas beneath future new slabs should
be over excavated to provide at least 300 millimeters of compacted non expansive select
granular fill material below the bottom of the slab cushion If zones of soft loose or
saturated soils are encountered at the over excavated subgrade level further
over excavation may be required to expose the underlying firm material The resulting
over excavation should be backfilled with compacted non expansive select granular
material
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3752 00 B Page 12
The over excavated subgrade surface should be scarified to a minimum depth of
200 millimeters moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91
test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
Slab subgrades should be kept moist and free of shrinkage cracks until covered by
slab cushion fill or concrete Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the
subgrade we recommend that the soil be re scarified and prepared as recommended
above at no additional cost to the Owner Saturation and subsequent yielding of the
exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted engineered fill at
no additional cost to the Owner
Non expansive select granular fill material required under the slab areas should
consist of imported granular material such as select borrow crushed coral or basalt
gravel The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles greater
than 75 millimeters in largest dimension The material should have a laboratory California
Bearing Ratio CBR value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when
tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 It should also contain less
than 30 percent passing the No 200 sieve Any imported fill material should
be tested and approved by Geolabs Hawaii prior to delivery to the project site for its
intended use
Non expansive select granular fill material required for the capping layer below the
new sidewalks and floor slabs should be moisture conditioned placed in level lifts not
exceeding 200 millimeters in loose thickness and compacted to not less than 90 percent
relative compaction

Pavement Design
Asphalt concrete pavements are anticipated for the new driveway and parking
areas at the subject site The anticipated subgrade soils for the proposed new
pavements as encountered in our borings generally consist of the stiff clayey soils
Based on our field exploration and laboratory testing the anticipated subgrade soils
generally exhibit moderate to high shrink swell potential when subjected to moisture
fluctuations Therefore we recommend that the subgrade soils be properly prepared and
capped with a minimum 300 millimeters of select borrow subbase fill to reduce the
potential for distress resulting from the swelling of the potentially expansive subgrade
soils Considering the light to medium traffic anticipated for the proposed roadways we
recommend the following preliminary pavement design for budgeting purposes

Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines such as sewer and water lines may be required
for the project In general a granular bedding consisting of 150 millimeters of No 3B
Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel should be used for the
trench backfill up to about 300 millimeters above the pipes to provide adequate support
around the pipes and to reduce compaction of the backfill thus reducing the potential for
damaging the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 300 millimeters above the

materials The on site clayey soils may be used as trench backfill in landscape areas

relative compaction

Existing Underground Utilities
Existing underground utility lines may traverse the subject housing development
Therefore we recommend that all existing utilities within the project site be accurately
located by the contractor prior to construction The contractor has the sole responsibility
to devise and implement an effective program to protect the utilities during construction
Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the resulting
excavation should be _ properly backfilled with select granular fill material
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in 200 mm level loose lifts
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Utilities that are to be
abandoned in place under the proposed structures should be backfilled by pumping lean
concrete under low pressure
Drainage
The finished grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations
and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of our
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be
properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as
possible and should be maintained to drain all surface water run off away from the slab
and foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
twelve borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 12 to a depth of about 3 5 meters
below the existing ground surface using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight augers The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 12

ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
California sampler with a 63 5 kilogram hammer falling 760 millimeters The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the last 300 millimeters of a 450 mm drive are shown on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
