INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed renovations and addition to existing structure for the Hilo Hattie Beach Walk
project in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for
the proposed project These recommendations are intended for the design of the new
building foundation and site grading only The findings and recommendations presented
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed Renovations and Addition to Existing Structure for the Hilo Hattie Beach
Walk project is located at the western corner of the intersection of Kalakaua Avenue and
Beach Walk Avenue in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 Based on the
information provided an additional one story building with a partial 2 level will be
constructed adjacent to the existing building which will be renovated The new building will
be constructed at the site where an old building was recently demolished The new building
will include approximately 5 800 square feet of area with about 2 200 square feet of
additional area on the 2 floor
We understand the existing footings will not be used for the new building foundation
due to poor conditions exposed during our field exploration and will be removed The new
building will be supported by precast concrete driven piles Therefore we anticipate that
some over excavation of the existing footings and backfill will be necessary We also
anticipate that over excavation backfilling and raising the final grading elevation will
commence prior to the pile driving operation in order to minimize the potential for downdrag
loading on the pile foundation

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the
western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern
two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still
active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in Central Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of Koolau and its geomorphology and
subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea level
during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain The
subsurface conditions in the Waikiki area are dominated by fossil Pleistocene Age coral algal
reef deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of the sea which are intercalated
with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic section in this area is composed
of Koolau Basalt
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes as
a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world These glacio eustatic
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower relative to present
sea level on Oahu About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred
During that rise the valleys in the project area were drowned In the last 10 000 years or so
the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine sediments have
continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in thick
deposits of loose and or soft sediments along the coast in the areas that were formerly
valleys and drainageways
In the early part of this century the Waikiki area consisted of low marshy areas As
the City of Honolulu grew and the Waikiki area was urbanized man made fills were placed

W O 4633 00 Page 5
to reclaim the marshy areas and lagoons The fill was placed upon lagoonal deposits
consisting of silty sands with coral fragments The lower clayey material was deposited
during periodic influxes of non marine alluvial debris which apparently smothered the coral
reef development Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years
have brought the Waikiki area to its present form Many of the resulting fills are of poor
quality in terms of supporting heavy structural loads
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is located near the western corner of the intersection of Kalakaua
Avenue and Beach Walk Avenue in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project site
is generally level and rectangular in shape The site is bounded by Beach Walk Avenue to
the east Hawaiiana Hotel to the south two existing parking structures to the west and an
existing building to the north as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on information provided the project site was previously occupied by a
three story building which was recently demolished Based on our field observations the

building The existing ground surface elevations at the site generally range from
approximately 4 to 5 feet MSL
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
one boring extending to the depth of about 74 feet below the existing ground surface near
the middle of the proposed building area The approximate location of the boring is shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our boring encountered 5 feet of medium dense stiff surficial fill materials
consisting of silty gravel and silty clay with some coral gravel The surficial fill was underlain
by soft loose compressible lagoonal deposits extending to a depth of 38 feet below the

and silty coral gravel Medium dense stiff alluvium soils were encountered below the

the existing ground surface The alluvium consisted of mixtures of silty sand silty clay and
sandy silt It should be noted that a thin layer of cobble boulder about 1 5 feet thick was

Groundwater was encountered at a depth of about 4 5 feet below the existing ground
surface in the boring at the time of our field exploration However it should be noted that
groundwater conditions at the site could change due to variations in rainfall groundwater
withdrawal construction activities and or other factors that were not apparent at the time of
our field exploration

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered 5 feet of surficial fill over 33 feet of
soft loose lagoonal deposits underlain by 25 feet of stiff medium dense alluvium soils and
highly weathered coral formation extending to maximum depth drilled of approximately
74 feet below the existing ground surface at the project site The soft loose lagoonal
deposits may be considered compressible when subjected to structural loads
Based on our field exploration and engineering analyses we recommend that a deep
foundation system consisting of 12 inch square or 16 5 inch octagonal precast prestressed
concrete piles be used for foundation support of the new building An allowable compressive
load capacity of 70 kips may be used for the foundation design of the recommended piles
The driven pile should extend through soft loose lagoonal deposits and derive support
primarily from skin friction in the alluvium and partially from end bearing in the
highly weathered coral formation Estimated pile embedment depths on the order of about

the ground surface are anticipated

Pile Foundations
Based on the current design concept we envision that driven pile foundations will be
used for support of the proposed building addition In general we recommend that 12 inch
square or 16 5 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles be used to support the
proposed building Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site we believe
that the piles would extend through the soft loose compressible lagoonal deposits and derive
support primarily from skin friction in the stiff medium dense alluvium and partially from
end bearing in the highly weathered coral formation
An allowable compressive load capacity of 70 kips per pile may be used for the
foundation design of the new building The allowable compressive load capacity for the piles
is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as
wind or seismic forces To achieve the allowable compressive load capacity we recommend
that estimated pile lengths on the order of about 70 and 62 feet be used for the 12 inch
square and 16 5 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles for preliminary cost
estimating purposes Due to possible variation in the length of the piles unit prices should
be obtained during bidding for add ons shorter piles etc
Piles should be spaced a minimum of 2 5 times the width of the pile center to center
to avoid reduction in vertical capacity due to group action and to facilitate pile driving

Pile Foundation Settlements
Settlement of the pile foundations will result primarily from elastic compression of the
pile member and the subgrade response We estimate that the total settlement of the
pile supported foundation to be less than 0 5 inches Differential settlements between
adjacent piles should not exceed about one half of the total foundation settlement
We believe that these settlements are essentially elastic and should occur as the
loads are applied
Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a rated energy of
about 39 000 foot pounds per blow The hammer should be equipped with an energy
control level such that driving within the soft soils may be controlled at a lower energy
to reduce tensile stress development within the piles which could damage or cause
cracking of the piles
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the matching of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be
driven This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy to
the top of the pile and also reducing the possibility of damage to the piles Geolabs
should review the pile driving equipment proposed to evaluate whether the equipment
is capable of installing the piles to the recommended capacity
The contractor should forward pile and driving equipment data forms for review prior
to construction for evaluation Geolabs should review the submittal and assess the
driveability of piles with the use of the selected hammer A wave equation analysis
should be performed after selection of the pile driving equipment to assess the
driveability of the piles and pile driving criteria Due to possible variation in the length
of the piles unit prices should be obtained during bidding for add ons shorter piles

Pile Driving Criteria
For preliminary design of the 12 inch square and 16 5 inch octagonal precast
prestressed concrete piles pile driving may be terminated when the aggregated blow
counts are at least 3 blows per inch for the last 12 inches or 8 blows per inch for the
last 4 inches The preliminary driving criteria should be confirmed and or modified
after selection of the pile driving equipment and performance of a wave equation
analysis In addition the pile driving criteria may be modified during the pile driving
operation to accommodate variations of soil conditions in the field
Each pile should be driven continuously without interruption A pile may be rejected
or its design capacity may be reduced when the driving resistance is interrupted for
more than 4 hours and the pile cannot be driven to the required depth During soft
driving such as when the pile tip is within soft soils the hammer stroke or energy
should be carefully controlled such that significant tensile stresses do not develop
within the pile
Because variations in soil strength characteristics are likely to exist in the bearing
soils the supporting capacity of each pile should be confirmed in the field by a
representative from our office during the driving operation It should be noted that the
pile design was developed from our pile analysis using the field exploration data

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared Vegetation debris and other unsuitable materials should be
removed and disposed of properly off site to reduce the potential for contamination
of the excavated materials If soft or wet soils are encountered during clearing and
grubbing over excavation should be required to remove the soft or wet soils to
expose firm soils The resulting over excavation should be backfilled with compacted
select granular fill material
Slabs and foundations of existing structures to be demolished should be completely
removed Over excavation resulting from the demolition should be backfilled with lean
concrete or compacted select granular fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned
should be removed and the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with
select granular fill material placed in 8 inch loose lifts and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction Utilities to be abandoned in place under the
proposed structures should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete under low
pressure
After demolition clearing and grubbing the exposed subgrade should be scarified
to a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The
compaction requirement for finished subgrades under areas subjected to vehicular
traffic should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry

weight corresponding to the maximum dry density It should be noted that use of the
vibratory action of the compaction equipment should be closely monitored to reduce
the potential for pumping of the subgrade due to shallow groundwater conditions
Fills and Backfills
In general the excavated on site surface fills may be re used as a source of general
fill and backfill materials provided that they contain no lumps or fragments greater
than 3 inches in maximum dimension The soft and wet clayey and silty soils
generated from the deeper excavations should not be re used as fill and backfill
materials and should be disposed of properly off site
Where imported fill materials are required as general fill and backfill materials the
imported fill and or backfill materials should consist of well graded granular materials
with a maximum particle size of 3 inches or less The materials should have a
laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 20 or more and should have a
maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM Tesi
Designation D 1883 In addition the materials should contain between 10 and
30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve Imported fill material should be tested
by Geolabs for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the
project site for the intended use
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
The fill and backfill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in
loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The fill and backfill
materials within the upper 2 feet below the finish subgrade in pavement areas should
be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction The pavement
finished subgrade should be kept moist until covered by the aggregate base course

Observations and soil density tests should be performed during grading to assist the
contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper moisture
content on each fill lift Where compaction is less than required additional
compaction effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture content as
necessary to obtain the specified compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the structure should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems
be installed around the building structure and that the discharge be diverted away from the
foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and
slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have
concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
on grade structures since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent heaving or loss of strength The building excavations should be properly
backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce
the potential for water infiltration In addition drainage swales should be provided as soon
as possible and should be maintained to drain surface water run off away from slabs and
foundations

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling one
boring designated as Boring No 1 extending to a depth about 74 feet below the existing
ground surface The boring was drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight solid stem augers The boring was drilled at the approximate locations
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the boring were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD
Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as
the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Three sieve analyses ASTM C 117 C 136 were performed on selected samples
of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on Plate B 1
One direct shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed to evaluate the shear strength
characteristics of the materials sampled Results of the direct shear tests are presented on
Plate B 2
