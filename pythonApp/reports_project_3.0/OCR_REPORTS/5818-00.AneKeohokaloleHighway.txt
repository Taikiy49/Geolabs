1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our Preliminary Geotechnical Engineering
Evaluation performed for the proposed Ane Keohokalole Highway in the District of North
Kona on the Island of Hawaii The project location and general vicinity are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1 This report summarizes the findings from our site
reconnaissance and presents our geotechnical recommendations for design of the
proposed project
The recommendations provided herein are intended for preliminary design of site
grading pavements and underground utility lines only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
1 2 Project Considerations
The Ane Keohokalole Highway project site is in the Kailua Kona area in the
District of North Kona on the Island of Hawaii The study conducted for this report covers
the new highway alignment from Palani Road to Hina Lani Street The proposed alignment
is approximately 200 feet wide and 16 000 feet long Due to the preliminary nature of this
study the final alignment is still to be decided We envision that drainage structures street
lights and underground utility lines may be installed In addition intersection improvements
at Palani Road and other internal intersections may also be included
Based on the preliminary grading plans provided we understand that the majority
of mass grading for the proposed construction likely will involve excavations and
embankments of less than about 10 feet in height However some localized areas may
require cuts and fills of up to about 25 feet in height

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers
an area of approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of
the following five shield volcanoes Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea activity during
recent geologic time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 1803 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea both still active
The project site is situated on the western flank of Hualalai Mountain which
makes up the west central portion of the island The lava formation encountered at the
site appears to be of both a a and pahoehoe type flows which spread and ponded as
they approached the ocean A a lava is typically characterized by a porous rough and
irregular flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including
cobbles and boulders Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy
surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas especially pahoehoe type
lava Cavities form when lavas are still in a molten state and they represent both lava
tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes form
when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure
which may extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow Blisters and pockets
are generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water
quite readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is
transmitted through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and _ inter flow
contacts cavities and along clinker layers Clinker and cavities serve as the primary
water transmission features because of their high permeability

Site Preparation
Due to the presence of numerous cavities and or voids at the project site we
recommend giving special attention to the site preparation recommendations prior
to the commencement of fill placement and or construction of improvements at the
project site In addition the contractor should exercise caution while clearing and
grading near collapsed lava tube features and or cavities which may represent
potential lava tubes or cavities that may further collapse under the load of heavy
construction equipment
In general subgrade preparation consisting of ripping and or proof rolling the

commencement of grading The purpose of ripping and proof rolling the subgrades
is to assist in detecting and collapsing cavities and or voids contained in the porous
near surface materials encountered at the project site

Fills and Backfills
In general we recommend capping the fil areas of the project site with a minimum
2 foot thick layer of structural fill materials Structural fill materials should consist of
well graded granular materials generally less than 6 inches in maximum
dimension with sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted
mass In addition structural fill materials should contain less than 30 percent
particles passing the No 200 sieve by weight
Fill and backfill materials below the minimum 2 foot thick structural fill layer may
consist of general fill materials General fill and backfill materials should consist of
well graded granular materials the majority of which are less than 12 inches with
an absolute maximum dimension of 18 inches Materials between 12 and 18 inches
in particle size should be limited to about 15 percent or less of the total volume of
general fill materials
In general the excavated on site materials may be reused as a source of
structural and or general fill materials provided that they are processed to meet
the maximum particle size and gradation requirements presented above If the
excavated materials do not contain sufficient fines to produce a well graded
material off site borrow or on site rock crushing of larger rock fragments or
boulders should be considered to provide the required gradation and particle sizes
to develop a well graded material
Imported fill materials if required should be free of organics and deleterious
materials and should be suitable for the intended use based on the
recommendations for fill materials provided herein
Depending on the depths and extent of fills required for the final design
consideration may be given to placing materials greater than 18 inches in maximum
dimension as boulder fills In general materials consisting of boulders less than
5 feet in maximum dimension should be placed at least 4 feet below the finished

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in
loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction General fills and backfills
should be placed in level lifts of about 18inches in loose thickness
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or
other types of acceptable compaction equipment
Compaction equipment such as a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar heavy
compaction equipment providing suitable energy to achieve the required
compaction should be utilized The number of passes usually six to eight required
to achieve proper compaction should be determined at the on set of site grading by
the performance of a trial compaction test or by plate bearing tests Subsequent
layers of rock fill above the previously placed rock fill should provide a choking layer
of smaller sized boulder materials to fill in the voids between the boulders of the
preceding rock fill layer
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practical in fills composed of
rocks boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program should be conducted at
the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a qualified
geotechnical engineer to evaluate the number of passes needed to achieve the
desired level of compaction Based on this testing program the number of passes
may then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction

Cut and Fill Slopes
In general we envision that most of the cut slopes would expose dense basalt
formations In general we believe that the cut slopes planned at the site may be
designed with a slope inclination of one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V or flatter
In areas where clinker layers or highly fractured basalt are exposed at the cut slope
faces the cut slopes may need to be flattened to inclinations of 2H 1V or flatter
Therefore provisions to flatten the cut slopes and or construct retaining walls
should be considered in the project design A field exploration program should be
conducted during the design level study to evaluate whether the anticipated cut
slope materials are consistent with our assumptions
In general permanent fill slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V
or flatter This assumes that the fill slopes will be constructed using well graded
granular materials structural and general fills In addition fills placed on slopes
steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched
In general the filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in
level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement
recommendations Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back
to the design slope ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face The fill slope face
should be finished to a relatively smooth and well compacted surface The fill slope
face should be free of large boulders greater than 18 inches in largest dimension
and should be free of voids which would allow erosion and migration of fines to
occur In addition surface water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes
