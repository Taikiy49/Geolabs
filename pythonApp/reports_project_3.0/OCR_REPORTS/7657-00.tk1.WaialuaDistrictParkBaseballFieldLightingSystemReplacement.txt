EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
The project site is within the existing Waialua District Park in the Waialua area on 
the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1. The park is generally bordered by Waialua Beach Road to 
the north, Kealohanui Street to the east and south, and Goodale Avenue to the west. 
Based on our field observations and the electrical site plan provided, the baseball 
field is relatively flat with existing ground surface elevations ranging between about 
+11 and +13 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling five borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, extending to depths of about 46.5 to 58 feet below 
the existing ground surface.  The approximate boring locations are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by surface 
fills and alluvial soils, consisting of very stiff to hard silty clays and clayey silts, extending to 
depths of about 4 to 12 feet below the existing ground surface.  Coralline detritus and 
weathered sandstone, consisting of medium dense to very dense clayey gravel and loose 
to dense silty sands, were then encountered and extended to the maximum depth 
explored of approximately 58 feet below the existing ground surface. 
We encountered groundwater in the drilled borings at depths of about 9.2 to 
11.2 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration.  The 
groundwater levels measured generally correspond to about Elevations +0.8 to +3.5 feet 
MSL.  Due to the proximity of the project site to the Pacific Ocean, groundwater levels can 
fluctuate depending on tidal fluctuations, storm surge conditions, seasonal precipitation, 
groundwater withdrawal and/or injection, and other factors.

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the information provided, eight 90-foot tall light poles with 18 maximum 
light fixtures will be installed to replace the existing lighting system at the project site. 
Based on the loading demands provided by the project structural engineer and subsurface 
soil conditions encountered during our field exploration, we recommend supporting the 
new light poles on single cast-in-place drilled shaft foundations. Detailed discussions and 
recommendations for foundation design are presented in the following sections. 
Drilled Shaft Foundations 
In order to develop the required bearing and lateral load resistances, the proposed 
new light pole structures may be supported by a foundation system consisting of 
cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts.  Based on the pedestal dimensions for the new light 
pole structures and the subsurface soil conditions encountered at the project site, we 
recommend using a diameter of 60 inches for the proposed drilled shaft foundations.  
Based on the anticipated structural loads and the subsoil conditions encountered at the 
project site, we recommend using drilled shaft foundations with an embedment length of 
no less than 24 feet below the design finished grades for the new light pole structures 
planned for the project.  This corresponds to a drilled shaft length of no less than 25 feet 
with the top of the drilled shaft at 1 foot below the design finished grades.    
The cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally 
from skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding soils.  A net allowable 
compressive load capacity of up to 50 kips per shaft may be used for dead-plus-live loads 
and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as wind or seismic forces. 
Lateral Load Resistance 
The lateral load resistance of drilled shafts is a function of the stiffness of the 
surrounding soil, the stiffness of the drilled shaft, allowable deflection at the top of 
the drilled shaft, and the induced moment in the drilled shaft. The lateral load 
analyses were performed using the program LPILE-plus for Windows, which is a 
microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference, laterally loaded deep foundation 
program originally developed at the University of Texas at Austin. The program 
solves for deflection and bending moment along a deep foundation under lateral 
loads as a function of depth. The analysis was carried out with the use of non-linear 
“p-y” curves to represent soil moduli. The lateral deflection was then computed 
using the appropriate soil moduli at various depths.

Foundation Settlement 
Settlement of the drilled shaft foundation will result from elastic compression of the 
shaft and subgrade response of the foundation embedded in the soils encountered 
at the site. The total settlements of the drilled shafts are estimated to be less than 
0.5 inches. We believe that a significant portion of the settlement will be elastic and 
should occur as the loads are applied. 
Construction Considerations 
Drilled shafts are desirable for the light pole foundations because of the significant 
increase in lateral and uplift load capacities when compared to shallow foundations.  
However, the performance of the drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the 
contractor's method of construction and construction procedures.  In our opinion, the 
following conditions would have an impact on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft 
foundations. 
Loose Sandy Soils 
Based on our field exploration, the proposed new light pole locations are generally 
underlain by coralline detritus and weathered sandstone consisting of loose to very 
dense coralline sands and gravel below the surface fills and alluvial clays.  It should 
be noted that loose zones and/or highly weathered layers within the coralline 
detritus and weathered sandstone could be encountered within the depth of the 
drilled shaft foundations. In order to provide safe access by the workers and to 
reduce the potential for caving-in of the drilled holes, permanent steel casing should 
be provided for the drilled shaft foundation construction work.

Workmanship 
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend, to a large extent, on the 
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils.  Therefore, proper 
construction techniques are important.  The contractor should exercise care in 
drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes. 
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids is not recommended.  Placement of 
concrete by wet construction methods using a tremie pipe will be required due to 
the groundwater table anticipated within the drilled shaft depth at the site.  The 
concrete should be placed in a suitable manner by displacing the water in an 
upward fashion from the bottom of the drilled hole. 
A low-shrink concrete mix with high slump (6 to 9-inch range) should be used for 
the concreting operation.  The concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to 
reduce the potential for segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix.  If 
pre-cast concrete pole bases are used, the gap between the pole base and the 
surrounding soils should be backfilled with concrete or lean concrete. 
It is imperative for a Geolabs representative to be present at the project site to 
observe the drilling and installation of drilled shafts during construction. Geolabs 
observation of the drilled shaft installation operations is necessary to confirm the 
assumed subsurface conditions and should be designated as a “Special Inspection” 
item in accordance with Section 1704 of the International Building Code 
(2006 Edition). 
Utility Trench 
We envision that new electrical duct lines will be required to service the proposed 
new lighting system. In general, granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of clean sand or 
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) is recommended under the duct lines.  
If loose soils are encountered at or near the invert of the pipes, a stabilization layer 
consisting of an additional 18 inches of open-graded gravel wrapped in a non-woven filter 
fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) should be provided below the bedding layer for uniform 
support. 
Free-draining granular materials, such as clean sand or No. 3B Fine gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 
6 inches above the duct lines to provide adequate support around the pipes, and to reduce 
the compaction effort of the backfill, thus reducing the possibility of pipe damage. It is 
critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below 
the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. 
Improper backfill material and placement procedures could result in backfill settlement and 
pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 6 inches above the duct lines 
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on-site soils or imported select

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling five borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, extending to depths of about 46.5 to 58 feet below 
the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock materials were described in general 
accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested 
Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the 
International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977).  Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-5. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in 
general accordance with ASTM  D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample.  Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-3 and B-4.
