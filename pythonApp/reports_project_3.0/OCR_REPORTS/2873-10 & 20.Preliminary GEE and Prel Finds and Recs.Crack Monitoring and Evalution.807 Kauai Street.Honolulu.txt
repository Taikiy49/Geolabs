Discussion and Recommendations
The project site is located at 807 Kauai Street which is situated at the base of a mountain
ridge The slopes above the subject lot consist of in situ basalt rock formations with boulder
outcrops near the toe area The subject lot is susceptible to high water infiltration into the
underlying clayey subsoils from the mountain runoff and seepage during heavy rainfalls
The field exploration disclosed that the site is probably underlain by a boulder fill on top
of a deposit of highly plastic clay soils These clay soils which were classified as CH according
to the Unified Soil Classification System locally referred to as Adobe Clay exhibit high
swelling shrinkage potential and will lose considerable shear strength when saturated
The compounding effects of the existence of adobe materials with high water infiltration
at the subject lot may have contributed to the settling heaving problem which occurred at the
cited project
In order to formulate recommendations pertinent to the remedial work for the subject
house it is our recommendation that further exploration work involving deep borehole s drilling
using truck mounted equipment should be conducted to delineate the thickness of the adobe
deposit and to locate a competent bearing layer should an alternate foundation support system
be required This will be addressed separately in another proposal letter being forwarded for
your review

Findings
1
The field borings appear to indicate that the site is underlain by a thick layer
of highly plastic clay soils to depths of about 15 feet These clay soils
which were classified as CH according to the Unified Soil Classification
System locally referred to as Adobe Clay exhibit high swelling shrinkage
potential and will lose considerable shear strength when saturated When
the CH clay soils experience significant movement shear slip surfaces
will then develop which will further reduce the shear strength along the slip
surfaces to a very low residual shear value
The existing ground surface in the area slopes down at an average overall
slope gradient of about eight degrees 8 and therefore could be
susceptible to slope movements

3 The project site is situated at the base of a mountain ridge which is
susceptible to high water infiltration and accumulation from the up slope
ridge runoff and likely subsurface seepage during heavy rainfalls Prior to
the construction of the CMU wall against the hillside at the mauka side it
was reported by the resident that a significant amount of surface runoff
could be observed flowing down from the hillside during periods of heavy
rains thus flooding the area underneath the house structure
Interpretation of Mechanism of Movement
Due to the nature of the clay soils and the relatively steep natural ground slope
it is likely that the near surface soils were slowly creeping downhill Generally slope
creep movements are initially very slow and may go undetected for many years
However with time the clay soils eventually get saturated due to continual infiltration of
rainfall landscape water seepage etc and lose a portion of their initial soil shear
strength thereby increasing or accelerating downslope ground movements Water
infiltration then increases due to subsequent surface cracks in the ground caused by the
previous movement Due to the continued movement the soil strength continues to
decrease to its lowest value residual strength At this point the movements could
accelerate to rates that are very noticeable and measurable particularly if there is a build
up of water pressure or increased seepage
The above description applies to large scale movements covering tens of feet in
lateral extent On a smaller scale our observations indicate localized failures as
evidenced by the separation of the concrete sidewalk from the house structure cracking
of an interior drywall and tilting of the door frames It is our opinion that many of these
smaller localized failures could be a result of insufficient structural design to resist the very
high lateral soil pressures developed by the clay soils and over steepening of the ground
slopes

Recommendations for Remedial Work
It should be realized that due to the presence of existing house structure and utility
systems located in the subject lot major remedial work would be difficult costly and
would create considerable inconvenience for the tenants
It is our current opinion that the major causes of the existing distresses at the
subject residence are as follows
1 Poor subsurface soil conditions adobe clay deposits
2 Relatively steep hillside terrain
3 Excessive water infiltration and
4 Inadequate structural and foundation design
Therefore remedial measures should concentrate on the improvement of these
four areas More detailed discussions to further address these issues are presented
below
Improve Poor Soil Conditions
Due to the presence of the existing residential improvements removal and
replacement of the underlying clay soils is considered to be an optimal solution but
may not be a practical or acceptable solution

W O Page 10
Flatten Steep Ground Slope
The lateral ground movements at the site could be arrested if the ground slopes
could be flattened to ratios of about six horizontal to one vertical 6H 1V or less
However due to the existing housing development this does not seem practicable
and or feasible
Intercept Water Infiltration
Since water infiltration into the underlying clay soils would accelerate or increase
the potential slope movement immediate remedial work to intercept and drain the
groundwater would minimize water infiltration into the weakening soil mass This
may help to increase the hillside stability
In order to effectively drain the hillside runoff and seepage it is important to identify
the depth of influence from the water seepage Our borings indicated that the lot
area is underlain by the adobe deposits down to a depth of approximately 15 0
feet below the existing ground surface Therefore measures to intercept water
infiltration in the upper 10 to 15 foot depths may be effective in an attempt to
reduce further ground movements It should be recognized that this method may
be only partially effective however it is more feasible and less costly than the two
2 above approaches
A trench drain should be installed along the toe edge of the mountain base down
to a depth of approximately ten feet 10 below the existing ground surface This
deep trench drain would serve to intercept and drain the hillside runoff and
subterranean seepage A typical section of such a trench drain is shown on Plate
4 The trench drain should be designed to drain into a suitable outlet for proper

Underpinning of Existing Structure
Due to the creeping movements of the ground slope the house structure has a
tendency to slide downhill unless the existing foundation design transmitted the
structural loadings down to a competent bearing stratum However it appeared
that the existing house structure is probably supported by shallow foundations
bearing on top of the boulder fill Therefore underpinning of the house structure
may be considered in order to prevent further downhill sliding of the building This
consists of installing new foundation elements so as to provide additional support
or to transfer the structural loads to different soil or rock strata
Since our field exploration disclosed that the project site is underlain by a
competent bearing stratum of basalt formation at approximately 15 feet below the
existing ground surface it is our opinion that a new foundation system consisting
of cast in place drilled piers extending down to the underlying basalt formation may
be installed to transfer the structural loadings from the existing system
Temporary Remedial Measures
Other temporary remedial measures involves the utilization of a hydraulic ram to
drive slip jointed sections of steel pipe down to rock or suitable bearing stratum
When a solid base or other resistance is reached continued exertion of the
hydraulic ram will raise the foundation Once at grade the pipe is pinned through
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O Page 12
the jack bracket to hold the foundation in place The jack brackets can be
positioned from the interior or exterior Two basic problems associated with this
technique are the uncertainty of alignment and the imminent corrosion of the steel
pipe exposed to the wet soil
In the case of slab foundations the void beneath the slab must then be filled by
mud jacking Mud jacking is a process whereby a water and soil cement or soil
lime cement grout is pumped beneath the slab under pressure to produce a lifting
force which literally floats the slab to the desired position The grout is introduced
through small holes drilled through the concrete Mud jacking has the attendant
feature of some chemical stabilization of the soil which helps preclude future
differential movement produced by moisture variations Since the amount of
upheaval and the load distribution is not uniform across the entire site particular
care and a well prepared approach must be exercised to avoid shearing or
unevenly raising the house structure

instead of implementing the above remedial measures In either case it is important that
the crack gauges monitoring service be continued to provide further information of the
ground slope conditions and the effectiveness of the remedial measures if implemented

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling of four
4 borings to depths ranging between 1 0 to 29 0 feet below the existing ground surface
at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 3 The borings were drilled
using both portable and truck mounted equipment
The material encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who continuously monitored the drilling
operations These classifications were further reviewed by visual inspection and testing
in the laboratory All soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System A graphic presentation of the materials encountered are presented
on the Boring Logs Plates A 1 through A 4
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 3 O D Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer free falling 30
inches The blow counts to drive the sampler the last 12 inches are shown on the
Boring Logs at the appropriate sample depths
In soft cohesive soils samples were obtained using a thin walled tube sampler
Shelby Tube
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 and would be shown
on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting
any drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from
a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 and would be shown on the Boring Logs
as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
