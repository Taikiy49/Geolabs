2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Lanai is a shield volcano built by eruptions at the summit and along
three rift zones more than 1 20 to 1 46 million years ago The principal rift zone trends
northwestward as a broad ridge and is responsible for the conspicuous elongation of
the island in that direction A less conspicuous bulge on the south side of the island is a
result of building on the southwest rift zone The summit of the shield collapsed to form
a caldera from which a shallow graben bordered by an echelon of step faults extends
south southeastward toward Manele Bay Numerous dikes exposed in the sea cliff
indicate that this Manele graben lies along another rift zone
The caldera was largely but not completely filled by lava flows and the present
Palawai Basin is a remnant of the caldera Just to the west of it Miki Basin with an
average diameter of about 0 9 miles is a nearly filled pit crater The top of the ridge
between them is about 140 feet above the floor of the Palawai Basin On the south the
floor of Palawai Basin merges with that of the Manele graben through which the last
lava flows in the caldera overflowed onto the outer slope of the volcano
In the sea cliff on the west coast 3 000 feet south of Kaumalapau Landing up to
1 5 feet of red colluvium lies between the lava flows and other thin layers of colluvium
are interbedded with lavas in the walls of Kapoho Gulch The southwest side of the
island is exposed to the full fury of the waves of southwesterly storms which have eaten
deep into it resulting in the high sea cliffs on the leeward side This leeward cliff
extending for about 7 miles north of Kaumalapau reaches a height of 345 feet The
project site is located at Kaumalapau Harbor on the southwest shore of the Island of
Lanai The areas surrounding the wharf site generally consist of high sea cliff
composing of basalt rock formation with varying degrees of weathering Development
of the harbor wharf in the last century has brought the project site to its present
conditions

2 2 Site Description
The project site is located at the existing Kaumalapau Harbor Wharf at the
southwestern side of the Island of Lanai Hawaii The existing wharf is a rectangular
structure built in the 1920 s measuring approximately 30 feet wide and 405 feet long in
plan dimensions The wharf structure generally runs in a northeast and southwest
direction as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on our field observations the wharf deck is equipped with a rail supported
crane system for cargo loading unloading use Miscellaneous construction equipment
and materials as well as containers were observed around the wharf site Based on the
information provided and our field observations we understand that the seaward side of
the existing wharf deck is generally supported on driven pile foundations and the
majority of the landward side of the wharf deck is bearing on the in situ boulders and or
basalt rock formation According to the project structural engineer the existing pile
foundations supporting the wharf deck generally consisted of 16 inch square precast
reinforced concrete piles Based on the results of the underwater survey it appears
that the conditions of the majority of the driven piles may be categorized as good to
excellent with minimal amounts of corrosion and or spalling problems However the
bearing conditions of the driven pile foundations could not be confirmed due to the
inaccessibility of some of the driven piles and presence of cobbles and boulders at the
mudline level
Our field observations indicated that the western portion of the project site is
presently a breakwater consisting of abundant amount of concrete dolos in disrepair
condition Based on the plans provided the harbor floor in front of the wharf structure
was dredged to 27 feet below Mean Lower Low Water MLLW with the existing wharf
deck at an elevation of about 10 feet MLLW

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling thirteen on land
borings designated as Boring 2A and 3 through 12 extending to depths
ranging from about 10 to 32 feet below the existing pavement surface In addition two
water borings off the existing wharf deck designated as Boring Nos 13 and 14 were
drilled to depths of about 60 and 65 feet below the existing wharf deck elevation The
approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings
were drilled using truck mounted drill rigs equipped with casing and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual
observation and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 15
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and
Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third Ginches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration
Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock coral formations encountered at the site were
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core
drilling is a rotary drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation
The material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination
and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
Very Poor 0 25
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100
The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its
relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense rock formation with a
high RQD would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous
methods of excavation
