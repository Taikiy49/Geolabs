In accordance with our fee proposal dated August 18, 2022, Geolabs observed the 
micropile installation and two tension proof tests for the micropile foundations for the 
temporary Acrow Bridge abutments for the Yasutake Bridge Bypass project located in 
Wailua on the Island of Kauai. This letter report summarizes our geotechnical observations 
during the micropile foundation installation for the project. The deep foundation support 
system of the new structures consists of 16, 3.5-inch diameter micropiles. 
Geolabs provided geotechnical engineering consultation for the Yasutake Bridge 
Bypass project, and the findings and recommendations are presented in the 
above-referenced report. The purpose of our geotechnical observations was to monitor the 
geotechnical engineering aspects of the micropile foundation installation for conformance 
with the intent of the recommendations presented in our report. In addition, we provided 
“Special Inspection” services (as stipulated in Section 1704 of the 2006 International 
Building Code) by observing the micropile foundation installation for general conformance 
with the project plans. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is along Kamalu Road above the Wailua Homesteads Park on the 
Island of Kauai, Hawaii. A temporary 60-foot long ACROW type replacement bridge will 
span over the existing bridge structure crossing Kamalu stream. The Acrow bridge

structure will be a single-span structure consisting of two abutment structures supported 
on micropiles. 
Based on the geotechnical engineering report referenced above, we recommended 
supporting the structures on a deep foundation system consisting of 3.5-inch outside 
diameter (OD) micropiles with an unfactored shaft compression load capacity and Strength 
Limit State compressive load capacity of up to 64 and 45 kips per pile. The micropile was 
composed of 1.25-inch (32mm), Grade 75 hollow threaded bar and 6,000 pounds per 
square inch (psi) cement grout. The micropiles would extend through the near-surface fills 
and river deposits and derive support primarily from skin friction between the micropile 
bonded zone and the hard to very hard basalt formation anticipated across the project site. 
A total of 16, 3.5-inch diameter micropiles were specified for the project. The micropiles 
are not considered permanent. 
This report covers the observation of the micropile foundation construction and 
testing for the Yasutake Bridge Bypass project only. Goodfellow Bros. was the general 
contractor for the project and Prometheus Construction installed the micropile foundation 
support system. 
MICROPILE INSTALLATION AND TESTING 
Micropile Installation 
The micropile installation was performed by the subcontractor Prometheus 
Construction. A total of 16 micropiles were installed for the temporary bridge abutments 
between August 22, 2022 through September 2, 2022 under near-continuous (full-time) 
observations by our field representatives. During the micropile installation operation, logs 
were maintained for each micropile, which included information pertaining to micropile 
number, date installed, depth drilled, drilling rate, grout volume used, and other details 
pertaining to the construction. 
In general, the micropiles were installed by using hollow drill rods and/or the 
1.25-inch galvanized Grade 75 Hollow threaded bar with 3.5-inch diameter drill bits with 
side discharge holes for air and water discharge. Each micropile hole was advanced by 
rotary percussion using a top hammer mounted on a TEI Drill attached to a Komatsu 
PC238us Excavator. Drilling fluids consisting of air and/or clean water to flush the soil 
cuttings from the hole.  
Once the subcontractor has reached the design depth for the micropile, a 1.25-inch 
diameter Grade 75 hollow bar were installed (if it was not used to drill the micropile) in the 
drilled hole for the micropiles and load test micropiles. Centralizers were placed along the 
micropile at 10-foot intervals. The subcontractor then pumped the grout mixture consisting 
of approximately 94 lbs. of cement, 4.5 gallons of water, and 3.5 lbs. of MasterRoc FLC 
100 through the 1.25-inch galvanized Grade 75 Hollow threaded bar to create a grout bulb 
in the drilled hole. Grouting of the anchor was completed when good grout return was 
observed at the top of the hole. No permenant casing was utilized.

During drilling of Micropile No. 16, the 1.25-inch diameter Grade 75 hollow bar was 
utilized to advance the hole to approximately 26 feet below grade where the hollow bar 
broke at about 17 feet below grade. Due to the depth of the break, the subcontractor was 
unable to remove the remaining 9 feet of steel in order to utilize the same hole for the 
micropile, and the hole was grouted from approximately 17 feet below grade to the existing 
ground surface to abandon the location. The structural engineer of record therefore 
recommended that Micropile Nos. 15 and 16R1 be relocated about 6 inches east of their 
original locations. The approximate location of the abandoned hole is shown on the 
Micropile Plan, Plate 1. 
The micropiles were installed to tip elevations ranging from 259.5 to 261.8 feet 
Mean Seal Level (MSL) with bond zones ranging from 9.5 to 15.5 feet. The approximate 
locations of the 16 micropiles installed are shown on the Micropile Plan, Plate 1. A 
Summary of Micropile Installation is presented on Plate 2 for reference. The Micropile 
Installation Logs are presented in Appendix A of this report. 
Micropile Testing 
Two micropiles, designated as Micropile Nos. 5 and 15, were selected for tensile 
(pull-out) proof testing. The proof testing was generally performed in accordance with 
ASTM D3689 and the project geotechnical report. The design and maximum testing loads 
were specified as 45 kips for the micropiles. 
A hydraulic jack system reacting against a load frame supported by dunnage at 
each end was used to apply the test loads to the proof testing micropiles. A calibrated 
hydraulic Jack and pressure gauge was utilized to measure the test loads applied to the 
proof testing micropiles. The micropile displacements were measured with two dial gages 
attached to an independently supported reference beam. 
In general, the proof test micropiles were loaded to a maximum testing load of 
45 kips in 11.3-kip load increments. Each load increment was held for 5 minutes or until 
stable, and at the maximum test loads of 45 kips (about 100 percent of the Nominal Axial 
Compressive Load Capacity) was held for a minimum of 10 minutes to evaluate the effects 
of creep. Subsequently, the proof testing micropiles were unloaded in approximately 
four equal decrements and held until stable measurement at each decrement. The 
approximate locations of the two-proof tested micropiles are shown on the Micropile Plan, 
Plate 1. The results of the proof tests are presented in Appendix B of this report. 
Based on our field observations, we believe that the observed micropile installation 
for the temporary Acrow Bridge abutments for the Yasutake Bridge Bypass project have 
been installed in general conformance with the project plans and above-referenced report 
and within the limits of generally accepted construction practices. Based on our 
observations of the micropile installation, it is our professional opinion that the micropiles 
have been installed to sufficient depths to provide Nominal Axial Compressive Load 
Capacity and Strength Limit State Compressive Load Capacity of up to 64 and 45 kips, 
respectively, per pile.
