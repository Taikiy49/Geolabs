REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct 
shield volcanoes – Waianae and Koolau. The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of 
the western third of the island while the younger Koolau shield forms the majority of the 
eastern two-thirds of the island. It is believed that Waianae Volcano became extinct while 
Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas 
in Central Oahu.  
Pearl Harbor is located on the southwest flank of the Koolau shield, and its 
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to the glacio-eustatic 
fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch. The stratigraphic history of 
Pearl Harbor is quite complex due to its large areal extent, the large number of streams 
and drainageways entering it, and a complex series of sea level changes. The subsurface 
conditions at the project site are dominated by fossil Pleistocene Age coral/algal reef 
deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of the sea that are intercalated with 
terrigenous sediments. The base of the stratigraphic section in the Pearl Harbor vicinity 
is Koolau basalt.

The Koolau volcanic shield was built during the early Pleistocene by thinly bedded 
basaltic lava flows. The main shield-building activities ceased approximately 2½ million 
years ago. Evidence from drilled wells indicate that the Island of Oahu has subsided as 
much as 1,200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic activity (Macdonald and 
Abbot, 1970). 
During that period of subsidence, coral-algal reefs began to grow on the southern 
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth. The growth of the 
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence. A series of lagoons formed behind 
the barrier reefs, and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the lagoons 
(Macdonald and Abbot, 1970). 
During the later Pleistocene (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred as a 
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great 
continental glaciers advanced, the level of the ocean fell since there was less water 
available to fill the oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers melted, global sea levels 
rose because more water was available. The land mass of Oahu remained essentially 
stable during these changes, and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. These glacio-
eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower 
relative to present sea levels on Oahu.  
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of 
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations, and lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet waters 
protected by fringing reefs. The concurrent growth of reefs and lagoonal sediments may 
have also resulted in deposits of coral/algal limestone and marls within the predominantly 
lagoonal sedimentary unit.  
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef 
deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused 
desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the 
calcareous reef materials. In addition, renewed subaerial erosion of upper areas of the 
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions. 
During periods of no significant sea level changes, continued stream action extended the 
alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal sediments. 
The geologic history of the project area has been further complicated by the 
deposition of recent pyroclastic materials and lava flows from eruptions of the vents of the 
Honolulu Volcanic Series, including Aliamanu-Salt Lake-Makalapa, Kamanaiki, 
Punchbowl, Roundtop-Tantalus-Sugarloaf, and other minor vents. These post-erosional 
events following the cessation of shield building were contemporaneous with the 
Pleistocene sea level fluctuations. 
Many of the eruptions were rather explosive in nature due to the interaction of 
groundwater with the rising magma resulting in steam explosions, which expelled large 
quantities of pyroclastic material – predominantly ash and cinder. Some explosive

Some of the eruptions produced lava flows, which filled the valleys and moved seaward

The project site is located on the eastern side of Pearl Harbor. Geologic maps 
show that the project area is predominantly underlain by volcanic tuff material. Based on 
historic maps showing the coastline of Pearl Harbor before it was developed, is does not 
appear that the project site is located in fill areas. Therefore, Buildings 619 and 665 are 
located in areas of near-surface volcanic tuff formation. 
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
Visual site reconnaissance visits were conducted on February 17 and April 14, 2022, 
at Buildings 665 and 619, respectively, by our project engineer and principal engineer to 
observe the existing conditions of each building. The purpose for our field reconnaissance 
visits was to record and observe signs of building distress that may be due to foundation 
settlement, and also note other geotechnical engineering considerations that should be 
included as part of the structural assessment. The following sections present our 
observations from each of the two site visits performed. Site reconnaissance photographs 
depicting the observed conditions at the project site are presented on Plates 2.1 through 
2.5. Note that photographs were not allowed in the interior of either building for security 
purposes. Therefore, only photographs depicting the conditions of the exterior of the 
buildings are presented. 
Building 665 
In general, Building 665 was observed to be a relatively old structure with multiple 
additions added to the building over its lifespan. We understand that the original building 
consisted of the first floor cast-in-place concrete structure. The added second story and 
additions to the first and second story consist of wood construction. 
Based on the information provided, we understand that much of the observed 
building distresses are located in the second story and building additions. Uneven/warped 
floors were observed throughout most of the second floor. On the ground floor, 
uneven/warped floors were only observed in the northern corner office. Distorted 
doorways were also noted on both the first and second floors in the northern, wooden-
construction addition. 
In general, building distress due to possible foundation settlement was not 
observed on the exterior of the building. However, concrete cracking and spalling was 
observed on the southern end of the second floor slab-on-grade (Photo No. 3, Plate 2.2). 
From the observed pattern and nature of the concrete distress, the distress appears to 
be caused by a structural issue and not foundation settlement.

The first floor addition on the west side of the building was observed overhanging 
a ledge of volcanic tuff formation (Photo No. 4, Plate 2.2). The cantilevered building 
addition was observed to be supported by a post and pier foundation system bearing on 
the volcanic tuff formation. It was noted that one post and pier foundation was constructed 
on a sloping face of volcanic tuff formation (Photo Nos. 5 and 6, Plate 2.3). The concrete 
footing appears to have been cast in place on a stepped portion of the in-situ volcanic tuff 
formation. Behind the outer row of post and pier foundations, a second row of shallow 
concrete footings were observed bearing on volcanic tuff formation (Photo No. 7, 
Plate 2.4).  
On the eastern side of Building 665, a large concrete retaining wall separates the 
building from an adjacent parking lot approximately 15 feet lower in elevation 
(Photo No. 8, Plate 2.4). Volcanic tuff formation was observed at the top of the retaining 
wall.  
Building 619 
Based on the information provided, we understand that the majority of the 
observed building distresses at Building 619 were in the upper two floors consisting of 
wood construction. The original building, consisting of a two-story reinforced concrete 
structure, has generally not experienced observable building distresses. Because the 
purpose of our field reconnaissance was to observe possible areas of foundation 
settlement, we limited our field observations to the interior of the first floor and building 
exterior. 
In general, the interior of the first floor did not exhibit signs of building distress such 
as uneven/warped floors, cracks in walls, or uneven doors. It should be noted that the 
first floor did not contain windows; therefore, cracks around window frames (a common 
sign of structural distress and/or foundation movement) were not observed.  
The first floor exterior was generally observed to be in good condition. Surface 
cracks, concrete spalling, and signs of foundation movement were not observed on the 
exterior of Building 619 (Photo No. 9, Plate 2.5). An outcrop of volcanic tuff formation with 
coral finger fragment inclusions was observed on the south side of the building 
(Photo No. 10, Plate 2.5). The existing ground surface around the remaining of the 
building was covered with pavement or vegetative cover. Therefore, the conditions of the 
in-situ soils around the north, east, and west sides of the building were not readily 
observable.

Subsurface information was not available on the provided plans for Building 665. 
The provided geotechnical engineering report titled “Bachelor Enlisted Quarters, Naval

relatively dense coralline sand and gravel were encountered to depths of about 40 to 
45 feet, followed by volcanic tuff formation extending to the maximum depth explored of 
about 56.6 feet below the existing ground surface. However, based on our field 
observations, volcanic tuff formation was observed beneath the post and pier footings on 
the west side of Building 665, and near-surface volcanic tuff formation was observed on 
the east side above the existing retaining wall. Given the significant elevation difference 
between Building 665 and the adjacent parking lot (about 15 feet or more), we believe 
that the parking lot is located within an old drainage channel that accumulated deposits 
of coralline detritus and older alluvium above the in-situ volcanic tuff formation. Therefore, 
based on the higher elevation of Building 665, the observed volcanic tuff formation, and 
geologic data, we believe Building 665 is underlain by volcanic tuff formation and not 
near-surface older alluvium like the adjacent parking lot.  
Subsurface information near Building 619 was provided in the plans titled 
“Extension of Headquarters Bldg 619, Bldg 1310” dated in the year 1967. These plans 
were for the construction of Building 1310 which abuts Building 619 on the south side. 
The borings generally encountered volcanic tuff formation (a.k.a. mudrock) from the 
existing ground surface to a depth of about 10 feet. Coral formation was encountered in 
the upper 5 feet of two of the borings. Based on the available as-built drawings, we 
understand that Building 619 is supported by shallow foundations. The foundations of 
Building 619 were not visible during our field reconnaissance. However, based on 
available information, we understand that the reinforced concrete walls for the first two 
floors of Building 619 are 6 feet thick. Therefore, Building 619 is a relatively heavy 
structure supported on shallow foundations. Based on the available subsurface 
information, geology of the area, and the volcanic tuff outcrop observed on the south side 
of the building, we believe Building 619 also bears directly on volcanic tuff formation.

potential soil liquefaction. Seismic design of the proposed project will be based on the 
Unified Facilities Criteria “Seismic Design of Buildings” (UFC 3-301-01 dated 
October 1, 2019), which references the 2018 International Building Code (IBC 2018) and

Based on the International Building Code (2018 Edition), the project site may be 
subjected to seismic activity and should be evaluated for potential soil liquefaction. Based 
on the anticipated subsurface conditions, the phenomenon of soil liquefaction should not 
be a design consideration for this project site. The risk for potential liquefaction is 
non-existent at this project site based on the anticipated subsurface conditions (volcanic 
tuff formation).  
As mentioned previously, the geologic history of the project site is complicated 
based on multiple changes in sea level, deposition of both terrigenous and marine 
sediments, and the late-stage eruptions of the Honolulu Volcanic Series. Therefore, 
although the two structures may be underlain by volcanic tuff formation, soil deposits are 
likely present beneath the volcanic tuff. In the absence of soil borings, we have assumed 
that volcanic tuff underlain by soil deposits are present within the top 100 feet below the 
existing ground surface. Therefore, we believe that the project site may be classified as 
a “Stiff Soil Profile” site corresponding to a Site Class “D” in accordance with Table 20.3-1 
(Site Classification) of ASCE 7-16 (Minimum Design Loads for Buildings and Other 
Structures) referenced by the International Building Code, 2018 Edition. Based on Site 
Class D, the following seismic design parameters were estimated and may be used for

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field observations, multiple types of building distress were evident at 
Building 665. These included uneven floors, distorted doorways, and concrete spalling of 
the concrete slab-on-grade. However, based on the geologic setting of the area and the

observed volcanic tuff formation under and around the building, we believe that the 
shallow footings supporting Building 665 are bearing on volcanic tuff formation. Therefore, 
we do not believe that the observed structural distresses are a result of foundation 
movement. We understand that a recurring, significant termite problem has affected 
Building 665 for some time. Given that many of the uneven floors and other structural 
distresses are present in the wooden portions of the structure, we believe that the termite 
infestation may be a significant contributor to the building distresses observed that are 
structural in nature.  
The building tenant in the northernmost office on the first floor reported that the 
flooring has gotten wet during some past heavy rain events. Based on the location of the 
office and description of the water infiltration, we believe that water may be seeping up 
from the underlying soil. We recommend that the flooring in the office be pulled back to 
inspect the underlying slab for cracks. If cracks are present, the cracks should be 
appropriately sealed and waterproofed to prevent future water infiltration. In addition, 
underground utility lines should be checked for leakage.  
One post and pier foundation on the south side of the western first floor extension 
was observed to be constructed on a sloping face of volcanic tuff formation. During a 
seismic event, there is a possibility that lateral movement of the footing may occur. Due 
to how the footing was constructed, it is possible that the footing could slip off of the 
sloping face of volcanic tuff. The footing should be evaluated by a structural engineer and 
extended down to the road level (same elevation as adjacent post and pier footings), if 
necessary, or anchored into the volcanic tuff formation. 
Based on our field observations, building distresses were not apparent on the first 
floor of Building 619. The as-built drawings show that the first two floors of Building 619 
were constructed of reinforced concrete with 6-foot thick walls. As previously discussed, 
based on the anticipated weight of the structure, provided subsurface information, the 
geologic setting of the area, and our field observations, we believe the shallow 
foundations of Building 619 are bearing on volcanic tuff formation. Because we did not 
observe building distress or foundation movement in or around the first floor, we believe 
that building distresses observed on higher floors are not due to foundation movements.
