Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii, the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago, covers an area of
approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of the following
five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea (activity during recent geologic
time), Hualalai (erupted in 1801 — 1803), and Mauna Loa and Kilauea (both still active).
The project site is situated on the western flank of Mauna Loa Mountain, which
makes up the south-central portion of the island. The lava formation encountered at the
site appears to be of both a'a and pahoehoe type flows, which spread and ponded as
they approached the ocean. A’a lava is typically characterized by a porous, rough, and
irregular flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments, cobbles, and
boulders. Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth, rope-like or billowy surface and an
internal structure of vesicular (porous) rock.
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas, especially the pahoehoe type
lava. Cavities form when lavas are still in a molten state, and represent both lava tubes
(intra-flow cavities) and blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities). Lava tubes form when
molten lava drains from the cooling flow, leaving a hollow tube-like structure, which may
extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow. Blisters and pockets are generally
smaller in horizontal extent.
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions. In general, water is transmitted
through the porous rock matrix, along joints, fractures and inter-flow contacts, cavities, and
along clinker layers. Clinker and cavities serve as primary water transmission features
because of their high permeability.

general, we believe that the original topography of the project site (prior to site grading
activities) consisted of irregular (or hummocky) lava flow terrain with frequent topographic
depressions separated by adjacent higher relief mounds and low ridge features.

Discussion and Recommendations
Based on the grading plan for the project, the ground surface elevations prior to
commencement of this project in October 2007 ranged from about +20 to +36 feet Mean
Sea Level (MSL) at Makai Clubhouse and Lot Nos. 70, 71, 98, 99 and 100. Makai
Clubhouse and Lot Nos. 70, 71, 98, 99 and 100 generally are graded to a finished pad
elevation of about +17 to +38 feet MSL. Mass grading at Makai Clubhouse and
Lot Nos. 70, 71, 98, 99 and 100 generally consisted of placing up to about 8 feet of new
fills and excavating up to about 8 feet to achieve the design grades.
Subgrades in cut areas and areas to be filled were generally proof-rolled with a
Caterpillar 825 sheepsfoot roller compactor (minimum 20 tons) for about eight passes to
help detect and collapse near-surface cavities and/or voids.
Fill materials consisting of processed on-site materials, including volcanic ash, a’a
basalt and dense pahoehoe basalt, were placed in the fill areas of the project site.
Structural fills consisted of well-graded granular materials generally smaller than 6 inches
in dimension. The fill materials within the top 2 feet of the pads (within the fill areas of the
pad) consisted of structural fill materials. General fills consisting of boulders of up to about
18 inches in dimension generally were placed about 2 feet below the finished grades
within the pad areas of the lots and choked off with finer-sized materials. It should be
noted that boulders of up to about 3 feet in dimension generally were placed at least about
4 feet below the finished grades within the pad areas and choked off with finer-sized
materials.
It should be noted that some portions of the Makai Clubhouse were generally
graded to a cut condition. Therefore, the presence of basalt formation and/or a’a basalt
should be anticipated at or near the existing ground elevation in this area.

Limitations

occurring simultaneously on a construction site. Therefore, some of the earthwork may
have been performed without our observation. However, the level of earthwork

the geotechnical risks to the project. It should be recognized that there are variations in the
accuracy and statistical repeatability of the tests and methods used to monitor the
earthwork construction and materials. Our services were performed in accordance with

nor does it guarantee construction of any type. The contractor shall retain responsibility for
the quality of the work, for adhering to the plans and specifications, and for repairing
