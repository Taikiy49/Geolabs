This report presents the results of our soils investigation
conducted for the proposed new sewer force main from Pearl Harbor Naval
Shipyard Sewage Pump Station No 9 to the sewage treatment plant at Fort
Kamehameha in Hickam Air Force Base Oahu Hawaii The general alignment
of the proposed force main and its vicinity is shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
Our work was performed in general accordance with our revised
proposal of
The results of our field exploration and laboratory tests which we
used in the engineering analysis to formulate our conclusions and
recommendations are presented in the appendix of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATION
The proposed new sewer force main will be 30 inches in diameter and
about 8500 feet long The starting point will be from Sewage Pump
Station No 9 near Sixth and South Streets in the Pearl Harbor Naval
Shipyard The new line will traverse along Bishop Point Road near the
shoreline around Bishop Point and then along Fort Kamehameha Road into
the existing sewage treatment plant at Hickam Air Force Base
The new force main will be parallel to the existing 27 inch force
main which is identified as Sewer Line S on the as built drawings from
the Navy titled Pearl Harbor Sewerage Facilities Plan and Profile
Sewer Line S NAVFAC Drawing Nos 1229354 through 1229358
According to the preliminary design plan developed thusfar the new
force main will probably be located about 13 feet center to center from
the existing force main The new line will be located on the shore side
along the majority of the route except for a short segment along the
Fort Kamehameha Road It is our understanding that the invert elevations
of the new line will be similar to those of the existing 27 inch force
main which range from Elevation 108 75 at the pump station to 95 44 at
Station 69 20

GENERAL SITE DESCRIPTION
In general the new force main will be along relatively flat paved
roadways where the grades range from 105 to 112 feet U S Navy Datum
USN Datum The new line will be placed below numerous
existing buried lines and parallel to the existing force main see NAVFAC

A shallow drainage ditch crosses the alignment just before the
sewage treatment plant An antenna and its guy anchors are located on

GEOLOGY
After the formation of the Koolau Volcano on Oahu had ceased the
coastal plain developed along the southern shores of the island from a
complex series of erosional and depositional processes Numerous
glacio eustatic sea level changes produced fringing coral reef deposits
Post erosional pyroclastic volcanic activities from the Salt Lake
Makalapa vents deposited layers of ash and cinders over such fringing
coral reef in much of the Pearl Harbor and Hickam areas
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1406 00 Page 4
During lower stands of sea levels streams cut channels through the
coastal plain consisting predominantly of coral reef deposits while rises
of sea level resulted in redeposition of lagoonal and or alluvial
deposits in these channels
The project alignment follows along a portion of the Pearl Harbor
main channel entrance which is one of the major stream channels through
the southern coastal plain

Soil Conditions
Based on our field exploration and a review of geological
information the subsurface materials encountered may be generalized as
follows
1 FILL This consisted predominantly of tan white coral and or
brownish gray tuff fragments The particle size varied from silt
to gravel size fragments probably the result of earthwork
process The fill was generally dense under the roadways but was
loose to medium dense outside the roadway areas

In general the fill was encountered at the surface in all borings
along the alignment The thickness varied up to about 5 feet

CORAL SAND AND GRAVEL This material consisted of loose to mediun
dense coral sand and gravel near shore deposits resulted from the
rise in the sea level In general the upper portion above the
present water levels were medium dense and tannish brown to white
in color Below the water level these soils were loose with
generally gray or mottled gray color These deposits were
encountered in Borings 1 through 4 and 10 through 11
CORAL This consisted of tan white soft to moderately hard
cemented coral fragments with solution cavities of various sizes
The cavities are often filled with loose silt and sand The coral
was encountered at the invert levels in Borings 4 through 9

brown clayey silt with some tuff fragments This was probably the

was encountered at a depth of 21 feet in Boring 1

main will be similar to those of the existing 27 inch force main where

depth of trenching excavation will probably be about 7 to 14 feet below
existing grade Like the existing force main the new sewer will also
frequently pass below the existing utility lines where the alignments
Cross

The soil conditions along the new line appeared to be generally
fair with the exception that loose silty coral sand deposit was
encountered in and 4 areas in the northern third of the new
line Similar loose silty coral sand was encowutered in Boring 11 and is
expected in the ditch crossing area nearby For the remaining section
coral formation with filled cavities is anticipated
Because of the granular nature of the soils which are in loose to
medium dense conditions proper dewatering trench shoring and bracing
will be required for trenching to minimize potential damage to the
existing roadway pavement underground utilities and surface structures
Set Back Distance Between New and Existing Sewer Pipes
To minimize damage to the existing force main the new parallel
force main should be off set a minimum distance away from the existing
pipe The required set back is influenced by the depths of the lines and
the conditions of the existing pipe As the depth of the pipe increases
the vertical soil load on the pipe increases The amount of this soil
load which is transmitted to the soil adjacent to the pipe depends on the
flexibility of the pipe For a flexible pipe or one which is in poor
condition more of the loads is transferred to the adjacent ground
Therefore a larger lateral set back distance is required for deeper
pipes or pipes in poor condition
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1406 00 Page 8
Assuming that the existing pipe is very flexible or in an extremely
poor condition the calculated lateral soil thickness required is about 9
feet for a pipe invert located at about 13 foot depth Thus the
center to center spacing required would be about 13 feet with allowance
for some clearance of about 1 5 feet for the sheeting and work space in
the new trench For inverts that are typically at 6 5 to 7 0 foot depth
the calculated minimum lateral soil thickness required would be about 3
feet for the new trench Thus the center to center spacing would be
about 7 feet
If the existing pipe is in good condition then the load
transferred to the soil at the side of the pipe is much less and the
probability for failure is significantly reduced
Vertical Earth Pressure Loads on Pipe
Vertical loading on buried conduits are influenced by the width and
depth of the trench the size and flexibility of the pipe the unit
weight of backfill material and the frictional resistance between the
backfill material and the trench side walls To compute the vertical
loads on the pipes the unit weight of 120 lbs per cubic foot and a
coefficient of friction of 0 25 are recommended Earth forces upon the
pipe increase rapidly with the width of the trench Therefore the width
of the trench should be kept to a minimum
GEOLABS HAWAII
Bearing Capacity and Settlement
The new utility line construction in general will not materially
increase the vertical loading on the underlying soils if no additional
fills are placed upon the existing ground surface The net difference in
loading between the weights of the excavated material and backfill will
be essentially small Therefore significant settlements are not
anticipated if proper construction techniques particularly for shoring
and dewatering are utilized
Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a great
effect upon the load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the
pipe bedding is an important design consideration
For pipes bearing on coral at the invert level a minimum of six
inches of compacted granular bedding material should be placed underneath
the pipe The granular bedding should extend beyond the sides of the
pipe a minimum width of one fourth the outside pipe diameter and should
extend half way up the sides of the pipe The bedding material should
consist of clean granular material such as 3B Fine crushed rock
For pipes on the loose sand deposits an additional eight inch
layer of granular material such as 3B Fine crushed rock should be
placed underneath the pipe and bedding layer to provide more uniform
support for the pipe For typical bedding trench details see Plate 3

Trench Excavation
The anticipated depth of the force main excavation is 7 to 14
feet The method and equipment used for excavation should be the choice
of the contractor within practical limits
Relatively easy excavation is anticipated for the soils above the
coral Moderate excavation is anticipated in the coral layer and harder
excavation in the volcanic tuff
Trench Wall Support
The trench excavation should be provided with proper bracing in
accordance with the anticipated loading conditions traffic and
construction materials stockpile and equipment and to meet the
applicable safety requirements The design and safety of the trench
supports should be the responsibility of the contractor The new line
will be parallel and close to the existing force main Therefore the
lateral pressures on the new trench shoring would be affected by the
backfill in the adjacent trench or trenches The apparent earth pressure
diagram presented on Plate 4 may be used as a guide for the design
consultant s construction cost estimate purpose and for preliminary
design of the trench support system
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1406 00 Page ll
It is our opinion that some of the trench walls in the loose sand
deposits will require sheet piling or other means of support It is
important that adequate sheeting work be installed prior to the
excavation and maintained tight against the trench walls with proper
bracing to minimize lateral movements of the adjacent ground into the
trench excavation and resulting settlements of adjacent structures This
is particularly critical since the adjacent existing structures may be
supported on spread footings bearing on the upper sandy soils
Where practicable the trench sheeting should extend down to the
coral layer to increase the stability of the trench bottom and to
minimize the possibility of bottom heaving or quick conditions Where
the coral is too deep or not present the sheeting should extend down a
sufficient depth to minimize up lift seepage pressures
The ground adjacent to and near the top of the excavation should be
continuously monitored for cracks or dips and other indications of
movements and possible sloughing of the trench walls Such monitoring
is particularly critical in areas where existing structures and
utilities are adjacent to the trench excavation

Dewatering
Groundwater levels were generally encountered at sea level
throughout the project alignment Dewatering would normally be required
and the method should be left open to the contractor provided that
adequate stability of the excavation is maintained The deposits
encountered in the Borings 1 through 3 consisted mostly of silty sands
which are relatively permeable and susceptible to piping and quick
conditions
The project dewatering operation should be done without pumping out
the soil fines pumping clear water only and should be coordinated
with the excavation shoring support so that the stability of the
excavation is not jeopardized The operations should be carried out
without creating a quick condition or softening the bottom of the
excavation Based on the soil classification and laboratory testing it
appears that the permeability of the sandy soils may vary from 10 to
107 cm per second
To obtain better in situ information for dewatering requirements
field drawdown pumping tests could be made by the prospective bidder
since this is not included within our present scope of work
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1406 00 Page 13
Backfill
Granular material such as sand should be used for the backfill up
to about one foot above the pipe to reduce the compactive effort
required and possible damage to the pipe
Most of the excavated on site sandy and gravelly soils if free of
organic material or rock fragments in excess of 8 inches can be re used
as the upper portion of the trench backfill from above one foot over the
pipe to within 2 feet of the finished grade Backfill should be
mechanically compacted to a minimum of 90 of its maximum dry density
established by ASTM D 1557 In roadway areas the backfill below the
base course but within 2 feet of the finished grade should consist of
imported select borrow or suitable granular material approved by the
soils engineer and be compacted to 95 of its maximum dry density
If practicable the trenches should be kept free of water during
backfilling Should backfilling under the water be necessary no
mechanical tamping required coarse crushed rock material such as No
3B Fine crushed rock should be utilized to minimize excessive ground
settlement of the trench backfill in the future
Typical section of the pipe trench is presented on Plate 3

Thrust Blocks
It is our understanding that concrete blocks will be required at
pipe bends as thrust blocks These thrust blocks may be constructed on
undisturbed coral tuff stiff silt or compacted granular backfill
Fill However if the block is located in loose sand or backfill the
loose material should be over excavated laterally at least 12 inches
around the block except next to the pipe and then backfilled with
select borrow or suitable on site granular material approved by the
soils engineer The subsequent backfill should be placed in horizontal
lifts of 8 inches maximum and compacted to a minimum density of 90 of
the ASTM D 1557 78 maximum density
For the design of thrust block the following allowable lateral
earth pressures are recommended
Coral or Tuff 2000 p s f uniform lateral bearing
with 3 feet vertical soil cover
Stiff Silt Dense 200 p c f equivalent fluid pressure
Granular Backfill Fill
Corrosion Tests
To examine the corrosive characteristics of the natural soil
against the utility pipes soil resistivity tests were conducted on
selected samples The test results are summarized on Plates B 1 and B 2
in Appendix B
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1406 00 Page 15
Generally the soils tested have laboratory resistivity values from
280 to 3300 ohm cm Therefore the soils can generally be classified as
having moderate to high corrosion potential
Roadway Pavements
The borings indicate that the existing pavement sections consist of
3 to 5 inches of asphalt concrete over 4 to 8 inches of base course or
granular fill Generally the on site silty sand can provide good
pavement subgrade support For backfilling in the existing roadway the
new pavement sections should match those of the existing pavements
provided that the backfill material for subgrade soil within 2 feet of
the finished grade consists of compacted select borrow or suitable
approved granular material as discussed under the BACKFILL section
