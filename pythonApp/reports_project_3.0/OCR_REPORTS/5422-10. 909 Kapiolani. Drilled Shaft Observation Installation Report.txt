Dear Mr. Yi:
At your request and as required by the City and County of Honolulu, Geolabs, Inc.
provided geotechnical engineering observation and consultation services during the drilled
shaft foundation installation for the above project. The following letter report summarizes
our geotechnical engineering observations during installation of the drilled shaft
foundations for the 909 Kapiolani project in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.
Geolabs conducted a geotechnical engineering exploration for the 909 Kapiolani
project, and our recommendations are presented in the above report. The purpose of our
geotechnical engineering observation during construction was to provide engineering
consultation and assist in problem resolutions during the construction of the drilled shaft
foundation. In addition, we provided “Special Inspection” services by observing the drilled
shaft foundation installation at the 909 Kapiolani project for general conformance with the
project plans and specifications.
Project Considerations
The 909 Kapiolani high-rise condominium project is at 909 Kapiolani Boulevard in
the Ala Moana-Kaka’ ako area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii as shown on the
Project Location Map, Plate 1. The residential high-rise building project is situated within
the block bounded by Kapiolani Boulevard to the north, Ward Avenue to the west,
Waimanu Street to the south, and the existing Musicians Association Building to the east.

The residential development consists of a 34-story high-rise structure. The new
high-rise building (tower) has 29 levels of residential floors constructed over a five-level
base parking structure. The new high-rise building has a footprint of approximately 70 by
150 feet superimposed over the five-level base parking structure with plan dimensions of
about 140 by 300 feet.
The new high-rise building and parking structure are supported on deep
foundations consisting of cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts. Drilled shafts with
diameters of 30 and 42 inches and lengths ranging from about 45 to 120 feet were used
to support the new high-rise residential building project. As part of the construction
requirements, two static load tests were conducted to evaluate and confirm the
foundation design.
The scope of our work discussed in this letter report includes observation of the
drilled shaft foundation construction for the 909 Kapiolani project only. The general
contractor for the project is Hawaiian Dredging Construction Company, Inc., who also
installed the drilled shaft foundations.
Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the project site generally consist of fill materials at the
ground surface overlying lagoonal deposits. The fill materials generally consist of medium
dense to dense silty sands and gravel and stiff sandy silts and clayey silts extending to
depths of about 4 to 10 feet below the existing ground surface. The lagoonal deposits
underlying the fill materials generally consist of loose, highly compressible silty sands with
coralline gravel extending to depths of about 12 to 18 feet below the existing ground.
Below the lagoonal deposits, a relatively thick layer of very stiff to hard silty clays,
on the order of approximately 15 feet thick, generally was encountered and extended to
depths of approximately 30 feet below the existing ground surface. The silty clays were
followed by coralline detritus consisting of medium dense coralline sands and gravel with
some dense zones of coral formation extending down to the maximum depth explored of
approximately 132.5 feet below the existing ground surface.
It appears that the upper coral ledge generally present at depths of about 15 feet
below the ground surface in the Ala Moana-Kaka‘ako area is absent across the majority of
the project site (except the eastern end of the property), probably due to erosion by an
ancient alluvial stream channel. A lower coral/sandstone ledge, on the order of about 2 to
8 feet thick, generally was encountered across the project site between depths of about
70 and 80 feet below the existing ground surface. It should be noted that the
coral/sandstone ledges encountered at the project site are highly variable (erratic) in terms
of thickness and quality.
Groundwater was encountered in the borings drilled at depths ranging from about
4.3 to 5.0 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our current field exploration.

Summary of the Drilled Shaft Installation
A part of the project requirements, two sacrificial load test shafts were constructed
and tested. Following completion of the load tests, the drilled shaft lengths for the project
were revised based on the results of the instrumented load tests. The following
subsections provide descriptions of the load test shaft construction and discussions on the
production drilled shaft installation.
Load Test Shafts
As part of the project specifications, one sacrificial 30-inch and one sacrificial
42-inch diameter load test shafts were installed at the approximate locations indicated on
the Foundation Plan, Plate 2. The load test shafts were excavated by the drilled shaft
contractor from October 8 through 14, 2005. The 30 and 42-inch diameter load test shafts
were extended to depths of about 81.5 and 120 feet below the existing ground surface,
respectively. The drilled shaft contractor lowered the reinforcing cage with an Osterberg
Load Cell and embedment strain gage instrumentation into the drilled holes and placed
concrete in the load test shafts on October 12 and 14, 2005.
Temporary casing with a diameter of about 36 and 46 inches also were installed to
about 26 and 36.5 feet below the ground surface for the 30 and 42-inch diameter load test
shafts, respectively. Logs of the materials encountered during load test shaft excavations
and details of the load test shaft construction are presented in Appendix A of this letter
report.
After the load test shafts were installed and cured for at least 7 days,
Load Test, Inc. performed the two load tests. The 30 and 42-inch diameter load test shafts
were loaded to approximately 1,236 to 1,862 kips, respectively. Complete details of the
load test program are presented in the reports prepared by Load Test, Inc.
From the load test data, it appears that the lower bearing strata (from about 80 to
120-foot depth) are weaker and did not provide as high friction values as the intermediate
level bearing strata (from about 40 to 80-foot depth). Therefore, the deeper drilled shafts
will need to be increased slightly in length to support the structural loads imposed on the
foundations of the tower structure. Some of the intermediate depth drilled shafts may be
reduced in length slightly to support the design compressive load capacities. Based on the
results of the two load tests conducted, we recommend the drilled shaft lengths for the
project be revised as shown in the following table to support the allowable compressible
loads (Design Capacity).

Production Drilled Shafts
Prior to commencement of the production shaft installation program, the proposed
drilled shaft locations within the former Sultan Building footprint (between Gridlines A-H
and 9-12) were probed for existing pilings and obstructions. Each of the 30 and 42-inch
diameter drilled shaft locations were probed by drilling 2 and 3 holes, respectively, to a
depth of about 6 feet below the existing ground surface for a total of 85 probe holes at
35 shaft locations. In general, the probing program did not encounter obstructions that
appeared to consist of old pilings.
The drilled shaft contractor installed 106 production shafts from October 17, 2005
through January 6, 2006. The as-built diameters and the lengths of the production drilled
shafts installed for the project are summarized on Plates 3.1 through 3.4 of this letter
report. Representatives from Geolabs observed the drilled shaft excavation, placement of
reinforcing cage, and concrete operations during the installation of the production drilled
shafts.
In general, the production drilled shafts were excavated using a Bauer 28 drill rig
equipped with 30 and 42-inch diameter auger, core barrel, and clean-out bucket similar to
the load test shafts. Temporary steel casings were installed to depths of about 25 to
36.5 feet below the ground surface in the production drilled shafts. In general, 36 and
46-inch diameter temporary casings were used for 30 and 42-inch diameter drilled shafts,

respectively. Because the drilled holes stayed open without significant caving in of the
sidewalls, polymer slurry was not used during excavation of some of the production drilled
shafts. After excavation of the production shafts to their designated tip elevations, the
reinforcing cages were lowered into the drilled holes to the appropriate elevations with the
use of a crane. After the reinforcing cage was installed in the drilled hole, concrete was
placed through a 4-inch steel tremie pipe starting from the bottom. During concrete
placement, we observed that at least 5 feet of concrete head was maintained above the
bottom of the tremie pipe. In addition, sand content measurements were performed on an
as-needed basis prior to the concrete placement. Where the measurements indicated that
the sand content exceeded one percent, Insta-Floc (slurry flocculent) was used to remove
the fine-grained solids that were suspended in the slurry.
Due to the presence of groundwater within the drilled shaft holes, concrete was
placed in the drilled holes using the tremie method. Concrete with slump ranging from 7 to
9 inches was used for the drilled shafts. Mid Pacific Testing Services, Inc. also monitored
the concrete used for the drilled shafts, including the performance of slump tests and
compression tests. Logs of the individual drilled shafts installed and the materials
encountered in each of the drilled shafts during shaft excavation are presented in
Appendix A.
Subsequent to the installation of Shaft No. C-94, the design shaft length was
increased from 55 feet to 65 feet. Based on the actual foundation demand and as-built
length of the drilled shaft, it appears that the subject drilled shaft was slightly over-stressed
(1.033 of capacity). According to the project structural engineer, we understand that Shaft
No. C-94 is acceptable without the need for additional remedial efforts.
During drilling for Shaft No. C-65, several obstructions were encountered at about
4 to 6 feet below the existing ground surface. The obstructions were slightly visible and
appeared to be a sheet pile wall north of the shaft and two concrete piles south of the
shaft. Shaft No. C-65 was subsequently relocated 5 feet in the Ewa direction and Shaft
No. C-66 was relocated 5 feet in the Diamond Head direction to accommodate the
obstructions at the original locations.
Based on the observations of our field representatives, it is our professional opinion
that the production drilled shafts supporting the 909 Kapiolani project were installed in
general accordance with the intent of the project plans and specifications and the
geotechnical recommendations presented in our geotechnical engineering report for the
project dated August 29, 2005 and subsequent recommendations. In addition, it is our
professional opinion that the drilled shafts have been installed to sufficient depths to
provide the allowable design load capacities.
