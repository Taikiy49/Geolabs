REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae Volcano
became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank was partially
buried below Koolau lava banking against its eastern flank These banked or ponded
lavas formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau on which the project
site is located
The project area is composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lava of the
Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows of jointed
dense vesicular basalt rock formation with inter bedded thin clinker layers In situ
chemical weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million
years The weathering process has formed a mantle of residual soils which grade to
saprolite with depth In general saprolite is mainly composed of silty material and is
typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite grades to basaltic rock
formation with increasing depth

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by stiff to
hard residual and saprolite soil We did not encounter groundwater in the borings drilled at
_ the time of our exploration
We understand that the new GAC contactor units will be integrated with the
existing units within Waipahu Wells Ill We also understand new units will be connected
to the Waipio Heights Wells and Wells through approximately of transmission
pipe lines and other necessary appurtenances A detailed discussion and our
geotechnical engineering design recommendations are presented in the following
sections herein
Transmission Pipe Lines
Based on the current design concepts the project will include the installation of
new transmission lines consisting of of 16 inch diameter pipes and 500 LF of
24 inch diameter pipes Our borings generally encountered a stiff surface fill layer
underlain by stiff to hard residual and saprolite deposits to the maximum depth explored
of about 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface We did not encountered
groundwater in the four borings at the time of our field exploration
We anticipate that new transmission lines will be installed using conventional open
trench method and the invert depths of the new lines will be about 10 feet below the
existing ground surface From the results of our field exploration we anticipate that the
trench may be readily excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment such as
excavators However the remnant rock anticipated within the saprolite deposits might
required some chipping
Earth Pressure Loads on Transmission Pipe Lines
Loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the trench the size of the
pipes the unit weight of backfill material and the frictional resistance between the
backfill material and the trench walls To calculate the vertical loads on the buried
transmission pipes we recommend using an average unit weight of 120 pounds
per cubic foot pcf for the backfill material and a coefficient of friction of 0 35 Earth
forces upon the pipe generally increase rapidly with the width of the trench

loads on the buried pipes should also be considered for the portions of pipes within

Pipe Line Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe line has a great effect upon the
load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore pipe bedding is an important design
consideration In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the transmission
lines The granular bedding should extend beyond the sides of the pipe a minimum
width of 24 inches
Backfill
In general free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be used for the initial trench backfill up to
about 12 inches above the pipe lines to provide adequate support around the pipe
lines and to reduce the need for compaction of the backfill thus reducing the
possibility of pipe damage Compaction by water ponding or jetting should not be
allowed
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of general fill material The
backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
placed in maximum 8 inch horizontal loose lifts and mechanically compacted to no
less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable
future ground subsidence
Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill
below the pavement grade should be moisture conditioned to about 2 percent
above the optimum moisture and compacted to at least 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed
as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D 1557
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to
the maximum dry density
In order to reduce the potential for pavement distress resulting from settlement of
utility trench backfill a temporary pavement section may be considered at the
completion of trench work A permanent pavement section may be placed after the
backfilled area has come to equilibrium under backfill earth loads
In general backfill materials should consist of non expansive granular materials
generally less than 3 inches in maximum dimension with sufficient fines to prevent
the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass In addition we believe that the

Pipe Settlement
Primary settlement of the new pipe installation is generally caused by the difference
in the unit weight of the excavated original earth and the compacted backfill
material placed over the pipeline The net increase in loading may cause settlement
of the underlying subsoils below the trench invert Based on our exploration and
analyses small primary settlements on the order of about less than inch may be
anticipated for the new transmission line alignment
Excavation
In order to install the transmission pipe lines we anticipate that excavations below
the existing ground surface will be required Based on the soil condition and estimated
inverts elevation we envision that temporary shoring of the excavations will be required for
the transmission line installation
Excavation Method
In general the contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used
for excavation subject to practical limits and safety considerations Based on our
field exploration it is anticipated that normal heavy excavation equipment such as
excavators might be used to open the trench However some chipping might also
be required within the saprolite deposits The excavated soils should be stockpiled
no closer than a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the excavation measured
from the outside edge of the excavation in order to reduce the potential for
appreciable ground movement

Excavation Support
As previously mentioned we anticipate that the new transmission pipe lines will be
installed about 10 feet below the existing ground surface Because some of the
pipe lines will be along the roadway sloping of the sides of the trenches may not be
possible due to the limited space available Therefore temporary shoring of the
sides of the trenches may be necessary to facilitate the proposed trench
excavations and to protect personnel working in the trench
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with all applicable
safety requirements The contractor should retain a qualified geotechnical engineer
to design and evaluate the shoring system to be used The contractor should be
solely responsible for the adequacy and safety of the shoring installation His her
representative who should be required to be continuously present on site during
excavation and construction work will have the best opportunity to promptly
observe changing conditions during construction such as unforeseen subsurface
soil conditions unexpectedly high groundwater table inappropriate construction
sequence or techniques etc which may adversely affect shoring stability
Even if good construction procedures are followed some movement of the shoring
system and the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses
during excavation Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to
accurately estimate the magnitude of ground movement The magnitude of ground
movement also greatly depends upon workmanship such as how quickly and
tightly the shoring supports are installed the subsoil conditions the size of the
excavation and the rate of excavation Therefore it is important to realize that the
shoring should be installed properly and as early as practical and if necessary the
adjacent ground should be monitored continuously for cracks dips and or other
indications of movements
Manholes and Vaults
Manholes and vaults are anticipated along the new transmission lines We expect
that invert depth will be about 10 feet below the existing ground surface for the manholes
and less than 5 feet for the vaults Based on our field exploration we anticipate that both
structures will bear on the stiff residual soils
Manhole Structure
In general we recommend that the bottom of the manhole vault structures bear on
6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation over the stiff residual
soils encountered in our drilled borings Based on our engineering analyses we
believe that the net increase in bearing pressure on the underlying soil deposits
resulting from the manhole vault construction would be minimal Settlements on the
order of less than inch are anticipated for both structures

tests were designed to evaluate the corrosivity of the underlying subsurface materials
encountered at the project site Based on the test results refer to Plate B 11 the

contact with the soils be properly designed to be protected against potential corrosion
