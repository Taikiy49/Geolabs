As requested we have made a preliminary geologic reconnaissance of
the near surface soil conditions below the above referenced site The
purpose of the reconnaissance was to evaluate the suitability of the near
surface soils for design of the site grading and building foundation
support for the proposed fire station
The location of the project site and its vicinity is shown on the
Project Location Map Plate l
The scope of our investigation included the excavation of six test
pits visual observations of the existing site conditions laboratory
testing and engineering analysis
The results of our field explorations and laboratory tests which
were used in the engineering analysis to formulate our conclusions and
recommendations are presented in the appendices of this report

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by excavating 6
test pits to depths of 3 5 to 4 feet below the existing ground surface at
the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 Soil samples
obtained fron the test pits were selectively tested in our laboratory to
further examine their geotechnical engineering characteristics Detailed
descriptions of our field exploration the log of test pits the
laboratory testing and results are presented in the appendices
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed fire station consists of a one story CMU concrete
structure approximately 40 by 130 feet in plan dimension Based on the
preliminary plans provided to us the building will be set back about 300
feet from Queen Kaahumanu Highway
Conerete slab on grade construction is contemplated for the
structure Finish floor elevation will be about 116 feet
Fills up to about 10 to 12 feet deep are planned for the northwest
side of the site and lesser fill depths over the rest of the site Some
shallow cuts are proposed at the northeast corner and eastern sides of the
site where the access road enters the site from the highway
A large 8 9 feet diameter CMP culvert crosses under Queen
Kaahumanu Highway southeast of the site Water runoff through this pipe
may pass close to or through the southern boundary of the site

CLIMATIC CONDITIONS
The average annual rainfall at the project site is between 5 to 10
inches and the average temperature varies between 75 and 85 degrees F
The area may be subjected to strong tradewinds forced through the wind gap
between the Kohala Mountains and Mauna Kea
GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an
area of approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the
activity of 5 shield volcanoes These are Kohala which is long
extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity during recent geologic times
Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both
still active
The site is leoated on the western flank of Mauna Kea mountain
which makes up the northern part of the island and consists largely of
the lower member of the Laupahoehoe Volcanic Series The lava flows at
the site are Aa basalt types with dense zones of hard rock and loose
zones of clinkers The clinker phase is highly permeable Lava tubes are
generally not encountered in Aa flows
A thin dry desiccated and erodible layer of volcanic ash locally
referred to as Pahala Ash covers the surface of the flows
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The site slopes gently downward towards the west at about 2 to 3 per
cent gradients with steeper slopes along the northerly boundaries of the
site

The existing elevation of the site generally varies from about 96
to 123 feet Elevations near the highway are higher about 140 feet
The topography is characterized by fairly smooth rolling hills that
are sparsely covered with dry grasses and basaltic gravel and cobbles
Soil Conditions
The field exploration disclosed that the site is generally covered
with a thin less than 1 to 2 foot thick surface soil layer of dry
desiccated clayey to sandy silt volcanic ash mixed with cobbles and
boulders over lava rock formations The ash soil has little cohesion and
may be highly susceptible to wind and water erosion
The ash soil layer in the vicinity of Test Pit No 2 appears to be
thicker than the rest of the site and may vary from 3 feet or more in
depth Otherwise the subsurface conditions throughout the site appears
to be velatively uniform
Below the ash layer is the Aa lava flow which is composed of a
clinker gravel cobbles and ash mixture to dense basalt rock
No water was encountered in the test pits during the field
exploration
DISCUSSTION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The field explorations disclosed that the site is underlain by a
thin generally less than 2 feet thick layer of volcanic ash mixed with
cobbles and boulders over lava rock formations From a geotechnical point
of view the site is feasible for the proposed construction The designer
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1722 00 Page 5
should consider the close proximity of possible heavy water flow from the
highway culvert located about 300 feet southeast of the site that may
affect the stability of the proposed site filling
proposed structure related roads and parking areas
Volcanic ash soils extending down to about 3 to 4 foot depths were
encountered at one location at the site Fills up to about 8 feet or
more are planned for this location Laboratory consolidation test data
from similar soils nearby indicate that the ash soil structure may be
subject to collapse when completely saturated Therefore in order to
minimize the possibility of settlement in this area and below the building
site we recommend that the thin ash deposit be removed and stockpiled
and the excavation backfilled and compacted with on site select granular
materials Some of the ash soils can be mixed with the excavated rocky
materials for backfilling The estimated limits of this soft ash deposit
is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Site Grading
Site Preparation
Prior to construction of roadway and site grading surface
vegetation debris stockpiles and organic material should be removed
and wasted off site In areas to receive fill or at grade
construction tree roots should be removed to a minimum depth of 3
feet and the resulting excavation backfilled and compacted to 90 of
its maximum density as established by ASTM D 1557 78
In fairly level areas prior to receiving fill the surface ash
soils below the building should be removed and stockpiled for use

In general the excavated rock material less than 12 inches in
size may be used as general fill material If the excavated rock
material does not contain sufficient fines to produce a well graded
material the surficial ash soil may be mixed into the excavated
rocky material to increase the percentage of fines to provide a
better graded material for compaction and increase the stability of
the fills For fills within 2 feet of finished grade below
structures and for subsequent structural backfill within the
building limits the maximum size of particle in the fill should be
reduced tc 6 inches to facilitate compaction

Embankment fills should be placed in horizontal loose lifts up to 18
inches thick and compacted to a minimum of 90 of its maximum
density
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in
excavated voleanic rock material due to the large particle size
Instead a testing program consisting of visual observations and
tests strips to determine the lift thickness and number of passes of
a compactor required to achieve the desired compaction should be
conducted at the start of the project The number of passes can
then be used as the criteria for compaction

Slopes
In general two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V fill slopes are
recommended Low fills less than 10 feet in height may use a

It appears that most of the cut slopes will be in varying materials
such as ash clinker and dense rock An average design cut slope of

Soil slopes should be protected as soon as possible after
construction to minimize the erosion potential
Foundations
We believe the on site rocky ground and compacted granular fill
resulting from the above site grading can provide suitable foundation
support for the proposed building
Spread and wall footing foundations may be used for the proposed
structure
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 1722 00 Page 8
All exterior footing bottoms should be embedded a minimum of 24
inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade The minimum embedment
for interior footings may be reduced to 18 inches below the finished
grade
For the footings bearing on the compacted clinker rock fill an
allowable net soil bearing pressure of 3 000 p s f may be used The
recommended soil bearing could be increased for footing bearing on the
natural clinker densely backfilled with a choking fill material to fill
the near surface voids in the natural formation We believe if required
an allowable bearing value up to 4 000 p s f could be used These values
may be increased by one third when temporary wind or seismic loads are
considered
Slabs and spread foundations located close to the tops of the fill
slopes should have sufficient embedment to obtain a minimum horizontal
set back distance of 8 feet measured fron the outer edge of the footing to
the slope face
The ground surface should be shaped to divert surface runoff away
from building foundations and slabs
It is anticipated that the footings if constructed in accordance
with the above recommendations will have negligible settlements
On Grade Slab
Based on a non expansive slab subgrade condition anticipated at this
site on grade slab should be placed over a 4 inch layer of base course
compacted to 90 of its maximum density as established by ASTM D 1557 78

The thickened edge around the on grade slab should extend a minimum of 12
inches below the lowest adjacent finished ground surface
Depending on the structure of the underlying rock formation a
choking layer consisting of well graded rock materials may be required
between the underlying rugged rock surface and the upper cushion fill or
concrete slab
Utilities
It is anticipated that most of the trenching will be done in the
unconsolidated clinker material However some boulders or areas of dense
basalt may be encountered especially in the deeper trenches
Select on site material generally less than 6 inch in size may be
used as backfill to 2 feet above the top of culvert and could be compacted
by water tamping The ash soils are not suitable for water tamping and
would require mechanical tamping if this soil is used as trench backfill
Above this level general embankment fill of 12 inch minus may be used in
accordance with our recommendations for fill up to within 2 feet of
finished subgrade
Roadway Pavement
Based on our observation of the near surface materials on the site
ash clinker rock formation or rock fill is anticipated at the subgrade
level Therefore the preliminary pavement design is as follows

Design Review
Plans and specifications for site grading and utility constructions
should be forwarded to the soils engineer for review and written comments
prior to construction to see that the intent of our recommendations is
included
Construction Monitoring
To a degree the performance of any project is dependent upon the
construction procedures and quality Hence observations of site work and
fill placement should be carried out by a soils engineer to permit
correlation between the preliminary data obtained during this exploration
and actual conditions encountered during construction
If conditions encountered during construction are different from
those presented in this report then appropriate modifications to the
design should be made
The recommendations given in this report are contingent upon the
above observations
