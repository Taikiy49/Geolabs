INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Alii Care Home project located in Punaluu on the island of
Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations site
grading and pavements only The findings and recommendations presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to construct a new care home facility adjacent to the Ponds at
Punaluu Assisted Living Facility to the west of Kamehameha Highway in Punaluu on the
island of Oahu Hawaii Based on the information provided the care home facility will
consist of a 120 unit nursing home with related buildings a paved parking area and access
driveways The main building will be single story with three radiating wings and
encompass approximately 58 000 square feet of floor space It is anticipated that the
structure will be of composite masonry concrete and wood construction with slab on grade
floors
Based on the information provided we understand that it is proposed to raise the
finish grade elevation at the site to an elevation of approximately 10 feet above Mean Sea
Level MSL This will require the placement of new fills ranging from about 2 to 6 feet in
thickness above the existing ground surface

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island After the main shield building phase of Koolau the
volcanic dome became deeply eroded On the windward side of the island combined
wind rain and wave erosion cut the coastline back while forming steep cliffs
The project site is located on the windward northeastern flank of Koolau and its
subsurface conditions are directly related to this deep erosion and littoral processes at the
coast The subsurface conditions within the project area are dominated by beach and
lagoonal deposits which are intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the
stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The proposed Alii Care Home project site is located to the west of Kamehameha
Highway and to the east of the existing Ponds at Punaluu Assisted Living Facility in
Punaluu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The project site is bounded by fallow land to the
northeast and northwest the Ponds at Punaluu facility to the southwest and the access
road leading to the Ponds facility to the southeast as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on the current conceptual plans it is proposed to construct the care home
facility in the central portion of the site At the time of our field exploration the site was
fallow or vacant land lightly forested with vegetation consisting predominately of java plum
and california grass with some christmas berry haole koa and castor Trail cutting was
required to access the site for the field exploration work
The topographic relief of the site is low with a barely perceptible slope from the
southwest to the northeast A ditch traverses the northeastern portion of the site in a
southeast to northwest direction There are also two 2 marshy or wetland areas on the
site with the larger in the southern portion and a smaller area in the northern portion
Based on visual observations during the field exploration it is believed that the site
was previously used for agricultural purposes such as pasturage and row crops
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
eight borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 8 to depths ranging from about 11 5 to
21 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on the field exploration it appears that in general the subsurface conditions
at the project site are relatively uniform Our field exploration generally encountered a
surficial fill crust about 2 feet thick consisting of a dark gray to black clay topsoil overlying
compressible littoral deposits of loose silty sands to depths of about 11 to 20 feet below
the existing ground surface These loose sand deposits were underlain by soft to medium
stiff alluvial materials consisting of sandy clays with gravels extending to the maximum
depth explored of about 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface

may be influenced by seasonal precipitation and storm surge conditions

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Compressible loose soils as much as 20 feet thick were underlying the site These
compressible soils have an impact on the site grading infrastructure construction and
foundation design for the project
Based on the proposed construction and the level of groundwater encountered in
the borings we do not anticipate the need for dewatering during foundation construction
However dewatering may be required for infrastructure installation such as sewer and
drainage
Ground Settlement
Ground settlements are anticipated from the weight of the new site fill as well as
structural loads due to compression of the soft clay and loose sand layers below the site
Estimated settlements due to the various loading conditions anticipated are as follows
Two to six feet of new fill 4 to 8 inches
Structural column loads of up to 20 kips less than 1 inch
Due to the limited thickness of the clayey portions of the compressible layer and the
relatively high permeability of the loose sand it is anticipated that settlements will occur
relatively rapidly We estimate that 90 percent of the settlements may occur within about
2 to 3 months if the surcharge program recommended herein is implemented
Site Grading
Site grading involving the placement of about 2 to 6 feet of fill is anticipated to raise
the site to a finish grade elevation of 10 feet MSL In order for the majority of the ground
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4078 00 Page 6
settlements to occur a surcharge program with a waiting period of about 2 to 3 months is
recommended after completion of the initial site filling and prior to building construction
Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Unsuitable materials such as vegetation and
rubbish should be removed and disposed of properly at an off site location
Due to the thin surface crust encountered at the site and the shallow depth to
groundwater it is recommended that light equipment be used for the clearing and
grubbing operations to minimize the potential of pumping or bogging the equipment
down in the surface clay soils
In fill areas the original ground should then be lightly proofrolled for 4 to 6 passes
with rubber tired equipment to reveal soft and yielding areas Loose or soft spots
encountered at the subgrade level within the fill area should be recompacted if it is
sandy or removed if it is clayey or organic and then replaced with clean granular
material such as No 3B Fine ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
The open ditch traversing the project site should be cleaned of organic matter and
rubbish to native soils and backfilled to a minimum of 18 inches above water level
with clean granular material such as No 3B Fine ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
After the proofrolling and backfilling of the ditch and other soft areas the area under
the building footprint and extending for approximately 10 feet beyond the building
limits should be covered with a layer of geotextile filter fabric such as Mirafi 180N
to aid in supporting the building area and reducing future differential settlements
Fill Material and Placement
It is anticipated that off site borrow will be required for the fill material Imported fill
material should consist of non expansive select granular materials less than
3 inches in maximum size with less than 20 percent passing the No 200 sieve The
material should be tested and approved by the Geotechnical Engineer prior to
transportation to the site

The fill should be placed in maximum 8 inch thick loose level lifts and compacted
to a minimum of 90 percent of its maximum dry density as determined by ASTM
Test Method D 1557 91
In building and pavement areas the upper 12 inches of subgrade should be
compacted to 95 percent of its maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test
Method D 1557 91 to support the structure and pavements
Fill Settlements
Due to soft and loose subsoil conditions the estimated general ground settlements
induced by the weight of the new fill are on the order of up to 4 to 8 inches To
accelerate the rate of settlement and to reduce the remaining amount of settlement
to tolerable amounts it is recommended that a surcharge program be implemented
upon completion of the site mass grading With the surcharge program it is
estimated that 90 percent of the fill settlements may occur in about 2 to 3 months
Therefore the site filling should start as early as practicable and the building
construction and infrastructure installation activities should be delayed for a period
of about 2 to 3 months after completion of the site filling and surcharge placement
lt is further recommended that the construction of slabs on grade and rigid
pavements be delayed toward the end of the project in order to allow maximum
ground settlement to occur
Slopes
A maximum slope ratio of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V is recommended
for permanent fill slopes at the project
Surcharge Program
To aid in the acceleration of anticipated ground settlements we recommend a
surcharge program prior to construction The program should consist of the placement of
5 feet of temporary fill over the proposed fill area prior to building construction The
surcharge material should be similar to that of the construction fill material
A slope ratio of up to 1H 1V may be used for the temporary surcharge material The
surcharge fill should be placed in level lifts of 1 foot high and extend laterally a minimum
of 2 feet beyond the footprint of the fill area Mechanical compaction is not required for the
surcharge fill Adequate precautions must be taken to preclude erosion of the surcharge
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4078 00 Page 8
fill slopes during construction and the subsequent surcharge period to attain the required
consolidation Upon completion of the surcharge period the surcharge material should be
removed to finish subgrade elevation The subgrade should then be scarified to a depth
of about 10 inches moisture conditioned to near optimum moisture content and
recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Based on our engineering analyses we believe that a settlement waiting period of
about 8 to 12 weeks would be adequate to achieve approximately 80 to 90 percent
consolidation However it should be recognized that it is very difficult to accurately predict
the exact time required for the filled ground to settle since the settlement rate will be
affected by variations of the subsoil structure and the history of the subsoil deposition
For the compressible subsoils encountered at the site we believe that the estimated
settlement period could vary as much as 50 to 100 percent from the actual settlement
period required Therefore the actual settlement rate should be monitored and provisions
should be made for potential delays in the construction schedule if a longer surcharge
period is required
Settlement Monitoring
To monitor the actual settlement rate during the surcharge program we recommend
that a minimum of eight settlement gauges be installed in the fill areas A typical
settlement gauge is shown on Plate 3 The gauges should be read optically by a qualified
professional surveyor and the readings should be transmitted to Geolabs Hawaii for
review The readings should be taken on a weekly basis until the settlement rate has
stabilized
A proper contingency plan should be devised to accommodate the potential delay
in the construction schedule and the possible extra costs that may be involved t should
be recognized that it is very difficult to accurately predict the exact time required for the
filled ground to settle because of variable localized soil conditions Therefore the actual
settlement rate should be monitored during construction after the surcharge program is
complete
Spread Footing Foundations
It is our opinion that shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous
footings bearing on the new fill soils placed at the site may be used to support the

proposed building Based on the subsoil conditions encountered assuming the surcharge
program is followed and the structural loads anticipated for the proposed development
footing settlements on the order of about inch southern area to 1 inch northern and
northeastern areas may be anticipated with differential settlement of approximately the
estimated total settlement
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot psf may be
used for design of footing foundations bearing on fill soils placed compacted and
surcharged as recommended herein This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and
may be increased by 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces We recommend that the footing foundations be structurally connected together
with tie beams to improve the rigidity of the foundation thus reducing the anticipated
differential settlement
In general the footings should be placed as high as practicable to take advantage
of the fill crust thereby reducing the footing load applied over the underlying compressible
deposits To utilize the surficial fill crust we recommend that footings be embedded to a
maximum depth of about 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade If
foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be embedded
below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench
or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid
surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce
the potential for foundation settlement
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil Resistance due to
passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds
per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soil around footings is
well compacted or undisturbed Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive
resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected A coefficient of friction of
0 35 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted near surface soils
It is recommended that the bottom of all footings bear on compacted fill If soft
and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations they
should be over excavated to expose firm materials The over excavation should be

compaction or the footing bottom should be extended down to underlying competent

Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed building
Based on the existing topography and the proposed finish floor elevation we anticipate
that fills up to about 2 to 6 feet high will be required to raise the existing ground surface to
the proposed finished subgrade Because of the soft loose deposits underlying the site
some amount of ground settlement may be anticipated under the new fill loads Therefore
in order to reduce the potential for distress due to differential movements between the slab
and foundations we recommend that free floating slabs on grade with no structural
connections to the wall and column foundations be used Joint filler and sealant may be
used to fill the opening between the edge of slab and other structural elements In
addition construction of the slabs on grade should be delayed as long as practicable in
order to allow maximum ground settlement to occur Alternatively the slabs may be
reinforced and structurally connected to resist the differential movements
Slab subgrades should be prepared in accordance with the recommendations
presented in the Site Preparation section of this report For slabs on grade that are not
subjected to traffic loading we recommend that a 4 inch cushion layer of No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used below the slabs For slabs that will be
covered with moisture sensitive floor coverings a vapor barrier membrane may be
incorporated on top of the gravel cushion layer to reduce the potential for damage to the
floor coverings by infiltration of moisture A 2 inch layer of moist fine sand may be
considered above the vapor membrane to protect the membrane and to aid in concrete
curing
For slabs on grade that will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as concrete
driveways or similar a 6 inch layer of base course compacted to 95 percent relative
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4078 00 Page 11
compaction is recommended for more uniform support The base course should consist
of crushed basalt aggregate We recommend that the concrete slab be designed by a
Structural Engineer for the intended loads and should be reinforced as a structural slab to

Wetland Areas
Two wetland areas have been identified to the north and south of the site Site

water systems in both areas may be connected Since the site filling may reduce the flow
of subsurface water between the wetlands we recommend that a minimum of two
perforate pipe subdrains be installed between the wetland areas prior to filling

Dewatering
Since groundwater was encountered at depths of about 2 feet below the existing
ground surface at the project site it is anticipated that dewatering will be required for the
construction and installation of new utility lines Dewatering should be conducted so that
only clear water is pumped with no removal of fine material from the soils Dewatering
should also be conducted so that there is no excessive turbulence or boiling which could
induce a quick condition resulting in disturbance of the soils around the area being
dewatered
Discharge of dewatering effluent should be conducted in accordance with the
appropriate Local State and Federal requirements As previously discussed there are
clayey soils at the existing ground surface at the site These soils have relatively low
permeability in comparison to the underlying sandy soils and the anticipated imported fill
soils If the dewatering effluent is discharged on site by a recharge method such as an
infiltration basin or through backtrenches care should be taken that the recharge structure
penetrates the clayey soils to avoid perching of the groundwater at the base of the new fill
and possible disturbance of the fill Since it will be necessary to backfill the existing open
ditch traversing the site with clean granular material consideration should be given to using
the granular backfill as part of the dewatering effluent discharge system
Drainage
The finished grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems

be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from the
foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and
slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have
concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent loss of strength of the near surface soils The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of the concrete
to reduce the potential for water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon
as possible and maintained to drain all surface run off away from the slabs and footing
foundations

APPENDIXA
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling eight
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 8 to depths of about 11 5 to 21 5 feet below
the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight
augers and hollow stem augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 8
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch O D Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
It should be noted that the blow counts may be too high due to excessive friction on
the hammer line and presence of angular coral gravel in the subsurface materials
