SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered residual soils extending to the maximum 
depth explored of about five feet below the existing ground surface. The residual soils 
generally consisted of medium stiff to hard clayey silts, sandy clay, and silty clays. We did 
not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. 
We understand that the installation of retention and infiltration systems for the on-
site management of stormwater runoff is being considered.  Based on the infiltration 
testing performed at the project site, infiltration rates ranging from about 1.4 to 22.5 inches 
per hour were measured. Based on these results, it appears that the infiltration system or 
similar methods of stormwater management would be feasible for this project site.  
The following preliminary design infiltration rates may be utilized for the project: 
• 1.75 inches per hour at the existing retention basin 
• 2.50 inches per hour near the existing softball fields and the new tree farm 
• 1.50 inches per hour at the future multi-purpose field and parking areas 
Due to the variability of the subsurface conditions, the infiltration capacity of the 
disposal system should be confirmed by conducting additional infiltration tests during 
construction. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
design recommendations.

1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project involves the construction of a parking lot on Field 4 of the Patsy T. 
Mink Central Oahu Regional Park. The original new parking lot provided approximately 
323 parking spaces. We understand that the new parking lot will be reduced to about 
200 parking spaces. We understand that two infiltration tests are desired at the existing 
retention basin near the golf course for stormwater management purposes. Stormwater 
from the new parking lot will be drained into the existing retention basin. In addition, we 
understand that four additional infiltration tests are desired at multiple locations across 
the park complex for drainage analysis purposes. The proposed locations of the new 
parking areas and desired infiltration test locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  
We understand that the bottom of the stormwater disposal systems will be about 
5 feet below the existing ground surface. Based on the information provided, we envision 
that site grading may consist of minor cuts and fills on the order of about 5 feet or less.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early 
Pleistocene in age. 
The project site is located within the Schofield Plateau. The Schofield Plateau was 
formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded against the already eroded slopes 
of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene Epoch. The dips of the lava beds are near 
horizontal (between 3 to 5 degrees from horizontal). The lava flows on the plateau have 
undergone in-situ weathering to depths of 50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the 
reddish color of the soil. 
This in-situ weathering of the lava has formed a mantle of residual soils that grade 
to saprolite with increasing depth. In general, saprolite is composed mainly of silty 
material, while residual soils are more clayey. Both residual and saprolitic soils are typical 
of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks. The residual and saprolitic soils grade to 
basaltic rock formation with increased depth. Spheroidally weathered boulders within the 
residual and saprolitic soils are typical in the Schofield Plateau.

Subsurface Conditions 
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling six borings, designated as 
Boring Nos. I-1 through I-6, extending to a depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the new drainage improvement locations are 
generally underlain by medium stiff to hard residual soils extending to the maximum depth 
explored of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface. We did not encounter 
groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. 
The installation of retention and infiltration systems for the on-site management of 
the stormwater runoff is being considered.  Based on the results of the infiltration testing, 
it appears that infiltration systems for stormwater management are feasible for the project 
site. Detailed discussions of our findings and geotechnical recommendations are 
presented in the following sections. 
3.1 
Subsurface Infiltration 
In order to obtain subsurface infiltration information, we conducted falling head 
infiltration tests in six borings (Boring Nos. I-1 through I-6), as shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2.  
These tests were performed in general accordance with the procedures in 
Appendix D of the State of Maryland Department of the Environment “Stormwater Design 
Manual, Volumes I and II” (rev. 2009). These procedures are consistent with other state’s 
procedures and generally may be considered an industry standard. 
The field infiltration tests were performed by augering borings to the selected test 
depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface. Upon reaching the test depth, a 
4-inch inside diameter PVC solid casing was set to the bottom of the drilled hole to allow 
infiltration only through the soil exposed at the bottom of the boring. Falling head 
infiltration tests were performed to determine the infiltration rates of the underlying 
subsurface materials. Each test consisted of four trials of filling the casing with about 
24 inches of water and taking periodic readings over a one-hour trial period. The 
infiltration rate is calculated based on the result of the fourth and last trial for each test 
location. The results are summarized in the following table. Detailed information on the 
tests is presented in Appendix C.

information obtained from the field borings and bulk samples. Variations of subsoil

the methods used.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling six borings, designated as Boring Nos. I-1 through I-6, extending to a depth of 
about 5 feet below the existing ground surface.  In addition, two bulk samples of the near-
surface soils (designated as Bulk-1 and Bulk-2) were obtained. The borings were drilled 
using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with augers. The approximate boring and bulk 
sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2 Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-6. 
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows required 
for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation 
of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils.  The samples were remolded to near the optimum moisture content.  The test 
results are presented on Plates B-2 and B-3.

Infiltration Tests 
In order to provide information on the infiltration rates of the on-site soils, we 
conducted six falling head infiltration tests at the boring locations shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2.  These tests were performed in general accordance with the procedures in 
Appendix 
D 
of 
the 
State 
of 
Maryland, 
Department 
of 
the 
Environment, 
“Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I and II” (Rev. 2009). These procedures are 
consistent with other state’s procedures and may generally be considered an industry 
standard. 
The procedure used for the infiltration consisted of augering boreholes to depths 
of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface.  After reaching the test depth, a solid 
PVC pipe, or well casing, was installed in the borehole flush with the bottom so that water 
could only infiltrate into the soil through the exposed bottom soil. 
Water was placed into the open pipe and allowed to pre-saturate the soil prior to 
conducting the infiltration test.  Falling head infiltration tests were performed to determine 
the infiltration rates of the underlying subsurface materials.  Each test consisted of four 
trials of filling the casing with approximately 24 inches of water and taking periodic 
readings over a one hour trial period.
