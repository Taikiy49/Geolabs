2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the
island The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the
island
The project site is situated on a narrow coastal plain and pediment which was
formed by the accumulation of a relatively thick wedge of colluvium alluvium and
marine sediments along the base of an extensive wave cut cliff on the northern flank of
the Waianae Volcano Geological conditions at the site and in its immediate vicinity can
generally be delineated by the location and slope of the various portions of the site In
the area close to Farrington Highway where the slope is relatively level the shallow
subsurface conditions generally consist of marine sediments dune sands and recent
alluvium The soils in this area generally have poor engineering characteristics
e g soft soil conditions and high ground water table
As the slope steepens to the inland side of the site the subsurface conditions
become dominated by older alluvial materials that have accumulated in alluvial fans
originating from major drainageways draining to the north from the Waianae Range
These alluvial materials consist of interbedded clays silts gravels and some areas of
boulder conglomerate In some areas the alluvium has weathered to a residual type
soil and the underlying conglomerates have weathered to a saprolitic state The soils in
these areas are generally more competent however they exhibit moderate to high
shrink swell potentials
Further inland as the property approaches the base of the Waianae Range the
geology grades to an accumulation of talus and colluvium at the base of the wave cut
cliff This interbedded talus and colluvium generally consists of angular boulders and

2 2 Site Description
The project site is in the Mokuleia area on the northern side of the Island of
Oahu Hawaii The project site is along a narrow coastal plain and the northern
pediment of the Waianae Range and is bordered to the east by former sugar cane
plantation land and to the west by Dillingham Airfield and the now closed Dillingham
Quarry The project location is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on our field observations most of the project site is presently open ranch
land with a light to heavy cover of dry land vegetation such as keawe haole koa
lantana and grasses Based on our field observations and the available topographic
maps most of the project site generally slopes down toward the north with existing
ground surface elevations ranging from about 20 feet Mean Sea Level MSL at the
northern boundary to about 500 feet MSL along the southern boundary
A number of existing ranch trails traverse the site The ranch trails were generally
crude and followed the existing terrain We observed several flagged areas believed to
be archaeological features across the site Scattered boulders were observed along the
upper reaches of the site Locally at various locations there are mounds of soil debris
and other materials We believe that most of these piles are from previous clearing or
grading work

Further discussions on the general characteristics of the geologic units are
presented in the following subsections
Fills
The fill materials consist of variable soils which were placed during the
development of the area The fill materials generally consist of clayey and gravelly
soils There is no documentation on the placement and compaction of the fill
materials at the site Based on our borings the fill materials are in a stiff
consistency In addition the potential exists for organic materials to have been
buried in the fills during previous earthwork operations
Alluvial Deposits
Alluvial deposits generally consist of red brown gray or black soil consisting of
silts sands gravel and clays with some weathered cobbles and or boulders The
soils may have a mottled appearance and range from very soft and or very loose to
very stiff and or dense The alluvial deposits encountered at the site are generally
consolidated stiff to very stiff deposits
The materials are typically deposited along streams and mantling hills and
pre existing topographic low features The materials are derived from the erosion of
terrestrial rock and soils transported by stream or storm water runoff activity from a
source region and deposited elsewhere Older more deeply buried alluvium may
be transformed to form an indurated rock The indurated deposits may be identified
locally as older alluvium
Colluvial Deposits
Colluvial deposits are materials that have detached from the slope face and
transported down slope by gravitational forces The transportation methods of the
detached material from the slope include falling or sliding rolling washing of loose

materials by sheet or rill runoff of rain soil creep and landsliding Colluvial deposits
are often found at the base of cliffs in mountainous regions Colluvial deposits
characteristically contain large unsorted rock fragments ranging in size from a few
centimeters to a few meters in diameter There is no orderly arrangement of the
fragments and no bedding or layering The abrupt break in the slope is usually
evident between the colluvial deposits and cliff The angle that the surface of the
colluvial slope assumes is called the angle of repose
Basalt Formation
Basalt formations include a broad range of volcanic basaltic rock and the in situ
weathered products including residual soils and saprolitic materials The basalt rock
material was extruded as sequential layered lava flows from volcanic vents The
basalt rock which frequently contain interbedded porous clinker materials range
from very hard to soft rock Weathering decomposition ranges between highly
weathered and slightly weathered Fracture discontinuities within basalt rock
formations range from severely fractured shattered and broken to relatively
un fractured and massive in character
Extremely weathered rock is referred to as saprolitic material and represents rock
that has been reduced by weathering to soil like components silts clays and fine
sands with decomposed rock fragments However the material retains some
remnant rock texture such as layering vesicularity and some fracture patterns
Saprolitic materials are commonly mottled in coloration and contain more sandy
and gravelly components with zones of less weathered rock contained within
Completely weathered rock is referred to as a residual soil and has lost all visible
rock texture characteristics Residual soils are commonly composed of clayey and
silty components of uniform coloration Some relict boulders of hard rock may
occasionally be encountered in residual soils

removed Fills may be placed over the clay soils provided that they are scarified

metallic structure Conversely the higher the resistivity the less likely the soil will

Our field exploration program at the project site consisted of drilling and sampling
12 borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 12 extending to depths ranging from
about 16 3 to 44 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring

Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field He observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual
observation and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 12
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings in general
accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM
D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113

uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description

APPENDIX B
APPENDIX B
Test Pits
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by excavating and sampling
20 test pits designated as Test Pit Nos 1 through 20 to depths of about 3 to 11 feet
below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The test pits were excavated using a track excavator
The materials encountered in the test pits were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the excavation operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System
Soil samples were obtained as grab samples of the excavated materials and
placed in bulk sample bags for transportation to the laboratory Consistencies textures
and relative moisture contents were determined subjectively in the field during the
exploration

APPENDIX C
APPENDIX C
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 determinations were performed on selected
soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The test
results are presented on the Logs of Test Pits at the appropriate sample depths
Six Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on selected
core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the basaltic boulders
encountered The compression test results are presented on Plate C 1
Four Minimum Resistivity tests ASTM G 57 and one pH test AASHTO T 289
were performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration The test
results are summarized on Plate C 2
Six Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate C 3
Five Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested The test results are
presented on Plates C 4 and C 8
Five laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were
performed on bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils The test results are presented on Plates C 9 through C 13
