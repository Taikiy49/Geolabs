2 1 Regional Geology
of the Chagos Archipelago located at 7 degrees south latitude approximately 850 miles
south of India in the Indian Ocean The origin of coral atolls was first postulated by
Charles Darwin in 1842 His theory was that an atoll began as a fringing reef around a
volcanic island The island begins to subside tectonically at a slow rate so that the coral
growth maintains a reef line As the island subsides and becomes smaller the reef grows
larger and becomes a barrier reef When the island finally disappears below sea level the
upward growing reef encloses only a lagoon and becomes an atoll
This basic theory has been somewhat modified by other considerations such as
eustatic sea level changes and cooled waters brought on by glacial activity Generally the
ocean side of an atoll is built up of larger pieces of rock ripped off the hard reef and
worked up during storms while the lagoon side is composed of finer material The usual
atoll island stands between 1 5 to 10 meters above the sea level The reef rock platform is
generally about the low tide level The island detritus may spill off the reef rock platform
and build up towards the lagoon side of the island
The subsurface conditions in a coral atoll vary substantially in both the horizontal
and vertical dimensions Much of the large scale vertical variation is the result of
worldwide glacio eustatic sea level changes that occurred during the Pleistocene Epoch
Ice Age One significant low stand of the sea was a stand of relatively long duration at
about 18 meters below the present sea level Throughout the world many of the coral
atolls were eroded to this low stand and the materials above this level are the result of
rapid growth and accumulation of coralline deposits since the mid to late Pleistocene
Epoch The horizontal variation is largely dependent upon the environment in which the
coralline materials grew or were deposited

2 4 Soil Liquefaction
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess pore
water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes In this
process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration such as during
an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an increase in
pore water pressure If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the
build up of pore water pressure the effective stress internal strength of the soil is
reduced Under sustained vibrations the pore water pressure build up could equal the
overburden pressure essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to
behave as a viscous fluid During liquefaction the soil acquires a mobility sufficient to
permit both horizontal and vertical movements and if not confined will result in significant
deformations
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion It is generally acknowledged that liquefaction will
not occur if the deposit is greater than 12 to 15 meters below the ground surface At
deeper deposits the greater overburden pressure is generally sufficient to prevent
liquefaction from occurring The major factors affecting the liquefaction characteristics of a
soil deposit are as follows

3 2 Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be required for the construction of
the new facility Based on our field and laboratory test results we believe that new

project structural engineer Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should

In general paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be
maintained to carry the surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be
allowed on the site during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate
landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs be

layer to reduce the potential for migration of landscape water into the pavement section
Alternatively a subdrain system could be constructed to collect the excess water from

3 5 Utility Trenches
We envision that new utility lines will be required to serve the proposed new facility
In general good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling
of the trenches for the new utilities at the site The method and equipment to be used for
trench excavation should be determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and
safety considerations In addition the excavations should comply with the applicable local
safety requirements Trench shoring design and installation should be the responsibility of
the contractor
In general we recommend that granular bedding consisting of 150 millimeters of
coral sands or No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used for support
under the pipes Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C
33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about
300 millimeters above the pipes or 300 millimeters above the groundwater level to
provide adequate support around the pipes Select clean coral sand material may also be
used to backfill around the pipe provided that all sandy material is properly compacted
using water tamping method It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the
potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate
support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of the
backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 300 millimeters above the
pipes or groundwater level to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of
select granular material such as coral sands The backfill should be placed in maximum
250 mm level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground subsidence
3 6 Drainage
The finished grades outside the new facility should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from the
foundation areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should

Field Exploration
Our field exploration consisted of hand or truck augering and probing eight probe
holes designated as P 1 through P 8 extending to depths ranging from about 0 6 to
4 3 meters below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the probe
holes explored at the site are shown on the Site Plan Plate 3 The probe holes were
either first hand augered or truck augered to depths of about 0 6 to 2 7 meters below the
existing ground surface The probe holes were then hand probed to the maximum depth
explored using a hand held probing tool equipped with a 4 5 kilogram hammer
The materials encountered during the hand truck augering operation of the probe
holes were classified by visual and textural examination in the field by our engineer
These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation and testing in the
laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Probe Holes Plates A 1 through A 8
After the hand truck augering operation the probe holes were extended deeper into
the ground using a hand held probing tool equipped with a 4 5 kilogram hammer falling
450 millimeters The blow counts needed to drive the hand held probing equipment of an
300 mm drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Probe Holes at
