INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Marriott s Ko Olina Resort located in the Ko Olina area on the Island of
Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for
the proposed project These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations
slabs on grade retaining structures site grading and pavements only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age As the voicanic activity in the Waianae Volcano ceased lava flows from
the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau now
known as the Schofield Plateau The project site is located on the southwestem flank of the
Waianae Volcano
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of Schofield Plateau generated
sediments which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and to the
south these sediments accumulated and are interbedded with marine sediments and
coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge The thickness of the sedimentary
wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1 Highway to over 1 000 feet
at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation or
caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu Deposition of sediments has
continued from earlier geologic time through the present
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill
GEOLABS INC
W O 4541 00 Page 7
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea levels
rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of accumulated
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposited lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet
waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys
in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the calcareous reef
materials Placement of man made fills and commercial development within the last
20 years have brought the Ko Olina area to its present form

GEOLOGIC AND SEISMIC HAZARD EVALUATION
The entire Island of Oahu is designated as Seismic Zone 2A based on the Uniform
Building Code 1997 Therefore structures for the project may be designed based on the
requirements of Seismic Zone 2A The associated horizontal peak ground acceleration for
design of structures located in Seismic Zone 2A is 0 15g
As indicated previously extensive surface fill materials are limited to the southern
parcel and past basement excavation areas of the middle parcel at the project site The
on site native materials below the surface fill materials generally consist of interbedded
coralline detritus and coral formations The man made fills in the southern portion of the
project site generally consist of medium dense to dense silty sands and coralline gravel
overlying coralline and basaltic cobbles and boulders at greater depths In general the coral
formations encountered in our borings consist of algal coral reef and cemented sands
The coralline detritus encountered in our borings generally consist of loose to medium
dense sands silt and coralline debris with weak to virtually no cementation Rock like
materials may be anticipated at significant depths greater than 200 feet below the ground
surface in this area
Based on the average penetration resistance N values of the subsurface materials
encountered to a depth of about 100 feet in the borings we believe that the project site
should be generally classified as a Stiff Soil Profile Therefore we believe that the seismic
design of the structures at the project site should be designed based on a Soil Profile
Type Sp in accordance with Table 16 J of the Uniform Building Code 1997
An evaluation of geologic and seismic hazards addressing surface fault rupture
ground shaking ground failure liquefaction lateral spreading and ground lurching
tsunamis and seiches and seismically induced flooding was also conducted for this project
site Based on our evaluation of the project site conditions we do not believe that the
geologic and seismic hazards described above are significant considerations for this project
With the exception of moderate ground shaking we believe that the potential for surface fault
rupture ground failure liquefaction lateral spreading and ground lurching tsunamis and

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
As mentioned previously extensive surface fill materials are limited to the southern
parcel and past basement excavation areas of the middle parcel at the project site Based
on our borings and a review of the records available in our archives man made fills of up to
about 14 to 17 feet thick have been placed at the southern portion of the project site The
man made fills in this area generally consist of medium dense to dense silty sands and
coralline gravel overlying coralline and basaltic cobbles and boulders at greater depths The
past basement excavation at the middle parcel was backfilled with reinforced concrete piles
and granular fill materials and raised up to the existing ground surface We understand that
the past basement excavation extended to about Elevation 3 feet MSL
The on site native materials generally consist of interbedded coralline detritus and
coral formations Based on our borings and available information the subsurface condition
at the project site is very complex and highly variable across the project site In addition we
also anticipate that the stiffness and consistency of the subsurface materials at the site vary
substantially in short distances Therefore differential settlements between foundations
supported in and on these variable subsurface materials are of principal consideration
Several types of foundation systems including mat foundations drilled shafts and
driven piles were considered for support of the proposed structures In general we believe
that differential settlements across the building structure would be a concern for structures
supported on a mat foundation system considering the highly variable subsurface conditions
at the site In addition drilled shafts would be a technically feasible foundation system
however the cost for drilled shafts is generally appreciably higher than the cost for driven
piles in these subsurface conditions Therefore driven piles appear to be the appropriate
foundation system for support of the proposed structures

Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering energies in the range
of about 25 000 to 50 000 foot pounds in the field The hammer should be equipped
with an energy level control such that driving within the loose soft soils may be
controlled at a lower energy to reduce tensile stress development which could
damage or cause cracking of the piles
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the matching of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be
driven This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy to
the top of the pile and also reducing damage to the piles Geolabs should review the
pile driving equipment proposed to evaluate if the equipment is capable of installing
the piles to the recommended capacity The contractor should forward pile and
driving equipment data forms to Geolabs prior to construction for evaluation Geolabs
should review the submittal and assess the driveability of piles with the use of the

gravel encountered in our borings We envision that the loading dock areas will generally be
subjected to delivery trucks passenger vehicles and moderately heavy trucks We
understand that the main entrance will be subjected to the moderately heavy bus traffic
Therefore we recommend that the following rigid pavement section be used for the new
loading dock areas and main entrance
Rigid Pavement
6 0 Inch Portland Cement Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Subbase 95 Percent Relative Compaction
12 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on a Moist Compacted Subgrade
The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to not less
than 95 percent relative compaction The aggregate base and subbase course should
consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction CBR and density tests should be performed on the actual subgrade soils
encountered during construction to confirm the adequacy of the above section
in general paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be
maintained to carry the surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be
allowed on the site during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate
landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs extend a
minimum of 2 inches below the aggregate base and or subbase to reduce the potential for
migration of excessive landscape water into the pavement section

Underground Utility Lines
New utility lines and utility connections will likely be required for development of the
proposed project In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular
materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used
for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate
support around the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential
for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the
sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and
damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils with a
maximum particle size of 6 inches The backfill materials should be moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content placed in about 8 inch loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
appreciable future ground subsidence Where trenches are located below pavement areas
the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to not
less than 95 percent relative compaction
Percolation Tests
A total of eight falling head and two constant head percolation tests were conducted
in five drilled borings Boring Nos 101 through 105 to provide data for design of the
proposed seepage pits planned at the site The results of our percolation tests are
presented in Appendix C of this report
The percolation tests indicated that the subsurface materials generally have
coefficients of permeability k in the range of about 1 1 x 10 to 4 7 x 10 feet per minute
Based on these coefficients of permeability the disposal capacities of the shallow seepage
pits correspond to about 0 1 to 500 gallons per square foot per day of infiltration capacity It
should be emphasized that the hydraulic conductivity of the coral formation at the site is

highly variable due to the presence or absence of significant voids which greatly affect the
disposal capacity of a seepage pit
Drainage
The finished grades outside the new structures should be sloped to shed water away
from the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from the
foundations and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and
pavement areas should also be avoided Planters next to shallow foundations should be
avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for significant water
infiltration into the subsurface
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
shallow foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should
be properly backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration into the subsurface In
addition drainage swales should be provided as soon as possibile and should be maintained
to drain surface water runoff away from the foundations and pavement areas

One set of pH minimum resistivity sulfate content and chloride content tests was

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
12 borings designated as Boring Nos 101 through 112 at the approximate locations shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2 The depths of the borings extended to about 25 5 to 121 5 feet
below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled with a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools
at A ta
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the maiteriais
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 12
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition samples were also obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the hard coralline materials encountered at the site were obtained
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the coral formation The coral material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of coral masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Three Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The
results of the tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
Thirteen sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 D 1140 were performed on selected

Three direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples of the

presented on Plates B 5 through B 7
One consolidation test with time rates of consolidation ASTM D 2435 was performed
on the potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the
materials encountered Results of the consolidation test are presented on Plate B 8
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were performed
on bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils Results of the tests are presented on Plates B 9 and B 10
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The results of the tests are presented on Plates B 11 and B 12
One set of pH ASTM D 2976 and minimum resistivity ASTM G 57 tests was
performed to evaluate the corrosion potential of the soils The results of the tests are
summarized on Plate B 13

performed on the near surface soils to evaluate the corrosion potential of the soils These
tests were performed by Environmental Laboratory of the Pacific Results of the tests are
also summarized on Plate B 13

APPENDIX C
Percolation Testing
Percolation tests were performed using the constant and falling head test methods
in five of the borings drilled Boring Nos 101 through 105 The drilled borings generally
have a diameter of about 4 to 6 inches and extended to depths of about 5 to 10 feet at the
time of the test A portable water container was used to pump fresh water into the test
holes for the constant head test method The flow rate was recorded by timing and
calculating the volume as a relatively constant flow rate was achieved The head levels
Patatnt a
injection of water
For the falling head test method the boreholes were filled with water and the water
was allowed to percolate into the ground The water levels and corresponding times were
recorded for the calculations of subsurface permeability The results of the percolation tests
are presented on Plates C 1 through C 9
