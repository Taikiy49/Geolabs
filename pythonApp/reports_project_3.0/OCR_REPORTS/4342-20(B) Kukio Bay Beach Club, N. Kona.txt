INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Wastewater System Improvements for the Kukio Bay Beach Club located
in the District of North Kona on the Island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the proposed Wastewater
System Improvements project These recommendations are intended for the design of
foundations slabs on grade retaining structures site grading and pavements only The
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the
end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes which include the following Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has
had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa
and Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of Hualalai Mountain which
makes up the west central portion of the island The lava formation encountered at the site
appears to be of both a a and pahoehoe type flows which spread and ponded as they
approached the ocean A a lava is typically characterized by a porous rough and irregular
flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and
boulders Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an
internal structure of vesicular porous rock
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas especially pahoehoe lava
These cavities were formed when the lava was still in a molten state and they represent both
lava tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes are
formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure
which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Blisters and pockets
inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent
GEOLABS INC
W O 4342 20 B Page 5
Basalt is considered to be relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted through
the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and along
clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they serve as
the major water transmission features
SITE DESCRIPTION
As previously mentioned the project site is located to the southwest of the adjacent
Hualalai Resort in the Kaupulehu area of the District of North Kona on the Island of Hawaii
The proposed wastewater treatment plant site is located on the mauka side of the Queen
Kaahumanu Highway as shown on the General Site Plan Plate 2
The terrain at the project site is generally rugged which reflects the original surface
of thick basaltic lava flows of the a a and pahoehoe forms The majority of the project site
is underlain by a a and pahoehoe lavas at the ground surface Some cinder cones were also
observed within the limits of the development The pahoehoe lava flows are generally
located toward the northern portion of the site Some collapsed lava tube features were
observed during our reconnaissance of the site A a lavas are generally more prevalent at
the higher elevations to the south of the project site
Based on the topographic map provided the project site is gently sloping downward
from southeast to northwest The existing ground elevations at the project site generally
range from approximately 220 to 250 feet MSL Vegetation at the project site is
generally sparse with some wild grasses growing through the crevices of basaltic lava
flows Access to the site is off Queen Kaahumanu Highway running from northeast to
southwest An unimproved road presently traverses the site in a northwest to southeast
direction as shown on the General Site Plan Plate 2

Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we recommend
that shallow spread and or continuous footings be used to support the proposed structures
planned at the site An allowable bearing pressure of up to 4 000 pounds per square
foot psf may be used for the design of footings bearing on the basalt formation or new
structural fill materials This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased
by 50 percent for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade Where foundations are located next to utility trenches or
easements the foundations should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane
extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or extended to a depth as deep
as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent
below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for
appreciable foundation settlement
GEOLABS INC
W O 4342 20 B Page 8
lf soft and or loose materials are encountered at the footing subgrades the materials
should be over excavated and replaced with structural fill The over excavation should be
extended until the underlying dense materials are exposed The over excavation should be
backfilled with structural fill materials moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the bottom of
footing may be extended down to bear on the underlying competent material
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate total settlements of the foundations bearing on the basalt
formation and or structural fill materials to be less than 1 inch Differential settlements
between adjacent footings are estimated to be on the order of 0 5 inches
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 45 may be used for footings bearing on the basalt formation or structural fill
materials Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent
fluid pressure of 450 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the
soils around the footings are well compacted or relatively undisturbed Unless covered by
pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be
neglected
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative from
Geolabs prior to the placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation
bearing conditions and the required embedment depths
Slabs On Grade
We envision that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed structures
planned at the site For the interior slabs which will not be subjected to vehicular traffic we
recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill be provided below the slabs to
provide for more uniform support and to serve as a capillary moisture break The cushion

fill material should consist of open graded gravel such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation
To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor
coverings an impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is
recommended on top of the cushion fill layer Placement of 2 inches of moist fine sand
above the plastic membrane may be considered to provide for protection of the membrane
and to aid in curing of the slab concrete Provision of the 2 inches of moist fine sand above
the vapor barrier will be left up to the discretion of the project structural engineer Flexible
floor coverings such as sheet vinyl should be considered since they can better mask minor
slab cracking
For equipment pads and other slabs on grade that will be subjected to vehicular traffic

below the slab in lieu of the cushion fill layer for more uniform support The vapor barrier and
the 2 inch moist fine sand layer may also be omitted for these types of slabs The select
borrow subbase should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction

Dynamic Lateral Earth Forces
Since the project site is located on the seismically active Island of Hawaii Seismic
Zone 4 in accordance with the Uniform Building Code dynamic lateral earth forces
will need to be considered in the design of retaining structures The dynamic lateral
earth force due to seismic loading may be estimated using
16H pounds per foot of wall length for level backfill conditions where H is the
height of the wall in feet The dynamic lateral earth force should be assumed to act
at the mid height of the retaining structure The dynamic lateral earth force
presented above is in addition to the static lateral earth pressures An appropriately
reduced factor of safety may be used when dynamic lateral earth forces are
accounted for in the design of the retaining structures

The prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a
perforated pipe at the base of the retaining wall

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
In general structural fills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in
loose thickness moisture conditioned and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction General fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not
exceeding 18 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers
vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment
Rock fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of the
majority of the rocks or 5 feet whichever is less Placement should be performed
by using haul trucks traversing previously placed lifts and dumping at the edge of
the currently placed lift Spreading of the rock fill should performed using a
bulldozer to facilitate seating of the rock The rock fill should be watered heavily
during placement by water trucks traversing in front of the current rock lift face and
spraying water continuously during placement
Compaction equipment such as a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar heavy
compaction equipment providing suitable energy to achieve the required
compaction should be utilized The number of passes usually six to eight passes
to be made to achieve proper compaction should be determined at the on set of site
grading by the performance of a trial compaction test or by plate bearing tests

of a representative from Geolabs On the basis of this testing program the number
of passes may then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction For
preliminary estimating purposes compaction may be accomplished by using a large
vibratory drum roller with a static weight of at least 15 tons traveling at a speed of
about 300 feet per minute The roller should pass over the fill surface at least eight
times for structural fill and six times for general fill For general fill a Caterpillar D 9
bulldozer or similar size equipment may be used in lieu of the vibratory drum roller
Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on our borings it appears that most of the cut slopes at the project site would
expose the basalt formation encountered in the borings In general we believe that
the cut slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of one horizontal to one
vertical 1H 1V or flatter If a a cinder or other loose materials are encountered the
cut slope inclination should be reduced to 2H 1V or flatter
It is generally difficult to provide a smooth cut slope face when excavating in basalt
formations If over cutting of the cut slopes occurs consideration should be given
to flattening the slope or leaving the slope as is Patching of the over cut areas with
sliver fills is not recommended due to the potential for erosion of the fill materials in
the patched areas considering the steep cut slopes recommended
Permanent fill slopes constructed of the structural fill materials recommended herein
may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fills that are to be placed
GEOLABS INC
W O 4342 20 B Page 18
on existing ground steeper than 5H 1V should be benched The fill slope face should
be finished to a relatively smooth and well compacted surface The fill slope face
should be free of large boulders greater than 18 inches in largest dimension and
should also be free of voids which would allow erosion and migration of fines to
occur In addition slope planting or other means of slope protection should be
provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes
Construction of earth berms interceptor ditches and the use of geotextile fabrics
over the fill slope face may be considered to reduce the potential for erosion thus
enhancing the long term stability of the planned fill slopes Appropriate slope
planting or other erosion control measures to reduce the potential for erosion of the
exposed slopes should be implemented as soon as possible after the finished slope
faces are completed
Excavation
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by medium
hard to hard volcanic rock at or near the existing ground surface Generally the
volcanic rock at the site may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment
such as ripping with large bulldozers However deeper excavations and hard basalt
formation in some areas may require the use of hoerams chipping or blasting
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based on

drilled at the site We recommend that contractors proposing to bid on this project be

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
five borings designated as Boring Nos 101 through 105 extending to depths of about 15 to
15 5 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted
drill rig equipped with rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 5
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained by using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX B
Percolation Testing
Percolation tests were performed by using the falling and constant head methods
in two of the drilled borings Boring Nos 204 and 205 The drilled borings had a diameter
of about 3 5 inches and depths of about 10 to 15 feet at the time of the test A water truck
was used to pump water into the test holes for the constant head test method The flow
rate was recorded by timing and calculating the volume as a relatively constant flow rate
was achieved
The falling head method was tested by filling the boreholes with water and allowing
the water to percolate into the ground The water levels and corresponding times were
recorded for the calculation of subsurface permeability The head levels within the test
borings were monitored by an electric water level sounder during the test The results of
the percolation tests conducted are presented on Plates D 1 through D 3
