SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered alluvial soils with an interbedded layer of 
aeolian deposit at about 4.5 to 8.5 feet below the existing ground surface. We did not 
encounter groundwater at the time of our field exploration. However, it should be noted that 
groundwater levels are subject to occur or change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal 
precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors at the project site. 
We recommend using shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings bearing on the 
recompacted on-site soils and/or new compacted fills for support of the proposed new 
switchyard building and transformer pad with an allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 
pounds per square foot (psf). In general, footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 
inches below the lowest adjacent grades. The bottom of footing excavations in on-site soils 
should be recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. 
We anticipate that the transmission line power poles for the proposed switchyard may 
be supported by a deep foundation system consisting of cast-in-place concrete drilled 
shafts. The cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally from 
skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding soils. An ultimate skin friction of 1.5 kips 
per square foot (ksf) may be used to estimate the drilled shaft capacities for the proposed 
switchyard project. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
geotechnical recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Kuihelani Solar Farm Development – MECO Switchyard project in the 
Kahului area on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are 
shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project. 
These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of 
foundations and corrosion protection only. The findings and recommendations presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located off Kuihelani Highway (Route 380) in the southern Kahului 
area on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. We understand it is desired to develop a 60-MWac solar

will encompass approximately 500 acres of open land and will consist of solar panels

SECTION 2. SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes: the older West Maui (Tertiary 
Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala (Pleistocene Epoch). 
The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain between these two volcanoes. The 
project site is at the northern portion of this gently sloping plain. 
The Isthmus of Maui was created by lava flows from Haleakala ponding on West 
Maui. It is comprised of alluvium washed from the slopes of West Maui and East Maui 
(Haleakala). The erosional processes are dominated by the detachment of soil and rock 
masses from the mountain walls, and the soil materials are transported downslope toward 
the Isthmus primarily by gravity as colluvium. Once these materials reach the stream in 
the central portion of a valley, alluvial processes become dominant, and the sediments 
are transported and deposited as alluvium. 
In general, stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy, such that the stream 
flows transmit large volumes of water for a very short duration. Because of this, the 
transport of sediments is intermittent, and the bulk of the stream's hydraulic load consists 
of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders, cobbles, gravel, sands, and fines. When the 
erosional base levels change, these sediment loads are left as deposits. 
When deposits are left in-place for long periods of time, chemical processes begin 
to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the materials. 
Chemical processes also cause induration, or cementation, of the coarse-grained portion 
of the sediment into a poorly-consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate. 
Simultaneously, erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors and upstream in 
headwaters. This continued erosion generates material which is transported downslope 
covering the older alluvial soil deposits. Depending on the local base level and rate of 
transport, these newer sediments are generally transient in terms of geologic time. In 
addition, their consistency and density are generally less than those of the older, partially 
consolidated deposits.

Underlying the alluvial soil deposits are overlapping lava from the West Maui and 
East Maui volcanoes. The bulk of the Haleakala Shield was built during the late Pliocene 
and early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows of the Honomanu 
Volcanic Series. During the Pleistocene Epoch, the characteristics of the lava changed to 
very hard, thickly bedded flows of andesitic composition. These lavas have been grouped 
as the Kula Volcanic Series. 
Further, complicating the stratigraphy of the isthmus was the development of broad 
fringing reefs in the bay formed at the juncture between West Maui and East Maui; and, 
glacio-eustatic sea level changes that occurred during the Pleistocene Epoch in response 
to the advance and retreat cycles of continental glaciation. During the glacial advances, 
water was bound into the widespread glaciers as ice on a year-round basis and less water 
was available to fill the ocean basins. As a consequence, global sea levels fell below the 
current sea level. During the retreats, more water was available and sea levels rose. 
When the sea levels fell, the fringing reefs, with their complement of calcium 
carbonate sand derived from both detrital and bioclastic sources, were exposed to the 
prevailing tradewinds which blew in about the same direction as the current tradewinds 
but were estimated to have an average velocity of about 60 miles per hour. These winds, 
transporting the loose sand from the reef areas, resulted in a strip of sand dunes (aeolian 
deposits) that extended from the present Wailuku-Kahului area to as far as the south 
coast of the Maui Isthmus, blanketing the volcanic and alluvial deposits on the floor of the 
isthmus. Sugar cane operations in the past decades have brought the project site to its 
present conditions.

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling two borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 201 and 202, extending to the maximum depth drilled of about 
31.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, one bulk sample of the 
near-surface soils were obtained to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the 
near-surface soils. The approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by alluvial 
soils with an interbedded layer of aeolian deposit at about 4.5 to 8.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. Aeolian deposits generally consisted of loose to dense silty sand 
extending to about 14 to 15.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Alluvial soils 
generally consisted of soft to stiff clays/silts and loose silty sand extending to the 
maximum depth explored of about 31.5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
We did not encounter groundwater at the time of our field exploration. However, it 
should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to occur or change due to rainfall, 
time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors at the project 
site.

Shallow Foundations 
In general, we believe the proposed new switchyard building and transformer pad

3.2.2 Foundation Settlement 
Settlement of the drilled shaft foundations will result from elastic compression of 
the shaft and subgrade response of the foundation embedded in the soils 
encountered at the site. The total settlement of the drilled shaft is estimated to be 
less than 0.5 inches. Differential settlements between drilled shafts may be 
approximately half of the total settlements. We believe that a significant portion of 
the settlement will be elastic and should occur as the loads are applied. 
3.2.3 Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations 
In general, the performance of drilled shafts depends significantly upon the 
contractor's method of installation and construction procedures. The following 
conditions would have a significant effect on the effectiveness and cost of the 
drilled shaft foundations. 
The load-bearing capacities of the drilled shafts depend, to a significant extent, on 
the friction between the shaft and the surrounding soils. Therefore, proper 
construction techniques, especially during the drilling operations, are important. 
The contractor should exercise care in drilling the shaft holes and placing concrete 
into the drilled holes.

potential for “necking” of the drilled shaft. Therefore, a minimum 5-foot head of 
concrete above the bottom of the casing should be maintained during the removal 
of the casing. 
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids may have a significant effect on the 
supporting capacity of the drilled shaft; therefore, the use of drilling fluids would 
require prior evaluation and acceptance by Geolabs. 
A low-shrink concrete mix with high slump (7 to 9-inch slump range) should be 
used to provide close contact between the drilled shaft and the surrounding soils. 
The concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce the potential for 
segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix. 
In addition, the concrete should be placed promptly after drilling (within 24 hours 
after drilling of the holes) to reduce the potential for softening of the sides of the 
drilled hole. Furthermore, drilling adjacent to a recently constructed shaft (within 
five shaft diameters of the recently constructed drilled shaft) should not commence 
until the concrete for the recently constructed drilled shaft has cured for a minimum 
of 24 hours. 
It is imperative for a Geolabs representative to be present at the project site to 
observe the drilling and installation of the drilled shafts during construction.

3.3 Corrosion Potential 
Design of metallic substructures, such as electrical conduits or other metallic 
piping, should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure. 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
regarding the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the 
resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to the corrosion 
of metallic objects. Items of corrosion potential that are addressed in the subsequent 
subsections include the following: 
Field Resistivity Survey 
Laboratory Corrosivity Testing 
Laboratory Thermal Resistivity Testing 
3.3.1 Field Resistivity Survey 
Two field resistivity tests were conducted at a maximum electrode spacing of about 
300 feet, designated as R-1 (N-S) and R-1 (E-W), using the in-situ Wenner 4-Pin 
Method and an AEMC Model 4630 resistance meter manufactured by AEMC 
Instruments. 
The method utilizes four surface probes or pins (electrodes) to evaluate the average 
resistivity (an indication of corrosivity) of the underlying soil mass. The field resistivity 
surveys were used to evaluate the average resistivity values of the soil mass at the 
approximate survey locations. Results of the field resistivity surveys are presented in 
Appendix C. It should be noted that the field resistivity survey may be influenced by 
temperature, weather conditions, soil moisture, existing underground utilities and 
other factors.

Based on the results of our field resistivity surveys, the subsurface soils at the project 
site generally exhibited resistivity values ranging from about 2,200 to 15,500 ohm-
cm, corresponding to corrosion ratings between 2 (very corrosive) and 5 (mildly 
corrosive) using the Board of Water Supply (BWS) guidelines. 
3.3.2 Laboratory Corrosivity Testing 
One set of corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity (ASTM 
G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0) were 
performed by our office and Eurofins TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc on a selected 
near-surface soil sample obtained from our field exploration. A summary of the 
corrosivity tests is presented in Plate B-5 of Appendix B. Detailed discussions and 
test results of Chloride and Sulfate are provided in the report entitled “Analytical 
Report, Kuihelani Solar Farm (Laboratory Job ID: 320-73168-1),” dated May 18, 
2021, as included in Appendix D. 
Based on the results of laboratory corrosivity tests, the near-surface soils at the 
project site exhibit a minimum resistivity value of approximately 1,600 ohm-cm, 
indicating that the near-surface soils are very corrosive (Corrosion Rating of 2) to 
buried metallic structures. Therefore, we recommend properly designing 
near-surface metallic substructures (such as piping) for protection against the 
potential for corrosion. 
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete structures is 
dependent on the pH, chloride content, and sulfate content found in the soil. In 
general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm), sulfate 
content of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH of greater than 5.0 may be considered "non 
corrosive" to underground concrete pipelines and structures. 
Based on the values of the chloride and sulfate content tests on the in-situ 
near-surface soils, we believe that either Type I or Type II (Type I/II) cement may be 
used for the concrete in contact with the ground planned.

3.4 
Pavement Design 
We envision flexible pavements may be required for access roads to the proposed 
new switchyard facility. Details of the design traffic information were not available at the 
time this report was prepared. In general, we anticipate that after the initial heavy loading 
with delivery truck loads of transformer and other heavy equipment, the vehicle loading 
will consist of passenger vehicles, pick-up trucks, and occasional light maintenance trucks 
Therefore, we have assumed generally light to medium traffic conditions for pavement 
design purposes. 
Based on our field exploration and laboratory testing, on-site clayey/silty soils 
exhibited relatively good pavement support characteristics. Based on the California 
Bearing Ratio (CBR) and swell values of the on-site soils, we recommend using the 
following pavement sections for preliminary design purposes: 
Asphalt Pavements 
3.0-Inch Asphaltic Concrete 
6.0-Inch Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction) 
9.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade 
The subgrade soils below the pavement area should be scarified to a minimum 
depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, 
and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction. The subgrade soils should be 
thoroughly moistened and kept moist until covered by the pavement structural section.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling two 
borings, designated as Boring Nos. 201 and 202, extending to the maximum depth explored 
of about 31.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, one bulk sample of the 
near-surface soils was obtained to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the 
near-surface soils. The approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site 
Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil

are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2.  
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with

were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586,

Field Resistivity Surveys 
Geophysical survey equipment (i.e., resistance meter) was mobilized to the project 
site on March 28, 2021 for the field resistivity survey. Two field resistivity surveys were 
conducted, designated as R-1 (N-S) and R-1 (E-W), using the in-situ Wenner 4-Pin 
Method and an AEMC Model 4630 resistance meter manufactured by AEMC Instruments. 
The method utilizes four surface probes or pins (electrodes) to evaluate the average 
resistivity of the underlying soil mass. The average resistivity values of the soil mass, 
extending from the existing ground surface to the test depth, was assessed by using this 
method at the approximate survey locations. 
The soil resistivity surveys were performed along the existing ground surface at 
the selected survey locations. The average soil resistance along the proposed alignment 
was evaluated at each survey location with the electrodes aligned at equal spacing of 
generally 2.5, 5, 10, 25, 50, 75, 100, 150, 200, 250, and 300 feet. The depth of penetration 
of a resistivity sounding is a function of the electrode spacing. 
The resistance meter (AEMC Model 4630) provides a voltage between the outer 
electrodes, causing an electrical current to flow between them. The voltage drop between 
the inner electrodes is then measured by a sensitive, built-in voltmeter. As a result, the 
soil resistance (in ohms) is obtained from the voltmeter reading. The soil resistivity 
(in ohm-cm), which is directly related to both resistance and spacing, can then be 
computed. The evaluated soil resistances and computed resistivity values are presented 
on Plate C-1. In addition, the computed resistivity values with pin spacing at the test 
locations are presented in the graph on Plate C-2. At the time of resistivity surveying, the 
temperature was about 78º to 85º F. The existing ground surface was dry to damp. It 
should be noted that the soil resistivity may be influenced by temperature, weather 
conditions, existing underground utilities and other factors.
