INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering
investigation performed for the proposed P 517 Maintenance Hangar site
at the Naval Air Station Cubi Point in the Republic of the Philippines
The general location of the project site is shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
PURPOSE AND SCOPE OF WORK
The purpose of our investigation was to evaluate the subsurface
conditions at the near shore and offshore portions of the site to
provide pertinent soil data such as shear strength compressibility and
soil classification in order to assess the suitability of the project
site and the potential settlement effect on existing buildings and
airfield pavements due to the proposed 550 000 cubic yards of fill
placed on the project site
A preliminary geotechnical investigation was performed previously by
our firm to evaluate the general suitability of the onshore portion of
the site for the proposed fill Our findings were presented in our
report entitled Preliminary Geotechnical Investigation MILCON P 517
Maintenance Hangar U S Naval Station Cubi Point Republic of the
Philippines It was disclosed in that investigation that most of the
onshore portion of the site appeared suitable for the proposed
construction However the preliminary hangar and apron layout

indicated that a substantial amount of new fill would be placed into the
offshore areas Preliminary evaluation also indicated that the thicker
sand deposits under the embankment along the shoreline area may be
Susceptible to liquefaction during a major earthquake
This investigation was conducted to focus on the areas along the
near shore and offshore to determine the general subsoil profile and
provide additional data for stability and liquefaction evaluation of the
proposed high fill

SITE CONDITIONS
General
The project site is located on the western edge of the Cubi Point
Naval Air Station It is bordered by the South Taxiway on the north
San Bernardino Road and existing hangars on the east and the Triboa Bay
on the south and west
Most of the site is relatively flat and approximately 35 to 48 feet
lower than the adjacent upper hangar and taxiway area Photo 1
At present the site is being used as a recreational facility with
picnic tables a snack bar driveway and parking lot Photo 2
There is an existing concrete sea wall along the western side of the
site It is in a deteriorated condition and has fallen over in places
The existing ground elevation along the lower beach area is
approximately 7 to 9 feet
Geology
From available geologic literature the site is located within the
Western Volcanic Intrusive Province of Luzon and is composed of Pliocene
to Quaternary volcanic deposits At the project site the volcanic
deposits are primarily of pyroclastic origin and consist of lithic tuffs
believed to have been generated by explosive eruptions of Mount Natib to
the south
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1289 10 Page 6
Following the deposition of these lithic tuffs the rock began to
decompose due to chemical weathering The normal stratigraphic sequence
of chemically weathered rock would be a surficial layer of residual
soil an intermediate zone of saprolite followed by highly weathered
rock which would grade to fresh bedrock The boundaries in the sequence
would be gradual
At the project site the horizon of residual soil is absent
possibly due to stripping by wave action during Pleistocene
glacio eustatic sea level fluctuations and previous construction
activity
During the second half of the twentieth century the project site
was filled as part of the construction of NAS Cubi Point This fill
material generally consists of dredged coral materials of unknown origin
and residual or completed weathered tuff excavated from adjacent areas

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
In general the field investigation and engineering analysis
indicate that from a geotechnical engineering standpoint most of the
onshore portion of the site is suitable for the proposed fill However
we believe the loose sands underlying the offshore areas are susceptible
to liquefaction under the proposed high embankment fill To minimize
liquefaction potential we recommend that the loose sands be further
densified prior to or during embankment filling If properly
constructed the fill could provide adequate support for hangar
foundations and aircraft parking apron pavements

Liquefaction Potential
When a loose saturated sand deposit is subject to vibration such as
during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume
causing an increase in pore water pressure If drainage is unable to
occur rapidly enough due to the large extent of the sand deposit to
dissipate the build up in pore pressure the effective stress internal
strength of the soil is reduced Under sustained vibration the pore
water pressure build up could equal the overburden pressure essentially
reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to act asa
viscous fluid This phenomenon is called liquefaction The major
factors affecting the liquefaction characteristics of a soil are
1 Grain size distribution Fine and uniform sands are more prone
to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2 Initial relative density Loose sands are most susceptible to
liquefaction Liquefaction potential decreases with increased
relative density
3 Magnitude and duration of vibration Liquefaction potential
increases with increases in the magnitude and duration of the
earthquake
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1289 10 Page 11
The present beach and offshore areas are underlain by loose to
medium density sand and gravel The thickness of this layer varies from
about 10 to 15 feet inland and increases up to 40 feet in the offshore
areas approximately 150 feet from the existing seawall
A preliminary liquefaction analysis was performed using the
empirical method developed by Seed and Idriss Simplified Procedure for
Evaluating Soil Liquefaction Potential J Soil Mech and Found Div
No 1273 1971 The analysis used seismic
data from a draft report on the Earthquake Hazard Reduction Program
Naval Facilities PWC Subic Bay P I Phase 1 by S K Takahashi and
C V Chelapati indicating a maximum earthquake magnitude of 7 6 and a
peak ground acceleration value of 0 25g following the NAVFAC seismic
criteria of 80 probability of not being exceeded in 50 years This is
based on an earthquake originating from the San Antonio Fracture zone
which varies from about 4 to 83 miles from the Subic Bay area
The liquefaction analysis indicates that after site filling the
loose to medium density sands below the inland areas which would be
covered with a total of 35 feet or more of fill may have sufficient
surcharge to resist liquefaction The calculated factor safety against
liquefaction is generally greater than 1 2 However below the sloping
portions of the embankment where the total fill height would be
decreased from more than 35 feet to zero the factor of safety against

liquefaction would be reduced due to the reduction in surcharge
pressure Based on our analysis we believe the loose sands below this
sloping area are susceptible to liquefaction during an earthquake with a

Susceptible to liquefaction is indicated on the cross sections on Plates

Liquefaction of the underlying sands would cause extensive
deformation and slope instability of the embankment fill thus very
serious damage to structural improvements near the edge of the
embankment fill area could occur To minimize liquefaction potential
we recommend that densification of the loose sands below the fill slope
area be incorporated in the site development plan To achieve the
required densification we believe special deep compaction methods such
as those listed below should be considered
a Vibrating Probe A 30 inch 0 D open ended pipe pile is
vibrated by a vibratory pile driver into the loose sands causing
densification of the sands
b Vibroflotation A special crane suspended cylindrical
penetrator vibroflot is advanced into the loose sand by a
combination of vibration and water jetting When the vibroflot
reaches the desired densification depth the water jet is closed
and the vibroflot is withdrawn with vibration while sand or
gravel backfill is dumped into the hole from the ground surface
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1289 10 Page 13
c Dynamic Consolidation Heavy Tamping Heavy weights ranging
from 10 to 40 tons are dropped from a height of 50 to 130 feet
causing localized liquefaction and subsequent settlement
densification of the loose sands
d Compaction Piles Displacement piles are driven into the ground
at close spacing Granular backfill is then introduced into the
hole and compacted as the pile is withdrawn
The method used would depend primarily on the cost effectiveness of
each alternative and equipment availability Our analysis indicates
that where there is more than about 24 feet of fill planned
densification to about 70 relative density would provide a factor of
safety against liquefaction of about 1 2 In our judgement each of the
above densification methods could increase the relative density of the
site sand to about 70 The critical area appears to be near the toe of
the slope area where the height of the proposed fill is limited It is
our opinion that very high relative density in excess of 85 is
required to provide a factor of safety greater than one Even with the
above recommended methods it is doubtful whether sufficient
densification in excess of 85 could be achieved In this area it is
our opinion that the vibroflotation or compaction pile methods using
gravel backfill instead of sand would be more appropriate since the
gravel would help to relieve the excess pore pressure build up during an
earthquake In this way the liquefaction potential would be reduced by

a combination of increased relative density to 70 and improved
drainage in the layer At the normal spacing of 5 to 8 feet on centers
it is our opinion that sufficient drainage could be provided to minimize
the liquefaction potential
Settlement
Ground settlements are anticipated due to the weight of the
substantial amount of fill planned Settlements would occur due to 1
compression of the fill itself 2 compression of the existing loose to
medium dense sand and gravel layer and 3 compression of the underlying
saprolite and tuff formation We anticipate that these settlements
would primarily involve elastic compression and therefore would occur
rapidly Large long term settlements are not anticipated
Assuming that the thicker deposits of loose sand and gravel under
the beach area will be densified to minimize liquefaction potential
estimated total settlements due to 43 feet of fill would be about 12 to
18 inches in the central portion of the embankment area The settlement
would be reduced to about 6 inches at the edge of the embankment area
Ground settlements in the adjacent hangar and the existing runway
areas would occur primarily due to compression of the saprolite and tuff
formation Ground settlements caused by the new fill are estimated to
be about 2 to 3 inches in the existing ground which is 20 feet away from
the edge of the fill Beyond the 20 foot area that is away from the
GEOLABS HAWAII

drastically Therefore it is our opinion that ground settlements at

area
Based on the laboratory test results we anticipate that 90 of the

Wave Protection
The toe of the fill along the shoreline will need to be protected
from wave action At present most of the shores in the Cubi Point area
are protected by dumped riprap with larger rocks ranging 2 to 4 feet in
size Photo 5 or manufactured concrete blocks approximately 4 by 4 by 4
feet Photo 6
The 111 material to be generated from the P 516 project site will
generally be broken down after compaction to fine sand or silt sizes
which are moderate to highly erodible Therefore adequate filter
protection will be required between the fill and riprap Such a filter
layer may consist of filter fabric or graded filter rocks It appears
that a filter fabric system may have an economic advantage over graded
filter rock layers However care must be exercised during construction
to assure its proper placement and that the filter fabric is not
punctured or torn during the process of placement
In general we believe that the quality of weathered tuf rock from
the Cubi Point area may not meet normal standards for armor riprap
Therefore imported rocks may be required In general the riprap
protection blanket should extend at least 8 feet below the lowest low
tide in accordance with DM 7 1 or into competent rock

Site Preparation
At the onset of earthwork the areas to receive fill should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All existing vegetation mud
structures debris rubbish and any other unsuitable materials
should be removed and disposed of properly off site
The fill site should be proofrolled with a heavy vibratory roller
with a minimum weight of in order to densify the near
surface sands and detect soft spots
Existing foundations and structures could become obstacles for
future deep foundations and therefore should be removed and the
resulting depressions backfilled with compacted fill as specificed
in the following section Any soft or weak areas disclosed during
the clearing operations should be removed and properly repaired by
backfilling with compacted fill
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1289 10 Page 20
Following the clearing and grubbing all areas designated to receive
fill should be scarified to a depth of 6 inches water conditioned
and compacted to a minimum of 90 of the maximum dry density
established by ASTM D 1557 78
Fill Material
The saprolite and weathered tuff soils generated from the P 516
project excavation are generally suitable as fill material to be
placed above the water level
Where practical the better quality fill materials such as the
highly to moderately weathered tuff materials should be used within
the top 3 feet below the finished grade to provide a higher strength
subgrade condition which would allow thinner pavement sections for
the subsequent overlying aircraft pavements
Fills below the water level should consist of well graded granular
material generally less than 12 inches in size and with less than
10 passing the No 200 sieve fines The sand soils above the
water level over the present beach area could be used for such
underwater fill Some of the weathered to moderately weathered tuff
encountered in the deeper cut areas for the P 516 project may also
be suitable for underwater fill An 18 inch layer of coarse gravel
is recommended to be placed just above the high water level to
provide a capillary break to prevent the rise of excess moisture up
into the embankment f i11 soil during compaction

Compaction Requirements Above Water Fill
All fills should be placed in horizontal lifts not exceeding 9
inches in compacted thickness Fills should be compacted to a
minimum of 90 of maximum dry density for cohesive soils as
determined by ASTM D 1557 78 In the aircraft apron pavement areas
the ill within 12 inches of the finished subgrade level should be
additionally compacted to a minimum of 95
The in situ moisture content of the tuff soils generally varies from
about 40 to 60 while the proctor moisture content corresponding to
90 compaction varies from about 30 to 45 Therefore some amount
of drying of the excavated soil would be required prior to
compaction It is our opinion that sufficient drying of the
excavated soil could be achieved if the site grading is conducted
during the dry season
Where fills are to be placed on existing slopes steeper than 5
horizontal to 1 vertical the fill should be properly keyed and
benched into the existing slope to assure proper bonding to the
native ground For details see Plate 7

We anticipate that the select granular fill material would be dumped

APPENDIX C
SLOPE STABILITY ANALYSIS
Slope stability analyses for this investigation were performed
utilizing Slope II computer program which utilizes the limit equilibrium
theory to analyze the stability of an earth slope This program is a
resident library file in Control Data Corporation s CYBERNET Services
with data imput and output handled by an in house IBM PC micro computer
This comprehensive and flexible program analyzes slope stability by
the method of slices and factors of safety can be obtained for circular
and non circular failure surfaces considering earthquakes and live
loads Cross sections for analyses may consist of a maximum of 15 soil
layers and 1000 slices Various pore pressure conditions may be
incorporated in each analysis with any of the following six analytical
methods
1 The Fellenius or Ordinary method of slices
2 the Simplified Bishop method
3 the Spencer method
4 the Janbu s Simplified method
9 the Jjanbu s Generalized method
6 the Morgenstern Price method and
7 the Nonvellier method Lowe Karafiath method Corps of
Engineers method and modified Swedish method can be solved as
special cases of the Morgenstern Price method
For our analyses Janbu s Simplified method was utilized with
occasional cross check runs utilizing the Ordinary Method of slices and
the simplified Bishop method
