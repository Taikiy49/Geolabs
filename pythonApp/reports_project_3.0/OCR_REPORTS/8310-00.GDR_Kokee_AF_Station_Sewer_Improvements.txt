SECTION 1.  
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The FY22 Kokee AF Station Sewer Improvements project site is within the Kokee Air Force 
(AF) Station on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. Based on the information provided, we understand 
the proposed project includes the closing of two cesspools and replacing them with two 
Individual Wastewater Systems (IWS). Details of the proposed sewer improvements were not 
available at the time this report was prepared. The project location is shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1.  
Based on the agreed upon scope of services requested of Geolabs, Inc., our geotechnical 
field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling ten borings extending to depths ranging from 
about 3 to 13 feet below the existing ground surface at the locations provided. As requested, we 
conducted falling head percolation tests in eight of the soil borings at depths between about 3 and 
6.5 feet below the existing ground surface to evaluate the percolation characteristics of the 
subsurface materials encountered at the site in support of the planning and design of the IWS.

SECTION 3.  
SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
Of interest to geotechnical engineers for design are the subsurface materials encountered 
at the project site, the engineering properties of the materials encountered, and the variability 
of the subsurface conditions across the project site. Therefore, the following sections provide a 
description of the geologic setting of the project site, the surface and subsurface materials 
encountered at the site, and the groundwater conditions encountered. 
3.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Kaua`i is essentially a dissected basaltic dome that was initially formed by a 
single large shield volcano, which has subsequently been deeply eroded and partially covered 
with later (younger) volcanic materials. The Island of Kaua`i is the oldest of the main Hawaiian 
Islands and today encompasses approximately 555 square miles in area with a maximum 
elevation of about 5,170 feet above Mean Sea Level (MSL) at Mt. Wai’ale’ale near the center of 
the island. It is believed that during the Tertiary Period (about 5 to 6 million years ago in the 
Pliocene Epoch), basaltic lavas belonging to the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series built a roughly 
circular island volcano from the ocean floor that was estimated to have been about 20,000 feet 
deep at the time. Near the end of the Pliocene Epoch, this main shield building volcanic phase 
came to a halt. 
Following a long period of erosion and island mass subsidence, volcanism was renewed 
during the early Pleistocene Epoch (about 1.5 million years ago) with the eruption of the Koloa 
Volcanic Series from multiple vents throughout the island. This post-erosional volcanic activity 
laid a veneer of fresh lava and interbedded volcanic sediments over the older deposits of the 
Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series. Because of the long period of erosion between the eruption of 
the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series and the Koloa Volcanic Series, deposits of the Koloa Volcanic 
Series typically filled the erosional valleys and depressions that existed at that time. 
Consequently, the basaltic rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series are commonly found overlying thick 
residual soil and alluvial soil deposits. 
The dominant geologic processes of weathering, landform erosion, and sediment 
re-deposition followed during the late Pleistocene and Recent Epochs. Combined with the

The project site is underlain by basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lava of the Waimea

saprolite with increasing depth. In general, saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is 
typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks. The saprolite material grades with increasing 
depth to basaltic rock formation. 
3.2 
Subsurface Characteristics 
In general, we observed two main stratigraphic units at the proposed FY22 Kokee AF 
Station Sewer Improvements project site. The stratigraphic units consist of soil materials of 
varying character and engineering properties. The two main stratigraphic units are listed below 
in the general order of geologic age, beginning with the younger unit and ending with the older 
unit: 
1. 
Recent Fill 
2. 
Waimea Canyon Volcanics (Twnl) 
The following subsections briefly describe the general characteristics of each of the two 
primary stratigraphic units above based on our literature research, past experience with similar 
materials, and the field exploration and laboratory testing program.  
3.2.1 Recent Fill (Rf) 
This stratigraphic unit represents elevated areas and near-surface soils that were filled 
during construction of the AF station. At the project site where boreholes were drilled 
and sampled, the fill materials generally consisted of brown clayey silt of less than 1 foot 
thick.

The quality and composition of the fill materials are variable. These fills were frequently

3.2.2 Waimea Canyon Volcanics (Twnl) 
Waimea Canyon Volcanics is the oldest geologic unit observed in our test borings drilled 
at the Kokee AF Station. Generally, it consists of pahoehoe flows of basaltic lavas and may 
have a mantle of cobbles and boulders, or residual soils, overlying the top of the basalt 
formation. In addition, it is anticipated that various types of volcanic rock 
sub-components, such as clinker layers and lava tubes, also may be encountered at 
greater depths within the basalt formation. The following subunits are used in this 
geotechnical data report. 
Residual Soil 
This subunit consists of soils produced from the in-situ chemical weathering of the 
basic rock originating from basalt formation from the Waimea Canyon Volcanics. 
These soils generally consist of brown with varying colors of mottling, medium stiff 
to very stiff clayey silts. It should be noted that within the residual soils, Boring 
No. 2 encountered a layer of medium dense silty sand from about 2.5 to 4 feet 
below the existing ground surface and soft clayey silt near the bottom of the 
borehole. Residual soils are usually “structureless” and may have a blocky texture. 
Saprolite 
Saprolite essentially is derived from the tropical weathering of volcanic 
formations, which may contain intrusive rock bodies of variable composition, such 
as basalt, andesite, etc. Saprolite is a form of residual soil that exhibits a relict 
structure from the parent rock. Structural features, such as vesicles and joints, are 
visible in saprolite. This subunit consists of predominantly silty and clayey soils 
(and, in some instances, sandy and gravelly soils) containing variations in color 
with varying degrees of mottling.

SECTION 4.  
GEOTECHNICAL EXPLORATION PROGRAM 
The geotechnical exploration program conducted for the proposed Kokee AF Station 
Sewer Improvements project generally consisted of the following field and laboratory work 
components. A brief description of each work component is presented below. Detailed 
discussions, findings, and test results of the various components are provided in the respective 
appendices herein. 
4.1 
Test Borings and Associated Field Testing 
The test borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig utilizing 4-inch diameter 
continuous-flight solid-stem augers (SSA) to advance the borings. Our logger classified the 
materials encountered in the borings by visual and textural examination in the field and observed 
the drilling operations on a near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed by 
visual observation and laboratory testing. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend (Plate A-0.1). Deviations made to the soil 
classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil Classification Log Key 
(Plate A-0.2). Graphic representations of the materials encountered in the drilled borings are 
presented on the Logs of Borings in Appendix A. 
Sampling of the soils generally was performed between about 1.5 and 5-foot intervals. 
Drive samples were obtained using the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) split-spoon sampler 
(ASTM D1586) and the modified California sampler (ASTM D3550). 
In-situ testing of the subsurface materials in the borings included the SPT, which was 
performed in accordance with ASTM D1586. SPT sampling provides a measure of the resistance 
or consistency of a soil and is reported as the uncorrected N-value, which is defined as the 
number of blows required to drive a standard split-spoon sampler 12 inches with a 140-lb 
hammer falling 30 inches, after 6 inches of initial seating.

4.2 
Laboratory Testing 
Laboratory tests were performed on selected soil samples to determine the physical and 
engineering properties. Laboratory tests performed included moisture contents, unit weights, 
Atterberg Limits, grain size distributions, a direct shear test, a California Bearing Ratio (CBR) test, 
a modified Proctor compaction test, and one set of corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51), 
Minimum Resistivity (ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0) 
testing. The laboratory test results are presented in Appendix B. 
4.3 
Field Percolation Tests 
Field percolation tests were performed in Borings P-1 through P-6, P-5A, and P-6A in 
general accordance with the procedures in Appendix C of the State of Hawaii, Department of 
Health’s Administrative Rules, Chapter 11-62-31.2, “Wastewater Systems” to evaluate the 
percolation characteristics of the subsurface soils for wastewater disposal. The approximate field 
percolation test locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The individual percolation test 
results are presented on the Site Evaluation/Percolation Test forms in Appendix C. 
The percolation tests were performed in the subsoil material by drilling a 4-inch diameter 
borehole to depths between about 3 and 6.5 feet below the existing ground surface. During 
testing, the hole was carefully filled with clean water to a minimum depth of 12 inches above the 
bottom of the hole. The time intervals and water level drops were recorded to provide field data 
upon which calculation of the percolation test was based.

LIMITATIONS 
The geotechnical field and laboratory test data submitted in this geotechnical data report 
are based upon information obtained from the field exploration points. Variations of subsurface 
conditions beyond the field data points may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations 
may not become evident until construction is underway. 
The locations and elevations of the field exploration points indicated in this report are 
approximate, having been taped from existing visible features at the project site. Elevations 
noted on the borings were obtained from the Overall Sewer Plan, COSA, and Erosion Control Plan 
provided by the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers on July 29, 2021. The physical location and 
elevation of the borings should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the 
methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the field exploration 
points depict the approximate boundaries between soil types and, as such, may denote a gradual 
transition. We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration. 
However, it must be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in seasonal rainfall, surface 
water runoff and other factors.

Test Boring Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the FY22 Kokee AF Station Sewer Improvements 
project site by drilling and sampling ten borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2 and Boring 
Nos. P-1 through P-6A. The borings were extended to depths ranging from about 3 to 13 feet 
below the existing ground surface using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous-flight 
augers. The drilling and sampling work was conducted on July 15, 2021 and August 13, 2021. The 
boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  
The test borings were advanced with 4-inch diameter continuous-flight solid-stem augers 
(SSA) from the ground surface. After the borings were advanced to the maximum depth of the 
boreholes, the bored holes generally were backfilled with a cement/bentonite/water grout 
mixture. 
Our field personnel classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice for 
Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in the 
laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for 
Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on 
the Soil Log Legend (Plate A-0.1). Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with 
ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil Classification Log Key (Plate A-0.2). Graphic representations 
of the materials encountered and other observations, such as groundwater levels, etc., are 
presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-10. 
Soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled at the depths indicated in general 
accordance with ASTM D1586, Standard Test Method for Penetration Test and Split-Barrel 
Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound 
hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained 
from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D3550, Standard Practice for Thick 
Wall, Ring-Lined, Split Barrel, Drive Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD modified California 
sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. The blow counts needed to drive the 
sampler the second and third 6-inch increments of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples retrieved 
in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive 
strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on the Logs of Borings 
at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Samples recovered from our geotechnical field exploration program performed for the 
FY22 Kokee AF Station Sewer Improvements project were visually examined and classified in our 
laboratory in Waipahu, Hawaii. Selected samples were tested to evaluate the physical 
characteristics and engineering properties of the soils encountered. The laboratory testing 
program was formulated with the emphasis on evaluating the moisture, density, classification, 
strength, and corrosivity of the soils encountered. 
The laboratory tests performed included determination of dry density and natural 
moisture content, Atterberg Limits, grain size distribution, a direct shear test, a California Bearing 
Ratio (CBR) test, a modified proctor compaction test, and corrosivity tests including pH value and 
minimum resistivity testing. Results of the tests performed are presented on Plates B-1 through 
B-6. Detailed descriptions of the various tests performed for this exploration are presented 
below. 
Moisture and Density 
Moisture content and dry density tests were performed on selected soil samples to aid in 
defining the interface between the various soil layers encountered and correlating the layers 
between test borings. The moisture content tests were performed in general accordance with 
ASTM D2216. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample 
depths. 
Atterberg Limits 
To evaluate the liquid and plastic limits of the soil encountered, three Atterberg Limits 
tests were performed in accordance with ASTM D4318. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths, and graphic presentation of the test results is 
provided on Plate B-1. 
Sieve Analysis 
To provide information on the particle size distribution of the soils encountered, three 
Sieve Analysis tests were performed to evaluate the gradation characteristics and to aid in soil 
classification. The Sieve Analysis tests were performed in accordance with ASTM C117 and C136.

Field Percolation Tests 
Eight field percolation tests were performed in Boring Nos. P-1 through P-6, P-5A, and 
P-6A in general accordance with the procedures in Appendix C of the State of Hawaii, Department 
of Health’s Administrative Rules, Chapter 11-62-31.2, “Wastewater Systems” to evaluate the 
percolation characteristics of the subsurface soils for wastewater disposal. The approximate field 
percolation test locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  
The percolation tests were performed in the subsoil material by drilling a 4-inch diameter 
borehole to the specified depth ranging between about 3 and 6.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface. Prior to testing, the percolation test holes were prepared by removing the smeared and 
loose materials from the sidewalls and bottom of the borehole, respectively, and by placing about 
1-inch thick layer of coarse sand at the bottom of the borehole. During testing, the hole was 
carefully filled with clean water to a minimum depth of 12 inches above the bottom of the hole. 
The time intervals and water level drops were recorded to provide field data upon which 
calculation of the percolation rate was based. The percolation test results are presented on the 
Site Evaluation/Percolation Test forms, Plates C-1 through C-8.
