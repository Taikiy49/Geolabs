2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The
Koolau Volcano reached the end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years
ago
The Koolau Volcano was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the
northwest to southeast trending rift zones These lavas consist of interbedded
pahoehoe and a a type flows with pahoehoe lava being the dominant form A a is the
Hawaiian term for basaltic lava flow typified by a rough jagged spinose clinkery
surface Pahoehoe is the Hawaiian term for basaltic lava flows typified by smooth
billowy or ropy surfaces After the cessation of the main shield building phase of
Koolau Volcano there was a long period of erosion
During the Pleistocene Epoch renewal of volcanic activity occurred with the
eruptions of vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series Along the crest of the ridge along the
westerly side of Manoa Valley there are the Tantalus Roundtop and Sugarloaf vents
When these vents were active one or more lava flows from these vents spilled down
the westerly wall of the valley and moved across the valley floor The lava flows ponded
and filled the lower portion of Manoa Valley The ponding allowed the lava to densify as
it cooled forming dense massive rock The new lava flows from these vents blocked
Manoa Stream and forced the stream to the east between the East West Center and
Waahila Rise These flows generally terminated in the Moiliili District where the basalt
rocks were quarried in the past Reef limestone related to the Waimanalo stand of the
sea underlies the basalt at the former Moiliili Quarry

pressure diagram for one level of tieback anchors for the at rest condition are presented
on the Apparent Pressure Diagram Plate 4
In general the permanent tieback anchor system is constructed by drilling a
borehole installing a steel tendon or reinforcing bar into the borehole and grouting the
borehole The basic components of a grouted permanent tieback anchor consist of the
anchorage and the bonded and unbonded lengths of the steel tendon or reinforcing bar
In general the unbonded length of the tieback anchor is the portion of the steel tendon
or reinforcing bar that does not have direct contact with the grout allowing the tendon
or reinforcing bar to elongate elastically and transfer the resisting force from the
structure to the bonded length The bonded length of the tieback anchor is the portion of
the steel tendon or reinforcing bar that is directly bonded to the grout allowing it to
transfer the applied load into the ground Details of tieback anchor are shown on the
Tieback Anchor Detail Plate 5
We envision that the permanent tieback anchors would derive support principally
from the bond between the grout and the extremely to highly weathered basalt rock
anticipated at the project site The bond stress which is the pullout resistance per unit
area of the grout soil interface contact is variable depending on the type of soil and
grout the overburden stress and the construction procedures used in installing the
permanent tieback anchors An average bond stress of 2 000 pounds per square foot
psf may be used for the extremely to highly weathered basalt rock The bond stress
used in the design will need to be confirmed during construction by load testing The

Construction Considerations
The performance of the permanent tieback anchors depends significantly upon the
contractor s method of construction and construction procedures As a result of the
potential variations in the subsurface materials encountered at the project site a
Geolabs representative should be present to monitor the installation of the tieback
anchors during construction A specialty contractor experienced in the construction
of permanent tieback anchors should perform construction of the tieback anchors
In our opinion the following may have a significant impact on the effectiveness and
cost of the tieback anchors
Based on the subsurface conditions in the area drilling of the tieback anchor holes
may encounter hard basalt rock formation Our laboratory tests conducted on
selected rock core samples indicated that the unconfined compressive strength of
the basalt rock is up to about 16 700 pounds per square inch psi The contractor
should recognize that the actual hardness of the basalt rock would likely be higher
than 16 700 psi Therefore special drilling tools for drilling into and through the hard
rock will be required Temporary casing of the drilled holes for the permanent
tieback anchors will be required if caving in conditions occur during drilling of the
tieback anchor holes especially in the fill and saprolite layers
Grouting of the tieback anchors has a major impact on the load carrying capacities
of the tieback anchors A neat cement grout or sand cement grout mix should be
used Placement of grout should be by tremie methods beginning from the bottom
of the hole The grout should be placed through the tremie pipe by pressure
methods The grouting operations should be conducted promptly within 8 hours
after drilling to reduce the potential for significant caving in of the sides of the drilled
holes
Our borings encountered some severely fractured basalt rock Localized void cavity
may be also be encountered Due to the nature of the basalt rock formation it is

3 3 Keystone Retaining Wall
Based on the slope geometry and rock conditions we recommend installing
Keystone retaining wail system in front of the near vertical slope at the southeastern
quarry wall site to confine the severely fractured rock from raveling falling onto the
sidewalk and roadway adjacent to the slope as shown on the Schematic Keystone
Retaining Wall Plate 6
In general the Keystone retaining wall system is a composite wall system which
utilizes high density polyethylene or other reinforcing elements to reinforce the backfill
zone and improve the shear strength of the reinforced soil zone This composite system
essentially forms a gravity wall structure with an ability to tolerate significant total and
differential settlements In addition Keystone retaining walls are also desirable due to
the flexibility of the wall ease of construction high load carrying capacity and economy

Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling eight borings designated
as Boring Nos 1 through 8 extending to depths ranging from about 20 to 32 feet below
the existing ground pavement surface The approximate boring locations are shown on
the Site Plans Plates 3 1 through 3 3 The borings were drilled using both
truck mounted and portable drill rigs equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary
coring tools
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field and observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual
observation and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered in the test borings are presented on the
Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 8
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113 Diamond
Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a
hollow bit to cut into the coral rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description

74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples
of the clayey soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1 The test results are
presented on Plate B 1
Six Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on selected
core samples to evaluate their unconfined compressive strength of the basalt rock
formation encountered at the existing quarry wall The test results are presented on
Plate B 2
