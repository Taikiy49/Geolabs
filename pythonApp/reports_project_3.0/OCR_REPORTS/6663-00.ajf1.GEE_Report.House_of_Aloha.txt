SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered colluvial and/or alluvial soil deposits 
consisting of stiff to very stiff clayey soils with varying amounts of basaltic and coralline 
gravel extending to the maximum depth explored of about 21.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. Our laboratory test results indicate that these clayey soils exhibit 
moderate to high shrink/swell potential characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the 
soil moisture contents. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we believe a 
shallow foundation system (slab-on-grade with thickened-edge footings) may be used to 
support the chapel structure based on an allowable bearing pressure of 3,000 psf. 
Footings should have a minimum width of 12 inches and should be embedded a minimum 
of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent grade. Due to the moderately to highly expansive 
soil conditions encountered at the site, we recommend providing a minimum of 3 feet of 
non-expansive, select granular fill material beneath the building slab area. Prior to 
placement of select granular fill, the clayey soil subgrade should be scarified to a minimum 
depth of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture 
content, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. 
It should be noted that the generally dry climatic conditions in the Waianae area 
pose some challenges to the earthwork and chapel foundation construction at the site. 
Therefore, special attention should be given to the site preparation recommendations for 
the site grading and chapel foundation design to account for the moderately to highly 
expansive soil conditions. 
Conventional earthwork and construction methods may be used for the proposed 
project grading. It is anticipated that boulders embedded in the clayey soils underlying the 
site may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment. However, the use of 
hoerams may be required for excavation in some areas. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
design recommendations.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age 
and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. Waianae Volcano became 
extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried 
below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank. These banked or ponded lavas 
formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau. 
The Waianae Mountain Range is composed of layered basaltic lava flows and 
pyroclastic materials, which are grouped and classified as the Waianae Volcanic Series. 
The Waianae Volcanic Series is divided into the lower, middle, and upper volcanic 
members. The lower member comprises lava flows and associated pyroclastic rocks 
that built the main mass of the Waianae Shield Volcano. The middle member consists of 
rock that accumulated and gradually filled the vast volcanic caldera. The upper member 
is a relatively thin capping layer that covered the entire top of the shield volcano late in 
its history of evolution. 
Once the Waianae Shield Volcano formed, a long period of deep erosion, 
sedimentation, and subsidence of the Island of Oahu occurred, which produced the 
large valleys of the western side of the shield volcano. These erosional valleys were 
gradually filled with enormous accumulations of alluvium and colluvium deposited as a 
combined result of stream erosion, base level rise (sea level rise), and subsidence of 
the island mass. Over time, and as a result of sea level fluctuation, some of the older 
alluvial and colluvial deposits were buried and through consolidation and cementation, 
formed relatively dense deposits. However the current surface soil deposits above these 
older deposits are typically found to be unconsolidated. 
The project site is at the foot of Paheehee Ridge, an erosional remnant ridge 
from the western flank of the Waianae Mountain Range. Chemical and mechanical

Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
four borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, extending to depths ranging from 
about 6.5 to 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, a bulk sample of 
the near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1, was obtained to evaluate the pavement 
support characteristics of the near-surface soils. The approximate boring and bulk 
sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our field exploration generally encountered colluvial and/or alluvial soil deposits 
consisting of stiff to very stiff highly expansive clayey soil with varying amounts of 
basaltic and coralline gravel extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 
21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. These clayey soils graded with fragments 
of decomposed wood in Boring Nos. 1 and 2 at depths of about 10.5 and 12 feet, 
respectively. A relatively large boulder was encountered at a depth of about 16.3 feet in 
Boring No. 2, and a layer of cemented sand was encountered at a depth of about 
6.2 feet in Boring No. 3. 
It should be noted that the field percolation boreholes were drilled adjacent to 
Boring Nos. 3 and 4 to depths of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface. Similar 
subsurface conditions were encountered in these adjacent borings with the exception of 
the borehole adjacent to Boring No. 3 in which cemented sand was encountered at a 
depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface instead of 6.2 feet.  
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change 
due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other 
factors. Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered in the borings are presented 
on the Logs of Borings in Appendix A. Results of the laboratory tests performed on 
selected soil samples are presented in Appendix B. Results of the field percolation tests

2.4 
Seismic Design Considerations 
The project site may be subject to seismic activity, and seismic design 
considerations will need to be addressed. The following sections provide discussions on 
the seismicity of the Island of Oahu and the soil profile (site class) for seismic design. 
2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the 
tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to 
volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or 
during volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the 
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not 
erupt. The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but 
most are so small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. 
However, some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause 
minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes directly associated with the movement of magma are 
concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the Island 
of Hawaii. Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over 90 percent) are 
related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground shaking 
diminishes with increased distance from the Island of Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii 
has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, 
earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of Hawaii.  
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui also has experienced earthquakes greater 
than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have occurred in the 
County of Maui. The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Islands of Hawaii and 
Maui may be felt on the island of Oahu. For example, small landslides occurred on 
the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of 1938 (M6.8). Some 
houses on the Island of Oahu were reportedly damaged as a result of the Lanai 
Earthquake of 1871 (M7+).

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS

consisting of stiff to very stiff clayey soils with varying amounts of basaltic and coralline 
gravel extending to the maximum depth explored of about 21.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. Our laboratory test results indicate these clayey soils exhibit moderate 
to high shrink/swell potential characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the soil

In general, we believe that the primary geotechnical considerations for the project 
design include the following: 
Expansive clayey soils beneath foundations 
Site preparation and grading 
Pavement design 
It should be noted that the generally dry climatic conditions in the Waianae area 
pose some challenges to the earthwork and chapel foundation construction at the site. 
Therefore, special attention should be given to the site preparation recommendations

expansive soil conditions at the site.

moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The subgrade soils should be 
kept moist until covered with the select granular fill and/or improvements.

Shrinkage cracks at the existing ground surface were not observed at the time of

recurrence of the shrinkage cracks around the chapel foundation. 
Conventional earthwork and construction methods may be used for the proposed

3.1.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and 
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site or 
stockpiled in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the 
excavated materials.  
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be 
over-excavated to expose firm material, and the resulting excavation should be 
backfilled with well-compacted fill. 
Prior to filling, the existing ground should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. For pavement 
subgrades, the minimum relative compaction should be increased to 95 percent. 
Where shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade, we 
recommend thoroughly moistening the soil to close the cracks or preparing again 
as recommended above. Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed 
subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation 
of the soft areas and replacement with well-compacted fill.  
3.1.2 Fills and Backfills 
Generally, we anticipate the earthwork will likely involve two primary types of 
materials: select granular fill and general fill. Select granular fill should be used for 
the top 3 feet beneath the building slab area (and 2 feet beneath concrete 
walkways and exterior concrete flatwork) and consist of non-expansive granular 
material, such as crushed coralline and/or basaltic materials. The material should 
be well-graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest 
dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the 
No. 200 sieve. The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and 
should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with 
ASTM D 1883. Geolabs should test imported fill materials for conformance with 
these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use.

3.1.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
General fills and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to about 2 percent above 
the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Select 
granular fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction 
refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum 
dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. 
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to 
the maximum dry density.

for excavation in some areas. 
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based 
on field data obtained from the borings performed at the site. Contractors bidding 
on this project should be encouraged to examine the site conditions and soil data to 
make their own interpretation. 
3.1.5 Cut and Fill Slopes 
Fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to one 
vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be 
keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against 
sliding. The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in 
level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement 
recommendations. 
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope 
ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. Track-rolling of slopes should not be 
accepted. In the event that over-cutting of a slope occurs, keying and benching

requirements should be implemented instead of backfilling the slope to the design 
grade with sliver fills. Water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and 
slope planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for 
significant erosion of the finished slopes. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings, it appears that cut 
slopes should expose soils consisting of the very stiff clayey soils encountered. 
In general, we believe cut slopes exposing the soil materials encountered may be 
cut at an inclination of two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter.  
3.2 
Building Foundation 
We understand that a concrete slab-on-grade foundation with thickened edge 
footings likely will be used to support the proposed chapel structure. Due to the 
moderately to highly expansive soil conditions at the site, we recommend providing a 
minimum of 3 feet of non-expansive, select granular fill material beneath the building 
slab area. In addition, this select granular fill material should extend a lateral distance of 
at least 3 feet beyond the perimeter of the building slab.  
An allowable bearing pressure of 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be 
utilized for design of the chapel foundation bearing on properly compacted select 
granular fill material. This bearing value is for supporting dead-plus-live loads and may 
be increased by one-third (1/3) when considering transient loads, such as those caused 
by wind or seismic forces. Footings should have a minimum width of 12 inches and 
should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent grade. 
Footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping ground should be embedded 
deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal set-back distance of 8 feet measured 
from the outside edge of the footings to the slope face. 
We estimate that settlements for footings bearing on properly compacted fill, as 
recommended herein, to be less than 1 inch total. Differential settlement between 
footings supported on similar materials is estimated to be less than 0.5 inches.  
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure 
acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system and by friction developed

between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil. Resistance due to passive 
earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per 
square foot per foot of depth (pcf). This assumes that the soils around the footings are 
well-compacted. Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in the 
upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected. For footings bearing on properly 
compacted select granular fill, a coefficient of friction of 0.45 may be used. 
Foundations next to utility trenches should be embedded below a 1H:1V 
imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or the 
foundation should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This 
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with 
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement. 
We recommend placing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill below the 
slab. The cushion fill, consisting of open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), 
should provide uniform support of the slab and should serve as a capillary break. To 
reduce future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings, an 
impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the gravel cushion layer. Flexible 
floor coverings, such as carpet or sheet vinyl, should be considered because they can 
better mask minor slab cracking. It is also recommended to incorporate some flexibility 
into the interior wall design to accommodate small amount of possible ground 
movements due to the presence of expansive soils at the site. 
If exterior concrete walkways are used for this project, we recommend removing 
the expansive clayey soils below the chapel to a minimum depth of 2 feet below the slab 
and replaced with non-expansive, select granular fill material. In addition, we 
recommend providing a minimum 4-inch thick slab thickness with control joints at 
intervals equal to the width of the walkways and at right-angle intersections. 
It should be emphasized that the areas adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled 
tightly against the edges of the slabs with low expansion, relatively impervious soils. 
These areas also should be graded to divert water away from the slabs and to reduce 
the potential for water ponding around the slabs and foundations.

3.4 
Pavement Design 
We understand flexible pavements are being considered for the proposed 
driveway and parking area. In general, we anticipate vehicle loading for the new

our field exploration and laboratory testing, the on-site clayey soils exhibited marginal 
pavement support characteristics with a CBR value of about 10 and swell value of about 
3 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883. Based on the CBR and swell 
values of the on-site clayey soils, the following pavement section may be used for the

  6.0-Inch Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction) 
12.0-Inch Aggregate Subbase (95 Percent Relative Compaction)

The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a minimum depth of about 
8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and compacted 
to not less than 95 percent relative compaction. The subgrade soils should be 
thoroughly moistened and kept moist until covered by the pavement structural section. 
We recommend performing CBR and density tests on the actual subgrade soils 
encountered during pavement construction to confirm the adequacy of the above 
section. The recommended section also assumes that adequate drainage will be 
provided for the paved areas. 
Where minor shrinkage cracks are observed after subgrade preparation, we 
recommend thoroughly moistening the soil to close the cracks prior to re-compacting. 
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather 
and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with 
well-compacted fill.

Utilities 
It is anticipated that some underground utilities will be constructed for the 
proposed chapel. The contractor should determine the methods and equipment to be 
used for excavation, subject to practical limits and safety considerations. The 
excavation should comply with all applicable local, state, and federal safety 
requirements. The contractor should be responsible for trench shoring design and 
installation. Trench shoring and bracing should conform to the appropriate health and 
safety requirements. 
Based on our field exploration, it is anticipated that excavated trenches for 
utilities likely will encounter boulders embedded in the clayey soils underlying the site. In 
general, these soils may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment, such 
as excavating with a large backhoe. However, the use of hoerams may be required for 
excavation in some areas. 
In general, we recommend providing granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) under the pipes. The initial backfill 
up to about 12 inches above the pipes should consist of free-draining backfills, such as

No. 3B Fine gravel, to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from compaction of 
the backfill. It is critical to use a free-draining granular material to reduce the potential 
for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for 
the sides of the pipes. The use of on-site soils as backfill directly around utility pipes is 
not recommended. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes 
to the finished subgrade should consist of the approved on-site clayey soils with a 
maximum particle size of 6 inches or less or select granular fill material. The backfill 
material should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed 
in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence. 
The upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted 
to not less than 95 percent relative compaction. Mechanical compaction equipment 
should be used to compact the materials at the project site. Water tamping, jetting, or 
ponding should not be used to compact the backfill material.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
four borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, extending to depths of about 6.5 to 
21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  
Our field engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of 
Borings, Plates A-1 through A-4. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) 
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and 
evaluation of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at 
the appropriate sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth. Graphic presentation of the test 
results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Three one-inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils. The test 
results are summarized on Plate B-2. 
One Modified Proctor compaction test (ASTM D 1557) was performed on a bulk 
sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships. The test results are presented on Plate B-3.  
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test (ASTM D 1883) was performed on a 
bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plate B-4.

Field Percolation Tests 
To evaluate the permeability of the subsurface materials for wastewater disposal, 
percolation testing was performed at two locations at the proposed leach fields in 
general accordance with the State of Hawaii, Department of Health’s Administrative 
Rules, Chapter 11-62-31.2, “Wastewater Systems” and Chapter 10 of the Ten States 
Standards. To evaluate the subsurface soil profile and permeability of the soils, 
percolation tests were performed at about 5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Approximately 1 to 2 inches of gravel was placed at the bottom of the borehole to 
protect the bottom of the hole from scouring and sediments. During the test, the hole 
was carefully filled with water to a height of 6 inches above the gravel pack. The time 
intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which calculation of 
the percolation rates were based. Results of the field percolation tests are presented on 
Plates C-1 and C-2.
