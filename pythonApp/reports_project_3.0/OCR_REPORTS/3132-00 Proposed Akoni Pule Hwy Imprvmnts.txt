REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is situated on the southwestern flank of Kohala Mountain which is
the oldest of the five shield volcanoes forming the island of Hawaii Kohala Mountain was
built during the Pleistocene Epoch by basaltic lava flows from the Pololu and Hawi
Volcanic Series
Typically the lava flows of the Pololu series are composed of tholeitic olivine
basaltic lava flows grading to oceanite Generally such flows are characterized as thinly
bedded with a high degree of porosity
During the later stages of the active phase of Kohala Mountain vents of the Hawi
Volcanic Series opened
These events extruding lavas of andesitic and trachytic
composition built cinder cones along the main rift zones of the mountain and extruded
lavas which covered most of the Pololu rocks These andesitic rocks generally form
thicker and denser flows

SITE DESCRIPTION
The proposed Akoni Pule Highway Improvements project is located at Kawaihae
in the District of North Kohala on the island of Hawaii The portion of Akoni Pule Highway
that will be improved is to the northwest of Kawaihae as shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 1
The proposed highway improvements project begins at about Station 512 00 and
ends at about Station 540 00 as shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 and 3 We believe
that portions of the existing highway alignment along the widening project were
constructed on fill embankments of up to approximately 25 feet in height and portions
of the highway were constructed on cut ridges The slopes of the existing highway
embankment were observed to be as steep as 1½H 1V horizontal to vertical In some
areas the embankment slopes appear to be 2H 1V with loose cobbles and boulders on
the surface of the slope The existing cut slopes observed on the sides of the highway
varies from ¼H 1V to 1½H 1V with a maximum height of about 25 feet along the
alignment of the proposed highway improvements
The proposed highway improvements project traverses four drainage channels
and culverts were constructed at the bottom of drainage channels beneath the highway
embankments as shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 and 3 These culverts are located
diameters from 2 to 10 feet
The sides of the highway are generally vegetated with a thin grass cover with
isolated stands of algaroba Keawe trees Below the grass cover the ground surface
appears to be covered with boulders and cobbles creating generally a very rugged
surface

Lateral Earth Pressures
All retaining structures should be designed to resist the lateral earth pressures due
to adjacent soils and surcharge effects The recommended active lateral earth pressure
for design of cantilever walls expressed in equivalent fluid pressure is 40 p c f for an
assumed level backfill 6H 1V or flatter If the backfills behind the conventional retaining
walls are sloped up to an inclination of 2H 1V the walls should be designed with an active
lateral earth pressure of 55 p c f
The recommended lateral earth pressures above do not include hydrostatic
pressures that might be caused by groundwater trapped behind the walls Therefore
retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic pressures
A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 foot to 2 foot wide zone of permeable
material No 3B Fine gravel or ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation immediately adjacent to the
wall with a perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the wall discharging to a
discharge facility or weepholes A filter fabric such as Mirafi 140N or equivalent should
be placed between the drainage material and the compacted backfill material The
drainage material should extend from the base of the wall to a height within 2 feet below
finish grade The remaining backfill should consist of an impervious capping of select
non expansive soil choker fill material

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed
All vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to reduce
potential contamination of the excavated materials
Any soft weak or yielding areas disclosed during clearing operations should be
over excavated to expose firm ground and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with engineered fill compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction
Prior to placement of the backfill the over excavated subgrade
should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture content and compacted to at least 90 percent relative
compaction The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed
of off site Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices
for over excavation and engineered fill construction to account for variations in the
over excavation quantities
Due to the dry and sometimes windy site conditions it is advisable to institute a
dust control program during site grading operations

APENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling 4
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 at the approximate locations shown on
the Site Plans Plates 2 through 3 The depths of our borings range between about 26
and 34 ½ feet below the existing ground surface They were drilled using truck mounted
drill rigs equipped with continuous flight augers and advancing rotary coring tools as
appropriate
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory
Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A of Appendix A Graphic presentation of the
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring Logs as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
