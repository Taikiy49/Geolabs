SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The subsurface conditions at the proposed project site were explored by drilling 
and sampling two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 3 and 4, extending to depths of 
about 44 and 41.5 feet, respectively, below the existing ground surface.  In general, our 
drilled borings encountered a fill layer underlain by basalt formation and welded clinker 
extending to the maximum depth drilled of about 44 feet below the existing pavement 
surface.  The fill layer generally consisted of 2 inches of asphaltic concrete pavement 
underlain by very loose to medium dense silty sand with gravel and cobbles 
approximately 9 to 13 feet thick.  Basalt formation encountered generally consisted of 
medium hard to hard, moderately to slightly weathered basalt. Welded clinker 
encountered generally consisted of brecciated basalt and very dense silty/sandy gravel 
extending to the maximum depth drilled of about 44 feet below the existing pavement 
surface.  Due to time constraints and the need to open the road to traffic, groundwater 
was not measured in the borings drilled at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on the Damage Monitoring Inspection Report dated March 15, 2022 by 
CONSOR, undermining was observed on the upstream West (Kahului) Abutment 
measuring 22 feet long by 6.33 feet high by 7.5 feet deep. The majority of the 
undermining was observed beneath the upstream wingwall and also extends into the 
upstream bank. The observed undermining of the downstream end was 10 feet long by 
up to 3.5 feet high by up to 1 foot deep. Undermining was also observed beneath the 
East (Hana) upstream wingwall and East bank. The undermining measured 16 feet long 
by up to 10 feet high, generally 7.5 feet deep but up to 9 feet deep. Basalt rock layer 
was observed at the base of the CRM abutment wall. The undermining was located 
below this layer. 
Based on the borings, the abutment structures are generally bearing on 
moderately weathered basalt with Rock Quality Designation (RQD) values ranging from 
13 to 40. These RQD values are below the Federal Highways Administration (FHWA) 
requirement for non-scourable rock consisting of greater than or equal to 50. Therefore, 
the basalt rock that the abutments are bearing on is considered scourable. The welded 
clinker layers and basalt rock encountered below the abutment bearing layer are also 
considered scourable. 
It is our understanding that emergency remedial measures are desired for 
undermining observed at the abutment, wingwalls, and the upstream banks of the 
Kaaiea Stream Bridge. We believe that remedial measures consisting of the installation 
of grouted concrete fabric bags to fill the observed undermining and voids may be 
implemented for the emergency remedial measures.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the proposed Kaaiea Stream Bridge project, located in the Hana area on 
the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations based 
on data from our borings and laboratory testing performed for the proposed project. The 
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at 
the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
Based on a recent critical findings report, undermining caused by stream 
scouring was found beneath the Kahului abutment structure at the Kaaiea Stream 
Bridge in the Hana area on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. It was reported that the 
undermining was up to about 4.17 feet high by 4.17 feet deep by 6.5 feet in length, 
starting from the downstream end of the Kahului abutment going towards the upstream 
end. 
We understand that emergency remedial measures are desired for the 
undermining of the Kahului Abutment structure and the upstream banks of the Kaaiea 
Stream Bridge. In addition, we understand that a subsurface profile across the bridge 
structure is desired to determine if the existing abutment structures are bearing on 
non-scourable rock.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui is comprised of two major volcanoes: the older West Maui 
(Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala 
(Pleistocene Epoch). The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain between 
these two volcanoes. The project site is located on the northeastern lower flank of the 
Haleakala Volcano. 
The East Maui Volcano is represented by the older rocks of Honomanu Volcanic 
Series, buried beneath younger volcanic deposits. The Honomanu Series is overlain by 
the Kula Volcanic Series, which generally consists of thick lava flows with appreciable 
embedded pyroclastic (ash and cinders) materials. Rocks of the Kula Series cover 
much of the present-day surface of the East Maui Volcano. 
A long period of deep erosion and sediment deposition occurred between the 
end of the Kula Volcanic Series and the beginning of the Hana Volcanic Series. The 
Hana Series generally consists of the youngest lava flows and cinder cones presently 
encountered at the East Maui ground surface. Although the Hana lavas were generally 
more fluid and the lava flows tended to be thinner and less massive than the preceding 
Kula lavas, thick accumulation of lava did occur in the valleys and valley heads. 
The erosional process of volcanic islands is dominated by the detachment of 
weathered soil and rock materials from the interior steep mountain slopes, and the 
movement of the eroded materials downslope by gravity toward the lowland mountain 
perimeter areas. Once these eroded slope materials (colluvium) reach a valley stream, 
alluvial processes become dominant, and the sediments are further transported by 
flowing water and deposited as alluvium. 
In general, mountain stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy, such that 
the stream flows may transmit large volumes of water for a very short duration. Because 
of this, the transport of sediment is intermittent, and the bulk of a stream's hydraulic load 
may consist of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders, cobbles, gravel, sands, and fine soil 
particles. When the erosional base level of a land mass changes through long-term

changes in sea-level or the sinking/raising of the island mass, sedimentary deposits 
may either by reactivated renewed incision and erosion or remain deposited in-place as

When unconsolidated sedimentary deposits are buried in-place for long periods 
of time, chemical processes begin to alter the materials, simultaneously causing 
additional breakdown and weathering of the materials. The continued upland erosion 
generates new eroded materials, which are transported downslope and may cover the 
older alluvial and colluvial soil deposits. Depending on the erosional base level and the

of geologic time.  
The project site is located along Hana Highway traversing steep slopes on the 
sides of large drainage ravines. Because the bridge site is in steep terrain containing 
excavated cut slopes, surface alluvial and colluvial soil cover is anticipated to be limited 
at the project site. 
2.2 
Site Description 
The project site is located at the existing Kaaiea Stream Bridge, which is a 
single-span bridge about 22 feet long and consists of reinforced concrete tee beams 
covered with asphaltic concrete with one traffic lane in each direction.  The 
substructures consist of Cement Rubble Masonry (CRM) abutments and wingwalls on 
both the Hana side and Kahului side. The Kaaiea Stream Bridge was built in 1928 and 
is a historic bridge on the State/National Register. The East Maui Irrigation System 
components and a waterfall are located upstream of the bridge.

Seismic Design Parameters 
Based on the AASHTO LRFD Bridge Design Specifications Seventh Edition, 
2014, the project site may be subject to seismic activity, and seismic design 
considerations will need to be addressed. The following subsections provide 
discussions on the seismicity, soil profile type for seismic design, and the potential for 
liquefaction at the project site.  
2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by 
shifts in the tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked 
primarily to volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally 
occurs before or during volcanic eruptions.  In addition, earthquakes may result 
from the underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but

does not erupt. The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each 
year, but most of the earthquakes are so small that they can only be detected by 
instruments. However, some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and 
a few cause minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes (associated with volcanic activity) are most common on 
the Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement 
of magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa 
Volcanoes on the Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of the earthquakes in 
Hawaii (over 90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic 
activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from 
the Island of Hawaii.  The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous 
earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not 
confined only to the Island of Hawaii. 
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced numerous earthquakes 
greater than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have 
occurred in the County of Maui. Due to the relatively short period of documented 
earthquake monitoring in the State of Hawaii, information pertaining to 
earthquakes on the Island of Maui may not be complete. However, the following 
are two examples of reported earthquakes within the County of Maui. A 
Magnitude 6.8 earthquake occurred on the Island of Maui in 1938, and the Lanai 
Earthquake of 1871 was estimated at Magnitude 7 or greater. Based on these 
two reported earthquakes, the Island of Maui may be considered seismically 
active.

2.4.3 Liquefaction Potential 
Based on the AASHTO LRFD Bridge Design Specifications Seventh Edition, 
2014, the project site should be evaluated for the potential for soil liquefaction. 
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils near the 
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build-up of 
excess pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by 
earthquakes. In this process, when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected 
to vibration (such as during an earthquake), the soil tends to densify and 
decrease in volume causing an increase in pore water pressure. If drainage is 
unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up of pore water pressure, 
the effective stress (internal strength) of the soil is reduced. Under sustained 
vibrations, the pore water pressure build-up could equal the overburden 
pressure, essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to 
behave as a viscous fluid. During liquefaction, the soil acquires a mobility 
sufficient to permit both horizontal and vertical movements, and if not confined, 
will result in significant deformations. 
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained 
sands and loose silts with little cohesion. The major factors affecting the 
liquefaction characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows:

Emergency Remedial Measures 
It is our understanding that emergency remedial measures are desired for

undermining and voids may be implemented for the emergency remedial measures.

grout underwater.  Prior to placement of the fabric bags, any loose/soft material, sharp 
objects and debris should be removed from the location of work and properly disposed 
of by the Contractor. The Contractor should provide underwater video documentation 
for a clear visual inspection of the underwater work at the end of each underwater 
workday and at the completion of work.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon 
information obtained from our field borings and laboratory testing.  Variations of the 
subsurface conditions between and beyond the field data points may occur, and the 
nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until construction is 
underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the 
recommendations presented herein. 
The field boring locations indicated herein are approximate, having been 
estimated by using a hand-held Global Positioning System (GPS) device in the field and 
shown on the Topographic Survey Map prepared by Controlpoint Surveying, Inc. dated 
December 3, 2021. Elevations of the field borings were interpolated between the spot 
elevations shown on the same Topographic Survey Map. The physical locations and 
elevations of the borings should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by 
the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict 
the approximate boundaries between soil types and, as such, may denote a gradual 
transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown on the 
graphic representations and/or presented in the text herein. These data have been 
reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. However, it must be 
noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in rainfall, tides, temperature, and other 
factors.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 3 and 4, extending to depths of about 41.5 and 
44 feet below the existing pavement surface. The approximate boring locations are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig 
with continuous flight augers and coring tools.  
Our geologists classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock cores were described in 
general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log 
Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested 
Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the 
International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through 
A-2.2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM 
D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts 
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample. Results of the pocket penetrometer 
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that

uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core

Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered 
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities, discounting drilling 
induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5.0-foot 
core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of 
Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality 
of the rock, based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and 
Minerals."

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Four Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of the test 
results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Three Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plate B-2. 
Five Uniaxial Compressive Strength tests (ASTM D7012, Method C) were 
performed on selected intact core runs to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength 
of the basalt formation encountered.  The test results are presented on Plate B-3. 
Two 
Unconsolidated-Undrained 
Triaxial 
Compression 
(TXUU) 
tests 
(ASTM D2850) were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained 
shear strength of the silty gravel soils encountered. The approximate in-situ effective 
overburden pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the relatively 
“undisturbed” soil sample. The test results and the stress-strain curves are presented on 
Plates B-4 and B-5. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected soil sample 
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the in-situ soils. The test results are 
presented on Plate B-6.
