2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the western
third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern two thirds
of the island It is believed that Waianae Volcano became extinct while Koolau Volcano was
still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in Central Oahu
The Koolau Volcanic Shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by
thinly bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities ceased approximately
2 5 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicate that the Island of Oahu has
subsided by as much as 1 200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic activity
Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern coast
of the Island of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of
the reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed behind
the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the lagoons
Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes as
a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to fill the
oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels rose
because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained essentially stable
during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These giacio eustatic
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower relative to the
present sea level of Oahu

The higher sea ievel stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of terrigenous
sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at correspondingly higher
elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters protected by
fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and
reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the calcareous

deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action extended
the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal sediments

reef limestone at elevations roughly conforming to the level of the sea during that period
About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred During that rise the

adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued to

soft loose sediments along the coast in areas that were formerly valleys and drainageways
The area north of the project site is geologically situated on the southwestern flank of
the Koolau Volcanics These volcanics are composed of basaltic lava flows of early
Pleistocene Age The basalt is overlain by a complex inter layered system of terrestrial and

silt and clay eroded from the older rock of the area In recent time the efforts of man have
shaped the low lying land to its present form by the placement of fill in the low lying area

3 1 Liquefaction Potential
Liquefaction occurs when a build up of excess pore water pressure is induced in
saturated cohesionless soils resulting in a substantial loss of strength The most common
cause of this phenomenon is the cyclic loading due to earthquakes This process may permit
the soil to move both vertically and horizontally and if not confined can result in significant
deformations Loose uniformly graded fine grained sands and loose silts with little cohesion
are most susceptible to liquefaction Generally liquefaction is less likely to occur if the loose
deposit is deeper than 50 feet below the ground surface or the deposit contains an appreciable
percentage of clay materials
We believe that the loss of soil strength and associated ground movements could
potentially have a severe impact on the proposed development The subsurface information
obtained from the drilled borings indicate that the site is generally underlain by very loose to
loose silty sand lagoonal deposits at depths ranging from about 10 to over 50 feet below the
existing ground surface Based on our analysis the loose iagoonal deposits encountered at
the project site have the potential to liquefy and lose significant strength under seismic
conditions assuming a peak ground acceleration of 0 15g Zone 2A based on the Uniform
Building Code 1997 We estimate that the area within about 40 to 50 feet behind the top of
slope or shoreline may experience some lateral ground movements during a seismic event

3 2 Ground Settlements
Based on the borings the project site is underlain by a thick lagoonal deposit of very
loose and soft soils The placement of additional fill at the project site should be minimized to
reduce the potential for additional ground settlements due to consolidation of the underlying
very loose and soft soils due to the new fill loads As indicated previously we anticipate that
some new fills of about 2 to 4 feet will be required to raise the existing ground surface at the
site Ground surface settlements will occur as a result of the new fills planned at the site
Therefore we recommend that a settlement monitoring program be implemented at the site
to evaluate the magnitude and rates of settlements We recommend that a settlement waiting
period be observed in the construction schedule after placement of the new fills Construction
of pavements utility line installation and building construction should be delayed as long as
possible until after a majority of the settlements has occurred
In general our borings indicate that the highly compressible lagoonal deposits are
present at about 10 to over 50 feet below the existing ground surface Based on our
laboratory test results the very loose and or soft soil deposits may be considered to be
normally consolidated It should be noted that due to variations in the subsurface profile
across the project site it is very difficult to accurately predict the total settlements and
therefore the differential settlements

order of about 4 to 8 inches at the site Based on our analyses we believe that about 60 to

3 3 Settlement Monitoring
For a deposit of highly compressible soils such as the lagoonal deposits encountered
at the site the rates of the estimated settlements could vary by as much as 50 to 100 percent
from the actual settlement period required Therefore the actual settlement rate should be
monitored and provisions should be made for potential delays in the project construction
schedule if a longer period is required
To monitor the actual settlement rate we recommend that a series of settlement
gauges be installed in the fill areas at the site The locations of the settlement gauges should
be established by Geolabs when the grading plan is developed for mass grading of the site
In general the gauges should be read optically by a professional surveyor and the readings
should be transmitted to Geolabs for review in a timely manner The settlement gauges
should be established on the existing ground surface prior to any filling operations Readings
should be taken once a month for at least 12 to 18 months or until the settlement rate has
stabilized

3 5 Site Grading
Based on the borings the project site is underlain by a thick lagoonal deposit of very
loose and soft soils In general we recommend that the placement of additional fill at the

settlement In addition additional fill placement is discouraged to reduce the long term
settlements of the underlying very loose and soft soils due to the new fill loads However we

disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and
grubbing below areas designated to receive fill or future improvements should be
over excavated to expose firm and or dense material and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with well compacted fill The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly
disposed of off site

and should have a CBR value of 20 or greater with a CBR swell value of 1 percent or less
The fill material should also be free of vegetation and deleterious materials In general the
surface fill material encountered in the borings consisting of silty sands and sandy gravel
should be suitable for fill material

We anticipate that the vehicle loading for the project will consist primarily of passenger
vehicles light pick up trucks and occasional moderately heavy to heavy trucks We have
made our preliminary pavement design assuming that the pavement subgrade soils will be
similar to the silty sands and gravels encountered in our borings Based on the above
assumptions we recommend that the following pavement section be used for preliminary
design purposes

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
six borings extending to depths ranging from about 61 5 to 182 feet below the existing ground
surface The approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight
hollow stem augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials encountered are
presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches
In addition samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the
field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive
strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the Logs
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples of the
soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of the tests
are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
Two consolidation tests ASTM D 2435 with time rates of consolidation were
performed on samples of the soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the
materials encountered The results of the consolidation tests are presented on Plates B 2 and
B 3
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test ASTM D 1883 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils
The test results are presented on Plate B 4
Eight sieve analysis tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples of soils
to determine the grain size distribution of the soil and to aid in soil classification Results of the
tests are presented on Plates B 5 and B 6
