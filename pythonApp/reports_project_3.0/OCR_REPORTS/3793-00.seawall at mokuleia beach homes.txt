INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Seawall at Mokuleia Beach Homes across Dillingham Field
on Farrington Highway in Mokuleia on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location
and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
GEOLABS HAWAII

This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for the proposed
project These recommendations are intended for design input only and are subject to
the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
We understand that a new seawall is planned along four adjacent lots at the
project site Based on the information provided the seawall will be a gravity wall
constructed of grouted stone masonry approximately 18 feet high and 14 5 feet wide at
the base We also understand that on site material will be used as back fill for the
seawall Detailed information regarding foundation loading was not available to us at the
time this report was prepared
Our previous experience in the area suggests that the project site may be
underlain by layers of coral sand coral sands with gravel coral detritus stiff silty clays
and coral formation Special attention was given to the presence of hard subsurface
materials such as coral formation for foundation support and or anchors

formulation of geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the design of
foundations and site preparation for the proposed project Our work was performed in
general accordance with our revised fee proposal dated April 28th The scope of work
included the following tasks and work efforts

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age and forms
the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3793 00 Page 4
was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking
against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad plateau referred
to as the Schofield Plateau
The project site is located on the north flank of the Waianae Shield Volcano After
the cessation of volcanic activity in the late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Epoch erosional
dissection of the original dome created a thick alluvial apron along the seaward margins
of the Waianae Range thus providing a salient for the growth of coral reefs During the
Pleistocene Epoch sea level fluctuated widely as the result of glacio eustatic changes
When the sea stood at higher levels than the present the coral reefs grew to elevations
above present sea level At lower stands of the sea erosion of the adjacent land mass
resulted in the accumulation of alluvial deposits The materials encountered at the project
site are products of these Pleistocene depositional processes and are composed of
interbedded sediments of both marine and terrigenous origin
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located across Dillingham Field on Farrington Highway in
Mokuleia on the island of Oahu Hawaii At the time of our field exploration the project
site consisted of four beach homes on lots with existing seawalls in various stages of
disrepair At the home where the borehole was drilled the existing seawall had eroded
away exposing a 10 foot wide layer of backfill consisting of large boulders and cobbles
The backfill had settled approximately 1 to 2 feet below the existing ground surface Of
the four lots included in the subject project this seawall was in the worst condition

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered a thin layer of clayey silt overlying coral
sand The coral sand encountered graded from medium dense to very loose Ideally the
walls should bear on hard erosion resistant formations however hard formations were
not encountered during our field exploration Therefore special attention should be given
the Wall Foundations section of this report
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering design
recommendations are presented in the following sections of this report
eawall
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our boring at the project site it
is our opinion that the following general guidelines may be used for the design of the
proposed seawall structure

Wall Foundations
In general we believe that retaining wall foundations may be designed with an
allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot p s f bearing
on the in situ coral sand This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third for transient loads such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces
Ideally the walls should bear on hard erosion resistant formations to resist
undermining by wave action however hard layers were not encountered within
the depth of our exploration In order to provide some resistance the wall
foundations should be embedded as deep as practicable It should be understood
that the walls will be susceptible to undermining by wave action
Other methods to increase the resistance of the wall to undermining are available
but are generally costly These may include cement or chemical grouting under
the wall foundations and installation of permanent sheetpiles to depths below the
wall foundations

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures above the water level A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to
2 foot wide zone of permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation immediately around a perforated pipe perforations down at an
elevation above the water level discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone should consist of granular fill material
less than 3 inches in maximum dimension Unless covered by concrete slabs the

reduce water infiltration behind the walls
Site Gradin
Based on our understanding of the proposed project we anticipate that site
preparation work for the proposed construction will consist of excavating and backfilling
up to about 18 feet for the seawall
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared All debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed
and disposed of properly off site For the excavation the method and equipment to be
used should be determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety
considerations The excavation should comply with all applicable local state and federal
safety requirements Areas designated to receive fill should be over excavated to
expose firm natural material and kept as dry as possible prior to placement of fill
In general backfill materials should consist of well graded granular materials less
than 3 inches in largest dimension The on site excavated sandy materials may be
reused as a source of backfill materials provided they are processed and reduced to a
relatively well graded select granular material with a maximum size of about 3 inches
Any imported material if needed should be well graded from coarse to fine with no
particles greater than 3 inches in largest dimension The material should have a
laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of
less than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 The
fill material should contain between 10 and 30 percent passing the No 200 sieve Any
imported material should be observed and or tested by Geolabs Hawaii for its intended
use prior to being transported to the site
GEOLABS HAWAII

Fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the
in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as
determined by ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Site preparation operations should be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii It is important that a representative from our office observe the site
grading operations to evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the
excavation process and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those
encountered during our field exploration

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based in part
upon information obtained from the field boring Variations of subsoil conditions away
from the field boring may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not
become evident until construction is underway If variations then appear evident it will be
necessary to reevaluate the recommendations provided in this report
The location of the field boring indicated in this report was taped and or paced
with reference to the features shown on the Topographic Survey Map transmitted by Tom
Nance Water Resource Engineering on The elevation of the field boring
was estimated from the contour lines shown on the same plan The physical location and
elevation of the field boring should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by
the methods used
The stratification lines shown on graphic representations of the boring depict the
approximate boundaries between soil rock types and as such may denote a gradual
transition Water level data from the boring was measured at the times shown on the
graphic representations and or presented in the text of this report This data has been
reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report However it must be
noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in rainfall tides temperature and other
factors
