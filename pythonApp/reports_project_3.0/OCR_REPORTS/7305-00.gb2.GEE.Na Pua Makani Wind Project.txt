SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The WTG locations at the western portion of the development are generally 
underlain by residual/saprolitic soils overlying soft to medium hard, highly to moderately 
weathered basalt rock formation. The WTG locations at the eastern portion of the 
development generally encountered variable subsurface conditions.  The subsurface 
conditions at WTG-6 location consists of residual/saprolitic soils; WTG-7 location consists 
of alluvium; and WTG-8, WTG-9, substation/switchyard, and the O&M building locations 
generally consist of coralline sandstone and medium dense to dense coralline deposits. 
Based on our evaluation of the subsurface conditions, the principal geotechnical 
engineering considerations for the proposed project include foundation support for the 
wind turbine generators; probing and grouting for potential cavities beneath the wind 
turbine generator and O&M building foundations bearing on basalt rock and coralline 
sandstone/coralline deposits; design and construction of cut and fill slopes; and site 
preparation and earthwork construction in high rainfall environment. 
 Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, we recommend supporting the 
wind turbine generators on mat foundations. The mat foundations would bear entirely on a 
cut condition or on a cut-and-fill transition.  To provide more uniform support and to reduce 
the potential for appreciable total and differential settlement, we recommend placing a 
minimum 3-foot thick layer of structural fill below the mat foundations.  The structural fill 
should also extend a minimum of 3 feet beyond the perimeter of the mat foundations.   
We anticipate voids and/or cavities may be present in the basalt rock, coralline 
sandstone, and coralline deposits.  Based on the proposed finished grades at the WTG 
locations, the foundation subgrades at WTG-3/3A will likely consist of basalt rock.  The 
foundation subgrades at WTG-8, WTG-9, substation/switchyard, and the O&M building will 
likely consist of coralline sandstone and coralline deposits. Therefore, we recommend 
implementing a program of cavity probing and grouting of the foundations during 
construction in order to reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from 
collapse of cavities below the foundations. 
Immediately after construction of the cut and fill slopes, planting or other means of 
erosion control such as Erosion Control Matting (ECM) should be placed on the slope 
face.  The slope planting or ECM should reduce the potential for erosion of the exposed 
soils at the cut and fill slope faces.  In addition, drainage ditches should be provided at the 
top of the slopes to collect and divert surface water from the slope faces to appropriate 
discharge outlets.

SECTION 1. GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This geotechnical engineering exploration report presents the results of our 
analyses and geotechnical recommendations for the Na Pua Makani Wind Project in 
Kahuku on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are 
shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations derived 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses. The 
recommendations presented herein are intended for design of the wind turbine 
generator foundations, building foundations, retaining structures, site grading, 
pavements, and underground utilities only. The findings and recommendations 
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project involves the development of a proposed wind turbine electrical 
generation facility in the Kahuku area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The proposed 
project consists of nine (9) Wind Turbine Generators (WTG) designated as WTG-1 
through WTG-9. In addition, alternate locations are proposed for WTG-1 through 
WTG-3 and WTG-5. The alternate locations are designated as WTG-1A, WTG-2A, 
WTG-3A, and WTG-5A and are adjacent to the primary location sites. A 
substation/switchyard, Operations and Maintenance (O&M) building, transmission lines, 
access roadways to each WTG location, and other site improvements are also planned. 
The substation/switchyard and O&M building are planned at the eastern portion of the 
site near WTG-8 and WTG-9.  
For discussion purposes, the WTG project site may be separated into two 
geographic regions comprising the eastern and western portions of the project site. The 
western portion of the project will include WTG-1 through WTG-5 and their respective 
adjacent alternate locations. The main existing access road serving the western portion 
of the project is through the agricultural access road westerly of Pualalea Street.  The

SECTION 2. SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was formed by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The 
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) 
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. Waianae Volcano 
became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active, and the eastern flank of Waianae 
Volcano was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank. 
Based on our review of available geological information, the upper elevation hills 
at the western and southern portions of the project site are generally composed of 
weathered basaltic rock and derived soil products resulting from lava flows of the 
Koolau Volcanic Series. Weathering of the parent basaltic rock has developed a 
near-surface horizon of residual and saprolitic soils. In general, the residual and 
saprolitic soils are primarily composed of silty and clayey materials that are typical of the 
deep tropical weathering of the parent volcanic rock. In general, the stiff saprolitic soils 
grade to hard basaltic rock formation with increasing depth.  Embedded within the 
saprolitic soils are occasional hard basaltic boulders that remain intact and more 
resistant to chemical weathering processes. We observed some of these basaltic 
boulders sporadically at the ground surface in the higher elevations of the hilly terrain at 
the western and southern portions of the project site. The existing boulders represent 
erosional remnant “corestones” of the parent rock material.  The hard and typically 
spherical shaped boulders are mainly present on the hill top summits and ridgelines of 
the project site. The remnant boulders typically occur as embedded rocks, partial 
surface outcroppings, and as isolated surface rocks. 
Based on our review of the available geological information, the eastern and 
northern portions of the project site are underlain by clayey alluvial soils, lithified 
calcareous dune sand (sandstone), and some unconsolidated calcareous dune sand. In 
places, a generally thin mantle of alluvial soils cover the buried sandstone and dune 
sand deposits. In general, the eastern and northern portions of the project site contain

2.2 
Site Description 
The project site is south and west of Kahuku town adjacent to existing rural 
agricultural farmland, as shown on the General Site Plan, Plate 2.  In general, the 
proposed WTG’s are clustered at two separate portions of the project site, previously 
referred to as the western and eastern areas of proposed WTG development.   
The western portion of the WTG development contains WTG-1 through WTG-5 
and their respective alternate sites, which are generally within the large parcel identified 
as TMK: 5-6-008: 006.  The main existing paved access route to the western portion of 
the development is via the existing paved farmland access roads westerly of 
Pualalea Street.  The eastern portion of the development contains WTG-6 through 
WTG-9, substation/switchyard, and O&M building, which is generally within the large 
parcel identified as TMK: 5-6-006: 018. The main existing access to WTG-6 through 
WTG-9, substation/switchyard, and O&M building is via the existing dirt road identified 
as Malaekahana Valley Road.  
The proposed WTG development within TMK: 5-6-008: 006 at the western 
portion of the project site generally resides within existing undeveloped hilly forested 
lands that are generally densely vegetated with trees, shrubs, and grasses. The western 
portion is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 3.1. The proposed development at the western 
portion of the project is bounded by existing agricultural farms toward the east and 
undeveloped natural hills and gullies toward the north, west, and south of the proposed 
development. Some existing dirt access trails occupy the hills within the WTG 
development area.

2.4.1 Residual Soils 
Residual soils are completely weathered volcanic rocks that have lost all visible 
rock texture characteristics. The residual soils at the project site are typically brown 
to reddish brown in color. The residual soils may be mottled with various colors. 
Residual soils are comprised mainly of clayey materials with stiff to very stiff 
consistency. Typically, residual soils occur overlying saprolitic soils or weathered 
basalt formation. It should be noted that relict boulders of hard rock may be 
encountered in residual soils. In addition, there is potential for encountering hard 
basalt rock ledges in residual soils because the volcanic rock may not have 
weathered uniformly throughout the rock formation.  
2.4.2 Saprolitic Soils 
Extremely weathered volcanic rock is referred to as saprolitic soils (or saprolites). 
Saprolitic soils represent rock that has been reduced by weathering to soil-like 
components with weathered rock fragments. The soil material retains the remnant 
rock texture such as layering, vesicularity, and some fracture patterns. Saprolitic 
soils are typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks and are composed of 
mainly silty materials. The coloring scheme of saprolitic soils depends on the type 
of parent volcanic rock from which the soils are derived and the degree of chemical 
weathering that the material has experienced. Because the parent material does 
not weather uniformly throughout the formation, relict boulders of hard rock and/or 
hard rock ledges occasionally may be encountered within saprolitic soils. 
2.4.3 Basalt Formation 
Basalt formations include a broad range of volcanic basaltic rock. The basalt rock 
material was extruded as sequential layered lava flows from volcanic vents. The 
basalt rock, which frequently contain interbedded porous clinker materials, range 
from very hard to soft rock. Weathering (decomposition) ranges between highly

2.4.4 Older Alluvium 
Older alluvium generally consists of semi-consolidated, eroded, and transported 
terrestrial sediments including silts, clays, sands, and some gravel, which are 
deposited by flowing water. Alluvial deposits mainly are encountered adjacent to 
stream and estuary environments, but they also may be encountered where 
sediment laden runoff emanating from higher elevation terrain is deposited on 
adjacent lower elevation, flatter terrain. The deposits may be encountered as wide 
spread valley fill deposits or as elongated channel filling deposits. The consistency 
of the older alluvial deposits at the site was generally very stiff. 
2.4.5 Consolidated (Sandstone) and Unconsolidated Calcareous Dunes 
Calcareous dune sands consist of thin-bedded and cross-bedded aeolian limestone 
composed of uniform fine calcareous sands. The fine sands were blown inland from 
ancient beaches. Over time, the fine sands became lithified resulting in sandstone 
with relative hardness ranging between medium hard and hard. The sandstone 
may contain sufficient solution channels to be permeable. 
2.4.6 Coralline Detritus 
Coralline detritus deposits generally consist of unconsolidated to weakly 
consolidated granular marine deposits containing variable coralline reef detritus 
(debris) mixed with finer-grain sediments. Coralline detritus may include both 
grayish (marine) and brownish (terrestrial) sediments mixed with various coralline 
gravel, shell fragments, and some sands. The materials typically are deposited in 
shallow bays, lagoons, estuaries, stream deltas, and along outer fringing reefs 
where higher wave energy environments exist, which assist in generating the 
variable detrital materials. The deposits typically are encountered as loose granular 
materials, but they may become medium dense in relative density when buried and 
consolidated for longer periods of time. Coralline detritus may be encountered

Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the International Building Code, 2006 Edition (IBC 2006) as amended, 
the project site may be subject to seismic activity and seismic design considerations will 
need to be addressed. The following subsections provide discussions on the seismicity 
and soil profile type for seismic design. 
2.5.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic 
plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic 
activity; therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground 
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The 
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so 
small that they can be detected only by sensitive instruments. However, some of 
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate 
damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on 
the Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of earthquakes in Hawaii 
(over 90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and 
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of 
Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than 
Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of 
Hawaii. 
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes 
greater than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have 
occurred in the County of Maui. The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Islands

3.5.3 Retaining Structure Drainage 
Retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the potential for build-up of 
hydrostatic pressures behind the structure. A typical drainage system for the 
retaining structures should consist of about 1 cubic foot of permeable material, such 
as open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), wrapped with non-woven 
filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) at each of the weep hole locations. The weep 
holes should be spaced no more than 6 feet apart. Unless covered by concrete or 
asphaltic concrete, the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively 
impervious materials, such as the on-site clayey soils, to reduce the potential for 
significant water infiltration behind the retaining structure.

3.6.1 Keying and Benching 
Due to the relatively extensive areas of steep slopes at the project site, keying and 
benching will be required for earthwork. These keys and benches should be seated 
firmly into the stiff/dense soils.  
At the toe of fills on ground where the existing slope is steeper than 5H:1V, 
a keyway should be cut down into the in-situ soils. This key should be a minimum 
of 2 feet deep at the toe of the fill and should be a minimum of 8 feet wide 
extending back under the toe of the fill. To bond the fill into the existing slope, the 
floor of the key should be sloped back under the base of the fill at about 10H:1V or 
flatter. 
As fill placement progresses up the existing slope, benches should be cut into the 
existing slope to bond the mass of the fill to the existing ground. These benches 
generally should follow the prevailing slope of the existing ground, but they should 
be a minimum of 3 feet high and approximately 10 feet wide.  A typical detail for 
keying and benching for fills on slopes steeper than 5H:1V is shown on Plate 4.

3.6.4 Fill and Backfill Materials 
The fill and backfill materials below and around the structural fill layer and below the 
pavement subgrade may consist of the excavated on-site soils, provided that they 
are screened of vegetation, deleterious materials and particles greater than 
6 inches in maximum dimension. Based on our field exploration and laboratory test 
results, it appears that the on-site clayey soils have high in-situ moisture contents. 
Therefore, drying or aerating the excavated clayey materials may be necessary 
prior to use as general fill.  
In addition, we anticipate that large cobbles and boulders will be encountered 
during the mass excavation of the site. Boulders encountered in the excavated soils 
should be removed and should not be re-used as fill or backfill materials unless 
they are crushed to particle sizes of 6 inches in maximum dimension. 
Imported fill materials should consist of soil materials with a maximum particle size 
of 6 inches or less with sufficient fines (between 10 and 60 percent particles 
passing the No. 200 sieve) to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted 
mass. In addition, imported fill materials should have a CBR value of 12 or greater 
and a swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883.  
Structural fill materials should consist of non-expansive, select granular fill 
materials, such as crushed basalt or coral. The materials should be well graded 
from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and 
should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The 
materials should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a 
maximum swell of 1 percent or less.  The excavated on-site coralline deposits may 
be used as a source of structural fill provided that they meet the requirements 
above. 
Aggregate base and subbase course materials required for the project should 
consist of crushed basaltic aggregates and should conform to Subsections 703.06 
and 703.17 of the Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction 
(2005).

3.8 
Transmission Line Foundations 
Based on the information provided, overhead transmission lines supported on 
poles are planned from the substation/switchyard to Kamehameha Highway.  The 
transmission poles include some large-angle turning pole structures with relatively high 
groundline loads. 
Based on our field exploration, the subsurface conditions along the proposed 
transmission main alignment can be separated into two soil profiles.  The subsurface 
conditions along the proposed access road to the substation generally encountered very 
stiff to hard alluvial deposits over weathered coralline sandstone consisting of medium 
dense sandy gravel.   
The subsurface conditions along Malaekahana Valley Road consist of fill over 
soft/loose lagoonal deposits.  The thickness of the lagoonal deposits ranged from about 
3 to 11 feet.  In addition, the lagoonal deposits may extend down to a depth of about 
25 feet below the existing ground surface near Kamehameha Highway.  Below the 
lagoonal deposits, very stiff alluvial deposits were encountered.   
Based on the anticipated groundline loading information and the subsurface conditions 
encountered along the proposed transmission line alignment, we recommend using a 
single cast-in-place concrete drilled shaft to support the large turning pole structures.  In 
general, drilled shaft foundations are constructed by drilling a hole down into the bearing 
strata, placing reinforcing steel, and then pumping high slump concrete to fill up the 
hole. The result is a cast-in-place concrete drilled shaft for foundation support. 
The cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally 
from skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding soils.  The end-bearing 
component of the drilled shaft should be discounted in the compressive load capacity 
analysis due to difficulties associated with obtaining a clean bottom during construction.  
The net allowable compressive load capacity of the shaft may be used for 
dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as 
wind or seismic forces.

3.8.1 Lateral Load Resistance 
Lateral loads imposed on the large turning pole foundations may be resisted by 
resistance of the shaft-soil system to deflection.  Lateral load resistance of the 
drilled shaft is a function of the stiffness of the surrounding soil, the stiffness of 
the shaft, allowable deflection at the top of shaft, and induced moment in the 
shaft.  We understand the lateral load analyses will be performed by others using 
the program LPILE-plus for Windows, which is a microcomputer adaptation of a 
finite difference, laterally loaded pile program originally developed at the 
University of Texas at Austin.  The soil parameters to be used for lateral load 
analyses of the cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts along the Substation Access

3.8.3 Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations 
Drilled shafts are desirable for the transmission line pole foundations because of 
the significant increase in lateral load capacity when compared to shallow 
foundations. However, the performance of the drilled shafts will depend significantly 
upon the contractor's method of construction and construction procedures.  In our 
opinion, the following conditions would have a substantial effect on the 
effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft foundations. 
Based on the field exploration, loose/soft lagoonal deposits were encountered in all 
of the borings drilled along Malaekahana Valley Road.  Due to the loose/soft nature 
of these materials, caving-in and/or sloughing of these materials likely will occur 
during the drilling operations.  To reduce the potential for caving-in of the drilled 
holes, temporary casing of the drilled holes may be required during the drilled shaft 
installation. 
Weathered coralline sandstone was encountered in the boring drilled on the access 
road to the substation.  Localized hard layers of coralline sandstone may be 
present.  Therefore, difficult drilling conditions may be encountered at the site and 
should be expected.  The drilled shaft contractor will need to have the appropriate 
equipment and tools to drill through the hard layers of coralline sandstone, where 
encountered. 
Since groundwater will likely be encountered in the drilled holes along 
Malaekahana Valley Road, placement of concrete by tremie methods is 
recommended.  During placement of concrete, a minimum of 5 feet of concrete 
head should be maintained above the bottom of the tremie pipe.  A low-shrink, 
high-slump (7 to 9-inch slump range) concrete mix should be used to provide close 
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils.  The concrete should 
be placed promptly after drilling to reduce the potential for softening and caving-in 
of the sides of the drilled holes.  A Geolabs representative should be present to 
observe the installation of drilled shafts during construction to confirm the subsoil 
conditions and embedment depths.

Underground Utility Lines 
We envision new underground utilities are planned. Based on the subsurface 
conditions encountered at the project site, we anticipate the underground utility trenches 
will be excavated in the residual/saprolitic soils, weathered basalt rock, and coralline

excavated soils from the trench excavation should be stockpiled no closer than a

comply with all applicable local, state, and federal safety requirements. 
In general, we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) under the pipes to support the utility 
lines. The initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes should consist of 
free-draining backfills, such as open-graded gravel, to reduce the potential for pipe 
damage from compaction of the backfill. It is critical to use a free draining granular 
material as the initial trench backfill to reduce the potential for formation of voids below 
the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Use 
of clayey soils as initial trench backfill directly around utility pipes is not recommended. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes 
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on-site soils having 
particles less than 6 inches in largest dimension or structural fill material. The backfill 
should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in about 8-inch 
loose lifts, and mechanically compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction. Where trenches are located in the roadways, the upper 3 feet of the trench 
backfill below the finished grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in 
accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by 
dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. Mechanical compaction 
equipment should be used to compact the backfill materials. Compaction efforts by 
water tamping, jetting, or ponding should not be allowed.

3.12 Drainage 
The finished grades outside the structures should be sloped to shed water away 
from foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding. Excessive 
landscape watering near the foundations and slabs also should be avoided. 
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above 
foundation recommendations because ponded water could cause subsurface soil 
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength. 
The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the walls or 
foundation/slab edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the potential 
for appreciable water infiltration into the subsurface. In addition, drainage swales should 
be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface runoff away 
from foundations and slabs.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling 
twenty-two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 109, 101A through 103A, 
105A, 201 through 205, and 301 through 304. The borings extended to depths of about 
27 to 91.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are 
shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 and 3.2. The borings were drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools. 
Our geologists classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.   Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-22. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample.  Results of the pocket penetrometer test results 
are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core 
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description.  Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3.  The Rock Description System is based on the

publication “Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in

Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered 
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the total core run in rock that 
is sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting 
drilling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 
5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs 
of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality 
of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and 
Minerals."

Seismic Shear Wave Velocity Tests 
Seismic shear wave velocity profiling using seismic cone penetration testing 
(SCPT) equipment was performed in an effort to more closely analyze the seismic 
design considerations for the project site. Shear wave velocity testing using seismic 
cone penetration testing (SCPT) equipment was performed in Boring Nos. 101A, 103, 
107, and 109.  The approximate locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 and 
3.2.  
After drilling and sampling at these test boring locations, the boring was 
backfilled with 0.25-inch coated bentonite pellets. Water was placed into the hole to 
allow the pellets to swell, soften, and conform to the borehole sidewalls. The bentonite 
acted as a coupling medium from the seismic cone penetrometer with the surrounding 
soil. The test was performed to depths of about 46.6 to 52 feet below the existing 
ground surface using seismic cone penetration testing (SCPT) equipment. 
The seismic shear wave velocity test consists of hydraulically pushing a 10-ton 
steel electronic subtraction cone with an apex angle of 60 degrees and an area of 
1.55 square inches (10 square centimeters) into the softened bentonite backfill. The 
cone carries a uniaxial horizontal geophone to detect the arrival of a shear wave 
propagated from the ground surface. The seismic measurements are made when the 
SCPT has stopped and a shear wave is sent into the subsurface. A shear wave is 
generated at the surface by striking a loaded plank with a switched hammer. The 
propagation time of the wave from the hammer blow to the cone was measured at each 
depth interval. The vector difference of these depths divided by the time difference 
provided the average shear wave velocity over the depth interval. The shear wave 
velocity profile measured at the probe location is presented on Plates B-1 through B-4.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation 
of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the 
appropriate sample depths. 
Two one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples of the near-surface clayey soils to evaluate the swelling potential of 
the subsurface soils. The test results are summarized on Plate C-1. 
Fifteen Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are presented on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of the test 
results is provided on Plates C-2 and C-3. 
Eighteen Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136), including three Hydrometer 
tests [ASTM D422-63(2007)e2], were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate 
the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification.  Graphic 
presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on Plates C-4 through C-7. 
Three Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the shear strength parameters. The test results are presented on 
Plates C-8 through C-10. 
Eight Unconfined Compression tests (ASTM D2166) were performed on selected 
soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strength characteristics. The test results 
along with the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates C-11 through C-18. 
One consolidation test (ASTM D2435) was performed on a selected soil sample 
to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the materials encountered. Results of 
the consolidation test are presented on Plate C-19. 
Four Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships. The test results are presented on Plates C-20 through C-23.  
Five laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) tests (ASTM D1883) were 
performed on the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils. The test results are presented on Plates C-24 through C-28.
