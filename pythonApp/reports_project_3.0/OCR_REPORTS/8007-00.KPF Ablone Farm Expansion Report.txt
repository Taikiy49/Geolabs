SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered dense basalt formation interbedded with some 
a’a clinker. In general, the basalt formation is hard to very hard and relatively unweather 
to slightly weathered. A’a clinker generally consists of loose to medium dense gravel and 
cobbles. It should be noted that numerous voids and blisters were observed at the ground 
surface.  In addition, many voids of up to about 15 inches in height were encountered in 
our borings. In general, cavities and/or voids are commonly present in the basaltic lava 
flows of the vicinity. Therefore, the presence of cavities and/or voids should be expected 
during construction.  We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our 
field exploration. 
Due to the presence of numerous cavities and/or voids in the project vicinity, we 
recommend giving special attention to the site preparation recommendations prior to the 
commencement of fill placement and/or construction of improvements at the project site. 
In general, we recommend ripping subgrades of fill areas to a depth of about 3 feet 
below the existing ground surface to detect and collapse near-surface cavities and/or 
voids. As a minimum, ripping of the subgrades should extend at least 3 feet laterally 
beyond the limits of fill areas. After the fill surfaces have been ripped to a depth of about 
3 feet below the ground surface, the ground should be proof-rolled with a Caterpillar D-10 
bulldozer or similar size bulldozer with a minimum of four passes to provide a relatively 
level surface. 
After leveling of the rugged surfaces with a bulldozer, we recommend proof-rolling 
the subgrades with a large vibratory drum roller (minimum 20 tons static weight) for a 
minimum of eight passes to help detect and collapse near-surface cavities and/or voids. 
We also recommend proof-rolling the cut subgrades with a minimum 20-ton (static weight) 
vibratory drum roller for a minimum of eight passes to help detect and collapse 
near-surface cavities and/or voids. Yielding areas, loose areas, or cavities disclosed 
during clearing and proof-rolling operations should be over-excavated and backfilled with 
compacted fill materials. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend using 
the shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings to support the new structures planned. 
As an alternative, foundations for support of the structural loads of the new structures 
planned also may consist of thickened-edge slab footings. In general, the new structures 
should be constructed on similar foundation subgrade materials to reduce the potential 
for differential settlement and to provide uniform support across the structures. To provide

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the KPF Hawaii Expansion project at the National Energy Laboratory of 
Hawaii Authority (NELHA) in the District of North Kona on the Island of Hawaii. The project 
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. This report 
summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing and presents 
our geotechnical recommendations for the design of the proposed project. 
The recommendations provided herein are intended for the design of site grading, 
foundations, slabs-on-grade, underground utility lines, and pavements only. The findings 
and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of 
this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The KPF Hawaii Expansion project is located off Makako Bay Drive within NELHA 
on the Island of Hawaii.  Based on the information provided, it is desired to expand the 
existing Abalone Farm operation facility. Details of the expansion project were not 
available at the time this report was prepared.  In general, we envision that the expansion 
includes the construction of several buildings, parking lots for vehicles and tour buses, 
loading zones, and new underground utility lines.  As part of the project, we envision that 
a stormwater disposal system, such as drywells and/or seepage pits, may be required.  
In addition, individual wastewater systems (IWS) with leach fields may be required for the 
new expansion. 
We envision that site grading consisting of cuts and fills of up to about 2 to 3 feet 
in height may be required to achieve the design grades.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers 
an area of approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of the 
following five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea (activity during the 
recent geologic time), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 – 1803), and Mauna Loa and Kilauea 
(both still active). 
The project site is situated on the western flank of Hualalai Mountain, which makes 
up the west-central portion of the island. The lava formation encountered at the site 
appears to be of both a'a and pahoehoe type flows, which spread and ponded as they 
approached the ocean. A’a lava is typically characterized by a porous, rough, and 
irregular flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments, including 
cobbles and boulders. Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth, rope-like, or billowy 
surface and an internal structure of the vesicular (porous) rock. 
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas, especially pahoehoe type 
lava. Cavities form when lavas are still in a molten state, and they represent both lava 
tubes (intra-flow cavities) and blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities). Lava tubes form 
when molten lava drains from the cooling flow, leaving a hollow tube-like structure, which 
may extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow. Blisters and pockets generally 
are smaller in horizontal extent. 
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite 
readily in both horizontal and vertical directions. In general, water is transmitted through 
the porous rock matrix, along joints, fractures and inter-flow contacts, cavities, and along 
clinker layers. Clinker and cavities serve as the primary water transmission features 
because of their high permeability.

Earthquakes and Seismicity 
Generally, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the 
tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic 
activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground movement

experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so small that they can 
only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of the earthquakes are strong 
enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of magma

to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes 
with increased distance from the active volcanoes located in the southern portion of the 
Island of Hawaii.

3.1.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and 
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site. 
Loose and/or soft soils, where encountered, should be over-excavated to expose 
firm and/or dense materials, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
well-compacted fills. The contractor should exercise caution while clearing and 
grading near collapsed lava tube features and/or cavities, which may represent 
potential lava tubes or cavities that may further collapse under a load of heavy 
construction equipment. 
After clearing and grubbing, areas to be filled should be ripped using a tooth of a 
large bulldozer to a depth of about 3 feet below the existing ground surface at the 
commencement of grading. The purpose of ripping the subgrades is to assist in 
detecting and collapsing cavities and/or voids contained in the near-surface 
materials encountered at the project site. 
After the fill surfaces have been ripped to a depth of about 3 feet below the surface, 
the ground should be leveled with the use of a Caterpillar D-10 bulldozer or similar 
size bulldozer working the surface a minimum of four passes to provide a relatively 
level platform. After leveling the rugged surfaces with a bulldozer, we recommend 
proof-rolling the fill subgrades with a large vibratory drum roller (minimum 20 tons 
static weight) for a minimum of eight passes to help detect and collapse near-
surface cavities and/or voids. The vibratory drum roller should be operated at a 
speed of about 300 feet per minute (3½ miles per hour). 
Finished subgrades in cut areas should be proof-rolled with a minimum 20-ton 
(static weight) vibratory drum roller for a minimum of eight passes. The vibratory 
drum roller also should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute. The 
requirement for ripping the ground surface by a depth of about 3 feet (in fill areas) 
may be omitted for cut subgrades. In addition, proof-rolling of the subgrade with a 
Caterpillar D-10 bulldozer for a minimum of four passes may be omitted in the cut

3.1.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, fill materials required to raise the grades at the site should consist of 
structural fill materials. Structural fill materials should consist of well-graded 
granular materials generally less than 6 inches in maximum dimension with 
sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass. In 
addition, structural fill materials should contain less than 30 percent particles 
passing the No. 200 sieve by weight. In general, the excavated on-site materials 
may be reused as a source of structural fill materials, provided that they are 
processed to meet the maximum particle size and gradation requirements 
presented above. 
Where over-cutting of the finished cut subgrades occurs during the grading 
operations, backfilling of the over-cut area should be treated as fill areas. 
Therefore, the above fill material recommendations should apply to the over-cut 
areas, and the fill materials required for backfilling will depend on the depth of 
over-cut below the finished subgrade.

3.1.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in 
loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted adequately, meeting the criterion established by a test program. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practical in fills composed of 
rocks and/or cobbles. Instead, a testing program should be conducted at the start 
of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a Geolabs 
representative to evaluate the number of passes needed to achieve the desired 
level of compaction. Based on this testing program, the number of passes may 
then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction. 
For preliminary estimating purposes, compaction may be accomplished by using 
a large vibratory drum roller having a static weight of at least 20 tons and traveling

at a speed of about 300 feet per minute (about 3½ miles per hour). The roller 
should pass over the fill surface at least eight times for structural fill and six times

Aggregate base and subbase courses required for the project should be placed in

tamping, jetting, or ponding should not be allowed to compact the fills. 
3.1.4 Excavation 
Based on our field exploration, the project site generally is underlain by basalt 
formation at the ground surface. It is anticipated that the fractured basalt formation 
may be excavated readily with normal heavy excavation equipment, such as 
excavators, and ripped with large bulldozers. However, some of the excavations 
in the dense basalt formation will likely require the use of hoerams or chipping. 
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the subsurface materials are 
based on our visual observation of the field data from the borings. Contractors 
should be encouraged to examine the site conditions and the subsurface data to 
make their own reasonable and prudent interpretation. 
3.1.5 Cut and Fill Slopes 
In general, we believe the cut slopes planned at the site exposing the basalt 
formation may be designed with a slope inclination of one horizontal to one vertical 
(1H:1V) or flatter. In areas where clinker layers or highly fractured basalt are 
exposed at the cut slope faces, the cut slopes may need to be flattened to 
inclinations of 2H:1V or flatter. Therefore, provisions to flatten the cut slopes and/or 
constructing retaining walls should be considered in the project design where 
1H:1V cut slopes are planned.

Permanent fill slopes constructed of the structural fill materials recommended

3.2 
Foundations 
New buildings are planned for the expansion project at the site. In general, the new 
structures should be constructed on similar foundation subgrade materials to reduce the 
potential for differential settlement and to provide uniform support across the structures.  
To provide similar foundation subgrade materials for the new structures, we recommend 
over-excavating the subgrades within the new building pads by at least 2 feet. The 
over-excavation should be backfilled with compacted structural fill. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site and anticipated 
conditions after additional site grading, we recommend using the shallow spread and/or 
continuous strip footings to support the new structures planned. As an alternative, 
foundations for support of the structural loads of the new structures planned also may 
consist of thickened-edge slab footings.  An allowable bearing pressure of up to 
4,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the design of footings bearing on a 
minimum of 2 feet of compacted structural fill. This bearing value is for supporting 
dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as 
those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
In general, the bottom of the footings should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches 
below the lowest adjacent grade. Footings constructed near the tops of slopes or on 
slopes should be embedded deep enough such that the horizontal distance measured

from the outside edge (at the base of the footing) to the face of the slope is no less than 
6 feet. 
In addition, foundations next to other foundations, utility trenches, easements or 
other retaining walls should be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending 
upward from the bottom edge of the structure or utility trench, or the footings should be 
extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the structure foundation. This requirement 
is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with additional 
structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement. 
Where soft or loose ground conditions are encountered at the footing subgrades, 
the footing subgrades should be over-excavated until firm materials are encountered and 
backfilled with select borrow subbase. Select borrow subbase materials should be 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a 
minimum 90 percent relative compaction. As an alternative, the footings may be extended 
down to bear directly on the firm materials. 
If the foundations are designed and constructed in strict accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate the total settlements of the foundations to be less than 
1 inch. Differential settlements between adjacent footings supported on similar materials 
may be on the order of about 0.5 inches or less. 
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction developed 
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing material and by passive earth 
pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A coefficient of 
friction of 0.45 may be used for footings bearing on the compacted structural fill. 
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid 
pressure of 450 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf), assuming the soils around 
the footings are well-compacted. The passive pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches 
of the soil should be neglected unless covered by pavements or slabs. 
A Geolabs representative should observe the footing excavations prior to the 
placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation bearing conditions 
and the required embedment depths.

3.3 
Probing and Grouting 
As mentioned above, cavities and/or voids are commonly present in the basaltic 
lava flows at the project site. In addition, numerous small voids were encountered in our 
borings.  To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from the collapse 
of cavities below foundations, we recommend implementing a program of probing and 
grouting for the new foundations during construction. 
We recommend drilling probe holes at 10-foot on centers for the continuous strip 
footings. In addition, probe holes should be drilled at each isolated spread footing (or 
column) location (one probe hole per 50 square feet of footing area). The probe holes 
should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to a depth of at least 10 feet 
below the planned bottom of the foundation.  
If cavities and/or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operation, 
additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacing to help delineate the vertical 
and lateral extent of the cavity and/or void. The probe holes and cavities discovered 
should be backfilled with Controlled Low Strength Material (CLSM) with a slump of about 
6 to 9 inches. The CLSM should be injected at low to moderate pressures. As an 
alternative to CLSM, sand-cement grout with a slump of about 6 to 9 inches also may be 
used for the grouting operations. 
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and/or voids at the site, we 
recommend obtaining unit prices for additional probing and grouting during bidding. In 
addition, the probe drill should be available on-site until the probing and grouting 
operations are completed. The contractor also should be made aware that longer lag time 
between probing/grouting operations and foundation construction might be required in the 
construction schedule. 
The probing and grouting program, where implemented, should be conducted 
under the observation of a Geolabs representative. This would allow our firm to monitor 
the presence of cavities and to allow additional recommendations to be made if excess 
grout take and/or changed conditions are observed.

3.4 
Slabs-On-Grade 
We anticipate concrete slabs-on-grade will be used for the ground floors of the 
new structures. For the interior slabs (not subjected to vehicular traffic), such as the new 
terminal building slabs, we recommend providing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion 
fill below the slabs for uniform support. The cushion fill should consist of open-graded 
gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) to serve as a capillary moisture break. 
To reduce the potential for excess moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to 
floor coverings, an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the gravel 
cushion fill layer. Where provided, flexible floor coverings should be considered above 
the floor slab because they can better mask minor slab cracking. 
Where slabs are subjected to vehicular traffic or sustained vibrations, such as the 
hangar slabs, a minimum of 6 inches of compacted select borrow subbase materials 
(3-inch minus granular materials) should be provided below the slabs in lieu of the 4-inch 
thick cushion fill layer. The select borrow subbase material layer should be 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. The impervious moisture barrier may be 
omitted for these slabs. 
In addition, exterior concrete walkways and exterior flatwork will likely be required. 
Generally, we recommend providing a minimum 4-inch thick cushion fill layer of 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) or select borrow subbase below 
exterior concrete slabs that will not be subjected to vehicular traffic. Select borrow 
subbase, where utilized, should be compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. 
To reduce the potential for substantial shrinkage cracks in the concrete slabs, 
crack control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways (or 
slabs) with expansion joints at right-angle intersections.

the compacted fill and/or dense basalt rock formation encountered in our borings.

In general, granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of open-graded gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) should be provided below the pipes for uniform bearing 
support. Free-draining granular materials, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, 
No. 67 gradation), should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above 
the top of the pipes. It is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential 
for the formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support 
for the sides of the pipes. Improper backfill material around the pipes and improper 
placement of the backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
As an alternative, the granular bedding and the initial trench backfill (up to about 
12 inches above the top of the pipes) may consist of 1-inch minus, well-graded granular 
materials (aggregate base materials). Where the 1-inch minus, well-graded granular 
materials are used, the granular bedding and the initial trench backfill should be 
moisture-conditioned and compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of well-graded granular materials 
less than 6 inches in maximum particle size. The backfill should be moisture-conditioned 
to above the optimum moisture, placed in about 8-inch level loose lifts, and mechanically 
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Where trenches are located in 
paved areas, the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be 
compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction. 
Because basaltic rock formation was encountered at the ground surface during our 
field exploration, it appears that most of the trench work for utility installation likely will 
involve excavation in the basaltic rock formation and will require hard ripping or the use 
of hoerams. The contractor must exercise care to avoid over-ripping, which would disrupt 
the structure of the rock formation and result in a potential loss of bearing strength for 
footings in its vicinity.

3.7 
Permeability Testing 
Two constant head permeability tests were conducted in selected drilled borings 
to provide data for the design of the stormwater disposal system planned at the project 
site. The permeability tests were performed within the basaltic rock formation at depths 
of about 5 and 5.5 feet below the ground surface.  The permeability test results are 
presented in Appendix C. 
Based on our field permeability tests, the subsurface materials generally have a 
coefficient of permeability (k) of over 0.4 feet per minute at the locations tested. Based on 
the field permeability test results, we believe an average hydraulic conductivity of about 
0.4 feet per minute may be assumed for the subsurface materials encountered at depths 
of about 5 and 5.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The hydraulic conductivity value 
roughly corresponds to an infiltration rate of about 4,300 gallons per day per square foot 
of surface area (0.0067 cubic feet per second per square foot) at those locations. 
It should be emphasized that the hydraulic conductivity of the on-site basaltic rock 
formation is highly variable due to the presence or absence of significant voids/fractures

capacity of the retention basin. In general, the greater the number of clinker seams and/or

3.8 
Percolation Testing 
Two percolation tests were performed in selected borings in general accordance 
with the procedures outlined by DOH for the design of IWS. 
The percolation tests were performed in our borings at a depth of about 5 feet from 
the existing ground surface. Approximately 1 inch of gravel was placed at the bottom of 
the boring to protect the bottom from scouring and sediments. 
During testing, the test holes were filled with water to a depth of about 6 inches 
above the gravel layer and refilled as necessary. The time intervals and water drops were 
then recorded to provide data upon which the calculations of the percolation rates were 
based. 
Based on the percolation tests conducted, the percolation rates at locations tested 
are less than about 1.8 minutes per inch at the test elevations. In accordance with the 
"Manual of Septic-Tank Practice," prepared by the U.S. Department of Health, Education, 
and Welfare, the leach field locations with the percolation rates obtained at the site would 
be suitable for siting of the absorption system at these locations. The percolation test 
results are presented in Appendix D.

foundations and slabs also should be avoided. Planters next to foundations should be 
avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for excessive water 
infiltration into the subsurface, especially subsurface conditions consisting of fill materials.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling 11 borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 11, extending to depths of about 5 to 17 feet below the 
existing ground surface. The borings were drilled with a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers and coring tools. The approximate boring locations are shown on 
the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory.  Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1.  Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.  Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-11. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained by 
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, Diamond 
Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow 
bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit is 
mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were described in 
general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3.  The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods 
for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International 
Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from 
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of material is 
recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown 
on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative quality 
of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is sound 
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any drilling 
induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core 
run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as 
RQD = 50%.  Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock 
based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals."

Permeability Tests 
As part of our field exploration program, we conducted two permeability tests in 
selected borings using the constant head test method. In general, the borings had a 
diameter of about 4 inches and extended to depths of about 5 and 5.5 feet below the ground 
level at the time of the tests. 
The constant head tests were performed by pumping water into the boreholes until 
the water surface stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a sufficient period of time. The 
water height and the rate of pumping were then measured to provide data upon which to 
base calculation of the subsoil permeability. Results of the permeability tests conducted in 
selected borings are presented on Plates C-1 and C-2.
