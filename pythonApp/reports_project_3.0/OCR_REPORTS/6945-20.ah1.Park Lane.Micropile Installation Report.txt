PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
In general, the Park Lane Ala Moana project involves expansion of the makai 
(southern) portion of the shopping center by demolishing the existing parking deck in this 
area and constructing a three-level parking structure below multi-level residential 
condominium structures. The site is generally bounded by Neiman Marcus to the east, 
Ala Moana Boulevard to the south, Piikoi Street to the west, and the Bloomingdale’s to the 
north. The general project vicinity is shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions and structural loads, a deep 
foundation system consisting of augered cast-in-place (ACIP) piles was recommended to 
reduce the potential for appreciable total and differential settlements from the structural 
loads. Micropiles were installed to supplement the constructed ACIP piles. 
The scope of our work discussed in this letter report includes observation of the 
micropile foundation construction for the Park Lane Ala Moana (Commercial Parking) 
project only. Albert C. Kobayashi, Inc. (ACK) was the general contractor for the project and 
the micropile subcontractor was Kelikai, Inc. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
The subsurface conditions at the project site consist of surface fill materials 
overlying soft and/or loose lagoonal deposits extending to depths of about 32 to 33 feet 
below the ground surface. Below the highly compressible lagoonal deposits, coral ledges 
of variable thickness and hardness were generally encountered across the project site. 
The lagoonal deposits and/or coral ledges were underlain by medium dense coralline 
detritus materials with localized loose and dense zones. 
We encountered groundwater at an elevation of about +0 feet Mean Sea Level 
(MSL) at the time of the micropile installation. Due to the proximity of the project site to the 
Pacific Ocean, water levels are expected to vary with tidal fluctuations. It should be noted 
that water levels also may vary with seasonal precipitation, storm surge conditions, 
groundwater withdrawal, and other factors.

Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions at the site, the micropiles were 
specified to extend through the loose lagoonal deposits and derive load bearing support 
from the coralline materials encountered at greater depths. To achieve the allowable 
compressive load capacity of 150 kips with a factor of safety of 2, the micropiles were 
specified with a minimum grout bulb diameter of 7 inches and to extend a minimum of 
about 35 feet into the hard coral ledge and/or dense coralline materials.  To reduce the risk 
of micropile “necking” within the loose/soft lagoonal material, permanent steel casing was 
specified to be left in-place at the top of each micropile.  
Production Micropiles 
Kelikai, Inc. installed a total of six production micropiles from October 7, 2014 
through April 26, 2015 at the approximate locations shown on the Micropile Location 
Plans, Plates 2.1 and 2.2.  Details of the micropile installation are presented in the 
Micropile Installation Records in Appendix A and summarized on Plate 3. 
In general, the micropiles were installed using either a Beretta TD 75 or 
Casagrande C6 drilling rig equipped with a bull rock under reamer advancing a 7-inch 
O.D. (Outside Diameter) steel casing to the specified depth of the micropile to effectively 
create a drilled hole of at least 7 inches. Advancing steel casing to the bottom of the 
specified depth was required in order to facilitate the micropile drilling and ensure the 
quality of the grouting. Air pressure up to 100 pounds per square inch (psi) was utilized 
during the drilling operation to create a bulb around the drilling bit (7-inch diameter) for 
advancing the temporary steel casing (7-inch O.D.). 
After the drilled holes were extended to the specified depth and cleaned out, a 
reinforcing bar (Grade 150 all threaded #18 rebar) was placed in the cased hole with 
centralizers. Grout was batched on site utilizing a colloidal high-shear and high-speed 
ChemGrout CG-620 mixer equipped with a grout pump. The grout mix consisted of 
one sack of 94-pound Portland Cement Type I and/or II, 87.4 pounds of masonry sand 
and 5 gallons of water with a design 28-day compressive strength of 4,000 psi. 
The grout was placed in the cased hole through a 1-inch diameter PVC tremie pipe 
starting from the bottom of the cased hole. After sound grout appeared at the top of the 
cased hole, the temporary steel casing was removed one section (5 feet long) at a time 
until the bottom of the steel casing approached the tip of the permanent casing elevation 
or the specified top of the grout bulb zone. Positive grout in-take was maintained during 
the grouting operation. No significant micropile “necking” was indicated based on review of 
the micropile grouting records.

and MP-3). In general, the micropiles were loaded to a maximum test load of about 
225 kips (150 percent of the design load) in increments equivalent to approximately
