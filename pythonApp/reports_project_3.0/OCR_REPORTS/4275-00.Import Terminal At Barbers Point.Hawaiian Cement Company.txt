REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the
western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern
two thirds of the island As volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from Koolau
banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as the
Schofield Plateau or Central Oahu
The forces of weathering and erosion attacked this plateau generating sediments
which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and further south these
alluvial sediments accumulated and interbedded with marine sediments and coral algal reef
formations to form a sedimentary wedge ranging in thickness from zero feet in the area of
the Interstate Route H 1 Highway to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the
Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal
aquifers of Southern Oahu Deposition of these sediments has continued from earlier
geologic time through the present
GEOLABS INC
W O 4275 00 Page 6
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill the
oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea levels
rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of accumulated
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposited lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet
waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys
in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials Land development and reclamation projects
within the last century have brought the project site to its present form

Due to the potential for presence of cobbles and boulders and loose zones below

compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by
dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density

Foundation Probing
Cavities and or voids are commonly encountered within the coral formations in Hawaii
In addition the coral formation typically encountered in the Barbers Point area is generally
susceptible to the presence of cavities and or voids To reduce the potential for loss of
foundation support resulting from collapse of cavities and or voids below the foundations we
recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting be conducted below the
foundations of the structures
In general we recommend that probe holes be drilled at each isolated spread footing
and along continuous footings at about 10 feet on center for the proposed dome structures
Additional probe holes should be drilled and spaced at 10 feet on center starting at the
center of the dome structure and radiating out to the perimeter in about 10 foot increments
in radius The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to
about 15 feet below the finished ground elevation or at least 10 feet below the bottom of
foundation elevation Probe holes do not appear to be necessary for the tunnel structure due
to the loose coralline materials anticipated below the proposed tunnel structure
If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operation
additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacing to help delineate the vertical and
lateral extent of the cavity and or void Following the drilling of each probe hole a 2 inch
PVC pipe should be inserted into the probe holes to preserve the integrity of the open hole
for grouting A field engineer or geologist from our office should be present to log the probe
holes and to determine the locations of additional probe holes needed if cavities and or voids
are encountered
The probe holes cavities and or voids discovered should be backfilled with a fluid
sand cement grout We recommend that a low strength sand cement grout mixture with an
approximate 6 to 9 inch slump range pumped at low to moderate pressures be utilized for
the grouting operation In lieu of the sand cement grout a highly fluid lean concrete such
as controlled low strength material flowable fill may also be used for the grouting operation

and should be conducted under the observation of a representative from Geolabs This
would allow our firm to monitor the presence of cavities and or voids and to allow additional
recommendations to be made if excess grout take and or changed conditions are observed
Slabs On Grade
Based on our field exploration we anticipate that new concrete slabs on grade for the
proposed import terminal will likely bear on coralline materials therefore we believe that
conventional slabs on grade design may be utilized for the other on grade structures
However if loose soils are encountered at the subgrade level these soils should be
over excavated and replaced with select granular fill materials compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction
As previously mentioned a 2 foot thick layer of compacted fill material should be
provided below the bottom of footings and slab subgrades of the dome structures and
underground tunnel for more uniform support The over excavation for the compacted fill
should extend beyond the edges of the footings and slabs a minimum of 2 feet Prior to
placement of the compacted fill the bottom of the footing and slab over excavations should
be proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton vibratory drum roller a minimum of six passes to detect
the presence of near surface cavities or loose zones If soft and or loose soils are
encountered at the over excavated subgrade levels deeper over excavation may be required
to expose the underlying firm material
The 2 foot thick compacted fill layer would not be required below the slabs of the other
structures such as the electric substation and truck scales However we recommend that
the slabs for the other structures be supported on at least 6 inches of aggregate subbase
The aggregate subbase materials should consist of crushed basalt aggregate and should
conform to Section 703 17 of the Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road Bridge and
Public Works Construction 1994 In addition the aggregate subbase material should be
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction

Retaining Structures

Retaining Structure Foundations

Lateral Earth Pressures

Pavement Joints
Due to the rigidity of the Portland Cement Concrete pavements significant stresses
may develop in the concrete from variations in temperature and moisture content In order
to relieve the high level of stress and to reduce the potential for cracking adequate joints
should be provided in the Portland cement concrete pavement The following describes the
various types of joints and general guidelines that should be implemented in the design of
concrete pavements
Crack Control Joints Contraction Joints
In general decreases in temperature or moisture content such as that during
concrete curing will cause the concrete to contract and possibly crack Crack control
joints are used to provide controlled cracking due to volume changes and relieve the
stresses caused by curling or warping Crack control joints such as saw cut joints or
formed grooves are provided to a depth of generally one fourth 4 the slab
thickness These saw cut joints or formed grooves crack control joints provide a
GEOLABS INC
W O 4275 00 Page 23
weakened plane which will likely crack through the full depth as the concrete shrinks
during the curing process
In general the spacing of crack control joints should be limited to a maximum of
20 feet Smaller joint spacing should be used if the concrete pavement will be
subjected to extreme temperature gradients during the placement and or life span of
the pavement The crack control joints should be provided in both longitudinal and
transverse directions The transverse joint spacing should not vary from the
longitudinal joint spacing by more than 25 percent
Expansion Joints Isolation Joints
Expansion joints are typically used to relieve compressive stresses developed at
critical locations due to fluctuations in temperature Expansion joints are cut to the full
depth of the concrete slab and compressible filler materials are placed in the cut
These joints allow for isolated lateral movement of the slabs and reduce the potential
for damage to the adjoining concrete spalling of the edges of the concrete slabs
Expansion joints should be used at pavement intersections and at intersections of
pavements with fixed structures Because expansion joints generally do not provide
for load transfer across the joint placement of dowels with lubrication on one end of
the dowel should be considered for load transfer across the expansion joint where
required
Construction Joints
Construction joints are generally required when two abutting slabs are placed at
different times Therefore construction joints usually form the edges of each day s
work For the relatively thick concrete slabs 8 inches planned for this project the
construction joints should consist of a keyed or butted joint In general construction
joints should be aligned with the crack control or expansion joints to reduce the
number of pavement joints required

APPENDIX B

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Eight sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 ASTM D 1140 were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on Plates B 1
and B 2
Two direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested Results of the direct
shear tests are presented on Plates B 3 and B 4
Three consolidation tests with time rates ASTM D 2435 were performed on samples
of the potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the
materials encountered Results of the consolidation tests are presented on Plates B 5
through B 7
Four laboratory Califomia Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on the
near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils The test
results are presented on Plates B 8 through B 11
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on the typical
near surface soils encountered at the site to evaluate the moisture content and dry density
relationship The test results are presented on Plates B 12 and B 13

APPENDIX C
Field Permeability Testing
The permeability of the in situ soils were evaluated by performing one falling head and
one constant head test at two selected locations designated as P 1 and P 2 The tests were
performed at a depth of about 8 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate
locations of the field permeability tests are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
During the falling head test the borehole was carefully filled with water to the top of
the borehole The time intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon
which to base the calculation of the permeability values of the subsoils
The constant head test was performed by pumping water into the borehole until the
water surface stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a sufficient period of time The water
height and the rate of pumping were then measured to provide data upon which to base the
calculation of the subsoil permeability The test results of the field permeability tests are
presented on Plates C 1 and C 2
