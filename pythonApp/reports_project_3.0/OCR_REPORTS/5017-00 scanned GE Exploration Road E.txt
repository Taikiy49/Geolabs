In accordance with our fee proposal dated we have performed
a geotechnical engineering exploration for the Road E project in the Kapolei area on
the Island of Oahu Hawaii This report summarizes the findings from our field
exploration and laboratory testing and presents our findings and recommendations for
the design of the project The findings and recommendations are intended for design
input only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
Based on the information provided it is desired to construct a new roadway off
Kamokila Boulevard across the Kapolei Entertainment Center in the Kapolei area on the
Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1
The new roadway designated as Road E will measure approximately 40 feet
wide and 430 feet long as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 Based on our field
observations and the plan and profile drawing provided we anticipate that fills of up to
about 5 feet thick will be required to achieve the finished grades for the project
construction In addition new waterline and drain line connections are planned as part of
the roadway construction
Based on our experience in the area we anticipate that the project site may be
underlain by surface fills overlying clayey alluvial soil deposits and coralline materials at
greater depths

was utilized to develop a soil data set for the formulation of geotechnical engineering
recommendations pertaining to the design of pavements and site preparation only To
accomplish this we conducted an exploration program consisting of the following tasks
and work efforts

FIELD EXPLORATION
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling two borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths of about 26 5 feet below the
existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers A bulk sample designated as
Bulk 1 was obtained from the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics The approximate locations of the borings drilled and bulk sample
collected at the site are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field representative who monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on the Boring Log Legend Plate 3 Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings
Plates 4 and 5
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 3 inch outside diameter OD Modified California sampler with a
140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the
drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration
Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration
sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler
the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration
Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS

existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings the principal
geotechnical considerations for the design of the Road E project include proper site
preparation requirements to take into consideration the expansive nature of the
near surface soils and the relatively dry condition of the soils and the site Detailed
discussion of our geotechnical engineering recommendations for design of pavements and
site preparation are presented in the following sections of this report
Pavement Design
Based on our field exploration the proposed new roadway alignment is generally
underlain by very stiff to hard silty clays and clayey silts at the ground surface

Laboratory testing performed on the surface clayey soils indicated a CBR value of about
10 and a swell of 1 8 percent Based on the field exploration and laboratory test results
we recommend that the following new pavement section be used for preliminary design
and cost estimating purposes
Pavement Design
2 5 Inches Asphalt Concrete
6 0 Inches Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
6 0 Inches Aggregate Subbase 95 Percent Relative Compaction
14 5 Inches Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade
The subgrade soils below the pavement section should be scarified to a depth of
about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction The aggregate base course and aggregate
subbase should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates compacted to not less than
95 percent relative compaction CBR and field density tests should be performed on the
actual subgrade soils encountered during construction to confirm the adequacy of the
above section The recommended section assumes that good drainage will be provided
for the paved areas
Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due
to saturation of the base course subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the
pavement surface should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to
carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water
ponding should not be allowed anywhere on the site during or after construction
Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the
pavement areas we recommend that the curbs extend a minimum of 2 inches into the
subgrade soils below the base and subbase course layers to reduce the potential for
migration of excessive landscape water into the pavement section Alternatively a
subdrain system could be constructed to collect excess water from the landscaping
irrigation For long term performance we recommend that a subdrain system be
considered along the sides of the roadway
Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and the anticipated design finish grades for the
proposed roadway project we anticipate that site grading work consisting of up to about
5 feet of new fills will be required for the project construction In general the area within
the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed at the on set of
earthwork All vegetation debris deleterious material and other unsuitable materials
should be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas

encountered during clearing below areas designated to receive fill or future
improvements should be over excavated to expose firm and or stiff material and the
resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill The excavated soft
and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site
Existing structures and pavements that are to be demolished should be
completely removed The over excavations resulting from demolition should be
backfilled with compacted granular fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned
should be removed and the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with
granular fill material moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
placed in 8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction Utilities that are to be abandoned in place under the proposed structures
should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete under low pressure
Our field observations indicate that abundant shrinkage cracks and ground
depressions are present at the existing ground surface across the project site In
general shrinkage cracks will develop in the ground due to the lack of maintenance of
the soil moisture content as the surface soils start to dry out under the sun and dry
weather conditions The development of shrinkage cracks in the ground was apparently
intensified by the prolonged dry climatic conditions characteristic of the Kapolei area
resulting in large fluctuations of the soil moisture content
If the existing ground cracks are left unattended for an extended period of time
and are allowed to penetrate deeper below the surface the ground cracks could
adversely affect the performance of the future roadway The ground cracks could serve
as conduits for water to percolate into the pavement subgrade soils and cause loss of
strength of the subgrade soils resulting in potential settlement of the pavement
structure Therefore remedial work to correct or mitigate the observed ground cracks
should be implemented prior to fill placement and construction of the proposed new
roadway
After clearing and grubbing the exposed subgrades should be proof rolled with a
minimum 10 ton vibratory drum roller a minimum of six passes to assist in detecting and
collapsing near surface voids and loose zones created by the shrinkage cracks and
ground depressions The exposed subgrades should be proof rolled without
moisture conditioning of the subgrade soils at the pre existing moisture contents
Loose areas disclosed during the proof rolling operation should be backfilled with
general fill material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction The
proof rolling operations should be conducted under the near continuous observation of
a representative from Geolabs
Subsequent to the proof rolling operations the subgrades should then be
scarified to a minimum depth of 12 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil

Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend that the soils be scarified and re prepared as recommended above
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather
and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted fill Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit
prices for over excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the
over excavation quantities
The aggregate base course and aggregate subbase materials should meet the
requirements of Sections 30 and 31 of the City and County Standard Specifications
September 1986 The materials should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content placed in 8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Imported fill materials below the subbase course layer should consist of
non expansive select granular material such as crushed coral or basalt The material
should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in
largest dimension In addition the material should contain between 10 and 30 percent
particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value
of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 The materials should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in 8 inch level
loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Imported fill
materials should be tested by Geolabs for conformance with these recommendations
prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use
Site grading operations should be observed by a representative from Geolabs It
is important that a representative from our office observe the site preparation to
evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and
scarification process and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those
encountered in our field exploration
Utility Trenches
We anticipate that the utilities for the new roadway project will consist primarily of
water and drain lines In general good construction practices should be utilized for the
installation and backfilling of the trenches for the new utilities at the site The method and
equipment to be used for trench excavation should be determined by the contractor
subject to practical limits and safety considerations In addition the excavations should
comply with the applicable federal state and local safety requirements

Based on the results of our field exploration it appears that conventional
excavation methods may generally be utilized for utility line excavation at the site The
upper clayey soils encountered at the site are generally very stiff to hard However some
of the utility trench excavations will likely encounter the dense to very dense coralline
materials as described in the boring logs which may pose some excavation difficulties
during the trench excavation
In general we recommend that granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used for support under the pipes
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above
the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes It is critical to use this
free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of
pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill
could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on site soils with a maximum
particle size of 6 inches or select granular fill material The backfill should be placed in
maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent
relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground subsidence
Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below
the pavement finished grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction
