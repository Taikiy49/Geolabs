PERMEABILITY TESTING REPORT
PROPOSED KAILUA ELDERLY HOUSING
W O 2840 00 MARCH 10 1992
In accordance with your request we have conducted field permeability testing at
the above project site This report summarizes our initial pre excavation findings and
geotechnical engineering recommendations resulting from the field exploration laboratory
testing and engineering analyses These recommendations are intended to aid in the
preliminary selection and operation of the dewatering system for the project and may not
be suitable for other uses
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project consists of a four 4 story building with one level of basement The
basement excavation will extend about 10 feet below the existing ground surface about
elevation 0 feet Mean Sea Level except for the sump pit area which will extend to about
7 feet below mean sea level
Dewatering is required in order to construct the basement level and sump pit area
in the dry
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain subsurface information pertaining to
the permeability characteristics of the overlying sandy subsoils to aid in the planning of
the dewatering of the construction site Also of concern was the strength of the soils to
be excavated to determine the excavation support system that may be required
The work was done in accordance with our fee proposal dated

SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is located within the block bordered by Kuulei Road Oneawa
Uluniu and Aulike Streets in Kailua Oahu The project is situated in the center of the
block and is bordered on all sides by low rise commercial buildings
The site was used as a on grade parking lot and therefore was covered with
asphaltic concrete pavement The site is relatively level with ground elevations generally
ranging from 9 to 11 feet above Mean Sea Level
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Previous Exploration
A total of five 5 borings were drilled at the site by another geotechnical
engineering consultant in order to develop recommendations for the design of the
structure
The previous borings encountered relatively uniform subsurface conditions
consisting of about 19 to 24 feet of relatively clean fine sand over calcareous
rubblestone consisting of partially cemented coral fragments sand and silt to 35 to 36 5
feet the depths drilled
Groundwater was encountered at depths of 8 3 to 9 2 feet below the existing
ground surface
Current Exploration
The field work for our exploration included one 1 source well to pump water out
of and seven 7 observation wells to monitor the groundwater levels at various areas of
the site Four 4 of the observation wells were logged and sampled during the drilling
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2840 00 Page 4
to obtain additional subsurface information at the site One 1 additional boring was
drilled near the center of the site to a depth of 75 feet to attempt to determine the
thickness of the permeable soils as this would have a bearing on the amount of water
which may be pumped during dewatering
Our recent subsurface information generally confirms the previous boring data with
the following exceptions
1 The thickness of the overlying surface fine sand layer was thinner varying
from 14 to 18 feet as compared to the 20 to 24 feet previously
encountered
2 The coralline rubblestone layer was found to consist of loose coralline
gravel grading to a highly weathered coral formation The previous borings
found this layer to be generally dense
3 The deep boring near the middle of the site encountered relatively
impermeable alluvial clay soils below the highly weathered coral formation
at a depth of about 70 feet
Groundwater level was measured at depths of 8 6 to 9 6 feet below the ground
surface at the time of the drilling corresponding to an elevation of approximately 1 3 to

FIELD PERMEABILITY TESTS
Falling head permeability tests were also performed during the drilling of some of
the observation holes to evaluate the relative permeabilities of the fine sand and
underlying coralline gravel These tests indicated that the coralline gravel may be at least
twice or several times more permeable than the fine sand
Field pumping tests were performed to evaluate the overall permeability of the
subsoils within the anticipated zone of influence below the site The tests were performed
by pumping water out of the 12 inch diameter source well at a constant rate and
measuring the resulting drawdown in the observation wells around the site
The results of the permeability and pumping tests are summarized in Appendix C
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Dewatering of Basement Excavation
Both pump out permeability tests and falling head permeability tests were
performed at the site to evaluate the range of permeability that may be anticipated from
the subsoils which need to be dewatered Based on the available subsurface information
it appears that the excavation would be entirely within the fine sand deposit However
in order to obtain the necessary drawdown around the entire excavation the pumps
would need to be placed lower and probably have to extend into the underlying more
permeable coralline gravel layer
Our falling head tests indicated that the lower coralline gravel layer may have a
permeability of about 3 x which is about two times higher than the
permeability of the upper fine sand layer Therefore we believe the elevation of the
underlying coralline gravel layer is an important factor to determine the amount of water
that would be pumped Based on this it is advisable that the pumps be placed as high
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2840 00 Page 6
as practicable to minimize penetration into the more permeable underlying gravel layer
and thereby attempt to reduce the volume of water which would need to be pumped
The preliminary fieid pumping test results were used to estimate the number of
wells and pumps that may be required to dewater the excavation However due to the
Stratified subsurface conditions and limited depth of the dewatering versus the total
thickness of the aquifer or permeable layer a rigorous theoretical analysis could not be
made Therefore the test results were used to estimate the probable range of flow rates
and corresponding number of pumps that may be required
Based on our analysis of the dewatering requirements and subsurface conditions
at the site it is our current opinion that one of the feasible system applicable for the
proposed dewatering would consist of a network of gravel backfilled trenches connected
to perimeter shallow sump pumping pits or wells
Our initial analysis indicated that four 4 to seven 7 shallow sump pits or wells
placed around the excavation would be needed to dewater the site The estimated total
pumping rates would probably vary between 3200 to 4500 gallons per minute depending
of the successful proper installation of a currently proposed perimeter shoring system
using driven C channel sections The perimeter shoring system will be discussed in a
following section
Following discussions with the general contractor and dewatering subcontractor
it is our understanding that the deep sump pit area and elevator pad near the center of
the site would be constructed first This would require dewatering an area of about 20
to 25 feet square at each location up to an elevation of about 10 feet below mean sea
level After construction of the deep sump pit and elevator pad the remainder of the
basement level will be excavated and constructed
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2840 00 Page 7
Based on this anticipated construction schedule it appears that a prudent course
of action would be to install wells next to the deep sump pit and elevator pad areas and
monitor the actual drawdown around the site due to the dewatering of these areas
These preliminary data would help to refine the initial pump test data and determine the
most efficient locations for the additional wells around the subsequent basement
excavation At this time we recommend that provisions for minimum six 6 submersible
pumping wells around the excavation be made We also believe continuous pumping
using electric submersible pumps in lieu of trash pumps would be very useful and critical
to reduce surging action due to daily shutting and starting of pumps The daily surging
actions in our opinion would contribute to an increase of the subsoil permeability thereby
increasing the amount of dewatering Loss of soil fines could also contribute to ground
subsidence over a longer period of dewatering operation
Design Limitations
Due to the inherent heterogeneity of natural soils especially those of coral origin
it should be understood that the calculated in situ permeabilities represent the
percolation characteristics of the underlying subsoils only for the specific test
locations depths and conditions that prevailed at the time of our field permeability
testing
The dewatering recommendations presented in this report are based on the
calculated permeabilities derived from the test data of the field permeability testing
performed in small diameter boreholes for relatively short durations of time
Therefore the estimated dewatering requirement may vary significantly from the
actual dewatering required for the large basement excavation We recommend
that provisions be made to install additional pump wells inside the basement
excavation if necessary

Pumping Details
The lower coralline gravel layer is significantly more permeable than the upper fine
sand Therefore as much as feasible we believe it would be advantageous to place the
pumps as high as practicable and preferably within the upper fine sand to reduce the
quantity of water pumped In addition to the perimeter well or sump pumping pits an
additional network of dewatering trenches with filter gravel backfill should also be used
to connect to the individual sump pit locations
The pumps should be placed in wells that are adequately screened and gravel
packed The size of the wells would depend on the size of pumps which are used It
appears that a well design may consist of 30 inch diameter perforated pipes in which a
submersible pump may be placed The space around each pipe should be about 6 to
8 inches of filter gravel packing
The gravel pack should consist of material that is sufficiently fine to prevent the
natural fine sand from entering the pump and yet be sufficiently permeable to allow the
water to flow freely into the well Sieve analysis tests were conducted on the in situ fine
sand to evaluate possible readily available materials which would be suitable for the gravel
pack The results of the tests are presented in Appendix B The grain size analyses
indicate that pea gravel a quarry product readily available may be suitable for use in
the gravel pack A selection guideline for the filter material is shown on Plate 3
It is generally preferable that the pumping be continuous to avoid surging
conditions caused by the starting and stopping of the pumping which could result in loss
of fines from the adjacent in situ soils It appears that electric submersible pumps may
be appropriate for the dewatering with diesel backup pumps in case of power outages
Effects of Dewatering
Dewatering and the subsequent lowering of the natural groundwater level can
cause ground settlements if the site is underlain by soft compressible soils In this case
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2840 00 Page 9
the lowering of the water table increases the weight of the dewatered soil from a
submerged unit weight approximately 60 pcf to a total wet unit weight approximately
120 pcf This increase in soil weight results in additional load on the soft soils and can
result in settlement due to compression of the soft soils The subsurface information from
the boring explorations indicate that the general area appears to be underlain by dense
granular coralline soils It is our opinion significant ground settlement due to lowering of
the groundwater level is not anticipated if proper dewatering procedures as stated above
are followed
Another way in which ground settlements can occur near a dewatered site is by
loss of fines caused by removal of the finer portions of the in situ soil resulting from the
water flow through the soils during the pumping Loss of fines would also increase
permeability of the underlying coral soils and formations This loss of fines can be greatly
reduced by careful selection of a filter or gravel pack material around the well to prevent
the fines from entering the pump Limiting the starting and stopping of the pumps can
also help to reduce surging action which promote transmission of fines
The amount of drawdown which may occur adjacent to the site would depend on
several factors such as the depth of the pump placement amount of water pumped
location of the pumps and local soil conditions A rough estimate of the drawdown that
may be experienced at a distance of 50 feet away from the pump well is two 2 to four
4 feet
Monitoring
We recommend that a monitoring program be established for the project to verify
that the dewatering operations are not adversely affecting the surrounding area The
monitoring program may consist of the following
1 A damage survey of the existing buildings and structures adjacent to the
construction site to document the existing conditions of the structures
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2840 00 Page 10
before excavation and dewatering commences The survey should include
photographs and crack surveys
2 Settlement reference points should be established around the perimeter of
the site and at locations in the adjacent areas The reference points may
be surveyed at intervals of about one 1 week for the first three 3 months
and less frequently thereafter if they do not indicate movements
3 The drawdown of the water level should be measured and recorded daily
to detect trends or changes in the dewatering operations that may affect the
adjacent properties Observation wells consisting of 2 inch diameter slotted
pvc pipes were installed along the perimeter of the project site for water
level measurements during our testing These observation wells may be
used to monitor the water level fluctuations during the dewatering
operations
Excavation Supports
An excavation depth of about ten 10 feet is anticipated for the basement
construction It is our current understanding that the lower portion of the excavation will
be structurally supported with aluminum C channel sections driven below the bottom of
the excavation while the upper three 3 to five 5 feet of the excavation will be sloped
back
We believe that a temporary cut slope ratio of one and one half horizontal to one
vertical may be used for sloping back the in situ sandy soils For calculating
the earth loads acting on the perimeter shoring system horizontal bracing or struts we
suggest that the pressure diagram presented on Plate 4 be used as a guide for design
It should be noted that the lateral earth pressure presented in the pressure diagram does
not include any factor of safety

Our preliminary analysis indicates that a minimum penetration of about 7 feet below
the excavation bottom may be required for the proposed cantilever shoring system To
aid in dewatering of the basement excavation we recommend that the C channel
section be driven with the flanges interlocking or adjoining side by side with each other
to form a perimeter cut off wall around the excavation
Construction Monitoring
it is recommended that the geotechnical engineer be retained to provide

dewatering design should be made

APPENDIX C
Field Percolation Testing
A twelve 12 inch diameter boring was drilled to approximately 21 8 feet below the
existing ground surface to install the pump well PW A total of seven 7 four 4 inch
diameter borings were drilled to approximately 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface
for use as observation wells OW 1 thru OW 7
After drilling six 6 inch diameter PVC casing and well screens was installed in the
pump well and two 2 inch diameter PVC casing and screens were installed into the
observation wells The bottom ten 10 feet of the PVC casings consisted of slotted
screens to allow infiltration of water into the surrounding subsoils
Following casing installation the pump well was developed by surging and airlifting
utilizing an air compressor The supply of compressed air was turned on and off to
create a surge effect for cleaning out sediments from within the pump during airlifting
The surging and airlifting process was continued for approximately four 4 hours until
minimal amounts of sediments were developed with each surge At this time the well
was considered clean and ready for percolation testing
After development a pump test was performed by continuously pumping water out
from the pump well into a discharge manhole using a 1 4 inch 1 D centrifugal pump at
a constant flow rate The water level inside the pump well and observation wells were
measured using a well sounder at regular time intervals until the drawdowns inside the
wells have stabilized for at least an hour The data obtained during the pump test are
shown on Plate C 1
The data obtained for the pump well at the end of the pump test are used to
calculate the permeability of the subsoils using the constant head method The constant
head percolation test data and the corresponding calculated permeability is shown on
Plate C 2
In Boring No OW 8 falling head tests were performed to determine the
permeabilities of the upper sand layer and the underlying coralline gravel layer The tests
were performed by advancing a four 4 inch diameter solid casing with flushed bottom
to the desired depths The rate of water level drop were measured after filling up the
casing with water The falling head percolation test data and the corresponding
permeabilities of subsoils are shown on Plate C 3 and C 4
