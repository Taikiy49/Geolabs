Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by hard to
very hard clay (fill and alluvial soil) about 16 feet or more in thickness. Coral formation was
encountered only in the deepest portions of Boring Nos. 1, 12, 13, 14, 19 and 25.
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings at the time of our field exploration.
Our laboratory testing on the surface clay materials indicated that these soils have
relatively high expansion potential. Proper site preparation, earthwork procedures and
foundation design are necessary to provide adequate support for pavements and
structures.
Due to the very high costs for over-excavation and replacement of the expansive
soil with select non-expansive structural fill for the entire site, selective over-excavation
and replacement under walkways and driveways will be performed. Additionally, a
proprietary slab system, the Wafflemat, will be used for the building foundations to resist
the expansive soil effects.
For foundation design of the anticipated lightly loaded residential structures, an
allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for
footings bearing on the compacted fill placed in accordance with the recommendations
presented herein.

design recommendations.

SECTION 1. GENERAL
1.1. Introduction
This report presents the results of our updated geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the Franciscan Vistas residential and community use project
at the corner of Renton Road and O‘ohao Street (TMK: (1) 9-1-126: 009) in the Ewa
area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown
on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
Geolabs, Inc. issued a Geotechnical Engineering Report for the project dated,
June 23, 2006. The report contained recommendations for removal and replacement of
the expansive clay soil under the site and a post-tensioned slab alternative. This
updated report reflects the selection of a “Waffelmat” foundation system for the
residential buildings.
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations derived from our previous field exploration, laboratory testing, and
engineering analyses. The recommendations presented herein are intended for the
design of site preparation, infrastructure installation, building foundations and road
pavements. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report.

utilities (e.g., water, sewer, electric, communications, and lighting) as well as parking
areas.
Site grading consists primarily of cutting down of existing soil berms and piles at
the eastern portion of the site along with filling of an existing channel along the southern

SECTION 2. SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2.1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be
late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age.
The project site is situated on the Ewa Coastal Plain, which extends seaward off
the southeastern flank of the Waianae Volcano and the southwestern flank of the
Koolau Volcano. This plain was formed by the accumulation of a thick wedge of
sediments and coral reef formations on the flanks of the two volcanoes.
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea.
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level.
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials.
The soils and rocks underlying the project site were described by H.T. Stearns
and K.N. Vaksik, as follows:
Pleistocene Non-Calcareous Deposits (Qa) - This deposit consists chiefly of alluvium.
The surface soils underlying the project site are described by the U.S. Soil Conservation
Service in their publication "Soil Survey of Islands of Kauai, Oahu, Maui, Molokai and
Lanai, State of Hawaii," (1972), as follows:
Honouliuli Clay, 0 to 2% slopes - These soils consist of well-drained soils developed
in alluvium derived from basic igneous rocks. The soils have generally high shrink-swell
potential and low shear strength.

SECTION 2. SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2.2 Site Description
The Franciscan Vistas project site is on the southern side of the Town of Ewa
and to the east of the former Ewa Sugar Mill site in the Ewa District on the Island of
Oahu, Hawaii, as shown on the Project Location Plan, Plate 1. The project site is former
sugar cane plantation land that was used partly for a mill yard area and partly for
associated sugar cane processing.
At the time of our field exploration, most of the project site was vacant land with a
light to heavy cover of dry land vegetation, such as low shrubs and grasses.
Recreational facilities such as basketball and tennis courts were at the northwest corner
of the site and a baseball/softball field was situated at the southwest corner.
The existing ground surface at the western half was generally level with ground
surface elevations ranging from about 39.5 to 43.5 feet above Mean Sea Level (MSL).
Two concrete lined ditches, about 3 to 4 feet deep, traversed the eastern portion from
north to south in a “Y” configuration.
The topography at the eastern half was more complicated with the central portion
being relatively level at about 43.5 feet MSL. This central area was generally enclosed
by soil berms about 10 feet high. Along the southern boundary in the eastern half, an
existing channel about 10 feet deep led to a culvert structure.
Locally, in various locations at the eastern portion, there were mounds of soil,
debris and other materials. It is believed that most of these piles were left from the
former agricultural use.

SECTION 3. DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS

to very hard clay (fill and alluvial soil) about 16 feet or more in thickness. Coral

SECTION 3. DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
3.1 Site Grading
Items of grading addressed in the following subsections: (1) Site Preparation, (2)
Keying and Benching, (3) Fills and Backfills, (4) Fill Placement and Compaction
Requirements, (5) Excavation, and (6) Cut and Fill Slopes.
Fills up to about 12 feet high will be required to meet the design finished grades.
We anticipate that the suitable stockpiled material on site will be used for fills.
Additionally, import borrow soils will be required to complete the mass grading of the
project.
A Geolabs representative should monitor site-grading operations to observe
whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation process and to
confirm whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar to those encountered in our
field exploration.
3.1.1 Site Preparation
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other
unsuitable materials, should be removed and disposed properly off-site or
stockpiled in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the
excavated materials.
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing, grubbing, and over-excavation
work should be over-excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting
excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted engineered fill. The excavated
soft soils may be used as fill, provided that fill material requirements are met.
3.1.2 Keying and Benching
The ground surface is nearly level; however, there may be some localized areas
with steeper slopes. Therefore, this section gives guidance in design and
construction of grading for those areas. Keys and benches required should be
firmly seated into existing ground.

A keyway should be cut down into the in-situ material at the toe of fills on ground
where the existing slope is steeper than five horizontal to one vertical (5H:1V). This
key should be a minimum of 2 feet in depth at the toe of the fill and should be a
minimum of 8 feet in width extending back under the toe of the fill. To bond the fill
into the existing slope, the floor of the key should be sloped back under the base of
the fill at about 10H:1V.
To bond the mass of the fill into the existing ground as fill placement progresses up
the existing slope, benches should be cut into the existing slope. These benches
should be a minimum of 3 feet in height and approximately 10 feet in width.
Based on the site conditions encountered, we recommend, prior to the placement
of fill, proof-rolling the keys and benches with vibratory sheepsfoot compactor or
similar heavy equipment for a minimum of six passes to help verify the achievement
of adequate fill support. The proof-rolling operations should be performed in the
presence of a Geolabs representative. Yielding areas, loose areas, or cavities
disclosed during clearing and proof-rolling operations should be backfilled with
compacted fill materials.
3.1.3 Fills and Backfills
In general, we anticipate that the on site stockpile will be used for the initial filling.
The stockpile material generally consists of moderately to highly expansive clay
soil. The stockpiled soil that is generally less than 3 inches in size and without
organic or other deleterious material may be used in the mass grading fills. As
much as practical, the less expansive soil should be used in the upper portions of
the fill.
Imported soil will be required to complete the mass grading at the site. Imported fill
should be less than 3 inches in largest dimension and should have a laboratory
CBR value of 8 or more and should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent
when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883. Geolabs should observe and/or test
imported fill materials for conformance with these recommendations prior to
delivery to the project site for its intended use.

3.1.4 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills using on-site clayey soils should be moisture-conditioned to about
5 percent above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches
in loose thickness, and compacted to between 85 and 90 percent relative
compaction.
Fills and backfills consisting of import fill material should be moisture-conditioned to
about 2 percent above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding
10 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction.
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight)
corresponding to the maximum dry density.
Our recommendations provide elevated moisture contents and reduced compaction
requirements for fill and backfill work to limit the potential swell capacity of the
clayey soils encountered at the site. Therefore, we wish to emphasize that moisture
conditioning of the fill materials is an integral and critical part of the fill and backfill
placement recommendations provided herein. Compaction should be accomplished
by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction
equipment.
Base course and select borrow subbase materials in roads, driveways and parking
areas should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in
level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum
of 95 percent relative compaction.

Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the subgrade soils are critical

3.1.5 Excavation
It is anticipated that the on-site clayey soils may be excavated with conventional
heavy excavation equipment. However, our past experience in the Ewa Villages
area indicates that the surface of the underlying coral formation is karstic in nature
with large elevation variations in the top of the formation. There may be subsurface
mounds or pinnacles of coral at shallower depths that were encountered in the
borings drilled for our field exploration. Therefore, excavation for subsurface
infrastructure construction may encounter hard coral, which may require the use of
hoerams or trenching machines. It should be noted that the coral material in the few
borings that encountered coral was penetrated using auger-drilling methods but
some of the sampling efforts encountered refusal conditions.
The above discussion regarding the rippability of the surface materials is based on
data obtained from the field borings. Contractors bidding on this project should
examine the site conditions and soil data to make their own interpretation.

3.2 Foundations
For foundation design of the anticipated lightly loaded residential structures, an
allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for
footings bearing on the compacted fill or backfill placed in accordance with the
recommendations presented herein. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and
may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces.
Footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade. Footings adjacent to or on slopes should be embedded to a
sufficient depth to provide a minimum horizontal setback distance of at least 6 feet
measured from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope. Foundations
next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary

plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench, or the footings should
extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines.
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated light design loads for
footings bearing on the structural fill to be less than 1 inch if the foundations are
designed and constructed as recommended herein. Differential settlements between
adjacent footings should be on the order of about 0.5 inch or less.
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by a combination of friction
resistance between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive
earth pressure developed against the near-vertical faces of the embedded portion of the
footings. A coefficient of friction of 0.30 may be used for footings bearing on the on-site
materials. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be calculated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 275 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf) for
footings embedded in the on-site materials. The passive earth pressure value assumes
that the concrete for the footings is cast neat against on-site materials. Otherwise, the
soils around the footings should be well compacted (minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction). Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive pressure resistance in

3.3. Residential Slabs-On-Grade
The on-site soils generally have moderate to high expansion potential. Therefore,

considered to reduce the effects of the expansive soll:

e Post-tensioned slab system. A “stiff foundation system may be designed

preparation is required prior to construction of the post-tensioned slab.

3.6 Retaining Structures
For the construction of the proposed residential development, retaining walls may
be required for grade separation. In general, the following guidelines may be used for
preliminary design of the retaining structures.
3.6.1 Wall Foundations
In general, we believe that retaining wall foundations may be designed in
accordance with the "Foundations" section presented herein. The foundations
should have a minimum width of 18 inches and should be embedded a minimum of
24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade at relatively flat areas. For

the slope face. The horizontal setback distance may be reduced to a minimum of

footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should be constructed in

3.6.3 Drainage
To reduce the build-up for hydrostatic pressures, retaining structures should be well
drained. A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide zone of
permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation),
placed directly around a perforated pipe (perforations down) at the base of the
retaining structure discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes. As an
alternative, a prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may
be used in lieu of the drainage material. The prefabricated drainage product should
also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall.
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of granular fill or
excavated on-site coral soils less than 3 inches in maximum dimension. To reduce
the potential for water infiltration behind the retaining structures, the upper
12-inches of backfill should consist of relatively impervious materials, unless
covered by concrete slabs.

3.8 Basic Utility Installation
We anticipate that utility installation will consist of electric, telecommunications,
water, sewer, and drain lines to support the residential development. In general, good
construction practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling of the
trenches for the new site utilities. The contractor should determine the method and
equipment to be used for trench excavation subject to practical limits and safety
considerations. In addition, the excavations should comply with the applicable federal,
state, and local safety requirements. The contractor should be responsible for trench
shoring design and installation.
Based on the results of our field exploration, it appears that most of the utility
trench excavations will be in the upper clay soils and that conventional cut-and-cover
construction may be used for infrastructure construction. The upper surface of the
underlying coral formation is very irregular and some trenches may encounter the coral
formation that may pose some excavation difficulties during trenching work.
In general, granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel
(ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) is recommended below the pipes. To provide adequate
support around the pipes and to reduce compaction of the backfill, thus reducing the
possibility of pipe damage, free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel
(ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), should be used for the trench backfill up to about
12 inches above the pipes.
The upper portion of the trench backfill should consist of material generally less
than 6 inches in maximum size compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction.
The upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement finished grade should be
compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction in places where trenches will
be in paved areas. Refer to Plate 3, Typical Trench Detail.

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling 30 borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 30, extending to depths of
between about 20.8 and 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate
boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using
truck-mounted rotary drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers.
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist, who observed the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed by visual
observation and testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings,
Plates A-1 through A-30.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving
a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In
addition, some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths.

APPENDIX B
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937)
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings
at the appropriate sample depths.
Nine Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. Test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth.
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plates B-1 and B-2.
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D 3080) was performed on a selected sample to
evaluate peak and residual shear strength characteristics. The test results and shear
stress versus normal stress plots are presented on Plate B-3.
One Modified Proctor compaction test (ASTM D 1557) was performed on a bulk
sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships. The test results are presented on Plate B-4.
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) tests (ASTM D 1883) were
performed on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-5 and B-6.
Twelve one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on natural and remolded
samples of the soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils. The test
results are presented on Plate B-7.
Five sets of corrosion tests, including pH (AASHTO T 289) and
Minimum Resistivity (ASTM G 57), were performed to evaluate acidity and corrosive
properties of the saturated soil samples obtained from our field exploration. The test
results are presented on Plates B-8 and B-9.
