2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was formed by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The
younger shield Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae
Volcano became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and the eastern flank of
Waianae Volcano was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern
flank
The project site is composed generally of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of
lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks generally are characterized by flows
of jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers In situ chemical
weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years The
weathering process has formed a mantle of residual and saprolitic soils In general
saprolite saprolitic soils is composed mainly of silty and clayey materials and is typical
of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite grades to basaltic rock
formation with increased depth

2 4 Geologic Units
Considering the large areal extent of the project site the various geologic
deposits encountered at the project site are relatively uniform and may be grouped into
three generalized types of geologic materials The generalized types of geologic
materials encountered at the project site include the following
e Fill
e Residual Soils

Further discussions on the general characteristics of the geologic units
encountered in the borings are presented in the following subsections
Eill
The fills encountered at the project site consist of both residual and saprolitic soils
which have been excavated and placed in several areas that have been developed
previously The residual and saprolitic fill soils consist of silty and clayey soils We
believe that there is no documentation on the placement and compaction of the fill
materials Based on the borings the fill materials are in a stiff to hard consistency
however there is no certainty as to the uniformity of the fill materials in terms of the
consistency Some organic materials were encountered in the fill materials within
the borings drilled Therefore there is potential for encountering organic materials
within the fill materials during the site grading work
Residual Soils
Residual soils are completely weathered volcanic rocks that have lost all visible
rock texture characteristics The residual soils at the project site are typically brown
to reddish brown in color The residual soils may be mottled with various colors

Residual soils are comprised mainly of clayey materials with stiff to very stiff
consistency Typically residual soils occur overlying saprolitic soils or weathered
basalt formation It should be noted that relict boulders of hard rock may be
encountered in residual soils In addition there is potential for encountering hard
basalt rock ledges in residual soils because the volcanic rock may not have
weathered uniformly throughout the rock formation
Saprolitic Soils
Extremely weathered volcanic rock is referred to as saprolitic soils or saprolites
Saprolitic soils represent rock that has been reduced by weathering to soil like
components with weathered rock fragments The soil material retains the remnant
rock texture such as layering vesicularity and some fracture patterns Saprolitic
soils are typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks and are composed
mainly of silty materials The coloring scheme of saprolitic soils depends on the
type of parent volcanic rock from which the soils are derived and the degree of
chemical weathering that the material has experienced Because the parent
material does not weather uniformly throughout the formation relict boulders of
hard rock and or hard rock ledges occasionally may be encountered within
saprolitic soils
2 5 Seismic Considerations
Based on the Unified Facilities Criteria 01 which references the
International Building Code IBC 2000 the short period spectral acceleration S for
the project site is 0 55g and the long period spectral acceleration S is 0 16g Based
on the above the project site may be subjected to seismic activity therefore seismic
design considerations will need to be addressed for the project The following sections
provide discussions on the seismicity of the Island of Oahu and the site classification at
the project site
Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by shifts
in the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to

volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before
or during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not
erupt The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but
most of the earthquakes are so small that they may only be detected by sensitive
instruments However some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and
a few cause minor to moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii
over 90 percent of earthquakes are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic
activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the
Island of Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5 M5 however earthquakes are not confined only to the
Island of Hawaii
To a lesser degree the Island of Maui has experienced numerous earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5 Therefore moderate to strong earthquakes have
occurred in the County of Maui The effects of earthquakes occurring on the
Islands of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu For example
several small landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui
Earthquake of In addition some houses on the Island of Oahu were
reportedly damaged as a result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 M7
Over the last 150 years of recorded history we are not aware of reported
earthquakes greater than Magnitude 6 occurring on the Island of Oahu We
understand that an earthquake of Magnitude 4 8 to 5 0 occurred along the
Diamond Head Fault in 1948 on the Island of Oahu The moderate tremor resulted
in broken store windows ruptured building walls and broken underground water
mains

Site Classification
Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered residual and saprolitic
soils extending to the maximum depths explored of about 6 6 meters below the
existing ground surface Based on our experience in the area of the project site we
believe that saprolitic soils and basalt rock formation may be extrapolated to depths
of about 30 meters below the ground level Based on the average penetration
resistance N values of the subsurface materials encountered we believe that the
project site may be classified as Class D in accordance with Table of the
IBC 2000 The Class D site classification represents deposits of soils with a
weighted average Standard Penetration Test SPT blow count between 15 and
50 blows per 300 mm for the soil profile within the upper 30 meters of the ground
surface

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris deleterious and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft and
yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to
receive fill should be over excavated to expose firm natural materials and the
resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill The excavated
soft and or organic soils should be disposed of properly off site or used in
landscaping areas if appropriate

and re compacted as recommended above Saturation and subsequent yielding of
the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted fill

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling 92 borings extending to depths ranging from about 6 1 to 6 6 meters below the
existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the General Site Plan
Plate 2 Enlarged site plans showing the approximate borings and bulk sample
locations are provided on Plates 3 1 and 3 2 The borings for the project were drilled
using truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineers or geologists who observed the drilling
operations on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by
visual observation and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general
conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered in the borings drilled are presented on the
Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 92
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils by driving a 75 mm OD Modified California sampler with a 63 5 kilogram
hammer falling 760 millimeters In addition some samples were obtained from the
borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration
Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 50 mm OD standard penetration
sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the
sampler the second and third 150 mm of a 450 mm drive are shown as the Penetration
Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket
penetrometer tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Seventeen one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface
soils The test results are presented on Plates B 1 and B 2
Four Expansion Index of Soil tests ASTM D 4829 were performed on selected
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface
soils The test is a standardized test used to determine the expansive characteristics of
the soil The test results are presented on Plate B 3
Twelve Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected
samples of the soils to determine the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil
classification Results of the tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depth Graphic presentations of the test results are provided on
Plates B 4 and B 5
Four unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 2166 were performed on selected
samples of the soils to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the soils The
results of the tests and the stress strain curves are presented on Plates B 6 through B 9
One unconsolidated undrained Triaxial Compression test ASTM D 2850 was
performed on a selected sample of the soils to evaluate the undrained shear strength of
the soils The results of the unconsolidated undrained triaxial compression tests are
presented on Plate B 10
Nine Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the shear strength characteristics Results of the direct shear tests
are presented on Plates B 11 through B 19
Seven laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were
performed on the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
the soils Results of the tests are presented on Plates B 20 through B 26
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on the
near surface soils to determine the dry density and moisture content relationship Results
of the tests are presented on Plates B 27 and B 28
