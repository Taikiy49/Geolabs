INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our subsurface exploration geotechnical
laboratory testing and review of available subsurface data in the vicinity of the proposed
pipeline alignment at the southern boundary of the HOST NELH facility Keahole Point
North Kona Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site is shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our work on the project and presents our findings pertinent
to the subsurface geology at the project site The work was performed in general
accordance with our fee proposals dated April 8 and May 7 1993
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is situated on the island of Hawaii in the district of North Kona to
the south of Keahole Point at the southerly boundary of the Hawaii Ocean Science and
Technology Park Natural Energy Laboratory of Hawaii HOST NELH
It is proposed to implement microtunnelling technology to install a new pipeline
through the coastal basalt formation Efforts to utilize directional drilling have in the past
met with only limited success Therefore it was desired to have geotechnical information
pertaining to the subsurface conditions along the proposed alignment to better quantify
the geologic strata which will likely be encountered during the microtunnelling effort

GENERAL SITE DESCRIPTION
Regional Geology
The project site is situated on the western flank of Hualalai one of the five 5
basaltic shield volcanoes which built the island of Hawaii Shield volcanoes are
characterized as being low dome shaped mountains built by quiescent eruptions of very
fluid lavas The general slope of the flanks of the domes range from about 5 to 15
degrees above horizontal
Hawaiian volcanic activity is typified by an abundance of flank eruptions along rift
zones or zones of weakness in the earth s crust The northwest rift zone of Hualalai is
situated approximately seven 7 miles to the north of the site
The majority of the HOST NELH facility is underlain by prehistoric basaltic lava
flows of the Hualalai Volcanic Series which were most likely deposited during the late
Pleistocene Stearns and Macdonald 1946 In the northern portion of the HOST NELH

facility the historic lava flow of 1801 1803 reached the ocean The geologic map by
Stearns and Macdonald shows that this historic flow is found only at the most northern
portion of the facility However the exposed lava between the coast and the eastern
boundary of the HOST NELH facility from the vicinity of Wawaloli Beach northward to the
mapped boundary of the 1801 1803 flow is very fresh and relatively unweathered It is
possible that this relatively unweathered rock formation may be an early phase of the
historic flow which was not included in the mapping
Most of the ground surface is exposed in situ basalt rock formation in the
pahoehoe form which is characterized by a relatively smooth rock surface with numerous
hummocks tumuli pressure ridges and cracks In some areas the pressure ridges and
cracks form very sharp topographic relief Grading with heavy equipment such as
bulldozers may be required to provide access for wheeled vehicles
Project Site Description
The proposed pipeline project site is located at the coastline along the southern
boundary of the HOST NELH facility in the District of North Kona on the island of Hawaii
The five 5 exploratory borings were drilled along the proposed pipeline alignment
extending from near the edge of the ocean to a point approximately 400 feet inland near
the junction of two jeep trails The borings were drilled at approximately 100 foot intervals
on the exposed pahoehoe basalt formation No rough grading was required for access
to the boring locations however Boring Nos 4 and 5 had some beach sand deposits
covering the basalt formation
The site is generally barren rocky land with a thin vegetation cover that grades
from bare rock to grasses with some haole koa keawe naupaka and other dry land
vegetation Elevations at the boring locations range from about 5 to 9 feet Mean Sea
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 10 Page 5
Level MSL Some isolated depressions between the borings have a ground surface
elevation less than 5 MSL These depressed areas typically contain coral sand and
cobbles deposited by wave action
The exposed surface basalt formation is of the Pahoehoe type which generally
consists of smooth billowy or ropy surfaces forming a hummocky topographic expres
sion The rock texture contains fairly regular spheroidal vesicles which are typical of this
type of lava flow and can be used to identify Pahoehoe flows in the field The ropy
surface of Pahoehoe is the result of dragging and wrinkling of the solidifying but still
plastic crust by the moving liquid beneath Because the movement of the liquid stream
is fastest in the center the ropy looking wrinkles are curved with their convexity often
pointing in the direction of flow
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geologic Conditions
In general our field exploration encountered subsurface conditions which were
fairly consistent along the proposed pipeline alignment Please refer to Plate 3 for an
idealized geologic cross section along the proposed pipeline alignment
The borings encountered a surface layer of dense very hard basalt extending to
depths of about 25 to 30 feet below the existing ground surface The physical character
istics of this flow unit suggest that the flow ponded when it was deposited creating a
ledge of dense relatively massive rock intercalated with minor zones of porous and
fractured rock
Beneath this layer a horizon of closely to severely fractured hard basalt was
encountered extending to depths of about 31 to 37 feet below the existing ground

DISCUSSIONS
General Background
By definition basalt is an igneous volcanic rock formed by the extrusion of a
magmatic melt onto the surface When the magma is extruded as lava it moves across
the surface under the influence of gravity until it cools and solidifies This solidification
process is actually analogous to freezing
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 10 Page 7
The lava when initially extruded has little or no crystalline structure in the melt
When the molten lava cools and begins to crystallize the groundmass of the resultant
rock is highly vitreous i e contains a high percentage of volcanic glass In addition
Hawaiian basalts in their molten state contain a high percentage of entrained gases and
fluids These gases and fluids are actively exsolving from the melt forming bubbles as it
cools If the melt freezes before the gases and fluids are completely exsolved the
bubbles remain in the rock as vesicles and vugs
If the majority of the gases and fluids exsolve while the lava is still moving the
viscosity and density of the melt increases The surface of the flow begins to solidify into
a scoria Internal dynamic fluid and thermal stresses break up this scoria crust and
reposition it at the surface of the flow and down the frontal face where it is covered by the
advancing lava This forms the spiny broken scoriaceous fragments referred to as clinker
which are characteristic of the margins of pahoehoe flows and the bulk of a a flows The
viscous inner core of the flow crystallizes into a dense but vitreous rock
Since basalts are formed by the solidifying of an intensely hot melt there are
considerable thermal stresses generated when the melt cools and crystallizes at ambient
temperatures Relief of these thermal stresses causes jointing and fracturing in the rock
mass Generally the cooling does not occur at an even rate therefore the jointing is
frequently irregular
Cavities occur in basalt as large bubbles or blisters of exsolved gases and fluids
and as lava tubes Lava tubes form when molten lava drains from the interior of a cooled
portion of the flow Cavities range in size from a few inches to large caverns which are
tens of feet in diameter and may extend for miles in length No extensive cavities or lava
tubes were encountered in the borings however the possibility exists that cavities or lava
tubes may be encountered during the microtunnelling operation
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 10 Page 8
Because of the porosity of the rock resulting from interconnected vesicles the
open structure of clinker and the jointing of the rock mass basalts tend to be highly
permeable The open cavities also serve as conduits for fluids Therefore basalts are
capable of accepting and transmitting large quantities of fluids
Summary of Geologic Conditions at the HOST NELH Facility
Portions of the HOST NELH facility were previously explored by the drilling of
borings These borings were drilled for geotechnical engineering exploration and for the
installation of groundwater monitoring wells
In general the borings were drilled by small rotary rigs such as Mobile B 40
Mobile B 61 and CME 55 rigs using diamond coring methods Both wireline and conven
tional core barrels were utilized for core recovery The depths of the borings ranged from
about 8 5 to about 135 0 feet below the existing ground surface
The majority of these borings were drilled along the main access road into the
HOST NELH facility and in the immediate vicinity of the Natural Energy Laboratory
complex With the exception of the deepest boring which was drilled at the entrance to
the park the previous borings encountered what appears to be a relatively thick flow unit
of pahoehoe lava at the surface
Some of the previous borings which penetrated this surface Pahoehoe flow unit
encountered sand to gravel and cobble sized basalt fragments which appear to be a
littoral scree deposit of debris generated by explosions and cavitation resulting from the
intense thermal interaction between the molten lava and the cool ocean water
Examination of recovered samples of this scree indicate that the grain sizes are generally
in the sand and gravel range with occasional cobble and boulder sized fragments Visual
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 10 Page 9
observations made during SCUBA dive reconnaissances of excavations at the
HOST NELH facility also indicate that the scree deposit contains some pillow lavas
This littoral scree horizon seems to track fairly consistent across the coastal areas
of the HOST NELH facility It is generally encountered at elevations ranging from about
24 to 28 feet MSL which is consistent with the findings made during the current
exploration
The borings from previous explorations which encountered the scree indicate that
it ranges in thickness from about 19 feet to more than 34 feet Only one of the these
borings completely penetrated the screen deposit to encounter what appeared to be a
subaerial basalt flow
The available information leads to the conjecture that the Keahole Point area was
at one time an embayment in the coast and that the lava flow exposed at the surface
entered this lower area The underlying scree deposit was formed by the advancing flow
front and established a base on which the flow continued its seaward advance developing
what is now Keahole Point
Drilling Considerations
Hawaiian basalts present a unique set of difficulties for drilling work These
difficulties are related to the nature structure and certain physical characteristics of the
rocks The following discussions provide a brief summary of some of the major difficulties
encountered or observed during past experiences
Physical Properties
Since basalt is igneous in origin it is considered to be a hard rock Laboratory
testing on six 6 core samples selected from borings along the proposed align

ment yielded unconfined compressive strengths ranging from about 1 500 to about
15 000 pounds per square inch psi Review of available literature indicates that
a range of about 0 2 is typical for the Poisson s Ratio of basalt rock These core
samples were selected to represent the various types of basalt rock core
recovered from the explorational drilling and were tested in general accordance

A series of gradation analysis were run on select samples obtained from the littoral

Hardness testing was also performed on selected core samples utilizing the
geologic Moh s Hardness scale of classification Moh s Hardness scale ranges
from one 1 to ten 10 with a specific mineral assigned to each number on the
scale The selected samples tested range in Moh s Hardness from six 6 to seven
7
The strength of the rock can be highly variable and may change over a depth of
inches We have encountered very slow penetration rates in the core section of
lava flows followed by extremely rapid penetration in clinker or highly vesicular
pahoehoe rock This has been noted to occur within a five foot 5 core run
A review of field notes from some of the borings drilled at the HOST NELH facility
indicated diamond coring penetration rates ranging from about seven 7 to over
30 minutes per foot utilizing a rotary CME 55 or equivalent drilling rig

During the drilling of these borings cavities were occasionally encountered Most
of these cavities were on the order of one foot 1 or less in vertical height
However it is believed that most of these cavities have a much greater horizontal
extent
Since basalt is highly permeable with fractures cavities and punky zones it can
be considered a thief formation capable of taking large quantities of drilling fluids
Review of the field notes for the borings drilled at the site indicates that circulation
was lost within the first few feet of drilling Clear water was used for the drilling of
these borings The field notes indicate that bentonite or polymer mud was used
for the deeper portions of some borings when the rotary tools began to bind ina
dirty hole or in the scree deposit Circulation was not restored by the use of
muds
Experience at other locations indicate that when using mud rotary drilling
methods the use of lost circulation materials or cementing is frequently required
to maintain circulation or to obtain adequate hole cleaning
In addition basalt has a relatively high unit weight The bulk unit weight of the
core samples tested for this exploration and report ranged from about 129 to 158
pounds per cubic foot This high unit weight may require high rates of circulation
or thick muds for adequate hole cleaning
Physical Characteristics
Since basalt is generally vitreous and contains vesicles vugs and scoriaceous
zones it is an abrasive drilling environment Our own experience indicates that
diamond bit life may be as short as a few tens of feet but generally averages about
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 10 Page 12
400 feet of penetration for a surface set core bit There is also noticeable abrasion
wear on drill rods and casing when drilling in fresh unweathered basalts
Caving in of the holes have been experienced in clinker or debris zones and in
highly fractured lava flows A situation which is particular to Hawaiian flows occurs
when drilling in a clinker zone after penetrating the dense core section Fragments
of clinker will tend to fall in on top of the drilling tools and will lock the tools in the
core section when attempting to pull out of the hole
The variable strength of the rock may also cause drilling difficulties When
advancing through either hard or soft material the transition to material of a
different strength may be irregular and cause bit deviation
Voids or cavities generally do not present significant difficulties for vertical drilling
other than loss of circulation and bit deviation However angle holes may require
the cementing or grouting of cavities that intersect the bore at low angles to
reduce the potential of the drill string following the cavity rather than the planned
course

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling of five
5 borings with depths ranging between 40 and 60 feet below the existing ground surface
at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled
using a truck mounted drill rig advancing HQ wireline core drilling tools
The materials recovered from the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation and
testing in the laboratory All soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System A graphic presentation of the materials encountered are
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of four inches 4 in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0
foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring Logs as
RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quality RQD

Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100
