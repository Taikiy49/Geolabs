INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Fagasa Road Route 5 Reconstruction project from the
village of Fagasa to Mafa Pass on the island of Tutuila American Samoa The general
location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical engineering recommenda
tions resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses
The recommendations presented herein are intended for design input and may not be
appropriate to form the basis for preparation of final construction cost estimates nor for
construction bidding purposes

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Tutuila was built during the Pliocene and early Pleistocene age by
the extrusion of basaltic lavas and pyroclastic materials from five volcanoes Stearns
1944 Following the cessation of the main shield building activity there was a long
period of erosion accompanied by the growth of fringing coral reefs During the late
Quaternary volcanism was renewed by eruptions of lava and pyroclastic materials at
several vents on the south side of the island forming the Tafuna Leone Plain
The main mountain mass of Tutuila was divided into seven formations Stearns
1944 The rocks are thought to be of Pliocene Age consisting of thinly bedded to
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 9
massive flows of basalt andesitic basalt and andesite interbedded with cinder cones
and tuff beds with thrachytic dikes plugs and crater fills The rock units were erupted
from as many as five eruptive centers but the principal volcano was near the middle
of the island Pago Pago Harbor is believed to occupy part of the collapsed caldera of
that principal volcano Erosion along a major fault along the caldera wall formed the
near vertical cliff on the northeast side of the harbor
Prior to the eruption of the Leone Volcanics beach and lagoonal deposits
accumulated on and behind a submerged barrier reef at the present site of the Leone
Plain These deposits were later buried under several hundreds of feet of volcanic
materials These older beach and lagoonal deposits consist partially of sand and
coralline gravels
Three tuff cones and a thick sequence of lava flows form the plain between
Tafuna and Leone The rock is thought to be of Holocene Age possibly only a few
thousand years old as evidenced by geologic relationships in the area The lava flows
are mainly thinly bedded basalts with interbeds of tuff and breccia
Clays silts sands and gravels have been deposited by alluvial processes on the
many valley floors located adjacent to shoreline areas At some valley mouths these
alluvial deposits may be on the order of more than 100 feet in thickness

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
On the basis of our geotechnical exploration clayey soils were encountered
along the majority of the proposed roadway reconstruction alignment below the existing
pavement surface to the maximum depths explored The soils generally grade from
medium stiff to stiff with increasing depth At some boring locations medium dense to
dense sandy soils were encountered below the existing pavements to depths ranging
between 5 and 8 feet below the existing ground surface Hard basalt formation was
encountered at shallow depths ranging between 1 and 4 feet below the ground
surface in Boring and 13
Based on our borings the existing pavement section consist generally of 1 to
2 inches of asphalt concrete over 6 inches of sandy coral gravel material which is the
base course material In Boring No 4 a base course layer was not observed below
the asphalt concrete pavement In Boring Nos 9 and 10 a layer of sand fill was
observed below the surface pavement in lieu of a typical aggregate base course layer
Based on our field exploration and site reconnaissance portions of the existing
Fagasa Road pavement have deteriorated significantly such that its serviceability is
currently poor to unsatisfactory We believe that one of the most significant causes of
the observed pavement deterioration is a result of poor surface and subsurface
drainage conditions at the site In addition the lack of a proper base course layer
below the asphalt concrete pavement has also contributed to the failures of the
pavement Poor subgrade soil conditions such as saturated clays and non uniform
subgrade soils encountered in the borings have also contributed to the pavement
deterioration observed
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 12
Based on observations at the site the surface and subsurface drainage
conditions of the roadway are poor as evidenced by ponding of rain water in depressed
areas where the pavement had settled Drainage structures such as catch basins or
storm drains were not observed along portions of the road alignment Where drainage
structures were observed they were generally obstructed by vegetation and debris
Therefore we recommend that the existing drainage ditch along the road alignment be
reconstructed to intercept the runoff water from slopes
As a result of the poor surface drainage water may be introduced into the
pavement section and subgrade from cracks in the pavement surface from seepage
into the subgrade soils and from poor drainage adjacent to paved areas Water in the
subgrade soils may cause softening of the clayey subgrade soils which can result in
pavement distress and subsequent failure Therefore we recommend that the existing
roadway pavements be removed and be replaced with new pavements designed in
accordance with the recommendations presented herein In addition the existing
pavement section does not appear to be adequate for the projected traffic loading due
to inadequate and or lack of a base course layer Therefore pavement overlays are
not recommended
In general the subsurface soils encountered along the roadway alignment should
provide adequate support for the new roadway pavements provided that the subgrade
is prepared as recommended herein and that the surface and subsurface drainage is
diverted to reduce potential saturation of the base course and subgrade soils Our
geotechnical engineering recommendations addressing pavement design pavement

Pavement Drainage System
One of the primary distress mechanisms observed in pavement structures is
pumping due to saturation of the base course and or subgrade soils In addition free
water voids in the pavement section repeated heavy wheel loads and an erodible
base can also cause pumping
In general the primary source of free water in the pavement section is surface
water infiltration through cracks and joints in the pavement A major source of
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 16
infiltrated moisture is the longitudinal pavement shoulder joint Water also enters the
pavement section from shallow ditches To reduce moisture infiltration into the
pavement structure all pavement joints and cracks should be sealed to reduce
infiltration and pavement drainage systems should be installed to collect and divert free
water from the pavement section Therefore considering the high rainfall in this area
we recommend a subsurface drainage system be provided on the sides of the roadway
alignment to divert subsurface water The pavement drainage system recommended
consists of three elements
1 A permeable base to provide rapid drainage of free water that may enter
the pavement structure
2 A longitudinal edge drain collector system to convey accumulated water
from the permeable base to appropriate outlet structures
3 A filter separator layer to reduce the potential for migration of fines into
the permeable base from the subgrade subbase or shoulder base
material

Filter Layer
The function of a filter is to provide both drainage and filtration by allowing water
to pass through drainage with minimal head loss while retaining soil particles
filtration The major criteria considered for a geotextile drainage filtration
application include 1 soil retention piping resistance 2 permeability
3 clogging potential 4 chemical composition requirements considerations and
5 constructability and serviceability requirements
For a geotextile to perform effectively as a filter in a geotextile drainage system
it must remain free draining by having opening characteristics compatible with
the surrounding soils The choice of a correct geotextile is critical to the
formation of a stable and effective soil filter Unless proper fabric piping
resistance clogging resistance and constructability strength requirements are
obtained it is doubtful that the desired results will be obtained Construction
installation and monitoring must also be provided to ensure that the materials
have been installed properly
Therefore we recommend that a layer of geotextile fabric such as Mirafi 140N
or equivalent be provided between the upper permeable base and the
underlying subgrade material for the rigid pavement section to reduce the
potential for migration of fines into the permeable base from the underlying
subgrade or shoulder base material Generally non woven needle punched
geotextiles are better for pavement drainage applications than heat bonded
geotextiles Woven or slit film geotextiles should not be used The geotextile
fabric used should conform to the specifications as presented in Appendix C
For the flexible pavement section the aggregate subbase course layer see
Appendix C for gradation requirements would serve as a filter for the permeable

Pavement Joints
Where rigid pavements will be used we recommend that pavement joints and
joint sealants be provided Joint sealants should be used to provide a seal to reduce
potential infiltration of water from surface runoff Joint sealants also provide a
compressible joint to help control cracking due to volume changes in the concrete as
a result of fluctuations in the temperature In general joints should be provided in rigid
pavements for the following
1 To reduce potential curling and warping stresses in the pavement caused
by temperature and moisture gradients across the pavement slabs
2 To reduce and control cracking due to volume changes in the concrete
3 To reduce damage to immovable structures
4 To facilitate construction

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed The existing pavement sections should be
removed and the asphalt concrete should be disposed of off site All vegetation
debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed
of properly off site to reduce potential contamination of the excavated materials
After clearing and grubbing the exposed subgrade should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 8 inches properly moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95
percent relative compaction For clayey subgrades the compaction requirement
may be reduced to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
During the scarification process of the subgrade if over sized materials greater

Wall Foundations
We believe that the future planned retaining walls may be supported on shallow
continuous footings bearing on the stiff clayey silts silty clays and basalt rock
encountered at the project site An allowable bearing pressure of 3 000 p s f
may be used for design of footings bearing on the stiff soils or hard basalt
formation The bearing value above is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third when considering transient loads such as wind or
seismic forces If the footings are designed in accordance with our recommen
dations total settlement of wall footings is estimated to be on the order of less
than 1 inch
Wall foundations should be embedded at least 2 feet below the lowest adjacent
finished grade If fills are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations
the footings should be embedded deeper to bear on the native soils or the
existing undocumented fills should be removed and replaced with engineered fill
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1557 91
Where footings are located adjacent to slopes or on slopes the footings should
be embedded to a depth to provide a minimum horizontal setback distance of at
least 8 feet measured from the edge of the footing to the face of the slope
Where footings are to be located adjacent to below grade structures or utilities
the footings should extend to a depth below an imaginary 45 degree plane
This requirement is necessary to reduce the potential for surcharging adjacent

Drainage
A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of permeable
material No 3B Fine gravel or ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation immediately
adjacent to the wall with a perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the
wall discharging to a discharge facility or weep holes A filter fabric such as
Mirafi 140N or equivalent should be placed between the drainage material and
the compacted backfill material The drainage material should extend from the
base of the wall to a height within 2 feet below finish grade The remaining
backfill should consist of an impervious capping of select non expansive soil
As an alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or
EnkaDrain may be used instead of the drainage material with filter fabric The
prefabricated drainage product should also hydraulically connect to a perforated
pipe at the base of the wall

Underground Utilities
For support of utility pipes we recommend that a granular bedding consisting of

utility pipes and the finished grade to facilitate construction of the pavement structure
and compaction of the trench backfill The backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on selected soil
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Four 1 inch ring swell tests were performed on selected samples to evaluate the
swelling potential of the subsoils The test results are summarized on Plate B 1
Three Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of the
tests are summarized on Plates B 2 and B 3 and also on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on
Plate B 4
Four sieve analyses tests were performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification The
test results are summarized on Plates B 2 and B 3 Graphic presentation of the grain
size distribution is presented on Plate B 5
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 91 were performed on
bulk samples of the soils retrieved to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationship The maximum dry densities and optimum moisture contents are
summarized on Plate B 3
Two California Bearing Ratio tests were performed on the bulk samples retrieved
to evaluate the pavement support characteristics The test results are summarized on
Plate B 3
