INTRODUCTION
The purpose of this exploration and testing was to provide subsurface
material and permeability data for use in the design of the proposed
seepage trench for the disposal of spent ocean water from the Natural
Energy Laboratory of Hawaii NELH and the leachfield for disposal of
sanitary wastes from the proposed beach restroom facilities at Hawaii
Ocean Science and Technology HOST Park
The initial exploration consisted of the drilling of five 5 test
wells and four 4 observation wells by a contract driller under the
supervision of our firm Upon completion of the drilling 4 inch well
casings were installed in the test wells and standpipe piezometers were
installed in the observation wells After the installation of the casings
and piezometers the wells were completed and developed as appropriate
Subsequent to the completion of the wells permeability testing was
performed to obtain data for the design of the disposal systems

SITE DESCRIPTIONS
The project sites are located in the area of Keahole Point in North
Kona on the west coast of the island of Hawaii
The disposal trench site is located to the north of the existing NEIH
facility in a relatively barren pahoehoe lava field The ground surface
is hummocky but in general has relatively low relief Surface
elevations vary between about 8 and 14 feet above Mean Sea level After
the completion of our initial exploration and testing the westernmost
portion of the site was partially rough graded by the placement and
levelling of stockpiled material from the nearby pump station excavation
W O Page 4
The proposed restroom facility site is located at the northern end of
Wawaloli Beach along the west side of the HOST NELH access road The
ground surface is relatively level with an elevation of approximately 6 0
feet above Mean Sea Level The southern portion of the site is covered
with coral beach sand and the northern portion is a pahoehoe lava flow
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is currently proposed to excavate a seepage trench for the
disposal of spent ocean water from the NEIH facility This spent ocean
water will be composed of a mixture of warm and cold water which has been
cycled through the ocean thermal energy conversion project and other
projects at the laboratory It is anticipated that the start up flows are
about 4 MGD while the ultimate rate of disposal will be on the order of 22
MGD
It is also proposed to construct a public restroom facility at
Wawaloli Beach This facility if constructed will utilize a septic tank
and leachfield for the treatment and disposal of sanitary wastes
GENERAL SUBSURFACE CONDITTONS
Geology
The project sites are located on the west flank of Hualalai about 5
miles south and west of the northwest rift zone Hualalai is one of the
five volcanoes which built the island of Hawaii and was last active in
approximately 1801 1803 The project sites are underlain by Hualalai
basalts which consist of interbedded pahoehoe and a a lava flows
originating from the northwest rift zone
At the project sites the basalts encountered were relatively fresh
and unweathered and are estimated to be of an age on the order of
hundreds of years or less The lavas exposed at and near the project
sites were generally of the pahoehoe form with some clinkery areas The
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O Page 5
exposed surfaces of the flows were hummocky and had numerous pressure
ridges These features appear to indicate that the lava at time of
deposition was moving slowly over a relatively level surface
Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the project sites were initially
explored by drilling four 4 test wells and four 4 observation wells a
the proposed disposal trench location and one 1 test well at the
location of the proposed restroom facility at Wawaloli Beach
Subsequently the vicinity of the proposed disposal trench was explored by
the drilling of five 5 additional borings Details of the field
explorations are presented in the appendices of this report
In general at the proposed trench site the drilled wells and
borings encountered a relatively thick flow unit of basalt which extended
to a depth of about 12 feet below the existing ground surface This flow
unit which was vesicular pahoehoe on the surface graded with depth to
relatively dense basalt which appears to indicate that the lava flow had
ponded
Below this flow unit the wells encountered gravel to cobble sized
basalt fragments possibly representing an a a clinker stratum This
stratum varied in thickness between 2 and 11 feet in the drilled wells
Below the clinker the wells encountered relatively dense basalt to the
maximum depths drilled Most of the additional borings encountered
similar conditions however the clinker stratum was essentially absent in
two of the borings

fifteen feet more than originally planned In these deeper borings a
cinder clinker stratum was encountered at about elevation 26 feet below
Mean Sea Level This generally conforms to findings of similar materials
at about the same elevation in the groundwater monitoring wells and in the
Seawater Pump Station excavation Therefore it appears that the seaward

Well Drilling and Testing
The test and observation wells were drilled utilizing truck mounted
rotary drilling equipment The test wells were drilled using a inch
diameter core barrel which provided samples for visual examination The
wells were then reamed to a minimum diameter of 6 inches and slotted Pvc
well casing was placed in the wells at appropriate depths to provide
representative tests of the different strata encountered After the
installation of the casing the wells were completed with sand packing
bentonite and grout seals and then developed by airlifting if possible
The observation wells were drilled using an NX core barrel which
provided samples for visual examination and comparison to the samples
obtained from the test wells Upon completion of the drilling the
observation wells were finished by installation of a piezometer sand
packing bentonite and grout seals
The test wells were tested by the injection of water and where
possible the withdrawal of water Wells were tested by either the
constant head method or the falling head method as appropriate For the
constant head tests injection and withdrawal rates were monitored by the
use of a paddle wheel flow sensor which gave direct reading of the flow in
gallons per minute In the falling head tests the wells were filled toa
datum level and the drop in water level was recorded with respect to time

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
our initial field exploration at the proposed NEIH trench disposal
site encountered a thin semi confined aquifer at an average depth of
approximately 12 feet below the ground surface This aquifer consists of
coarse basalt clinker and has an average thickness of 10 feet Our field
injection permeability testing indicates that this strata has an average
net permeability ranging from about 100 to approximately 450 feet per day
and that the overlying semi confining layer of dense basalt has an average
net permeability of about 2 feet per day
It should be noted that the test wells or observation wells did not
encounter significant cavities or voids although numerous large cavities
and cracks were observed on the surface of the exposed lavas at the
proposed disposal sites Any such gross permeability features should
enhance the efficiency of any disposal system if encountered during
construction
The additional five 5 borings did not indicate the presence of a
shallow more pervious formations within the general area explored
However during the additional exploration two 2 of the borings were
extended to greater depths than had been proposed to explore for a deeper
clinker stratum which had been found in the Pump Station excavation and in
those groundwater monitoring wells which had been installed prior to the
additional exploration efforts This stratum was encountered at about
elevation 26 feet below sea level which is consistent with its occurrence
in other locations around the facility
Based on our explorations and analyses it is our opinion that the
efficiency of the trench disposal system may be greatly enhanced by
creating a hydraulic connection between the upper and lower clinker strata
W O Page 8
At the proposed Wawaloli Beach restroom site our field exploration
encountered approximately 3 feet of beach sand overlying a layer
approximately 3 feet thick of relatively dense basalt underlain by a thin
1 foot stratum of basalt clinker which was in turn underlain by
relatively dense basalt to 8 feet below the existing ground surface the
maximum depth drilled at this site our field percolation permeability
testing indicates a net permeability in the basalt formation of about 5
feet per day most of which may be attributed to the clinker horizon
encountered at a depth of 6 feet
NELH Disposal Trench
Based upon our analyses using an average permeability k value of 175
feet per day as derived from our field testing we estimate that a 10 foot
deep trench with a base width of 10 feet is capable of infiltrating
approximately 16 000 gallons per day per lineal foot of trench with a
build up of 10 feet of head in the trench Therefore approximately 250
lineal feet of trench will be required to handle the anticipated start up
flows of approximately 4 0 million gallons per day This does not include
any allowance for clogging air entrainment surge flows or excessively
high ground water conditions
The simplified model also assumes a uniform geometry with the upper
dense basalt and the underlying clinker both being 10 feet in thickness
whereas the logs of the drilled test and observation wells indicate a more
complex subsurface geometry The actual thickness of the upper confining
layer encountered in the drilled borings varied from 11 to 13 feet below
the existing ground surface while the permeable clinker stratum varied from
2 to 11 feet in thickness in the drilled borings
A disposal trench excavated to a depth of 10 feet below the existing
ground surface may not completely penetrate the dense surface layer of
basalt Therefore the final trench design should call for a minimun
excavation depth of 15 feet
W O Page 9
Our analyses indicate that the thickness of the clinker stratum is the
limiting factor in surface disposal Therefore little advantage is gained
by increasing the width or depth of the trench
However our analyses indicate that if the permeability of the lower
layer of dense basalt between the two clinker layers is increased from an
estimated range on the order of 2 feet per day to a range on the order of
100 feet per day for a width of 15 feet to either side of the centerline of
the trench the efficiency of the trench will increase by about eighty
percent by utilizing the permeable lower clinker layer It is our opinion
that it is possible to affect such an increase by fracturing the dense rock
formation and opening the fractures Discussions with experienced blasting
operators indicate that this fracturing and opening of the lower dense
stratum could be accomplished by controlled blasting This scheme is
schematically illustrated on Plate 5
The calculated injection permeability of the upper clinker layer
ranged from 70 to 450 feet per day This is well within the range of
variability expected due to variations in the stratum as well as testing
limitations The lower clinker layer was not tested however we estimate
that its permeability would be on the same order of magnitude Based on
these assumptions we estimate that a 250 foot long trench could dispose of
about Due to budget considerations it is our understanding that
it may be desirable to construct the trench incrementally as the discharge
flows increase A rough cost estimate and construction schedule along with
the estimated disposal capacities are presented in Appendix C
We recommend that a full scale testing program be developed and
implemented during the construction of the trench es to evaluate the
actual disposal capacity This program should consist of step testing the
absorption rate of the trench es during excavation i e introducing
water into the trench es at different rates and observing the build up of
the water level in the trench es with respect to time This testing
program will provide the actual capacity of the trench es as constructed
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 10
and provide data for the design of future expansion of the disposal
system The testing could also allow testing of the potential improvement
in permeability achieved by blasting or otherwise fracturing the dense
basalt underlying the clinker stratum
If multiple trenches are to be constructed on the site we recommend
that they should be separated center to center by a minimum distance of 5
times the nominal trench width to reduce the interference between the flow
regimes induced by the individual trenches
It is also suggested that the disposal system include a one hundred
per cent backup capacity This capacity could be a redundant system in the
form of a completely separate disposal component e g additional
trench es injection wells etc The use of separate components will
allow for periodic maintenance and will also provide lead time in the event
of sudden failure of one of the systems
Construction Considerations
The drilling data as well as previous experience indicate that hard
excavation utilizing hoe rams and probably blasting will be needed for
efficient excavation Blasting will also be needed to fracture the
underlying stratum of dense basalt
The rock formation is generally hard and may be cut at a slope ratio
of about H V
Dewatering of the excavation will not be required However it is
anticipated that some diving observations may be required to verify that
the excavation has reached the permeable clinker layer
It is our understanding that the disposal trenches will be left open
and that a high fence will be constructed around the facility for safety
W O Page 11
Future Expansion of the Trench System
It is anticipated that the initial trench disposal system may need to
be expanded incrementally as the flows through the facility increase
and as the disposal formation clogs due to blockage of the formation
by suspended solids loading biological accumulation and entrained
gases
As previously discussed the disposal system should also incorporate a
back up system It is our opinion that the back up system could
consist of additional trenches connected to the primary disposal
system by headers and gates This design would allow for the
expansion of the system as shown in the example on Plate 6
Environmental Monitoring
Due to the large quantity of water which will be disposed of through
the trench system it is our opinion that a groundwater monitoring
program be developed and implemented specifically for the trench
system This program should consist of a series of monitoring wells
placed both upgradient and downgradient of the disposal system The
actual monitoring of these wells could be carried out along with the
monitoring already proposed for the entire HOST NELH complex
Wawaloli Beach Restroom Facility
Based upon our field permeability testing the basalt formation in the
vicinity of the proposed restroom facility has an average net permeability
of 5 feet per day However leachfields for septic systems are normally
designed utilizing the rate of fall occurring in a falling head test in a
drilled boring This rate of fall is expressed in terms of minutes per
inch Our field testing yielded an average rate of fall of 5 inches per
minute or 0 2 minute per inch This rate exceeds the highest rates used
for septic system design in Public Health Service Publication No 526
Manual of Septic Tank Practice In this publication it is recommended
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 12
that the maximm rate of application of sewage to a leaching system should
not exceed five 5 gallons per day per square foot of trench bottom It
is our recommendation that this guideline should be adhered to in the
design of the proposed leachfield
Tt is our further recommendation that the trenches for the proposed
leachfield be overexcavated to reach the ciinker material at a depth of
approximately 6 feet below the existing ground surface and that the
overexcavated trench be backfilled with clean granular material such as
3B Fine This overexcavation will allow more rapid seepage of the
effluent and the length of the trench may be reduced in accordance with the
guidelines presented in the Manual of Septic Tank Practice
Due to the close proximity of the proposed restroom facility to the
beach and the relatively high public usage of the beach we suggest that
the septic system effluent be disinfected prior to its disposal through the
leachfield

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based
in part upon information obtained from field data points such as borings
probes and or observation wells Variations of conditions between the
field data points may occur and the nature and extent of these variations
may not become evident until construction If variations then appear
evident it will be necessary to re evaluate the recommendations given in
this report
The locations of the field data points in the initial exploration
were approximately determined by taping from points surveyed by R M
Towill Corporation Elevations of the surveyed points were provided by
the surveyors and elevations of the field data points were set by hand
level at the time of our exploration The physical location and elevation
of these field data points should be considered accurate only to the
degree implied by the method used
The location of the field data points in the additional exploration
were approximately determined by taping from points shown on the Area
Plan Plate 3 provided by the Department of Accounting and General
Services Elevations of the field data points were estimated from the
same plan The physical location and elevation of these field data points
should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the method
used
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the
field data points depict the approximate boundaries between soil types
and as such may denote a gradual transition The strata lines shown on
profiles or cross sections are based upon interpolation between field data
points and may not represent actual subsurface conditions

Gravity injection tests were performed in 3 test wells installed to
evaluate the permeability of the subsurface Pumping withdrawal tests
were performed in the same 3 tests wells to further evaluate the
subsurface permeability Falling head percolation tests were performed in
two 2 wells to evaluate the permeability of the shallower subsurface
formation
eam aete t At
Following the construction of the test wells preliminary percolation
testing was performed on the wells The wells were first developed by
airlifting with an air compressor After development gravity injection
Constant Head tests were performed using water from a nearby aquaculture
facility These test data are presented on Plates B 1 through B 3
Pumping withdrawal tests were performed by pumping the wells using a
2 inch or 3 inch centrifugal pump and monitoring drawdown These data are
shown on Plates B 4 through B 6
Percolation Falling Head tests were performed in the 2 shallower
test wells by filling the well to a known level and measuring the fall in
water level with respect to time These data are shown on Plates B 7 and
B 8

Task 2 Site Grading
This task consists of the grading of about 4 0 acres of raw lava land
to the north of the existing NELH compound The site grading should be
performed concurrently with the blasting and excavation work The
excavation work will provide some material for fill
The estimate is based on the assumptions that the earthwork will be
balanced i e no import or export of material and that hard ripping and
blasting will be required It is estimated that approximately 10 000
cubic yards of grading will be required
Estimated Cost 9 to 18 cubic yard totalling 90 000 to 180 000
Task 3 Drill Blast for Subsurface Fracturing
This task consists of the drilling loading and shooting of blast
holes to fracture the dense basalts below the trench site to enhance the
subsurface permeability The blasting will also fracture the upper
basalts to allow conventional excavation of the disposal trench thereby
reducing excavation costs In order to assure adequate coverage of the
fracturing the shot pattern should extend beyond the limits of the trench
excavation area

The estimate is based on the assumption that the contractor will use
a 6 foot on center spacing of shot holes that the shot hole will be cased
and stemmed and that water gel explosive will be required It is
estimated that an area of 350 feet long by 50 feet wide by 50 feet deep
will be blasted totalling 32 600 cubic yards
Estimated Cost 6 to 8 cibic yard totalling 196 000 to 262 000
For comparison Hard excavation of the trench without blasting
would cost about 30 to 50 cubic yard
Task 4 Excavation of Disposal Trench
This task consists of excavating the finished disposal trench The
blasting will allow conventional excavation and the site grading will
provide a disposal site for the excavated material
The estimate is based on conventional excavation on site disposal
and a trench that will be 300 feet long by 25 feet wide at the top and 15
feet wide at the bottom by 15 feet deep totalling 4 200 cubic yards
Estimated Cost 8 to 10 cubic yard totalling 34 000 to 42 000
Task 5 Fusion of 600 L F of 48 HDPE Pipe
This task consists of the mobilization demobilization of the 48
fusion machine and technician from Dupont Canada Inc to the project site
and fusing the 48 pipe for installation in the pipe trench
Included in the estimate are the rental of the machine and
technician living transportation expenses and contractor support
Estimated Cost 40 000
Task 6 Install 48 HDPE pipe
This task consists of the excavation of 600 L F of trench for the
bedding placement and backfill of a 48 HDPE pipe from NEIH to the
trench This task should be completed by the initial trench excavation to
allow testing of the trench to determine its capacity
The estimate assumes that the pipe will be provided joined by
others that hard excavation will be required and that on site back fill
material will be used The estimated amount of excavation is 800 cubic
yards The estimate does not include connection of the pipe to the NEIH
nor construction of a weir at the disposal trench
Estimated Cost 30 to 50 cubic yard totalling 24 000 to 40 000

Task 7 Trench Testing and Standby Time
This task consists of the contractor stopping construction activity
during trench capacity testing and assisting in the testing
It is assumed that this will require about one week No data is
available for estimation Therefore an estimated cost is given
Estimated Cost 15 000 week
