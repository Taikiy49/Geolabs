PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The proposed Makena U-1 Ranch project [TMK (2) 2-1-008: 108, 090] is in the 
Makena area in the District of Makawao on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project location 
and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
The current master plan generally consists of mass grading for infrastructure 
development for a new 37-lot agricultural subdivision. Based on the preliminary plans 
provided, mass grading consisting of cuts of up to about 20 feet in depth and fills of up to 
about 24 feet in height may be required to achieve design grades.   
The project will be designed with cut and fill slope inclinations of two horizontal to 
one vertical (2H:1V).  In accordance with the requirements of the County of Maui, a slope 
hazard assessment will need to be conducted to evaluate whether potential slope and 
rockfall hazards may exist that will require mitigation and/or treatment measures.

SITE RECONNAISSANCE 
A site reconnaissance was conducted on July 16, 2021, to obtain a visual overview 
of the existing site conditions with respect to the occurrence of potential slope hazards such 
as existing slope instability and potential rockfall conditions. Our reconnaissance included 
visual observations of the existing ground surface within the proposed development area. 
Our geologist performed the site reconnaissance under dry weather and ground conditions 
using the preliminary grading plan for location reference.  
The primary purpose for the site reconnaissance was to obtain an overview of the 
existing ground surface conditions within the project site. We recorded photographs of the 
existing ground conditions, including typical surface soil and rock exposures observed at the 
project site. Selected site condition photographs are presented in Appendix A.  The 
approximate locations of the photographs taken are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
The project site is located in the Makena area on the Island of Maui, Hawaii.  The 
project site encompasses an area of over 700 acres.  Based on our field observations, the 
project area consists of undeveloped open land generally covered with grasses, with some 
scattered shrubs and kiawe trees in localized areas.  Several unpaved access roads 
traverse the project site.  Based on our field observations and the preliminary grading plans 
provided, the topography at the site is generally slightly sloping down from the east to west

In most areas, dry silty volcanic ash soils were observed at the ground surface.  
Based on our observations, it appears that the surface volcanic ash layer is generally thin

formations were observed at the existing ground surface throughout the project site.  The

Based on our site reconnaissance, no visible signs of earth movement and/or slope 
instability were observed at the existing ground surface.  In addition, we did not observe 
any potential rockfall source materials on the existing hillside surrounding the proposed 
agricultural subdivision site. Therefore, we believe the risk for potential rockfall hazard 
emanating from off-site with loose boulders rolling down the existing hillside and into the 
subject development is very low to non-existent. 
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our site reconnaissance, visible evidence of active surface slope instability 
or visible evidence of past landslide/debris flow deposits was not observed at the project 
site. 
It is anticipated that future ground disturbances at the project site, such as grubbing 
of existing trees and shrubbery and other excavation activity, have the potential to generate 
additional loose rocks that could present potential rockfall and nuisance erosion and 
accumulation of loose rocks.  Detailed discussions of our slope hazard assessment are 
presented in the following subsections of this report. 
Potential Rockfall Hazards 
Based on our observations, it is our professional opinion that the very limited 
occurrence of loose (un-rooted) smaller dimension surface rocks at the adjacent hillside and 
within the development site should not present a dangerous falling rock hazard for the 
agricultural subdivision development. 
However, the existing surface rocks and additional rocks that may be exposed by 
construction development of the site do have the potential to ravel from exposed and 
unvegetated slopes to create nuisance-type accumulations of rocky debris on the lower 
slope. Therefore, to reduce the potential for rockfall and nuisance-type rock raveling and 
lower slope accumulation, we recommend the following: 
New cut slope should be graded no steeper than 2H:1V inclination. 
Non-embedded (unrooted) surface rocks including loose cobbles and 
boulders greater than 6 inches in dimension should be removed from the 
slope surface prior to placing erosion control materials and permanent 
vegetation.

We believe that the potential for rockfall hazard occurring from the faces of the new

Global Stability of New Cut Slopes 
We understand that new cut slopes up to 20 feet in vertical height at an inclination of 
2H:1V are planned for the project construction. Based on the anticipated subsurface 
conditions at the project, we envision that the new cut slopes planned for the project would 
be underlain by a thin layer of surface soils over basalt formation.  A slope stability analysis 
was performed based on this anticipated subsurface condition and the proposed cut slope 
design described above. Based on the results of our engineering analyses, it is our opinion 
that the new cut slopes planned for the project would have a factor of safety against global 
slope instability greater than 1.5, the normal standard for a long-term condition. 
LIMITATIONS 
The findings and recommendations submitted herein are based upon information 
obtained from visual site observations only. Variation in the anticipated surface and 
subsurface conditions between our observation points may occur, and the nature and extent 
of these variations may not become evident until additional field exploration or construction 
is underway.  If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the findings 
and recommendations provided herein. 
It should be noted that slopes composed of rock materials deteriorate with the 
passage of time due to natural weathering processes, wet-dry and hot-cold cycles, and 
erosion conditions.  Due to the inherent deterioration of rock slopes resulting from natural 
processes (weathering, wet-dry and hot-cold cycles, and erosion conditions), the potential 
for rockfall hazard at any site changes with the passage of time.  Therefore, the findings and 
recommendations contained in this letter report may be used only within a reasonable time 
from the date of issuance of this letter report.  Land use, site conditions, and/or other factors 
may change with the passage of time.  Therefore, additional work to evaluate the 
applicability of the findings and preliminary recommendations contained in this letter report 
due to changes with the passage of time will be required.  Finally, there are no guarantees 
in the professional engineering and architectural design fields with respect to potential 
rockfall hazards due in large part to the unpredictable nature of rockfall activity, which is 
affected by many external variables, including natural and man-induced causes.
