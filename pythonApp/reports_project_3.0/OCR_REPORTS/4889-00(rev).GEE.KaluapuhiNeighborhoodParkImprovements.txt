GEOTECHNICAL ENGINEERING EXPLORATION
KALUAPUHI NEIGHBORHOOD PARK IMPROVEMENTS
Dear Mr Lau
In accordance with our proposal dated May 31 2001 we have performed a
geotechnical engineering exploration for the Kaluapuhi Neighborhood Park Improvements
project in the Kaneohe area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii This letter report summarizes
the findings from our field exploration at the project site and presents our findings and
recommendations for the proposed project These findings and recommendations are
intended for design input only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
letter report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located at Kaluapuhi Neighborhood Park in the Kaneohe area
on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 The improvement project consists of the
demolition and replacement of the existing basketball and volleyball courts and
construction of a new parking area
Based on the information provided we understand that the existing basketball and
volleyball courts have cracks along the edges of the play courts The cracks are present
along the northern and southern sides of the courts Based on our observations the play
courts appear to be located on an embankment fill We understand that a new concrete
rubble masonry CRM wall will be constructed along the north west and south sides of
the basketball court
In addition we understand that a new parking area and driveway are planned as
part of the improvement project The parking area will be located to the southwest side of
the play courts The driveway is to be located at the end of Popoki Street We envision
that the new parking area and driveway will consist of concrete pavements Based on the
existing grades at the project site we anticipate minor cuts and fills will be required to
achieve the design finished grades

PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering exploration was to obtain an overview
of the surface and subsurface conditions at the site The subsurface information obtained
was utilized to develop a soil rock data set for the formulation of geotechnical engineering
recommendations pertaining to the design of retaining walls site preparation and
pavements only To accomplish this we conducted an exploration program consisting of
the following tasks and work efforts
1 Mobilization and demobilization of a truck mounted drill rig and
two operators to and from the project site
2 Drilling and sampling of four borings extending to depths of about 11 5 to
21 5 feet below the existing ground surface
3 Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the borings by a field
engineer from our firm
4 Laboratory testing of selected samples obtained during the field exploration
as an aid in classifying the materials encountered and evaluating their
engineering properties
5 Analyses of the field and laboratory data for the formulation of geotechnical
engineering recommendations pertaining to the design of retaining walls
site preparation and pavements for the proposed project
6 Preparation of this letter report summarizing our work on the project and
presenting our findings and recommendations
7 Coordination of our overall work on the project by an engineer from our firm
8 Quality assurance of our work and client design team consultation by a
principal engineer from our firm
9 Miscellaneous work efforts such as drafting word processing and clerical
support
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration and Logs of Borings are presented on
Plates 3 through 7 of this letter report The results of the laboratory tests performed on
selected soil samples are presented on Plates 8 through 12

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is located at the Kaluapuhi Neighborhood Park in the Kaneohe
area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project site is located near the vicinity of Popoki
Street The location of the project site is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on our observations and the topographic map provided the basketball
courts are located on an embankment fill area As built drawings for the park indicate that
a subdrainage system was installed below the embankment fill The existing ground
surface elevations range from about 109 to 114 feet Mean Sea Level MSL The slope
on the west side of the basketball court has an inclination of about four horizontal to
one vertical 4H 1V and is about 8 feet in height The slope on the north side of the
basketball court has an inclination of about 3H 1V and is about 8 feet in height The slope
on the south side of the basketball court has an inclination of about 4H 1V and is about
3 feet in height At the time of our exploration 1 to 2 inch wide cracks were noted along
the southwest and north sides of the basketball courts The cracks were more substantial
along the northeast side of the basketball courts The cracks were generally 2 feet from
the edges Based on our observations the existing ground near the edge of the
basketball courts appear to have negative drainage and ponding water was observed near
the edges of the courts in localized areas
The volleyball court and the new parking lot and driveway areas appear to be
relatively level with existing ground surface elevations ranging from about 106 to
109 feet MSL at the new parking area and about 113 to 114 feet MSL at the volleyball
courts No distress was observed at the volleyball courts at the time of our exploration
FIELD EXPLORATION
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling four borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 extending to depths of about 11 5 to 21 5 feet
below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers The approximate locations of the
borings drilled at the site are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on the Boring Log Legend Plate 3 Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings
Plates 4 through 7
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 3 inch outside diameter OD Modified California sampler with a
140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the

LABORATORY TESTING
Selected soil samples from the field exploration were tested in the laboratory with
an emphasis on evaluating the moisture density plasticity and strength characteristics
The following laboratory tests were performed on selected soil samples obtained from the
field exploration
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Three one inch ring swell tests were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results of the tests are
summarized on Plate 8
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic presentations of the test results
are provided on Plate 9
One direct shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected sample to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested Results of the direct
shear test are presented on Plate 10
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR test was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the
soils The test was performed in accordance with ASTM D 1883 and the test results are
presented on Plate 11
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The results of the test are presented on Plate 12

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration at the basketball courts generally encountered fill materials
consisting of stiff silty clay extending to depths of about 10 feet below the existing ground
surface Seams of soft silty clay were encountered in our boring drilled on the north side
of the basketball courts at a depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface The
fill materials were underlain by residual soils consisting of stiff to very stiff silty clay and
clayey silt extending to the maximum depths explored of about 21 5 feet below the existing
ground surface
Our field exploration at the volleyball courts generally encountered fill materials
consisting of stiff silty clay extending to a depth of about 1 foot below the existing ground
surface The fill materials were underlain by residual soils consisting of very stiff to hard
silty clay extending to the maximum depth explored of about 11 5 feet below the existing
ground surface
Our field exploration at the new parking area generally encountered fill materials
consisting of medium dense silty gravel about 0 5 feet thick The fill materials were
underlain by residual soils consisting of very stiff silty clay extending to the maximum
depth explored of about 11 5 feet below the existing ground surface
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings at the time of our field exploration
However groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall seasonal precipitation
surface water runoff and other factors
FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Detailed discussion of our geotechnical engineering recommendations for design of
retaining walls site preparation play court pavements and parking pavements are
presented in the following sections of this letter report
Retaining Walls
We understand that retaining walls are planned for the project The following
general guidelines may be used for preliminary design of retaining walls
Retaining Wall Foundations
In general we believe that retaining walls may be supported on shallow continuous
footings An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot psf
may be used for footings bearing on the in situ soils This value may be increased
by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind and or seismic
forces The bottom of footing elevations should be recompacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction

Higuchi Associates Inc Page 6

Based on our field exploration seams of soft soils were encountered in the boring
drilled on the north side of the basketball courts Soft and or loose soils
encountered at the bottom of footing elevations should be over excavated to the
underlying firm or dense materials The resulting over excavation should be
backfilled with select granular fill materials compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction
The foundations should have a minimum width of 18 inches and should be
embedded a minimum depth of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade
The as built drawings for the park indicate that a subdrainage system was
installed below the site The invert of the subdrain pipe was designed at about
elevation 106 0 feet near the proposed retaining wall The new retaining wall
footing should be placed above the subdrain pipe to avoid interference with the
subdrain system For sloping ground conditions the footing should extend deeper
to obtain a minimum 6 foot setback distance measured horizontally from the
outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope Foundations located next to
utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a one horizontal to one
vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the
utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is
necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional
structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
Lateral loads acting on the wall may be resisted by frictional resistance developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A
coefficient of friction of 0 85 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted
in situ soils or engineered fills Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be
estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot
of depth pcf This assumes that the soil around footings is well compacted The
passive pressure should be reduced for foundations located on slopes Unless
covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of
soil should be neglected

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system should consist of about 1 cubic foot of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
wrapped with non woven filter fabric at each of the weephole locations The
weepholes should be spaced not more than 6 feet apart Unless covered by
concrete or asphaltic concrete the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of
relatively impervious materials such as the on site soils to reduce the potential for
significant water infiltration behind the walls

Exterior Concrete Walkways
We understand that concrete walkways are planned for the project site Based on
our laboratory test results the silty and clayey soils encountered in our borings exhibit a
moderate to high potential for shrinking and swelling when subjected to fluctuations in
moisture content To reduce the potential for future distress to the concrete walkways we
recommend that the slabs be supported on a minimum 6 inch thick layer of non expansive
select granular fill or select borrow subbase compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction The concrete walkway subgrades should be prepared in accordance
with the recommendations presented in the Site Preparation section of this letter report
The select granular fill or select borrow subbase should be compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction To reduce the potential for substantial
shrinkage cracks in the slabs crack control joints should be provided at intervals equal to
the width of the walkways with expansion joints provided at right angle intersections
The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at
least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade Areas adjacent to the slab
should be backfilled tightly against the slab edges with the on site soils and graded to
divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the slabs
Site Preparation
Based on the existing ground surface elevations we envision that the site grading
work for the proposed project will generally consist of minor cuts and fills to achieve the
design finished grades
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared Debris deleterious materials and other unsuitable materials should
be removed and disposed of off site Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing
below areas designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over excavated to
expose firm material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with engineered fill
The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site Contract
documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and
engineered fill placement to account for variations in the over excavation quantities
After clearing and demolition the play court area should be excavated down to the
bottom of the aggregate base course layer The areas to receive fill and finished
subgrades in cut areas should be proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton drum roller for a
minimum of 8 passes to help in detecting soft and or loose areas If soft and or loose
areas are detected the areas should be over excavated to the underlying firm or dense
materials The resulting over excavation should be backfilled with compacted select
granular fill material

The on site soils generated from the cut areas should not be reused as a source of
fill material Imported fill material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no
particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and
30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory
CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of one percent or less when
tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 Select borrow subbase and
aggregate base course materials should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates and
should conform to Sections 30 and 31 as specified in the Standard Specifications for
Public Works Construction 1986 Imported fill materials should be tested by Geolabs for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the
intended use
General fill materials should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Imported fill materials
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts
not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction Select borrow subbase and aggregate base course required under
the pavement areas should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum moisture is the
water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or
other types of acceptable compaction equipment Water tamping jetting or ponding
should not be allowed to compact the on site silty and clayey soils
Site grading operations should be observed by a representative from Geolabs It
is important that a representative from our office observe the site grading operation to
evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the scarification
process and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those encountered
in our field exploration

The subgrade soils should be proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton drum roller for a

As previously mentioned cracks were observed near the edges of the play court
pavements Based on our observations the near surface soils around the existing play
court edges were soft and wet In addition the existing grades around the edges of the
play court pavements were sloping towards the pavements We believe that the softening
of the soils around the existing pavement edges is caused by ponding of water due to the
negative drainage at the pavement edges The cracks observed at the play court
pavement edges may be caused by the shrinking and swelling of the near surface soils
due to moisture fluctuations
Therefore we recommend that the finished grades along the edges of the new play
courts should be sloped to shed water away from pavements and to reduce the potential
for ponding Excessive landscape watering near the play courts should also be avoided
These requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent loss of strength The pavement excavations should be properly backfilled
against the edges immediately after construction of the pavements to reduce water
infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be
maintained to drain all surface run off away from the play court pavements
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to
carry surface water off the pavement Surface water ponding should not be allowed on or
near the pavement areas during or after construction
