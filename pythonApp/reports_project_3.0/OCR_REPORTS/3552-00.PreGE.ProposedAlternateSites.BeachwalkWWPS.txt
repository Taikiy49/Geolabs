Background Information
Based on the information provided the main facility that houses the Beachwalk
Wastewater Pump Station WWPS was constructed in 1966 The Beachwalk WWPS
serves Waikiki and other surrounding areas with 70 percent of its flow originating in
Waikiki Discharge from the Beachwalk WWWPS is conveyed to the Ala Moana Park trunk
sewer
The existing Beachwalk WWPS is a one story structure with a below grade level
extending to depths of approximately 30 to 35 feet below the existing ground surface We
understand that the facility has exhibited significant building and foundation problems
such as cracking tilting and settlement of the building walls and floor slabs etc
Therefore it was desired to investigate the condition of the existing Beachwalk WWPS in
regards to the feasibility of rehabilitation of the existing pump station A letter report
entitled Geotechnical Engineering Evaluation Existing Beachwalk Wastewater Pump
Station Waikiki Oahu Hawaii dated March 1 1996 has been prepared by our office to
address our field observations and findings and presents our geotechnical engineering
evaluation derived from our analyses of the existing Beachwalk WWPS

As part of the project scope of work it is also desired to evaluate the feasibility of
constructing a new WWPS to replace the existing Beachwalk WWPS The new pump
station will be sized to handle future flows from the Waikiki and other surrounding areas
Currently there are three alternative sites under consideration for the proposed new
WWPS which include the following
1 Next to the Ala Wai Golf Course Maintenance Facility
2 At the Kobayashi Hill within the Ala Wai Golf Course
3 Within the parking lot of the existing Beachwalk WWPS
At this time it is desired to conduct a preliminary geotechnical engineering
evaluation of each of the three sites to aid in the selection of the most feasible location
Our preliminary geotechnical evaluation will include consideration of the substantial
underground footprint of the structure as well as the potential effects of site construction
and dewatering on surrounding structures Other considerations would be given to
foundation types bearing stratum and probable shoring systems for the proposed
excavations which are anticipated to be on the order of about 45 to 50 feet below the
existing ground surface
Preliminary Findings and Evaluations
We believe that the anticipated subsurface soil conditions at the three alternative
sites will pose significant geotechnical engineering considerations for the design and
construction of the new WWPS The significant subsurface soil conditions include the
presence of soft and or loose subsoil conditions hard coral ledges a_ shallow
groundwater table and a corrosive subsurface environment In addition the potential for
presence of oil and other hydrocarbon contaminated soils and or groundwater is a major
consideration for the design and construction of the new WWPS
Based on our knowledge of the area and our experience with construction
difficulties on similar sites we anticipate the following four principal geotechnical
engineering considerations for the proposed sites

Alternative 1 Maintenance Facility Site
Alternative Site No 1 is located approximately between the existing Ala Wai Golf
Course Maintenance Facility and the Manoa Palolo Drainage Canal at the northwest
corner of the Ala Wai Golf Course Our review of available soils and geologic data in
the vicinity of this alternative site suggest that the area may be underlain by a surficial
fill crust of about 5 to 10 feet thick The surficial fill crust is generally underlain by loose
lagoonal deposits varying from about 10 to 25 feet in thickness Dense coralline
materials underlying the loose lagoonal deposits may be anticipated at an elevation of
about 10 to 25 feet Mean Sea Level MSL Because of the proximity of the proposed
site to the drainage channel it should be noted that the vertical extent of the loose
lagoonal deposits at the proposed alternative site could vary substantially from its
vicinity Shallow groundwater table at depths of about 10 to 15 feet below the existing
ground surface may be anticipated at this Alternative 1 site
Foundation Support
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions described above it appears that
excavations for the below grade level of the pump station building will likely encounter
dense coralline materials at the bottom of the basement level of the proposed pump
station building Therefore we believe that the below grade level of the pump station
building may be supported by a mat foundation bearing on the dense in situ soils
Based on the surficial fill crust anticipated at this alternative site we believe that
the on grade portion of the pump station building may be supported by shallow
foundations without significant footing settlements Special attention should be given to
the structural connections along the intersection between the on grade and below grade
portion of the building to reduce the potential for cracking due to differing foundation soil
conditions
Underpinning
Construction of the proposed basement levels for the new pump station building
will likely entail relatively deep excavation and dewatering of the excavation In general
excavation and dewatering operations may pose significant impact on the surrounding
structures especially those supported by shallow or mat foundations
Based on the proposed location of the new pump station building relative to the
existing golf course maintenance building and surrounding structures we believe that the
potential impact on the existing structures resulting from the project construction should
be minimal provided that the excavation shoring system and dewatering operations are
properly designed and implemented We believe that underpinning of the existing
structures would not be required under these conditions

Shoring
Based on the information provided the ground floor and basement level of the new
pump station will have finish floor elevations of approximately 12 5 and 32 50 feet MSL
respectively Therefore excavations on the order of up to about 50 feet below the
existing ground surface may be required for the project construction
Because of the loose lagoonal deposits anticipated at this alternative site shoring
of the deep excavations will be a major consideration during project construction The
shoring system will serve to reduce the potential for cave ins of the loose lagoonal
deposits as well as a cut off wall for the dewatering operations The use of conventional
sheet pile shoring may not be feasible due to the potential presence of hard coral ledges
that may be encountered at shallow depths which may result in penetration refusal
during installation Therefore other types of shoring methods such as mixed in place soil
wall or jet grouted sheet pile wall would need to be considered and evaluated based on
the actual field conditions encountered at the proposed site
Dewatering
Dewatering of the relatively deep excavation will be necessary for the proposed
construction Due to the relatively shallow groundwater table the permeable coralline
deposits at the proposed WWPS site and the proximity to the Manoa Palolo Drainage
Canal large quantities of water will need to be removed or displaced from the excavation
Based on our experience on similar projects we believe that improvement of the existing
ground conditions by jet grouting should be seriously considered to aid in the dewatering
and construction of this project
Jet grouting is a ground modification method in which thin high pressure jets of
cement grout are discharged sideways into the borehole wall to simultaneously excavate
and then mix with the soil The result is a column of modified soil with low permeability
and improved strength In this construction approach the jet grouting operation is
conducted after the installation of the sheet pile shoring system Jet grouting the existing
ground outside the sheet pile wall and below the excavation bottom will create an in situ
cemented geometry of soil that will serve to reduce the amount of water infiltration into the
excavation In addition the grouted ground below the excavation bottom will provide an
improved bearing surface for the foundation support and construction of the proposed
pump station building

Alternative 3 Beachwalk Site
Alternative Site No 3 is located to the east of the existing pump station building
within the parking lot of the existing Beachwalk WWPS The project area is generally
blanketed by a fill layer of approximately 6 to 8 feet thick placed as part of the overall
development in this area The surficial fill is generally underlain by alluvial soils consisting
of loose silty sands with coral fragments extending to a depth of about 45 feet below the
existing ground surface These materials were deposited in a lagoonal or shallow
off shore platform environment which existed behind the outer barrier reefs The
lagoonal deposits are underlain by an interbedded sequence of hard coral and layers of
coral sand developed during periods of changing sea levels Shallow groundwater table
at a depth of approximately 5 feet below the existing ground surface may be anticipated
at the project site
Foundation Support
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions described above it appears that
excavations for the below grade level of the pump station building will likely encounter an
interbedded sequence of hard coral and layers of coral sand Therefore we believe that
the below grade level of the pump station building may be supported by mat foundation
bearing on the in situ soils Depending on the thickness of the surficial fill crust and the
structural loads the on grade portion of the pump station building may be supported by
shallow foundations without significant footing settlements Special attention should be
given to the structural connections along the intersection between the on grade and
below grade portion of the building to reduce the potential for cracking due to differing
foundation soil conditions

Underpinning
The existing deep below grade level and aged underground piping of the existing
Beachwalk WWPS would pose significant design and construction limitations on the new
pump station that may be developed at the subject site In general in order to keep the
existing pump station functional and operational during and after construction of the new
pump station at this site we believe that future development involving basement
construction at the site would have significant cost impacts on the project construction
Construction of proposed basement levels for the new pump station building will
likely entail relatively deep excavation and dewatering of the excavation Excavation and
dewatering operations will have a significant impact on the existing VWWPS which is
essentially a floating structure on the underlying subsoils Both these operations will likely
cause settlement of the WWPS and the associated piping network Therefore we
anticipate that underpinning of the existing WWPS and possibly the piping network would
be required in order to stabilize the existing structure and transfer the building load to a
more competent bearing stratum below the bottom of the proposed basement level of the
new development Underpinning of the structure involves installing a new foundation
system for the structure by extending the foundation to a deeper and competent bearing
stratum
Due to the relatively shallow groundwater table permeable coral formation at the
existing Beachwalk WWPS site and proximity to the Pacific Ocean large quantities of
water will need to be removed or displaced from the excavation Since the existing
WPS is essentially floating on the underlying subsoils any dewatering operations in its
vicinity will likely cause subsoil consolidation resulting in potential foundation settlement of
the existing WPS unless the foundation of the WPS is underpinned
In order to alleviate some of the potential problems discussed above shoring of
the deep excavation will be a major consideration for any planned basement construction
of the new development The shoring system will serve to reduce the potential impacts
on the existing WPS due to undermining from excavations and foundation settlement
from dewatering operations
