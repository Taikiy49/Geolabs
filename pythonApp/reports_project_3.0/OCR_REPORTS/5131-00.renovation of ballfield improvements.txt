conducted for the Renovation of Ballfield Improvement project located within the
existing Kahaluu Community Park in the Kahaluu area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii

presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two separate submarine shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano
Waianae is estimated to be middle to late Pliocene in age 2 7 to 3 4 million years ago
and Koolau Shield is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age 2 2 to
2 5 million years ago After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These more recent
volcanic eruptions are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the worldwide glaciers grew and advanced less
water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present
stands of the sea When the glaciers melted and receded a larger volume of water
became available such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level The
larger glacio eustatic sea level fluctuations ranged from about 250 feet above the
present sea level to about 400 feet below the present sea level Therefore the island
shorelines have transgressed and regressed in response to the sea level fluctuations
The processes of terrestrial erosion and the associated depositional
environments were affected by these glacio eustatic sea level fluctuations When the
sea level was low the erosional base level was correspondingly lower and valleys were
carved to depths below the present sea level As a result terrestrial sediments were
carried farther seaward and deposited in what had been marine environments When
the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations In addition the higher sea level allowed for the
deposition of marine deposits such as coral and limestone in environments that had
been covered with terrestrial soils and rock

In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock mass from the slopes and movement of the material
downslope toward the axis of the valley by gravity to form colluvial soil deposits Once
the colluvium reaches streams in the valley alluvial processes become dominant and
the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium Large areas of alluvium can
be found in the lowland areas and areas bordering streams
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the volcanic dome of
the Koolau The headward erosion of these valleys reached the easily eroded materials
in the caldera complex and carved broad amphitheater headed valleys The heads of
these valleys coalesced to form a long steep cliff or pali When the sea level rose
again sediments accumulated in the valleys In the vicinity of the project site these
sediments accumulated in a marshy low energy environment along the shores of
Kaneohe Bay and Kahaluu Pond
The project site is situated in the middle portion of Waihee Valley When this
area was developed man made fills were placed over the soft marshy sediments and
the stiff alluvial deposit to reclaim the land

In general our field exploration encountered stiff surface fills and alluvium
overlying marsh deposits consisting of soft clayey soils extending to the maximum depth
explored of approximately 29 feet at the project site Groundwater was encountered at
depths ranging between about 14 5 and 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface in

In order to develop the required bearing and lateral load resistances for the new
light pole structures we recommend that drilled shaft foundations embedded into the
upper fill alluvium and underlying soft marsh deposits be used to support the proposed
lighting system Based on the anticipated structural loads and the subsoil conditions
encountered at the project site we recommend that drilled shafts with a minimum
diameter of 48 inches and an embedment length of not less than 23 feet below finished
subgrade be used for the 60 foot high new light poles planned for the ballfield area
The performance of the drilled shafts depends significantly upon the contractor s
method of construction construction procedures and workmanship Therefore the
contractor should follow the recommendations and general guidelines presented in this
report during the drilled shaft foundation construction Since the project site is generally
underlain by stiff and soft soils temporary casing may be required during the foundation
construction work to reduce the potential for caving in of the drilled holes In addition
placement of concrete by wet construction methods using a tremie pipe will be required
due to the shallow groundwater table encountered at the site

of cast in place concrete drilled shafts Based on the anticipated structural loads and
the subsoil conditions encountered at the project site we recommend that drilled shafts

The cast in place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally
from skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding soils An allowable
compressive load capacity of up to 50 kips per shaft may be used for dead plus live
loads and may be increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as wind or seismic
forces The anticipated settlements of the light pole foundations are estimated to be on
the order of less than 0 5 inch
Foundation analyses were conducted based on a combination of axial and lateral
loads in addition to the ground line moments using a computer program LPILE Plus for
Windows Lateral loads imposed on the light pole foundations generally may be
resisted by resistance of the shaft soil system to deflect Lateral load resistance of the
drilled shaft is a function of the stiffness of the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft
allowable deflection at the top of the shaft and induced moment in the shaft Based on
the information provided each drilled shaft for the 60 foot high light pole will be
subjected to a cyclic lateral load of 9 1kips and an overturning moment of about
449 kip feet at the top of the drilled shaft Our lateral load analyses indicated that the
estimated lateral deflection at the top of the drilled shaft should be less than 0 1 inch
3 2 Construction Considerations
Drilled shafts are desirable for the light pole foundations because of the
significant increase in lateral and uplift load capacities when compared to shallow
foundations However the performance of the drilled shafts will depend significantly
upon the contractor s method of construction and construction procedures In our
opinion the following conditions would have an impact on the effectiveness and cost of
the drilled shaft foundations Due to the soft consistency of the subsoils we anticipate
that additional concrete volume beyond the theoretical size of the drilled shaft will be
required to construct the shaft

Soft Clayey Soils
Based on our field exploration a part of the project site is underlain by soft marsh
deposits extending to depths over 29 feet below the existing ground surface To
reduce the potential for caving in of the drilled holes temporary casing may be
required during the foundation construction work Therefore we recommend that
casing of drilled holes be considered a contingent item with additive and deductive
unit prices included in the contract documents
Due to the soft consistency of the subsoils we anticipate that additional concrete
volume beyond the theoretical size of the drilled shaft will be required to construct
the shaft
Workmanship
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in
drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids is not recommended Placement of
concrete by wet construction methods using a tremie pipe will be required due to
the high groundwater table anticipated at the site The concrete should be placed
in a suitable manner by displacing the water in an upward fashion from the bottom
of the drilled hole
A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 6 to 9 inch range should be used to
provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soil The
concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce the potential for
segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix If pre cast concrete pole
bases are used the gap between the pole base and the surrounding soils should
be backfilled with concrete or lean concrete A representative from Geolabs should
be present to observe the installation of drilled shafts during construction to confirm
the assumed subsoil conditions and recommended embedment depths

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths
ranging between about 21 5 and 29 feet below the existing ground surface The borings
were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers The
approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual
observation and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and
Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third Ginches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration
Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
