subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion incised deep valleys
into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned to the Island of Oahu with a series of lava
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These most recent volcanic series are
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series The Punchbowl crater located to the west of the
project site is one such extinct tuff and cinder cone
The project area is located on the southern flank of the Koolau Range and is composed
of basalt rock formation built by the extrusion of lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series These
rocks are generally characterized by layered flows of jointed dense vesicular basalt with
interbedded clinker layers For the past 1 2 million years weathering of the Koolau lava flows
has occurred creating the valley and ridge topography visible today on the southern side of the
Koolau mountain range
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by mass
wasting which is essentially the detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and
transportation downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity and deposited as
colluvium Once these colluvial materials reach a stream typically in the central portion of a
valley alluvial processes become dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited
as alluvium
The project site is located on the south facing hillside of the ridgeline located toward
the northwest from the Tantalus Makiki area The ridgeline is bounded by Kanaha Stream to
the east and Pauoa Valley to the west Due to the location of the project site on the side
slopes of the ridgeline the site is generally underlain by some thin alluvial and colluvial soils

overlying saprolitic soils and weathered basalt rock Some of the surficial dark brown colored
alluvial colluvial soils derived from the weathering of the volcanic rock appear to have a
relatively high shrink and swell potential
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
Based on our reconnaissance of the project site generally shallow depth basalt rock
conditions were observed throughout the site Some stiff silty and clayey light colored
residual and saprolitic soils were observed at the northern portion of the project site near
the top of the ridgeline The saprolitic soils were observed to grade with highly weathered
basalt rock in exposed slope cuts
Elsewhere the surface soils were observed to generally consist of dark brown
adobe clay overlying shallow depth basalt rock Based on observations of the ground
surface within the residential lots the clay soils appear to range generally between 2 and
4 feet in thickness with widespread areas of exposed basalt rock comprising the ground
surface The thicker surface soil deposits appear to be concentrated at the western and
southern corners of the project site where existing grades are less steep The thinner
surface soils and exposed basalt rock condition is more prevalent at the northern central
and eastern portions of the project site
Based on our laboratory testing the dark brown clay soils encountered at the project
site may have a moderate to high shrink swell potential when exposed to moisture
fluctuations as evidenced by the highly plastic properties of the soils Observation of some
existing retaining walls and other fixed structures located throughout the site exhibit
evidence of some deformation possibly related to the expansive soil character and slow soil
creep movement in response to gravity acting upon the hillslopes In addition desiccation
cracks were observed in the dark brown soils during periods of dry weather
The basalt rock observed at existing slope cuts located in the backyard and roadway
areas of the project site appears to be layered pahoehoe with some interbedded thin
clinker seams Often the rock outcroppings exhibit a stepped character consisting of steep
GEOLABS INC
W O 3829 10 B Page 7
rock banks separated by flatter slope segments containing some alluvial and colluvial soil
accumulation
Based on our observation and a review of the available construction plans for the
Papakolea development it appears that the existing roadways were constructed mostly in
cut conditions that followed the general trend of the existing topography Areas of
significant fill placement appear to be less extensive In general it may be anticipated that
the roads paralleling the slope contour were constructed by cutting the inside of the slope
and filling along the outer edge to create the road base Therefore shallower soils
overlying basalt rock may be anticipated at the inside edge of the existing roadways that
parallel the contour of the slope Thicker fill materials may be encountered more frequently
along the outer edge of the roadways
Observation of the general condition of the ground surface within the individual lots
located on sloping ground indicates that only minor site grading had been performed The
slopes appear to be naturally developed with frequent exposure of layered rock formation
and some soil deposits composing the flatter slope segments
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling eight borings designated as
Boring Nos 1 through 8 that were spread across the project site The borings extended
to depths of about 15 feet below the existing ground surface In addition two bulk samples
of the near surface soils designated as Bulk 1 and Bulk 2 were collected at selected
locations within the project site
Our subsurface exploration indicates that the majority of the project site is generally
underlain by a near surface layer of expansive soils consisting of stiff to very stiff adobe
clay extending to depths of about 2 to 4 feet below the existing ground surface Because
the borings were drilled within the city street right of way the adobe clay soils were
encountered below the pavement and base sections of the roadways
GEOLABS INC
W O 3829 10 B Page 8
Below the surficial adobe clay soils our field exploration generally encountered
dense saprolitic material and highly to moderately weathered vesicular basalt rock The
depth to the weathered rock materials varied from about 2 to 4 feet below the existing
ground surface The weathered basalt rock extended to the maximum depth explored of
about 15 feet below the ground surface
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field
exploration which was conducted under generally dry weather conditions In general the
site is located well above the level of the basal groundwater table however localized
perched water conditions are possible Furthermore groundwater conditions can vary
significantly depending upon rainfall temperature surface runoff groundwater withdrawal
and other factors
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered from our exploration are presented

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the results of our field exploration it appears that much of the flatter and
lower elevated areas of the project site are generally blanketed by a surficial layer consisting
of 2 to 4 feet of stiff alluvial colluvial silty clay soils Other areas primarily located at the upper
elevation and steeper slopes of the project area are generally underlain by thinner
alluvial colluvial soil cover with exposed basalt rock formation at the ground surface The dark
brown colored clayey soils encountered in the shallow subsurface appear to have moderate
to high shrink swell potential wnen exposed to soil moisture fluctuation Our geotechnical
engineering recommendations pertaining to the design of the retaining structures and paved
swales and interceptor ditches are further discussed in the following sections of this report

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area to receive fill material should be thoroughly cleared
and grubbed Vegetation organic debris construction debris and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site or in a designated area
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with properly compacted select granular fill The soft soils should not be
reused as fill material
The subgrade should be scarified a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil in accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend
that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close cracks or prepared again as
GEOLABS INC

recommended above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade
due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft
areas and replacement with well compacted engineered fill
Fills and Backfills
Generally fills areas will be limited to behind retaining walls Therefore select
non expansive fill should be used The fill material should have no particles larger than
3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles
passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or
more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 The on site dark brown colored clay
and silty clay materials are unsuitable for use as select fill
Fills placed on sloping ground steeper than five horizontal to one vertical 5H 1V
should be properly keyed and benched into the natural ground to bond the fill to the
slope
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Base course and select borrow subbase
materials should be moisture conditioned to near the optimum moisture placed in level
lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage
by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density

Retaining Walls
Based on our field exploration conducted for the proposed project we anticipate that
portions of the project site where retaining walls are planned are generally underlain by some
thin stiff adobe clay and clayey silt soils overlying weathered rock formation In general we
believe that retaining wall footing foundations should bear on the dense weathered rock
materials residual saprolitic soils due to the sloping ground and potentially expansive soil
conditions In addition retaining wall foundations should be embedded a minimum of
24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grades For sloping ground conditions the
footing should be extended deeper to obtain a minimum 8 foot setback distance measured
horizontally from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope Wall footings oriented
parallel to the direction of the slope should be constructed in stepped footings An allowable
bearing value of may be used for the design of wall foundations bearing on the
weathered rock materials
For footings bearing on the stiff clayey soils the bottom of the footing excavations
should be recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction to provide a relatively firm
and smooth bearing surface prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete If soft and or
loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations they should be
over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation may be
backfilled with on site soils compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the
footing bottom may be extended down to the underlying competent materials For footings
bearing on new compacted fills the bottom of the footing excavations should also be
GEOLABS INC
W O 3829 10 B Page 12
recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior to placement of reinforcing
steel or concrete
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the
utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to
avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for foundation settlement
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative from Geolabs
prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation bearing
conditions and the required embedment depths
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of friction of 0 35
may be used for footings bearing on weathered rock materials Resistance due to passive
earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square
foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soil around footings is well compacted The
passive pressure should be reduced for foundations located on slopes Unless covered by
pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected

Paved Swale Interceptor Ditch
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by near surface
clayey soils with moderate to high expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations
To reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded concrete swale ditch resulting
from shrinking and swelling of the clayey soils due to changes in the moisture content we
recommend that the subgrade soils be properly prepared and capped with at least 12 inches
of non expansive select granular fill capping fill material below the concrete swale ditch The
capping fill should also extend beyond the new swale ditch a minimum of 12 inches Since
cobbles and boulders were encountered at various depths below the existing ground surface
across the project site the 12 inch capping fill will also serve to provide more uniform support
for the swale ditch that may otherwise bear directly on the cobbles and boulders
The non expansive select granular capping fill should be placed as soon as possible
after the subgrade soils are properly prepared The prepared capping fill and the underlying
subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist prior to the final placement of concrete Where
shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soils
be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks Saturation and subsequent yielding of the
exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation
of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted engineered fill

additional 6 inch layer of base course compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative

Utility Lines
We understand that underground utility lines drainage will be required for the
proposed project It is anticipated that most of the trenches for utilities will be excavated
in the near surface soils and or hard basalt rock Based on the boring information it is
anticipated that the shallow excavations will encounter expansive clay soils The expansive
clay soils should not be reused as trench backfill
In general good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and
backfilling of the trenches for the proposed utilities The method and equipment to be used
for excavation should be determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety
considerations The excavation should comply with the applicable local state and federal
safety requirements Trench shoring design and installation should be the responsibility
of the contractor Trench shoring and bracing should also conform to the appropriate
health and safety requirements
In general we recommend that granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under the pipes for support of
the utility lines The initial trench backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist
of free draining backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging
the pipes from compaction of the backfill It is critical that a free draining granular material
be used as the initial trench backfill to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Use of
clayey soils as initial trench backfill immediately around utility pipes is not recommended
GEOLABS INC
W O 3829 10 B Page 16
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to the
finished subgrade should consist of the approved on site soils or general fill material The
backfill material should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence The
upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to not
less than 95 percent relative compaction Mechanical compaction equipment should be
used to compact the backfill materials at the project site Compaction efforts by water
tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed considering the expansive soil conditions
at the site

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
eight borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 8 extending to depths of about 15 feet
below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the test borings are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 8
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the
field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive
strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of the
bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative quality
of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from a coring
attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material is recovered
from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs
of Borings as REC 74
Appendix A continued
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced fractures or
breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would be
50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the
following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the Practical Handbook
of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
Very Poor 0 25
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100
The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its
relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense basalt with a high RQD would
be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous methods of excavation

Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentations of the test results are
provided on Plate B 1
Two sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 ASTM D 1140 were performed on
selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate B 2
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates B 3 and B 4
