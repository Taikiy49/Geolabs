INTRODUCTION
In accordance with the request of Mr John Sullivan we have observed a flow test
of the disposal trench for the Net Power Producing Experiment project at the Natural
Energy Laboratory of Hawaii Seacoast Test Facility
Our work was performed in general accordance with our proposal to Natural
Energy Laboratory of Hawaii dated May 27 1992
This report summarizes our observation of the flow test and presents our findings
and recommendations pertaining to the length of trench required for disposal of mixed
seawater at the site
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Natural Energy Laboratory of Hawaii Seacoast Test Facility is located at
Keahole Point in the District of North Kona on the island of Hawaii One of the on going
projects at the Natural Energy Laboratory involves the conversion of ocean thermal
energy into electrical power
The project has progressed to the stage of development of the Net Power
Producing Experiment in which the ocean thermal energy conversion OTEC plant will
generate power in excess of the amount of power required to operate the plant At full
operational capacity the OTEC process for the experiment will require flows of up to

about 17 000 gallons per minute or about of cold and warm seawater through
the plant After use in the OTEC process the mixed seawater will be disposed of through
an open trench situated northerly of the plant
Previously we conducted permeability testing at the site of the disposal trench to
provide preliminary data pertaining to the design of the trench This testing was
conducted in small diameter wells constructed in borings drilled at the site Analyses of
the data obtained from our previous testing indicated that the rock materials at the trench
location probably have coefficients of permeability ranging between about 70 to about 450
feet per day For our design recommendations we utilized an average coefficient of
permeability of 175 feet per day
In our report dated for this testing we indicated that flow
through the trench as originally designed would be limited by the thickness of the clinker
layer underlying the upper ledge of dense basalt encountered at the surface of the site
In the report we recommended that the dense basalt rock below the clinker zone be
fractured by blasting to allow additional pathways for water to infiltrate
The original design length of the trench was 250 feet to dispose of flows of up to
about Due to the indeterminate effect of the additional blasting it was
recommended that the trench be constructed incrementally and that full scale flow tests
be conducted after each increment of construction to allow evaluation of the flow capacity
of the trench
Currently available funding for the project was only sufficient for the construction
of approximately 50 feet of open trench

PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our work on the project was to observe a full scale flow test of the
first increment of open trench which has been consiructed in order to evaluate the
potential capacity of the trench in its current configuration and to provide additional field
data for the formulation of geotechnical engineering recommendations pertaining to any
possible modifications to the current configuration
In order to accomplish this task we have conducted a program consisting of the
following work efforts
1 Observation of the field testing by a Project Engineer Geologist from our
firm
2 Analyses of the field data for the formulation of geotechnical engineering
recommendations pertinent to any proposed changes in the trench
configuration
3 Preparation of this report summarizing our work on the project and
presenting our findings and recommendations
4 Coordination of our overall work on the project by a Project Manager from
our firm
5 Project quality assurance and client design team consultation by a Principal
Engineer of our firm

DISCUSSIONS AND FINDINGS
Flow Testing
A full scale flow test of the existing trench was conducted on The
testing consisted of pumping water into the trench at nominal rates of 10 000
gpm and respectively and monitoring the increase in water level or head
in the trench with respect to time The increase in head was monitored by the use of a
staff gauge installed by Natural Energy Laboratory personnel Flow rates were monitored
by Natural Energy Laboratory personnel using gauges installed on the cold and warm
water pumping systems
After each increase in flow rate the water level was monitored until a generally
steady condition had been attained Once steady state was achieved the flow rate and
water level was held constant for a period of about 45 to 60 minutes
Upon completion of the constant head step testing the pumps were shut down
and the fall in water level was monitored with respect to time to provide data for analysis
as a falling head test
We analyzed the flow test data using infiltration from an open ditch as the model
for the flow paths Our analyses indicated that the materials underlying the trench have
an average permeability on the order of about 430 feet per day This value is generally
consistent with the findings of our previous exploration and testing at the site

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
In our previous report we had assumed that the facility once constructed would
become an on line power production facility and that conservative design would be
needed to allow for contingencies such as clogging of the trench bottom or high
groundwater conditions It is our current understanding that the facility will be operated
on an experimental basis and that the facility can be shut down in the event of overflowing
of the trench
Based on our theoretical analyses of the flow test results it appears that a
minimum trench length of about 110 feet is required for the disposal of 18 000 gallons per
minute of mixed seawater However this minimum length does not consider any factor
of safety nor does it consider high groundwater levels which may result from spring tides
seasonal fluctuations or storm surge conditions In addition the minimum length does
not provide any allowance for clogging of the trench due to biological activity or other
means Therefore we recommend that the length of the trench be extended to 150 feet
to provide some allowance for variations in permeability or for higher groundwater levels
It is our further recommendation that the trench be flow tested again after the
additional length is excavated This is to allow evaluation of the capacity of the trench in
the event that variations in permeability are encountered along the longer trench
During our visit to the project site it was noted that cement powder and debris
were left on the bottom of the trench after construction of the trench and the inlet pipe
It is recommended that these foreign materials be removed from the trench to reduce
potential clogging of the trench bottom It is believed that a small airlift suction dredge
could be fabricated at the site and used to clean out these materials
