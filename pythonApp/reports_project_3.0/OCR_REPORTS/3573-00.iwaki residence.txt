INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed new residence at 3802 Old Pali Road in the Nuuanu area of
Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project
site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and recommendations resulting from our field
exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses These recommendations are
intended for the design of foundation and site preparation only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

REGIONAL GE
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
of the middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be of the late Pliocene to
early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which erosion
incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3573 00 Page 5
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported downslope
toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these materials reach
the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become dominant and the
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the stream
flows transmit large volumes of water for very short durations Because of this transport
of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load consists of a
poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When the erosional
base levels change these sediment loads are left as deposits

Foundations
Based on the subsoil conditions encountered at the site we believe that a shallow
foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be used for
support of the proposed new residence An allowable bearing pressure of up to
2 500 pounds per square foot p s f may be used for the design of foundations bearing
on the undisturbed in situ stiff to very stiff silty soils This bearing value is for dead plus
live loads and may be increased by one third for transient loads such as those caused by
wind or seismic forces
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grades For footings constructed near top of slopes or on sloping ground
the footings should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal set back
distance of 8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the slope face
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3573 00 Page 8
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for
footings bearing on the undisturbed in situ stiff to very stiff soils as recommended herein
to be less than 1 inch total with differential settlements on the order of inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil Resistance due to
passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300
pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f This assumes that the soil around
footings is well compacted The passive pressure should be reduced for foundations
located on slopes Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the
upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected A coefficient of friction of 0 3 may be used
for footings bearing on the in situ stiff to very stiff soils
The bottom of the footing excavations should be lightly compacted to provide a
relatively firm and smooth bearing surface Footing excavations should not be allowed to
dry before placing concrete If shrinkage cracks appear in the footing excavations the
soil should be thoroughly moistened to close all cracks and recompacted to provide a firm
base prior to concrete placement

labs On Gr
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed structure
The near surface soils encountered at the proposed site consist of clayey soils which
exhibit a slight shrink swell potential Therefore we recommend that the subgrade be
properly prepared and kept moist prior to placement of cushion fill and slab concrete In
general the subgrades for slabs on grades should be scarified to a depth of at least
8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction If the in situ subgrade soils
are greater than 5 percent above the optimum moisture content the subgrade soils may
be proof rolled with a drum roller to obtain a firm and unyielding subgrade in lieu of
scariification and compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined
in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
For more uniform support of interior slabs we recommend that the concrete slab
on grade be supported on a minimum 4 inch cushion fill The cushion fill layer should
consist of open graded gravels such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C33
No 67 gradation which will also serve as a capillary moisture break The slab cushion
fill should be placed as soon as possible after the scarification and recompaction of the
existing subgrade soils as recommended above For slabs on grade with moisture
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3573 00 Page 10
sensitive surfacing we recommend that an impermeable membrane be placed over the
cushion fill to reduce potential migration of moisture vapor through the concrete slab In
order to promote more uniform curing of the slab concrete and to provide protection of the
vapor membrane we recommend that a 2 inch layer of moist fine sand be placed above
the membrane prior to placing the slab concrete The sand should be moistened slightly
prior to placing concrete
Where concrete slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as the garage
slab we recommend that the slab be supported on a 6 inch layer of base course
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction in lieu of the 4 inch cushion
fill layer and 2 inch sand The base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate
The subgrade should also be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture
conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and compacted to
not less than 95 percent relative compaction For design of structural slabs a modulus of
subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per cubic inch p c i may be used for the compacted
base course The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to any unpaved area should be
embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project We
recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer of select borrow or No 3B Fine
gravel be provided below the concrete walkway slab The select borrow should be
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction The subgrade below the concrete
walkway should be prepared as recommended before Construction joints should be
provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at right
angle intersections

Swimming Pool
Based on the information provided we anticipate that a swimming pool is also
planned for the proposed residence The walls of the swimming pool should be designed
as retaining walls to resist lateral earth pressures General guidelines for the design of
the retaining walls are as follows
1 Foundations for retaining walls should be designed in general accordance
with the recommendations presented in the Foundations section of this
report The footing embedment for retaining walls should be a minimum of
24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade
Where footings are to be located adjacent to below grade structures or
utilities the footings should extend to a depth below an imaginary
45 degree plane projected upward from the bottom of the below grade
structure or utility This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging
adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential of foundation settlement
2 All retaining structures should be designed to resist the lateral earth
pressures due to adjacent soils hydrostatic pressure and surcharge
effects An active lateral earth pressure of 40 pounds per cubic foot p c f
expressed in equivalent fluid pressure is recommended for design For an
at rest earth pressure an equivalent fluid pressure value of 55 p c f may be
used
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3573 00 Page 12
The value provided above is for on site soils or imported granular soils
Backfills behind retaining walls should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to between 90 and 95 percent
relative compaction Over compaction of the wall backfill should be
avoided
3 The surcharge effect due to traffic areal surcharges line loads and point
loads within a horizontal distance equal to the height of the wall should be
considered in the design For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the
loaded side of the wall a rectangular distribution with uniform pressure
equal to 35 percent of the surcharge pressure acting on the entire height of
the wall which is free to deflect cantilever may be used in the design
4 For lateral load resistance a passive earth resistance of 300 p s f per foot
of depth may be used In addition a base friction factor of 0 3 may also be
used to evaluate the resistance against sliding of the walls
5 Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide
zone of permeable material No 3B Fine gravel or ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation immediately adjacent to the wall with a perforated pipe
perforations down at the base of the wall discharging to a discharge facility
or weep holes A layer of filter fabric such as Mirafi 140N or equivalent
should be placed between the drainage material and the compacted backfill

At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be

pressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in

content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Any soft weak or yielding areas disclosed during clearing operations should be

horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V
should be keyed and benched

Drainage
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations
and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce water infiltration
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be
maintained to drain all surface run off away from the slab and footing foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling four
borings to depths of approximately 16 and 21 feet below the existing ground surface
at the approximate locations shown on Plate 2 The borings were drilled using truck
mounted drilling equipment
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered is presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch O D Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch
drive or the middle 12 inches of a 24 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
