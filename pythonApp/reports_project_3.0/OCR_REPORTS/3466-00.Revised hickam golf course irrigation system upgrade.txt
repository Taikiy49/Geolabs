INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Hickam Golf Course Irrigation System Upgrade project
located within Hickam Air Force Base in the District of Honolulu on the island of Oahu
Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input
only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

the existing irrigation system at the Mamala Golf Course Based on the information
provided the proposed project will consist of construction of a 1 0 million gallon M G
reservoir of approximately 9 feet deep a pump station and approximately 750 feet of golf
course irrigation line

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
GEOLABS HAWAIl
W O 3466 00 Revised March 4 1997 Page 5
Pleistocene in age When the building of the Waianae and Koolau volcanoes came to an
end only a slight embayment existed on the southern coast of Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
During the recent epoch as the sea level rose to its current stand the area was
covered with soft and loose lagoonal deposits These deposits were subsequently
covered by man made fills placed during the middle portion of this century for land
reclamation projects during construction of the existing Hickam Air Force Base

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by sands and
gravels ranging from loose to medium dense in relative density extending to the
maximum depth drilled of about 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface Thin layers
of soft to medium stiff silty clays of up to 2 5 feet in thickness were interbedded within the
sandy soil deposits
Groundwater levels encountered in our borings were measured at an elevation of
about 1 foot MSL at the time of our field exploration Due to the proximity of the site to
the Pacific Ocean we anticipate that the groundwater levels would be influenced by tidal
fluctuations To reduce the potential for damage to the impervious liner system due to the
buoyant uplift forces and to reduce the potential for difficulties during subgrade
preparation the bottom of the proposed irrigation reservoir should be designed to be
about 1 to 2 feet above the measured groundwater level
Based on the proposed reservoir bottom elevation at 2 5 feet MSL and the
schematic reservoir sections we anticipate that the bottom of the reservoir will be near
the groundwater level Therefore special attention should be given to the earthwork
recommendations presented herein to reduce the potential for pumping subgrade
conditions when performing earthwork operations near the groundwater level In addition
soft soils were encountered near the bottom of the reservoir in Boring and 6
Therefore provisions should be made in the contract documents to remove and replace
the soft soils encountered during construction
For support of the proposed wet well we believe that a shallow foundation system
bearing on the in situ sandy soils may be used We understand that the bottom of the
wet well will be about 9 5 feet below the ground surface at an elevation of about 2 0 feet
MSL Based on the subsoils encountered depths of excavation anticipated and the

As previously indicated site grading will involve cuts on the order of about 4 feet

Site grading operations should be observed by a representative of

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris generated from demolition
of the existing structures rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be
removed and disposed of properly off site to reduce potential contamination of the
excavated materials
Any soft weak or yielding areas disclosed during the clearing and grubbing
operations should be over excavated to expose firm ground and the resulting
GEOLABS HAWAII

excavation should be backfilled with approved on site fill material compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Prior to placement of the backfill the
over excavated subgrade should be proof rolled to obtain a firm and unyielding
condition
Following clearing and grubbing subgrades to receive fill should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to above optimum moisture and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior to placement of
fill material Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by
ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Soft to medium stiff soils were encountered near the bottom elevation of the
reservoir in Boring and 6 Therefore loose and or soft soils may be
encountered in localized areas at the bottom of the reservoir Where loose soft
soils are encountered they should be removed or over excavated to a maximum
depth of 2 feet below the bottom of the reservoir The resulting over excavation
should be lined with a filter fabric such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent and backfilled
with free draining materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation We recommend that the contract documents include additive and
deductive unit prices for over excavation and fill placement to account for
variations in the over excavation quantities
Based on our field exploration the proposed bottom of the reservoir will be near
the groundwater table Therefore in lieu of scarifying and compacting the bottom
of the reservoir subgrade we recommend that the finish subgrade of the reservoir
bottom approximately 2 5 feet MSL be proof rolled with a smooth drum roller
5 ton minimum 10 ton maximum to reduce the potential for pumping conditions
of the subsoils and to obtain a relatively smooth and unyielding surface for

Fill and Backfill Materials
In general the on site granular materials may be reused as a source of fill and
backfill materials provided that they contain no lumps or fragments greater than
3 inches in maximum dimension However soft and wet silty and clayey soils
were encountered in our borings therefore the wet silty and clayey soils
generated from the excavation should not be used as fill and backfill materials and
should be disposed of off site The fill and backfill materials should be placed in
level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture content and compacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction The fill and backfill material within the upper 2 feet below the finish
subgrade in roadway areas should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction The finish grade of the reservoir bottom should be proof
rolled with a smooth drum roller 5 ton minimum 10 ton maximum in order to
obtain a relatively smooth and unyielding surface for placement of the impervious
liner system

Settlement
Fills on the order of about 4 to 6 feet will be required for construction of the
reservoir embankment at the project site Because soft clays up to 2 feet thick and
loose sands up to 5 feet thick were encountered in our borings we believe that the new
fills planned would induce some ground settlements as a result of consolidation of the
underlying soft and loose soils We estimate settlements on the order of about 2 inches
may occur due to the anticipated 4 to 6 feet of new fills Due to the granular nature of
the sandy soils and the relatively thin soft clay layer we believe that the majority of the
settlements would occur within about 4 to 6 weeks after fill placement Therefore
construction of the impervious liner system any utility lines through the reservoir
embankment and other improvements on the reservoir embankment should be delayed
by 4 weeks after completion of the reservoir embankment to allow the anticipated
settlements to occur prior to construction of improvements on or within the reservoir
embankment

that a shallow foundation system may be used to support the proposed wet well structure

Excavation
The bottom of the wet well will be about 9 5 feet below the existing ground surface
In order to construct the proposed wet well we anticipate that an excavation up to 10 to
142 feet below the existing ground surface will be required Based on the subsurface
conditions encountered we anticipate that conventional excavation equipment may be
used for the proposed excavation The method and equipment to be used for excavation
should be determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety
considerations The excavated materials should be stockpiled no closer than a horizontal
distance equal to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce
the potential for ground movement
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our borings and the depths of
the excavations anticipated we believe that shoring of the sides of the excavation will be
required to support the excavation and to aid in dewatering All excavations shoring
support and construction works should conform to Federal State and other safety
requirements
A flexible wall shoring system such as sheet pile wall may be used for temporary
shoring support If sheet pile shoring is used it would also serve as a cut off wail for the
dewatering operations The sheet piles should be driven to a sufficient depth to reduce
the potential for areal ground subsidence and to reduce the amount of water to be
dewatered within the excavation due to the relatively high permeability of the sandy soils

system and the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during

accurately the magnitude of ground movement The magnitude of ground movements
also depends greatly upon workmanship such as how quickly and tightly the shoring
supports are installed the subsoil conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of
excavation
Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed
properly and as early as practical and if necessary the adjacent ground should be
continuously monitored for cracks dips and or other indications of movements with
instruments
Dewatering
We understand that the bottom of the excavation for wet well construction will be
about 2 5 feet MSL and the invert of the 20 inch suction pipe will be at about Mean Sea
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3466 00 Revised March 4 1997 Page 18
Level Based on the groundwater encountered at about 1 foot MSL we believe that
dewatering will be necessary in order to construct the wet well and to install the 20 inch
suction pipe
Because of the granular nature of the subsoil conditions encountered it is
anticipated that dewatering of these excavations would involve large volumes of water
The dewatering operation should be conducted in such a manner that the dewatering will
not cause areal ground settlements As previously indicated a dewatering system
including a cut off wall should be considered to reduce the volume of water to be
removed within the excavation and to reduce the areal extent of groundwater draw down
outside of the excavation Dewatering by means of a well point system along the outside
of the excavations is generally not recommended
lf the excavation dewatering will involve discharge of groundwater from the
dewatering operation into the adjacent drainage system a National Pollutant Discharge
Elimination System NPDES permit will be necessary The contractor should consult
their independent consultant or the State of Hawaii Department of Health for the latest
regulations and information pertaining to the NPDES permit application
Dewatering for construction is the sole responsibility of the contractor The
dewatering operations should be coordinated with the shoring support so that the stability
of the excavations is not jeopardized and the operations should be carried out without
softening the bottom of the excavations The selection of equipment and methods of
dewatering should be left up to the contractor and he she should be aware that
modifications to the dewatering system may be required during construction depending
on the conditions encountered The dewatering method selected should have minimal
impact on the existing groundwater levels surrounding the proposed excavation
Dewatering operations should be performed such that only clear water is pumped
Excessive pumping which removes soil fines may result in a blowing or heaving of the
excavation bottom or sides

written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural groundwater table

not limited to well pumping grouting cut off walls tremie plugs or any combination of the
above and or other possible methods We suggest that the following three basic criteria
be considered in selection of a suitable method of dewatering
a The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or damage to

Impervious Liner and Underlining
We understand that a synthetic liner will be provided for the irrigation reservoir to
reduce water infiltration through the underlying relatively permeable material It is our
opinion that the material type performance installation and protection details of the
impervious liner should be designed in accordance with the recommendations by the liner
manufacturer It is critical that the liner maintain its integrity to prevent saturation and
build up of seepage water pressures within the embankment fill
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3466 00 Revised March 4 1997 Page 20
Our experience with similar synthetic liner installations indicates that there is
sometimes a tendency for the liners to become detached at the top of the water
impoundment embankment It appears that this may be the result of inadequate
anchoring of the top of the liner and insufficient flexibility of the liner material It is our
recommendation that the liner manufacturer be consulted regarding proper construction
detailing and installation of the liner with particular attention to proper anchoring to reduce
the potential of liner failure Effects of fluctuating water levels on the performance of the
impervious liner near the top anchoring should also be considered
Technical representatives of the manufacturers of the impervious liner should be
required to be on site at all times during installation to assure that proper construction
procedures and precautions are followed A sales representative of the supplier s may
not have the technical expertise to adequately perform this function
Irrigation Line
We understand that a new irrigation line will be installed for development of the
project In general a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended below the pipes Where soft loose soils
are encountered at the pipe inverts a stabilization layer consisting of at least 18 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation wrapped in a filter fabric such as
Mirafi 180N or equivalent should be provided below the 6 inch bedding layer for support
of the pipes
For the trench backfill from the bedding material up to about 12 inches above the
pipes free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation should also be used to provide adequate support around the pipes and to
reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the
pipes It is critical that free draining materials be used to reduce the potential for
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support around
the sides of the pipes

Corrosion Protection
Three samples of the subsurface soils retrieved from our field exploration were
tested for pH and minimum resistivity in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designations D 2976 and G 57 The results of these tests are presented on Plate B 5 of
Appendix B The minimum resistivity values ranged from 300 to 680 ohm cm which are
indicative of a highly corrosive environment to steel and metals in contact with the soils
Therefore we recommend that metal pipes and other sub structures be properly
designed for protection against potential corrosion
