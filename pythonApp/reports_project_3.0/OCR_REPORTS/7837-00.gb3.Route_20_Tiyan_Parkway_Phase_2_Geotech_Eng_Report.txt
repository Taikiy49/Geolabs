SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our literature research, site reconnaissance, and field exploration 
performed at the deep cut area along the proposed new roadway project between 
Station 210+00 and Station 218+95.75, we anticipate the proposed deep cuts along the 
roadway alignment would encounter a surface layer of soil of about 2 to 10 feet thick 
overlying the detrital coralline limestone formation. The detrital limestone generally 
consists of friable to well-cemented, coarse to fine-grained, generally porous and 
cavernous, white limestone. This limestone has variable engineering properties and has 
shown that when it weathers with the passage of time, it will lose its cementation when 
subjected to the environmental factors.  
Based on the available plans, the current design cut slope inclination between 
Station 210+00 and Station 218+95.75 is planned at approximately 1.5 horizontal to one 
vertical (1.5H:1V) or flatter. We understand it is desired for the new cut slopes to have 
stable slope inclinations steeper than 1.5H:1V. Consideration was given to evaluating a 
soil nail retaining wall system and/or a steepened cut slope with anchored wire mesh for 
implementation at the project site. 
A soil nail retaining wall system consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars 
grouted into drilled holes to stabilize an excavation or slope. A shotcrete facing 
generally is applied to the face of the reinforced excavation to provide the appearance 
of a conventional concrete retaining wall. In general, we recommend the face of the soil 
nail retaining wall system be slightly battered at a slope inclination of about 1H:12V for 
aesthetic purposes considering the height of this wall. Based on our analyses, we 
recommend the minimum length of the soil nails to be about 65 percent of the height of 
the wall using a square grid pattern for the soil nails with a center-to-center spacing of 
6 feet (horizontal and vertical spacing) to promote acceptable global slope stability. The 
soil nails should be installed at a slope inclination of approximately 15 degrees from 
horizontal. The soil nail bar should consist of ASTM A615 Grade 75 threaded bar with a 
minimum bar diameter of 1.375 inches. The diameter of the drilled hole should be a 
minimum of 6 inches. 
As an alternative to a soil nail retaining wall, consideration may be given to 
installing an anchored wire mesh system over a steepened cut slope. The anchored 
wire mesh system should be placed on a steepened cut slope of approximately 0.5H:1V 
slope inclination. An anchored wire mesh system is an active slope stabilization system 
where an alloyed, high-tensile steel wire mesh is anchored into the slope face by 
applying a defined torque force over the grouted soil and/or rock nails and spike plates.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
Of interest to our geotechnical analysis are the surface and subsurface materials 
encountered at the project site, the engineering properties of the materials encountered, 
and the variability of the subsurface conditions across the project site. Therefore, the 
following subsections provide a description of the geologic setting of the project site, the 
surface and subsurface conditions encountered at the site, and a discussion on the 
items needed for seismic design, such as the seismicity and the soil profile 
characteristics for seismic analysis. 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Guam is the largest and southernmost of the Mariana Island 
archipelago, which is an island arc parallel to the trend of the subduction zone forming 
the Mariana Trench. The Mariana Trench is part of a belt of trenches, which extend from 
Japan to the Palau Islands and include the Japan Trench, Mariana Trench, Yap Trench, 
and Palau Trench. 
The Island of Guam can be divided broadly into three geologic provinces: 
the southern mountains, the central mountains, and the northern plateau. The island 
formation started during the Eocene Epoch by the eruption of basaltic rhyolite lavas. 
The mountainous central portion of the island is composed of these Eocene volcanic 
rocks and conglomerates, tuffs, and limestones of the Eocene and Oligocene Epochs. 
Lava flows and limestone formations of Miocene Epoch make up the majority of 
the southern one-third of the island. The plateaus of the northern portion of the island 
were formed while this part of the island was still beneath the sea level. During the 
Oligocene and Miocene Epochs, a complex series of coral and algal reefs formed an 
elongated platform of limestones and related deposits. Tectonic activity has lifted these 
coral-algal limestones above sea level to form the northern Guam Plateau. 
The central portion of the island consists of the transition from the volcanic 
materials of the southern mountains to the limestone formations of the northern plateau. 
The Tiyan Parkway project site is situated in the central portion of the island. Geology 
maps from the Water and Environmental Research Institute of the Western Pacific

2.3 
Site Description 
The project site generally is located in the forest area north of the A.B. Won Pat 
International Airport on the Island of Guam. The project site generally is bounded by 
Lower Sunset Boulevard to the south, South Marine Corps Drive to the west and 
Highway 10A to the east. The approximate limits of the site are shown on the Site 
Plans, Plates 2.1 through 2.3. 
Based on our field observations, the existing land is densely vegetated with 
brush and tall trees. The project area generally slopes down to the north with 
inclinations between approximately 10H:1V and 5H:1V. Portions of the ground 
inclination were steeper than 2H:1V near the northern end of the project site. Based on 
the available plans, the existing ground surface elevations range from about +165 to 
+230 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). A fill embankment of about 3 feet in thickness was 
required to create a level working platform to drill Boring No. 102 and to provide access 
to Boring No. 101.

3.1 
Cut Slope 
In general, we believe the cut slopes planned at the site exposing the limestone 
formation may be designed with a slope inclination of 1.5 horizontal to one vertical 
(1.5H:1V) or flatter except the top 10 feet below the existing ground surface where the 
subsurface profile likely will expose the soils encountered in the borings drilled at the 
project site. The upper 10 feet of the cut slopes should be flattened to an inclination of 
2H:1V or flatter.  
We have evaluated the global slope stability of the 1.5H:1V cut slope planned for 
the project. Based on our analyses, the factor of safety for short-term and long-term 
stability of the cut slope is at least 1.5, which is the minimum factor of safety normally 
recommended for cut slopes based on the Soils and Foundations Reference Manual,

We reviewed and evaluated the two alternatives to address the long-term 
maintenance by including permanent erosion control measures on the surface of the 
1.5H:1V cut slope. Based on our evaluation, we suggest installing a fabric form concrete 
system on the face of the steep cut slope to serve as a permanent erosion control 
measure. The fabric form concrete system consist of a geotextile fabric interlocked 
together and is filled with 5,000 psi concrete to form an armor system. An anchor trench 
is used to anchor the fabric form system at the top of the slope to provide slope stability. 
The anchor trench should be about 12 inches wide and deep enough to allow a 
minimum anchor embedment of about 18 inches below the finished grade. The anchor 
trench should be backfilled with concrete. 
The alternative permanent erosion control measure would be to install a 
minimum 4-inch thick layer of fiber reinforced shotcrete over the entire cut slope face as 
a form of permanent erosion control measure. We anticipate that the slope bench would 
be subjected to vehicular traffic. Therefore, we recommend increasing the thickness of 
the fiber reinforced shotcrete to a minimum of 6 inches at the slope bench. 
Based on our evaluation, we recommend implementing the fabric form concrete 
system as it has a rougher surface to assist in controlling surface water runoff on the 
slope. A drainage ditch or concrete swale will be required at the bottom of the cut slope 
to collect and divert surface water runoff on the large cut slope to appropriate outlets for 
proper discharge for either of the two alternatives.

3.3 
Soil Nail Retaining Walls 
Instead of a cut slope with an inclination of about 1.5H:1V that would extend up 
to about 60 feet in vertical height, consideration may be given to a soil nail retaining wall 
system to be installed using top-down construction. A soil nail retaining wall system 
consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars grouted into drilled holes to stabilize an 
excavation or slope. A shotcrete facing generally is applied to the face of the reinforced 
excavation to provide the appearance of a conventional concrete retaining wall. The 
reinforcing bars provide both additional tensile and shear strengths to the soil and 
reinforce the sidewall of the excavation. The reinforced soil mass behaves in a manner 
similar to a gravity retaining wall but lacks a footing. In general, the face of the soil nail 
retaining wall system is slightly battered at a slope inclination of about 1H:12V for 
aesthetic purposes. A typical soil nail retaining wall detail is provided on Plate 3 for 
reference. 
The soil nail retaining wall system would retain a cut condition exposing the 
upper soil horizon and the lower limestone formation consisting of friable to sometimes 
well-cemented coarse to fine-grained, generally porous, white detrital limestone. Based 
on the information provided, we understand that retaining walls ranging from about 20 to 
45 feet in height will be required. The soil nail retaining wall system would serve both as 
temporary shoring of the excavation and the permanent retaining wall.

3.3.3 Drainage 
The soil nail wall should be well drained to reduce the potential for the build-up of 
hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system for the soil nailed slope would 
consist of 2-foot wide strips of a prefabricated drainage product, such as 
Miradrain or Enkadrain, placed on the face of the excavation between the soil 
nails. The prefabricated drainage product should extend from the top to the base 
of the wall and should be hydraulically connected to a weephole at the base of 
the wall.

3.3.4 Soil Nail Testing 
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated behind the soil nail retaining 
walls, we recommend performing a minimum of four (4) pre-production 
verification tests on sacrificial soil nails. Verification tests also should be 
conducted on a minimum of fifteen (15) production soil nails. In addition, proof 
tests should be performed on at least 5 percent of the production soil nails during 
construction to confirm the bond stresses used in the design. 
The verification tests should be loaded gradually to 125 percent of the design 
load for the creep test and subsequently to the maximum test load of about 
150 percent of the design load. In addition, the load at every loading increment 
should be held for at least 10 minutes, and creep tests should be held for at least 
60 minutes. The proof tests should consist of subjecting the soil nail to at least 
133 percent of the design loads and the load should be held for at least 
10 minutes (until stable). The test nails may be incorporated into the permanent 
soil nail retaining wall provided they satisfy the test criteria. Pullout tests and 
creep tests on the soil nails are integral components of the design for the soil nail 
retaining wall system. Therefore, we recommend conducting pullout tests and 
creep tests in the presence of a Geolabs representative.

Anchored Wire Mesh 
Consideration may be given to installing an anchored wire mesh system over a 
steepened cut slope instead of a soil nail retaining wall. The anchored wire mesh 
system should be placed on a steepened cut slope of approximately 0.5H:1V slope 
inclination. The steep slope face would be stabilized by installing an anchored wire 
mesh with an erosion control matting to better control the potential raveling of the steep 
cut slope face due to weathering effects with the passage of time.  
An anchored wire mesh system is an active slope stabilization system where an 
alloyed, high-tensile steel wire mesh is anchored into the slope face by applying a 
defined torque force over the grouted soil and/or rock nails and spike plates. The 
anchored wire mesh system should be designed to withstand the static and dynamic 
forces generated from rocks or soils moving under the permanently installed system. 
Typical details on the anchored wire mesh system are provided on Plates 4 and 5. 
Similar to the soil nail retaining wall system, construction of the anchored wire 
mesh system should be performed by a specialty contractor experienced in the 
construction of anchored wire mesh systems. At this time, we believe the local 
contractors likely do not have the expertise to construct the anchored wire mesh 
system; therefore, the contractor and the specialty equipment needed to drill the lateral 
soil anchors likely will need to be from off-island. Due to the specialized nature of the 
anchored wire mesh construction, a Geolabs representative should be present to

3.4.1 Anchored Wire Mesh 
The anchored wire mesh system is a slope treatment that involves installation of 
high-strength steel wire mesh panels anchored onto the slope face with grouted 
soil/rock anchors in an effort to prevent the soils and rocks from moving or falling 
thus providing stability. The primary intent of this stabilization system is to closely 
pin the wire mesh to the slope face as a retention system.  
The slope to be anchored with steel wire mesh must be trimmed of vegetation 
and scaled of loose rocks prior to the installation of the anchored wire mesh 
system. Detailed descriptions of the individual components of the anchored wire 
mesh system are presented below: 
3.4.2 High-Strength Wire Mesh 
The wire mesh should be alloyed high-strength carbon steel wire with a minimum 
tensile strength of 256,000 psi. The wire mesh should be woven construction, 
diamond shaped, and made with 4-mm (0.157-inch) diameter wire. The ends of 
each wire should be formed into a loop and should be twisted. The loops of the 
wire mesh should be fastened together to prevent unraveling of the mesh. The 
wire should be galvanized with a zinc-aluminum coating with a minimum weight 
of 120 grams per square meter (0.40 ounces per square foot) for Level 3 drawn 
Zn5Al wire in accordance with ASTM A1007.  
3.4.3 Press Claws (Compression Claws) 
The press claws (formerly known as compression claws) should be 6-mm 
(0.236-inch) diameter carbon steel bars and hot-dipped galvanized with a 
minimum layer thickness of 85 microns. The smaller (standard) press claws 
should be used to fasten the mesh panels together, and the larger (special) press 
claws should be used to fasten the mesh panels to the boundary ropes.

3.4.7 Grouted Soil/Rock Anchor Testing 
We recommend performing a minimum of four (4) pre-production verification 
tests on sacrificial anchors. Verification tests should also be conducted on a 
minimum of fifteen (15) production anchors. In addition, proof tests should be 
performed on a minimum of 5 percent of the production anchors.

load for the creep test and subsequently to the maximum test load of about 
150 percent of the design load. In addition, the load at every loading increment 
should be held for at least 10 minutes, and creep tests should be held for at least 
60 minutes.  
Proof tests should be performed by incrementally loading the anchor to a 
maximum test load of 150 percent of the design pullout capacity. An electronic 
load cell should be used to measure the applied load to the anchor. The anchor 
head movement during the test should be measured and recorded.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the deep cuts for the new roadway 
by drilling and sampling five borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 105, 
extending to depths of about 52.3 to 68.5 feet below the existing ground.  The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 through 2.3. The 
borings were drilled using truck-mounted drill rigs equipped with continuous flight 
hollow-stem augers. 
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock materials were described 
in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log 
Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” 
by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). Graphic representations 
of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 
through A-5.2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in 
general accordance with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving 
a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance 
with ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation 
of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the 
appropriate sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits to aid in soil classifications. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Graphic presentations of the test results are provided on Plate B-1. 
Eight Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plates B-2 and B-3. 
Three Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-4 through B-6. 
One Moisture, Ash, and Organic Matter of Soils tests (ASTM D2974) was 
performed on a selected soil sample to evaluate the ash and organic matter of the soils.  
The test results are presented on Plate B-7. 
Two Minimum Resistivity (ASTM G57) tests were performed on selected soil 
samples obtained from our field exploration. The test results are summarized on 
Plate B-8.
