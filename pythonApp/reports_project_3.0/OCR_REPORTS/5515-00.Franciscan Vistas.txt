SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by hard
to very hard clay (fill and alluvial soil) about 16 feet or more in thickness. Coral
formation was encountered only in the deepest portions of Boring Nos. 1, 12, 13, 14, 19
and 25. Groundwater was not encountered in the borings drilled at the time of our field
exploration.
Our laboratory testing on the surface clay materials indicated that these soils
have relatively high expansion potential. Proper site preparation, earthwork procedures
and foundation design are necessary to provide adequate support for pavements and
structures.
Since it is anticipated that minimal elevation change will be required to achieve
design finished grade at the western half of the site, we recommend excavating this
portion to a depth of 3 feet below finished grade to allow for placement of select
non-expansive structural fill during mass grading. At present, it is assumed that, other
than the material needed to backfill the existing channel at the eastern portion, the
material from the berms will be removed and the finished grade elevations will be close
to the elevations at the western portion. Therefore, this portion should also be
over-excavated to a depth of 3 feet below finished grade to allow for placement of select
non-expansive structural fill during mass grading.
For foundation design of the anticipated lightly loaded residential structures, an
allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for
footings bearing on the compacted fill placed in accordance with the recommendations
presented herein.
If the alternate scheme of post-tensioned slabs-on-grade is used for the
proposed structures, the structural portions of the site should be over-excavated to a
depth of about 2.5 feet below finished subgrade. The over-excavated clayey soils
should be placed in loose lifts not exceeding 8 inches in thickness, moisture conditioned
to about 5 percent above optimum moisture content and compacted to between 85 and
90 percent relative compaction. The backfill should be protected from drying by capping
the structural areas with a minimum of 6 inches of structural fill material compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific
design recommendations.

SECTION 1.0 - GENERAL
1.1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Franciscan Vistas residential and community use project at the corner
of Renton Road and O‘ohao Street (TMK: (1) 9-1-126: 009) in the Ewa area on the
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the
Project Location Map, Plate 1.
This report summarizes the findings and _ geotechnical engineering
recommendations derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering
analyses. The recommendations presented herein are intended for the design of site
preparation, infrastructure installation, building foundations and road pavements. The
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at
the end of this report.
1.2 Project Considerations
Based on the information provided, the project site is approximately 23 acres of
former sugar plantation land and is proposed for residential and community-use
development. The current development scheme involves multi-family buildings and
detached cluster-type housing units. The project also includes an adult day care center
and a senior community center with swimming pool and spa amenities. Infrastructure
will include roadways, underground utilities (e.g., water, sewer, electric,
communications, and lighting) as well as parking areas.
Details of the proposed site grading were not available at the time this report was
prepared. However, it is anticipated that the major portion of earthwork will be the
cutting down of existing soil berms and piles at the eastern portion of the site along with
filling of an existing channel along the southern boundary of the eastern side. Minimal
grading work is anticipated at the western portion to achieve the design finished grades.

SECTION 2.0 - SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2.1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be
late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age.
The project site is situated on the Ewa Coastal Plain, which extends seaward off
the southeastern flank of the Waianae Volcano and the southwestern flank of the
Koolau Volcano. This plain was formed by the accumulation of a thick wedge of
sediments and coral reef formations on the flanks of the two volcanoes.
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea.
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level.
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials.
The soils and rocks underlying the project site were described by H.T. Stearns
and K.N. Vaksik, as follows:
Pleistocene Non-Calcareous Deposits (Qa) - This deposit consists chiefly of alluvium.
The surface soils underlying the project site are described by the U.S. Soil Conservation
Service in their publication "Soil Survey of Islands of Kauai, Oahu, Maui, Molokai and
Lanai, State of Hawaii,” (1972), as follows:
Honouliuli Clay, 0 to 2% slopes - These soils consist of well-drained soils developed
in alluvium derived from basic igneous rocks. The soils have generally high shrink-swell
potential and low shear strength.

SECTION 2 — SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2.2 Site Description
The Franciscan Vistas project site is located on the southern side of the Town of
Ewa and to the east of the former Ewa Sugar Mill site in the Ewa District on the Island of
Oahu, Hawaii, as shown on the Project Location Plan, Plate 1. The project site is former
sugar cane plantation land that was used partly for a mill yard area and partly for
associated sugar cane processing.
At the time of our field exploration, most of the project site was vacant land with a
light to heavy cover of dry land vegetation, such as low shrubs and grasses.
Recreational facilities such as basketball and tennis courts were at the northwest corner
of the site and a baseball/softball field was situated at the southwest corner.
The existing ground surface at the western half was generally level with ground
surface elevations ranging from about 39.5 to 43.5 feet above Mean Sea Level (MSL).
Two concrete lined ditches, about 3 to 4 feet deep, traversed the eastern portion from
north to south in a “Y” configuration.
The topography at the eastern half was more complicated with the central portion
being relatively level at about 43.5 feet MSL. This central area was generally enclosed
by soil berms about 10 feet high. Along the southern boundary in the eastern half, an
existing channel about 10 feet deep led to a culvert structure.
Locally, in various locations at the eastern portion, there were mounds of soil,
debris and other materials. It is believed that most of these piles were left from the
former agricultural use.
2.3. Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions were explored by drilling and sampling 30 borings,
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 30, extending to depths ranging between about
20.8 and 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate locations of the
drilled borings are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. Borings could not be drilled near the
eastern edge of the site due to the existing stockpile. Field observations and borings or
test pits will be conducted in this area after the stockpile is removed.

3.1.1 Site Preparation
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other
unsuitable materials, should be removed and disposed properly off-site or
stockpiled in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the
excavated materials.
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing, grubbing, and over-excavation
work should be over-excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting
excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted engineered fill. The excavated
soft soils may be used as fill, provided that fill material requirements are met.

A keyway should be cut down into the in-situ material at the toe of fills on ground

3.1.4 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills using on-site clayey soils should be moisture-conditioned to about
5 percent above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches
in loose thickness, and compacted to between 85 and 90 percent relative
compaction. Fills and backfills consisting of structural fill material should be
moisture-conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture, placed in
level lifts not exceeding 10 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same

soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water
content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. Our
recommendations provide elevated moisture contents and reduced compaction
requirements for fill and backfill work to limit the potential swell capacity of the
clayey soils encountered at the site. Therefore, we wish to emphasize that moisture
conditioning of the fill materials is an integral and critical part of the fill and backfill
placement recommendations provided herein. Compaction should be accomplished
by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction
equipment.
Base course and select borrow subbase materials in roads, driveways and parking
areas should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in
level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum
of 95 percent relative compaction.
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the subgrade soils are critical
elements of earthwork, particularly for the reduced compaction requirements,
Geolabs should provide observations and perform soil density tests during site
grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the appropriate degree of compaction
and the proper ranges of moisture content. It should be noted that the moisture and
reduced compaction requirements for fills and subgrades are important criteria for
use of the on-site clayey soils. Where compaction is less than required, additional
compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture content as
necessary, to obtain the specified compaction. Similarly, in situations where the
reduced compaction requirements are applicable, it may be necessary to scarify the
soil and re-compacted it to the proper range of relative compaction, if the required
rang of compaction is exceeded.

3.1.6 Cut and Fill Slopes
It was assumed that, other than the removal of the berms and backfilling of the
channel, elevation changes to reach design finish grade will be minimal and that
few cut or fill slopes will be involved for the overall site grading. However, this
section gives guidance for design and construction of slopes in the event that they
may be required. Based on the encountered subsurface conditions, it appears that
cut slopes required for this project would expose soils consisting of stiff to very stiff
clay. In general, we believe that cut slopes into the soil material may be at an
inclination of 2H:1V or flatter.
Fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. To provide
stability of the new fill against sliding, fills should be keyed and benched into the
existing slope. The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up
in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the fill placement
recommendations.
To obtain a well-compacted slope face, fill slopes should be constructed by
overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio. Track-rolling of slopes should
not be accepted. In the event that over-cutting of a slope occurs, keying and
benching requirements should be implemented instead of backfilling with sliver fills

3.2 Foundations
For foundation design of the anticipated lightly loaded residential structures, an
allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for
footings bearing on the compacted fill or backfill placed in accordance with the
recommendations presented herein. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and
may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces.
Footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade. Footings located adjacent to or on slopes should be embedded
to a sufficient depth to provide a minimum horizontal setback distance of at least 6 feet
measured from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope. Foundations
located next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a 45-degree
imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench, or the
footings should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines.
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated light design loads for
footings bearing on the structural fill to be less than 1 inch if the foundations are
designed and constructed as recommended herein. Differential settlements between
adjacent footings should be on the order of about 0.5 inch or less.

bearing on the on-site materials and structural fill, respectively. Resistance due to
passive earth pressure may be calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 275 and
350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf) for footings embedded in the
structural fill and for the on-site materials, respectively. The passive earth pressure

3.3 Slabs-On-Grade
Slabs-on-grade should bear on a minimum of 3 feet of structural fill. For interior
building slabs (not subjected to vehicular traffic), we recommend providing a minimum
4-inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67
gradation) below the slabs. The open-graded gravel cushion fill would serve as a
capillary moisture break and would provide for uniform support of the slabs. To reduce
the potential for future moisture infiltration through the slab and to reduce subsequent
damage to floor coverings, an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of
the open-graded gravel cushion layer. It also is recommended that interior walls be
designed to incorporate some flexibility to accommodate a small amount of possible
ground movements.
For slabs that will be subjected to vehicular traffic (such as garage slabs), we
recommend providing a 6-inch layer of aggregate base course below the slabs in lieu of
the 4-inch thick gravel cushion layer. The base course should consist of crushed
basaltic aggregates compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. For the
design of structural slabs supported on 6 inches of base course, a modulus of subgrade
reaction of about 175 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection (pci) may be used.
A minimum slab thickness of 6 inches should be used. In addition, provisions should be
made for proper load transfer across slab joints that will be subjected to vehicular traffic.
The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at
least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade. It should be emphasized that the
areas adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly against the edges of the slabs
with relatively impervious soils (well-graded on-site granular soils). These areas also

3.4 Post-Tensioned Slab Alternative
To reduce the potential for structural distresses induced by expansion of the
subsoils, we generally recommend over-excavating the on-site soils and replacing with
structural fill. However, our past experience in the Ewa Villages area indicates that such
over-excavation and replacement schemes are sometimes limited in their ability to
reduce the deflections resulting from cyclical shrink/swell movements of the subsoils.
Further, due to the lack of embankment areas at the site and the relative scarcity of
structural fill, another alternative was desired. Our opinion is that the use of
post-tensioned structural slabs-on-grade to support the proposed structures could be a
suitable alternative for this site.
In order to reduce the potential for moisture change and corresponding
shrink/swell deflections, we recommend over-excavating the existing soils under the
structures be over-excavated to a depth of about 2.5 feet below finished subgrade. The
clayey soils removed from the excavation should be placed in loose lifts not exceeding
8 inches in thickness, moisture conditioned to about 5 percent above optimum moisture
content and compacted to between 85 and 90 percent relative compaction. The backfill
should be protected from drying. To prevent the re-compacted backfill from drying, the
structural areas should then be capped with a minimum of 6 inches of structural fill
material compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction, moisture
conditioned to about 5 percent above the optimum moisture content, and re-compacted.
To prevent the soils from drying after recompaction, we recommend sprinkling water on
the structure subgrades to keep them moist until the slab is poured.

3.5 Retaining Structures
For the construction of the proposed residential development, retaining walls may
be required for grade separation. In general, the following guidelines may be used for
preliminary design of the retaining structures.
3.5.1 Wall Foundations
In general, we believe that retaining wall foundations may be designed in
accordance with the "Foundations" section presented in this report. The
foundations should have a minimum width of 18 inches and should be embedded a
minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade at relatively flat
areas. For sloping ground conditions, the footings should extend deeper to obtain a
minimum 8-foot horizontal setback distance measured from the outside edge of the
footing to the slope face. The horizontal setback distance may be reduced to a
minimum of 3 feet if the footings are embedded at least 12 inches into the coral
formation. Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should be
constructed in stepped footings.

retaining structure discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes. As an
alternative, a prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may
be used in lieu of the drainage material. The prefabricated drainage product should
also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall.

3.7. Corrosion Protection for Water Lines
Three sets of pH and minimum resistivity tests were performed to evaluate the
corrosivity of the encountered clayey soils. The selected samples were tested to
determine the pH value, and resistivity. The pH value was determined in general

with ASTM G 57. Results of the soil analyses (pH, resistivity) are summarized on Plate

Based on the test results of soil resistivity and pH value, we believe the on-site
material has a moderate corrosivity to both metallic materials and concrete.

3.11 Special Environmental Considerations
Although environmental consulting is not in our scope of work, the following
discussions are offered as advisory information in the interest of providing a higher
standard of care for our work. It is believed that part of the western portion of the project
site may have been used as a yard area for the old Ewa Sugar Mill. There is some
anecdotal history regarding environmental contamination at the old Ewa Sugar Mill site.
Since Geolabs was not actively involved in any environmental investigations at this site,
we do not have any details regarding this matter. However, in view of the existing
anecdotal history, we recommend retaining a qualified environmental consultant to
further investigate this matter and to determine whether potential exposure risks exist at
the site for construction personnel or future residents.
Further, it is anticipated that the bulk of the material in the berms in the eastern
portion of the site will be exported for use at other sites. In Hawaii's recent past,
incidents have occurred where exported fill material was found to contain environmental
contaminants after export and delivery to other sites. Since these berms were
constructed by the plantation and there is no known history regarding potential
contamination in the material, it is also recommended that an environmental consultant
investigate the potential presence of contaminants in the export soils.

The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling 30 borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 30, extending to depths of

examination in the field by our geologist, who observed the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed by visual
observation and testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings,
Plates A-1 through A-30.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving
a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In
addition, some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths.

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937)
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings
at the appropriate sample depths.
Nine Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. Results
of the tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth.
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plates B-1 and B-2.
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D 3080) was performed on a selected sample to
evaluate peak and residual shear strength characteristics. The test results and shear
stress versus normal stress plots are presented on Plate B-3.
One Modified Proctor compaction test (ASTM D 1557) was performed on a bulk
sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships. The test results are presented on Plate B-4.
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) tests (ASTM D 1883) were
performed on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-5 and B-6.
Twelve one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on natural and remolded
samples of the soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils. The test
results are presented on Plate B-7.
Five sets of corrosion tests, including pH (AASHTO T 289) and
Minimum Resistivity (ASTM G 57) were performed to evaluate acidity and corrosive
properties of the saturated soil samples obtained from our field exploration. The test
results are presented on Plates B-8 and B-9.
