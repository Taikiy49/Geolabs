Introduction
This geotechnical engineering exploration report presents the results of our
analyses and geotechnical recommendations for the HRT Site Improvements Royal
Kunia Phase 2 project in the Waipahu area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for site grading and design of
drainage structures only The findings and recommendations presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2
Project Considerations
The project site is presently fallow land that was formerly used for the cultivation of
sugar cane Most of the site gently slopes down towards the south however a major
swale crosses the site from the northeast down to the southwest Visual observations
indicate that this swale provides drainage for the site and that it may have been used as
an irrigation reservoir
It is proposed to replace the swale with a 10 foot by 12 foot box culvert
approximately 4 000 feet in length As part of the project the swale will be filled and the
remainder of the site will be rough mass graded Portions of the fill material will be
derived from existing stockpiles at the southern portion of the site

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the
island The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age and forms the eastern two thirds of the island As volcanic activity in
Waianae Volcano ceased lava flows from Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded
eastern slope forming a broad plateau known as Schofield Plateau
Following the extrusion of lavas in the early Pleistocene Epoch the island
underwent a long cycle of erosion and weathering forming the prominent ridgelines and
summits as we know today During the erosion period the interfluvial ridges and areas
such as the Schofield Plateau were subjected to deep chemical weathering forming a
mantle of residual soil and saprolite which grade with depth to weathered basalt rock
formation
The project site is situated on the southwest portion of the Schofield Plateau
The majority of the project site is underlain by residual clayey silt soils which grade with
depth to saprolite over basalt rock formation Some minor surface fills are present due
to agricultural development in the area A large rock faced earth berm has been
constructed across the swale traversing the site forming an open reservoir which is now
abandoned In addition there are two relatively large stockpiles of soil in the southern
portion of the site and smaller piles of soil and miscellaneous materials in the
northeastern portion of the site

Subsurface Conditions
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling 10 borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 10 extending to depths of about 15 to 26 5 feet
below the existing ground surface Most of the borings were drilled along the proposed
culvert alignment to provide subsurface information for the culvert structure Boring
and 4 were drilled in the existing reservoir impoundment berm to provide
information pertaining to demolition of the berm
In general our borings encountered a surface layer of residual soil consisting of
reddish brown clayey silt that extended to depths between about 5 and 17 feet below
the existing ground surface Below the residual soil the subsurface material graded to
saprolite i e soil that retains the relict structure of the parent rock generally consisting
of grayish brown clayey silt with gravel
In Boring Nos 1 and 7 the borings penetrated the saprolite layer and
encountered weathered basalt rock formation at a depth of about 15 feet below the
existing ground surface In Boring Nos 7 and 9 our exploration encountered a surface
layer of brown clayey silt representing sediments in the former reservoir overlying the
residual soils

Site Preparation
In general the areas within the contract grading limits should be cleared and
grubbed thoroughly at the on set of earthwork Vegetation debris deleterious
material and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly
off site or disposed in a designated area to reduce the potential for contaminating
the excavated materials
Soft and yielding areas encountered during the clearing and grubbing should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should
be backfilled with well compacted engineered fill In general the excavated soft and
wet soils may not be re used as a source of fill and backfill materials
After clearing and grubbing the existing ground surface in areas designated to
receive fill or to remain at grade should be scarified to a depth of 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum
dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557

Keying and Benching
At the toe of the mass fill a keyway should be cut down into firm undisturbed
natural ground This key should be a minimum of 5 feet deep at the toe of the fill
and should be a minimum of 25 feet wide extending back under the toe of the fill
The floor of the key should be sloped back under the base of the fill at about
10H 1V to bond the fill into the existing slope
As fill placement progresses up the existing slope benches should be cut into the
existing slope to bond the mass of the fill to the existing ground These benches
should be a minimum of 5 feet in height and approximately 10 feet in width The
bench shelf should as much as practicable be founded in firm undisturbed natural
ground
A Geolabs representative should monitor keying and benching operations to
observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation
process and to confirm whether the exposed soil conditions are similar to those
encountered in our field exploration

Subdrainage
We did not encounter seepage zones in our exploration however there is some
potential for seepage to develop along the interface between the new fill and the
existing ground during periods of heavy precipitation The accumulation of water
along the base of the fill could potentially have an adverse effect upon the
performance of the fill Therefore we recommend including provisions in the plans
for installation of subdrains in case seepage zones are encountered during
construction
Fill and Backfill Materials
In general the excavated on site materials and on site stockpiled soils may be
used as a source of general fill materials provided that they are free of deleterious
materials
In the large stockpiles on the site our exploration encountered clayey soils with a
moderate expansion potential These soils are suitable for deeper fills but should
not be placed within 5 feet of finish grade These soils should be capped with a
minimum of 5 feet of select non expansive material
The smaller stockpiles in the northern portion of the site contained a significant
amount of boulders construction debris and other miscellaneous materials These
soils are not suitable for use as fill unless processed to remove the deleterious
materials
Imported fill materials required for the project construction should consist of
non expansive select granular material such as crushed coral and basalt The
material should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than
3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent
particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
The material in the large stockpiles on the site are clayey soils with a moderate
expansion potential These soils are suitable for deeper fills but should be capped
with a minimum of 5 feet of select non expansive material
The material in the smaller stockpiles in the northern portion of the site contained a
significant amount of boulders construction debris and other miscellaneous
materials These soils are not suitable for use as fill unless processed to remove
the deleterious materials
Fill materials clayey on site stockpiled soils and imported materials should be
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned
to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and compacted to at
least 90 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density Compaction
should be accomplished using sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other types of
acceptable compaction equipment
Cut and Fill Slopes

project will be mass grading work involving filling of the existing swale area We
envision that cut slopes at the project site will be minimal and will generally expose
the stiff to very hard clays and silts encountered in the borings
In general cut slopes and permanent fill slopes constructed of the on site soils may
be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fills placed on slopes
steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide

Excavation
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by stiff to very
hard clays and silts grading to basalt formation We anticipate that the near surface
materials may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment such as a
backhoe excavator or ripped with a large bulldozer However deep excavations
and excavations into basalt formations may require the use of hoerams
In addition based on our understanding of the subsurface conditions at the site
there is a potential for encountering remnant boulders in excavations at the site

Box Culvert Inlet and Manhole Foundations
Based on our exploration laboratory testing and analyses an allowable bearing
pressure of up to 4 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of
the drainage structures when bearing on undisturbed cut soils For foundations
bearing on fill an allowable bearing capacity of may be used If the
footings are designed in accordance with our recommendations total settlements of
the structures are estimated to be on the order of less than 1 inch
We anticipate that the drainage structures will consist of cast in place and pre cast
concrete As a minimum we recommend providing a 12 inch cushion layer
consisting of aggregate subbase materials between the drainage structure base
slab and the underlying bearing soils to provide uniform support The aggregate
subbase cushion layer should be compacted to at least 90 percent relative
compaction Aggregate subbase materials below the drainage structures should
conform to the requirements stipulated in Subsection 703 17 of the State of Hawaii
Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction 2005
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by friction between the base
of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure developed
against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of the foundation
A coefficient of friction of 0 40 may be used for design purposes based on bearing
on the aggregate subbase materials Resistance to lateral loads due to passive
earth pressure may be calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds
per square foot per foot of depth pcf These values assume that the concrete for
footings is cast neat against the surrounding soils or that the surrounding soils will
be well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance
in the upper 12 inches below the finished grade should be neglected
It is important that a Geolabs representative monitor the footing excavations to
evaluate the competency of the bearing materials If unsuitable materials are
encountered in the footing excavations these materials should be over excavated
to expose the underlying firm materials and replaced with compacted aggregate
subbase materials

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions along the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling ten borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 10 to maximum depths
ranging between about 15 to 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface The borings
were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with auger equipment The
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In addition to the borings a total of 15 test pits designated as Test Pit Nos 1
through 15 were excavated and sampled at selected locations within existing stockpiles
at the site The approximate test pit locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings and test pits by
visual and textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling and excavation
operations on a near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A
Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in

a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings
at the appropriate sample depths
Eight one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the swelling potential of the soils under surcharge pressures These tests were
run on natural and remolded samples The test results are presented on Plate B 1
Four Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are presented on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic presentation of the test
results is provided on Plate B 2
Four Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the shear strength parameters The test results are presented on Plates B 3
through B 6
Two Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 2166 were performed on selected
in situ cohesive soil samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the
soils The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depth and graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plates B 7 and B 8
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D 1883 were performed
on bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils The samples were remolded to near the optimum moisture
content Test results are presented on Plates B 9 through B 11
One pH ASTM G 51 and one Minimum Resistivity ASTM G 57 were
performed on a selected soil sample obtained from our field exploration The test results
are summarized on Plates B 12 and B 13
