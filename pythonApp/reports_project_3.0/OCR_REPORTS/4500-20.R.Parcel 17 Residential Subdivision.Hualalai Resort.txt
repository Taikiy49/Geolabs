2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of Hualalai Mountain which
makes up the west central portion of the island The lava formation encountered at the
project site appears to be of both a a and pahoehoe type lava flows which spread and
ponded as they approached the ocean A a lava is typically characterized by a porous
rough and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments
including cobbles and boulders Pahoehoe lava is characterized by a smooth rope like
or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas especially pahoehoe type
lava Cavities are formed when the lava is still in a molten state and represent both lava
tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes
intra flow cavities are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a
hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the
flow Blisters and pockets inter flow cavities generally are smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water
quite readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is
transmitted through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and _ inter flow
contacts cavities and along clinker layers The permeability of the clinker layers and
cavities is high therefore they serve as the primary water transmission features

2 4 Earthquakes and Seismicity
in general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by
shifts in the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily
to volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or
during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The Island of
Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most of the earthquakes
are so small that only sensitive instruments could detect the small tremors However
some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to
moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on
the Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the
Island of Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii over 90 percent of
earthquakes are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic activity and degree of
ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the southern portion of the
Island of Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater
than Magnitude 6 M6 Based on information obtained from the United States

2 5 Seismic Design Considerations
The current building code in effect for the County of Hawaii is the 1991 Uniform
Building Code UBC Based on the 1991 UBC the Island of Hawaii is located within
Seismic Zone 3 with an effective peak ground acceleration of 0 30g However the
County of Hawaii has upgraded the seismic zonation for the Island of Hawaii from
Zone 3 to Zone 4 with an effective peak ground acceleration of 0 40g Therefore the
project site should be designed for strong ground shaking based on an average peak
ground acceleration of 0 40g in accordance with Seismic Zone 4
Based on the subsurface materials encountered at the project site basalt rock
formation and the geologic setting of the area the project site may be classified from a
seismic analysis standpoint as a rock site corresponding to a Type Soil Profile in
accordance with the 1991 UBC A seismic site coefficient of 1 0 may be used for
seismic analysis of new structures at the project site

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in
loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction General fills and backfills
should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 18 inches in loose thickness
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers
vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment
Rock fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of the
majority of the rocks or 5 feet whichever is less Placement should be by
haul trucks traversing previously placed lifts and dumping at the edge of the
currently placed lift Spreading of the rock fill should be by a large bulldozer to
facilitate seating of the rocks The rock fill should be watered heavily during
placement by water trucks traversing in front of the current rock lift face and
spraying water continuously during placement Compaction equipment such as a
Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar heavy compaction equipment providing suitable
energy to achieve the required compaction should be utilized The number of
passes usually six to eight passes to be made to achieve proper compaction
should be determined at the on set of site grading by the performance of a trial
compaction test or by plate bearing tests Subsequent layers of rock fill above the
previously placed rock fill should provide a choking layer of smaller sized boulder

Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material
left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and
description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD also is a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
