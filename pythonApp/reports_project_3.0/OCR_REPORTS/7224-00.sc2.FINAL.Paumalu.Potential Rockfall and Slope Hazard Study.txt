REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae and 
Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late 
Pliocene in age (2.7 – 3.4 million years ago) and forms the bulk of the western one-third of 
the island. The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early 
Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age (2.2 – 2.5 million years ago) and forms the majority of the 
eastern two-thirds of the island. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Waianae and Koolau shields, volcanic activity 
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations mainly at 
the southeastern portion of the Island of Oahu.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the 
sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such 
that the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic sea 
level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
filled pre-existing depressions and accumulated at higher elevations. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii and at the heads of valleys, erosional 
processes are dominated by detachment of soil and rock materials from the steep valley 
walls and transportation down slope toward a valley axis primarily by gravity as colluvial 
earth materials. Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley,

The project site encompasses Paumalu Gulch and associated foothill terrain at the 
mouth of the northwest to southeast trending gulch. Paumalu Gulch was formed by stream 
incision and mass wasting (gravity driven landslide and rockfall) processes which acted on 
the steepened gulch sidewalls as the deeper gulch erosion progressed. The gulch is 
incised into layered basaltic lavas erupted by the Koolau shield volcano. The lava flow 
deposition formed broad upland plateaus that were subsequently incised by stream 
erosion and mass wasting processes over hundreds of thousands of years during the early 
geomorphic evolution of the Island of Oahu. 
The gulch sidewalls show exposure of varying types of basaltic lava flows ranging 
from thinner and more fluid pahoehoe flows to thicker and more viscous a’a lavas with 
appreciable rubbly interbedded clinker seams. The a’a and thicker pahoehoe flows tend to 
form prominent rock ledge exposures that are generally more resistant to chemical and 
physical decomposition than surrounding thinner lava flows and clinker material. 
Paumalu Gulch is a primary area drainage that extends inland towards the 
Pupukea/Paumalu Forest Reserve in the Koolau Mountains. Several tributary streams 
from adjacent smaller gulches feed runoff to Paumalu Gulch. At the time of our site 
reconnaissance, stream flow was not observed in Paumalu Gulch within the project limits. 
Based on the drainage basin area involved, appreciable stream flows should be 
anticipated during storm conditions.

The CRSP is a computer program that is a widely accepted engineering tool used 
to estimate potential rockfall behavior by simulating rockfall activity based on input 
parameters that are assigned on a site-specific basis. The input parameters for this project 
were assigned based on our observations and measurements collected in the field. The

parameters used to quantify the typical ground surface conditions. Information obtained 
from the CRSP analysis includes the potential for rockfall initiation, and the predicted 
falling rock velocity, bouncing height, kinetic energy, and roll-out distance. The output 
information is useful to assist in the understanding of potential rockfall processes that may 
occur and the site-specific design of various rockfall mitigation schemes such as 
development setbacks and other rockfall containment and control systems such as 
catchment ditch and rockfall impact barrier structures. The summarized results obtained 
from the CRSP analysis are presented in Appendix B, Plate B-1.

support of our CRSP analysis. The model slope profiles generated from the USGS 
topographic information were refined based on our reconnaissance of the slope along the 
model profile line. 
Our rockfall simulation analysis was performed using falling rock characterized as 
spherical basalt rocks with varying dimensions of 3 to 5 feet in diameter based on the 
observation of the existing conditions. The simulated falling rock sizes were randomly 
initiated from specified interval positions on the modeled slope.  Numerical input 
coefficients were used, which approximated the observed typical ground surface relief and 
roughness conditions. For each rockfall simulation run, 1,000 simulated source rocks of 
each rock diameter were utilized to develop a statistical distribution of the results.  
It should be noted that our rockfall protection criterion for the design of rockfall 
safety setbacks and various falling rock interception systems (rockfall impact barrier fence,

target that is commonly used in engineering practice for evaluation criteria that can be 
quantified by a statistical and probability analysis using model data for natural occurrences 
such as rockfall activity. Since the analyses are based on statistics and probability, a 
100 percent criterion is typically impractical to achieve.

Potential Rockfall Hazards 
Rockfall is a type of natural mass wasting process that is in-part responsible for the 
evolution of mountain slopes. Rockfall is a common natural slope hazard due to the steep 
volcanic terrain comprising the mountains and valleys of the Islands of Hawaii. Rockfall 
activity involves the detachment of a rock mass from the slope face and the fall of rock 
materials under the force of gravity. Rockfall may involve a single unstable block of rock 
outcrop or it may involve a rockslide which encompasses multiple rock components.  
Falling rock behavior can be complicated by the varying character of the ground 
surface and topography encountered along the rock fall line. Rocks may either roll, 
bounce, or have combined movement vectors. Falling rock may also hang up temporarily 
or permanently on the slope without reaching the bottom under the effects of terrain 
roughness, topography, vegetation, and the presence of natural features such as berms 
and terraces formed by other rock outcroppings. 
Under the current state of engineering practice combined with the use of proven 
rockfall control methods, it is still impractical to mitigate 100% of all potential rockfall 
activity at a site. However, with careful engineering and quality construction during the 
installation of appropriate rockfall mitigation controls, the risk of rockfall activity can be 
substantially reduced.  
For this project, the identified potential rockfall hazards represent ledge rock 
exposures of hard, massive to slightly fractured basalt rock that span laterally across the

identified an estimated safe setback boundary for future dwelling development at the 
mouth of Paumalu Gulch. Based on our analysis, we believe that development below

Based on this preliminary study, it appears that the most feasible rockfall protection 
alternative may consist of the installation of rockfall impact barrier fencing above the

Rock slope scaling is the careful, systematic removal of loose rock and debris from 
a slope face using manual labor methods such as hand and pry bar removal. Rock slope 
scaling is typically performed by an experienced scaling crew(s) working methodically with 
hand tools from top-down and across the slope face. Care is taken to avoid 
over-steepening of the slope and the creation of new rock slope instability.  
Rockfall impact barriers is a specialized flexible fence constructed of steel support 
posts with micropile and concrete block foundations, and wire mesh nets attached 
between support posts via a network of cable ground anchors and braking devices. The 
barrier fences are designed to safely catch and absorb high energy (velocity) falling rock.  
The certified barriers are manufactured in multiple industry standard energy classes and 
heights to meet site-specific rock interception needs. The primary advantage of the barrier

about 1.5H:1V. The maximum slope height tapers down toward the west along the 
ridgeline containing the water storage tank and transmission pipeline. Based on our 
observations, the upper slope surfaces consist of clayey soils with some widely 
scattered rock outcroppings and two rock ledges spanning discontinuously across 
the slope at different elevations. The approximate location of the rock ledges, 
identified as Ledge No. 1 and Ledge No. 2, are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 3. 
The majority of both rock ledges were observed encased in a dense network of 
large banyan tree roots which appear to aid in erosion control and binding of the 
rock mass. Potential rockfall produced from Ledge Nos. 1 and 2 appear to be very 
limited due to the stable slope setting and solid rock mass character. 
The lower slope surfaces generally consist of areas of nested boulder deposits and 
clayey soils. Vegetation throughout the slope is heavy and consists of shrubbery, 
trees and scattered very large banyan trees with wide trunks and extensive 
networks of aerial roots spanning from the limbs to the ground surfaces. Based on 
our observations and the CRSP results, the lower slope conditions (rough ground 
surfaces and dense banyan growth) should act as a good natural buffer for 
potential falling rock from the upper slope. 
Based on our reconnaissance, some other scattered rock outcroppings were 
encountered on the hillside. A stable appearing but high elevation rock ledge 
exposure of about 20 feet in sub-vertical height (identified as Ledge No. 2) was 
encountered at the approximate location shown on the Site Plan, Plate 3. The 
ledge outcropping appeared solid, intact, and continuous with limited fractures and 
no visible sign of potential instability. However, a single un-rooted, angular-shaped, 
5-foot basaltic boulder was encountered at the outer edge of the ledge top. Based 
on our observation, the boulder appears to have a center of gravity near the edge 
of the ledge; therefore, there is potential for rock mobilization and release from the 
ledge, if impacted by a substantial external force such as a large earthquake or 
proximal mechanized construction activity. We recommend stabilization treatment 
of the existing un-rooted boulder to reduce the potential rockfall hazard on the 
northwest facing slope. Stabilization treatment may include controlled demolition 
and fragment dispersal or stabilization in-place with appropriate rock bolt and/or 
netting installation. 
The lower mountain slope below Ledge Nos. 1 and 2 and above the gently sloping 
pediment contained extensive boulder deposits that appear to have been placed 
and tracked by machinery into well seated stabilized deposits. The stable lower 
slope boulder deposits may be a result of the water storage tank and utility

South Side of Gulch Mouth 
We conducted a foot traverse of the north-facing slope at the mouth of the southern

dwellings on an adjacent property. Based on our observations and confirmed by

most potential rockfall emanating from upslope. Based on the CRSP results, the 
existing ditch structure is an effective rockfall catchment as the statistical analysis 
shows all potential rockfall involving boulders up to about 5 feet in spherical

analysis of potential rockfall activity only. Variation in the surface and subsurface

extent of these variations may not become evident until additional field exploration or

re-evaluate the findings and recommendations provided herein. 
It should be noted that slopes composed of rock materials (rock slopes) deteriorate 
with the passage of time due to natural weathering processes, wet-dry and hot-cold 
cycles, and erosion conditions.  Due to the inherent deterioration of rock slopes resulting 
from natural processes (weathering, wet-dry and hot-cold cycles, and erosion conditions), 
the potential for rockfall hazard at any site changes with the passage of time.  Therefore, 
the findings and recommendations contained herein may be used only within a reasonable 
time from the date of issuance of this letter report.  Land use, site conditions, and/or other 
factors may change with the passage of time.  Therefore, additional work to evaluate the 
applicability of the findings and preliminary recommendations contained in this letter report 
due to changes with the passage of time will be required.
