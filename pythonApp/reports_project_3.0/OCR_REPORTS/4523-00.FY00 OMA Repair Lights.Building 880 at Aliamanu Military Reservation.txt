REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated
to be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to
early Pleistocene in age As the volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from
Koolau banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known
as the Schofield Plateau
After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion incised deep
valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows
followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the Honolulu
Volcanic Series
The project site is located near the center of the Allamanu vent of the Honolulu
Volcanic Series As the molten magma ascended through these vents it reacted with
the groundwater generating steam This steam caused explosive eruptions which
deposited low cones of ash material that have consolidated to form tuff locally referred
to as mudrock
The Aliamanu tuff is an older water laid ash deposit that was laid down when the
sea level was higher than the present level The Aliamanu eruptive event deposited its
material over alluvium originating from Halawa Stream

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration indicates that the proposed light pole locations are generally
underlain by medium stiff silty clay overlying hard or very dense alluvium to the depths
explored In consideration of the lateral loads and moments acting on the foundations of
the proposed light pole foundations we recommend that drilled shafts embedded in the
clayey soils be used to support the proposed lighting system We recommend that the
drilled shafts be embedded a minimum of 5 feet below the lowest adjacent finished

grades for the proposed light pole foundations located on level grounds Detailed
discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering recommendations are

Drilled Shaft Foundations
In order to develop the required bearing and lateral load resistances the proposed
new light pole structures may be supported by a foundation system consisting of
cast in place concrete drilled shafts Based on the base plate dimensions and our lateral
analysis for the new light pole structures we envision that a minimum diameter of
24 inches should be used for the proposed drilled shaft foundations Based on the
anticipated structural loads and the subsoil conditions encountered at the project site we
recommend that drilled shaft foundations with an embedment length of not less than
5 feet below the design finished grades be used for the new light pole structures located
on level ground conditions If the actual loading of the light pole is higher than the
assumed structural loads the drilled shaft foundation should be re evaluated for the new
foundation demands
The cast in place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally
from skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding soils An allowable
compressive load capacity of up to 15 kips per shaft may be used for dead plus live loads
and may be increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces The anticipated settlements of the drilled shaft foundations due to
compression loads are estimated to be on the order of less than 0 25 inches We
believe that the settlements are elastic and should occur as the loads are applied
Lateral loads imposed on the light pole foundations may be resisted by resistance
of the shaft soil system deflection Lateral load resistance of the drilled shaft is a function
of the stiffness of the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable deflection at
the top of the shaft and induced moment in the shaft Based on the information
provided each drilled shaft will be subjected to a lateral load of 680 pounds and an
overturning moment of about 9 000 pound feet at the ground surface Based on our
GEOLABS INC
W O 4523 00 Page 7
foundation analyses the maximum moment induced in the drilled shaft increases slightly
to 9 750 pound feet at a depth of about 1 foot below the top of the shaft Our lateral load
analyses indicated that the estimated deflection at the top of the drilled shaft should be
less than 0 25 inches
Construction Considerations
Drilled shafts are desirable for the light pole foundations because of the significant
increase in lateral and uplift load capacities when compared to shallow foundations
However the performance of the drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the
contractor s method of construction and construction procedures In our opinion the
following conditions would have a substantial effect on the effectiveness and cost of the
drilled shaft foundations
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in drilling the
shaft holes and placing concrete into the holes
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids is not recommended Placement of
concrete by wet construction methods using a tremie pipe will be required where
groundwater is encountered in the drilled holes Concrete should be placed in a suitable
manner by displacing the water in an upward fashion from the bottom of the drilled hole
A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 6 to 9 inch range should be used to
provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Concrete
should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce the potential for segregation of the
aggregates from the concrete mix If precast concrete pole bases are used the gap
between the pole base and the surrounding soils should be backfilled with concrete or
lean concrete A representative from Geolabs should be present to observe the
installation of drilled shafts during construction to confirm the subsoil conditions and
embedment depths

Utility Trench
We envision that new electrical duct lines will be required to service the proposed
new lighting system In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of clean sand or
S4C is recommended under the duct lines Free draining granular materials such as
clean sand or S4C should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 6 inches
above the duct lines to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the
compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is
critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Improper backfill material and placement procedures could result in settlement of the
backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 6 inches above the duct lines
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on site soils or imported select
granular fill material The backfill should be placed in maximum 6 inch level loose lifts
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet
of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established
in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water
content corresponding to the maximum dry density

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of
about 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using
a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and
drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
