REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of the lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene
Epoch more than 2 million years before present Following the cessation of this
main shield building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of
basaltic lavas of the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition
of the alluvial sediments of the Palikea Formation
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic series cover most of the eastern half of the island
of Kauai Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic series are generally characterized as thick flows
of dense basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various
locales Associated with the vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low
cinder cones at the vent
During the Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result
of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available
to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea
levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Kauai remained
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature
These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher
and lower relative to present sea level on Kauai
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs

Most of the soils in the project area are residual soils derived from the in situ
weathering of igneous rocks Thin beds of ash and tuffaceous soils are intercalated

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the proposed water tank site is underlain by very
stiff to hard silty and clayey soils Based on our evaluation of the subsurface conditions
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3312 00 Page 8
with respect to the proposed water tank construction the principal geotechnical
engineering considerations for the project include the following
1 foundation support for the proposed water tank
2 site preparation and pavement design for the access road and
3 trench excavation and backfill of the water line
In general the water tank will likely be founded on the very stiff silty and clayey
soils As with any storage tank total and differential settlements are important design
considerations Based on our boring data we believe that a shallow foundation system
consisting of spread footings and or continuous ring footing may be used to support the
water tank
Detailed discussions and recommendations for design of foundation site
grading trench excavation and backfill pavements and other geotechnical aspects
of the project are presented in the following sections of this report
Foundations
Based on the subsurface soil conditions encountered at the project site we
believe that the on site very stiff clayey and silty soils would provide suitable foundation
support for the proposed water tank structure Continuous ring and or spread footings
may be used to support the proposed water tank structure The ring and or spread
footings should bear directly on the stiff native clayey and silty soils
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 500 p s f may be used for design of
the shallow foundations bearing directly on the stiff native soils The allowable bearing
GEOLABS HAWAIl
W O 3312 00 Page 9
pressure is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third for transient
loads such as temporary wind and or seismic forces
Footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grade If footings are located adjacent to slopes the footings should be
embedded deep enough to provide a minimum 8 foot setback distance measured
laterally from the outside edge of the footings to the slope face Where footings will be
located adjacent to below grade structures or utilities the foundations should extend
to a depth below an imaginary 45 degree plane projected upward from the bottom of
the below grade structure or utility line trench This requirement is necessary to avoid
surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for foundation settlement
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the footings and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings
A coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used for the friction between the base of the
footings and the subgrade soils Resistance due to passive pressure may be
calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of
depth p c f These values assume that the concrete for footings is placed directly
against the footing excavations Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive
resistance in the upper 12 inches below the finish grade should be neglected
If the footings are designed in accordance with the recommendations presented
herein total settlement of footings is estimated to be on the order of 1 inch with
differential settlements on the order of inch
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3312 00 Page 10
If shrinkage cracks appear in the footing excavations they should be thoroughly
moistened to close all cracks prior to placement of concrete Therefore foundation
subgrade should be thoroughly wetted prior to concrete placement to allow it to remain
in a moist condition
If soft and or loose soils are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavation
they should be over excavated to expose competent native material and replaced with
select granular material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement
weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and
replacement with well compacted select granular material
It is important that a representative from Geolabs Hawaii observe the foundation
excavations and placement of structural backfills prior to placement of reinforcing steel
to evaluate the competency of the bearing materials and the embedment depths
Slab On Grade
Concrete slabs on grade will be used for the interior of the proposed water tank
Based on the ring swell test results the near surface soils exhibit slight to moderate
shrink swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in moisture content
Therefore we recommend that slabs on grade be supported on 6 inches of base
course compacted to 95 percent relative compaction to reduce the shrink swell potential
and to provide a uniform support for the slabs on grade A modulus of subgrade
reaction of 200 p c i may be used in the design of the slab foundation A coefficient

We anticipate that minor fills may be required for this project In general
permanent fill slopes constructed of the compacted on site materials may be

Drainage
The on site surficial soils are susceptible to erosion As a result water should
be diverted away from the tops of slopes and adequate slope planting should be done
as soon as possible to reduce the potential for erosion Concrete V ditches may be
provided at the top of slopes to reduce the potential for erosion especially on fresh cut
slopes Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to
drain surface run off away from the slab foundations and the edges of slopes
The finish grades outside the proposed water tank should be sloped to shed
water away from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive
landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided Planters
next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce
water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
foundations constructed on cohesive soils since ponded water could cause subsurface
soil saturation and subsequent loss of strength of the near surface soils The
foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings
immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce water infiltration

Trench Excavation
It is anticipated that the trenches for the water line will be excavated in the stiff
to hard silty and clayey soils as encountered in Boring Nos 4 through 6 and in
cobbles boulders and moderately to highly weathered basalt rock formation as
encountered in Boring No 7
Based on the subsurface soil conditions encountered it is anticipated that
conventional excavating equipment may be used to excavate the trench in the
stiff to hard soils encountered However it should be noted that cobbles
boulders and basalt rock formation encountered in our Boring No 7 may require
hard excavation such as chipping and ripping In general the method and
equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by the contractor
subject to practical limits and safety considerations
We believe that trench walls on the order of about 5 feet or less may stand near
vertical without support for a period of a few days Where trenches will extend
to depths deeper than about 5 feet shoring of the sides of the trenches will likely
be necessary to protect personnel working in the trench The slopes and sides
of the excavations must comply with all federal state and local requirements
The excavated soils should not be stockpiled within the zone of a 45 degree
imaginary line projected upward from the toe of the excavation to reduce the
potential for ground movement toward the excavation
Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the
load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the pipe bedding is an important
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3312 00 Page 16
design consideration For pipes bearing on the stiff to hard soils and basalt rock
formation a poorly graded granular bedding material with minimum fines such
as S4C or equivalent is recommended below the pipes Other alternate pipe
cushion material may consist of black sand manufactured sand coral and
crusher screenings S4C containing minimum fines and no particles larger than
1 inch in maximum dimension In general the bedding material should meet the
minimum requirements specified by the County of Kauai Department of Water

12 inches above the top of the water line Poorly graded granular materials are

Field Resistivity Testing
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteris
tics with regard to the corrosivity of the soil In general the lower the resistivity the
greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure Conversely the
higher the resistivity the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion
A soil resistivity survey was performed to obtain an overview of the electrical
resistivity of the subsurface soils at the site In general soil resistivity surveys utilize
the in Pin Method to evaluate the corrosion rating of the near surface
soils within the depths of the proposed water line For this project the average depth
of the proposed water line is anticipated to be approximately 4 to 6 feet below the
existing ground surface A summary of the evaluated soil resistances the computed
resistivities and the corresponding corrosion ratings is presented on Plate C 1

APPENDIX C
Field Resistivity Testing
Geophysical survey equipment i e resistance meter was mobilized to the site
on August 1st and our field work was performed in sunny conditions with generally dry
surface conditions Our work on this portion of the project was performed in general
conformance with ASTM Test Designation G 57 78 and with the requirements provided
in the Water System External Corrosion Control Standards Volume 3 dated 1991
Sixteen soil resistivity tests were conducted at about 300 foot intervals at eight
locations along the proposed water line alignment using the in Pin
Method and a resistance meter Strata Scout R 40C manufactured by SoilTest Inc
with 120 volts and 24 watts output into the ground This method utilizes four surface
probes or pins electrodes to evaluate the average resistivity an indication of
corrosivity of the underlying soil mass The average resistivities of the soil mass
extending from the existing ground surface to the planned invert elevations of the water
line were assessed by using this method at the test locations listed on Plate C 1 The
approximate test locations are shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 and 3
The average soil resistance was evaluated at each test location with the
electrodes aligned at equal spacings of generally 4 and 6 feet at the existing ground
surface Among the spacings used one may have more significance than the other for
allowing more contribution to the evaluated soil resistivity from the closest mass of soil
to the proposed pipe invert Such spacings have the same distance as the depth from
the existing ground surface at the test locations to the proposed pipe invert Such
spacings are flagged with an asterisk on Plate C 1
The resistance meter R 40C impresses a voltage between the outer electrodes
causing an electrical current to flow between them The voltage drop between the inner
electrodes is then measured by a sensitive built in voltmeter As a result the soil
resistance in ohms can be found directly on the dial The soil resistivity in ohm cm
which is directly related to both resistance and spacing can then be computed The
evaluated soil resistances the computed resistivities and the corresponding corrosion
ratings are presented on Plate C 1
