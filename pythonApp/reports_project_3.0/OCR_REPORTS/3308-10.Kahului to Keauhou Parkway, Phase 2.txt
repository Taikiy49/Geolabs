2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is situated on the lower western flank of the Hualalai Volcano
which composes the west central portion of the Island of Hawaii Most of the near surface
basaltic lava formation at the site appears to be composed of the pahoehoe type lava flow
which spread and ponded as the flow approached the flatter terrain bordering the ocean
Pahoehoe lava is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal
structure of vesicular porous rock
In addition to the pahoehoe type lava some land area of the project site appears to
be locally covered by a a lava flows The ava lavas are typified by the appearance of
loose rubbly clinker layers interspersed with seams of very dense massive basalt rock
The dense basalt rock layers represent the hard interior core of the individual lava flow
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas especially the more fluid
pahoehoe lava Cavities are formed when the underground molten lava flow is insulated
from cooling occurring at the ground surface Cavities in lava rock represent both laterally
extensive lava tubes intra flow cavities and isolated blisters and pockets inter flow
cavities Lava tubes are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow interior
leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally
along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally closed cavity features that are usually
smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted
through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and

along clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they
serve as the primary water transmission features
2 2 Faulting and Seismicity
Based on a review of available geologic information for the region there are no
known mapped geologic faults located in the vicinity of the project site Furthermore the
absence of long parallel fissure vents normal faults and open surface cracks suggests
that volcanic rift extension and intrusion of magma that characterize the active rift zone
activity of Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes do not appear to operate on Hualalai
Volcano However it should be noted that Hualalai Volcano is not considered to be
dormant as recent age volcanic eruptions occurred in 1801 in the vicinity of Keahole
located about 11 miles northerly of Kailua Kona In addition a month long swarm of
earthquakes that occurred in 1929 at Hualalai indicates that the potential for movement of
magma below the volcano remains The potential for future eruption of Hualalai Volcano
is possible although the location and magnitude of possible future eruptions are not well
understood
It should be noted that a large normal fault system is located in the vicinity of
Kealakekua Bay which is located approximately 6 5 miles toward the south from the
southern terminus of the proposed alignment This active fault system is associated with
Mauna Loa Volcano The seaward block of land associated with the fault system has
been displaced in a downward and seaward direction due to seismic activity occurring
along the fault trace
Due to the location of the project site on the Island of Hawaii the site is considered
to be located in a seismic zone with moderate to high potential for recurring seismic
activity Earthquake activity and fault rupture of the ground surface are possibilities
however the location of the project site with respect to the relatively distant location of
known active faults reduces some risk for fault rupture
It should be noted that earthquake activity on the Island of Hawaii is usually
associated with the underground movement of magma beneath the active volcanoes of

limited space for construction of a temporary shoring system The soil nail retaining
wall system would serve both as temporary shoring of the excavation and the
permanent retaining wall at these locations A soil nail retaining wall system
consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars grouted into drilled holes to
stabilize an excavation or slope A shotcrete facing is generally applied to the face
of the reinforced excavation to provide the appearance of a conventional concrete
retaining wall The reinforcing bars provide both additional tensile and shear
strengths to the soil and reinforce the side wall of the excavation The reinforced
soil mass behaves in a manner similar to a gravity retaining wall but lacks a footing

slope inclination of 1H 12V or flatter

24 to 48 inches in diameter traversing the proposed realigned roadway will be installed
These pipe culverts will generally consist of reinforced concrete pipe culverts In addition
we also understand that eight aluminum pipe arch culverts are planned at the site The
eight aluminum pipe arch culverts are generally large diameter culverts ranging from single
barrel to a multiple of five barrels
Based on the subsurface conditions observed during our site reconnaissance we
anticipate that the proposed culvert structures would be underlain by medium hard to hard
basalt rock To provide for uniform support of the pipe culverts including the aluminum
pipe arch culverts we recommend that the culverts be supported on a minimum of
6 inches of bedding material consisting of free draining granular materials such as
AASHTO M 43 No 67 gradation material or equivalent
The initial zone of backfill material extending from the bedding up to 12 inches
above the top of the pipe culverts should consist of open graded material such as
AASHTO M 43 No 67 gradation material It is critical that free draining granular materials
be used to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to
provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper backfill around the pipe
culverts could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes

Runoff Disposal Systems
We understand that drywells are planned for stormwater runoff disposal for the
Kahului to Keauhou Parkway project Drywells on the Island of Hawaii are typically 5 feet
in diameter and 20 feet in depth The actual size and depth of the stormwater runoff
disposal system may vary and would depend on the design quantity of the surface water
runoff Extension of the drywell from the planned 20 foot depth may be required
depending on the specific site conditions at the proposed drywell locations Therefore we
recommend that unit prices for additional drilling be obtained during bidding Provisions
should be incorporated into the construction documents to extend the drywell depths from
the planned 20 feet to about 60 feet below the ground surface using a minimum 15 inch
diameter extension hole or 5 foot diameter drilling equipment The unit prices for drywell
extension should be obtained in 20 foot increments
Due to the potentially variable disposal rates of the subsurface conditions
anticipated along the length of the roadway alignment we believe that an average
disposal capacity on the order of about 4 to 6 cubic feet per second cfs may be used for
the preliminary design of a typical 5 foot diameter 20 foot deep drywell It should be
noted that the type of drilling operation may alter the hydraulic characteristics of formation
materials at the edges of the borehole and may result in significant reduction of the
hydraulic conductivity of the drilled hole We envision that the drilling fluid may seal the
permeable surfaces of the drywell Therefore pressure washing or water jetting to remove
the seal should be included in the specifications if the drywells are to be installed by
drilling The contractor should forward the proposed excavation method and equipment to
be used on the project to Geolabs for evaluation and approval Geolabs should review the
submittal and assess the impact of drywell construction procedures on the permeability
characteristics
Our field exploration indicated that volcanic ash deposits may be encountered at
shallow depths in localized areas along the roadway alignment Therefore we

3 8 Underground Water Lines
We understand that a new 12 inch diameter water line is planned along the new
roadway alignment It is anticipated that the trenches for the water line will be excavated
in the basalt rock formation or compacted fill material placed for the proposed construction
As previously recommended placement of boulders within the utility trench corridors and
within the anticipated depths of the utility lines should be avoided to reduce the problems
associated with excavating boulders from within a new utility trench
In general granular bedding consisting of 1 inch minus well graded granular
material aggregate base course is recommended under the pipes The 1 inch minus
well graded granular material aggregate base course should also be used for the initial
trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around
the pipes The bedding and initial trench backfill consisting of aggregate base course
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to
at least 90 percent relative compaction Improper trench backfill could result in settlement
of the backfill and damage to the pipes

12 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the new bridge site were explored by drilling and
sampling four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 extending to depths ranging
from about 35 3 to 55 5 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled
using both truck mounted and portable drilling equipment equipped with coring tools The
approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 3
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock formation and or boulder materials encountered at the
site were obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core
drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation
The material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination
and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and would
be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80

drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
