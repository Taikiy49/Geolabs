REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers
an area of approximately 4000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of the
following five shield volcanoes Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea activity during recent
geologic time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 1803 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea
both still active
The project site is within the lower elevations of Hilo Town which is situated on the
saddle between the southeastern flank of Mauna Kea and the northeastern flank of
Mauna Loa The near surface lava flows in this area are from the Mauna Loa Shield
Volcano overlying lavas from the Mauna Kea Shield Volcano The lava flows and
pyroclastic deposits from Mauna Kea are believed to be of Pliocene to Pleistocene in age
and are classified as members of the Hamakua Volcanic Series The near surface lava
flows and pyroclastic deposits from Mauna Loa are less than 1 500 years old
The lava formation at the site appears to be pahoehoe which is characterized by a
smooth rope like or billowy surface with an internal structure of vesicular porous rock
Cavities are commonly encountered in this type of lava flow Cavities form when lavas are
still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and
pockets inter flow cavities While blisters and pocket type cavities are generally small in
horizontal extent lava tubes may extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is at the northeastern corner of the intersection of Kilauea Avenue
and Kahaopea Street in Hilo Hawaii The area of the site covers approximately one acre
43 560 square feet and is rectangular in shape
The site is bounded by a service road for the Puainako Center to the east
Kahaopea Street to the south Kilauea Avenue to the west and private property to the
north as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 At the time of our field exploration it appeared
that the eastern half of the lot had previously been grubbed and graded while the western
half was heavily overgrown with small shrubs and 8 foot high wild grasses Several large
cobbles and small boulders were observed throughout the site
The topography of the site slopes upward from northeast to southwest with ground
surface elevations ranging from 120 to 134 feet Mean Sea Level MSL

FINDINGS AND DISCUSSIONS
Based on our field exploration at the proposed new parking lot site the subsurface
conditions consist of surface fill and medium stiff to stiff volcanic ash deposits overlying
hard slightly weathered basalt formation extending to the maximum depth excavated of
about 6 feet below the ground surface We did not encounter groundwater in the test pits
Based on relatively competent soil conditions and the relatively light to moderate
traffic loading anticipated we recommend using pavement sections consisting of 2 inch
asphaltic concrete and 6 inch aggregate base course over compacted subgrade for the
new parking lot
Detailed discussions and recommendations for pavement design site grading and
other geotechnical aspects are presented in the following sections herein
Pavement Design
Based on the current design concepts a new parking lot is planned with grading
consisting of cuts and fills less than 3 feet We envision that asphaltic concrete pavements
will be designed for the parking lot In general we anticipate that the vehicle loading will
primarily consist of passenger vehicles and light pick up trucks Considering the generally

Flexible Pavement Section

The subgrade soils below the new pavement areas should be scarified to a depth
of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to
no less than 95 percent relative compaction
As previously mentioned the basalt rock outcrop was encountered at shallow
depths of about 1 to 1 5 feet in the middle portion of the site Therefore the excavation for
the pavement section would likely encounter hard rock The contractor should have the
appropriate equipment and tools to excavate through these hard basalt formation where
encountered A sufficient contingency fund should be included in their bids
The aggregate base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregates
compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction CBR and field density tests
should be performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered during construction to
confirm the adequacy of the above section
In general paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be
maintained to carry surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be
allowed on site during or after construction
Site Grading
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly It should be noted portions of the project site are heavily
vegetated Vegetation concrete debris deleterious materials and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed properly off site to reduce the potential for
contaminating the excavated materials
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas
designated to receive fill should be over excavated to expose firm natural material and the
resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted general fill The excavated
soft soils should be disposed properly off site

In general the over excavated subgrades and areas designated to receive fills
exposing soils should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction
Qualified technical personnel should observe site grading operations to evaluate
whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and scarification
process and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those encountered in
our exploration
Imported material used as select granular fill should be non expansive granular
material such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The select granular fill
should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest
dimension The material should also contain less than 15 percent particles passing the
No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 25 or more and should
have a maximum swell value of 1 percent or less Imported fill materials should be tested
for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the
intended use
In general fills and backfills should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Fills and backfills within 3 feet of the
pavement grade elevation should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density Compaction should be accomplished by
sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment
Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed to compact the fills
It should be noted that on site volcanic ash soils generally exist in a moist to wet
condition and are very difficult to compact Therefore the ash materials should be
removed off site or blended with the excavated rock material
Our field exploration disclosed that the near surface basalt rock formation is
generally categorized as slightly weathered and hard Cobbles and boulders were also
encountered in the surface fill layer with localized concrete and steel debris In general
our opinion is that conventional heavy excavation equipment such as a large bulldozer
excavator or similar heavy construction equipment may achieve the excavations into
these materials However excavations into the harder areas will likely require the use of
hoerams or chipping
The method and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by the
contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations The excavations should
comply with all applicable local safety requirements The above discussions regarding the

Underground Utility Trenches
New utility lines and utility connections will likely be needed in support of the
proposed project In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining
granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should
also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes
or 12 inches above the groundwater level to provide adequate support around the
pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation
of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of
the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage
to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level of 12 inches above the pipes
or groundwater level to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the
excavated on site soils The backfill materials should be moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture content placed in about 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
appreciable future ground subsidence Where trenches are located below pavement
areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be
compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction
