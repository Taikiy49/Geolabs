2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Kauai was built up by a single large shield volcano During the
Tertiary Period basaltic lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series built a roughly
circular island to a height over 17 000 feet above the surrounding sea floor Near the
end of the Tertiary Period this main shield building volcanic phase came to a halt
Following a long period of erosion and subsidence vulcanism was renewed
during the Pleistocene Epoch with the Koloa Volcanic Series This post erosional
volcanic activity laid a veneer of fresh lava over the older Waimea Canyon Series
across most of the western half of the island
After the cessation of this period of renewed vulcanism the geologic forces of
weathering erosion and deposition during the late Pleistocene and Recent Epochs
have shaped the island to its present form
The rocks in the vicinity of the project site have been mapped as Koloa Basalts
In some locations a veneer of Recent Alluvium overlies the Koloa lavas In general
Koloa Basalts are poorly to moderately permeable The alluvium is characterized as
noncalcareous unconsolidated alluvium generally deposited along stream valleys and
coastal plains

very stiff clayey soils overlying basalt rock formation extending to the maximum depth

Fill and Backfill Materials
General fill material may be used to raise the existing grades to the bottom of the
non expansive select granular fill layer recommended below the building slab
General fill materials needed to raise the existing grades to the proposed finished
subgrades may consist of excavated on site soils or soil from the proposed borrow
site provided that the materials are screened of over sized materials maximum
particle size of 3 inches and are free of vegetation and deleterious materials
The soils from the borrow site consisted generally of stiff silty clay residual soil The
laboratory tests indicate that the borrow site soils exhibit moderate to high
expansion potential therefore they should be kept below 24 inches of finished
grade under structures
The 12 to 24 inch thick non expansive select granular fill layer required under the
building slabs should consist of select granular material such as crushed coral
basalt gravel or cinder sand The material should be well graded from coarse to
fine with no particles greater than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain

should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum
swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation

Keystone retaining walls may be appropriate for the site The Keystone retaining
wall system is a composite mechanically stabilized earth MSE wall system which
utilizes high density polyethylene or other reinforcing elements to reinforce the
backfill zone and improve the shear strength of the reinforced soil zone This
composite MSE system essentially forms a gravity wall structure with an ability to
tolerate significant total and differential settlements In addition Keystone retaining
walls are also desirable due to the flexibility of the wall ease of construction high
load carrying capacity and economy

2 translational sliding 3 overturning stability and 4 overall slope stability

presented in the following subsections Some of the geotechnical parameters

APPENDIX C
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Twelve one inch ring swell tests were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results of the tests are
summarized on Plates C 1 and C 2
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
the near surface clayey soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils
The test results are presented on Plates C 3 and C 4
