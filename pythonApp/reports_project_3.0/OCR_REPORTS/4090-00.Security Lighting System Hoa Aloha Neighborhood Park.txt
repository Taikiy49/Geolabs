performed for the proposed Security Lighting System project within the existing Hoa
Aloha Neighborhood Park in the Salt Lake area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu
Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4090 00 Page 4
be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age As the volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from Koolau
banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as the
Schofield Plateau
The project site is located near the southeastern flank of the Aliamanu vent and
on the northeastern flank of the Salt Lake vent complex of the Honolulu Volcanic
Series As the molten magma ascended through these vents it reacted with the
groundwater generating steam This steam caused explosive eruptions which
deposited low cones of ash material that have consolidated to form tuff locally referred
to as mudrock
The Aliamanu tuff is an older water laid ash deposit that was laid down when the
sea level was higher than the present level The Aliamanu eruptive event deposited its
material over alluvium originating from Halawa Stream
After this phase of volcanic activity ceased sedimentary deposits accumulated
over the Aliamanu tuff before the next phase of volcanic activity began Then the
Salt Lake eruptions began when the sea level was at about 40 to 60 feet below its
present level The Salt Lake eruptive event deposited its material over earlier tuffs from
the older Aliamanu events
The water that accumulated in the crater was believed to originate from
depressional springs and rain water During periods of little rainfall the shallow ponded
water was reduced by evaporation and over the years the mineral salt content of the
ponded water increased Recently man has developed the crater floor by filling and
grading the area for residential and golf course purposes

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration indicates that the proposed light pole locations are generally
underlain by surficial granular soils overlying stiff silty clays or volcanic tuff formation at
greater depths In consideration of the lateral loads and moments acting on the
foundations of the proposed light pole structures we recommend that drilled shaft
foundations embedded in the upper granular soils be used to support the proposed
lighting system Drilled shaft embedment of not less than 5 feet below the design finish
grades are recommended for the proposed light pole foundations Detailed discussion of
these items and our geotechnical engineering recommendations are presented in the
following sections of this report
Drilled Shaft Foundations
In order to develop the required bearing and lateral load capacities the proposed
new light pole structures may be supported by a foundation system consisting of
cast in place concrete drilled shafts Based on the anticipated structural loads and the
subsoil conditions encountered at the project site we recommend that drilled shafts with a
minimum diameter of 24 inches be used to support the proposed light pole structures
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site a drilled shaft
embedment length of not less than 5 feet below the design finish grades may be used for
the light pole foundations at the subject site
The cast in place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally
from skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding soils An allowable
compressive load capacity of up to 8 kips per shaft may be used for dead plus live loads
and may be increased by one third for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces
The end bearing component of the drilled shafts should be discounted due to the
potential for caving in of the granular soils in the upper 5 feet that would preclude
obtaining a clean bottom during drilled shaft installation
Lateral loads imposed on the foundations may be resisted by a combination of the
following 1 resistance of the shaft soil system to deflect and 2 passive resistance on
GEOLABS HAWAIl
W O 4090 00 Page 7
the vertical faces of shaft caps where applicable Lateral load resistance of the drilled
shaft is a function of the stiffness of the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft
allowable deflection at the top of shaft and induced moment in the shaft Based on the
information provided each drilled shaft will be subjected to a lateral load of 0 5 kips and
an overturning moment of about 6 kip feet at the ground surface Our lateral load
analyses indicated that the estimated deflection at the top of the drilled shaft should be
less than inch
The anticipated settlements are estimated to be on the order of less than inch
We believe that a significant portion of the settlements is elastic and should occur as the
loads are applied
Construction Considerations
Drilled shafts are desirable to the subject project because of the significant
increase in lateral and uplift load capacities when compared to shallow foundations
However the performance of the drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the
contractor s method of construction and construction procedures In our opinion the
following conditions would have a significant effect on the effectiveness and cost of the
drilled shaft foundations
Loose Subsoils Dense Rock Formation
Based on our field exploration the proposed new light pole locations are generally
underlain by dense sandy gravel to a depth of about 10 feet below the existing
ground surface However loose sandy soils may exist in localized areas and also
within the design depths of the light pole foundations To reduce the potential for
caving in of the drilled holes temporary casing may be required during foundation
construction
Based on our field exploration hard volcanic tuff formation may be anticipated at
shallow depths at localized areas across the project site Therefore coring into the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4090 00 Page 8
dense volcanic tuff formation may be required Appropriate drilling equipment and
coring tools should be utilized by the drilled shaft contractor to install the drilled
shafts to the depths and dimensions recommended herein
Workmanship
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in
drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes
A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 6 to 9 inch range should be used to
provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soil The
concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce segregation of the
aggregates from the concrete mix If pre cast concrete pole bases are used the
gap between the pole base and the surrounding soils should be backfilled with
lean concrete A representative of Geolabs Hawaii should be present to observe
the installation of drilled shafts during construction
Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines will be required to service the proposed new
lighting system In general a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of clean sand or
S4C is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials such as clean
sand or S4C should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches
above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the
compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is
critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Improper backfill material and placement procedures could result in settlement of the
backfill and damage to the pipes
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4090 00 Page 9
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the duct
lines to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on site soils
or imported select granular fill material The backfill should be placed in maximum
6 inch level loose lifts moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the
potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the
upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to
a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Since volcanic tuff formation was encountered at shallow depths during our field
exploration it appears that the trench work for utility installation may involve excavation in
the volcanic tuff formation The method and equipment to be used for excavation should
be determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations
Generally the near surface weathered volcanic tuff formation may be excavated with
normal heavy excavation equipment such as ripping with large bulldozers However
excavations into the deeper formations may require the use of hoerams or chipping
Care must be exercised by the contractor to avoid over ripping which would disrupt the
structure of the rock formation resulting in a potential loss of bearing strength for footings
in its vicinity

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings to depths of about 15 and 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers

examination in the field by our engineer who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the
materials encountered is presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the logs of borings at the
appropriate sample depths
A pocket penetrometer test was performed on a selected cohesive soil sample
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Result of the pocket penetrometer
test is presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth
