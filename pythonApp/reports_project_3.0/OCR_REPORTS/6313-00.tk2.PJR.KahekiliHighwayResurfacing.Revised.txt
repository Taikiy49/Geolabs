SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration along the proposed roadway rehabilitation project generally
encountered variable subsurface materials ranging from medium stiff to very stiff sandy
silts stiff to very stiff silty clays loose to very dense silty sands as well as cobbly materials
in the shallow subsurface We did not encounter groundwater in the shallow borings at the
time of our field exploration
Because the project will involve the reconstruction of the existing roadway we have
performed a detailed economic comparison involving both flexible and rigid pavement
sections for the project in accordance with the latest guidelines provided by the State of
Hawaii Department of Transportation Highways Division Material Testing and Research
Branch
Based on our field exploration program and the current design concept we envision
that the new pavements for the project will be constructed over variable materials ranging
from sandy silts silty clays silty sands as well as cobbly materials in the shallow
subsurface Based on the anticipated subgrade conditions the traffic data provided for our
pavement analyses and the economic analysis performed it is our opinion that new
pavements for the roadway rehabilitation project may consist of 4 inches of AC over
5 inches of ACB on 6 inches of ASB It should be noted that CBR tests and or field
observations should be performed on the actual subgrade soils during construction to
confirm that the above pavement design section is adequate
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement surface
should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface water off
the pavement to appropriate drainage structures
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific
pavement design recommendations

engineering analyses performed for the Kahekili Highway Pavement Rehabilitation

Regional Geology
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes the older West Maui
Tertiary Epoch and the more recent East Maui also known as Haleakala Pleistocene
Epoch The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow gently sloping plain located between these
two volcanoes The project site is along the south and southwest flanks of West Maui
The volcanic rocks of the West Maui have been divided into three volcanic
series The Wailuku Volcanic Series is the oldest and is comprised of the basaltic lava
flows and associated pyroclastic and intrusive rocks that built the major shield volcano
The Wailuku Volcanic Series was covered by a thin discontinuous frosting of andesitic
and trachytic flows domes and pyroclastic deposits known as the Honolua Volcanic
Series After a long period of inactivity when erosion incised deep valleys into the
volcanic flows volcanic activity returned with the eruptions that produced the flows and
cones of the Lahaina Volcanic Series
In situ chemical weathering of the lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to
2 million years The weathering process has formed a mantle of residual and saprolitic
soils In general saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is typical of the
tropical weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation
with increased depth
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock mass from the slopes and movement of the material
downslope toward the axis of the valley by gravity to form colluvial deposits Once the
colluvium reaches streams in the valley alluvial processes become dominant and the
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium Large areas of alluvium can be
found in the lowland areas and bordering streams
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes
begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the
materials Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the
coarse grained portion of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or

Existing Site Pavement Conditions
The existing Kahekili Highway within the project limits is generally a two lane
undivided roadway except for the additional turn lanes at the intersection areas The
approximate limits for this roadway rehabilitation project generally begin at the
intersection of North Market Street Piihana Road and Mokuhao Road in the south and
extend for approximately 1 7 miles towards the north and terminate at the intersection
with Waiehu Beach Road as shown on the General Site Plan Plate 2 Based on our
field observations and the information provided the traffic volume along this portion of
the roadway may be considered low with the average daily traffic count on the order of
about 4 400 vehicles both directions in the Year 2010
Along the east side of the roadway alignment a small 2 foot wide by 1 5 foot
deep trapezoidal ditch runs parallel with the roadway throughout the project limits The
4 foot wide shoulder areas beyond the edge of pavement generally consist of short cut
grass and utility posts The surface soils along the shoulder areas below the vegetation
generally consist of brown sandy silts to dark brown silty clays with gravel The existing
Kahekili Highway roadway alignment generally slopes down from south to north with
the exception of the southernmost portion of the roadway for approximately 500 feet
where the roadway alignment slopes up from south to north
Our office conducted a site reconnaissance in May 2010 to collect general
information for the existing pavement conditions along the portion of the roadway under
consideration Detailed descriptions of our field surveys are presented in the following
subsections It should be noted that the term Structural Defects refers to areas where
the pavement deterioration are believed to be caused by failures in the underlying base

Areas with Minor to Moderate Distresses and Severity
Based on our site reconnaissance approximately one third of the existing
pavements under consideration falls into this category and appear to be generally
fair to good with no structural defects observed Overall pavement deterioration is

pavement was generally fair to good The approximate limits of these areas are
shown on the General Site Plan Plate 2
Areas with Base Subgrade Failures
In general the existing asphaltic concrete pavements under this category are in
poor condition with moderately to highly distressed areas Alligator and alligator
block cracking were observed to be widespread and generally moderate to high in
severity The poor areas consist of elongated severely distressed zones of alligator
block cracking with depressions of 1 to 2 inches rutting and some shoving of the
pavement within the tire lanes Potholes patched with AC material were observed at
various locations along the alignment The ride characteristic of the roadway was
observed to be generally fair to slightly irregular and bumpy in the areas of poor
pavements The approximate limits of these areas are shown on the General Site

Methodology of Pavement Design
The pavement structural section presented herein was generally determined
based on the methodology described in Chapter 3 of the Pavement Design Manual
dated March 2002 The Pavement Design Manual was prepared by the State of Hawaii
Department of Transportation Highways Division Materials Testing and Research
Branch The pavement design methodology is based on the Hveem Stabilometer
method developed and used by the California Department of Transportation Caltrans
The design procedures for rigid pavements are based on the design procedures of the
Portland Cement Association Thickness Design for Concrete Highway and Street
Pavements

Surface Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due
to saturation of the subbase and base course and or subgrade soils Therefore the
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be
maintained to carry all surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage
structures Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction
Development of good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in
the drainage structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement
deterioration or premature failure of the pavements

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway alignment by drilling and
sampling seven borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 7 The borings extended to
depths of about 6 to 6 5 feet below the existing pavement surface The approximate boring
locations are shown on the Site Plans Plates 3 1 through 3 8 The borings were drilled
using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous full time basis These classifications were further reviewed visually
and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with
ASTM D 2487 Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 7
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some samples
were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D 1586
Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths The
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows
required for the specific sampler type used The blow counts may need to be factored to
obtain the Standard Penetration Test SPT blow counts

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The test results are presented on the logs of borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Four Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits of the near surface clayey soils The
test results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
Three Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on
Plate B 2
Three laboratory Resistance R Value tests ASTM D 2844 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 3 through B 5
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test ASTM D 1883 was performed on a
bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The test results are presented on Plate B 6
