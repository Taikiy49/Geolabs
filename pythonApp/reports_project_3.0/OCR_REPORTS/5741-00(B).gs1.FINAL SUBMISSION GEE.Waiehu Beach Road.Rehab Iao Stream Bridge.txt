SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The subsurface conditions and soil properties at the Iao Stream Bridge site were 
estimated based on the results of our field exploration. As part of our work, we also 
evaluated the potential for geologic and seismic hazards including surface fault rupture, 
ground shaking, ground failure, tsunamis and seiches, and flooding. Based on our 
evaluation, the potential for these geologic and seismic hazards at the bridge site is low to 
moderate. However, a substantial earthquake caused by volcanic activity and faulting may 
result in strong ground shaking at the bridge site. 
Based on the design criteria provided, the bridge project site will need to be 
designed based on a seismic acceleration coefficient of 0.28g. Based on the borings, the 
bridge site is generally underlain by medium dense to very dense soils. The site-specific 
response spectrum (site specific design ground motion) was generated in general 
accordance with the AASHTO LRFD Design Criteria for the bridge site. The elastic 
response spectrum was prepared based on materials with 5 percent of critical damping. 
In order to evaluate the lateral load resistance of the existing bridge structure, 
foundation capacities and stiffness modeling parameters were estimated based on the 
boring information. In general, our engineering analysis for stiffness modeling parameters 
of the foundations included the following: 
1. 
Bearing Capacities of Shallow Foundations 
2. 
Lateral Load Resistance of Shallow Foundations 
3. 
Lateral Load Resistance of Abutment Fills 
4. 
Active Lateral Earth Pressure 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
recommendations for the evaluation and design of this project.

1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the design of foundations for the Waiehu Beach Road (Route 3400), 
Between Mileposts 1.50 and 1.65, Rehabilitation of Iao Steam Bridge project located in 
the Wailuku area on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity 
are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our 
geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the design of bridge 
foundations only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to 
the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The existing Waiehu Beach Road is a two-lane undivided roadway classified as 
“Primary Rural Arterial.”  The existing roadway consists of two 11-foot lanes with 3-foot 
to 5-foot wide shoulders.  The project will entail widening the existing roadway and 
bridge structure to accommodate two 12-foot lanes with 8-foot wide shoulders in each 
travel direction. The scope of work included the analysis and design for the seismic 
retrofit of the existing bridge structure. The geotechnical analysis was performed to 
provide a “no collapse” design for a seismic event.  
The existing bridge structure is a three-span bridge with a span length of about 
176 feet measured from abutment to abutment.  The span length between the piers is 
about 68 feet.  The span length between the piers and abutments is about 54 feet.  The 
bridge was built in circa 1954.  The abutments and piers are spanned by reinforced 
concrete tee girders.   
The abutment structures are supported on continuous strip footings with widths 
of about 13.5 and 14.3 feet for the North Abutment and about 12 and 13 feet for the 
South Abutment.  A cutoff wall about 2 feet thick extending about 4 feet below the 
bottom of the footing is located at the toe of the abutment walls.  The bottom of footing

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes, the older West Maui 
(Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala (Pleistocene 
Epoch). The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain located between these 
two volcanoes. 
The older West Maui Volcano generally is comprised of three lava flows. The 
lower unit was formed during the Pliocene to early Pleistocene Epoch and is identified 
as the Wailuku Volcanic Series. The lavas of the Wailuku Volcanic Series generally 
consist of thinly-bedded a’a and pahoehoe lavas. Overlying this unit are lavas of the 
Honolua Volcanic Series (late Pleistocene Epoch) that consist of alkalic lavas and stiff 
trachyte.  
Volcanic inactivity occurred during the Middle Pleistocene Epoch where 
weathering of the lavas, carving of canyons, and deposition of materials occurred. 
During the late Pleistocene Epoch, renewal of volcanic activity occurred near the 
southern coast with lava flows of the Lahaina Volcanic Series. The lavas of the Lahaina 
Volcanic Series generally consist of olivine-rich basalt and nepheline basanite. 
Stream erosion of the West Maui volcano produced broad alluvial fans on the 
eastern and southwestern sides of the mountain.  The large accumulation of alluvial 
material is partly due to the loss of water from the streams to the permeable lavas of 
Haleakala that built the Maui Isthmus.  These alluvial fans may be more than 900 feet in 
thickness. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, the sea stood about 350 feet lower than the 
present sea level.  During this period, sand was blown inland and extensive lithified 
calcareous dunes were formed on the Isthmus over the alluvial fans.  Near the northern 
coast, some of the dunes are as much as 180 feet high.  The project site is located 
within the northern portion of the Isthmus of Maui and east of the West Maui Volcano.

2.2 
Site Description 
The bridge widening project site is located along the portion of Waiehu Beach 
Roadway at the Iao Stream crossing in the District of Wailuku on the Island of Maui, 
Hawaii.  The existing bridge is a three-span, concrete bridge structure.  The existing 
bridge is approximately 183 feet in length, and is about 32 feet in width (supporting 
two lanes of traffic). 
The streambed channel is lined with concrete from the sidewall aprons to the 
channel floor.  We did not encounter flowing water within Iao Stream during our field 
exploration. 
The existing ground along the bridge approaches and bridge structure generally 
slopes down away from the bridge structure at about level to thirty-five horizontal to 
one vertical (35H:1V) inclination. Steeper slopes are present at Iao Stream where the 
existing ground surface slopes down toward the stream at about 2H:1V inclination. 
Based on the topographic map provided, the elevations of the existing roadway grade 
range from about +31 to +47 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).

the fill layer, an alluvial deposit was encountered extending to 62 feet below the existing

Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the design criteria provided by the State of Hawaii - Department of 
Transportation, Highways Division, the project site will need to be evaluated based on a 
seismic acceleration coefficient of 0.28g. The following sections provide discussions on 
the seismicity of the Island of Maui and the site class for seismic design at the project 
site. 
2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the 
tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to 
volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or 
during volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the

underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not 
erupt. The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but 
most of the earthquakes are so small that only sensitive instruments can detect the 
ground motions. However, some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, 
and a few cause minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes directly associated with the movement of magma are 
concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the Island 
of Hawaii. Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over 90 percent) are 
related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground shaking 
diminishes with increased distance from the Island of Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii 
has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, 
earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of Hawaii. 
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced numerous earthquakes 
greater than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have 
occurred in the County of Maui. The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Island 
of Hawaii may be felt on the Island of Maui.  
2.4.2 Site Class 
Seismic shear wave velocity testing was performed in one of the borings to 
determine the average shear wave velocity for the underlying materials at the 
project site.  Based on the average shear wave velocity of 1,490 feet per second, 
the Iao Stream Bridge on Waiehu Beach Road may be classified from a seismic 
analysis standpoint as Site Class C in general accordance with AASHTO  LRFD 
Bridge Design Specifications (2010). 
A site-specific elastic response spectrum was developed for the Iao Stream Bridge 
on Waiehu Beach Road. The site-specific elastic response spectrum may be used 
for the seismic evaluation of the bridge. Discussion on the site-specific elastic 
response spectrum is provided in the “Discussion and Recommendations” section 
herein.

SECTION 3.  ASSESSMENT OF GEOLOGIC AND SEISMIC HAZARDS 
As part of our scope of work, we conducted an evaluation of potential geologic 
and seismic hazards that may have an impact on the Iao Stream Bridge site. The 
potential geologic and seismic hazards of concern include surface fault rupture, ground 
shaking, ground failure, tsunamis and seiches, and flooding. To evaluate the potential 
hazard associated with surface fault rupture, the proximity of the site with respect to 
nearby active fault traces generally is considered appropriate. However, seismically 
induced hazards such as ground failure and ground shaking also are dependent upon 
the local geologic and soil conditions and the magnitude and duration of the earthquake. 
Forms of ground failure can be separated into landsliding, liquefaction, lurching, lateral 
spreading, and differential compaction. 
3.1 
Surface Fault Rupture 
Based on our review of the geologic maps, the closest known significant mapped 
fault zones are located near the peak of the West Maui Volcano on the Island of Maui. 
The potential fault zone at the peak of the West Maui Volcano is located about 7 miles 
southwest from the project site. Due to the difference in elevation and distance from the 
fault, the potential for surface fault rupture at the bridge site is low. 
3.2 
Ground Shaking 
The Island of Maui is located within a region of moderate to high seismicity 
associated primarily with the volcanic activity occurring on/near the Island of Hawaii. A 
major earthquake associated with volcanic activity and faulting is likely to result in 
moderate to high ground shaking. 
3.3 
Soil Liquefaction 
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated granular soils undergo a 
substantial loss of strength and deformation due to increased pore pressure resulting 
from cyclic stress application induced by earthquakes. Soils that are likely to liquefy 
include saturated granular soils that are relatively clean and loose, uniformly graded silt, 
and fine sand deposits.

The bridge site is underlain by medium dense to very dense boulders and 
cobbles, and silty gravels and sands. The boulders and cobbles are not considered 
liquefiable due to their large aggregate size.  In addition, the silty gravels and sands 
have a low liquefiable potential. Therefore, the potential for liquefaction at this site is low 
to negligible. 
3.4 
Differential Compaction 
Differential or dynamic compaction is a term that describes the process of 
densification and settlement of relatively loose, dry, or partially saturated, granular 
deposits during earthquake ground shaking. 
The loose, poorly graded dune sands encountered near the ground surface at 
the north side of the bridge site have a low resistance to volume change during 
earthquake shaking. Therefore, we believe that the loose dune sands near the ground 
surface of the north approach to the bridge site have a moderate potential for significant 
differential compaction and ground settlement. 
3.5 
Ground Lurching and Lateral Spreading 
Lurching is a term used to describe a limited displacement of earth towards a 
free face such as a creek bank, slope, or cliff as a result of earthquake ground shaking. 
The existing approach embankments for the bridge abutments may be considered a 
free face feature. The embankment soils on the north side of the bridge site consist of 
poorly graded dune sand that are weakly cemented in portions of the deposit indicating 
that the potential for lurching is moderate. 
Lateral spreading is a limited type of ground failure associated with liquefaction. 
Due to the negligible potential for liquefaction, the potential for lateral spreading at the 
bridge site is non-existent. 
3.6 
Landsliding 
A concrete lined channel is located at the bridge site.  In addition, medium dense 
to very dense ground conditions were encountered at the project site. Based on the 
medium dense to dense ground conditions and the existing concrete channel lining, we 
believe the potential for landsliding is low.

3.7 
Tsunamis and Seiches 
A tsunami is a seismic sea wave generated by rapid movement of the sea floor 
during strong earthquakes. These waves can cause considerable damage in low lying 
coastal and bay shoreline areas. Considering the ground elevation of the bridge site at 
approximately +18 to +44 feet MSL, the potential hazard for a tsunami is considered 
low. 
A seiche is a standing-wave oscillation of the surface of an enclosed or 
semi-enclosed body of water that can be generated during an earthquake. The closest 
semi-enclosed body of water is Kahului Harbor, which is located about 1 mile from the 
bridge site. Since Iao Stream does not flow into Kahului Harbor, seiches are not 
considered a potential hazard at the bridge site. 
3.8 
Seismically-Induced Flooding 
The bridge site is not located within the flood path of any nearby reservoirs. No 
significant bodies of water are located nearby or upslope from the bridge site, therefore, 
the potential for seismically-induced flooding is low. 
3.9 
Flooding 
Based on a review of Flood Insurance Rate Maps (FIRM) provided by the 
National Flood Insurance Programs, the risk of flooding at the bridge site is low.

4.2.2 Lateral Load Resistance of Shallow Foundations 
For lateral load resistance of the shallow foundation, two components of lateral load 
resistance as shown on Plate 5 may be used in combination. It should be noted that 
the lateral load resistance of shallow foundations is primarily from friction and 
adhesion (where provided) between the base of the footings and the supporting 
subgrade materials. The passive pressure resistance component should be 
secondary lateral load resistance mechanisms. 
The component of passive pressure resistance should be reduced to account for 
strain compatibility due to the depth of the footings. Please note that the amount of 
deflection needed to mobilize the full passive pressure is indicated on Plate 5.  For 
deflections less than the deflection needed to mobilize full passive pressure, a 
linearly interpolated reduced passive pressure may be used in evaluating the lateral 
load resistance of the footing.

4.2.3 Lateral Load Resistance of Abutment Fills 
The abutment structure may be considered as a large footing bearing on the 
abutment fill soils for resistance of lateral loads during a seismic event in the 
longitudinal direction. For lateral loads imposed on the abutments, the stiffness of 
the abutment fill soils will generally be mobilized prior to the lateral resistance of its 
footings. 
Provided that intimate contact exists between the backfill soil and the abutments, a 
soil stiffness spring equal to approximately 4 ksf per inch of deflection may be used 
in resisting lateral loads. To reduce the potential for shear failure in the abutment fill 
soils, the lateral deflection should be limited to 1.25 inches or less. Therefore, the 
maximum lateral load resistance of the abutment structure should be limited to 
5 kips per square foot of abutment wall face. The structural engineer should check 
the structure displacement required in order to engage the abutment fill soils for 
lateral load resistance. 
Please note that the stiffness of the abutment fill soils only applies to forces that 
cause the abutment wall to move into the backfill soil (longitudinal direction normal 
to the abutment wall face). Therefore, only the lateral load resistance of the 
abutment foundation and wing walls may be utilized to resist the lateral loads in the 
transverse direction. 
4.2.4 Active Lateral Earth Pressure 
Based on our field exploration, loose to very dense fill materials consisting of sands, 
silty sands and silty gravels are anticipated behind the abutment walls.  For 
free-standing wall condition with level backfill, an equivalent fluid pressure of 
35 pounds per cubic feet may be used for the active earth pressures.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
eleven borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1, 2, 2A, 3 through 10, advanced to 
maximum depths of about 15 to 62 feet below the existing ground surface.  The borings 
were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with auger and coring equipment.  
The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plates 2.1 and 2.2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis.  Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified 
Soil Classification System, as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A.  Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1.1 through A-11. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings in general 
accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches.  In 
addition, some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance 
with ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop.  The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Core samples of boulder and cobble materials encountered at the site were 
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2113, Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation.  Core drilling is a rotary 
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the boulders and cobbles.  The material 
left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and 
description. 
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses.  Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered 
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt.  For example, if 3.7 feet of 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.

Seismic Shear Wave Velocity Test 
Seismic shear wave velocity profiling of the subsurface materials was performed 
using seismic Cone Penetration Testing (SCPT) equipment. Shear wave velocity testing 
using seismic cone penetration testing equipment was performed at one selected boring 
location, designated as Boring No. 3, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.2. 
The seismic shear wave velocity profiling was performed at approximately 6-foot 
intervals extending to a depth of about 58 feet below the existing ground surface. In 
order to conduct the shear wave velocity test in the boring, the test boring generally was 
advanced utilizing rotary coring methods to the maximum depth of the boring. Logs of 
the materials encountered in the boring are presented in the Logs of Borings in 
Appendix A. After the boring was advanced to the maximum depth of the borehole, the 
bored hole was backfilled with 0.25-inch diameter coated bentonite pellets. The 
temporary casing from the coring operations was used as a tremie pipe to place the 
bentonite pellets starting from the bottom and advancing upward. Water was added to 
the bentonite pellets in the borehole. As the bentonite pellets hydrate, they swell and 
soften. The probes were then pushed through the softened bentonite extending to a 
depth of about 58 feet below the existing ground surface using the seismic piezocone 
penetration testing equipment (SCPTU). 
The seismic shear wave velocity test consists of hydraulically pushing a 10-ton 
steel electronic subtraction cone into the bentonite backfilled borehole. The cone has an 
apex angle of 60 degrees and a projected surface area of 1.55 square inches 
(10 square centimeters) equipped with an electronic depth sensor attached to the cone 
rod. The seismic cone carries a uniaxial horizontal geophone to detect the arrival of a 
shear wave generated and propagated from the ground surface. Seismic measurements 
are made when the SCPT has stopped and a shear wave is sent into the subsurface. A 
shear wave is generated at the surface by striking a loaded plank with a switched 
hammer. The propagation time of the wave from the hammer blow to the cone was 
measured at each discrete depth interval. The vector difference of these depths divided 
by the time difference for the shear wave to arrive at the various depths provided the 
average shear wave velocity over the depth interval. 
The shear wave velocities measured and the average shear wave velocities 
calculated for the top 58 feet of the soil profile at the boring location are presented on 
Plates C-1.1 through C-1.3 in Appendix C.
