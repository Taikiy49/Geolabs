REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Kaho olawe is located about 11 kilometers to the south of Maui and
about 27 kilometers to the southeast of Lana i as shown on the General Site Plan Plate 1
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4073 00 B Page 6
Kaho olawe is approximately 117 square kilometers in land area with a maximum elevation
of about 450 meters above sea level at the Lua Makika summit on the eastern portion of
the island
Kaho olawe is believed to be composed of a single shield volcano formed by the
extrusion of successive layered thinly bedded pahoehoe and a a basalt lava flows that
emanated from the rift zones and caldera at Lua Makika The primary eruptive rift zones of
the former volcano extend westerly from the summit and to a lesser degree to the north and
east from the summit Two cinder cone remnants can be seen located at Puu Moiwi and Puu
Kamama The area at Lua Kealialalo is believed to represent the weathered remains of a
collapsed crater at the crest of the broad lava shield Some of the more recent aged rocks
composing the upper caldera filling layers of the island have been dated at about 1 million
years old
Intermittent stream flow has incised gullies and some deep gulches into the residual
soil and weathered rock surface of the island The climate is generally arid due to the leeward
location with respect to the island of Maui Most rainfall occurs during large Kona storm
events and as light misting showers carried by the tradewinds The geomorphology of the
island is most significantly affected by wind erosion which has created a generally barren
hard pan soil surface The lower slopes of the island show exposure of bare rock with thin soil
covering suggesting that some ocean wave erosion had occurred during higher stands of the
sea during the Pleistocene Epoch It is believed that much of the soil cover existing below
about elevation 240 meters Mean Sea Level MSL was historically swept into the ocean by
a large tsunami Similar large scale soil erosion and coral fragment deposition have been
found on the Islands of Maui and Lana i

The RC OC project area is located in the vicinity of Lua Kealialalo which is situated
at the central portion of the western half of the island of Kaho olawe as shown on the General
Site Plan Plate comprises the crest of the central upland backbone of the
island The project site generally consists of relatively gently sloping terrain underlain by
GEOLABS HAWAII

brown and reddish brown silty sand with some clays representing both residual and saprolite
soil products derived from the complete weathering of basalt rock Photographs showing the

The existing ground surface elevations range from about 251 meters MSL at the
southeastern portion of the site near the beginning of the proposed RC OC access road to
about 232 meters MSL at the northwestern portion of the site near the proposed
Pad No 3 The site slopes down gently toward the northwestern direction from the upland
plateau area located to the south of the RC OC project site
In general the RC OC project site consists of gently sloping terrain vegetated with
scattered Kiawe trees and dryland grass Much of the windswept ground surface is barren
and devoid of vegetation thus exhibiting a hardpan soil surface At the time of our site visit
some existing wooden structures were observed at the LZ Seagull site The ground surface
is covered with a scatter of loose friable gravel fragments believed to represent mineral
concretions and basalt rock fragments created by the weathering of basalt rock materials
Some basalt boulders were observed at the ground surface scattered throughout the
proposed RC OC site The boulders were observed to be generally spheroidal in shape and
slightly to moderately weathered The boulders are believed to represent erosional remnant
basalt rock and were observed to be on the order of about 0 5 to 1 meter in diameter Some
piles of boulders and a few boulders larger than about 1 meter in size were observed along
the southwestern margin of the proposed RC OC project site however we believe that these
large boulders likely exist outside of the RC OC project limits
The land area encompassing the proposed RC OC facility slopes gradually down to
the north and northwest and contains several existing wood framed sheds and the existing
LZ Seagull helicopter landing pad Steeper slopes border the southwest through northeast
perimeter of the site These slopes support an irregular topography of dissected gullies

SURFACE SOIL CONDITIONS
The soils observed at the RC OC project site appear to be relatively uniform in
appearance and may be classified as low plasticity silts and clays with sands and some gravel
representing the in situ residual and saprolitic soils derived from the complete weathering of
basalt rock In general the on site soils were observed as reddish brown brown and
yellowish tan mottled silts and clays with sands and some embedded highly weathered
cobbles and boulders The dry and friable soils typically represent saprolite soils generally
defined as completely weathered rock imparting some visible remnant rock texture The
clumps of soil produced by manual excavation may be broken down relatively easy by hand
to constituents of clay silt sand and some friable gravel The ground surface is generally
compact and scoured of loose soil deposits
Based on our site reconnaissance the soils appear to be very stiff though friable due
in part to the arid environment The soils appear to have a low shrink swell potential Most
of the flat to gently sloping terrain has developed a hardpan crust of soil which is relatively
resistant to the initiation of erosion Abrasion and scouring by wind appears to be responsible
for the formation of the surficial hardpan crust However once the surficial crust is exposed
resistance to erosion from stormwater runoff is lowered forming gullies
Significant topsoil development and the occurrence of loosely deposited alluvial and or
windblown materials were not observed likely due to exposure to the stripping action of wind
When excavated the on site soils were observed to have a blocky and friable texture that
promotes the formation of soil clumps The soil clumps may be broken down fo fine particles
of clay silt sand and some gravel Based on our observation of the RC OC project site and

encountered buried in the subsurface profile Where encountered during construction these
boulders may require processing such as crushing for use as fill and soil cement pavement
or alternatively may require removal and disposal from the project site

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field reconnaissance and laboratory test results we believe that the
principal geotechnical engineering considerations for construction of the proposed Range
Control and Operations Center facility include the following
e The near surface soils are relatively dry and will require a significant amount of
water to moisture condition the soils for site preparation grading and
soil cement mixing
e Excavation of the near surface soils may encounter soil clumps cobbles and
boulders which would affect the mixing of the on site soils to form a
soil cement pavement structure especially in cut subgrades
Proper backfilling around the excavated areas and grading to shed water away
from and around the building structures slabs pavements and heli pads
As previously mentioned the on site near surface soils exist in a relatively dry
condition about 10 to 20 percent below the optimum moisture content Therefore in order
to prepare the on site soils from the existing conditions to near the optimum moisture content
for site preparation grading and soil cement mixing a large amount of water will be needed
at the site to facilitate the site grading work Based on our estimates we believe that
approximately 220 to 300 liters of water would be needed for each cubic meter of controlled
fill for the grading operations Therefore an adequate water supply and number of water
trucks should be available at the site to moisture condition the on site soils to the specified
moisture contents to prepare the subgrades and work the on site soils as controlled fills
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4073 00 B Page 10
It should be noted that the soil cement pavement mixture is quite sensitive to the
moisture content of the on site soils and during the mixing procedures The contractor should
avoid over watering of the soil cement pavement mixture which would reduce the unconfined
compressive strength of the soil cement product Conversely if too little water is used in the
mixing of the soil cement the specified unconfined compressive strength may not be
achieved Therefore care should be exercised by the contractor to work with the on site
materials in a relatively small range of moisture contents for the soil cement pavement
structure Based on our soil cement testing performed for the project we believe that the
range of moisture contents for soil cement mixing should be between about 5 percent below
the optimum moisture and the optimum moisture content based on ASTM Test Designation
D 698

Building Foundations
We understand that it is desired to utilize post and beam construction for support of
the proposed modular buildings Therefore we envision that the building posts will be
supported on a concrete pad foundation bearing on the near surface soils at the site Based
on the near surface soil conditions anticipated at the project site we believe that the
recompacted near surface soils should provide adequate foundation support for the proposed
modular buildings For foundations bearing on the recompacted silty soils or compacted fill
an allowable bearing pressure on the order of about may be used for
design of foundations The bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased
by one third for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
Since the existing near surface silty soils may become friable when exposed to the
windy environment and or lose substantial bearing strength upon saturation we recommend
that the concrete foundations be embedded below the zone of seasonal moisture fluctuations

W O 4073 00 B Page 11
As a minimum we recommend that the concrete foundations be embedded at least
500 millimeters mm below the lowest adjacent finished grades In addition special attention
should be given to the recommendations presented in the Drainage section of this report for
post and beam type foundations
Where footings are located adjacent to slopes or on slopes the footings should be
embedded to a sufficient depth to provide a minimum horizontal set back distance of at least
2 meters measured from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope If
foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be embedded below
a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom
edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines
lf the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with the
recommendations presented herein we estimate that footing settlements under the
anticipated light design loads for footings bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or
compacted fill as recommended herein to be less than 25 mm total with differential
settlements on the order of 15 mm
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance between
the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth pressure
developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings A coefficient
of friction of 0 30 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or
compacted fill Resistance due to passive pressure may be calculated using an equivalent
fluid pressure of 50 kilonewton per cubic meter kKN m for footings embedded in the
recompacted in situ silty soils and compacted fill These values assume that the concrete for
footings is placed directly against well compacted materials or cast neat against undisturbed
material Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 300 mm
below the finished grade should be neglected
Uplift loads acting on the foundations may be resisted by the dead weight of the
footings and the side shear along the vertical faces of the footings A side shear value of

Slabs On Grade Concrete Pads
Based on the plans provided we envision that some concrete pads will be constructed
at the site The near surface soils at the site appear to exhibit a low shrink swell potential
when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture content However the near surface soils
appear to be susceptible to erosion and loss of strength when saturated Based on the
generally stiff subsoil conditions anticipated at the site we believe that slabs on grade with
thickened edges bearing on the recompacted on site soils or compacted fill may be used for
support of the proposed concrete pads
For slabs on grade bearing on the recompacted on site soils or compacted fill the slab
subgrade should be scarified to at least 200 mm moisture conditioned to between 5 percent
below the optimum and optimum moisture content and compacted to not less than 95 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 698 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Perimeter thickened edges should be embedded a minimum of 300 mm below the
lowest adjacent finished grade The base of the thickened edge should have a minimum
width of 300 mm The bottom of the thickened edge excavations should be recompacted to
a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction prior to placement of reinforcing steel or
concrete If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of thickened edge
excavations they should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The
over excavation should be backfilled with controlled fill compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4073 00 B Page 13
The slab subgrades should be kept moist prior to placement of concrete To reduce
the potential for drying of the subgrade soils and to reduce the costs of form construction we
believe that the slab edges should be cast neai against the soils The slab edges should
incorporate a sufficient amount of top and bottom longitudinal steel reinforcement The top
and bottom steel reinforcement bars should be connected by stir ups The ends of the
stir ups should extend into the floor slab area and should be tied to the welded wire mesh
In general we recommend that a 150 mm layer of aggregate base course consisting
of crushed aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction based on
ASTM D 1557 be provided below the concrete slabs for more uniform support The
thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at least 300 mm
below the lowest adjacent grade The area adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly
against the slab with compacted on site soils and graded to divert water away from the slab
to reduce the potential for water ponding around the slabs
Soil Cement Pavement
We understand that the RC OC access road and the access roads leading to the three
Heli Pads from the RC OC access road are planned to be surfaced with soil cement using the
on site fine grained soils We also understand that the access roads will generally be
subjected to relatively light duty vehicles such as pedestrian traffic pick up trucks light
vehicles and occasional moderately loaded trucks
Based on the relatively light traffic loading conditions anticipated for these access road
pavements we believe that a 150 mm thick soil cement pavement may be used for the
access road pavement section Our laboratory tests conducted for the overall project indicate
that an unconfined compressive strength of day curing period may be achieved
based on a cement content of 22 percent by weight in laboratory To account for the field
environment which is not as ideal as in the laboratory the cement content is normally
increased by 1 to 2 percent for field conditions For this project considering the relatively high
cement content needed to achieve the desired unconfined compressive strength of 5 MPa
we believe that a 1 percent increase in cement content would be reasonable Therefore we
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4073 00 B Page 14
recommend that a cement content of 23 percent by weight be used for the RC OC access
roads for cost estimating purposes The relationship between unconfined compressive
strength and cement content for the various soil samples conducted for the overall project is
presented on Plate 4 for reference It should also be noted that additional soil cement testing
should be conducted during the construction phase of the project to confirm and or modify the
cement content of the soil cement pavement
The soil cement pavement should be moisture conditioned to between 5 percent
below the optimum moisture and the optimum moisture content based on ASTM Test
Designation D 698 and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 558
Prior to placement of the soil cement pavement structure the subgrade soils should
be scarified to a depth of about 200 mm moisture conditioned to between 5 percent below
the optimum moisture and the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction The subgrades should be finished to provide a relatively
smooth and unyielding surface prior to placement of the soil cement pavement structure
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D 698 test procedures Standard Proctor Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Helicopter Landing Pads
As previously mentioned the three helicopter landing pads are to be designed to
accommodate CH 53 helicopters Based on the plans provided we understand that the
helicopter landing pads will be constructed with Perforated Steel Plate PSP landing mats
placed over a firm base Based on the anticipated site conditions we recommend that the
base below the PSP landing mats consist of at least 150 mm of aggregate base course in
order to provide a firm base on which to construct the PSP landing mats and to withstand
the repeated loading from the helicopter take offs and landing As an alternative to the use
of aggregate base course a layer of soil cement base may be used as the base in lieu of
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4073 00 B Page 15
the aggregate base course The soil cement base should be a minimum of 200 mm thick
The recommendations presented in the Soil Cement Pavement section may also be used
for the soil cement base for the helicopter landing pads
We understand that a piastic membrane is to be provided below the aggregate base
course to reduce the potential for migration of POL deeper into the subsurface We
recommend that the plastic membrane below the aggregate base course be cushioned by
50 mm of fine sand above the plastic membrane to aid in protecting the plastic membrane
from damage due to the compaction operations of the aggregate base course The plastic
membrane should be placed on 50 mm of fine sand The fine sand should be moistened
and densified with a vibratory plate prior to placement of the aggregate base course The
150 mm thick aggregate base course may be placed in one lift and should be compacted
to at least 95 percent relative compaction based on ASTM Test Designation D 1557
Because the base of the PSP landing mats will consist of aggregate base course
there may be a concern with Foreign Object Debris FOD from the potentially loose gravel
exposed at the Heli Pad areas Therefore consideration may be given to the use of some
cement treatment in the top 75 to 100 mm of the aggregate base course to reduce the
potential for FOD at the surface of the Heli Pads For preliminary estimating purposes the
cement content for the cement treated surface may be assumed to be about 4 percent by
weight to provide some cohesion and to reduce the hazard associated with FOD

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials including the cobbles and boulders noted at the site should be removed
and disposed of properly off site or stockpiled in a designated area to reduce the
potential for contamination of the excavated materials
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should
be backfilled with well compacted general fill or controlled fill The excavated soft soils
should be properly disposed of off site and or stockpiled in a designated area where
appropriate
Finished subgrades and areas designated to receive fills should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 200 mm moisture conditioned to between 5 percent below the
optimum moisture and the optimum moisture content and compacted to a mininum

of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 698 Optimum moisture is
the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend
that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or that the soil subgrade be
prepared again as recommended above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the
exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted general fill
controlled fill
Fills and Backfills
In general the excavated on site soils may be re used as a source of general fill or
controlled fill materials provided that they are free of vegetation and deleterious
materials and the maximum particle size should be less than 150 mm in maximum
dimension It should be noted that the on site soils appear to be relatively dry
between 10 and 20 percent below the optimum moisture content Therefore an
ample water supply and number of water trucks will be necessary during the grading
operations to provide adequate water to achieve the specified moisture and
compaction requirements
Based on the remote location of the project site from available sources of imported fill
material we do not envision the need for imported general fill material for this project
Where additional general fill material is necessary we envision that the fill material
would be obtained from nearby projects on the island Therefore the borrow materials
should conform to the requirements of general fill as described above In addition we
understand that the project will likely import the aggregate base course

specified below the PSP landing mats for the Heli Pad construction and below the
concrete pads The aggregate base course should consist of crushed basalt
aggregate with a maximum particle size of 50 mm and should contain not more than
10 percent particles less than in size
As previously mentioned a plastic membrane polyethylene sheet is to be provided
below the aggregate base course to reduce the potential for migration of POL
deeper into the subsurface To provide some protection of the plastic membrane
from damage due to the placement of the aggregate base course it is advisable to
provide a minimum of 50 mm of sand below and above the plastic membrane as a
cushion layer The sand layer should have a maximum particle size not to exceed
13 mm and should be moistened and densified with a vibratory plate to a dense
state prior to placement of the aggregate base course above the sand
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
General fill materials and or controlled fill should be moisture conditioned to between
5 percent below the optimum moisture and the optimum moisture content placed in
level lifts not exceeding 200 mm in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density
of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 698 test procedures Optimum moisture is
the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
The aggregate base course materials should be moisture conditioned placed in level
lifts not exceeding 200 mm in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction based on ASTM Test Designation D 1557
Cut and Fill Slopes
We anticipate that some minor cut and fill slopes on the order of 2 meters or less will
be constructed at the site to provide the level pads for the proposed construction We
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4073 00 B Page 19
envision that the cut slopes would generally expose the residual and saprolite soils
described in the Surface Soil Conditions section of this report In general we believe
that cut slopes exposing the very stiff residual and saprolite soils may be designed with
a slope inclination of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter As previously
indicated graded surfaces exposing the on site materials are highly susceptible to
erosion by wind and surface water run off Therefore freshly graded cut slopes should
be protected by appropriate slope planting or other means to reduce the potential for
erosion of the exposed materials as soon as possible after the slope faces are
exposed
Permanent fill slopes constructed using compacted general fill or controlled fill
materials may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fills placed
on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the existing slope
to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations should start
at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in
accordance with the fill placement recommendations presented herein In general
fill stopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope
ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face In addition slope planting should be
provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished
slopes
Drainage
The finished grades adjacent to and beyond the soil cement pavements and heli pads
should be sloped to shed water away from the pavements and pads to reduce the potential
for ponding In addition the finished grades outside the building structures and concrete pads
should be sloped to shed water away from foundations and slabs It is also advised that
gutter systems be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and slab areas Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site
during or after construction
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4073 00 B Page 20
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the building
foundations slabs on grade soil cement pavements and heli pads since ponded water could
cause subsurface soil saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The edges of
the foundation slab and heli pad excavations should be properly backfilled to reduce water
infiltration In addition drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should
be maintained to drain surface water run off away from the buildings slabs soil cement
pavements and heli pads
