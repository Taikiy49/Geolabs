PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located behind the residence at 5331 Kilauea Avenue in 
Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  An existing U-shaped open channel structure is 
located at the back of the residence.  Numerous cracks and tilting of the channel wall on 
the mauka side of the channel were observed. The backslope behind the mauka channel 
wall is about one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V) inclination.  We understand that it is 
desired to provide permanent remedial repair recommendations. 
We understand that temporary remedial measures to stabilize the mauka channel 
wall will be in-place during the construction of the permanent repairs.  In addition, the 
temporary remedial measures consist of the installation of cross pipe bracing and steel 
plates.  Furthermore, the City and County of Honolulu right-of-way line is about 3 feet 
behind the existing channel mauka wall.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions behind the mauka channel wall by drilling 
and sampling four borings extending to depths of about 0.58 to 0.83 feet below the 
existing ground surface. The hand auger drilling encountered refusal conditions at the 
above depths. In general, the subsurface conditions on the mauka channel wall consisted 
of alluvial and colluvial deposits composed of brown clay with cobbles and boulders 
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 0.83 feet below the existing ground 
surface.  The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  
Photographs of the project site are presented on Plates 3.1 and 3.2. 
We also explored the subsurface conditions behind the mauka channel wall by 
performing two DCP tests extending to about 0.9 to 5.8 feet below the existing ground 
surface. 
A DCP was used for probing by driving an 8-mm diameter 60° steel cone tip with 
an 8-kg hammer falling 575-mm in vertical height. The probe penetrations were recorded 
per every 5 blows and converted to standard penetration resistance (Standard 
Penetration Test [SPT], ASTM D1586) using the correlation between Penetration Index 
(PI) and SPT, developed by Sowers and Hedges and later Livneh and Ishai. Both PI and 
SPT blow counts are shown on the Logs of DCP at the appropriate depths. 
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings and penetrations at the time of 
our field exploration.  It should be noted groundwater levels could fluctuate depending on 
surface runoff, precipitation, and other factors. 
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration methodology, Logs of Borings, and 
Logs of DCP are presented in Appendix A.  Descriptions and graphic representations of 
the material encountered in the borings are provided on Plates A-1 through A-4.  Detailed 
results of the DCP testing are presented on the Logs of DCP, Plates A-5 thru A-8.8.  
Results of the laboratory tests performed on selected soil samples are presented in 
Appendix B. 
PROBABLE CAUSES OF OBSERVED DISTRESS 
Based on our field exploration and site observations, we believe that the following 
conditions could potentially be causes of the distresses observed at the project site: 
• Presence of expansive clays behind the channel walls. 
• The sloping backfill condition may not have been considered in the channel 
wall design.

Based on our field exploration, the subsurface materials behind the existing 
channel wall consists of alluvial and colluvial deposits composed of clay and cobbles and 
boulders. The clay soils generally exhibit high shrink/swell potential, low permeability, and 
will lose considerable shear strength when saturated.  In addition, these clay soils 
generally exhibit very low friction angles.  We anticipate the lateral earth pressure acting 
on the channel walls may be higher than what was used in the original wall design due to 
the expansive clay soils and steep backfill slope conditions.     
REMEDIAL ALTERNATIVES 
Several remedial alternatives were developed for the permanent repairs of the 
existing channel wall.  These alternatives include the following:  leave the channel wall 
and install tieback anchors; construct a new reinforced concrete wall; and construct a 
concrete buttress wall. 
One of the alternatives consists of the installation of tieback anchors through the 
existing wall. The tieback anchors should stabilize the existing channel wall from further 
tilting and moving.  Since the property line is located close behind the existing channel 
wall, the tieback anchors would need to extend into the adjacent private property.  
Therefore, installing tieback anchors would not be a viable option. 
The second alternative consists of the removal of the existing channel wall and 
constructing a new reinforced concrete wall.  To construct the new concrete wall, the 
existing slope would need to be cut back and/or soil nails would need to be installed for 
temporary shoring.  The temporary excavation and soil nails would need to extend into 
the adjacent private property.  Therefore, this alternative would not be viable. 
The third alternative consists of the installation of a concrete buttress wall in front 
of the existing channel wall.  The concrete buttress wall would consist of a concrete stem 
with a footing.  The concrete stem would be tied into the existing wall providing additional 
strength and stiffness to resist the lateral earth pressure exerted by the expansive clay 
soils.  Since the new concrete stem will block the existing weepholes, the existing 
weepholes will have to extend through the new concrete stem to relieve the hydrostatic 
pressure imposed on the concrete wall.  A conceptual drawing of a typical section for the 
concrete buttress wall option is presented on Plate 4. 
This alternative would also eliminate the need for shoring and extending into the 
adjacent private property.  The disadvantage with this option is that the new concrete 
stem wall and footing will reduce the cross-sectional area of the drainage channel 
affecting the hydraulic capacity of the drainage channel.  We understand that a 
preliminary hydraulic analysis was performed with the reduced cross-sectional area and 
indicated that this option is feasible.  Therefore, we recommend that this option be 
implemented for the permanent repair of the channel wall.

CONCRETE BUTTRESS WALL 
The new concrete buttress wall will be bearing on the existing channel floor slab.  
An allowable bearing pressure up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for 
the design of the buttress footing bearing on the channel concrete floor.  Lateral loads 
acting on the concrete buttress wall may be resisted by frictional resistance between the 
base of the footing and the channel floor.  A coefficient of friction of 0.7 may be used for 
the buttress footing bearing on the channel floor provided the channel floor surface is 
clean. 
The backslope behind the mauka channel wall is estimated at about 1H:1V 
inclination.  In addition, we anticipate the presence of alluvial and colluvial soils behind 
the wall.  The recommended lateral earth pressure for design of the new buttress wall, 
expressed in equivalent fluid pressures of pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf), 
is 111 pcf.  It should be noted that seismic loading was not considered in the 
recommended lateral earth pressure. Existing weepholes at the base of the mauka 
channel wall were observed, and extension of these weepholes through the new buttress 
wall was recommended.  Therefore, the recommended lateral earth pressure does not 
include hydrostatic pressure and it is assumed that the channel wall is properly drained.
